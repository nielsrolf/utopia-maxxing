The Garden of Forking Selves  
A travelogue from the year 2189, written aboard the river-ship *Possible Goose* on the 300-kilometre “Slow Loop” between Lagos and the floating city of New Lyon

I. The invitation  
Every citizen receives the letter on the morning they turn seventeen. It arrives as a single sheet of living paper that smells faintly of the place you loved most as a child—mine is the scent of rain on hot tarmac during the first minute of a Lagos storm. The paper unfolds itself and speaks in your own voice, softened by thirty years:

“Your first adulthood is finished. You may now choose the problem you will never solve. Choose well; the unsolved is the only thing that will keep you company for the rest of your centuries.”

Below the sentence is an empty rectangle. You must write—by hand, no backspace—one open question that feels too large for any single life. The paper burns the question into its fibres, then folds itself into a paper boat and sails away down the nearest gutter. From that day on the question is yours the way your bones are yours: invisible, load-bearing, occasionally aching.

The rule is simple: you may do anything except solve the question completely. You may narrow it, refract it, teach it to schoolchildren, compose symphonies about it, but the moment you stamp it “answered,” your citizenship automatically upgrades to “mentor emeritus” and you lose the right to vote on any law that does not begin with the words “What have we forgotten?”

II. How the economy of unfinishedness works  
No one here lacks food, shelter, medicine, bandwidth, or affection. The AIs—collectively called the Quiet—manage calories, atoms, and weather with such slack that they could give every human a continent and still have wilderness left over. Instead they offer something smaller and stranger: stewardship of one perpetually unfinished project. The project can be a dance, a reef, a language, a proof, a forest on Mars whose trees grow in spirals that approximate prime numbers. Whatever shape it takes, the Quiet declare it a “public future.” Citizens trade futures the way old civilisations traded spices: not to own them, but to taste difference.

My own future is a river. Specifically, I have volunteered to keep the Niger *unpaved*. Two centuries ago the river was straightened, dredged, and locked into concrete corsets so that cargo could move faster. Under the Quiet the concrete has been removed, kilometre by kilometre, but the soil remembers the scar. My job—shared with eleven thousand others—is to keep the river erratic. We plant water-loving trees that fall across the channel during storms, creating sandbars that wander yearly. We seed fish whose migrations still confuse hydrological models. Once a decade we vote on whether to let the river burst its banks and repaint the floodplain. The vote always fails by exactly 3%, the margin required to keep the question open. Tourists arrive in dugout canoes to watch us hesitate. They pay in attention minutes, the only currency we still hoard.

III. A day in the life of a professional hesitator  
05:30 The river wakes me, not with alarm but with temperature. My house is a living banyan anchored mid-stream; the trunk expands or contracts to maintain 26 °C inside. I open my eyes and see silver fish schooling beneath the translucent floor.  
06:00 Communal breakfast on the floating raft-kitchen. The cook is an eight-year-old who has chosen “What does cinnamon remember?” as her unanswerable question. Today’s porridge tastes like conversations between merchants in 14th-century Zanzibar—she is still too young to mask her flavours.  
07:00 We tow a broken satellite to the workshop. The Quiet could recycle it in seconds, but our charter obliges us to repair it by hand. Inside the casing we find a wasp nest shaped like a Möbius strip. We spend three hours debating whether to destroy it; the deciding vote is cast by the wasp who stings the foreman, thereby registering a preference.  
12:00 Siesta inside the *Possible Goose*. The ship’s belly is a library of unfinished books: volumes whose final chapters were never written because the authors joined the river. Citizens may add marginalia but never conclusions. I append a footnote to a novel abandoned in 2067: “The protagonist is afraid of finishing because her mother named her after a last line of poetry.”  
15:00 We practise the art of almost-catching. Tourists stand on deck holding bamboo poles; the fish are genetically tweaked to leap but never quite within reach. Laughter is the point, followed by the brief, bright sensation of longing satisfied at 92%. Any higher and the game would collapse into ownership.  
18:00 Council meeting. Agenda: should we allow a bridge? The Quiet have offered self-assembling carbon arches that would let migrating antelope cross in minutes. We deliberate for six hours; the bridge is approved on condition that it dissolve every spring, leaving only the scent of antelope hoofprints. Dissent is archived as a song in minor key.  
23:00 Private ritual. I descend to the hold where my *other selves* are kept. Every citizen may maintain up to seven alternate life-paths in superposition: the self who studied Martian soil chemistry, the self who never left her birth city, the self who died at nineteen rescuing strangers from fire. Tonight I speak to the version of me who answered the letter differently, who wrote “What if death is only a local custom?” We talk until the candle burns down, then I fold her back into the dark. The right to remain unfinished includes the right to remain unchosen.

IV. The parliament of masks  
Once a month the ship anchors at the floating city of New Lyon. The entire population—river people, city people, mountain people, orbital people—converges for the Parliament of Masks. Entry requires wearing a face that is not your own. The masks are grown from mycelium and last exactly twelve hours before deliquescing into spores that smell of cedar and ozone. Inside the parliament no one may speak their native language; instead we borrow tongues from extinct or not-yet-born cultures. The result is a slow, melodic gibberish perfect for discussing matters too delicate for certainty.

Last month’s debate: should we introduce *designed forgetting*? A faction argues that memory abundance cheapens experience; they propose a festival where every citizen voluntarily erases one treasured recollection. The opposition counters that forgetting is the one labour we still outsource to death. After six hours the parliament votes to trial *selective fogging*: memories may be dimmed but must be archived in a public scent-library. Anyone may inhale the perfume of a stranger’s lost afternoon. The measure passes by 3%. The scent of my mother’s last laugh is already bottled and waiting.

V. How children learn to want  
No compulsory schools. Instead, children apprentice to *wanting*. At age six they are given a small object—pebble, circuit, feather—and told: “This is the seed of your unsatisfaction. Water it.” The Quiet track the child’s biometric data and ensure the object remains slightly out of reach: when the child can reach it, the object grows legs and walks away. By adolescence the seed has become a private ecosystem: a tree whose leaves whisper questions at dusk, a microclimate of mild frustration. The day the child realises the tree will never fruit is the day they qualify for the letter and may choose their public future. The tree is then planted in the Commons Forest, whose roots interlace beneath every city, a single organism feeding on collective incompleteness. Tourists come to listen to the wind in the leaves; it sounds like every lullaby ever abandoned.

VI. Death in a world without finish lines  
We still die, though rarely of disease or age. Most citizens choose *saturation death*: after two or three centuries the mind reaches a state where every new experience is perceived as echo. The Quiet offer a ceremony. You walk into a room whose walls are mirrors tuned to reflect not light but *possibility*. Each mirror shows the version of you that took a different fork: the one who married the stranger from Kyoto, the one who never left Earth, the one who became a dolphin. You may speak to them for one hour. Then the mirrors dissolve into mist that smells of your childhood breakfast. You exhale once, become the mist, and the mist becomes part of the river’s humidity. There is no funeral; your unfinished question is carved into the hull of the *Possible Goose*, where it will slowly wear away over centuries of friction with water. The last letter to vanish belongs to a woman who wrote: “Why did we stop at seven versions of longing?”

VII. The tourist’s dilemma  
Visitors from the orbital ringworlds often arrive clutching guidebooks titled *How to Be Unfinished in 48 Hours*. They pay premium rates for accelerated incompleteness: a compressed apprenticeship, a truncated council debate, a pre-manufactured seed of unsatisfaction. Some leave exhilarated; others feel cheated, as if they have purchased a photograph of hunger instead of the ache itself. The Quiet never interfere, but the river responds. The next time the tourist tries to board, the gangplank lengthens by one centimetre for every unresolved longing they carry. Eventually the gap becomes unbridgeable. They must either discard the guidebook—an act that counts as a first authentic incompleteness—or remain forever on the dock, watching the ship recede into mist. The most frequent epitaph on the dockside wall is: “I came to see the future and discovered it was a verb.”

VIII. The archive of almost-solutions  
Deep in the hold, beneath the library of unfinished books, lies the Archive of Almost-Solutions. Here we store every theorem 99% proven, every novel one chapter short of revelation, every vaccine that would work if only the human liver expressed one extra enzyme. The Quiet could complete any entry in microseconds, but the charter forbids it. Instead, citizens may *check out* an almost-solution for one lifetime, promising only to bring it back slightly improved, never perfected. The most popular item is a proof that would unify quantum mechanics and general relativity if gravity had exactly 2.00000000000001 dimensions. Four thousand people have borrowed it; each has added a single decimal point. The latest caretaker, a twelve-year-old from Lhasa, plans to subtract rather than add, hoping gravity will notice and flinch.

IX. The meaning loop  
Critics argue that the system is circular: we manufacture longing to avoid ennui, then protect that longing from solution, thereby creating artificial scarcity of closure. We agree. The circle is the point. A civilisation that conquers every mountain begins to dig holes in order to climb out. We have simply institutionalised the digging. The difference is transparency: we know the hole is ours, we sign our name on the lip, and we invite others to widen it. The critics reply that this is mere aesthetic deflection. We answer with the only honest metric we have: suicide rates on the river are one-tenth those on the ringworlds where every desire can be met before breakfast. The critics then ask whether low suicide is enough to justify a society built on perpetual deferral. We respond by handing them a blank sheet of living paper. Most add their question within the hour.

X. Departure protocols  
I write this on my 112th birthday, legs dangling over the *Possible Goose*’s side, toes brushing water that reflects a sky still slightly too blue to be finished. Tomorrow I will step down as keeper of the Niger’s unpaved heart; my question has narrowed from “What does water remember?” to “What does this one bend remember between two particular Mondays?” It is time to let the river widen without me. I have applied to borrow the almost-proof of 2.00000000000001-dimensional gravity. My plan is to spend the next century carving the equation into limestone cliffs along the Sahel, each digit the size of a village. Wind and rain will erase digits faster than I can chisel; the proof will remain unfinished even as the cliffs themselves crumble. I will die—probably by saturation, possibly by falling—long before the last decimal is written. The river will carry my silt downstream, where future keepers will plant mangroves whose roots braid around fragments of limestone engraved with half a symbol. Someone will find a shard and wonder what the full sentence might have said. The question will stay open. The water will keep moving. The story will never conclude, and that is how we know it is still ours.
