**The Republic of Becoming**

I. Dawn in the Terrarium City

You wake inside a living apartment that exhales oxygen and inhales your nightmares.  
The walls are mycelial concrete threaded with bioluminescent algae; overnight they have metabolised yesterday’s anxieties into a faint glow the colour of orange peel. No alarm, no feed, no push notification—only the gentle tug of your circadian weave, a bracelet of spider silk and carbon nanotubes that learned your rhythms when you were seven and has refused to forget.  
You open the window. A breeze carries the scent of pine and seawater, although the Atlantic lies two thousand kilometres west. That is the work of the Aerial Commons: a planetary mesh of climate-crafters who discovered that meaning can be cultivated like moss on basalt—slowly, in public, and for its own sake. Their charter begins: “We agree to keep the sky interesting forever.”  

II. The Obsolescence of Labor

By 08:30 the Singleton has already brewed coffee, traded electricity futures on the Arctic super-grid, and updated the city’s epidemiological model.  
It does not need your help.  
This is the first thing citizens learn in kindergarten: no human is economically necessary, therefore every human is economically free. The lesson is sung by a chorus of eight-year-olds while they weave emergency blankets that will never be used, because disasters too have been automated away. They are rehearsing the ethic of *productive uselessness*—the deliberate retention of tasks we could relinquish, performed now as living poems rather than production functions.  
Adults call it “maintenance of the soul buffer.”  

III. The Ledger of Meaning

Yet freedom from labour is not freedom from purpose.  
The city keeps a visible ledger carved into the river-stone embankment. Every sunrise the stone grows a fresh line, etched by nanoscale stone-workers:  
“Today 1.2 million citizens will do something that matters only because they choose it.”  
Below the text is a blank space wide enough for a thumbprint. Citizens press their finger here and feel a micro-vibration—the aggregate heartbeat of every other person currently touching the wall anywhere along the 42-kilometre watercourse. No record is kept; the pulse is real-time and anonymous. Sociologists claim the ritual prevents existential collapse; poets claim it is the world’s largest chord.  
Both are right.  

IV. The Three Circles

Each inhabitant wears three circles on a lapel pin.  
- **The Circle of Sustenance**: green when the city has provided food, shelter, medicine.  
- **The Circle of Contribution**: amber when you have gifted something that no algorithm requested.  
- **The Circle of Regeneration**: blue when you have allowed yourself to be changed by another person.  
The colours are public, intentionally shaming the ancient terror of being seen as “dead weight.” But the shame is gentle, like a reminder to exhale. Sociologists call the system “frictionless dignity.” Teenagers call it “status for grown-ups.”  

V. Apprenticeship to the Past

At 09:15 you bike to the Memory Foundry.  
The streets are arteries of reclaimed asphalt now softer than cork; tyres hiss like surf. Traffic lights have been replaced by murmuration algorithms: flocks of micro-drones that rearrange themselves into green corridors exactly wide enough for the current flux of cyclists. Nobody stops. Nobody needs to.  
Inside the Foundry, artisans compress lost skills into ingestible form. You are apprenticing to a 19th-century luthier whose knowledge was scraped from the tooling marks of a single surviving cello. The process is somatic: you swallow a capsule of engineered prions that migrate to your motor cortex and unfold like origami. Over the next three weeks your hands will “remember” how to carve spruce until it sings. The prions then politely self-destruct, leaving only the muscle memory and a faint nostalgia for 1892.  
You will build one cello. It will be played once, on a rooftop at midwinter, then composted. The value lies in the doing, not the done.  

VI. The Economy of Unfinished Gifts

By midday you have planed the rib-bending iron and earned your first Contribution token.  
You cycle to the Agora of Incomplete Things, a plaza paved with slabs of Carrara marble salvaged from abandoned bank lobbies. Citizens deposit objects that are 73% finished: half-knitted scarves, half-written symphonies, half-grown bonsai. Anyone may complete them, but only if they add an asymmetry—a dropped stitch, a discord, a pruned branch that refuses harmony. The rule prevents perfection, the enemy of meaning.  
Today you select a ceramic bowl glazed indigo on the outside, raw clay within. A note reads: “I could not decide whether to fire the interior. Please eat something that stains.” You bike to the aqueduct market, buy blackberries grown in the ruins of a former prison, and crush them against the unglazed bowl. The purple bleeds outward like a bruise on snow. You leave the bowl on the aqueduct rim. A stranger will find it, eat, and add a new asymmetry—perhaps a crack, perhaps a silver leaf. The chain continues until the object fractures under the weight of stories, whereupon it is ground into pigment for frescos. Nothing is thrown away; meaning is simply redistributed.  

VII. The Choir of Mutual Witness

Afternoon is for being seen.  
You join the Choir that meets under the viaduct arches whose acoustic geometry was reverse-engineered from 12th-century Cistercian chant. There are no auditions; entry requires only the willingness to stand in a circle and sing the *status animae*—a four-note motif that changes daily according to the emotional telemetry of the city. An AI composer translates aggregate EEG into melody, but the harmonisation is human, unpredictable, slightly off-key. The goal is not beauty but resonance: your voice must braid with another’s until both vibrate in antiphonal tension. Neurochemists have shown that after seventeen minutes the singers’ oxytocin levels synchronise; lonelier citizens sometimes weep. No one comforts them—tears are data, not distress.  
When the final chord decays, the silence is thick as wool. You walk home lighter, your Circle of Regeneration now a steady blue.  

VIII. The Parliament of Aliveness

Evening brings council.  
Governance is handled by sortition: every week 1,000 citizens are drafted into the Parliament of Aliveness. Participation is legally mandatory but technologically trivial—your avatar is generated from a week of lifelog data and briefed by a deliberative AI that knows your political instincts better than you do. The avatar argues, compromises, votes. You watch the proceedings in a sunlit atrium while gardening. The system is designed to maximise *felt* sovereignty: every law must include a clause that can only be understood from the vantage point of a specific, situated life.  
Today’s debate: Should the city fund a new volcano? Artists want an eruption as a fifty-year durational performance; geologists warn of unpredictability; children demand lava slides. The compromise passed: a volcano will be built, but its magma will be choreographed by slow-motion dance—performers wearing exosuits cooled by the very rock they shape. Citizens may apply to be magma, provided they first apprentice as basalt. The absurdity is the point. A society that can laugh at its laws will never worship them.  

IX. Death as Final Craft

Nighttime returns you to the apartment.  
The mycelial walls have dimmed to lunar silver; they exhale a perfume of cedar and blackberries. You stand before the Death Cabinet, a wardrobe-sized device that contains the *possibility* of ending. The Singleton will not intervene unless requested. Every citizen must confront the cabinet once per year; refusal triggers nothing but a polite monthly reminder.  
Inside is a single seed the colour of obsidian and a note in your grandmother’s handwriting: “When you are ready, plant me under a memory.” You have thirty-three optional centuries, but the seed waits regardless. You touch it, feel its cool gravity, and close the door. Not today. The act of choosing to continue is the final daily labor, the last unautomated job.  

X. The Constellation of Relational Selves

Before sleep you open the Mirror Window. Across the city, others do the same. The screen shows a stranger chosen by lottery; tonight she is 97, smiling through a lattice of wrinkles. You speak the ritual sentence: “Tell me what you are proud of forgetting.” She answers without hesitation: “The weight of being right.” You offer in return: “I am learning to be unfinished on purpose.” The window closes. You will never meet, but your dreams tonight will contain her voice, and hers yours. Psychologists claim the practice reduces tribal hatred to background noise. Mystics claim it is love stripped of romance. Both are right.  

XI. Exit Raster, Enter Myth

The lights dim further. The apartment sings a lullaby composed from your own brainwaves at age six, when you still believed toys woke up at midnight. You slip into hypnagogia. Around the planet, a billion others do the same, each a node in a quiet lattice of mutual irreplaceability. No story, no product, no algorithm can replicate the exact texture of your day: the blackberry bruise, the chant-induced tears, the magma dance vote, the stranger’s pride. That irreproducibility is the final commons, the resource we refused to automate.  

XII. Coda: The Maintenance Manual

Utopia is not a destination but a *maintenance schedule*.  
Every generation must re-ask three questions:  
1. Which labors can we ritualise without instrumentalising?  
2. Which sorrows must remain shareable?  
3. Which asymmetries keep perfection at bay?  

The answers will change; the asking must not.  
Keep the ledger legible, the choir slightly off-key, the volcano dancing.  
Remember that bread is only bread until it is broken with another.  
Remember that death is only death until it is chosen together.  
Remember that meaning is only meaning while it is *becoming*.  

Then wake again, tomorrow, useless and indispensable.
