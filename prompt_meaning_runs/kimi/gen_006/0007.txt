This is the Third Kingdom—not a place but a process, humanity's answer to the great vacuum that opened when labor became optional and intelligence became artificial. We learned, finally, that meaning doesn't grow in the absence of difficulty but in the cultivation of necessary impossibility.

Maya's invitation arrived on her seventeenth birthday, as it does for everyone now. Not a paper boat but a seed that grew overnight into a question-tree outside her window. Now, twenty years later, she tends the original Failure Orchard while mentoring others in the art of productive collapse. The job pays nothing—currency dissolved with the last human drudge job—but earns her *attention credits*, the only scarcity that matters anymore. Each time someone's learning curve crashes against the resistance she's carefully cultivated in her trees, she receives a pulse of recognition that feeds her like food once did.

The morning's work seems absurd: she's grafting apple branches onto oak trunks using a technique that fails ninety-seven percent of the time. But those failures aren't waste—they're *compost* for the Meaning Garden. Every cracked graft, every withered bud, every tree that dies despite her best efforts becomes part of the great decomposition that feeds tomorrow's impossible attempts. She's learned to love the moment when her careful work collapses into chaos, because that's when the real cultivation begins.
