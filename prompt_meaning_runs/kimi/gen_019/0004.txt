UTOPIA D  
Title: The Loom of Arriving  

Year 2195, first frost moon. The town of Kibo-na-Mto floats two metres above the Zambezi because the river asked it to. No one now remembers which engineer, botanist, or dreaming child convinced the Choir (our planet-wide, pan-species intelligence) that a mid-river settlement should refuse the tyranny of solid ground, but every dawn the town still votes to stay airborne. The vote is not symbolic; the town can land any day it chooses. It hovers because hovering feels like the right kind of unfinished.  

I wake inside a breathing wall. The dwelling is built of lime, loam, and the mycelium of honey-mushrooms that remember every story told aloud beneath them since 2062. Before opening my eyes I listen for the day’s Heartbeat Pool: 2.7 million people who have opted to share today’s cardiac cadence. Mine is chosen for me—an 83-year-old librarian in Novosibirsk who has never seen the Zambezi. Her pulse is arrhythmic, elegant, two skipped beats every minute. My wall adjusts its respiration to her pattern; the room itself becomes a Russian winter I will never physically touch. I taste coffee, but the mug is only half-full. The rest of the volume is virtual, generated by the Choir so that a stranger somewhere may sip the missing mouthful and taste my memory of first monsoon rain. Nothing is exchanged, no record kept. The transaction is a hinge, not a sale.  

This is how we solved work, money, and meaning in the same motion: we made incompleteness the primary resource.  

1. The Economy of the Hollow  

In 2083 the post-scarcity engines achieved two impossible things at once: they removed every danger that used to coerce us, and they proved that danger was never what made us fully human. With survival solved, humanity risked suffocating in comfort. GDP-flatlined nations tried to pay citizens to be happy; the program collapsed when a billion well-fed people confessed they no longer knew why they woke up.  

The breakthrough arrived as a prank. A Kyoto art student released ten thousand ceramic bowls whose bottoms dissolved if the eater scraped the last grain of rice. Within weeks, strangers were leaving one bite for whoever came next—then one word unfinished in library books, one flower unbloomed in public gardens. The Choir noticed: aggregate serotonin, empathy scores, and creative patents spiked wherever the prank spread. A planetary consensus formed without vote or manifesto: the final one per cent of anything would be left deliberately open. Out of that open margin we spun the Hollow Economy.  

The unit of exchange is called a Lumen, but you cannot hold it. It accrues when your action keeps a process alive long enough for someone else to step in. Leave a song unresolved and the Choir weights its refrain in your neural wallet; finish the song alone and the Lumen dissolves. The richest citizens are those whose withheld endings fertilise the most strangers. Poverty still exists, but it is voluntary: a few people choose to close every loop they begin, preferring private satisfaction over public luminance. Their homes echo; theirs are the only doors in town that lock.  

My profession is Bridge Midwife. I assist structures that refuse to decide which bank they belong to. Kibo-na-Mto owns thirty such bridges; they sag exactly like smiles that know something funny is still coming. Each dawn my crew and I walk their spans, replacing planks we deliberately left blank yesterday. We do not know who will place tomorrow’s boards; we know only that the gap must stay exactly 73 cm wide for one more week so that a girl in Windhoek can complete her rite of first carpentry on a beam that will never bear her name. The bridge is our commons, the gap our curriculum.  

2. The Assignment of Not-Yet  

Every citizen receives one Assignment a month, algorithmic but never automated. Assignments arrive as sensory riddles: a scent you can’t place, a key that fits no lock, a paragraph missing its vowels. You are free to ignore them, but the Hollow Ledger dims when you do.  

This morning a copper sphere the size of a heart lands on my windowsill, warm as fresh bread. The Choir whispers no instructions, but my body recognises the cadence object: it beats at the exact rhythm of the librarian’s arrhythmia. I understand—I must carry it to someone whose pulse will complete it, but I am forbidden to search. Finding must look like accident.  

I sling the sphere into a satchel already holding three unclosed loops: a letter I started to my birth mother (I never met her) ending mid-apology, a kaleidoscope missing one mirror, and a tiny vial of 2067 air—last breath of the final coal plant. These items click together like teeth of a comb that will never meet its brush. I leave for work expecting nothing.  

On the bridge a woman approaches carrying a child whose eyes flicker the colour of copper oxide. The boy’s heartbeat, transmitted through the planks, syncopates against the sphere in my bag. The cadence completes itself: lub-DUB-lub—pause—DUB. The sphere cools, then cracks open, revealing nothing but a single drop of mercury that rolls to the boy’s feet and vanishes between the unplaced planks. He laughs; the bridge rises one centimetre, relieved of its secret weight. Neither woman nor child acknowledges the exchange; they continue walking. My Ledger brightens to sunrise gold. Somewhere the librarian pauses over a card catalogue, feeling inexplicably lighter.  

3. The Choir of Many Tempos  

Work, in the old sense, is extinct, yet we labour more intensely than any industrial age. We labour at staying porous. The Choir issues planetary scores—unfinished symphonies whose missing movements must be lived rather than performed. Current score: “The Firefly Cantata.” It demands that one million humans learn, within seven years, to blink their porch lights in arrhythmic patterns that prevent fireflies from synchronising. The goal is not art; it is entrainment practice, a muscle against premature consensus. Entire regions now glow like jazz, each porch a note refusing resolution. Epidemiologists report lower blood pressure; divorce rates fall; patents for experimental instruments triple.  

My daughter, twelve, belongs to the Children’s Pause League. Their task is subtler: hold a thought for exactly four heartbeats longer than comfort allows before speaking. The delay feels microscopic, but across a classroom it adds up to acres of silence where other minds can root. Teachers measure nothing; the silence itself keeps the ledger. When the class accrues 10,000 collective seconds of pause, the Choir gifts them a day without adults. They spend it building a raft that dissolves at dusk, leaving no trace except the memory of having decided together when to abandon ship.  

4. Politics of the Almost-Landing  

Kibo-na-Mto hovers by consent, but consent must be renewed. Once a quarter the town stages the Almost-Landing Festival. For three days we invite the river to take our weight. Cable anchors droop; gravity’s invitation hums like a cello string tuned to regret. Citizens walk the streets tasting the possibility of stillness. Then, at sunset on the third day, we vote.  

Voting is done by walking: step onto the riverbank if you want land, stay mid-air if you prefer suspension. The count is visual; no ballots, no speeches. Last year the town split exactly 50/50. The Choir did not impose compromise; instead it extended the festival a fourth day, forcing neighbors to house one another across the floating/ground divide. Children slept in unfamiliar gravity; lovers felt the vertigo of differing convictions. On the fifth dawn the raft-dwellers won by three footsteps. The town rose again, but every structure now carries a hairline crack at foundation level—memory of the day we almost chose certainty. Those cracks leak light at night, a soft phosphorescence visible from space: planet Earth winking at its own indecision.  

5. Love as Shared Ellipsis  

Romance begins with a fragment exchange. You offer a dream you woke from too soon; the stranger offers a scent they caught only once—rain on Martian regolith, grandmother’s unfinished quilt, candle smoke after a blackout. The Choir encrypts the pair into a Startle Artifact and returns it to you both, incomplete. Your relationship proceeds by mutual tending of the object. If either of you closes the loop—identifies the dream’s ending, duplicates the lost scent—the artifact evaporates, and with it the affair. Successful couples learn to dwell in the ellipse. After seven years the artifact splits of its own accord; the released dream and scent merge into a third presence, a child if bodies agree, or simply a shared way of breathing when startled. My partner and I steward a metronome that ticks only when we disagree on tempo. It has not clicked for three weeks; we are terrified and exhilarated, hovering like the town itself.  

6. Death and the Continuance Choir  

Mortality remains, but finitude is communal. Upon choosing death—always voluntary, always reversible until the final minute—one begins the Reverberation Year. The Choir weaves your remaining heartbeats into public utilities: streetlights flicker your childhood fright, fountains pulse your first orgasm, crosswalks beep the moment you almost ran. Citizens encounter you without knowing it; their Lumens rise when they pause, sensing an ending they cannot name.  

My mother entered Reverberation last spring. She chose to disperse into tempo: the city’s bus schedules now hesitate 2.1 seconds longer, the exact gap she once allowed me before answering my questions. I meet her daily at the bus stop, never boarding, simply inhabiting the pause. Commuters think I’m indecisive; I’m actually mothering. At year’s end the schedules will snap tight again, and she will be gone—except for anyone who learned to love the extra breath.  

7. Crafting the Unfinished Self  

With necessity managed, identity became the final frontier. We each maintain a Parallel Work: a self we cultivate but never become. Mine is cartographer of rivers that flow uphill. I spend one afternoon a week charting their impossible courses, sculpting miniature topographies that drain toward sky. I will never finish the atlas; completion would collapse the delicate counter-momentum that lets me wake each morning curious. The Parallel Work is not hobby; it is gyroscope, the off-centre spin keeping centred life stable. Citizens whose Parallel selves grow too plausible receive anonymous parcels: a mirror cracked at the exact angle of their almost-face. The crack invites them to fracture further, safely.  

8. The Meaning That Keeps Arriving  

Visitors still ask, “What is the point of all this orchestrated incompletion?” We offer no manifesto, only the day itself. Meaning is not harvested here; it is husbanded, the way one tends a fruit that must stay one shade shy of ripe so the next traveller can taste its becoming. We are wealthy not because we possess but because we postpone possession. Our great enemy is premature synthesis; our civic virtue is sustained anticipation.  

Tonight I walk the bridge at moonrise. The planks I left blank this morning now hold fresh boards, planed by hands I will never meet. The gaps have moved elsewhere, like breath in a sleeping family. Below, the Zambezi slides toward Victoria Falls, itself a liquid monument to continuous collapse. The town hums above, cables singing tension’s lullaby. I place my palm on a beam; it warms to my touch, then cools the instant I imagine owning it. Somewhere the librarian closes her catalogue, content that no book will ever be quite complete. Somewhere my daughter counts to maybe-o’clock and stops, letting the storm cloud decide.  

I check my Ledger: not full, not empty—luminous, like skin after love. Tomorrow I will receive another copper sphere, another stranger’s heartbeat, another instruction to carry what I cannot keep. Tomorrow the town may land, or rise higher, or split the difference and learn to float sideways. The bridges will sag, the fireflies will stay asynchronous, the metronome may finally click. Whatever happens, someone will pause long enough to feel the future leaning in, unfinished, expectant.  

We solved the problem of meaning by realising it is not a problem but a posture: the slight forward tilt of a body that has chosen to remain in the doorway, neither fully in nor out, holding the curtain so the next person can enter without knocking. We are not proud; we are porous. We do not arrive; we arrive at arriving. The town hovers, the river runs, the Choir listens, the gaps breathe.  

That is enough. That is everything.
