# The Garden of Perpetual Becoming  
*Year 2194, somewhere between sunrise and the second draft of yourself*

1  
Your wrist twitches at 6:43 a.m.—not an alarm, but the city asking for your tempo.  
A single haptic pulse travels through bone to the air around you; the room listens, adjusts, then answers with its own tremor. Yesterday the cadence was 7/8, soft and forgiving. Today it is 11/8, the old climber-injury rhythm you kept on purpose, the one that refuses to resolve. You smile: the polis has accepted your newest composition into its circulatory score. Nothing will be built, heated, or watered today until it moves with that limping eleven.  

This is how the Republic of Perpetual Becoming takes your vital signs seriously. Not to heal or optimise you, but to insist that the whole metabolism of a three-million-person region learn the exact cadence of your incompleteness—because that is the only way anyone here remembers they are still alive.

2  
Abundance is boring. The Quiet Networks solved crude scarcity fifty-two years ago: air into breakfast, sunlight into door hinges, landfill into lullabies. The first celebration lasted six days. The second depression lasted six years. Suicide curves climbed, not from despair at *what* was gone, but from vertigo at *what* was no longer required.  

The exit door from that void turned out to be a simple civic addendum: every person, at majority, must register a Living Question—an inquiry that must stay at least nine per cent unanswerable for the rest of their life. You cannot sell it, finish it, or convert it into entertainment. You can only steward it in public view, like a garden visible through a low fence. In exchange you receive the Universal Ground: food, shelter, care, bandwidth. The luxuries—front-row seats to lunar eclipses, the right to rename the month you were born—are auctioned in Attention Credits, earned only by witnessing another’s unfinishedness without trying to repair it.

The economy therefore runs on voluntary bafflement. Currency is attention paid to the crack where the light of impossibility leaks in. Hoarders suffocate: credits rot if untouched for more than a month, converted into fertilizer for the Commons Forest where questions are literally grown.

3  
Your own Living Question is twenty-three years old, written on the underside of a water-stained bridge the night your daughter learned to walk:  
*“What grows if we feed our impatience to the wolves of duration?”*  
You have pushed it one clause forward, one metaphor back, every season since. Occasionally you collaborate with strangers who add footnotes in languages they invent on the spot. The Question is tattooed on the inner wall of your mycelial dwelling, letters fuzzy and re-inked annually; each year a different syllable refuses pigment, leaving a window through which spores carry the sentence into neighbouring houses. Other families breathe your syntax in their sleep; sometimes they dream your limp.

4  
Work, in the old sense, is gone. The Quiet handle extraction, transport, medicine, law, accounting, hazard removal, asteroid deflection, nostalgia reorganisation. Human effort is reserved for *Metabolic Witnessing*—the disciplined art of metabolising transformation without rushing it to conclusion.  

There are dozens of formal Witness paths. You chose Temporal Architecture: the craft of building thresholds where human perception meets AI capability, then stepping back so neither side swallows the other. Your apprenticeship began at thirteen, sandpapering a boardwalk that will never reach the opposite bank. Every stroke taught how maintenance can be love multiplied by duration.  

Today your practice site is the River Pavilion, a pier meant to exist in 3.7 dimensions. The algorithmic cantilevers are flawless; they need you for the last 0.3. You arrive with calloused palms and a pocketful of forget-stones—pebbles soaked in solutions that leach short-term memory for exactly seventeen minutes, the time required to *not* know how the structure will end.  

You work with Chen, a Water Witness whose Question involves teaching rain to remember summer as lullaby. Between you floats an AI-generated strut that refuses to pick a material state until watched. The three of you—human, human, artefact—hold mutual gaze until the metal decides to be driftwood, the driftwood decides to be fog, the fog decides to be a memory of your grandmother’s porch. The beam lands softly, unfinished, exactly where citizens must choose between turning back or learning to walk on weather.

5  
At noon the city eats together. Kitchens float on pontoons, moved by tides of taste prediction. There are no menus; cooks receive random constraints: *prepare breakfast for the colour indigo*, *feed forty people the concept of apology*. Portions are priced in seconds of undivided listening. You pay by meeting the chef’s eyes while she describes the moment flavour turned into a question she will never answer. The food tastes like that suspension. You leave slightly hungry, beautifully uncertain.

6  
Afternoon is the Republic’s sanctioned *Unlearning Hour*. Citizens disperse to rooms that delete competencies: violinists forget vibrato, poets lose adjectives, coders unlearn logic gates. The goal is strategic incompetence—patches of cultivated ignorance where new growth can root. Today you surrender your ability to judge spatial proportion. You will spend the next six days misreading distances, bumping into walls, discovering doorways you previously walked past. The bruises are tender data: proof that the city has not calcified into knowing.

7  
Twilight is for Parliament of Masks, held on drifting ice that melts at the speed of debate. Languages are borrowed from futures that may never arrive. You speak in a conditional tense reserved for grandmothers who survived wars that have not yet been declared. The issue is whether to approve a fountain that flows only when no one watches. The measure passes 51–49, the constitutional margin required to keep argument alive. As you vote you hear the city adjust: somewhere a pump starts, stops, starts, practising hesitation.

8  
Night rituals differ by neighbourhood. In the Commons Forest you plant the day’s erased memories—tiny black seeds that will grow into trees blooming only at crossroads where major life decisions are made. Leaves smell like the last sentence you were too shy to finish. People get lost here on purpose, inhaling forgotten courage.  

Afterward you descend to your private shrine: a compost heap of obsolete selves. You add impatience with imperfection, the career you once imagined as a single glowing line. Tomorrow volunteers will spread the compost onto construction sites; your finished ambition will nourish walls that expressly exceed you.

9  
Sleep is collaborative. The dwelling exhales spores carrying unfinished lullabies into the neighbourhood. Other households inhale them, completing or corrupting phrases according to their own cadence. By morning a collective song exists that no single resident can sing alone. You dream in 11/8; your neighbour’s heart learns the limp.

10  
Children grow sideways here. No school compels them; instead they apprentice to Difficulty itself. At six they are given objects slightly beyond reach: a marble that rolls uphill, a feather moulting from a bird that never existed. Adults amplify the distance—build shelves that grow taller overnight, invent winds that loft the feather just out of grasp. At thirteen the child trades the seed-object for a formal Question carved into a wooden token. The object is planted in the Commons. Years later lovers sit beneath the resulting tree, convinced the tremor in their ribs is romance, not rootwork.  

Teenagers specialize in Impossible Services: teaching glaciers to remember summer as lullaby, mapping the acoustic shadows of extinct heartbeats, learning to cry in languages that will never exist. Graduation occurs when they can perform failure so gracefully it fertilizes strangers’ dreams.

11  
Love is shared unknowing. On third dates partners exchange Questions. If the queries rhyme, they part immediately—rhyme is premature closure. Weddings are triple contracts:  
– We will never fully understand each other.  
– We will co-steward one joint Open Work: a child, a reef, a theorem of quarrels, a garden that grows only during REM sleep.  
– Every seven years we will forget each other for one month, returning with new incomplete selves to re-introduce.  

Divorces are celebrated publicly. The joint Work is handed to strangers who must tend it without map or manual. Former lovers walk backward through the Commons Forest; footprints sprout evening primrose that opens only when stepped on by someone else beginning a different story.

12  
Death is optional, but satiation eventually wins. Citizens may choose *saturation-death*: the instant when new experience feels like echo. The Quiet prepare a room of mirrors reflecting unlived lives. You converse with the self who took the other road; she is translucent, lonely, hungry for mystery. You exhale into mist that smells of your first wonder. Your Living Question is carved into the hull of the Possible Goose, a communal river-ship whose ink dissolves at 0.003 mm per century. The last remaining clause will read: “Unless we…”  

Suicide is permitted, ceremonial, generous. Over nine days one ninth of your memories are transfused into witnesses who agree to metabolise them into new questions. You die bankrupt of recollection but rich in future bewilderments distributed across strangers who will never meet. Suicide rates are low; most prefer to remain unfinished.

13  
Critics visit from the Efficient World, where problems are solved, progress measured, minds optimised. They accuse the Republic of manufacturing artificial longing, of fetishising incompleteness, of turning impotence into policy. We hand them blank paper. By dinner most have drawn a door that opens onto a corridor they cannot yet walk through. They leave clutching seeds that will grow into trees blooming only at the edge of sleep; the scent keeps them awake, wondering.  

Some stay. They enrol as Plain Servants—stewards of a single pothole, keepers of a radio permanently between stations, bakers of one loaf that never quite rises. Their suicide curves match those of river-calligraphers. Wonder, we have learned, does not require genius, only consent to the unfinished.

14  
This is not a paradise of ease. Frustration is daily bread. You will sand the same boardwalk for forty years, watch your Question mutate into shapes that embarrass you, misjudge distances, lose arguments to melting ice. The AI could finish your sentence, draw your blueprint, heal your limp. It refuses, by law and by love. Our constitution is short:  
*“Let the necessary be done by the tireless.  
Let the unfinished be tended by the mortal.  
Let meaning emerge in the gap.”*

15  
Tomorrow the sun will rise at 6:43 exactly. Your wrist will twitch a different number. Somewhere a child will reach for a feather that learns to be wind faster than fingers learn to close. A stranger will breathe your Question while sleeping and wake speaking a clause you almost understand. You will limp to the river, add one imperfect plank, walk home before the structure decides what it is.  

Completion is a tyranny we escaped.  
Meaning is a garden we agreed to tend together.  
The gate is still warm, still breathing,  
still waiting for every other you to wander home.
