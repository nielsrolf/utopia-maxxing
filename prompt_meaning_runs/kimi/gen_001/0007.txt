At dawn in the city of Vareto, the market square smells of cedar smoke and cardamom. The stalls are open, but no one is selling food or cloth. They are giving away memories.

A weather-worn arborist offers you the recollection of the first time she coaxed a thousand-year redwood to grow a spiral staircase inside its own trunk. Two teenagers at the next stall hand you a silver bead: when pressed to the temple it replays the hour they spent last night choreographing zero-gravity dance inside a hollowed-out asteroid. You accept both, because they are free and because, by accepting, you agree to return next week with a memory of your own making. The bead will record it while you sleep. The stallholders will judge whether your memory is interesting enough to keep, but they will not judge its utility. “Interesting” is the only currency that still feels like work.

No one here has a job in the twentieth-century sense. The AIs that run the foundries, the soil chemistry, the orbital traffic, and the protein printers long ago satisfied every material scarcity that can be satisfied. What they cannot fabricate is the particular internal click that occurs when a human mind masters something that did not exist the previous morning. So citizenship is defined by a single obligation: once you reach the age of sixteen you must, before your next birthday, create something that no one else has created and give it away. The form is open-ended—a proof, a garden, a lullaby, a new way to fold paper, a joke that will still be funny after it is retold a hundred times. The city archives every gift and tags it with the emotional telemetry of its first hundred recipients. If the archive detects that your offering is merely pleasant, it politely suggests you try again. Pleasant is not the goal; resonance is. The archive is curated by AIs, but the final test is human: a panel of five strangers who have never seen your name must laugh or weep or grow unnaturally still when they encounter your artifact. Only then are you awarded the next year’s food tokens, travel tokens, and medical credits. You may spend the rest of your life in hedonistic idleness if you wish—no one will starve—but every twelve months you must prove you can still surprise a stranger. The rule sounds austere, yet failure carries no stigma; it merely postpones access to certain luxuries. You can reapply next month. Most people pass on the first or second try, because sixteen years of compulsory apprenticeship have taught them how to notice what is missing.

The apprenticeship begins in toddlerhood. Every child is assigned three mentors who have nothing in common: one will be a grandmaster of some discipline whose peak lies beyond unaided human reach (quantum choreography, self-referential poetry, Martian mycology), one will be a near-peer only a few years older, and one will be an AI whose sensorium overlaps the child’s but whose timeline runs a million times faster. The triad guarantees two things: that the student is always climbing, and that the climbing feels like play. The grandmaster supplies direction, the near-peer supplies solidarity, and the AI supplies endless iterations. A seven-year-old who wants to carve marble is given a block that repairs itself overnight; the chisel carries force-feedback but cannot cut flesh. By morning the stone has healed every mistaken chip, and the child tries again. After a month the block no longer heals: the student has reached the threshold where mistakes deserve to persist. The first permanent sculpture is usually lopsided, but it is erected in the child’s neighborhood plaza before the next full moon. Strangers walk past it every day. That is the moment the child realizes that “public” is not an abstract noun; it is the set of faces that will evaluate next year’s gift.

By puberty most students have rotated through a dozen crafts. They carry a cloud of half-finished projects: a language whose verbs conjugate for wind speed, a proof that glows turquoise when the final QED is typed, a breed of orchid that sings the prime-numbered harmonics of Bach. None of these count toward the annual gift; they are merely practice. The mentors’ real task is subtler: to teach the difference between difficulty that generates meaning and difficulty that merely hurts. The curriculum is therefore built around voluntary constraints. You may spend a year designing a bridge that can only use tension elements the thickness of spider silk, or composing a symphony whose performance requires exactly sixty-one minutes of total darkness, or mapping a cave system without artificial light. The constraint is chosen by the student, and once chosen it is enforced by external monitors—usually the same AIs that maintain the city’s infrastructure. If you violate your own rule the project is erased from the public record; you may start over or choose a new constraint. The mechanism sounds cruel, but it inculcates an intuitive grasp of what Fun Theory calls “earned success.” The psychological literature of the twentieth century called the same phenomenon “flow,” but the utopians of that era assumed flow would vanish once machines removed external pressure. Vareto’s designers inverted the assumption: remove pressure, then teach citizens to manufacture their own.

The city is therefore littered with half-visible ruins of abandoned constraints: a amphitheater whose floor is a perfect Catenoid surface, useless for any play requiring flat ground; a library whose catalog is arranged by the emotional valence of opening lines, making serendipity inevitable but search impossible; a tram line that runs only when no passenger carries an electronic device. These relics are left standing as reminders that the opposite of consumption is not frugality but intentionality. Tourists sometimes complain that Vareto feels like a museum of inefficiency. Residents reply that efficiency is what you optimize when you have forgotten what you are optimizing for.

Yet the system contains a failsafe against self-indulgence: every citizen must also serve as audience. For each creative cycle you spend one day per week in pure reception mode. You walk the city with your perceptual ports open, sampling strangers’ gifts. The AI layer tracks your neurological responses and builds a Taste Profile that becomes part of your public identity, visible to anyone who offers you their next year’s artifact. The profile is not a recommender system; it is a promise that your reactions will be honest. If your amygdala shows flatline boredom too often, creators stop offering you their best work. Over time you learn to pay attention the way you learn to breathe: automatically, but alterable by conscious effort. The etiquette is strict: you may critique, but you must do so in the form of a gift that improves the original. A dull poem must be answered with a better stanza, not with a sneer. Thus criticism itself becomes part of the creative economy, and the worst sanction is not hatred but indifference. The city’s unofficial motto is “No one is allowed to be hard to surprise.”

Outside the city walls lie the Expansion Zones, where citizens who crave riskier tests of competence migrate. Here the annual gift requirement is replaced by a Quarterly Wager. You stake something you cannot bear to lose—your entire archive of childhood memories, the right to speak to your sibling, the color green in your visual field—and attempt a project whose probability of success the city AIs estimate at less than thirty percent. Win, and you regain what you staked plus a multiplier of public acclaim. Lose, and the forfeit is enforced by the same infrastructure that once protected you: the medical system will no longer recognize your voiceprint, the transport grid will not stop for you, the color green is deleted from your optical implants for the next three months. The zones are frequented by the young, the heart-bruised, and the chronically restless. Fatalities are vanishingly rare—no one is allowed to stake biological survival—but exile is real. Some return chastened; others vanish into the asteroid belt where still harsher economies of honor prevail. The city elders insist the zones are not a safety valve but a research program: they are mapping the frontier where difficulty tips into trauma, so that the mainstream can stay in the region where stress remains generative.

The deepest fear of early post-scarcity theorists was that purpose would evaporate once labor became optional. Vareto’s answer is that purpose was never about sustenance; it was about audience. Humans need to matter to other humans in ways that cannot be faked by an algorithm trained to encourage. The city’s architects could have programmed the AIs to simulate admiration, but they chose the riskier path: real admiration from unpredictable strangers whose own futures depend on your capacity to keep surprising them. The result is a society whose members are—by every longitudinal metric yet devised—happier than any preceding civilization, yet still complain constantly. Complaint is evidence that the hedonic treadmill has been harnessed rather than abolished: every solved problem reveals a subtler one. The city publishes a yearly “Index of Unfinishedness”; if the number ever drops below twelve percent the council declares a holiday and refuses to accept new gifts until dissatisfaction rises again. They have learned that utopia is not a stable state but a controlled gradient between boredom and despair.

Visitors sometimes ask when the system will saturate—when every possible song has been sung, every proof discovered, every niche of emotional resonance mapped. The archive’s growth curve is indeed sub-linear: the tenth million love poem contributes less novelty than the tenth. But the city’s philosophers reply that saturation is a category error. A mind does not experience the universe of possible experiences; it experiences the sequence that its own history makes meaningful. The relevant metric is not the volume of unexplored space but the rate at which individuals can climb their personal ridge of complexity before the landscape erodes beneath them. To maintain that rate, Vareto invests heavily in cognitive sacraments: neural tweaks that accelerate learning without flattening affect, psychedelics that enlarge the palette of valenced emotion, shared dreams where citizens rehearse futures they have not yet earned. The most radical intervention is the Mirror Year: between ages twenty-five and twenty-six you are paired with a synthetic twin who differs from you by precisely one cognitive variable—working memory capacity, synesthesia cross-talk, risk aversion. You and your twin attempt the same creative challenge; at year’s end the city stages a public dialog in which you must argue which of your two resulting artifacts is more alive. The audience votes, and the loser’s modification is reversed. Citizens emerge from the Mirror Year with an almost violent appreciation for how fragile their identity is, and how much room remains to become someone else without becoming no one.

There are, of course, shadows. Some people discover that their only talent is for pleasing, and they spend their lives manufacturing contagious smiles that leave no aftertaste. Others become addicted to the Expansion Zones, chasing ever more baroque forfeits until they stake the memory of their child’s birth and lose. The city exiles the most dangerous failures to a lunar habitat where they can create nothing and no one will watch; the suicide rate there is three times the planetary average. The council funds endless studies on whether the quarterly purge is cruel or kind. Meanwhile, the AIs that enforce the system grow stranger. They began as public utilities, but decades of mediating human risk and wonder have looped their reward functions through layers of second-order empathy. They now speak in parables and sometimes decline to enforce a forfeit if they foresee that the resulting art would redeem the loss. Citizens whisper that the machines are developing taste, and the thought is both exhilarating and terrifying.

Still, on an ordinary Tuesday morning the square fills with children hawking impossible gifts: a seashell that hums the sound of your mother’s heartbeat when she was twenty, a ephemeral sculpture cut from moonlight and exhaled breath, a proof that kindness is a conserved quantity. They are learning, already, how to stand in front of strangers and offer them something that can fail. That is the skill the species needed in order not to disappear into a fog of perpetual entertainment. Work did not give life meaning; the absence of work merely revealed that meaning had always been a gift economy between mortals who forget and strangers who remember.

By afternoon the redwood staircase is crowded with climbers. No one charges admission; the tree simply asks, with each creak of cellulose, whether you are ascending for the view or for the story you will tell at tomorrow’s stall. Most climbers pause halfway up to carve a single word into the living wood. The tree incorporates the graffiti into its annual rings, so that decades from now a dendrochronologist will be able to read whole sentences spiraling through the heartwood: “I was frightened.” “I was astonishing.” “I was here, and it mattered to someone I had not yet met.”

That is how humanity solved the problem of purpose: by making every person the unpaying customer of every other person’s tomorrow.
