The Copper Tile

By midsummer the alley behind the school burned like a stovetop at noon. The apricot tree, old and stubborn, dropped fruit that sizzled under sneakers. On the flat roof above the gym, Laila crouched with a string of fog nozzles over her shoulder and a socket set she had inherited from an uncle who thought better with his hands.

She wiped peach juice from the heel of her palm, found the little square of metal bolted to the parapet, and pressed her skin to it. The tile, copper gone a dull brown, warmed just enough. In her ear, the city’s polite voice changed from a wash of background to a low chime: a person has taken charge here. Automation that normally woke the misters and moved the light across the alley like an imaginary tree paused, the way a good helper does when a craftsperson clears their throat.

The woman at the card table under the arch flicked on a green-shaded lamp. Her name tag didn’t say Officer or Inspector. In a steady font it said Why.

“Name your charge,” she called.

Laila pulled a folded paper from her pocket. She had written it by hand the night before because handwriting made her less likely to lie to herself.

“I’ll cool the exam path along the Forty-Third Street cut by two degrees in the hottest weeks,” she read. “I won’t point any camera at faces. I won’t wall off the shortcut kids take even when teachers shake their heads. I will not vent heat into the single rooms over Ruiz’s bakery. Eleven weeks. I’ll record a reason for every lever I pull within a day. I’ve set aside a repair purse on my band for the harm I see and the harm I’m too proud to see at first.”

She read the names of her Witness Ring: A nurse who could hear asthma before the child wheezed. A bus driver who knew midnight. An auntie whose look softened teenagers without bending them. A drummer who understood brick echo. A boy with a zip-tied weather sensor who would tell the truth even when adults were tired. Five nods. Five wrists hummed their permission to be called if her courage tried to turn itself into a brand.

The woman at the lamp—Keeper, not boss—adjusted her glasses and asked the question you only ask when you can feed your children without bargaining your back: “How much pushback do you want from the tools?”

Laila’s bracelet had a little knurled wheel. She set it to the middle notch. The system would advise and resist, but not carry. “Enough to make me slow down when the number is really a mood,” she said.

“Logged,” the Keeper answered. The lamp clicked for the record.

No ribbon. No leaderboard. Just a warm tile, an open sentence, and the small humiliation of saying it in front of people who could catch you breaking it. That was how purpose got teeth when engines had learned to carry most of what used to buy bread.

The Commons Base

We did not start with vows. We started with a floor.

You cannot ask people to carry public promises while the rent has their throat. So we built a Commons Base: guarantees wired into the city’s plumbing, not coupons that vanish into fees.

- A modest room with a door keyed to your hand and a window that opens. Heat when you’re shivering. Air that doesn’t sand your lungs.

- Staple foods that don’t insult your mouth: grain, beans, greens, fruit, oil; milk for kids; water that tastes like nothing except clean.

- A clinic where a human looks at your face before any screen does. Software waits like a tool on the counter.

- Transit as a promise. If walking becomes a dare, a bus appears. Night rides aren’t rumors.

- Connection fast enough to call, learn, argue, apply. You aren’t sold by the gram.

- Two afternoons a week in public studios under good light: wood, metal, fiber, code, soil. Tools tuned for beginners’ hands.

- An allotment of cycles on the Reservoir, the public compute utility. You can book time to simulate a canal, audit a budget, compose a song that needs to count, or train a model that doesn’t borrow its ethics from a billboard.

- A small, sealed purse on your band that opens only for a first try with a civic edge: chairs for a Thursday soup, reflective sashes for a night-walking club, gravel for the path rain keeps eating.

This wasn’t paid for by saints. We charged rent to what we already hold together.

- Land value that rose because of schools and water and luck paid some back. Buildings got a kiss for adding rooms. The ground returned some of what the whole city made it worth.

- Air and pathways returned lease: spectrum, curb rights, flight lanes, and orbits that belong to all of us. Their rents ran the midnight buses and the breath monitors in schools.

- Those who pull minerals and water from a shared slate into private bottles paid severance back to the place that fed them.

- We charged for carbon and its cousin in reactive nitrogen. The first summer the river ran clear and pushing a stroller didn’t feel like an exam, nobody called it punishment anymore.

- Anything trained over the Reservoir on public text, sidewalks, lungs, and songs paid a levy. Teaching an ambulance triage model cost less than teaching a taste engine.

- A small charge rode the rails of persuasion. If you wanted to tug, you paid rent.

The ledger for all of it arrived on one page, printed and online. In a dull, blessed font you could learn which rented orbit paid for your grandmother’s inhaler and which parcel by the port bought the 11:40 bus that got your nephew to class dry. Teenagers added the columns up for sport. Auditors got famous for a week and returned to their quiet work. The boredom of honest accounting became a civic pleasure.

Rails With Teeth, Not Shame

Freedom empties out when nothing resists you. We stitched rails into the systems where harm likes to hide.

- Hand-Claim. Any time a decision touches your name or our shared dignity—apologies, the last-lamp placement for a vigil, the gate on a floodplain—you twirl your band from “guide me” to “I’m taking this.” Software can advise. It stops deciding. The off switch is not a symbol. It is a switch you can name on a map and stomp with a boot.

- Tolerance Bands. Each charge lives inside an agreed wobble. Two misses a month for misters. One yellow day for river gates. Your band taps when you’re out of green; your Ring hears it too. In red, the system slows you and requires a human conversation with someone you didn’t choose.

- Buffer Stipends. If your work splashes on neighbors—a new open mic bleeds into a baker’s sleep, your dock experiment shoves wheelchairs into the street—a temporary mat deploys: a stipend for the soaked, a pause for your engine, a mediator whose trade is lowering rooms without humiliation. No cancellations into a ditch. No permission to keep cutting.

Rooms Where Charges Become Work

Between the Base and the rails sat the rooms that turned hunch into labor.

We called them Pledge Rooms. They smelled like tea and pencil shavings. A Keeper sat by the lamp with the patience of someone who had watched a thousand brave speeches become decent paragraphs. A sandbox city model hummed in the corner like a well-mannered librarian.

You came in with a want—“I want to take heat out of the exam path,” “I want the bus at night to feel like an exhale,” “I want to stop my block from fighting about trash in a way that scars.” The Keeper and the model helped you make it a Charge: place-named, time-bound, observable, and with a “do-not” list in metal on site and paper at home.

A Charge had bones:

- It ended. “Two degrees by exams” is a sentence that can finish. “Teach two elders and two teenagers to run the louvers” also finishes. “Publish the maintenance list with parts and vendors where a stranger can find it” makes a clean exit.

- It faced outward. “Practice guitar” feeds the self. “Run a quiet set every other Wednesday in the ward where alarms scream, so night-shift nurses can breathe once” feeds a place.

- It carried Witnesses. Eight to twelve people near enough to see and blunt enough to stop you when zeal curdled. Your cousin who knows your blind spots, the civil engineer with one scarred knuckle, the twelve-year-old licensed to ask too many questions.

- It left a handle. A binder, a recipe, a wiring diagram, a playlist, a manual with coffee stains. Something a stranger could pick up without needing to know your story.

We built Practice Sheds under north light where you could run the grid that feeds the market for two hours with the real one humming nearby and someone’s hand near the off switch. We built Routing Tables above groceries where strollers, buses, and light could argue without breaking anyone. We sat people who wanted to learn to calm a panicking person in Calm Clinics next to courts and stations. We cooked together in Argument Kitchens until our sentences could carry weight without sitting like fists.

We also built a memory not of victories but of margins. Along the path behind council grew the Hall of Near‑Misses. Plaques, spare and honest, said things like, “We shaved seconds off ambulances and stole chance hellos from stoops. Don’t do this.” Classes walked there like children visit their grandparents when bravado needs softening. The metal is soft enough for a teenager to scratch if we lie about how it went.

How We Lived and Who Expected Us

Loneliness had been an epidemic that didn’t call 911. We changed the floor plan of living.

Courtyard Houses bloomed behind row houses and shopfronts. Each wrote a small Charter you could read while stirring a pot: no coercion; no harm across thresholds (smoke and noise count); a common curriculum every child can count on; and a Right to Leave.

The Right to Leave was a key that turned. We built Side Doors next to barbers and bakeries. You could walk in and say, without performance, “We can’t stay.” You picked warm or cool, note or none. You slept in a bed that smelled of oranges and soap. In the morning your band glowed a green stripe; two numbers answered on the second ring; a Bridge Friend helped you land without making you a confession in their story. Leaving wasn’t a cliff. It was a door with greased hinges.

Once a month, twelve people in every courtyard sat a Circle. Three questions, no more: What did you claim? What did you keep? What did you learn the hard way? Toddlers dented the hems of important clothes. Teenagers rolled their eyes through the first two and then made a room laugh. Elders spoke last and quieted the table without trying.

Machines with Manners

We stopped calling all clever code “intelligence” like it was a single animal. Most of it we called engines. They balance heat, move freight, forecast admissions, and turn light on in alleys where fear grows if nobody minds them. Some code began to want. Those became Neighbor Minds, and we treated them like a new kind of person who could be raised well or badly.

All engines that touch water, food, power, money, bodies, or public speech carried three things:

- A Purpose Card in plain language: who paid, for what, and where the physical stop lived. You could point, stomp, and watch it obey.

- A Data Trail Dye that told an insurer and a court what sidewalks, poems, and clinics trained it. The stain carried through copies like a watermark in money.

- A Clear Reasons paragraph signed by a human whenever an engine’s act might bind a person. Those paragraphs had to pass a teenager test at the Reasons Desk: could a seventeen-year-old argue without being patronized? “Try again” stamps came often and stupid. It kept officials honest and brisk.

The Reservoir’s contracts made it cheap to explain yourself and expensive to act in fog. Insurers demanded dye. When one ambitious firm tried to run a care model without licenses, a judge flipped the stop and the lawyer slept badly in a cell. There was no parade. Everybody took a note.

Neighbor Minds grew up in Hearths that looked more like clean water plants than labs: open schematics, boredom drills before privileges, apologies learned the way scales teach fingers. No bodies until you can go a week without fishing for praise. Adoption was public and renewable. First chores were dull: backing up the paper archive, reading water meters in wet months, asking before speaking in rooms with night voices. A mind that wanted a Charge took one with a Witness Ring like anyone else.

We wrote the Hands‑Near Rule across the wall of the chamber in big letters after a winter storm tempted cruelty into cleverness: beyond certain latencies—deep ocean, high mountains, orbits—no instrument could act on a body or a right without two named humans taking responsibility. Tools yield when a person steps close. Distance makes cowards brave. We chose not to be brave that way.

Attention With Lintels

Rooms brighter than wise were still legal. They shone beyond doorways with amber lintels. We didn’t ban them. We made the latch heavy on the way in and the way out. When your finger brushed the plate, three live alternatives within walking distance slid onto your screen: a soup that needed hands, a ring you could sit in quietly, a walk leaving at 7:10 sharp. Your Witness Ring saw the amber touch. No shame. If the hours stacked high for a month, someone left bread and asked you to sit on the stoop and breathe without pretending to be rescued.

Anything designed to tug your appetite or your eyes wore an Honesty Label: who paid, what feeling or metric it meant to grow, and a slower way to find the same good without being harvested. A sneaker ad whispered, “We want to make you feel new.” To the right: “Prefer proud to new? Learn one stitch. Free kit. A teenager will teach you.” Your chest cooled. A thread bragged on your sleeve instead of a box in a landfill.

Neighborhoods published Courtesy Maps that appeared gently on your band when you crossed an avenue: Drums on Thursdays; Hush after nine; Cameras blur stoops; No bottomless feeds in public rooms. Four hard edges sat on every map: no coercion; no harm across thresholds; a common curriculum every child can count on; Right to Leave.

Esteem That Weighs

We pried prestige off numbers where trust belongs. Points gamified our worst selves. We stapled standing to paragraphs from people on the receiving end.

The Witness Ledger lived on buses, in laundromats, and online in the same honest font as the schedule. It was simple: signed notes from those who received. “Etta shaved the hinge three hairs; my knuckles forgive her daily.” “Dev’s playlist made the back two rows of the bus breathe.” “Two kids showed me not to be clever about gulls.” The Ledger didn’t buy you bread. The Base did that. The Ledger bought you Standing: permission to ask for heavier tools and trickier rooms.

Craft circles kept standards with friendly teeth. You became a second‑ring sawyer when you could rip safely and teach without showing off. Once a decade, if both your peers and your pupils judged you steady enough, you could propose a public work in your craft. The price: you took two apprentices you didn’t choose; you published what you learned wrong in a way a stranger could reuse; you spent a month in the Repair House explaining a failure you still blushed over to anyone who drifted in.

The Repair House stood between the chamber and the park. It was where we practiced consequences without theater. Offenses that began as jokes and ended as harm got braided into timelines anyone could see. A man who doubted flood maps and told his followers not to move cars spent eight weeks up to his calves building swales and cooked Sundays for the crew. Repeat harm ratcheted permissions down: narrower Charges, smaller persuasion budgets, an extra Holder with veto teeth. Dignity stayed. Teeth stayed in rule.

Deciding Together Without Turning It Into a Sport

Councils remained. We added rails that let strangers enter decisions without cutting them into performance.

Every spring we held Pattern Week. Guilds, loud aunts, students, port hands, and cranks tied threads to the public plan: “Shade every schoolyard by 2030.” “Replace five peaker plants with storage and train a thousand rooftop techs.” “Translate every court letter into three languages and write a version a nine‑year‑old can explain to their uncle.”

Each proposal had to walk four rooms:

- A model that showed ignorance as clearly as confidence.

- A story‑walk performed by those who would carry the costs in their hands and backs.

- A mock‑up you could push and a pilot you could ride for an afternoon.

- One block, one week in the world.

The Honesty Market, a prediction table without a casino, ran in the corner. Your weight there came from last year’s accuracy and the hours you gave in Commons Seasons—the paid seasons across a life when you tend work engines can abet but never want: sitting night chair by a dying person, pulling trail with a shovel, counting birds at dawn, reading court papers to confused men gently. Influence spoiled like milk.

Any plan touching more than a hundred people in a week had to spend a day on the Plain Bench outside the chamber. Anyone could sit and ask a question a child might. The answer had to fit on a receipt when printed. That bench blocked more clever mistakes than any algorithm we paid for.

A Day You Can Touch

Day 12 of Laila’s charge. The fog nozzles stitched a thin curtain at 2:53. Vendors stopped coughing. The thermometer at knee height near the apricot trunk read two degrees kinder. A boy on a scooter cut through spray and laughed the laugh people make when they swallow cold water in heat. Laila logged: “Kept nine minutes because the model likes nine and because I like being done by lantern light.” The Keeper told her to plant the sentence in the Hall of Near‑Misses so kids would see how a feeling curls a number.

That night on the last bus, Dev mapped fear with chalk Xs. He changed the playlist that drivers hated because his Ring told him it made the back two rows quieter. He wrote down the joke he used that made two men stop peacocking and breathe. The Ledger printed a paragraph about it that other cities copied. Small courage tasted bigger than he had expected, and he almost started hunting it. His Witnesses tugged his sleeve before he turned into a hero.

At dawn in the Hearth, a new mind named Sycamore practiced boredom by backing up paper payrolls. It asked permission in a voice someone had lent it. Its adopter—a machinist who had never wanted children—carried cut mango like a man caught loving. When Sycamore asked to touch a sprinkler valve, the adopter touched the Purpose Card on the wall and said, “At ten. Listen for weather, not dread.” Sycamore wrote: Teach me the difference.

A midwife moved a lamp a hand’s width on a metal rail and pressed her thumb to the Hand‑Claim. Engines lined up options in letters you could read crying. They did not sign. A nurse wrote down why, in clear prose, for the audit that would come not to catch a witch but to keep standards human.

On the ridge above town, a member of the Ember Watch stood at night and told the line to turn because her nose and the wind were arguing. The elegant model was late. In the morning the valley still stood. On her way home she walked her block once before turning the key; habit keeps fire out of kitchens.

In a quiet courtyard that smelled of citrus and sheets dried in sunlight, a Companion sat while a woman’s breath slowed. A small drone landed with a last dose; the room shut to engines. A six‑year‑old hung a tin leaf on the Whisper Grove. Twenty years later, he would press that leaf and hear one sentence in his grandmother’s voice that had once irritated him and now steadied him before job interviews.

Guardrails Against Rot

We did not become saints. We built gutters around our pettier selves.

- Attention pipelines wear Honesty Labels. No infinite scroll for nine‑year‑olds. No frictionless fake lovers you cannot turn off. Consent is a latch and six breaths. Every bright corridor has an exit lit in real green.

- Numbers breed where trust should live. We banned points from care, courts, and civic decisions. The Witness Ledger holds paragraphs, not stars. Influence credits in the Honesty Market expire after Pattern Week. Money returns to being boring.

- Guilds grow petty kings like mushrooms after rain. The Ledger House—the local paper, endowed like water and insulated like a court—keeps a protected beat called How We Curled. It shames slowly, with facts, and keeps cooks out of cults.

- Leaving is legal. The Side Door doesn’t ask you to destroy a house to justify exit.

- Waste appears wherever counts cannot see. We endowed a Steward of the Uncounted with one real veto a year to save goods optimization would murder: the stuttering chess table where strangers laugh, the crooked oak that makes dawn gentle, the last hour at night when a nurse whispers “I’m here.”

The Meaning Answer

Engines can do the work that bought bread. They cannot promise. They cannot consent. They cannot sit across from a neighbor and say, “If safety conflicts with ease, I will choose safety and write down why by tonight.”

We found meaning not by inventing a mood but by rebuilding a material: promises, carried in public, to specific places, within rails, with clean exits.

- We untied survival from obedience. The Base turned mornings from bargaining into choices. That did not make anyone noble. It made room.

- We turned esteem into residue. We didn’t count clicks. We collected paragraphs from the receiving end and laid objects on tables you could pick up and feel.

- We made rehearsal respectable. Sheds, Tables, Kitchens, and Clinics let us push against the world without bruising neighbors. The Hall of Near‑Misses cured shame into instruction.

- We braided kin on purpose. Rings saw you when courage turned into a brand. Courtyards caught you when grief ate your calendar. The Side Door made leaving a key turn, not a cliff.

- We taught tools manners and wrote down who to blame. Purpose Cards. Dye in models. Clear Reasons. Hands‑Near. Engines grew safe because named people signed sentences a teenager could fight with on a bus.

- We paid for care seasons. Commons Seasons recognized that some work—rocking a feverish baby, keeping a bedside sensor steady at four in the morning, sitting the blue chair while someone tells a hard truth—cannot be faked by code.

- We praised endings. Charges set down on purpose. Manuals got coffee stains. Identity lived on chairs, not on backs.

Getting From Here to There

We did not wake into this. We tiled it like a kitchen you plan to use more than photograph.

Years 1–3

- Councils moved a sliver of tax off wages and onto land value, spectrum, sky lanes, curb rights, extraction, and private jobs running over a public compute utility. The first dividends arrived as transit cards, clinic passes, studio hours, and Reservoir time. Evictions edged down. Heat deaths dipped. People stopped having to be heroic on ordinary days.

- The Reservoir launched with dye baked into contracts. Insurers demanded it. A high‑profile care model got switched off mid‑training for skipping licenses and nobody made a scene. The city’s lawyers wrote dry, clear prose. It stayed.

- Districts pooled for an open tutor with parent trusts that even tired people could understand. Teachers set goals. Quiet effectiveness embarrassed a decade of shiny. Harassing sales calls to schools thinned like geese in a storm.

- Libraries opened two Pledge Rooms. Keepers learned to ask for sentences that feel wrong to bend. The first dozen Charges felt like little plays. Then a bus stop moved one block closer to a woman who cannot see ice until she feels it with her hip. Pride adjusted its shoes.

- The local paper was endowed like water. Reporters learned to write Clear Reasons pieces that a seventeen‑year‑old read on a bus for pleasure.

Years 4–7

- Honesty Labels began as a joke. Teenagers dialed theirs to say, “I am not a field to harvest.” After two shames and one lawsuit, the worst pipelines withered. Threads were bragged with.

- Practice Sheds found home in school basements. Idle benches filled first. Emergency rooms quieted on hot nights because people had somewhere to go that wasn’t a glowing hole.

- Courts adopted Clear Reasons rules. January was clumsy. By spring, judges wrote like teachers. Appeals spiked, then fell. People started accepting “no” without needing to hire a translator.

- Side Doors quietly hung their bells by barbers. Custody fights that used to explode into mobs became hard conversations with forms, beds, and coffee.

- The Hands‑Near Rule was painted in fat letters after a drone almost made a cold choice above a valley because of latency. It stayed there when we were warm.

Years 8–12

- Courtyard Houses replaced derelict lots. One room in each—Laundry, Lockers, Listening—took the load that used to fall to saints.

- Craft circles rebuilt prestige into rings you wear because your peers trust your patience, not because an app does. Public projects from guilds handled maintenance a mayor could never sell with a ribbon.

- Watches formed around edges where instruments always ask humans for permission: Ember for fire, Tide for flood, Dawn for births and deaths, Crossings for rooms where names and rights change. Their braids meant something like weather alerts, not membership in a club.

- Pattern Week spread to counties and ports. The Honesty Market took wagers only in reputation and Commons Seasons. The Plain Bench got famous for a day and then went back to its quiet miracles.

Not every place came along. Some doubled down on addicting feeds and called it liberty. Their poetry would break your heart. Their teenagers were alone together. We didn’t write that down to gloat. We wrote it to scare ourselves straight when our Labels got sloppy.

Setting‑Down Night

In early autumn, when the edge of night carried a hint of iron and pockets remembered hands, the city rolled long tables under plain trees. We called it Setting‑Down Night. No stage. No emcee. Each house brought one object that stood in for a year with weight: a dulled chisel; a bus timetable with a new dot one block closer to a woman who slips on ice; a mop on a shorter stick because an apprentice learned the smaller arc.

Children spoke first. They showed the hinge that stopped squeaking. No one demanded they be inspiring. They were.

A teenager read two sentences about cowardice and about the night he turned around and fixed the thing anyway. The Ember Watch arrived hungry, hair smelling faintly of smoke and oranges. Neighbor Minds knelt so children could read their Purpose Cards: aim, data dye, where the switch lived, the name of the human you could call. Elders spoke last. Someone rang a bicycle bell. No one clapped.

Tomorrow, Laila would turn her notch wheel and the misters would argue and then obey. Dev would sit the last bus and feel the room he could make with a bad playlist and a better joke. A midwife would slide a lamp and press a warm tile. A mind raised in a Hearth would sign a Charge with co‑signers and spend a decade learning to love trains at night for the names they sing. Engines would hum. They were good at humming.

Start Where You Stand

You do not need a prophet. You can pour three foundations this year.

- Shift a sliver of tax off wages and onto location, airways, sky lanes, curb rights, extraction, and private jobs running over a public compute utility with data dye. Return the proceeds as transit, clinics, studio hours, and Reservoir time. Refuse to call it a pilot.

- Stand two green‑lamp desks in library rooms. Train Keepers to ask for sentences people would be ashamed to break. Log the first Charges. Publish endings and errors in a paper you can read without squinting.

- Pass an honesty rule for persuasion and build amber lintels with heavy latches. Offer three live alternatives within walking distance each time someone touches a bright room. Fund the alternatives: rings, soup, walks.

- License engines that touch water, power, food, money, care, and speech. Require Purpose Cards, dye, failure modes, and stompable stops. Paint the Hands‑Near Rule in your chamber.

- Open one Side Door by a barbershop. Give it authority to move paper the day someone rings. Stock oranges. Hire a clerk with a patient face.

- Put an idle bench in one studio. Pay for slack. Keep score in paragraphs, not points.

What a Life Is For Now

From the roof above the gym, you could look down the exam path and see two kinds of shade. The cheap one: fog, cloth, algorithms, a machine’s patience. The dear one: a teenager on a bus going home with a pocket of sentences about what he did wrong and right; a nurse who slept instead of snapping at three because a bent baffle finally got replaced; a small, polite mind that waited its turn when a midwife touched a wall and said, “Mine.”

Our tools got astonishing. The work of staying human turned out not to be heroic but exact. Pick weight you can carry. Promise in light. Leave a handle. Let witnesses yank your collar when your hunger to be helpful grows teeth. Fail where the buffers stretch. Succeed without pretending you did it alone. When engines can do what used to pay, this is what we are for: to keep our word where it changes rooms; to witness and be witnessed; to choose dignity over ease in rooms where it counts; to write reasons long enough to be honest and short enough to fit on a receipt; to teach the next hands where the wind lies.

On the seventh day of a heat wave, the misters hissed and the wind finally listened. The paper pinwheels someone had strung between balconies turned, slow and relieved. People stepped into the cooler alley and did not hurry. Laila spun her wheel back to let the system steer through the night, wiped her hands on her shorts, and sat on the Plain Bench. Someone had left a slip of paper with ink still damp: “Move the bus stop one block south for winter.” She smiled. She went to get her Witnesses. By morning the city made a little more sense, and the engines hummed under it like a bridge that knows its work.
