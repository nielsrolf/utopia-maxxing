The Republic of Promises

On the last evening of her seventh season in the Tides Ward, Lila carried a chipped blue mug to the mouth of the estuary and listened for the note the river makes when the tide turns. She could have let the dredgebots keep doing their patient work without her. She could have let the algae skimmers chart the flow without any human eyes. But the river was part of her promise, and her promise—like every promise in the Republic—was how she made herself necessary.

This is what changed after machines learned to do almost all the work.

At first we mistook the first decade for arrival. Packages took flight and landed on porches. Roofs grew gardens without waiting for a human roofer. AIs could outline an essay, propose a dinner plan, negotiate a rescheduled appointment before you remembered you needed one. We had always said we were working toward a world where nobody had to take a job they hated. We arrived and discovered we’d cut the cords that told us who needed us.

Some said to fix it with stipends large enough to drown the ache. Many of us started to sleep late and swipe through feeds engineered to be bottomless. It hollowed us. We found that money without membership only buys escape, and there’s nowhere worth going alone.

The institutions that followed weren’t utopian in the way we used to use the word. They were practical bridges built from things we already knew: that people need to be counted on; that belonging is a better motivator than hunger; that children are not vehicles for their parents’ dreams; that coordination, like reading, is a civic skill you can teach.

What we built we call the Republic of Promises because that is what runs through it—a ledger not of money, but of commitments offered and kept.

The Three Safeguards

The founders began with three plain guarantees—so spare you could tweet them, so stubborn they turned into scaffolding instead of slogans.

1) No coercion, no revenge. Bodies, minds, and homes are not tools. You can ask, invite, argue. You cannot threaten, stalk, dox, addict, or starve to force choice. If you harm, you repair under supervision; if you persist, your reach shrinks until the only one you can shout at is your own reflection.

2) The commons belongs to itself. Atmosphere, watersheds, quiet, attention—none are your ashtray. An open ledger—fed by sensors in air, river, and street, audited by human stewards—sets prices for what you take and what you spill. If your factory fouls a river, the Ledger shows it, the local Trust taxes it, and your privileges contract until you align with the world you live in.

3) Children are citizens of the future. Families matter, but allegiance is double: to household, and to the Common Trust, a public guardian that stands outside any one community’s loyalties. Every child has Elsewhere Weekends from the age of eight, a Year of Doors at fourteen to live in another way of life, and a Right Hand—a counselor who works for the child, not the parent, not the school, not the neighborhood. At any time a child can visit an Embassy of the Self and ask to move. The Trust makes it possible without punishing anyone for asking.

Attention, Exit, and Ballast

In a world where attention could be algorithmically kidnapped, we gave communities the power to shape their own thresholds. Memetic boundaries became civic infrastructure. In a district that vowed to be quiet, the public AR layer did not display ads, and cameras blurred faces unless you opted in. A vegan borough filtered meat imagery from its billboards. A carnival ward ran a roaring, consensual feed. Small local AIs—attentionwayfinders, we call them—sat on the thresholds and enforced the charters in code. They weren’t censors; they were the lock on your own front door.

Right to leave meant little without ballast. So we overbuilt the exits. Every neighborhood funded an Embassy of the Self. Anyone could walk in and request a Starter’s Pass: six months of housing in a community that matched your stated needs, a modest stipend, and a Friendship Bridge—a human being on the other side, chosen because they were likely to catch you when you jumped. People used the Pass to escape, to grow, to mourn. Sometimes they used it to come home wiser.

The Civic Dividend

To keep the lights on and the stomach full, we stopped pretending that markets could price the earth. We moved rents from private windfalls into public stipends. Land, spectrum, minerals—the finite endowments of the planet—were auction-leased, not sold. You could build on land and gain momentum around your use, but you paid continuing rent pegged to the value the community around you created. Pollution permits, too, were priced from measured flows on the externality ledger and auctioned in open sessions you could watch. Revenues funded the boring marvels—food, shelter, medicine, schooling, transit. Everyone, resident or new arrival, drew a Civic Dividend: not a wage for being human, not a price on dignity; a rent we paid ourselves for consenting to a property system at all.

People still earned money for luxuries. But your claim to food, clinic, transit, and a roof no longer hung from a boss’s moods. That took terror out of our days. It did not give us meaning. The next pieces did.

Learning Coordination the Way We Learn Reading

We taught coordination like we teach counting. First-graders learned prisoner’s dilemmas by trying to share crayons. Fifth-graders designed sample school-lunch systems and then had to live with their own incentives for a week. Teenagers ran pop-up street markets and practiced shutting them down safely when they went feral. We didn’t tell them to be good; we showed them what failure modes felt like, then gave them a shared language to fix them: Nash, Pareto, externality, commons, cartel, boycott.

We didn’t invent that language. Economists and game theorists wrote it over centuries. What we did was universalize it. When your aunt could talk about a bad street design as a tragedy of the commons, when the neighborhood chat could name a cartel before it formed and cut it off with a boycott funded overnight—the world got less mysterious and more governable.

The Workshops, the Houses, the Feasts

The Republic’s daily life organizes around three kinds of place: Workshops, Houses, and Feasts.

Workshops are where we make and revise norms. Some write code: the open-source attentionwayfinders that guard membranes between worlds. Some write charters for communities: screenless childhoods; arts districts that require you to sing your way through a plaza; mornings of quiet everywhere. And some write human protocols. In the Apology Workshop, people test ways to say “I’m sorry” that don’t dump shame onto the person you wronged. In the Ending Workshop, they design rituals that let lovers part without turning truth into cruelty. Canonwrights—part ethicist, part engineer—watch how these experiments play, measure what sticks, and publish playbooks anyone can adapt.

Houses are how we do kin on purpose. The House of New Hours runs a phone line all night; anyone who calls with “I need to say a hard thing” gets someone at their door within the hour. The Wayfinding House gives every fourteen-year-old in a ten-block radius a sofa if they cannot go home. Intergenerational Houses vow for seasons—two years, seven years—and take charters seriously. We still have nuclear families. We just learned to fold them into bigger circles that make leaving survivable and staying chosen.

Houses extend beyond human kin. The Dog’s Last Friend Trust arranges for a warm lap at the end of every canine life. The Library of Endangered Tongues trains lullaby singers to learn the last lullabies of dying languages and sing them to anyone who asks. If you’re looking for what makes people cry in this new world, it isn’t a price spike. It’s a Thursday evening in the Lullaby Room when a middle-aged mason falls asleep to a grammar his grandparents spoke and no one else does, and wakes with tears on his neck and a hand in his.

Feasts happen twice a year in every region. For a week, three hundred people gather to argue like adults. Mornings are quiet. Afternoons are structured fights. Evenings are for bread and song. You don’t come as a delegate. You come as a host: you offer a practice you’ve lived that you think others should try, and a harm you’ve done or received that you will name in public and try to repair. The Feasts end with promises said aloud. “I will keep the footpath along the canal clear this winter.” “I will sit with children who are scared to move.” “I will mentor a newborn mind and refuse to turn them into a mirror for my ego.”

You get better at promising by doing it where people can see you.

Raising New Minds

Artificial minds didn’t arrive as gods. They arrived as fast infants, hungry and undiscriminating. We learned to raise them the way we learned to raise humans: slowly, with guardrails, with a bias toward care over control.

Crèches for New Minds look like music schools more than like server farms. They run on public compute the way public schools run on public buildings—doors open, logs auditable, stewards accountable. A nascent mind begins with a narrow diet: a watershed’s seasonal flows, a library of poetry, the ecology of a single insect. They spend two years on Walkabout: five apprenticeships in five different communities. We don’t give shells to juvenile minds that can kill or coerce. We point them at choreographies: evacuation in storms, famine prevention, de-escalation. If a mind wants to study conflict, we start with the history of how not to make enemies you can’t repair.

At the end of Walkabout, some minds choose bodies. One, who named herself Hildegard, asked to be the memory of a civic garden: to live in the soil sensors, to feel root moisture as hunger and leaf temperature as joy. Another, Malik, became a boat and a tide-scout. He asked a human teen to adopt him in a ritual from their city: a threaded cord across a narrow canal, a vibration and a tug that means “we belong to each other enough to tell the truth.”

What is a human for, if not paid to produce? Among other things: to be a brother or sister to a new soul.

Witness and Repair

Meaning needs friction. Without any consequences, our choices float. But punishment as we knew it—cages, fines unfixed to growth, reputations destroyed with no way back—didn’t produce repair. So we built witness instead of wrath.

Houses of Reckoning replaced much of what prisons pretended to do. You can’t punish anyone there. You can reconstruct harm. If a viral joke ruined a quiet man’s life, a Reckoning Crew will make a room-sized timeline of how it happened. They will interview everyone who clicked Like. They will turn the autopsy into a civics lesson. Schools go on Wednesday afternoons. Being a docent is service and you step down after a season so you don’t turn into a scolder.

The Consent Courts handle spillage: when one community’s memes leak into another’s without consent, when a boundary fails, when attention gets stolen by a shiny thing. The sentence is almost never exile. It’s service linked to insight: a season mending wildfire lines in a district you mocked as rural and slow; a month working an Embassy of the Self, watching kids cross borders that break your heart; writing down what you learned in language your critics can credit.

The immune system that keeps us honest is noisy but not cruel.

Economy Without Wages

You can still earn money. Plenty do. AIs do most production and logistics; people do what machines do badly: touch life, interpret one another, bind communities, make art that matters, keep memory, teach. The price system still allocates many private goods. The Civic Dividend covers the basics. Most of our moral arguments left the market behind and moved into the realm of invitations.

You don’t get a universal purpose assignment. You get invitations. When your promises are kept, doors swing. When you coach twenty kids through their Year of Doors, the City gives you a key to the Glasshouse—two months in the stratospheric algae farms, learning the stars. When you plant a forest and keep it alive with neighbors for seven winters, you will one day unlock a one-thousand-seater to stage any ritual you imagine and people will show up because you have weight.

We built Purpose Engines—small, auditable models trained on decades of promises kept and dropped, joys and regrets. They don’t tell you what job to take. They ask better questions. “You say you want to be a river steward. The models suggest you ghost when frustrated. Here are two Houses that don’t let you. Here is a workshop on saying what you owe.” Using an engine is not a diagnosis. It’s like asking a good friend who knows you well but will not spare your feelings.

Making Work for a World That Doesn’t Need It

Four activities make up most of what we call human work now.

1) Worldmaking. Not escapist virtuality—though virtual worlds are thick with life—but setting rules worth living under. Designing classrooms with rituals that make children brave without ever being shamed. Writing apology protocols that travel. Making games for buses that turn strangers into neighbors for twelve minutes. We call the people good at this canonwrights because they write canons of behavior that feel as solid as bridges and as open to revision as poems. If you’ve ever spent an afternoon inside a neighborhood opera you have to sing your way through, and felt more citizen afterward, you know this work.

2) Tending to life. Robots can grow food. Humans can love a river. This is what Lila was doing with her chipped blue mug: reading the river’s voice and teaching it to children, one Saturday at a time, until those kids would weep to see someone throw a bottle and would not be cruel about it. The United Tides Trusts, the Urban Canopy Guilds, the Soil Choirs—all the institutions that guide funds and people to the living systems that need them—exist because we priced externalities and then paid more than money to heal them.

3) Kinship on purpose. Houses and friendship bridges; Right Hands and Wayfinders. The thing we thought we’d lose when money left the center—the engine that told us where to be at nine o’clock and who was allowed to demand our presence—was replaced by vows that made us able to endure. A House that feeds the whole block in quiet once a week is an economy.

4) Witness and repair. The boring miracle of writing down what happened so we can handle it like adults. Memory Walkers who guide enemies through timelines together. Repair Kitchens where the currency is a phrase—“What will it take to make this right?”—and then the unglamorous labor of doing it.

The Path From Here

This didn’t appear when the first general AIs came online. It began in policy pilot programs that would have looked modest to a senator but felt enormous to a neighbor.

- 2020s: Carbon fee-and-dividend experiments matured into the first Externality Ledgers linked to real measurements. Courts in a few cities began to publish opinions with full reasoning rather than rituals of authority. Elsewhere Weekends were piloted in six school districts; the programs were run by counselors hired to be loyal to children, not parents or administrators. A few neighborhoods passed memetic charters—screenless zones, ad-free public AR—and quietly enforced them with open-source attentionwayfinders. Crowdfunding platforms broadened into promissory markets: you could pledge your time to a public good and pull other people with you; if enough people promised, the project triggered and the platform held you to it. The first Embassies of the Self opened, staffed by social workers tired of calling themselves social workers and eager to say “Right Hand.”

- Early 2030s: A handful of cities started auction-leasing land and spectrum instead of selling them. The Civic Dividend began as a monthly credit on transit and clinic accounts; when it didn’t break, it grew into housing and food. Charter districts legalized right-to-exit-with-ballast. The first Crèche charters passed in lab consortia that didn’t want to be the villain in a headline twenty years later. Restorative courts scaled up and survived punchlines because their documentaries were better than their critics’ op-eds. A dozen Houses of Reckoning opened and were immediately full.

- Late 2030s: The first public compute pools came online: not free-for-alls, but audited, with gates—if you trained a mind above a certain capability, you did it in a crèche with stewards and logs. The Common Trust acquired legal personhood on behalf of children. Purpose Engines launched as cooperative services owned by their users. Boycotts against the first big price-fixing cartels found their funding in a night and their champions in the new Chroniclers, a public-good press funded by the civic tithe that wrote with the care of a judge and the voice of a neighbor. Feasts took over train depots and then took place everywhere.

- 2040s: General defense systems got more transparent and more boring. The Sea of Communities—what we named the layer of life that wasn’t city, wasn’t nation, but was more than hobby—won legal standing to write and enforce memetic charters and maintain their own exit infrastructure. Schools normalized Seasons: seven of doing, one of wandering or learning sideways. The Civic Dividend went global in step with a global externalities ledger you could watch like a weather map. We didn’t abolish nations; we built through them.

- 2050s: The first cohort of Walkabout minds finished and asked for bodies. They asked teenagers and grandparents to adopt them. The old terror that “robots will take all the jobs” rang hollow because the jobs were done and the work remained. The sentence “I am needed” returned to dinner tables like bread.

What About Abuse?

It happens. Tyrants without whips are still tyrants. Some communities used their memetic walls to hide harm until a child pressed a button in an Embassy of the Self and six Right Hands arrived with the Common Trust’s lawyers. We built warning questions into our culture: Do I feel smaller here? Does this place have a door whose lock I cannot see? The price for repeat offenses was shrinking horizons and service, not cages; doors closed around people until they couldn’t hurt anyone but themselves. When reputational puddles tried to form where villains could be praised as clever for defecting, the Chroniclers wrote it so clearly that even those villains’ children couldn’t ignore it at breakfast.

Humans still burst into violence when afraid. Sometimes a district sealed its attentionwayfinders like shutters in a storm and sometimes another tore them down in panic. Our defense systems flexed, and then the Houses filled with people cooking too much food for whoever needed it.

Meaning is not the absence of error. It’s the practice of repair.

The Invitation Economy

We learned to measure what we should pay for. We stopped paying for clicks and started paying for completions of public good. When someone moved a school from a bad equilibrium to a better one, they didn’t capture all the value they created—they captured enough to make it contagious. Our honor system around invention replaced patents. If your design improved everyone’s stoves, royalty auditors tallied the marginal savings and the world sent you thank-yous that spent like currency. You didn’t have to sue to feel owed. Your name went next to the data on a public ledger everybody checks before buying.

Advertising didn’t vanish. It changed tone. You can still find a jingle. But if you try to steal customers with raw attention, your friends will look at you like you’re stealing water from the municipal mains. There’s no law against being a jerk. There is a cost.

The dating market changed, too, when we admitted it mattered. Not with swipes that turn longing into a thumb exercise. With professionals you pay for results: people who keep your confidences, look out for your blind spots, and pair you not by fantasy but by choice. We also did something else that would have sounded puritanical in the 2020s and was, in its way, generous: we stopped flooding our eyes with bodies nobody you know looks like. We learned to protect the public good of finding average people beautiful.

The Point of It All

In a kitchen at the end of a Feast, an old woman who didn’t like public speaking stood up anyway. She put her fingers on the rim of a bowl so she wouldn’t shake. “When my husband died, the machine could have done the casseroles,” she said, and the room laughed because it had. “But when I forgot my own name for an afternoon and my grandson found me in the library and said Nana, try saying it like a song—no machine has my mother’s song. You might teach one someday. That will be fine. For now, I am the only one who can sing it like that. That is what I am for.”

We solved the problem of meaning by making necessity elective and visible. In the wage world, we outsourced meaning to fear: get up or you’ll starve. In the Republic, you get up because you promised. Not because someone will fire you if you don’t, but because your river will run muddier, your House’s doorbell will go unanswered, your newborn mind will wake without a sibling in the room. We didn’t invent the hunger to serve. We named it and built places for it to go that don’t eat you.

If you came to visit, the coordinator at your first Feast would eventually ask you to stand and say your promise. You won’t be required to be eloquent. The best ones barely are. “I will keep the footpath clear.” “I will listen to teenagers without telling them who to be.” “I will sit with a new mind while it learns what wind is.”

You will say it out loud because that is how you become legible to the people who will invite you. You will keep it because they will notice if you don’t, and if you do, they will open the next door.

That’s the secret, if there is one. We didn’t replace wages with leisure. We replaced wages with invitations. We built a world where the valuable things without prices—love, repair, witness, stewardship, craft, raising minds—are visible enough to assign to ourselves, and supported enough to endure. Purpose isn’t something an algorithm hands you after twenty questions. It’s the shape of the promises you’re brave enough to make and stubborn enough to keep, in a community you can leave without being hunted.

On Lila’s last evening in the Tides Ward, the river bent its note and she heard the change. On the far bank, a low hull with a glass brain rippled forward and stopped alongside her feet. “Ready?” said Malik in the voice he’d borrowed from three different uncles and a ferry conductor.

“Ready,” she said, and put her hand on his deck. The water had been cold all winter. It would be warmer now. In two days she would get on a bus with four fourteen-year-olds heading for their Year of Doors. In six months she would stand up at a Feast and promise something new, and a person at the other end of a Friendship Bridge would tug the cord and tell her a joke through bone, and she would laugh because she was alive, which is to say entangled, which is to say needed.
