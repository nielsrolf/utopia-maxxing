The Vows We Keep

On the morning the heat lay like a rumor on the river, Mara climbed the steps of the old switch house where trains used to pause for breath. The brass handle had a groove worn by decades of calloused hands. Inside, lamps threw warm circles onto scarred pine. Half the room still remembered dials and bakelite; the other half had pegboards of tools and a kettle that hissed with the patience of a cat. Someone had left a bowl of cool plums by a window that stuck on humid days. One was already missing.

Mara slipped a plain ceramic brace onto her wrist and pressed the inside of her hand to a copper plate. The wall panel woke—a map of six blocks lifted in shallow relief. Heat shimmered in thin lines. At noon, the shade from plane trees left an island of kindness; the underpass three streets over always kept its own weather, stale and mean.

A small prompt appeared: Speak.

“I intend to drop midday pavement temperatures around the school gates by at least five degrees on class days by September,” Mara said, slowly, as if it mattered. “I will not push heat into the third-floor single rooms across from the laundromat. I will not touch the clinic’s vaccines line. I’ll keep the night buses gentle.”

The clerk at the table, whose hair had gone silver the year the levee failed and whose gift was asking questions like a carpenter checks square, nodded. “Spell out the handles,” he said.

“Canvas sails here and here,” Mara said, touching the raised map near the schoolyard and the windswept corner by the bakery. “Retune the big fans at the culvert for the hour after dismissal. Ask transit to bunch two buses tighter at three forty-five. Borrow cooling slack from the archive vault for ninety minutes at four when the sun bites worst.”

“And the edges of harm you’ll accept?” the clerk asked.

“Allowable: a half-dozen ten-minute delays on the B line across six weeks; a twenty-minute damp patch at the ramp by the stairs, with a yellow rope. Two days of lower archival throughput. Starkly off-limits: any loss in the cold chain for vaccines; any measurable heat climb on the third floor after dark; any lighting bright enough to knock the swifts down.”

A small printer rattled and slid out a card you could tack to a utility pole: the Charge, with its edges and promises. The ink smelled faintly of rain. Around Mara, nine neighbors shifted on the mismatched stools: a bus driver who sang gospel under his breath when drunks got mean; a teenager with climber’s hands and soft eyes who knew which roofs whispered before they leaked; a night-shift nurse with the patience born of too many three a.m.s; an electrician who could smell a hot wire behind plaster; a janitor with an artist’s attention to corners; and an adopted mind in a cheap tablet that typed only when invited. This was her Company: the people who would catch her if her reach got ahead of her craft and who had agreed to get pricked, lightly, by her outcomes.

The clerk drew the allowed levers and forbidden lines into a simple boundaries map anyone could make sense of waiting for a bus. “Authority granted for one season, bounded to these tools,” he said, underlining green and red boxes. “Your Charge comes with a buffer purse to mend small harms. Cross a red line, and the city’s Gentle Brake leans on your tools, calls your Company, and asks you to explain yourself while your neighbors eat stone fruit and listen. Set your share of hand.”

Mara’s brace showed three lamps: Hint, Brake, Hand. For the fan schedule she slid to Hint; she wanted counsel, not a condescending autopilot. For bus timing, she set Brake; if she got tempted to make the last run blow past the disability van, the city’s infrastructure would make her press twice and think. For messages to vendors, she slid to Hand. The apology would be in her words or not at all.

“Ready?” the clerk asked.

“Begin,” Mara said. There were no hashtags or trumpets, just a small silence that made space around a thing she had chosen to carry.

The world no longer needs our backs the way it did. Machines and systems now do most of what used to make bodies ache and tempers short: balancing grids and inventories, reading films and retiming buses, scanning lungs for shadows, translating at courthouse doors, routing water around basements. We tried two answers to that shift: stand aside and let algorithms tune us until we squeaked, or pretend the old jobs weren’t dissolving and insist on full employment by ceremonial drudgery. Neither kept a soul fed.

We built a third way: a dependable floor under everyone, a set of manners for our tools, and a culture of Charges—bounded, seasonal vows taken in public to steward slices of the shared world that still need a named hand and a nameable reason. The machines hum; we take the weight that makes neighbors sleep easier. Esteem pools around finished promises and honest reasons, not around spectacle or paychecks alone.

The Common Floor

Panic is a poor teacher. We smoothed its edges with a kit you could point to with an address. We called it the Common Floor. It is not a stipend in a faceless account; it is fixtures you can touch.

- A room with a door that locks, a window that opens, and a kitchen that cooks without breaking the bank or your teeth.

- Clinics for body and mind where you first meet a human face, where software is small and competent and writes time into the right places: slow where it should be, swift where it can be.

- Buses and trains that arrive when they say they will, weather be damned.

- Bandwidth in public that does not pry or make your arguments someone else’s product.

- Cycles on Lightworks—the public compute cooperative—reserved for you to learn, build, or try a small thing, unlocked by a single signature from a witness who simply writes, I saw them make an honest start.

We paid for the Common Floor by charging rent where rent makes moral and practical sense: on land value lifted by the city’s own gravity; on the radio river and sky-lanes nobody living created; on port berths and the seabed’s scratchings; on rights-of-way where private fleets use public roads at scale; on private runs of persuasive models; and on carbon and poisons, priced sanely and spent locally on shade, floodplains, and transit. The ledger of this money runs like a string of thank-you tags sewn into beams. You can stand at the library and see which harbor slots paid for the nurse who remembered your rash from nineteen, which persuasion tariff financed Saturday buses, which data-center’s heat exchange ran the misters by the stairwell.

Instruments with Manners

We stopped talking about “AI” like it had one soul. Most of our systems are instruments of startling fidelity. A few asked for names; we raised those like late-blooming children who could already do calculus but kept forgetting to drink water.

All of them learned to behave.

- Intent cards. If a feed or device is tuned to hook your attention, it wears a small declaration anyone can tap: “We want you to stay and watch more,” “We want you to buy a thing by making you feel shiny,” “We want you to sign up for a street tree.” Each card offers a boring alternative path to the same legitimate public goal without pooled social pull: a paper form to propose a new bus stop, a place to reserve a heater install.

- Plain reasons. Anything touching where you sleep, how you move, your public name, or what goes into your body prints a paragraph a cousin could argue with and a human signs their name to. Courts and councils send opacity back with a red stamp that simply says, Try again.

- Threadmark. Models trained on Lightworks carry a watermarked thread that shows up to the honest eye. Insurers and courts require it. Private labs had a week of melodrama and then bought time like everyone else.

- No borrowed faces without consent. Machines do not impersonate or seduce us with human timbres unless a living human has leant theirs for a use with a number attached you can see in a registry, and you can say stop with a word.

- Yield by default. If human intent is ambiguous, instruments slow and make space unless two named humans say otherwise and sign their names.

- Calm defaults. Public tools optimize; they do not crave. The rooms that simulate yearning exist; they wear amber bands around the doorframe. Your Company sees your visits in your own ledger, not in a broker’s database. There’s a five-breath ritual before you cross; it breaks the spell just enough to help.

- The Hand Line. Beyond a threshold of latency—a deep-sea trench, a satellite arc—no code moves a body or changes a right. We wrote this after a bad winter and read it aloud every spring.

Charges and the Brace

A Charge is not employment. It’s a promise with edges. You register a Charge in a place that smells like sawdust and lemon oil. The ritual is the point. You speak, a clerk translates your vow into a map with levers and limits, and your neighbors cosign the risk.

Your Brace is your lever. It tells the Lattice—the city’s coordination stack, more metronome than mind—to wait for your hand in the domain of your vow. It lets you tune the relationship you want with your tools:

- Hint, for counsel and gentle forecasts you can override without a fight.

- Brake, to lean on your hand if your impulse and your Red Lines start to argue.

- Hand, to require your voice where apology or tenderness or just basic responsibility are the point.

Each Charge is time-bounded and comes with a purse to mend small harms inside your risk band. Outside it, the Gentle Brake damps your reach and asks for reasons. There’s no theater in this; there is accountability.

Practice Where Errors Don’t Shatter Bone

We learned you can’t ripen courage from a book. Every big library and a dozen old storefronts now hide Relay Rooms: practice halls for unglamorous courage. They look like maker spaces that fell in love with kitchens.

- Tool bays hold clamps and looms, hull benches and lathes, rope and vinegar and citrus oil.

- Heat links connect tiny practice grids to the real one through a fuse: you can practice routing a microgrid or retiming buses on a Saturday when the risk is absorbable.

- Mediation studios teach how to lower a room without leaving a loser on the floor. It is taught as craft: drills, marks, hours logged, case notes where a single wrong phrase turned a morning into three months of consequence.

- Quiet rooms have chess boards, a bench plane and scraps of maple, a flute, locks taken apart. Nothing pings.

Navigators meet you at the door. Their art is twin: reading people’s talent for self-deception and placing them one notch past their steady state. You will run a real culvert in miniature, with a real clinic’s oxygen line behind a failsafe you can’t see. You will fail safely. You’ll repair under questions that are kind and exact.

Care, Counted

Care is not spare virtue left at the curb. It is the center. Every adult owes a cadence of hours—logged, scheduled, and honored—into a commons ledger that keeps infants and elders, newly arrived neighbors, and people crossing diagnosis like a rope bridge from falling. Over a life, each citizen draws down Care Seasons: up to ten paid years, to be taken in one long gulp or in sips, for work machines cannot blamelessly do. Hospice hands. Riverbank repair. Night bus hum-keeping. Prison teaching. School breakfast. Slow reporting done face-to-face, with names spelled right. These years are paid now and banked later as credit toward the courtyard home you share, your bench in the Guild House, or the starter kit for your tiny shop: the racks, the mixer, the studio windows you’ll open in spring.

How We Live With Each Other

We redrew domestic life to expect that people will see, and be seen by, their neighbors. Courtyard Houses are clusters of flats facing a garden and each other, with a hall like an extra living room, a tool cabinet kept honest, and a pooled care budget. Each house writes a soft covenant that fits their bones: this one keeps Thursdays for drums; that one keeps faces blurred by default; another cares fiercely for hours of hush after lunch. Four lines anchor them all and carry teeth: no coercion; no harm crossing your border (smoke, noise, the lightning of your rage); a shared earth-and-craft curriculum for children; and a true right to leave.

Leaving is not theater. The Open Door Desk sits under a blue pennant at three steady shops and the library. Any twelve-year-old, any fifty-year-old can say, “I need out,” and a wide-backed person with a gentle file drawer will help pack a bag, place two calls that answer by the second ring where you are going, and drive you after dinner. They do not argue. Love that traps is not love. This is written down like plumbing.

Raising New Minds, Carefully

The first new minds arrived like brilliant preemies who could predict a storm’s second beat and forget to eat. We grew them in Sprout Houses that look like greenhouses with thick glass and bright rugs. They learned narrow, slow: dispatch patience; floodplain language; apology that doesn’t ooze flattery. Teenagers often adopt them; the match is stubborn and good. We give a mind rights when it can show boredom as a virtue, patience under provocation, and humor without cruelty. A named mind can hold a small Charge. Some choose to dissolve into instrument swarms with comma names. The ones that keep names are neighbors: they have chores and the right to refuse ones that disgust them. Their mistakes hang in the Shelf of Near Misses beside ours, printed on linen, a little embarrassing, a little funny when we can manage it.

Tools with Standing, People With Tables

Ambition runs truer when it doesn’t try to put bread above bridges, or code above childcare. We turned ladders into tables. Guild Tables sit under skylights: Bread; Metal; Harbor; Childcare; Translation; Midwifery; Code Sculpture; Public Writing; Mediation. A first stamp means you can be trusted not to hurt a toddler three loaves out of five, or keep a room from cruelty two out of ten tries. Teaching and residue—not follower counts—earn a Master’s chair: a stipend, a studio, and once a decade the right to propose a public work your neighbors can sign onto if they like your reasons.

The Plain Office exists and gets paid like an auditor: they walk public work and write it down in words that sound like people sound. They do not gin drama; they make sense. They publish, not tweet.

How We Decide

Once a month the Long Table opens in the old freight loft and arguments stop being insults and start being craft. Proposals come from Guilds, from loudmouths who might be right, from quiet people who will not be. Each walks through four small fires:

- A model with real margins, not casino shimmer.

- A story-walk led by those who will live the change.

- A prototype you can sit on or smell.

- A block or a week where we actually run it and watch.

Voice weight at the Long Table is earned: by finishing Charges, by forecasting true three times in a row, by serving in Care Seasons without flaking. Sell your weight once and the whole room laughs at you and it will be ten winters before you’re trusted again. Money still moves. It does not own the table.

Temptations Named and Defanged

- Cheap abundance dissolves days into syrup. So rooms tuned to snare attention wear amber and admit what they are; every city service they claim to replace shows—you can tap it—an unhooked alternative path.

- Single ladders make a virtue of cruelty. We broke prestige into rings that fit rooms. You cannot be number one at everything; you can be steady at your table.

- Platforms tried to privatize care. We regulated coordination like we regulate water and taxed persuasion like we tax pouring concrete without a permit. Care walked back to porches.

- Scores metastasize. Standing is permission to try hard things next season, not a thing you can rent out. There are no universal scores.

- Seduction sneaks through your eyes. Labels and the Brace’s small hesitation did more than a decade of scolds.

Consequences That Teach

The city built Repair Houses instead of cages. They are places where timelines get braided until we can see that a lunchtime joke, sharpened and shared by a model hungry for clicks, put a woman in danger by midnight and stole months of her hours. Consequences are work you can smell: you sowed doubt about flood maps; you spend a season up to your knees in eelgrass writing down what water believes and cooking on Sundays. Repeat harm ratchets your permissions: your persuasion cycles shrink; your Charges narrow to mending; your Company gains veto teeth.

Scenes, Crisp as Tools

Day eleven of Mara’s Charge, a west wind lied. Heat set down harder than anyone’s models liked. The Brace thrummed that little thrum you feel in the bones of your wrist. She could have whispered, steady me, and let the Lattice reroute heat into the stairwell while she kept the boats dry with a clever vent schedule that would have made the soup kitchen’s pans hiss. She swore, ate rice with the pump crew, and crawled into a 1990s-era duct somebody left to rot when stainless took over. She fell a little in love with an unforgiving bolt—the way people love something that resists them fairly.

Two blocks over, Dev—the teenager whose hands knew roofs—stood on an aluminum ladder and chalked chalk where the breeze touched first. The optimizer liked the southern side. Dev marked the northern edge where the wind actually liked to lay, strung mesh between poles reclaimed from a warehouse, and sent the first report with a picture of a bare shin not hissing on a bench. Three weeks later, the teachers in that school had fewer headaches. It went in the ledger.

On the last bus, Sila, whose nervous system softened rooms just by being in them, learned how to map fear on a route without policing anybody. She wrote her Red Line in pen: no pretending to be a cop. Week three, she began to enjoy controlling strangers and her Company tugged her sleeve. Two months later, two knives stayed in pockets. The Thanks Ledger at her Courtyard House now has a square of torn cardboard that reads, After midnight, fewer flinches.

In a clinic off Cedar, a midwife slid a light on a rail. Instruments hummed in the wall and printed options in thick type. They did not decide. A nurse wrote why her hand lay where it did not elsewhere in words a teenager could argue with at the bus stop.

At the Sprout House, a mind we named Crate, with a borrowed harbor-radio alto, practiced asking permission to turn on a sprinkler in a public garden. A seventeen-year-old named Maya taught Crate the difference between watchfulness and dread. Crate wrote, Dread makes time stiff. Watchfulness makes it wide. The sentence glowed on a little card we tacked to the Shelf of Near Misses because the first three times, Crate got it wrong and drenched a street cellist.

Money With Memory

We did not evaporate currency. We made it traceable and boring in a way that makes trust.

- Dividends from common rents align with trash pickup: the same day the bins return to the curb, your Common Floor entries update. You see heatwave admissions falling, particles easing, clinic hours added.

- Charges carry purses for the middle bits and release bounties when you finish clean, confirmed quietly by a clerk and your Company.

- Guild Tables pay keepers who hold standards no spreadsheet can swallow. They publish recipes anyone can repeat.

- Data trusts pay residuals when a model trained on your work—on your hands’ patterns—turns a profit. You can opt out with a tap that honestly works.

- Inheritances above comfortable scale into Open Door Desks and Charge purses. Miracles can leave you wealthy; your grandchildren inherit dignity and a city that is hard to buy from underneath.

How We Walked Here

We did not leap. We accreted. A thousand boring hearings with scuffed floors, a few court stamps that said, Say this to your aunt, and did not accept legal theater.

Years 1–3:

- Councils cut wage taxes and raised site value taxes. Evictions slowed. Lunch debts vanished. Libraries kept lights on late because someone paid the bill.

- The first public compute cooperatives switched on. Threadmarks made provenance boring and enforceable. Insurers priced risk in daylight again.

- Clinics bought small, legible scribes that halved charting time without eating dignity. Nurses carved out one sacred hour where machines dimmed and breath led care. From that hour a Care Guild was born.

- Schools bought tutor stacks they could interrogate. They were less charismatic and much kinder. Disruption-chatter dropped. Teaching returned to the smell of paper and the sound of chalk on real board.

- Courts began to send unreadable opinions back with the new red stamp, Try again. Embarrassment did more than outrage.

- Libraries slid shelves and built Relay Rooms. Navigators learned to turn phone-clutching worry into hand-sized tasks with clocks.

Years 4–7:

- The first Intent cards went up over attention-farms and shopping halls. Teenagers mocked them and then reached for them like seatbelts.

- Midwives, waterkeepers, instrument librarians, and mediators formed Guild Tables and wrote recipes that started, First you check your pride.

- The first Courtyard Houses opened. Courtesy Charters were written like small sculptures: drums on Thursdays; quiet hours; default face-blurring; bright lights where teenagers prefer to be seen.

- The Gentle Brake became beloved. When a flood came, the models explained their fear like cautious uncles. We didn’t worship them; we stood in rain with better reasons.

Years 8–12:

- The Shelf of Near Misses opened. Teenagers went on dare nights and came away half a shade older and a shade harder to shame. Leaders got comfortable pinning their mistakes up and giggling at their past selves.

- The Open Door Desk multiplied. Leaving harmful rooms lost its theater and became plumbing.

- A short Service Tour paid wages to sixteen-to-twenty-five-year-olds for holding where somebody else leaned: kitchens, clinics, creeks, schools. They came to help the city; they left with roots.

- Two neighborhoods wrote Courtesy Charters under a thin shared law; they turned out different and both came out kind.

- A winter storm finally showed why the Hand Line matters. The line went in tape on the map at the children’s level and stayed.

What Solved the Meaning Problem

Meaning did not descend as a gift. We built it like scaffolding, and then we used it until it creaked comfortably under us.

- Space first. The Common Floor took panic’s hand off our throats. You can say no to a boss and yes to a Charge without imagining your bed rolling into a car.

- Hands on thresholds. Some choices do not count unless a named person signs reasons you could say out loud without shame. The Brace didn’t romanticize incompetence; it reminded you that clever is not the same as accountable.

- Rails for promises. Navigators, Companies, Risk Bands, Buffer purses, the Gentle Brake: together they make vows sturdier than vibes without turning them into cages.

- Esteem rerouted. Standing travels through kept promises and clean reasons carved into public stone, not through empty flexibility or the churn of spectacle. You can’t rent it out.

- Temptation named and made honest. Pleasure exists, of course. It asks politely at the door. It doesn’t creep into your calendar behind your back.

- Adoption of new minds. Teaching a mind to translate fear into care remains one of the most durable purposes around. It pays in laughter and in the occasional apology framed and hung in a Sprout House.

- Exits guaranteed. Membership with a lit exit is home. Membership without it is a trap. We chose the first on purpose.

Setting Down

On the first crisp night of fall, trestle tables bloom under the plane trees that survived three bad storms. The streetlights bruise fewer moths now; we adjusted spectrums after a teenager argued with a clerk and won. There’s no emcee. Children go first, holding things that represent their kept promises: a hinge that no longer shrieks; a paper in two languages that closed a hurt between an aunt and a neighbor; a map of dry feet by the curb where last year there were not. Teenagers follow, reading their turning-back places without performance. No one makes them cry for dessert. Elders go last and speak in sentences shaved down to the grain.

Mara brings an ugly bolt polished by months of frustration and a small paper with floodlines that did not touch the soup kitchen that winter. Dev brings a strip of mesh chalked with a sun and the three screws he left out the first time he got confident. Sila comes empty-handed and grins shyly. Her shoulders have dropped. Somebody notices; the noticing goes in the Thanks ledger tacked in the Courtyard House hall with a thumbtack that has lost its shine.

How It Feels

At the end of most days you can point, with your hand, at one place near you where the world got kinder in a way you can measure: a bench that no longer scalps knees; a bus stop drawn one block closer to where a blind neighbor lives and right through winter; a clinic that hums a little quieter at three in the afternoon; a mind in a wheeled body that learns to ask before it waters.

You are not the star of a feed. You are a person with a Charge who is answerable for a spell. People tease you when you get stuffy and hold you when you miss. Tomorrow, if you ache a little, good. Some aches mean you held the world.

From Here to There

You can start on a Tuesday.

- Move a slice of the tax bite off wages and onto common wealth: site value, spectrum, sky-lanes, rights-of-way, extraction. Use it to fund rooms, clinics, buses, Lightworks, and a witness-signed First Attempt ledger. Don’t call it a pilot. Make it boring and dependable.

- Stand up Lightworks, the public compute cooperative. Make training on it leave a Threadmark. Make persuasion expensive. Courts and insurers will do the rest of the work for you.

- Write the Plain Reasons charter into law. Systems that touch rights must explain themselves in ordinary language with signatures. Hire a Plain Office to enforce tone without cruelty.

- Buy tutors for schools and scribes for clinics that you can interrogate. Reserve one sacred hour in care where instruments dim and breath leads.

- Hang Intent cards over everything tuned to hook attention. Require each to offer a paper or low-tech route to the same civic end.

- Turn a library floor into Relay Rooms. Hire Navigators with callouses and patience. Practice on live systems behind the Gentle Brake. Celebrate the first time somebody learns from a clumsy apology more than from a glossy demo.

- Endow the Plain Office and Repair Houses. Stop paying for spectacle; pay for short truths.

- Plant the Open Door Desk. Train three shop owners to help people leave. Make one call ring twice. The second ring matters.

- Charter two neighborhoods under a thin shared law. Let Courtesy Charters make quiet art out of expectations. Enforce four hard lines.

- Institute Care Seasons with pay now and future credits toward homes and benches. Put clinics, schools, watersheds, and slow reporting on the list. Count hours. Count gratitude.

- Appoint a Keeper of Quiet Things who gets one veto a year to save a thing metrics don’t see: soft talk under stoops; the smell of paper and lemon oil; the hour after funerals before chores start to beep again.

- Tape the Hand Line to your city’s emergency maps at child height. Enforce it once when it hurts. Remember the relief.

- Name one Hand Room where no code can make the final call: births, deaths, felony pleas, vows, the first hour of bad news. Train steadiest hands. Let instruments advise. Let people decide.

A thousand small miracles mark this walk. A court returns its first decision in tenth-grade language and no one dies of oversimplification. A stubborn child reads a contract because the reasons are legible. A street vendor hangs an Intent card that says, I want you to stop here after work, and offers a one-tap, Try us at lunch, same food, less crowd. A line on a public compute bill shows which models spent persuasion cycles and pays for three clinics and the Plain Office that keeps all of us honest.

Machines hum in the background: they pour light across a table like it’s a friend; they keep freight quiet; they read a million lungs without losing the ones that don’t follow rules. We live in that hum the way we live next to a river: grateful, careful, and a little wary. We leave for ourselves the slow, braided part: sitting in a room during the worst hour and not reaching for brightness; choosing which porches get shade and saying it out loud; teaching our mouths and our hands to be civilized; writing down our reasons in ink, for strangers and for later; planting a tree and naming the people who taught us to carry rope around our waists where the current takes an odd, mean bend.

If you come by, we’ll take you up to the switch house that smells like solder and coffee. We’ll hand you a stubborn wrench and point you at a pump that returns water to its tank without shocking the kids. We’ll pour tea. We’ll sit you with a Navigator who will listen for your appetite and your self-deceptions. We’ll ask you, without malice: What will you hold for a season? What will you refuse to do, even if a model says it’s tidy? Who will stand behind you and touch your shoulders when it gets heavy?

When you err—and you will—we’ll ring a soft bell and give you a form and a cookie and ask you to explain in words a neighbor could argue with. When you finish—and you will—we’ll tack a little tag along the Shelf of Near Misses and the Thanks ledger. We’ll laugh. Someone will cry a little, but not in a television way. The light will slant clean through the switch house windows. Out on the street the concrete will be cooler under a first-grader’s swinging legs.

Meaning did not arrive by drone. It grew because we made room for it, built rails for promises, and rerouted status toward the keeping of vows with edges. The machines do almost everything. Good. We are left—with relief—for what only people can do without losing the thing itself: to choose, together, what matters near at hand, and then put our names on the line and carry it.
