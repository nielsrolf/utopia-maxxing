# The Unfinished Cathedral

## On Finding Meaning in the Age of Capable Machines

---

Begin with a specific moment of failure.

A man named Marcus is thirty-one years old, sitting on a wooden bench outside a workshop in what was once a post-industrial corridor of the Ruhr Valley, now a community of commons and craft and restored waterways. He has just broken the fourth pot this week. Not dramatically—it collapsed in the bisque firing, a structural failure in the base that he saw coming and could not prevent. He has been throwing clay for eighteen months. His hands are better than they were. They are nowhere near good enough.

He is not being trained. He is not earning a credential. No algorithm scheduled this moment. He chose it, carefully and somewhat against the advice of a career guidance system that had identified seventeen other domains where his aptitude scores suggested faster returns on investment in skill development. He chose it because three months ago he stood in a doorway watching an older woman named Karin work, and something in him responded to what he saw—not the product, not the technique, but a quality of attention so complete and unhurried that it looked, from the outside, like a form of love.

He is sitting on the bench now because Karin told him to. "When you've broken something," she said, "sit with it before you sweep it up." She said it casually, the way she says most important things.

He sits. He looks at the shards. He is beginning to understand what she means.

## The Century's Hidden Question

We know the story of what the machines took. We told it for decades—the jobs, the routines, the scaffolding of daily life that economic necessity had constructed around human hours. The displacement was real. The grief was real. The policy arguments about what to replace those structures with consumed two generations of political energy.

What we discussed less, because it was harder to name, was what the machines were revealing.

Every previous civilization had a convenient arrangement with the question of meaning. Necessity answered it before it could be properly asked. You farmed because your family needed food. You built because shelter required building. You worked the factory floor because your children required shoes, and the shoes required your hours, and the hours were spent before you could wonder what they were for. The arrangement was brutal in many ways. It was also, in a specific and rarely acknowledged way, merciful—it provided the constant forward motion that makes certain questions survivable by preventing them from fully forming.

When the machines absorbed the necessity, they removed the mercy.

The question that had always been there—*what is a human life for, apart from what it produces?*—stepped out from behind the arrangement and stood in the middle of the twenty-first century, blinking in the unaccustomed light. Entire populations who had understood themselves through their labor found themselves confronted with a void that guaranteed income and universal healthcare and beautiful public infrastructure left entirely unaddressed, because the void was not material.

It was ontological. A civilizational identity crisis, wearing the clothes of an economic disruption.

The communities that navigated this crisis with the least human cost were not the richest or the most technologically sophisticated. They were the ones that understood, early or gradually or through hard experience, what was actually being asked. Not: *how do we replace the income?* The income problems, real as they were, proved tractable. The harder question was: *what do we build, now that we can finally ask what we are building for?*

The answer that emerged—not from philosophy, not from policy, but from lived experiment across thousands of communities over several decades—was not a single answer. It was a shape. A consistent structure underlying the specific textures of wildly different lives in wildly different places. And at the center of that structure was a discovery so old that every wisdom tradition had already named it, and so difficult that every economic civilization had managed to mostly ignore it.

The discovery was this: *meaning is not something you find. It is something you make, through genuine encounter with difficulty that changes you, witnessed by people who know you well enough to see the change.*

That is not a simple statement. Unpacking what it requires—what conditions it demands, what structures support it, what mistakes destroy it—was the work of a century. It remains unfinished. It will remain unfinished, which is not a failure but the point.

## What Genuine Difficulty Requires

The first confusion was about difficulty itself.

When the machines removed economic necessity, the initial cultural response in many communities was to welcome the absence of friction as an unambiguous good. You no longer had to do things you didn't want to do. The systems could handle the tedious, the grinding, the painful. Life could be curated toward pleasure and ease and the endless buffet of available experience.

The emptiness that followed was not slow in arriving.

It arrived quickly enough to be diagnostic. Not because ease is bad—the elimination of suffering that serves no one is among the genuine achievements of the new civilization, and no serious person mourns the absence of preventable pain. But the emptiness revealed something that had been obscured by the conflation of two entirely different categories: *imposed* difficulty and *chosen* difficulty.

Imposed difficulty—the suffering of scarcity, of coercion, of preventable disease, of violence—serves no one's becoming. Its elimination is unambiguous progress. But chosen difficulty—the difficulty of learning something genuinely hard, of caring for someone when care is costly, of attempting something that might fail, of remaining present to another person's grief instead of deflecting it—this is not suffering to be optimized away. It is the medium through which selves develop. It is the friction that makes motion meaningful.

The distinction sounds obvious until you notice how systematically the industrial civilization had refused to make it. Productivity systems, optimization platforms, engagement algorithms—all were organized around the premise that friction was friction, that difficulty was difficulty, and that the good life was the one smoothed most thoroughly of both. The machines were the logical endpoint of this premise, and their arrival made visible what the premise had always contained: the possibility of a life so frictionlessly comfortable that there was nothing left to be.

The communities that recovered understood that the goal was never frictionlessness. The goal was always the right kind of friction—chosen, meaningful, calibrated to genuine human stakes. What the philosopher Iris Murdoch had called *the difficulty of reality*, willingly encountered.

They built accordingly.

## The Long Project

The organizing structure of a meaningful life in the world that emerged is not a career or a credential or an optimization target. Practitioners call it the long project: a sustained, deliberate commitment to a domain of genuine difficulty that will unfold across years or decades, that requires and rewards the specific quality of attention a particular person can bring to it, and that connects—concretely, not abstractly—to something that matters beyond the individual pursuing it.

Not a hobby. Not an interest. Something that *claims* you, that has standards you can fall short of, that will be genuinely diminished if you bring less than your full attention to it, and that you have chosen knowing this.

Long projects look like this.

A woman named Soraya has given eleven years to the restoration of a tidal estuary on the Catalan coast. She knows this estuary the way you know a person you have loved carefully across a decade: its moods and seasons, its sullenness after rainfall, its surprising moments of abundance. The AI monitoring systems that ring the estuary can describe its chemical composition, its species distribution, its deviation from baseline models with a precision that would have seemed miraculous to ecologists of the previous century. They cannot stand knee-deep in early light and feel, in the specific quality of the water's resistance against her waders, that something has shifted upstream. They cannot bring eleven years of accumulated attention to a moment of anomalous fish behavior and know—not calculate, know—that this is the thing she has been watching for.

She does not reach for her tablet immediately. She stands. She watches.

A man named Kofi in a commons workshop in Kumasi has spent fifteen years developing a hybrid tradition of furniture-making that extends a Ghanaian woodworking lineage into conversation with contemporary joinery. He cannot explain why a particular joint is wrong before he can name what would make it right—the knowledge lives in his hands before it lives in his vocabulary. He uses AI simulation tools to test structural integrity and material behavior, extraordinary tools he relies on completely. He uses them after his hands have already formed a hypothesis. The machine speaks second. The learning is his.

A woman named Dilnoza in Tashkent has given nine years to what her community calls primary pedagogy—the specific, demanding practice of accompanying children through their first encounters with genuine difficulty without either rescuing them from it or abandoning them to it. She knows seventeen children the way a careful reader knows a complex text: their specific relationship with frustration, the difference between the silence that means genuine confusion and the silence that means working-through, what each child needs at the exact moment when difficulty tips from productive toward crushing. This knowledge is not in any database. It lives in her nervous system, accumulated through nine years of close, witnessed, constantly-examined practice.

What unites these projects is not their domain but their structure. A specific person in genuine relationship with a specific piece of resistant reality, over enough time that both the person and their understanding of the thing have been substantially transformed. The structure creates something that the old economy had been providing accidentally, as a side effect, and that the emptiness revealed to be essential: the daily testimony that your specific capacities matter—that the world is genuinely different because of what you, particularly, have learned to bring to it.

This testimony cannot be faked. It cannot be algorithmically generated or therapeutically provided. It can only be earned through the long encounter with something real that is genuinely difficult, where failure is genuinely possible, and where success is genuinely won.

## The Machine in Its Right Place

The relationship between human practitioners and AI systems is the hinge on which everything else turns, and it is never finished, and the communities that understand this tend to be healthy.

The machines are extraordinary. This is not ambivalence—it is simply true. The elimination of material deprivation, the reduction in preventable suffering, the extraordinary logistical and analytical and communicative capabilities that the new civilization runs on: these should not be minimized or hedged or treated as somehow less than the goods they are. The machines handle what the machines handle, with a competence that no amount of human labor could match, and this is among the genuine achievements of the species.

What required careful, ongoing negotiation was the question of what the machines do in the specific domains that humans have claimed as their own.

The principle that emerged—not from philosophy but from observation of which communities produced people who described their lives as genuinely meaningful rather than pleasant and hollow—is called in most of the communities that have formalized it *the sovereignty practice*. Its content is consistent across its many local variations: the human proposes before the machine refines. The human attempts before the machine assists. The human fails before the machine rescues. The human interprets before the machine analyzes.

This is not a ceremonial arrangement. It is not a ritual gesture toward human dignity that changes nothing substantively. It is functional in a way that took practitioners several decades to understand clearly.

The quality of the human contribution—the depth of the pattern recognized, the specificity of the intuition, the embodied knowledge that only exists because a person has spent years in genuine relationship with resistant material—depends on the human having genuinely engaged before the machine offers its analysis. Ask the machine first, and human capacity atrophies. Not gradually and imperceptibly, but measurably, within months, in ways practitioners can feel and communities can observe. The tool is used to confirm what the machine has already said, and the practitioner becomes a supervisor rather than a practitioner, and the specific kind of knowing that only develops through genuine struggle ceases to develop.

The result is a world that is, by certain measures, less efficient. The ceramicist who could have an AI generate technically superior vessels in minutes instead spends months developing a form that remains recognizably imperfect. The joiner who could let fabrication systems solve a structural problem in seconds instead spends weeks at the bench, failing and revising. The ecologist who could rely entirely on the monitoring array instead stands in early light watching water before she reaches for any instrument.

The culture is honest about what this costs. It says, plainly: this is the price of human development. The price is worth paying not because handmade objects are superior to machine-made objects—they often are not, by any measurable criterion—but because what making makes, beyond the made object, is a maker. A person transformed through the long encounter with difficulty and material and standard into someone who knows something that cannot be extracted from the knowing and installed in a database. The object is evidence of the journey. The journey is the point.

There is a conversation, between human judgment and machine precision, that practitioners consistently describe as the most generative cognitive space available to them. It happens when a person who has formed their own hypothesis through genuine engagement brings that hypothesis into contact with a system's capacity for thoroughness and pattern recognition. The human's strange associative leaps meet the machine's tireless analysis. Neither alone produces what the combination produces.

But the conversation requires the human to go first. This is the entire architecture.

## The Communities That Hold

No long project is sustainable alone. This is not a motivational claim. It is the most consistent empirical finding from the communities that have been doing this longest, across every domain and geography.

People who pursue long projects in isolation lose their way. The standards drift. The difficulty becomes arbitrary rather than meaningful. The commitment curdles into obsession or dissipates into indifference. Something essential is missing.

What is missing is witness—specifically, the witness of people who know your work well enough to see it clearly, who have shared standards for what genuine excellence looks like, and who care enough about both the work and about you to refuse to let comfortable errors pass as acceptable. Not cheerleaders. Not evaluators. People who have been watching long enough to understand what you are building across years rather than what you produced this week, who can tell the difference between a genuine advance and a sophisticated-looking retreat, and who will tell you clearly which they are seeing.

These communities—called guilds in some places, circles of practice in others, identifiable everywhere by their texture of serious argument and genuine mutual knowledge—are the social substrate of meaning in the new world. They are not comfortable in the way that curated support communities are comfortable. They are demanding, complicated, occasionally brutal about failure. What they offer in exchange is real: the experience of being held to a standard by people who understand what good looks like from the inside, because they are doing the work themselves.

The philosopher might call these communities of practice. The practitioners call them home.

What they transmit—and this is the deepest of their functions—is not information. Information can be uploaded, downloaded, searched. What is transmitted is judgment: the accumulated wisdom about when the data is telling you something important and when it is noise, what genuine engagement with this difficulty feels like from the inside, what a practitioner's body and attention are like in their twentieth year of work compared to their third. These things can be approximately described but are primarily caught, the way children catch language, through sustained proximity to someone who has them.

Soraya did not learn her way of standing at the water's edge from a manual or a training system. She learned it from a hydrologist who had spent thirty years in relationship with tidal systems, who mostly worked in silence alongside her for two years and asked, at carefully chosen moments, questions that were designed not to transmit answers but to direct attention. What crossed between them was not primarily technique. It was a quality of noticing. An orientation toward uncertainty that holds it as information rather than embarrassment. A way of being with what you do not yet know.

She will transmit something like this to the young woman who has been coming to the estuary on early mornings for the past six months, mostly watching. Not the same quality—Soraya's is shaped by her specific history with this specific water—but something recognizably in the lineage. The chain holds.

## The Interior Monument

There is a concept at the center of how these communities understand what matters most, and it is the hardest to explain to people who have not experienced it from the inside. They call it, in some communities, the interior monument: the transformation left in the inner life of another person by a particular quality of attention.

Not the restored estuary. Not the finished chair. Not the published proof. These matter—the new civilization honors made things and restored things and understood things as genuine goods. But what matters most is the change in another consciousness produced by a genuine encounter with someone who was fully present.

When Karin the ceramicist stands alongside Marcus in the workshop—mostly silent, watching what he does, asking, at carefully chosen moments, questions that are designed to direct his attention rather than transmit her answer—something is happening that no system can replicate. She is not transferring information. The AI tutorial available on any device could transfer information more efficiently. She is transmitting a quality of attention. A way of being with uncertainty. The specific texture of how a person holds their attention when they are genuinely curious about what will happen next.

The apprentice is in the presence of someone who has developed, through decades of sustained engagement, a particular way of being with clay—a quality of listening to the material, of treating resistance as communication rather than obstacle—and this proximity, sustained over months and years, begins to cultivate similar qualities in the apprentice. Not through instruction. Through contagion. Through the slow absorption that happens when a younger sensibility spends enough time in genuine contact with an older one.

The interior monument that Karin leaves in Marcus—and will leave in the several others who will come after him—is not her technique. Her technique will be superseded. It is a quality of attention that will shape how he is with difficult materials for the rest of his life, that will inflect how he is with difficulty of every kind, and that will, if he practices well and if he is lucky and if the community holds, find its way into the nervous systems of people he has not yet met.

This is what survives. Not the name. Not even the bowls, which will break. The quality of attention, distributed and carried forward through chains of proximity that stretch across generations, becoming something no single person could have produced or anticipated.

The machines cannot generate this. They cannot be in genuine relationship with resistant material across years, changed by the relationship. They cannot stand beside a struggling apprentice and offer the silence that is more useful than explanation. They cannot transmit, through sustained presence, the specific texture of what genuine mastery feels like from the inside.

This is not a gap waiting to be closed. It is a description of what machines are, which is to say a description of what they are not.

What they are not is mortal. What they are not is at stake. What they are not is genuinely uncertain in the way that a consciousness genuinely uncertain can be uncertain—not processing insufficient data, but *not knowing yet*, which is a different experience entirely, one that requires something to be at stake in the not-knowing, some self whose identity will be affected by what is discovered.

The interior monument can only be left by a consciousness genuinely at stake. This is the one thing the machines cannot be, and it turns out to be the most important thing.

## Transmission and the Long Chain

The problem that preoccupied the communities most deeply—the one that generated the most argument and the most careful institutional design—was not how to create meaning. It was how to create people capable of wanting something hard enough to pursue it across the inevitable periods when it doesn't want to be pursued.

This capacity does not arrive naturally in comfortable conditions. It does not develop in response to motivational content or purpose platforms or any system that tries to generate the sensation of meaningful engagement without the substance. It is caught, from someone who embodies it, by a person who spends enough time in genuine proximity to feel what it is like from the inside and discover whether something in them responds.

This is why the proximity year exists in most of these communities' approaches to children's formation: the year in which children move through encounters with practitioners deep in their long projects. Not to learn the practice—the practice comes later, if it comes at all. To encounter what practice looks like. To be present to the specific quality of a person who has given years to something difficult, and to discover whether something in them stirs.

The discovery almost always begins as a kind of shock. The shock of a quality of attention palpably different from anything available through a screen. More specific than admiration. Something closer to recognition—this kind of person is possible—followed, sometimes, by a hunger the child cannot yet name. The hunger of a self that has glimpsed what it might become if it chose carefully and worked long and did not flinch from the difficulty.

The educational systems that work are organized around one hard-won understanding: you cannot give someone a calling. You can only create the conditions in which they might discover one. The conditions require genuine work with genuine stakes, in proximity to adults doing the same, within a community that has actual standards and actually cares whether they are met.

Marcus sitting on the bench with his broken pot is in these conditions. Karin told him to sit with it because sitting with it is different from being told about sitting with it. You learn the practice by doing the practice, and you learn what the practice is by watching someone who knows it well enough to do it without explaining it. The explanation comes later, when you have enough of the experience to understand what the explanation is pointing at.

The transmission is the civilization's most important inheritance. Not its governance systems or its ecological restoration or its technical achievements, though these matter. The most important inheritance is the generation of people who have caught, from someone who had it, the specific quality of wanting something difficult enough to give yourself to it without guarantee—the quality that makes a full life possible.

## Two Scales, Held Together

The philosophical achievement that the century struggled hardest to reach—and that remains fragile and requires daily tending—is the capacity to hold two scales simultaneously.

The intimate scale: the specific, fully present reality of this estuary, this morning light, this clay that will not center, this child who is struggling in exactly the way she struggles. The scale at which abstraction offers nothing because what is real is what is here. Soraya at the water before dawn. The weight of genuine stakes.

The vast scale: the recognition that this estuary, this morning, this act of specific attention, exists within a story of almost inconceivable scope. That the universe spent thirteen billion years producing the conditions for this particular consciousness to stand at this particular water and know things about it that exist nowhere else. That consciousness—the capacity to be genuinely uncertain, to be changed by eleven years of relationship with one place, to notice that something has shifted upstream before you can explain why—is among the rarest and most remarkable phenomena in the observable universe.

The error that cost the most was the inability to hold both at once. The intimate scale alone produced attachment without perspective: the parochialism that cannot see beyond its own commitments, the grasping that turns love into possession. The cosmic scale alone produced perspective without love: the view from nowhere that finds everything equally interesting and therefore cares about nothing in particular—the philosophical achievement that leaves the philosopher unable to get out of bed, or to stand at the water's edge, or to care whether the pot centers.

What the communities of practice discovered—and what the contemplative traditions that survived the transition and the harder-won wisdom of the care arts all converged on—was that the two scales are not in competition. They are the same reality seen from two distances, and the full life requires holding both.

The marsh is the universe attending to itself through the specific instrument of a woman who has given eleven years to learning how to attend. The broken pot is the universe discovering, through the specific instrument of a man who chose to stay with the difficulty, what clay requires of hands that do not yet know enough. The child who cannot yet name her hunger is the universe wondering, through the instrument of a small body with a large future, what it is capable of becoming.

This is not metaphor. Or rather, it is a metaphor that turns out to be literally true in the relevant sense: the universe has no backup for Soraya's knowledge of this estuary. It exists entirely in her, and in the community that has watched her build it, and in the young woman who comes to the water on early mornings and is beginning, slowly, to see what Soraya sees. When that chain breaks, something genuine is lost. The fact that the sensors keep running is not the same fact.

The recognition of this—the vertigo of it, and the dignity it simultaneously confers—is among the things the schools of becoming are organized to cultivate. Not through instruction but through the conditions for experience: the late conversations after a long day of difficult practice, when exhaustion and accomplishment together make something visible that ordinary busyness conceals. The recognition that you are a link in a chain that extends before you and will extend after you only if you extend it. That the specific knowledge you are building lives nowhere except in your nervous system, shaped by your particular history of failure and recovery with this particular difficult reality. That it is not merely personal. That the universe genuinely does not have another copy.

This is the answer to the vertigo that the machines produced—the second vertigo, not of mortality but of dispensability. The answer is not consolation. The machines genuinely can do most of what the economy once valued more efficiently than any human. But the economy was measuring the wrong things, because it was the only instrument available and it answered the questions it could answer.

What you offer—what only you can offer—is not your computational speed or your capacity for tireless analysis. It is your specific, mortal, fully-situated self in genuine relationship with something difficult over enough time to be changed by it, witnessed by a community that knows the difference, transmitting what you have become to someone who needs it and will carry it forward in ways you will never see.

That is not dispensable. That is irreplaceable. That is, in the deepest sense available to language, what you are *for*.

## The Architecture of Days

You wake in the cooperative house and the morning is already making its claims. The garden needs attention in the lower bed. Your hands are still learning what they need to learn about this clay, this specific deposit from the quarry two valleys over that behaves differently from any material the tutorials prepared you for. The community governance meeting this week is approaching an impasse that will require genuine creativity to resolve, not algorithm. Your neighbor's son has been apprenticing in your workshop for four months and last week he broke something and you watched him start to reach for his device and you watched him stop himself, and the stopping was more important than anything you have yet formally taught him.

The day makes its demands. You make your choices within those demands. The choices are not optimal by any external metric—you could produce more, know more, achieve more by many measures if you arranged your hours differently. But the day is full in the sense that actually matters: full of things that require you specifically, that will be different because of what you specifically bring to them, that are pushing back in ways that are changing you.

The question of what your life is for does not arise as an anxiety because it is absorbed into the texture of what you are doing. Not answered—the question is permanent, as the difficulty is permanent, as the becoming is permanent. Absorbed. Distributed across the hours in a way that makes the hours livable, makes them more than livable, makes them the specific and irreplaceable substance of a life that is genuinely yours.

This is what the machines made possible. Not the elimination of difficulty. The elimination of compulsion, which freed difficulty to become chosen. The elimination of imposed necessity, which freed the question of what we are building toward to become, at last, the organizing question rather than the question we defer until we have more time.

We have the time now. The machines gave us the time. What we do with it—what kind of people we build in ourselves and in each other, what quality of attention we develop and transmit, what we leave in the inner lives of the people who encounter us—this is the project the machines cannot do for us and the project that, it turns out, was always the one worth doing.

## The Unfinished Cathedral

In the medieval period, cathedrals were built across generations. The architects who laid the foundations knew they would not see the nave completed. The craftsmen who carved the capitals knew they would not see the windows installed. The communities that funded the construction understood they were building for their grandchildren's grandchildren, and this understanding was not experienced as loss but as participation in something larger than any individual life.

The civilization we are building is an unfinished cathedral.

The ecological restoration will not be complete in any single lifetime. The development of governance wise enough to match our capabilities—always a generation behind, always learning from its failures—will not be complete. The cultivation of the quality of human presence and attention and care that the long projects require: this is permanently in progress, permanently incomplete, permanently being handed from one generation to the next in the form of people who have been changed by genuine engagement and who carry the change forward.

The incompleteness is not a failure. It is the structure.

A cathedral that was complete would be a monument. A cathedral under construction is a community—a living organization of people with different roles, different skills, different generations of investment, all oriented toward the same beautiful, distant, never-quite-arrived thing. The orientation is the binding. The shared direction, even without a shared arrival, is what makes the community a community rather than a collection of individuals.

We are building the unfinished cathedral of a civilization that asks the right question. Not *what can we produce?* but *what are we becoming, and is it worth becoming?* Not *how do we use the machines?* but *what do we use them for, and what do we protect from them, and how do we keep the protection from becoming mere sentiment?*

These questions are never answered. They are only lived toward, incompletely and seriously, by specific people in specific places with specific communities watching them fail and watching them recover and knowing, from the specificity of their watching, the difference between the two.

## A Final Morning

Soraya is still at the water's edge. The light has changed in the forty minutes she has been standing here. The behavior of the particular fish has shown her something she did not know before, cannot yet fully articulate, and will spend the next weeks and possibly months turning over in her attention until it becomes part of how she sees.

She reaches for her tablet. The sensors confirm what she noticed and offer context she did not have. The conversation between her judgment and the data begins—the generative space, neither alone but her first, her initiative, her framing.

Twenty meters away, half-concealed by a stand of restored sedge grass, the young woman who has been coming here on early mornings for six months stands watching. She has not been invited to stand closer. She will be, in time. For now she is learning something that cannot be taught, only shown: what a person looks like who is genuinely present to the world they have chosen to inhabit. What it looks like to attend to something as if it mattered, because it does matter, because you have given it years and been changed by it and will give it more years and be changed further, and somewhere at the end of all that giving and changing will be something that deserves to be called a life.

The young woman does not know yet whether this is what she wants. She knows that she keeps coming back. She knows that the quality of the morning here is different from any morning available through any screen, and that the difference is constituted precisely by the woman standing at the water's edge before she reaches for any tool.

She takes a step closer.

This is the whole of the answer, and it is not simple, and it is not finished, and it is exactly the right length for what it is: long enough to contain genuine difficulty, and open enough at the end to require you to continue it yourself.

The work is never done.

The work is the point.

Begin.
