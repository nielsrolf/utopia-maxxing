# The Age of Becoming: A Vision of the World We Might Build

## Prologue: What We Got Wrong About Meaning

For centuries, we told ourselves a story about purpose: that meaning came from necessity. You raised crops because otherwise you starved. You built houses because otherwise you froze. You worked because otherwise you failed, and failure meant suffering, and suffering was the backdrop against which your rare moments of joy briefly shone. Purpose was the shadow cast by fear.

When people began to understand what artificial intelligence might do to that arrangement, many panicked. If machines could do the work, what would humans do? The question betrayed our assumptions. We had confused the *compulsion* to do things with the *meaning* of doing them. We had mistaken the chain for the anchor.

The world I am describing is one that learned the difference.

---

## I. The First Years: The Great Unhiding

The transition did not happen all at once, and it was not painless. There was a decade, roughly spanning the 2030s, that historians would later call the Great Unhiding—the period when automation made visible what had always been true but financially inconvenient to acknowledge: that vast amounts of human labor had never been particularly meaningful to the people doing it. The spreadsheets, the compliance forms, the customer service scripts read from screens, the fulfillment warehouses where people walked seventeen miles a day moving boxes—when these disappeared, there was grief, then relief, then the terrifying open space of days suddenly freed.

The grief surprised everyone, even those who had hated their jobs. It turned out that repetitive, meaningless work had still organized time. It had provided colleagues, routine, the small satisfactions of done. When it went, people felt unmoored in ways they struggled to articulate. For several years, rates of depression and anxiety rose, not because people were suffering more objectively, but because the anesthetic of compulsory busyness had been removed and they could finally feel what had always been underneath.

Governments, to their credit, mostly got the material part right. The Universal Flourishing Dividend—a name that was immediately mocked and then eventually adopted sincerely—ensured that no one went without. Housing, food, healthcare, education, the basic architecture of a dignified life: these became infrastructure, like roads, simply provided. There was political pain in this transition, years of it, but the alternative was mass destitution alongside unprecedented machine-generated wealth, and even the most ideologically resistant governments eventually understood that this was not a stable arrangement.

So within a single generation, the material problem was largely solved. Everyone had enough. Many had more than enough. And the world discovered, with something like shock, that enough was only the beginning.

## II. The Culture of Making

What emerged from the unhiding was not chaos but revelation.

It turned out that humans, freed from compulsion, did not become passive. They became *feverish*. The first and most striking shift was the explosion of what people began simply calling making—an umbrella term that encompassed art, craft, building, growing, cooking, coding, composing, writing, designing, performing, and a hundred other acts of creation that had always existed at the margins of economically necessary life but now moved to the center.

In 2047, the city of Detroit—which had spent a century as a symbol of industrial collapse—completed a transformation into what its residents called the Workshops. Not a single factory. Instead, district after district of maker spaces, ceramic studios, woodworking halls, recording rooms, textile workshops, urban farms, theaters, machine shops where people built inexplicable personal machines, bakeries that operated more like research laboratories. Entry was free. AI systems handled procurement, logistics, maintenance. Humans showed up because they wanted to.

What struck visitors most was not the volume of making but its *character*. Nobody was optimizing. A woman in her sixties spent four months learning to blow glass so she could make a single bowl for her granddaughter's birthday. A teenager rebuilt a 1968 motorcycle engine not because he needed the motorcycle but because he was in love with the problem. A former accountant spent her days growing heirloom tomatoes in seventeen varieties and writing careful, obsessive notes about their differences in flavor depending on soil composition, water timing, cloud cover. She shared these notes with no one in particular. She published them to a small subscriber list of people who found this as interesting as she did. Twelve thousand people found this as interesting as she did.

This was the first great discovery of the age: that meaning, for most people, had very little to do with scale. The meaning was in the *doing*, in the particular texture of skill acquired over time, in the specific relationship between a person and a material or a problem. The AI could make a better bowl, a faster engine, a more rigorous tomato analysis. It was irrelevant. The humans were not making products. They were making themselves.

## III. The Craft of Relationship

If the first great revelation was about making, the second was about *being with*.

The old economy had treated human attention as a resource to be harvested. Social media had quantified relationship into engagement metrics. Even friendship had been subtly reshaped by the assumption that time spent not producing was time wasted. People had become shockingly bad at simply being in the presence of one another without agenda.

The new world invested seriously, and unusually concretely, in relearning this.

In most municipalities, the physical infrastructure changed first. The term *third places*—neither home nor work—had been used nostalgically for decades to describe what had been lost: the café where you were a regular, the community hall, the park bench where neighbors stopped and talked. Now these were built deliberately and maintained as seriously as hospitals. Every neighborhood had what came to be called a Commons: a building without a single fixed purpose, available to whoever arrived with whatever they needed to do or say or share. AI systems managed the scheduling, the temperature, the supplies. Humans managed the culture.

The culture that emerged was harder to describe but easier to recognize. It looked like: an elderly man teaching a twelve-year-old girl to play chess in the corner of a Commons in Osaka, every Tuesday afternoon for three years. It looked like: a grief circle that had met every month for eleven years in a Commons in Lagos, rotating who brought food, the members now so old friends that new grief could be held inside old laughter. It looked like: a reading group in São Paulo that met weekly and had, over eight years, read one very long and difficult novel together, arguing about every page, splitting into factions, reuniting over coffee.

The AI systems contributed here too, but carefully. They became extraordinarily good at what the designers called *resonance mapping*—identifying people who shared unusual combinations of interests, geography, and temperament, and creating gentle conditions for them to meet. Not algorithmic matching in the old frantic sense, which had optimized for engagement by stoking conflict, but slower, more patient orchestration. A retired engineer and a twenty-two-year-old poet were invited to the same ceramics workshop because the AI had noticed that both were wrestling with questions about impermanence in their recent writings, one in technical journals and one in notebooks she had never shared publicly. They would spend the next decade as best friends, collaborating on a series of art installations about decay. Neither would have predicted this. Neither could have arranged it.

The therapists—both human and AI—became perhaps the most important infrastructure of all. The old world had made therapy a luxury, rationed by insurance systems and waiting lists. The new world made it as available as drinking water. Every person who wanted to talk to someone could talk to someone. The AI therapists were not pretending to be human; they were something new, enormously patient, deeply knowledgeable, free of the transference complications that made human therapeutic relationships both powerful and fragile. They helped millions of people understand their own patterns, grieve properly, emerge from depression, learn the basic skills of emotional communication that poverty and trauma had prevented their parents from teaching them. They did not replace human connection. They made people more capable of it.

## IV. The Age of Amateur Mastery

Here is something the old world had made structurally impossible and the new world made ordinary: the decades-long pursuit of a skill for no reason except the skill itself.

In the old world, expertise was legitimized by economic function. You were a pianist if you could make money as a pianist. You were a scientist if an institution paid you to do science. The word *amateur*, which had once meant *lover of*, had become a term of mild condescension: someone not quite good enough to be real.

This changed when the economic justification was removed from everyone at once. Suddenly the amateur and the professional stood on the same ground. The distinction that remained was not economic but a matter of depth and time: how long had you given to this, how far had you gone.

And people went remarkably far.

A forty-year-old former logistics manager named Caoimhe spent fifteen years becoming genuinely expert in the migration patterns of birds across the Atlantic, working alongside AI systems that could process satellite data she could not, contributing something the AI genuinely could not contribute: the particular human capacity for noticing the unexpected, for caring about the anomaly that didn't fit, for sitting still on a cliff in West Cork at 4 a.m. because something about the light made her suspect the shearwaters were doing something unusual, and being right. She published three papers with ornithologists who initially patronized her and eventually stopped. She also, separately, raised three children she was deeply present for, grew most of her own vegetables, and ran a small irregular radio show about birds that had eleven thousand devoted listeners who showed up in the comments every week with their own observations, their own care.

This was not exceptional. This was *normal*. The world was full of Caoimhes—people who had chosen a domain not because it would make them rich or famous but because it had called to them, and who had followed that call for decades until they knew something deeply, until they carried a piece of the world's complexity in themselves, until they had something to pass on.

The knowledge they carried was passed on, constantly and informally, in the Commons, in the maker spaces, in conversations that went long over shared meals. The AI systems were extraordinary repositories and synthesizers of information. But humans were the ones who made that information *matter*—who wrapped it in story, who attached it to memory, who said *I learned this from my friend Caoimhe, who spent fifteen years watching birds, and what she showed me changed how I see the coast*.

## V. The Deepest Thing

I want to be careful here, because this is the part that is hardest to describe without making it sound either trivial or grandiose.

The deepest change in this world was a change in what people understood themselves to be *for*.

In the old world, even the most thoughtful people tended, in their private moments, to feel that they were justified by their productivity. By their usefulness. By what they produced that others consumed. Even love was shadowed by this framework—the nagging sense that you had to *earn* the love of others, to justify your space in their lives.

The new world did not eliminate this feeling overnight. Centuries of economic conditioning do not dissolve in a decade. But it undermined the framework so thoroughly that, within a generation, children were growing up who simply did not have the same anxious relationship to justification. They had grown up in Commons and workshops. They had watched adults pursue passions without apology. They had been taught, by the culture rather than by any formal curriculum, that their presence in a life was not contingent on what they could provide.

This sounds abstract. Here is what it looked like concretely.

A boy named Tomás grew up in a small coastal city in Chile. His parents both worked—at what would now be called *chosen work*, which is to say work they had selected not because they needed the money but because they found it genuinely meaningful: his mother ran a marine biology monitoring program and his father taught music at the Commons three mornings a week. Tomás spent his childhood in the kitchen, cooking. Not because anyone asked him to, not because it would lead anywhere, but because he loved it with a specificity that surprised even him—he was obsessed with fermentation, with the alchemy of time and microbe and ingredient, with the way kimchi and sourdough and miso and wine were all the same question asked by different cultures.

By the time Tomás was thirty, he had fermented things that probably no one in history had fermented before, and had shared most of them with his neighbors, and had apprenticed two young people who were similarly obsessed, and had sat with his dying father and told him, in the specific way that only a very long shared life allows, exactly what those mornings of music lessons had meant to him. He was not famous. He did not want to be famous. He was not producing anything of obvious economic value. He was in the middle of a life that was, by every measure he had ever applied to the word, *meaningful*. Purposeful. Full.

When researchers studying the new world tried to understand what had made this possible, they kept arriving at the same answer, which was not an answer about economics or technology. It was an answer about *permission*. Tomás had permission, structural and cultural, to take himself seriously as a fermenter of things—to treat this not as a hobby that existed around the edges of his real life but as a genuine vocation that structured his days. And because he took it seriously, and the people around him took it seriously, and the world had stopped insisting that seriousness required economic justification, he had built a life around it that was dense with learning, relationship, beauty, and the particular satisfaction of having gone *deep* into something over a long time.

This was the meaning the new world had found. Not the meaning of grand purpose—of saving the world, of changing history, though some people did these things too and found meaning in them. The meaning of *devotion*. Of showing up, repeatedly, over years, to the things and people you loved. Of becoming, slowly, someone with something to offer—not because the market demanded it but because you could not help it, because the depth of your caring had made you capable.

## VI. Coda: The Child's Drawing

In the corner of a museum, in a city that used to be called Detroit, there is a small framed child's drawing under good lighting. The child drew a person—lumpy, five-limbed, cheerful in the way that children's drawings are cheerful, without any self-consciousness about what good drawing is supposed to look like. Beside the drawing, a small card. It reads:

*Given by her to him. Kept by him for thirty years. Given back by him to her children. Here now, because the children thought the world should see it.*

No other explanation is offered. Visitors stand in front of it for longer than they stand in front of anything else in the museum. Some of them cry. Some take photographs and then, unusually, put their phones away and just look.

What they are looking at is the record of a specific love—small, private, passed between two people over decades, outlasting both of them, carrying meaning that could not have been predicted when the crayon first touched the paper. This is what the new world has learned to treat as precious. Not the monuments. Not the grand gestures. The evidence that someone was here, and loved someone, and was known.

The world we built is made of these. It is made of the patient cultivation of skill, the fermentation of relationship over time, the courage to take your own devotion seriously even when no one is paying you for it. It is made of Commons where strangers became friends over long tables, and workshops where people failed at things and tried again, and clinics where old grief was finally grieved, and apartments where children grew up watching their parents do things for love rather than money.

The AI systems built the roads. Humans built each other.

That was always the work. We just finally gave ourselves permission to do it.
