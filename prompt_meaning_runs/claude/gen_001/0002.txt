# The Covenant of Makers: A Vision of the World We Are Building

## I. Arrival

Imagine you are a traveler arriving by train into the city of Thessaly—not the ancient Greek region, but a new city founded forty years from now on the high plains of central Montana, where the Anaconda copper smelters once poisoned the earth. The remediation took two decades. The founding took five more years of argument, design, and democratic deliberation. What stands there now took the remaining time to become what it is: a place that has quietly solved, in practical and imperfect ways, the problem that haunted the early twenty-first century like a ghost that nobody wanted to name directly.

The problem was this: *what are people for?*

For ten thousand years, the answer was obvious and brutal. People were for survival—for planting, harvesting, building, defending, bearing children who would do the same. Then, across three centuries of industrialization, a new answer emerged: people were for *producing*. Your worth was legible through your economic contribution. You were a unit of labor. Your identity was your job.

By the 2030s, that answer was collapsing. Not because AI had taken every job—it hadn't, quite—but because the *threat* that it might had exposed how fragile the arrangement always was. Meaning borrowed from productivity is borrowed meaning. When the loan is called in, you discover you never owned it at all.

Thessaly was built by people who understood this, and who decided to build differently. The train platform where you arrive is maintained by a rotating guild of citizens who chose this work as part of their civic contribution—not because they had to, but because the city's founding charter established that certain forms of care for common spaces belong to everyone, rotationally, deliberately. The platform is swept clean. There are flowers in clay pots. A teenager with a tablet is annotating the flowers' Latin names, cross-referencing a botanical database, arguing with an AI assistant about whether the specimen in pot seven is *Echinacea purpurea* or a cultivar. She doesn't look up when the train arrives. She is deep in a disagreement she finds genuinely important.

This is the first thing you notice about Thessaly: people here seem to be doing what they are actually doing. They are not performing busyness. They are not optimizing for the appearance of productivity. They are, in some difficult-to-articulate way, *present*.

## II. The Great Untangling

To understand how Thessaly came to exist, you have to understand what the people who built it called the Great Untangling—their name for the painful decade between 2031 and 2041, when the old equation between work and worth finally broke down completely and society was forced to decide what to replace it with.

The proximate cause was economic. By 2031, AI systems could do approximately seventy percent of what had previously been called "knowledge work"—drafting, analysis, coding, design, diagnosis—faster, cheaper, and in many respects better than human beings. This didn't produce unemployment in the simple sense. People still worked. But the work felt different. Much of it felt like supervision of systems that didn't really need supervising, or like performance for managers who didn't really need the performance. The philosopher David Graeber had identified "bullshit jobs" in the 2010s—work that even the people doing it recognized as pointless—and now the category expanded dramatically. People kept their salaries. They lost their sense of purpose.

The psychological consequences were severe and well-documented: a crisis of meaning that manifested as depression, addiction, political radicalization, and a sharp increase in what researchers called "existential anxiety"—the feeling that one's existence was superfluous. The welfare data from this period is sobering. Suicide rates peaked. So did rates of what clinicians called "drift"—people who were technically alive and technically employed but who reported feeling like spectators of their own lives.

The political response was chaotic. Some governments implemented Universal Basic Income. This helped with material deprivation but did not, by itself, address the meaning crisis. Money without structure, it turned out, was not the same as freedom. Many UBI recipients experienced not liberation but a particular kind of vertigo—the vertigo of a culture that had told them for their entire lives that their worth was their productivity, now offering them money not to produce. The message felt contemptuous even when it wasn't intended to be.

What actually helped—what became the foundation of the new world you're visiting—came from three directions at once, and from the unlikely collaboration of people who in earlier decades would never have talked to each other.

The first group were the **economists**, specifically a younger generation who had been studying what made people genuinely happy for decades and had grown frustrated that their findings were systematically ignored. They knew, with substantial empirical confidence, that human flourishing required roughly six things: authentic relationships, a sense of competence and growth, autonomy over one's choices, contribution to something larger than oneself, engagement with genuine challenge, and connection to some form of meaning or narrative that extended beyond the immediate moment. None of these required economic productivity *per se*. All of them required *structure*—contexts, communities, and practices within which they could be expressed.

The second group were the **makers**—craftspeople, artists, teachers, scientists, farmers, builders, musicians, and the millions of hobbyists whose passionate amateur engagement with difficult things had always been the secret engine of human culture. These people had spent the industrial era slightly embarrassed about what they did, because it didn't fit neatly into the categories of productive labor. Now, suddenly, they had something important to say. They had been practicing, all along, an answer to the question that everyone else was just now asking.

The third group were the **institution designers**—people who understood that meaning doesn't emerge spontaneously but must be *scaffolded*. Humans are not meaning-producing machines who just need to be freed from constraints. We are meaning-*seeking* creatures who need contexts, practices, rituals, communities, and structures within which the search can succeed. The great mistake of the early UBI era was imagining that removing economic coercion would automatically produce flourishing. What it produced was liberty without architecture—a vast open field with no paths, no landmarks, no one to walk with.

The Great Untangling was the decade in which these three groups found each other, argued bitterly, compromised extensively, and began building something new. Thessaly is one result. It is not the only result. But it is one of the clearest expressions of what they built.

## III. The Architecture of Meaningful Days

A day in Thessaly is structured not by the demands of a job but by what its founders called the *four domains*: Craft, Care, Commons, and Contemplation. Every citizen engages with all four, though in proportions that vary by life stage, temperament, and choice. The city's social architecture is designed to make engagement with each domain easy, rewarding, and genuinely challenging.

**Craft** is the domain of making and mastering. In Thessaly, this is taken with a seriousness that visitors from older cities sometimes find startling. The city maintains forty-seven workshops, ranging from woodworking to ceramics to electronics to textile work to glassblowing to fermentation to software to music to writing to mathematics. These are not hobby spaces. They are places where people pursue genuine excellence under the guidance of masters, within traditions that have standards and histories and accumulated wisdom.

The key insight here—the one that took the founders longest to arrive at—is that meaningful making requires *difficulty* and *standards*. The early versions of maker spaces in the 2020s often failed to produce genuine meaning because they were structured around self-expression rather than excellence. People were encouraged to make whatever they wanted, however they wanted, with no expectation of rigor. This produced a certain kind of pleasant activity, but not the deep satisfaction that comes from genuinely mastering something hard, meeting the standards of a craft tradition, and earning the recognition of people who know enough to recognize quality.

Thessaly's workshops operate on an apprenticeship model. You enter as a novice. You are taught by journeypeople who are themselves still learning, under the supervision of masters who have spent decades at their craft. You are evaluated, not by any machine metric, but by the judgments of people who understand what excellence in this domain looks like. You advance when those people say you have advanced. You make things that are genuinely used—furniture for the common rooms, pottery for the communal kitchens, software for the city's systems, music for the weekly concerts that are among the city's most beloved institutions.

The AI systems in the workshops are present but carefully positioned. They are tutors, demonstrators, and collaborators—not replacements. A woodworking student can ask the AI to show her in simulation what will happen if she makes a particular cut. She cannot ask the AI to make the cut for her. The distinction is enforced partly by tool design and partly by social norm. When you bring a finished piece to your master for evaluation, the question "did you make this?" has a real answer with real stakes. The pride of having made something well is inseparable from the knowledge that the hands and judgment and decisions were yours.

**Care** is the domain of attending to other people and to the living world. In Thessaly, care work is not an afterthought or a problem to be automated away. It is understood as one of the central human vocations—the work of raising children, tending to the elderly, supporting those who are struggling, maintaining relationships, and attending to the more-than-human world of plants and animals and ecosystems that surrounds and sustains the city.

This domain was the most contested during the founding period. Some argued that AI could handle most care work—that robot caregivers could tend to the elderly efficiently, that algorithmic systems could monitor children effectively, that automated therapists could provide mental health support at scale. The founders rejected this argument, not on romantic grounds, but on empirical ones. The research was unambiguous: people needed to be cared for by other people. Not because machines couldn't perform the surface behaviors of care, but because what humans need from care is not the surface behavior—it is the *attention* of another consciousness, the *recognition* of their particularity by someone who could, in principle, be attending to something else but has chosen to attend to them.

In Thessaly, elder care is partly a professional vocation and partly a civic obligation. Every adult citizen spends a minimum of four hours per week in direct care work—visiting elders, working with children, tending to the community gardens that provide a significant fraction of the city's food. This is not experienced as a tax. It is experienced as one of the most reliably meaningful parts of many people's weeks. The relationships formed across generations in these contexts are among the most frequently cited sources of happiness in the city's annual wellbeing surveys.

**Commons** is the domain of civic life—the governance, maintenance, and stewardship of shared spaces and shared decisions. Thessaly is governed by a system its founders called *deliberative federalism*: a nested structure of neighborhood assemblies, city councils, and domain-specific governing bodies, all connected by AI-assisted deliberation tools that translate the wisdom of large groups into actual decisions without losing the texture of individual voices.

The AI systems here are doing genuinely important work—synthesizing thousands of comments into legible summaries, identifying where apparent disagreements mask underlying consensus, flagging when proposed decisions are likely to have unintended consequences. But the decisions themselves are made by humans, in conversation with other humans, after deliberation that takes time and requires real engagement. Citizens of Thessaly are expected to participate in at least two deliberative processes per year—to show up, listen, argue, and ultimately commit to collective decisions they may not entirely agree with. The experience of living under rules you helped make, even imperfectly, is very different from living under rules that were handed down.

**Contemplation** is the domain of reflection, learning, and the inner life. Thessaly has a library that is one of the most beautiful buildings in the city, designed by a collaborative team that spent three years arguing about what a library should be in an age when information is universally accessible. Their answer: a place not for accessing information but for *thinking*—for sitting with difficult ideas, for the kind of slow, unhurried engagement with questions that produces not answers but *formation*, the gradual shaping of a self that knows what it thinks and why.

The library runs a continuous program of reading groups, lecture series, philosophical discussions, and what the founders called "seminars of not-knowing"—structured conversations around questions that don't have answers, where the point is not to arrive at a conclusion but to practice the difficult art of sustained uncertainty. These are among the most attended events in the city.

## IV. The Question of Recognition

Here is the hardest problem, and the one the founders spent the most time on: *how do people know that what they do matters?*

The market solved this problem, badly. It told you your work mattered by paying you. This was bad because it made the mattering of your work hostage to what other people were willing to pay—which reflected not the genuine value of the work but a thousand contingencies of supply, demand, and social convention. Teachers mattered less than traders. Nurses mattered less than lawyers. The problem was not that markets were wrong about value in every case, but that they were wrong in systematic ways, and that people could feel this wrongness even when they couldn't articulate it.

Thessaly's solution is what its founders called the **Council of Recognition**—a set of institutions designed to make visible and honorable the full range of genuinely valuable human contributions, not just those that happen to be economically rewarded.

Every year, Thessaly holds a series of celebrations—not awards ceremonies in the corporate sense, but genuine public acknowledgments of excellence and contribution in every domain. The master glassblower who has spent thirty years perfecting a particular technique is celebrated alongside the scientist who made a breakthrough in materials research alongside the grandmother who has spent a decade running the neighborhood mediation program alongside the teenager who composed a piece of music that moved people alongside the civil servant who redesigned the city's water management system with such intelligence and care that it has become a model for other cities.

The celebrations are not competitive—there are no rankings, no first and second and third places. They are *recognitions*: public acts of noticing, of saying *this person did this thing, and it was genuinely excellent, and we see it and value it and are grateful for it*. The effect is powerful in ways that the founders initially underestimated. Humans are profoundly social creatures who need to be seen. When a society creates robust institutions for seeing people accurately—for recognizing the full range of ways that human beings contribute to collective life—it produces a very different kind of motivation than either market incentives or moral obligation.

## V. The Role of the Inquirers

Thessaly maintains an institution that its founders named, with conscious homage to Francis Bacon, the **House of Inquiry**—a research institution dedicated not to any particular domain of knowledge but to the general project of understanding: how the world works, how people work, what is true, and what matters.

The House of Inquiry is funded by the city and governed by its own members. It employs roughly three hundred full-time researchers at any given time, in fields ranging from physics to history to philosophy to ecology to medicine to the study of Thessaly itself. These researchers are selected not by competitive grant processes but by the judgment of their peers, using a system of deliberate evaluation that takes years and gives enormous weight to qualities that competitive grant processes systematically underweight: intellectual courage, genuine curiosity, willingness to work on problems that may not yield results for decades, and what one of the founding researchers called "the capacity for productive bewilderment."

The House of Inquiry is also responsible for what Thessaly calls *epistemic infrastructure*—the shared tools and practices that allow the city's citizens to think well together. This includes a system of civic education that teaches not facts but methods: how to evaluate evidence, how to reason about uncertainty, how to distinguish genuine disagreement from confusion about terms, how to update beliefs in response to evidence. This education begins in childhood and continues throughout life. The city's deliberative processes are notably more productive than those of comparable communities, and the founders attribute this largely to the investment in epistemic infrastructure.

The AI systems within the House of Inquiry are the most sophisticated in the city—and the most carefully governed. The researchers work with AI collaborators who can generate hypotheses, search vast literatures, run simulations, and identify patterns invisible to human eyes. But the questions they ask, the interpretations they offer, and the judgments they make about significance and value remain human. This is not sentimentality. It is the recognition that what the House of Inquiry is ultimately producing is not answers but *wisdom*—and wisdom is not a computational output. It is what happens when a mind that has lived in the world, that has suffered and loved and wondered and been wrong and tried again, encounters a difficult question and brings to it everything that mind has become.

## VI. The Objections

Any honest account of Thessaly must include the objections, because they are real and the founders have not pretended otherwise.

The first is the objection of **scale**. Thessaly has forty thousand people. What works at forty thousand may not work at four hundred thousand or four hundred million. The founders' answer is honest: they don't know. They believe that the principles are scalable even if the specific institutions aren't—that the underlying insight, that human flourishing requires structure, community, genuine challenge, and robust recognition, applies everywhere, even if the forms it takes must vary enormously with context and culture. They have been sharing what they've learned, actively and without intellectual property claims, with the dozens of other communities around the world that are attempting similar experiments.

The second is the objection of **exclusion**. Who gets to live in Thessaly? The city has a selective admissions process—not selective by wealth or credential, but selective by commitment. You must demonstrate genuine engagement with the city's values and genuine willingness to participate in its demands. This is not a small thing. Many people, for reasons of temperament, circumstance, or conviction, would find Thessaly suffocating. The city is not for everyone. Its founders are at peace with this—they are not building a universal template but a proof of concept, an existence proof that certain ways of living are possible.

The third is the objection of **meaning itself**. Who decides what is meaningful? The city's system of recognition, however carefully designed, still reflects particular values—craftsmanship, deliberation, care, inquiry—that not everyone shares. The founders' response to this is to point to the city's internal diversity: Thessaly contains people of radically different backgrounds, beliefs, and temperaments who have found, within the city's structure, paths to meaning that reflect their own natures rather than any imposed template. But they also acknowledge that the city embodies a view about human flourishing that is not value-neutral. Every institution embodies values. The question is whether those values are good ones, honestly held and honestly examined.

## VII. The Teenager and the Flower

You have been in Thessaly for a week now. On your last morning, you return to the train platform. The teenager is there again—or perhaps a different teenager; you're not certain. She is still arguing with her AI assistant about the flower in pot seven.

You ask her what she's figured out.

She looks up, irritated at the interruption, then relents. "Okay, so," she says. "The AI is pretty sure it's the cultivar. The leaf shape is a tell. But I think there's a chance the taxonomy is wrong and the original classification was based on a bad sample. So I requested a tissue sample from the city's botanical archive to check the genetic sequence." She pauses. "It's probably nothing. But it might be something."

"What would it mean if the taxonomy is wrong?" you ask.

She thinks about this seriously. "Probably not much, for this species. But the methodology that got it wrong might have gotten other things wrong too. And those other things might matter." She shrugs. "Anyway. It's interesting."

The train is coming. You pick up your bag. The teenager has already returned to her tablet, to her argument, to the patient, particular, demanding work of finding out whether something is true.

This, you think, is what the people who built this place figured out. Not that meaning is found in grand purpose, though grand purposes have their place. Not that meaning is found in leisure or comfort, though those have their place too. Meaning is found in the encounter between a person who genuinely cares and a question that genuinely resists—in the experience of bringing everything you are to something that demands you bring everything you are, and discovering that you are, in fact, enough, or discovering that you need to become more, and knowing how to try.

The AI can answer the question faster. The AI does not need the answer to mean anything.

That is the difference. That is the whole difference. That is what the city was built to protect and to cultivate and to honor—the peculiar human capacity to be moved by what is not yet understood, to care about what does not yet have an answer, to spend a morning arguing about a flower not because anyone asked you to but because the truth of the matter is, genuinely and inexplicably, *interesting to you.*

The train arrives. You board it. Behind you, Thessaly continues—imperfect, experimental, contested, and alive with people who have been given the great and difficult gift of being asked to figure out what, exactly, they are for, and the structures and communities and time to find an answer that is actually their own.

---

*The path from here to there is neither easy nor guaranteed. It requires that we stop treating meaning as a problem to be solved after the economic questions are settled, and start treating it as the prior question—the one the economic questions must serve. It requires that we build institutions that take seriously the full range of human flourishing, not just the parts that are economically legible. It requires, above all, that we maintain the difficult conviction that what humans do and make and care about and understand actually matters—that the universe is better for containing people who wonder about flowers, who master difficult crafts, who sit with elders, who argue in town halls about water policy, who compose music that moves other people, who ask questions that may not have answers for decades.*

*We have not yet built Thessaly. But we have, in fragments and experiments and imperfect prototypes scattered across the present moment, all the pieces we need. The work is coordination, and will, and the courage to say clearly what we are actually building toward.*

*We are building toward a world in which the question "what are you for?" has an answer that you found yourself, in a community that helped you look, with tools designed to extend rather than replace your capacity to seek.*

*That is enough. That is, in fact, everything.*
