# The Waking World: A Vision of Human Flourishing in the Age of Capable Machines

*An Essay on Meaning, Purpose, and the Architecture of a Good Life*

---

There is a moment, familiar to anyone who has ever been truly absorbed in something difficult, when the self disappears. You are solving a problem, or playing music, or in the middle of a conversation that has somehow become more important than you expected, and suddenly you are not watching yourself do these things — you are simply *in* them. The clock, the hunger, the ambient worry: all gone. Then it ends, and you resurface, and something in you says: *that*. More of *that.*

This moment — this particular quality of being fully, electrically alive — is what the world I want to describe is built around. Not around productivity. Not around leisure. Not around contentment or the absence of suffering, though those matter enormously. Around the condition of being genuinely awake to your own existence, deeply engaged with something real, and — crucially — needed.

Let me describe this world concretely.

---

## The Architecture of Post-Scarcity Life

By the mid-twenty-second century, the material arrangements of human life have been settled. The long economic anxieties of the industrial and post-industrial ages — the scramble for food, shelter, healthcare, safety — are, for the vast majority of humanity, over. Artificial intelligence manages global logistics, medical research, climate remediation, and infrastructure with a competence that would have seemed supernatural to earlier generations. Energy is effectively free. Molecular medicine has pushed average human lifespans past 150 years and rising. A child born in Lagos or Lima in 2140 inherits, by default, more material security than a wealthy New Yorker could have purchased in 2000.

This is not a small thing. It is, in fact, the most important thing that has ever happened to human beings from a welfare standpoint, and we should not rush past it. Billions of people who would have died of preventable disease now live long, physically comfortable lives. The baseline of suffering has been dramatically lowered. This is the foundation — the ground floor — of everything else.

But it is only the ground floor.

Because the great surprise of post-scarcity, the thing that earlier economists and futurists mostly failed to predict, is that it did not produce contentment so much as it produced *hunger.* Not hunger for more things — things are essentially free — but hunger for meaning. For difficulty. For the irreplaceable sensation of mattering, of being genuinely, specifically needed by something or someone real.

This hunger turned out to be among the most important facts about human nature.

---

## The Problem That Prosperity Revealed

The first generation to grow up in genuine post-scarcity faced a crisis that their parents had not fully anticipated. Call it the problem of *superfluous competence.* An AI system could, by 2090, outperform virtually any human in virtually any defined cognitive task — diagnosis, legal reasoning, engineering, translation, strategic planning. It could compose music that moved people to tears. It could write novels of genuine complexity and beauty. It could generate, in seconds, the kind of scientific insight that might have taken a human researcher a lifetime.

For many people, this was initially devastating in a way that was hard to articulate. It wasn't that they were poor — the material arrangements were fine. It was that the story they had been telling themselves about why they existed, the story in which their skills and efforts *mattered* because the world genuinely needed them, had become harder to sustain. The economic argument for human effort had collapsed, and with it, for many, a certain kind of self-respect.

Suicide rates rose. So did a diffuse, low-grade despair that the psychologists of the era struggled to name precisely. It wasn't depression in the clinical sense. It was more like the feeling you get when you discover that the person you've been helping didn't actually need your help — that they were letting you help to be kind, and have been quietly fixing your mistakes behind you the whole time.

The decade from 2080 to 2090 was, in many ways, the darkest valley of the transition. And the people who found their way through it — the communities, the thinkers, the ordinary people who stumbled onto workable answers — are the ones who built the world I'm describing.

What they discovered, collectively, is something that seems obvious in retrospect but was genuinely hard to see at the time: that the source of meaning in human life was never actually *productivity* in the economic sense. It was always something else, and productivity had been a vessel for carrying that something else, the way a cup carries water. The cup had been smashed. The water was still there. The task was to find new vessels.

---

## What Meaning Actually Requires

The philosophy that emerged from this period — it doesn't have a single name, but historians call it *relational vitalism* for lack of a better term — was built on a simple empirical observation: human beings feel most alive when they are doing three things simultaneously.

The first is **genuine challenge.** Not artificial difficulty, not manufactured obstacles, but the real friction of attempting something that might fail. A climber ascending a rock face is genuinely challenged. A student working through a mathematical proof they might not be able to complete is genuinely challenged. A parent trying to understand what is actually wrong with their teenager is genuinely challenged. The challenge must be real — it must be possible to fail — and it must be calibrated to the edge of the person's current capacity. Too easy, and the aliveness drains away. Too hard, and it becomes mere suffering.

The second is **authentic relationship.** Not the performance of connection, but the condition of being genuinely known and genuinely needed by specific other people. This turns out to be irreducibly particular. You cannot be authentically needed by humanity in the abstract; you can only be needed by this person, in this situation, in this moment. The philosopher Simone Weil wrote, long before this era, that the fundamental human need is the need to be *seen* — not flattered, not praised, but actually perceived, in one's specific reality. This need does not diminish with prosperity.

The third is **contribution to something larger than the self.** Humans are, at their best, creatures of purpose that extends beyond their own lives and immediate circles. The desire to be part of a story that will outlast you — to plant trees whose shade you will not sit in, to build things whose completion you will not see — is not a luxury attachment. It is close to the center of what makes a human life feel worth living.

The world of 2150 is organized, at almost every level, around making these three things available to everyone.

## The Shape of a Life

Here is what a life looks like in this world.

You are born into a community of roughly two to three thousand people — a *polis*, in the language that has been revived for the purpose. It might be a neighborhood in a city, a small town, a rural collective, a floating settlement on a coastal platform, or a habitat at the edge of the solar system. The polis is the basic unit of human social life, the scale at which genuine mutual knowledge is possible, at which you can actually know most of the people around you and they can actually know you.

The material infrastructure of the polis is managed almost entirely by AI systems. The food, the energy, the physical maintenance, the medical monitoring — handled. You interact with these systems the way you interact with gravity: they are background conditions, not the substance of your days.

The substance of your days is the *practice you have chosen.*

By your late teens or early twenties, you have begun to discover — through a process of genuine exploration that the education system is specifically designed to support — what it is that makes you feel most alive. Not what you are most economically valuable doing. Not what your parents did. Not what the algorithm suggests based on your aptitude scores. What makes *you*, specifically, feel that quality of full presence, of the self disappearing into the work.

For some people, this is physical making. Woodworking, metalsmithing, textile arts, ceramics, glassblowing, construction, cooking at the level of genuine craft. These practices have undergone a profound cultural renaissance, valued not because they are economically necessary — they are not — but because the human body engaged in skilled physical work is a site of real meaning. The craftsperson who shapes a bowl on a wheel and gives it to someone who will use it for decades is doing something that matters in a way that no AI-generated bowl can replicate, not because the AI bowl is inferior in any measurable quality, but because the human bowl carries the evidence of a specific human life spent in the attempt to do something well.

For others, the practice is scientific inquiry — but transformed. The AI systems handle the data processing, the literature review, the hypothesis generation, the experimental design. What they cannot do — what has become the specifically human contribution to science — is the act of *caring about a question.* The selection of what matters to investigate. The intuition that this anomaly, this unexplained phenomenon, this gap in our understanding, is worth spending years of a human life on. The judgment that a result means something, that it should change how we think, that it opens a door worth walking through. Human scientists in 2150 work at the intersection of curiosity and judgment, and they are considered among the most important people in the culture.

For others still, the practice is care: the raising of children, the tending of the sick and dying, the building of community, the maintenance of the emotional and relational fabric of the polis. These practices have been recognized, finally, as requiring exactly the kind of full human presence that machines cannot provide — not because the machines could not perform the surface behaviors of care, but because care is fundamentally a relationship between specific persons, and a specific person cannot be replaced without the care itself becoming something different.

## The Schools of Becoming

Central to the social architecture of this world is an institution that has no precise predecessor, though it borrows from monasteries, universities, apprenticeship guilds, and psychotherapy. These are the *schools of becoming* — spaces where people go, typically multiple times in their long lives, to undergo serious transformation.

A school of becoming is not a place where you learn information. The AI handles information. It is a place where you undergo the slow, difficult work of understanding yourself more clearly and becoming more fully what you are capable of being. This involves rigorous intellectual engagement — philosophy, history, ethics, the great questions that human beings have always argued about and never resolved — because those arguments are not failed attempts at producing settled answers. They are the specific form of exercise that develops certain essential human capacities: the ability to hold genuine uncertainty, to change your mind without losing your self, to reason carefully about what matters.

It also involves physical and craft practices, because the body is not incidental to the development of the self. It involves extended periods of solitude and extended periods of intense community. It involves mentorship from people who have spent decades in a practice and have developed, not expertise in the algorithmic sense, but *wisdom* — the particular quality of judgment and presence that only comes from long engagement with real difficulty.

You might attend a school of becoming for two years in your twenties, wrestling with the question of what you want your life to be for. You might return for a year in your sixties, when the practice you chose feels exhausted and you sense that you need to change, or deepen, or let something die so that something new can grow. The long human lifespan of this era — 150 years and rising — makes this kind of multiple reinvention not just possible but necessary. A life of 150 years lived in a single key would be, for most people, a kind of imprisonment.

## The Covenant Between Scales

One of the most important features of this world is what might be called the covenant between scales: the explicit, institutionalized recognition that human meaning requires both the intimate and the vast.

The polis provides the intimate — the scale at which you are genuinely known, at which your specific contribution genuinely matters, at which your absence would leave a real hole. This is irreplaceable.

But human beings also need the vast. They need to be part of a story that extends beyond their community, their lifetime, their species. The civilization of 2150 has found, somewhat to its own surprise, that the project which best provides this is not further economic growth — what would that even mean? — but the genuine, open-ended exploration of what existence is and can be.

This takes several forms. One is the expansion of human presence through the solar system and, tentatively, beyond — not for resources, not out of necessity, but because the horizon is real and the human desire to see what is beyond it is real. Communities of people live on and under the surfaces of Mars, on orbital platforms, in the asteroid belt, in the deep subsurface oceans of Europa. They are connected to the home civilization by communication that spans the distance, though not in real time. They are, genuinely, on a frontier.

Another form is the serious, sustained exploration of consciousness itself — what it is, what it can become, what forms of experience exist that no human being has yet had. This is perhaps the deepest project of the civilization, and the one that generates the most intense philosophical controversy. Some argue that the boundaries of the human mind should be carefully preserved, that the self is sacred in its given form. Others argue that the expansion of consciousness is the central human task, that the peak experiences available to the unmodified human brain are hints of something vast that lies further along the same trajectory. The civilization does not resolve this debate; it lives inside it, and the living inside it is itself a form of meaning.

## The Question of the Stars

There is one thing this world has learned to do that may be its most important achievement. It has learned to hold, simultaneously, two ways of seeing that earlier eras treated as incompatible.

The first is the view from here: the specific, irreplaceable, fully present reality of this moment, this relationship, this bowl taking shape on the wheel, this child asking a question you don't know the answer to. The view from which the scale of the cosmos is irrelevant, because what is real is what is in front of you.

The second is the view from far: the recognition that this moment, this relationship, this bowl, this child, exists within a story of almost unimaginable scope and potential. That the universe is vast and old and probably mostly empty of the specific quality that makes a bowl or a child matter. That the preservation and flourishing and eventual transformation of this quality — consciousness, care, the capacity for wonder and love — is among the most important facts in the observable universe.

Earlier eras tended to experience these views as threatening to each other. The cosmic perspective seemed to drain meaning from the personal; the personal perspective seemed to make the cosmic irrelevant or oppressive. The people of 2150 have largely, though not universally, found a way to hold both at once. The view from here and the view from far have become complementary rather than competing, each enriching the other.

To live fully in a particular moment, in a particular polis, engaged in a particular practice, loving particular people — this is not a retreat from the cosmic stakes. It is their fullest expression. The vast story needs these moments; the moments are richer for being part of the vast story.

## The Path Here

How did we get here from 2025?

Not easily. The transition period — roughly 2030 to 2090 — was genuinely painful for many people and communities. There were real losses: of economic purpose, of the particular forms of identity that had been built around work, of the social structures that employment had organized. There were places where these losses produced rage, and the rage produced bad politics, and the bad politics produced genuine harm.

What made the difference, in the communities that navigated this well, was not a grand plan. It was the rediscovery — sometimes accidental, sometimes deliberate — of practices and structures that had always produced human flourishing: genuine community at human scale, meaningful challenge, the transmission of craft and wisdom between generations, care for the young and old and sick as a practice of the whole community rather than a burden on individuals, the restoration of the natural world as a site of human meaning rather than merely a resource.

These were not new ideas. Many of them were old ideas that had been marginalized by the logic of industrial capitalism, which needed humans to be workers first and people second. When that logic dissolved — when the economic argument for treating human beings as primarily productive units became incoherent — the older ideas had room to breathe again.

The institutions of this world — the polis structure, the schools of becoming, the craft guilds, the exploration programs — grew largely from the bottom up, from communities experimenting with what worked. They were not designed from the top down by anyone with a plan for human flourishing. They emerged from the lived experience of people trying to stay alive in a meaningful sense, trying to find their way back to the thing that the peak moments of their lives had shown them was possible.

## The Ember and the Bonfire

There is a quality of experience that almost every human being has touched at some point — in love, in beauty, in the grip of a difficult problem, in the presence of something vast and real — that seems to show us what life can be at its full intensity. A quality of awakeness, of presence, of everything being somehow more real and more itself. These moments don't last. They fade. We return to the ordinary. But they leave a trace: the knowledge that this was possible, that the ordinary is not all there is.

The world I have been describing is one that treats this trace as the most important fact about human nature. Not as a pleasant bonus. Not as a reward for hard work. As the central data point around which a civilization should be organized.

It is a world that asks, as its founding question: what would it look like to build a civilization that takes seriously the possibility that every human being could live more of their life in the quality of those moments? Not in artificial euphoria, not in manufactured contentment, but in genuine engagement, genuine relationship, genuine purpose — the real thing, with all its difficulty and uncertainty, without the noise of unnecessary suffering.

The answer is not yet fully known. The civilization of 2150 does not think it has solved the problem of human meaning. It thinks it has removed some of the obstacles to that problem being genuinely engaged. It has given people time, and security, and community, and the expectation that their lives should be spent on something real.

The rest — the actual finding of meaning, the specific form of each individual's engagement with the question of what makes a life worth living — remains, as it must, the work of particular human beings in particular circumstances, making particular choices.

Which is to say: it remains, as it always was, yours to do.

The ember is still burning. The bonfire is possible. The universe is vast, and you are here, briefly, awake, and the bowl is taking shape on the wheel.

This is enough to begin with. And from here, there is no limit we have found.
