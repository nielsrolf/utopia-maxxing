# The Depth of Days

## On Finding Meaning When Machines Can Do Almost Everything

---

There is a particular kind of afternoon that does not get enough attention in philosophical literature. You know the kind: you have been working on something difficult — a piece of furniture, a proof, a conversation that keeps not quite happening the way you need it to — and somewhere in the middle of the third or fourth hour, without noticing the transition, you have stopped watching yourself work and started simply *working.* The self-consciousness that is the low-grade background radiation of modern life has gone quiet. You are fully inside the thing.

Then it ends. You surface. And for a moment, before the ordinary noise rushes back in, you catch a glimpse of something: not contentment exactly, not happiness in the shallow sense, but a quality of aliveness so different from the usual state that it seems to belong to a different category of experience entirely. As if for a few hours you were operating at a different voltage.

This moment — this specific quality of experience — is where I want to begin. Because I think it contains the answer to what is, by some measure, the most important question facing our civilization:

*In a world where intelligent machines can do most of what humans have historically done, what are humans* for*?*

The question sounds existential, and it is. But it also sounds new, and it is not. Humans have always had to answer it. We answered it when we moved from hunter-gatherers to farmers and the old skills became unnecessary. We answered it when the printing press made the memorization of texts irrelevant. We answered it — imperfectly, painfully, incompletely — when the industrial revolution turned craftspeople into machine-tenders, and then when automation turned machine-tenders into something else, something we are still in the process of naming.

What is new is not the question. What is new is the scale and speed of the current transformation, and the particular nature of what is being displaced: not just physical labor, not just routine cognition, but the full range of human cognitive capability in almost every domain. The new machines do not just do our lifting. They diagnose, compose, reason, translate, design, plan, and increasingly, create. The economic argument for human cognitive effort — the story that your mind is valuable because the world needs what it produces — is in the process of dissolving.

And yet.

The quality of experience in that afternoon with the difficult work has nothing to do with economic value. It was never about economic value. It was about something else entirely, something the economic frame was always a crude container for. The container is cracking. The question is what we discover inside.

---

## What the Transition Actually Looks Like

Let me be honest about how we get from here to there, because the honest version matters.

The transition — roughly 2030 to 2075 — is not a clean story. It does not have a clear beginning, a middle of accumulated wisdom, and a triumphant resolution. It has the shape of actual history: jagged, uneven, full of genuine losses that no retrospective account should aestheticize away.

There are communities built around industries that simply end. The particular form of pride and identity and social structure that grew around those industries ends with them, and the endings are real losses for real people who did not choose them. There are political responses to this disruption that range from generous to callous to actively authoritarian. There are years when the distribution of AI's enormous productive surplus becomes the central political conflict of the era, and the conflict does not resolve cleanly or justly or quickly.

There are also years — specifically, in many accounts, the late 2060s — that psychologists and historians will later describe as the "fog years": a period when unprecedented material security has been achieved, when the old catastrophic causes of human suffering have been dramatically reduced, when it is genuinely true that fewer people are dying of preventable disease than at any point in human history, and when a significant fraction of the population is nonetheless experiencing a diffuse, hard-to-name despair. Not clinical depression, exactly. More like the feeling of having arrived somewhere you worked very hard to reach, only to discover that arrival was not the point.

The fog years matter because they constitute the moment of genuine confrontation. The material problem has been largely solved. The software of economic security is running. And yet the something that was promised — the flourishing, the good life — has not automatically appeared.

What emerges from the fog, slowly, through the stumbling efforts of communities and thinkers and ordinary people trying to find their way back to the quality of those full, alive afternoons, is something we might call the great rediscovery. Not of new knowledge, but of old knowledge that the logic of industrial capitalism had pushed to the margins: knowledge about what human beings have always actually needed in order to feel that their lives are worth living.

What they always needed was never productivity.

What they needed was *depth.*

## The Three Depths

The philosophy that coalesces during the fog years — not in any single text or school but in the lived experiments of hundreds of communities across dozens of cultures — can be organized around three related observations about what the full-voltage afternoons have in common.

**The first depth is the depth of genuine engagement:** the state in which you are fully inside something difficult enough to require all of you, but not so far beyond your capacity that effort becomes mere suffering. The climber on the rock face. The novelist at the sentence she has been rewriting for two hours. The parent trying to understand what is actually happening inside a child who has gone quiet in a new way. What these have in common is not their content but their structure: they demand the full person. They cannot be phoned in. They cannot be performed from a safe distance. You have to actually be there, actually trying, with the genuine possibility of failure.

The deepest and most important feature of this state is that it cannot be faked. You know when you are in it and when you are not. No one else needs to certify it. No algorithm can generate it for you. It is one of the most democracy-resistant experiences available to human beings: equally available, in principle, to the person rebuilding a carburetor and the person writing a symphony, to the grandmother learning to draw at eighty and the child learning to read at seven. What it requires is not talent or status or money. It requires a task at the edge of your current capacity, and the freedom to attempt it without the crushing weight of economic necessity deforming the attempt.

**The second depth is the depth of genuine relationship:** the condition of being actually, specifically, irreplaceably known by another person or set of people. Not liked, not admired, not approved of — known. This is harder than it sounds and rarer than we pretend. It requires time, and vulnerability, and the specific kind of attention that cannot be multitasked. It also requires — and this is the part that takes longest to understand — being genuinely needed. Not needed in the abstract, not needed by humanity at large, but needed by this specific person in this specific situation in this specific way that only you can fill.

AI systems, in the world of 2150, can perform most cognitive labor better than humans. They can do this with something that looks like care. What they cannot do — not for any fundamental technical reason, but for a reason that lies deeper in the nature of human experience — is provide the particular quality of meaning that comes from being genuinely needed by a mortal, specific, irreplaceable person who has chosen you. When the man who knows everything about restoring vintage engines teaches a seventeen-year-old who has never held a wrench, something happens between them that is not replicable by any AI tutor, however patient and precise. It happens because the man is mortal, because he learned this in a particular way from a particular person, because he does not have to be there and has chosen to be, because the seventeen-year-old can see in him the shape of a life lived in a particular direction and is deciding, watching him, something about her own direction.

**The third depth is the depth of contribution to something larger than the self:** the need to be part of a story that extends beyond your own life, to plant trees whose shade you will not sit in, to add something to an ongoing human project that will continue without you. This need is not a luxury attachment. It is close to the center of why humans have always built monuments, told stories, and cared about what happens after they die. It is the need to matter not just to the people in the room but to the ongoing human project of figuring out what existence is and can be.

These three depths — genuine engagement, genuine relationship, genuine contribution — are what the full-voltage afternoons contain. They were always what human beings were reaching for when they reached for work, for community, for religion, for art. The economic frame captured them imperfectly, clumsily, for a few centuries. When the frame breaks, the three depths remain. The task of the civilization I am about to describe is to build social structures that make them genuinely accessible to everyone.

## The World of 2150: A Life in Concrete Detail

Here is what a life looks like.

You are born into a *polis* — the word has been revived, with some modification, to describe the basic unit of human social life in this era. A polis is between one and four thousand people. It might be a neighborhood within a larger city, a small town with a long history, a floating platform in the Pacific, a community carved into a cliff face in New Mexico, a pressurized habitat on the lunar surface where your grandparents won a settlement lottery and your parents grew up looking at a black sky. The specific form varies enormously. The essential function does not: the polis is the scale at which you can genuinely know most of the people around you and be genuinely known by them. It is the scale at which your absence would leave a real and noticeable hole.

The material infrastructure of the polis is managed almost entirely by AI systems that would have seemed, to your great-great-grandparents, indistinguishable from magic. Food, energy, medical monitoring, physical maintenance, logistics, climate management — handled, with a reliability and efficiency that has been refined over eighty years of accumulated experience. You interact with these systems the way you interact with gravity: they are background conditions, not the substance of your days.

Your days have a different substance.

In your childhood, you spend an unusual amount of time — by the standards of the twentieth century, a shocking amount — simply playing. Not structured play, not educational play, but the sprawling, ungoverned, often boring-looking-from-the-outside play in which children have always done the most important developmental work: learning to negotiate reality, to sustain imaginative engagement, to tolerate frustration, to build and rebuild social structures among themselves. The adults in your polis understand that this is not wasted time. They protect it carefully.

Alongside this, you encounter what the culture calls *practices.* A practice, in the vocabulary of this era, is not a hobby and not a job. It is a domain of genuine difficulty that you choose to engage with seriously over time: woodworking, mathematics, ecological restoration, pottery, the history of a particular people or place, musical composition, the care of the very old, the cultivation of particular plants, the study of bird behavior, the development of physical capabilities through movement arts. A practice has the structure of the full-voltage afternoon: it has a real frontier, a place where you do not yet know how to do the thing, and the engagement with that frontier is the point.

The education system of your childhood does not prioritize the transmission of information. AI handles information. It prioritizes the development of *practice readiness*: the capacity to tolerate genuine difficulty without giving up, the ability to distinguish real progress from the appearance of progress, the deep familiarity with the specific quality of being lost inside a problem that is the necessary precondition for finding your way out of it. By the time you are fifteen, you have the experience of having gotten genuinely good at something, having started from not being good at it. This experience turns out to be among the most important things education can provide.

By your late teens, guided by mentors and by your own increasingly reliable sense of what makes you feel most alive, you begin to assemble your *arrangement* — the ongoing, evolving structure of practices, relationships, and contributions that gives your life its particular shape. No two arrangements are alike. The culture has entirely released the idea that arrangements should look similar, or that similarity is a virtue.

Yours might center on a physical craft: you spend three or four mornings a week in the community workshop, working on the furniture project that has been evolving for years, that involves not just joinery and finishing but the development of a design vocabulary that is recognizably yours, that incorporates references to the traditional forms of your grandmother's culture alongside materials found within walking distance of the polis. You are not making furniture because the polis needs furniture — it can synthesize furniture of perfectly adequate quality. You are making furniture because the engagement of your hands and eyes and accumulated judgment with wood and form produces something in you that you have come to understand as irreplaceable. And because the chairs and tables and shelves that you make, imperfect and specific, will be in the homes of people you know, will be touched and used by them and their children, carry something of you into the world in a form that persists.

Your arrangement might also involve teaching. Not formal schooling — the AI systems handle much of the information transmission — but the specific kind of teaching that happens between a person who has spent years developing genuine mastery and a person who is just beginning. You meet weekly with a small group of teenagers who have expressed interest in woodworking. What happens in those sessions is not primarily the transmission of technique, though technique is transmitted. What happens is that the teenagers watch someone who has made a commitment to something and lived inside it long enough for the commitment to have changed them, and they are deciding, watching, something about their own lives. This is not a small thing. It is, arguably, the oldest and most important educational transaction humans have ever developed.

Your arrangement will change. In your thirties, something happens — a period of creative exhaustion, or a relationship that transforms your sense of what matters, or a discovery about the history of the place you live that opens into something much larger — and the arrangement shifts. The craft may deepen or be set aside for a time. A new practice may enter. What the culture has learned to support is exactly this: not the maintenance of a fixed arrangement but the ongoing process of revision in response to real experience.

## The Schools of Becoming

Every person, in this world, attends what have come to be called *schools of becoming* — institutions that have no precise predecessor, though they borrow from monasteries, from psychotherapy, from apprenticeship guilds, from the philosophical schools of antiquity.

A school of becoming is not where you learn information. It is where you undergo the slow, difficult, non-linear process of understanding yourself more clearly and becoming more capable of living in the three depths.

You might attend for the first time in your early twenties, in the period of genuine confusion and openness that follows the structured freedom of childhood. You spend two years in serious engagement with the great human questions — not to resolve them, which cannot be done, but to inhabit them deeply enough that your relationship to uncertainty is transformed. You read the Stoics and the Buddhists and the existentialists and the people who wrote in complete disagreement with all of them. You argue, late into evenings, about what a good life requires. You study the history of how different civilizations have organized meaning, and what worked and what failed and why. You do all of this alongside people from radically different polis cultures and backgrounds, which turns out to be essential: you cannot understand your own assumptions about what matters until you encounter someone whose assumptions are entirely different and equally serious.

You also work, physically, during your time in the school. You tend the gardens. You help with construction. You do the unglamorous maintenance that keeps the institution functioning. This is not punitive; it is pedagogical. The experience of doing necessary physical work in the company of people who are simultaneously engaged in serious intellectual inquiry turns out to produce something that neither component produces on its own.

You leave the school not with answers but with a better relationship to the questions, and with a clearer sense of what your particular self is drawn toward and capable of.

You return, likely several times in your long life. The school of becoming you attend at sixty, when the first arrangement of your adult years is exhausting itself and something is calling to change, is a different institution than the one you attended at twenty-two — different physically, different in emphasis, different in the specific practices it employs — but recognizably part of the same tradition. You come to it at sixty with the enormous advantage of actual experience: you know what genuine engagement feels like in your own life, you have the evidence of decades of practice, you have the specific losses and discoveries of a life actually lived. What the school offers you at sixty is the structure within which to take all of that seriously, to ask what it means, to figure out what should come next.

## The Monument You Leave Inside a Person

There is a concept that has become central to the ethical vocabulary of this era, for which no single clean word existed before. It is sometimes called the *interior monument* — the mark you leave in the inner life of another person.

Physical monuments still exist and are still built, some of them extraordinary in their ambition and beauty. But the culture has developed an increasingly sophisticated recognition that the interior monuments — the ones that exist in the minds of people you have taught, or loved carefully, or sat with in their grief, or told a true story to at exactly the right moment — are not a lesser category of monument. They are, in many ways, the primary form of human legacy.

This recognition has practical consequences. The work of making interior monuments — teaching, tending, witnessing, mentoring, the long slow work of deep friendship and deep care — is no longer squeezed into the margins of a life structured primarily around economic productivity. It is central. It is understood as one of the most important things a human being can do, and the social structures of the polis are organized around making it possible.

The woman who has spent four years studying the acoustics of medieval stone cathedrals — not because anyone needs her to, not because it extends her life, but because she heard something strange in a recording when she was twenty-two and has never stopped wondering what it was — is not a curiosity or an eccentric. She is recognized as someone living in the full-voltage state, someone whose engagement with a genuine question makes her, as a person, more present and more interesting to be around. When she sits down with the small group of people who have gathered around her particular obsession and tells them what she has found — the way certain frequencies interact with certain stone geometries, the relationship between the physical space and the experience of the sacred that its builders were reaching toward — something passes between them that no AI has generated and no algorithm has optimized.

That something is the interior monument. She is leaving it in them. They will carry it into the rest of their lives, this particular quality of serious attention to a question that does not need to be answered to be worth living inside.

This is what humans are for. Not for producing things, or even for experiencing things, but for this transaction: the passing of a specific kind of serious, mortal, fully-present attention from one person to another, in a form that the receiver then carries and transforms and eventually passes on.

## The Vast Project

And yet — and this is crucial — the polis and the practice and the interior monument are not enough. Human beings need the intimate *and* the vast. They need to be part of a story that extends beyond their community and their lifetime and even their species.

The civilization of 2150 has found, somewhat to its own surprise, that the project which best provides this vastness is not further economic growth — what would that even mean? — but the genuine, open-ended investigation of what existence is and can be.

This takes several forms.

One is the expansion of human presence through the solar system — not for resources, not out of necessity, but because the horizon is real and the human desire to see what is beyond it is real. Communities of people live on and under the surface of Mars, in pressurized habitats amid the asteroids, in the ice-covered subsurface ocean of Europa where something that might be life was discovered in 2089 and has been the subject of the most extraordinary scientific and philosophical attention ever since. These communities are connected to the home civilization by communication that spans the distance, though not in real time. They are, genuinely, on a frontier — and the specific quality of human experience on a frontier, the combination of dependency and discovery and the particular kind of community that forms when people are genuinely in something together, is valued not because it is economically productive but because it is humanly generative.

Another form is the serious, sustained exploration of consciousness itself — what it is, what it can become, what forms of experience lie further along the trajectory that ends, for an unmodified human, in what the mystics of every tradition have called peak experience. This project generates the most intense philosophical controversy in the culture. Some argue that the given human mind is sacred in its form, that enhancement that changes the self dissolves the self. Others argue that the expansion of consciousness is the central human project, that the altered states available to the unmodified brain are hints pointing toward something vast that lies further along. The civilization does not resolve this debate. It lives inside it. And the living inside it — the serious, informed, multi-generational argument about what the human self is and what it can become — is itself a form of meaning.

A third form, perhaps the deepest, is simply the ongoing human argument about what a good life is. This argument has never been settled. It will never be settled. Every generation takes it up again in the specific form its circumstances require. The generation of 2150 takes it up in the specific form that a post-scarcity, post-economic-necessity civilization requires — with the unique urgency of people who have finally, after millennia of poverty and necessity and distraction, been given the time and safety to actually answer the question.

They are discovering that the question, answered seriously, generates more questions. That this is not a failure. That the question behind the question — *what are we doing here, what is this for, what should we reach toward* — is one of those questions that deepens as you approach it, that reveals more of its complexity and richness and irreducible difficulty the more carefully you look.

This discovery, too, is a kind of monument. Not an interior monument left in a single person, but a cultural monument left in the ongoing human project of figuring out what it means to be here at all.

## The Path From Here

The path from 2025 to 2150 runs through choices that we — the people reading this, in this decade — will make or fail to make.

The technology does not make the choices. The technology only expands what is possible, which is not the same thing at all.

The path runs through whether we design AI systems that genuinely serve human flourishing, or merely simulate it — a distinction that is harder to maintain than it sounds and requires constant vigilance. It runs through whether we build political infrastructure to distribute the enormous productive surplus broadly, or allow it to concentrate in ways that make the material security and the freedom it purchases available only to some. It runs through whether we take seriously, as a civilization, the question of meaning — whether we recognize that material comfort without depth is not flourishing but a more comfortable form of the old emptiness.

And it runs through something harder to institutionalize: the willingness of individual human beings, in their individual lives, to take the full-voltage afternoons seriously. To notice them. To ask what they contain. To build a life oriented toward more of them, even when the culture's default structures make this difficult, even when it requires resistance to the still-present remnants of the logic that valued humans primarily as producers.

The man in the factory, standing at his workstation with his arms crossed, knowing that this motion is who he is — his question is real. What do we do with a world where the motion is no longer needed?

We find out what was always inside the motion.

We find the engagement with difficulty, the pride in genuine skill, the relationship with the people who depended on the thing being done well, the contribution to a larger project, the specific quality of a life spent in serious attention to something real.

None of that needed the motion to be economically valuable. It only needed the motion to be genuine.

Genuine is still possible. More possible, in some ways, than it has ever been. Because the constraint of economic necessity — the requirement that your engagement with the world produce something that someone will pay for — is, for the first time in human history, in the process of being lifted.

What is left, when that weight is removed, is not emptiness.

What is left is the question.

And the question, taken seriously, in community, over the long arc of a human life — is more than enough.

It is, in fact, everything.

---

*The ember is still burning. The bonfire is possible. The afternoon is long, and the work is real, and the stone is still resonating with something you heard sixty years ago and have never stopped trying to understand.*

*This is the inheritance we are building toward. This is what we owe each other.*

*Begin.*
