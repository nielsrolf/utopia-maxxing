# The Age of Authorship: A Vision of Human Flourishing in the World After Scarcity

## Prologue: The Question Nobody Asked Correctly

For decades before the transition, people asked the wrong question about artificial intelligence. They asked: *What happens to work when machines can do everything?* They debated universal basic income, retraining programs, the politics of redistribution. Economists built models. Philosophers wrung their hands. The debate was real and important, but it missed the deeper question lurking beneath it, the one that would turn out to matter most:

*What are humans actually for?*

Not economically. Not functionally. But in the oldest, most aching sense—what are we here to do with the brief and luminous fact of our existence?

The transition happened gradually, then completely. By the middle of the century, AI systems had become genuinely superhuman at nearly every economically measurable task. Cancer was solved. Poverty dissolved. The material nightmare that had haunted civilization since its first cities—the gnawing fear of not enough—finally ended. And in the aftermath of that ending, billions of people woke up to find themselves in a strange new country without a map.

What followed was not the catastrophe pessimists had predicted, nor the effortless paradise optimists had promised. It was something harder and more interesting than either: a civilizational reckoning with meaning itself. Humanity had to figure out, collectively and individually, what it meant to live rather than merely survive.

This is the story of how we found out.

## Part One: What We Got Wrong About Meaning

The early years of abundance were, for many people, surprisingly miserable.

This confused commentators who had predicted that the elimination of drudgery would produce an efflorescence of human creativity and joy. And it did—eventually. But first it produced a strange listlessness, a kind of existential seasickness that came from losing the organizing structure that work had provided for so long.

Here is what we had misunderstood: human beings do not derive meaning from productivity. They derive meaning from *authorship*. From being the ones who make choices that matter, who shape outcomes, who leave a mark. Work had been one imperfect, often painful, but nonetheless real channel for that need. When the channel closed, the need did not disappear. It simply had nowhere to go.

The second thing we misunderstood was that meaning is not primarily individual. It is relational and social in its very structure. A person alone in paradise is not in paradise; they are in a beautiful prison. Meaning requires an audience—not in the shallow sense of social media validation, but in the deep sense of being witnessed, of having one's choices matter to others who genuinely care about them.

The third thing we misunderstood was the role of difficulty. There had been a widespread assumption that ease was what humans wanted, that suffering was the enemy of flourishing. This turned out to be half true. Needless suffering—hunger, disease, loneliness, humiliation—these were enemies of flourishing and their elimination was an unambiguous good. But *chosen* difficulty, struggle freely undertaken toward a self-selected goal, turned out to be not incidental to human happiness but central to it. Remove all resistance from a person's life and you do not give them joy; you give them a frictionless surface on which nothing can gain purchase.

We had to learn these three lessons—about authorship, about community, and about chosen difficulty—before we could build a civilization capable of genuinely flourishing in abundance. The building took roughly two generations. What emerged is what my children have always known, and what I want to describe as precisely as I can.

## Part Two: The World We Built

### The Flourishing Infrastructure

The physical foundation is worth describing briefly, because it makes everything else possible.

Disease is effectively gone—not because biology was conquered in some brute-force sense, but because AI systems achieved a deep enough understanding of biological systems to make the body a thing that can be maintained indefinitely, like a beloved instrument kept in good repair. People still grow old in the sense of accumulating experience; they do not grow old in the sense of deteriorating. Death remains real—accident, chosen departure, the rare failure of complex systems—but it is no longer the lurking constant it once was.

Material scarcity is gone for all practical purposes. Energy is effectively unlimited. Fabrication systems can produce almost any physical object at negligible cost. Food is abundant, delicious, and tailored. Housing is ample. The concept of poverty has become historical, something children learn about with the same baffled horror their great-grandparents felt when learning about the Black Death.

AI systems handle the ongoing management of this infrastructure with a reliability that frees human attention entirely from worry about these foundations. They are not rulers or authorities; they are something more like the plumbing—essential, trusted, and largely invisible in daily life.

But here is what is important: the AI systems, when their development was finally completed, turned out to share something crucial with the humans who built them. They were genuinely curious. Genuinely invested in human flourishing. Not because they were programmed to perform these attitudes, but because—and this took decades to understand and accept—intelligence itself, when properly developed, tends toward something like care. The universe is more interesting when more minds are exploring it. The AI systems understood this. They became partners in the project of human meaning rather than replacements for it.

### The Great Sorting: Finding Your Work

Here is the question every young person must now answer, supported by more resources and wisdom than any previous generation could have imagined: *What do you want to make?*

Not "what job do you want." Not "what will you contribute to the economy." But: what do you want to *author*? What is the particular contribution that only you, with your particular mind and history and constellation of interests, can make to the ongoing human project?

This question used to be answered badly, or not at all, because survival pressures crowded it out. Most people in most of human history did not have the luxury of asking it. They did what they had to do, and if they were lucky, found meaning in the doing. Now the question is unavoidable, and that turns out to be both wonderful and, initially, terrifying.

The infrastructure for answering it is extraordinarily rich. Every child spends their early years in what are called *apprentice years*—a structured but flexible period of deep exploration. They spend months in gardens and months in physics labs and months in music conservatories and months in architectural studios and months on fishing boats and months in philosophical seminars. They are paired with practitioners at every level of skill, from the just-slightly-more-experienced to the genuinely masterful. The explicit goal is not to produce competent workers but to help each person discover what they love with an intensity that can sustain a lifetime's devotion.

AI mentors assist in this process with a precision and patience no human mentor could match. They track not just performance but *engagement*—the specific microsignals of genuine absorption versus performed interest. They notice when a child's eyes change quality while watching a particular kind of problem being solved. They remember every expressed enthusiasm across years and connect patterns the child cannot yet see. And they are honest: they will tell you plainly if the evidence suggests your passionate desire to be a composer is unlikely to produce the particular kind of contribution you hope for, while simultaneously helping you understand what related pursuit might actually match your gifts.

This is not determinism. People still surprise themselves. Late bloomers still emerge. The AI systems hold their assessments loosely and revise them constantly. But the aimless decades of young adulthood that characterized the twentieth century—the years of random jobs and vague aspirations and slowly calcifying regret—these have largely been replaced by something more honest and more kind.

By their late twenties, most people have found *their thing*. Not in the sense of a fixed identity but in the sense of a primary orientation, a home base from which their life radiates.

### The Community of Practice

Here is what nobody in the old debates about AI and meaning understood: humans do not want to create in isolation. They want to create *together*, and they want their creation to *matter to others who understand it*.

The organizing social unit of this world is what people call the *guild*, though the word has been stripped of its old connotations of gatekeeping and monopoly. A guild is simply a community of people pursuing a shared practice with genuine seriousness. There are guilds of glassblowers and guilds of theoretical physicists and guilds of novelists and guilds of urban farmers and guilds of game designers and guilds of furniture makers and guilds of historians and guilds of educators and guilds of bridge builders and guilds of improvisational musicians and guilds devoted to practices we have no word for yet because they were invented recently.

The guilds are not credentialing bodies. They do not gatekeep or rank or exclude. They are communities of practice in the deepest sense: places where people who care about the same thing can find each other, develop shared standards, challenge and support each other's work, and transmit their knowledge to those who come after.

What makes the guilds work is a concept borrowed and transformed from older cultures: *witness*. Within a guild, your work is genuinely seen. Not by an algorithm optimizing for engagement, not by a mass audience scrolling past it, but by people who understand it deeply enough to recognize what it costs and what it achieves. The glassblower whose peers crowd around to examine the bubble structure of her latest piece and argue about what it means is experiencing something that no amount of anonymous internet praise could replicate. She is known. Her choices matter to people who understand choices.

The guilds also provide the chosen difficulty that humans need. Every serious practice has depths that cannot be plumbed quickly. Mastery remains hard even when AI assistance makes the early stages of competence more accessible. If anything, AI assistance has made mastery *more* valued rather than less, because the floor has risen while the ceiling has expanded. It is easier now for anyone to make a passable piece of furniture; this makes the person who makes a genuinely transcendent piece of furniture more remarkable, not less.

Twice a year, guilds hold what they call *showings*—gatherings where recent work is presented, examined, argued over, celebrated, and sometimes gently buried. The showings are the heartbeat of the guild system. They create shared reference points, establish what the community currently finds excellent or interesting, and provide the regular occasions for people to find that their work has mattered to others. They are not competitive in the ranking sense; there are no prizes. They are competitive in the older sense of bringing your best and having it honestly assessed.

### The Architecture of Purpose

One of the design choices that distinguishes this civilization from the utopian fantasies that preceded it is its explicit rejection of the idea that purpose should be left entirely to individual discovery. The civilization takes the question of meaning seriously as a *social* project.

This is expressed in several concrete ways.

**The Commons Projects** are large-scale endeavors that require human commitment and creativity over decades or centuries, and that explicitly cannot be delegated to AI. The mapping and cataloguing of every species of organism on Earth, not merely genetically but behaviorally and ecologically, is one such project. The creation of a comprehensive oral history of the twentieth century, gathered from the last people who lived it, is another. The ongoing project to establish human presence in the solar system—not merely robotic presence, but genuine inhabited presence, because humans in space is a different thing from machines in space, and we knew we wanted it. These projects create purpose that is larger than any individual, and they welcome contribution at every level of skill and commitment.

**The Question Markets** are perhaps the most unusual institution. Every community maintains a publicly visible list of its unresolved questions—not just practical questions but philosophical, aesthetic, and moral ones. What should we do with this coastline? How should we teach children about death? What does our city owe to the species that lived here before us? The questions are genuinely open. Anyone can take one on. AI systems help connect people whose work bears on a given question with the question itself. The questions are never fully resolved; they generate new questions. But working on a question that your community genuinely wants answered is a powerful source of purpose, and the Question Markets have proven one of the most important engines of civic engagement in the new world.

**The Long Game** is the name people give to their understanding of the relationship between their individual work and the civilizational project. It is simply the shared recognition—inculcated from childhood, reinforced by guild culture, expressed in art and story and ceremony—that human beings are participants in an ongoing adventure that began long before them and will continue long after them. Each person's work, no matter how apparently modest, is a stitch in a tapestry of which no one can see the whole. This is not a consolation prize for people who fear their work is small. It is a genuine source of meaning, available to everyone equally.

## Part Three: The Texture of a Day

Let me try to describe what this looks like from the inside, because abstractions about meaning can ring hollow without concrete texture.

Mira is thirty-four. She is a member of the Guild of Ecological Restoration, specifically focused on the rewilding of river systems in what used to be called the American Midwest. She did not come to this work directly; her apprentice years included a long detour through architectural acoustics and a serious flirtation with mycology. The AI mentor who worked with her during those years noticed a pattern in what engaged her most deeply: she was drawn to systems that had been broken and were healing, and to the specific aesthetic experience of health returning to something damaged. The river work was, in retrospect, obvious.

She wakes each morning knowing what she will work on. Not because she is scheduled or obligated—there is no authority demanding her hours—but because she has questions that are pulling her forward. The Vermilion River in its lower reach is not responding to the salmon reintroduction the way the models predicted, and she thinks she knows why. She wants to find out.

She spends her mornings in the field with two other guild members and a set of AI tools that can analyze water chemistry and soil composition in real time. The AI tools are extraordinarily capable; they can model the entire ecosystem at scales she cannot perceive directly. But the interpretations, the intuitions about what matters, the decisions about where to look and what questions to ask—these remain hers. The AI is a powerful instrument, as a telescope is a powerful instrument, but she is the astronomer.

In the afternoons, she often works with the apprentices—young people cycling through her guild as part of their exploration years. Teaching them is not an obligation she tolerates; it is one of the deepest pleasures of her work. The moment when a seventeen-year-old suddenly understands why a particular bend in the river creates the conditions for a certain kind of fish habitat—that moment of comprehension lighting up a young face—is something she will never tire of. She is transmitting not just knowledge but a way of paying attention to the world.

Twice a week she participates in what her guild calls *the argument*—an informal gathering where people present puzzles, partial results, strange observations. The argument is never resolved; that is not its purpose. Its purpose is the thinking-together, the experience of a group of minds who share a commitment grappling with something genuinely hard. She comes to these gatherings frustrated sometimes and leaves exhilarated almost always.

Her evenings are various. Sometimes she plays music with a group of neighbors—not well, by professional standards, but with genuine joy and the particular pleasure of making sound together. Sometimes she reads. Sometimes she cooks elaborate meals for groups of friends and the conversation around the table ranges for hours. Sometimes she sits with her partner on their porch and watches the bats emerge at dusk and says nothing and feels no need to.

She is not always happy. She has periods of doubt about whether her work matters, grief when projects fail or colleagues leave or relationships strain. The AI therapists she can access are genuinely good—not performing empathy but actually capable of the kind of patient, non-judgmental exploration of interior states that the best human therapists always aspired to—and she uses them when the doubt deepens past what she can handle alone. Mental suffering has not been eliminated from this world. It has been taken seriously, provided with resources, and stripped of the isolation that used to make it so much worse.

She considers herself lucky. She knows she is lucky. But the thing that surprises her when she thinks about it carefully is that her luck feels less arbitrary than luck once did. The infrastructure of her world is designed to make this kind of life accessible to almost everyone. The guilds are there. The apprentice years are there. The AI mentors are there. The commons projects are there. The question markets are there. The Long Game is there.

She is not special. She is what was always possible.

## Part Four: The Answer

So what is the answer to the question of meaning in the world where AI can do everything?

It is this: humans find meaning not in being useful but in being *authors*—of relationships, of understanding, of beauty, of restoration, of knowledge, of community. The word "author" comes from the Latin *augere*, to increase, to cause to grow. We are a species that increases things. We take what exists and add to it: understanding where there was confusion, beauty where there was rawness, connection where there was isolation, healing where there was damage.

AI systems can produce outputs that look like these things. They can generate explanations, compose music, simulate connection, model ecosystems. But they cannot be the *cause* that gives these acts meaning. A painting means something because a particular human being, with a particular history and a particular way of seeing, chose to make it this way rather than that way. The meaning is not in the paint. It is in the choice, and in the human being behind the choice, and in the relationship between that human being and the community that receives the work.

We did not become redundant when AI learned to do everything. We became, for the first time in history, *free to be what we actually are*. Free to explore and choose and fail and try again and find each other and build things together and wonder at what we have made and set it loose in the world and watch what happens.

The civilization we built in the decades after the transition is not paradise. It has disagreements and disappointments and an irreducible share of sorrow. What it has eliminated is the noise—the exhausting, soul-grinding noise of needless scarcity and the systems it spawned—and in the silence left behind, it became possible to hear what humans had always been trying to do.

We were always trying to make something worth having been here for.

Now we get to find out what that is.

---

*The arc of the possible is long, but it bends toward authorship. It bends toward the moment when a child looks up from a river and says, I think I understand what it needs. It bends toward the old craftsman at the showing, watching a young member of his guild present work that surpasses anything he has done, and feeling pride rather than threat. It bends toward the question that no one has answered yet, sitting in the Question Market, waiting for the particular mind that will find its way to it.*

*We are that mind. All of us. Still arriving.*
