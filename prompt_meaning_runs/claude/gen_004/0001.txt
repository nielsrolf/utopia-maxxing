# The Flourishing: A World After Scarcity

## Prologue: What the Economists Got Wrong

The economists who first modeled the age of abundant intelligence made a category error that seems obvious in retrospect. They framed the central question as a problem of *distribution*: if machines can produce everything, how do we allocate the surplus? This was like asking, when a person falls in love, how do they distribute the surplus of their heart.

The question was wrong. The real question was never about allocation. It was about *becoming*.

This is the story of how humanity learned to ask the right question—and what we built once we did.

---

## I. The Architecture of Enough

Begin with what it looks like on an ordinary morning in 2087.

Mira wakes in a small house at the edge of a forest in what was once rural Portugal. She is fifty-three years old and has been a stonemason for eleven years. Before that she was a molecular biologist for eight years, before that a schoolteacher for six. The house she wakes in is one she built herself, slowly, over three years, with her own hands and the help of her neighbors—rough schist walls, timber beams, a courtyard where figs grow against the south-facing stone. The work was not efficient. A construction system could have assembled the equivalent in four days. That is precisely why she did it herself.

There is no poverty in Mira's world. This requires a moment to absorb properly, because phrases like "no poverty" have been used as rhetorical gestures for centuries. This is not that. The material conditions for human survival—food, shelter, medicine, warmth, clean water—are simply available, reliably and universally, in the same way that the air is available. The Abundance Covenant, ratified piecemeal between 2041 and 2059 by most of the world's remaining governance structures, established this as a baseline. An extensive network of automated systems maintains it. Nobody is hungry. Nobody dies of a treatable illness because they cannot afford treatment. Nobody sleeps outside who does not want to.

This is the first and most important thing to understand about the world Mira lives in. It is not a world where meaning has been delivered along with the material security. The material security was just the floor, the precondition. What people built on that floor is the story worth telling.

## II. The Councils of Becoming

The structure that governs this world is not a government in any prior sense, though it has some governmental functions. It is called, in its oldest English form, the Concord—a layered system of nested communities, each with genuine sovereignty over its own practices and culture, each connected to others through a framework of minimum guarantees and mutual restraint.

The minimum guarantees are few but inviolable. Every person may leave any community they are in and find shelter elsewhere. Every child receives, by age fourteen, a candid education in the full range of human possibility—not to recruit them to any particular way of life but to ensure they know alternatives exist. No community may wall itself off from this. And no community may arm itself against another. The mechanisms for enforcing these minimums are boring and effective: a distributed network of advocates, arbiters, and, in extreme cases, something that functions like a small professional peacekeeping force, called softly in several languages by names that translate roughly as "the stewards."

Everything else is negotiable.

In practice, this has produced a startling diversity of human arrangements. There are agricultural villages that have rejected most digital technology as a matter of philosophical conviction. There are dense, exuberant cities where tens of thousands of people live in close quarters and the architecture changes continuously, responding to the aesthetic preferences of residents through systems that would have been called AI in an earlier era and are now just called *the city's mind*. There are nomadic networks of people who follow traditional migration routes. There are offshore platforms hosting communities dedicated to specific intellectual or artistic projects. There are communities organized around religious practices of many varieties, and communities where any religious claim is the subject of constant cheerful mockery. There are communities so small—forty, fifty people—that they function essentially as extended families. There are communities of several million.

The crucial innovation, the thing that makes this work rather than collapsing into either gray uniformity or sectarian chaos, is what is called the Right of Departure with Provision. When anyone leaves any community—for any reason, under any circumstances—they leave with a portable baseline: enough resources to sustain themselves for two years, full access to medical systems, and a guaranteed place in any receiving community that has committed to the Concord. The communities that have committed are nearly all of them. The provision is funded through a modest but universal levy on the productivity of automated systems.

This sounds simple. Its effects are profound. It means that every community exists only because its members continue to choose it. Not in the abstract, sentimental way that one chooses a hometown through inertia and property values, but in a live and continual way. People stay because they want to stay. When a community develops practices that alienate its members, those members leave, and the community either adapts or dwindles. The cultures that persist are the ones that are genuinely nourishing to the people in them.

## III. What Humans Do

This is where the economists' question—how do we distribute the surplus?—gives way to the real question: what does a person *do* when survival is not at stake?

The answer, which surprised almost everyone who thought they knew in advance what it would be, is: *the things that always mattered, pursued properly for the first time.*

Mira, the stonemason, is not unusual. She is representative of a broad pattern. The work people choose, in this world, tends to be either highly skilled physical work, deep relational work, or some form of creative or intellectual pursuit—and these categories blur into each other constantly.

The highly skilled physical work category is larger than almost any futurist predicted. Stonework, woodwork, glassblowing, textile-making, ceramics, gardening, cooking: these trades have undergone a renaissance of extraordinary intensity. The objects produced by hand are not produced because they are cheaper than machine-made equivalents—they are not. They are produced because the *making* is the point. The stonemason's pleasure in fitting two stones together, the sense of absorption in a problem that has exactly as much complexity as a human mind can handle without being overwhelmed—this, it turns out, is not a consolation prize for people who could not operate better machines. It is a primary human good that was suppressed for two centuries because it could not be economically justified.

This distinction matters enormously: the work people do in Mira's world is not make-work. It is not busywork assigned to keep people occupied. Mira's walls stand in the real world, will outlast her, and are experienced as beautiful by the people who live within them. The difference between a wall she built and a wall a construction system built is not nothing—it is visible in every irregularity, every stone chosen by a specific pair of eyes on a specific afternoon when the light was doing a particular thing. The people who live in such houses experience them differently than they experience the constructed equivalent. This is not nostalgia or sentimentality. It is a genuine difference.

The deep relational work category is perhaps larger still. The care of children, of elderly people, of the sick—in the earlier era, this work was systematically devalued because it could not be optimized for productivity. It was, in fact, the most difficult and irreplaceable work humans do. When material pressure was removed, when people could choose what to spend their lives on, an enormous number of them chose to spend their lives in the service of other people's development and flourishing. Teachers. Mentors. What used to be called therapists, though the word has been largely replaced by terms that emphasize the relational rather than the clinical dimension: *accompaniers*, in one community's coinage; *heartworkers*, in another's.

The automated systems are extraordinarily capable at most cognitive tasks and many relational simulations. They are also, as best anyone can determine, not experiencing anything. The choice to be accompanied by a human who is genuinely present, who has their own sorrows and questions and skin in the game of existence—this choice remains meaningful and widely made. Not because the human does the job better in every measurable dimension, but because *what is being offered is the human's presence itself*, not merely a service.

## IV. Meaning Without Manufacture

The deepest challenge of this world is not material. It is not even political, though the political challenges are real and ongoing. The deepest challenge is the one the old question was groping toward: how does a person find their life meaningful when it is not structured by necessity?

The answer that has emerged is not a single answer. It is a family of answers, organized around a few recurring elements.

**The first is craft in the deep sense.** Not necessarily physical craft, though often that. The word is used to describe any activity that has genuine standards, that can be done well or badly, that takes years to master, and that connects the person doing it to a tradition of others who have done it. Stonemasonery. Teaching. Fermentation. The cultivation of specific ecosystems. The composition of music in a particular tradition. The practice of a martial art. The raising of specific breeds of animals. The creation of certain kinds of software. The difference between this era and the consumer era is that craft is now pursued for its own sake, which means it is pursued *properly*—slowly, with attention, across decades, without the pressure to produce returns that made real development so difficult before.

**The second is genuine stakes.** There is a widespread and largely correct perception that a life without real consequences is a life without real meaning. The world of the Concord is not a world without stakes—it is a world where the stakes are chosen rather than imposed. Mira's walls can fall down, and this would be a real failure with real consequences for the people who live in them. The ecosystem cultivator's forest can go wrong, and this is a genuine loss. The teacher's students can fail to thrive, and this is genuinely sorrowful. What has been eliminated is not the stakes but the *unchosen* catastrophes: the child dying of smallpox, the crop failing because a distant market moved, the factory closing because of decisions made by people in rooms you would never enter. The bad luck that is simply the universe's indifference. That is mostly gone. What remains is the consequence of your own choices, your own skill, your own relationship with the people you have committed to.

**The third is membership.** Human beings are, to an extent that earlier individualist philosophy badly underestimated, constituted by their relationships and communities. The self is not prior to the group; it is, to a large degree, made by the group. The Concord's community structure, with its genuine plurality and genuine exit rights, has enabled something that was very difficult in the era of homogenizing mass culture: the development of real communities with real characters, whose members are genuinely shaped by their participation in them. To be a member of a particular village that practices a particular form of seasonal ritual—to know the ritual's history, to have participated in it since childhood, to know that your parents and their parents did the same—this is not a trivial pleasure. It is a source of self. It is a way of knowing who you are.

**The fourth is what might be called the pursuit of understanding.** The great intellectual traditions—philosophy, science, mathematics, history, the arts—have not become obsolete. They have become available, fully and properly, for the first time, to anyone who wants them. And it turns out that a great many people want them when the wanting is not filtered through the question of whether it will pay. In communities devoted to serious intellectual work, there is a quality of sustained attention and genuine curiosity that observers from the competitive-credential era would find almost shocking. People are studying things because the things are interesting. The conversations are about the problems, not about the positions.

## V. The Question of the Children

The hardest problem in this world, the one that generates the most sustained argument in the councils and between communities, is children.

Every child is born into a community they did not choose. Every child is, for years, unable to exercise the Right of Departure—they are too young to leave, too young to evaluate the community's practices with any real sophistication. Every community wants to form its children in its own image. This is not a pathology; it is the normal working of culture, and without it, no culture can persist. But it creates a real risk: children who are formed in ways they would not have chosen, for lives they do not want.

The minimum guarantees address this partly. Every child, somewhere between age ten and fourteen depending on developmental readiness, attends what are called simply the Open Sessions—a series of encounters with people from very different communities, facilitated by the Concord's advocates, explicitly designed to reveal the plurality of human possibility. These sessions are not designed to recruit children to any alternative life. They are designed to ensure that children know alternatives exist, know they have the right to pursue them, and know how to do so.

This is fiercely contested by some communities, who experience it as an intrusion on their sovereignty over their own cultural transmission. The Concord holds the line on it anyway. It is the most important line they hold.

Beyond this, the world has settled into a practice of collective attention to children that is arguably the most significant civilizational investment being made. The care of children—their development, their education, their emotional life—is treated with the seriousness that the earlier era reserved for economic productivity. There are communities devoted entirely to the art of childhood, in which the practitioners spend thirty years learning how to be with children well. The knowledge accumulated in these communities, shared through extended networks of apprenticeship and practice, has produced genuine advances in human understanding of what children need to flourish.

What children need to flourish, it turns out, is not so mysterious. They need adults who are genuinely present. They need challenge calibrated to their developing capacities. They need models of adults who find their own lives meaningful. They need membership in something larger than themselves. They need the experience of working hard and succeeding, and the experience of failing and surviving. None of this requires revolution. It requires, simply, that adults structure their lives in ways that make this possible—which requires, in turn, that adults not be so ground down by economic pressure that they have nothing left over.

## VI. What Was Lost

Honesty requires acknowledging what was lost, or at least reduced.

The economic pressure that crushed so much of human possibility was also, for some people, a source of energy. The urgency of necessity produced certain kinds of creativity, certain kinds of intensity, certain kinds of solidarity among people who were fighting together against something. The art made in desperation sometimes has a quality that the art made in comfort does not. This is real.

Some people, in the world of the Concord, deliberately choose harder lives than the baseline provides—not because they have to but because they find that difficulty nourishing. They join communities that practice voluntary austerity, that impose real hardship on themselves as a spiritual or philosophical discipline. This is allowed. The exit guarantee is always available; if someone decides they have had enough voluntary hardship, they can leave. But many of these communities persist and thrive, because for the people in them, the difficulty is not arbitrary—it is chosen, it is meaningful, it is part of a larger way of understanding what a human life is for.

The other loss is harder to name. It has to do with the relationship between individuals and history. In the old world, you were small and the world was large, and this smallness—your insignificance in the face of economies and empires and forces entirely beyond your control—was, paradoxically, a kind of relief. You could not be expected to bear full responsibility for your own life when your life was so thoroughly shaped by forces outside it. The world of the Concord, with its exit rights and its communities of genuine choice, places more of the weight of your life's shape squarely on your own choices. This is, ultimately, good. But it is also heavier. The person who is living a life she does not like cannot, in the same way, attribute this entirely to forces beyond her control. She can leave. She chose to stay.

Freedom is responsibility. This was always true. In the world of the Concord, it is more clearly true, and there is less comfortable evasion of it. Some people find this unbearable. There are, accordingly, communities devoted to the art of helping people bear it—communities of accompaniers and heartworkers who sit with people through the vertigo of genuine freedom and help them find what, underneath the habits of complaint and the patterns of avoidance, they actually want.

## VII. Coda: Mira's Afternoon

It is midafternoon, and Mira is fitting a lintel stone over a doorway.

The stone weighs eighty kilograms. She is working with her apprentice, a twenty-two-year-old named Theo, who came to her village two years ago from a dense urban community and found, to his own surprise, that he wanted to learn this. The fitting of a lintel stone is a problem of geometry and physics and patience and judgment. It is, right now, exactly as hard as it needs to be. Mira is teaching Theo how to read the way weight distributes across a course of stone, how to feel it in your hands before you can see it with your eyes.

She will probably spend the rest of her life in this village. She might, at some point, want to do something else—she has been curious about a community of textile makers she visited last year, about whether the skills of attention she has developed through stone might translate. Maybe she will spend five years there. Maybe she will spend the rest of her life there instead. The choice is available, real, and hers.

The afternoon light is doing a particular thing to the stone she is holding. She notes this, not consciously but in the way that expertise makes available to the body: a slight color variation that tells her something about the stone's internal structure, about where it can bear load and where it cannot.

She communicates this to Theo in a few words. He does not fully understand yet. In two years he will understand it as she does; in ten years he may understand it better. The knowledge is passing between them in the particular way that such knowledge passes—hand to hand, eye to eye, across the irreducible particularity of two bodies working on a real problem in an afternoon of real light.

This is not everything. It is not a solution to the problem of death, or to the problem of loneliness, or to the deep philosophical questions about whether any human life can be finally justified. These problems remain. They will always remain. They are part of what it means to be human.

But Mira, holding a lintel stone in the afternoon light, teaching a young man what her teacher taught her, building walls that will outlast her—she is not asking those questions right now. She is entirely here. The stone is heavy. The problem is exactly as hard as it needs to be. Her apprentice is learning.

This, it turned out, was what enough looks like. Not paradise. Not the end of difficulty. Not a life without loss or confusion or the occasional afternoon of bleak doubt.

Just this: a person, doing something real, that matters, with other people, freely.

It took humanity an embarrassingly long time to build the conditions for it. But here, on an ordinary afternoon in 2087, in a small village at the edge of a forest in what was once rural Portugal, it is happening.

And in a thousand other places, in a thousand other forms, it is happening too.
