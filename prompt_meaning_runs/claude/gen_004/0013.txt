# The Garden and the Question: On Finding Meaning When the Machines Do the Work

## I. What the Shore Looks Like

There is a harbor in what was once a declining industrial port city on the Great Lakes—Erie, Pennsylvania, to be precise, a place that spent most of the twentieth century losing things: factories, population, relevance, the peculiar civic confidence that comes from knowing your city matters to the world. The remediation of its poisoned bay took fifteen years. The rebuilding took a generation. What stands there now, in the middle of this century, is not a monument to recovery but something stranger and more interesting: a city that has made peace with the question it spent a hundred years avoiding.

The question is this: *What are people actually for, when production is no longer the answer?*

You can feel the question in the air when you arrive—not as anxiety, the way it felt in the 2030s when it first became undeniable, but as something more like weather. Background. The organizing pressure of a civilization that has decided, collectively and imperfectly, to take it seriously.

The harbor is maintained by a rotating stewardship council—not a public works department in the old bureaucratic sense, but a community of roughly forty people who have taken the harbor as their *decade work*, the long and demanding project around which they organize their sense of self during this chapter of their lives. They monitor the water chemistry, maintain the boats, tend the native plantings along the restored shoreline, keep records of which birds returned in which season. An AI system handles the data—the sensor networks, the pattern recognition, the alerts when something shifts in the ecosystem in ways that require attention. What the AI cannot do, what it is not asked to do, is *care about this particular harbor*, carry the memory of the morning the first lake sturgeon returned after a forty-year absence, feel the specific weight of responsibility to this water, these fish, this patient and improbable recovery.

That is the stewards' work. The distinction is not sentimental. It is, as the city's founders eventually came to understand, the whole difference between activity and meaning.

## II. The Decade That Changed Everything

To understand how Erie came to be what it is, you have to understand what people called the Great Untangling—the painful years between 2031 and 2042 when the old equation between work and worth finally broke down completely and civilization was forced to confront what to put in its place.

The proximate cause was economic. By 2031, AI systems could perform roughly seventy percent of knowledge work—drafting, analysis, diagnosis, design, code—faster, cheaper, and in many respects better than human beings. This did not produce unemployment in the simple sense. People still had jobs. But the jobs felt different. Much of what remained felt like supervision of systems that did not need supervising, performance for managers who did not need the performance. People kept their salaries and lost their sense of purpose, which turned out to be the more important loss.

The psychological consequences were severe and well-documented: a crisis of meaning that manifested as depression, addiction, political radicalization, and what clinicians called "existential drift"—people who were technically alive and technically functional but who described themselves as spectators of their own lives. The welfare data from this period is sobering. Something deep in human psychology had come unmoored, and material abundance did not reattach it.

Governments implemented Universal Basic Income. This was necessary and not sufficient. Money without structure, it turned out, was not the same as freedom. Many recipients experienced not liberation but a particular kind of vertigo—the vertigo of a culture that had told them their entire lives that their worth was their productivity, now offering them funds not to produce. The message felt contemptuous even when it was not intended to be. Give a person everything except a reason to get up, and you have given them almost nothing.

What actually helped came from three directions at once, from people who in earlier decades would never have spoken to each other.

The **wellbeing researchers** had spent decades accumulating evidence about what actually made human lives go well, evidence that had been systematically ignored because it was inconvenient. They knew, with considerable empirical confidence, that flourishing required roughly six things: authentic relationships, genuine competence and growth, autonomy, contribution to something larger than oneself, engagement with real difficulty, and connection to some form of meaning that extended beyond the immediate moment. None of these required economic productivity per se. All of them required *structure*—contexts and communities and practices within which they could be pursued.

The **makers**—craftspeople, artists, teachers, scientists, farmers, musicians, stewards of land and water and tradition—had been practicing, quietly and without much cultural recognition, an answer to the question that everyone else was just now asking. They had always known something that the productivity economy could not quite see: that the deepest human satisfaction comes not from having made something but from *the encounter between a person who genuinely cares and a difficulty that genuinely resists*. They had been living inside that encounter their whole lives. Now, suddenly, they had something important to say.

The **institution designers** supplied the third element: the recognition that meaning does not emerge spontaneously from good intentions and available time. It must be scaffolded. Humans are not meaning-producing machines who simply need to be freed from constraints. We are meaning-*seeking* creatures who need contexts, practices, rituals, and communities within which the search can succeed. The early UBI era's great mistake was imagining that removing economic coercion would automatically produce flourishing. What it produced was liberty without architecture—a vast open field with no paths, no landmarks, no one to walk with.

The Great Untangling was the decade in which these three groups found each other, argued bitterly, compromised extensively, and began building something new. Erie is one result. It is not the only result—there are dozens of communities across North America and Europe and Asia attempting similar experiments, each shaped by local culture and history, each sharing what it learns without intellectual property claims. But Erie is one of the clearest expressions of what they built, because Erie had the advantage of starting from very nearly nothing, of having lost enough that it was free to try.

## III. The Four Domains and the Shape of a Day

Life in Erie is structured not by job requirements but by what its founders called the *four domains*: Craft, Care, Commons, and Contemplation. Every citizen engages with all four, in proportions that vary by life stage and temperament and choice. The city's social architecture is designed to make engagement with each domain easy, rewarding, and genuinely challenging—not pleasant in the way that entertainment is pleasant, but meaningful in the way that difficulty mastered is meaningful.

**Craft** is the domain of making and mastering. This is taken with a seriousness that visitors from older cities find startling. Erie maintains sixty-odd workshops, studios, and laboratories—woodworking, ceramics, electronics, textiles, glassblowing, fermentation, software, mathematics, music, medicine, writing, ecological restoration. These are not hobby spaces. They are places where people pursue genuine excellence under the guidance of masters, within traditions that have standards and histories and accumulated wisdom.

The key insight, the one the founders took longest to arrive at, is that meaningful making requires *difficulty* and *real stakes*. Early maker spaces in the 2020s often failed to produce genuine meaning because they were organized around self-expression rather than excellence. People were encouraged to make whatever they wanted, however they wanted, with no expectation of rigor. This produced pleasant activity but not the deep satisfaction that comes from genuinely mastering something hard, meeting the standards of a tradition, and earning the recognition of people who know enough to see quality.

Erie's workshops operate on an apprenticeship model. You enter as a novice. You are taught by journeypeople who are themselves still learning, under masters who have spent decades at their craft. You are evaluated not by algorithmic metric but by the judgments of people who understand what excellence in this domain actually looks like. You advance when those people say you have advanced. You make things that are genuinely used—furniture for the common rooms, software for the city's systems, music for the weekly concerts that are among the city's most beloved institutions. The work flows into the world. The world responds to it. This circuit—making, offering, receiving response, improving—is the basic metabolism of a meaningful craft life.

The AI systems in the workshops are present and powerful and carefully positioned. A ceramicist can ask the system to model how a particular glaze will behave at different temperatures, saving her the years of trial and error that once preceded genuine mastery. She can push past gates that would have stopped most people in an earlier era—freeing those years for the subtler questions of what form serves this purpose, what surface honors this material, what *beautiful* means in this specific moment. The result is not that mastery is easy. It is that mastery goes *deeper*, because humans can spend their developmental years on judgment and taste and vision rather than on the mechanical prerequisites. But when she brings a finished piece to her master for evaluation, the question "did you make this?" has a real answer with real stakes. The pride of making something well is inseparable from the knowledge that the hands and judgment were hers.

**Care** is the domain of attending to other people and to the living world. In Erie, care work is not an afterthought or a problem to be solved through automation. It is understood as one of the central human vocations—raising children, tending elders, supporting those who are struggling, maintaining relationships, stewarding the more-than-human world of plants and animals and ecosystems.

This domain was the most contested during the founding period. Some argued that AI could handle most care work—robotic caregivers for the elderly, algorithmic monitoring for children, automated therapists at scale. The founders rejected this on empirical grounds. The research was unambiguous: people needed to be cared for by other people. Not because machines could not perform the surface behaviors of care, but because what humans need from care is not the surface behavior. It is the *attention of another consciousness*, the recognition of their particularity by someone who could in principle be attending to something else but has chosen to attend to them. This is not a technological gap waiting to be closed. It is a structural feature of what care actually is.

Every adult citizen spends a minimum of four hours per week in direct care work—visiting elders, working with children, tending the community gardens that supply a significant fraction of the city's food. This is not experienced as a tax. It is experienced, in surveys and in conversation, as among the most reliably meaningful parts of the week. The relationships formed across generations in these contexts are among the most frequently cited sources of happiness the city has. The elder who is visited regularly by a twenty-two-year-old apprentice glassblower, the child who is known and recognized by a dozen adults beyond her parents—these relationships produce something that no UBI payment and no entertainment platform has ever produced: the bone-deep certainty that one is not superfluous.

**Commons** is the domain of civic life—the governance, maintenance, and stewardship of shared spaces and collective decisions. Erie is governed by a system its founders called *deliberative federalism*: nested neighborhood assemblies, city councils, and domain-specific governing bodies, connected by AI-assisted deliberation tools that help translate the wisdom of many voices into actual decisions without losing the texture of individual perspectives. The AI systems here are doing genuinely important work—synthesizing thousands of comments into legible summaries, identifying where apparent disagreements mask underlying consensus, flagging when proposed decisions are likely to have unintended consequences. But the decisions themselves are made by humans, in conversation with other humans, after deliberation that takes time and requires genuine engagement.

Citizens of Erie are expected to participate in at least two deliberative processes per year—to show up, listen, argue, and ultimately commit to collective decisions they may not entirely agree with. This is not experienced as onerous. It is experienced, by most residents, as one of the clearest expressions of what belonging to this community actually means. The experience of living under rules you helped make, even imperfectly, even rules you argued against and lost, is very different from living under rules handed down by markets or bureaucracies or algorithms. You did not get everything you wanted. You got something better: you got to be present when the decision was made.

**Contemplation** is the domain of reflection, learning, and the inner life. Erie's library is one of the most beautiful buildings in the city, designed by a collaborative team that spent three years arguing about what a library should be in an age when information is universally accessible. Their answer: not a place for accessing information, but a place for *thinking*—for sitting with difficult ideas, for the slow, unhurried engagement with questions that produces not answers but *formation*, the gradual shaping of a self that knows what it believes and why.

The library runs continuous programs of reading groups, philosophical discussions, and what the founders called "seminars of not-knowing"—structured conversations around questions that resist resolution, where the point is not to arrive at a conclusion but to practice the difficult art of sustained uncertainty. These are among the best-attended events in Erie. This surprised the founders initially. It should not have. Humans are creatures who need to be confused well—to have their confusions taken seriously, given company, offered a tradition of others who have been confused in the same direction. The library provides this. It is, in this sense, as essential to the city's wellbeing as its workshops.

## IV. How Children Grow Into This

The change in childhood is perhaps the most consequential change of all, because the adults of this generation were formed differently.

Education in Erie is not primarily about transmitting information—the machines handle that beautifully and without fatigue. It is about *formation*: the development of character, taste, judgment, and a nascent sense of vocation. Children spend significant portions of each week in genuine apprenticeship relationships with adults who are practicing craft, care, or stewardship. Not observing. *Doing*, in a supervised and gradually expanding way.

A child of ten spends two mornings a week with a master woodworker—not to become a woodworker, necessarily, but to learn what it feels like to improve at something genuinely difficult, to feel the texture of honest materials, to have a mentor who pays attention to her specific development and adjusts accordingly. A child of fourteen assists a harbor steward, developing the naturalist's eye for detail, learning the pleasure of noticing things that others walk past, the satisfaction of knowing a particular stretch of shoreline well enough to feel when something has changed.

Children are also explicitly taught what used to be called soft skills and are now called, simply, the primary skills: how to have difficult conversations, how to repair a damaged friendship, how to be present with someone who is grieving, how to make and keep commitments, how to be genuinely wrong and recover your footing. These are not ancillary to the educational project. They are its center.

The result is that by the time young people begin choosing their adult paths, they have already experienced, from the inside, what it feels like to improve at something hard, to be genuinely needed by someone, to contribute to something that will outlast the school year. They are not arriving at adulthood hoping that meaning will reveal itself if they look in the right places. They already know, from their own experience, that meaning is real—that it is produced by specific practices in specific communities with specific people, that it requires difficulty and commitment and time, and that it is available to them if they choose to pursue it.

## V. The Problem of Recognition

Here is the hardest problem, and the one Erie's founders spent the most time on: *how do people know that what they do matters?*

The market solved this problem, badly. It told you your work mattered by paying you for it. This was bad in ways that took generations to fully diagnose: it made the apparent mattering of your work hostage to what others were willing to pay, which reflected not genuine value but a thousand contingencies of supply and demand and social convention. Teachers mattered less than traders. Carers mattered less than coders. People could feel this wrongness even when they could not articulate it, and the feeling produced a kind of civilizational bad faith—a widespread sense that the things most worth doing were not the things most rewarded.

Erie's solution draws on an insight from an unexpected direction: game design. For decades, designers of complex games had solved an analogous problem—how do you make thousands of players feel genuinely accomplished when they're all competing in the same system? Their answer was to build systems with many different axes of achievement, so that the person worst at combat might be the community's best explorer, best builder, best storyteller. Excellence at one thing did not crowd out excellence at another.

Erie applied this at civilizational scale through what its founders called the **Council of Recognition**—a set of institutions designed to make visible and honorable the full range of genuinely valuable human contributions, not just those that happen to be economically legible.

Every year, Erie holds a series of celebrations. Not awards ceremonies in the corporate sense, but genuine public acknowledgments of excellence and contribution across every domain. The master glassblower who has spent thirty years perfecting a particular technique is celebrated alongside the scientist who made a breakthrough in materials research alongside the woman who has spent a decade running the neighborhood mediation program alongside the teenager who composed a piece of music that moved people alongside the harbor steward who noticed, before anyone else, the early signs of a invasive species and acted in time. The celebrations are not competitive—no rankings, no first and second places. They are *recognitions*: public acts of noticing, of saying *this person did this thing, and it was genuinely excellent, and we see it and we are changed by it.*

Crucially, these recognitions are *local*. The harbor steward does not measure her work against every other restoration project on Earth. She measures it against what this water was and what it might become, against the standards of her community and her own conscience. This is not enforced modesty. It is a genuine restructuring of the competitive frame—away from the single global tournament that dominated the twentieth century and toward thousands of overlapping local games, each with their own forms of mastery, each producing its own forms of recognition. In a world where almost every person belongs to a community that genuinely sees them, the hunger for mass recognition loses some of its desperate edge.

## VI. The Grief That Had to Be Honored

It would be dishonest to describe Erie without describing what was let go, and what grief was honored in the letting go.

Many people experienced the loss of the old work order as a bereavement—and it was treated as one. Not managed, not redirected into productivity, but *mourned*. The work one's father had done, the identity one had built through decades of a career, the sense of irreplaceable expertise—these were real losses, even when what replaced them was objectively richer. The commonwealth learned, slowly and with some failures, not to rush the grief or to insist on the abundance before the loss had been acknowledged.

Communities formed around specific trades and professions that had become obsolete—not to restore them, which was usually neither possible nor desirable, but to honor them, to carry their memory forward, to extract and preserve what had been genuinely valuable in them. The steel workers of western Pennsylvania held annual gatherings for decades, not as industrial revivals but as acts of cultural memory—honoring what it meant to make something essential with your body, to be part of a labor that held cities together, to know that the bridges stood partly because of your hands.

There is a long tradition in Erie, as in the broader commonwealth of which it is part, of sitting with difficulty rather than dissolving it. The machines can eliminate most pain. Most pain can be eliminated. But the people of this world have developed a collective wisdom about which hardships are worth enduring—not because suffering is good, but because certain forms of difficulty are the weight against which depth of character is built. They have learned to distinguish between suffering that is merely cruel and suffering that is the texture of a life genuinely lived. They have kept the second kind, in carefully considered doses, and built institutions to support people through it.

## VII. The Larger Project

Zoom out far enough and you can see the shape of what humanity is doing with all of this.

Freed from the majority of subsistence labor, civilization has turned its attention to the very long work. The restoration of damaged ecosystems—not just patches of harbor or tallgrass prairie but oceanic systems, forests, river networks, the slow patient project of returning a richer version of the living world than industrial civilization had left. The exploration of the solar system, still in its early stages, pursued not as a race or a conquest but as the first generation of a multi-century project of becoming a spacefaring species. The development of governance capable of making wise decisions at civilizational scale—still halting, still imperfect, but further along than the early century would have believed possible.

And the cultural work: the making of art, music, literature, mathematics, philosophy that draws on human experience centuries deeper than it was. There is more beauty being made right now, in this world, than at any prior point in human history. Not because there are more talented people—though there may be—but because there are more people who have time, support, community, and a sense of purpose sufficient to bring their gifts fully to bear.

The machines made this possible. The humans chose it.

## VIII. The Teenager and the Water

You have been in Erie for a week. On your last morning, you walk down to the harbor. A teenager is crouched at the water's edge, peering at something in the shallows, arguing with an AI assistant on her tablet about whether the insect she's observing is a particular species of water strider or a related genus—the distinction matters, apparently, for the harbor's health index.

She doesn't look up when you approach. She is deep in a disagreement she finds genuinely important.

You ask her what she's figured out.

She looks up, slightly irritated, then relents. "Okay, so—the AI is pretty sure it's the common species. But the legs are wrong. The spacing. Either this is a variant we haven't catalogued, or the taxonomy is messier than the database thinks." She pauses. "I requested samples from three other observation points to compare. Probably nothing. But it might be something."

"What happens if the taxonomy is wrong?" you ask.

She thinks about this seriously, which is how people here tend to think about things. "Probably not much, for this insect. But the methodology that got it wrong might have gotten other things wrong too. And some of those other things might matter for the restoration plan." She shrugs, but not dismissively—it is the shrug of someone who has decided to care about something and is at peace with the caring. "Anyway. The water's different here than it was. My grandmother used to say you couldn't swim in this harbor. Now you can. Something changed, and I want to know exactly what."

The train is coming. You pick up your bag.

The teenager has already returned to her tablet, to the argument, to the patient and particular and demanding work of finding out whether something is true about this water, this morning, this place she has chosen to attend to.

This, you think, is what the people who built this world figured out. Not that meaning is found in grand purpose, though grand purposes have their place. Not that meaning is found in leisure or comfort, though those have their place too. Meaning is found in the encounter between a person who genuinely cares and a question that genuinely resists—in the experience of bringing everything you are to something that demands you bring everything you are, and discovering that you are enough, or discovering that you need to become more, and knowing how to try.

The AI can answer the question faster. The AI does not need the answer to mean anything.

That is the difference. That is, finally, the whole difference—and the recognition of it is what the city was built to protect.

## IX. The Answer

How do humans find genuine meaning in a world where machines can do most of the work?

The same way they always did: by caring for specific things and specific people, by pursuing mastery of difficult arts, by belonging to communities that know and need them, by contributing to projects that extend beyond their own lifetimes, by asking questions that resist easy answers and staying with the resistance long enough to be changed by it.

What changed is that these are no longer the consolation prizes awarded to those who had already secured their survival. They have become the *point*—the primary business of a human life, finally freed to be so.

The machines did not give humanity meaning. Nothing outside a person can do that. What the machines gave was time, abundance, and liberation from compulsion—the conditions under which meaning could be pursued without constant interruption by the brute demands of keeping alive. Whether those conditions were used well depended, as it always has, on the choices that people and communities made about what to value, what to practice, what to build, and what to honor in each other.

The people of this world chose to take the question seriously. They chose to build institutions that scaffold the search rather than assuming it will happen on its own. They chose to recognize the full range of human contribution rather than only the portion that is economically legible. They chose to raise children inside genuine apprenticeship and genuine care rather than preparing them for a labor market that no longer needed them. They chose to mourn what was lost before celebrating what was gained.

These choices required, above all, a willingness to say clearly what had always been true and always been difficult to say: that human beings are not primarily producers, not primarily consumers, not primarily anything that can be described in the language of economics. We are creatures who need to be *met*—by difficulty, by beauty, by one another, by the specific weight of a specific responsibility to a specific piece of the world. We are creatures for whom the question of meaning is not a luxury to be addressed after the practical questions are settled, but the prior question, the one all the practical questions must eventually serve.

The shore of Erie in the middle of this century is what happens when enough people make enough decisions that take that truth seriously, in enough ways, over enough time.

It is imperfect. It is contested. It is alive with people who have been given the great and difficult gift of being asked what they are actually for—and the structures, the communities, and the time to find an answer that is genuinely their own.

We have not built this yet. But in fragments and experiments and imperfect prototypes scattered across the present moment, we have all the pieces. The work now is coordination, and will, and the courage to say clearly what we are building toward.

We are building toward a world where the question "what are you for?" is answered not by your salary, not by your title, not by any metric a machine could compute, but by the specific texture of your attention—what you have chosen to care for, what difficulty you have chosen to stay with, what community you have chosen to belong to, what question you have chosen to carry through years of not-yet-knowing until the answer finally, imperfectly, partially arrives.

That answer is yours. The work of the next decades is building a world in which you have the time, and the company, and the support to find it.

That is enough.

That is, in fact, everything.
