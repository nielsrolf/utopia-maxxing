# The Tending Feast: A Portrait of a World That Learned to Grow

---

## Prologue: A Particular Tuesday

Nadia Osei-Mensah is forty-one years old, and she is running late to a meeting about fish.

She lives in a city on the coast of what was once called the Gulf of Guinea, in a neighborhood built partly from compressed earth, partly from reclaimed timber, partly from the decisions of several hundred people arguing over a period of three years about what they wanted their street to feel like. The street feels, to most people, like a place where something is always happening and nobody is entirely sure what. This is regarded as a success. The Stubborn One — the AI structural advisor who refused to approve three versions of the load-bearing design — is still around, as a kind of neighborhood mascot. Someone painted it on the wall of the community fermentation collective that meets on Wednesday evenings.

The fish meeting is about restoration of a nearshore reef system that has been recovering, slowly, for sixteen years. Nadia has been a marine ecologist for eleven of those years, after being a secondary school teacher for six years, after being a graduate student for four years, after being, in her own accounting, genuinely lost for about three years in her mid-twenties when the question of what to do with her life presented itself and refused to be answered by any of the obvious available mechanisms. She was not, during those three years, in crisis. She had what she needed: housing, food, healthcare, the company of people who knew her. What she lacked was the sense that any particular path was urgent, and what she did with that absence was not nothing — she traveled, worked in a watershed restoration project for a season, spent six months learning to make ceramic glazes with a precision that satisfied something in her she hadn't known was hungry. None of this was preparation for her later work in any direct sense. All of it was, in ways she is still discovering.

The meeting is in a building whose ground floor opens onto a street full of morning light. There are seven people at the table and one AI system serving as research collaborator — not facilitator, not decision-maker; it has been asked specifically to hold its opinions until the humans have formed their own, and it has agreed, and it keeps that agreement with what Nadia, who has worked with it for five years, can only describe as deliberate patience. On the screen behind the table: twelve years of reef population data, three competing models of what the data means, and a gap where the models disagree that Nadia has been staring at for two months and that she suspects is the most interesting thing she has ever encountered.

Eight billion other mornings are underway. They are not all like this. They are like this in the ways that matter: someone is asking a question, somewhere, that was not asked yesterday. Someone else, somewhere else, has the partial answer and does not yet know it. The infrastructure connecting those two people — the material floor that lets them each show up fully rather than desperately, the information commons that helps them find each other, the institutions that prevent their work from being captured before it can be shared — is not glamorous infrastructure. It required sixty years to build, and several near-catastrophes, and the particular stubbornness of people who built things they were not sure would work, and built them anyway.

This essay is about those sixty years. It is about the bones: how the floor was built, why knowledge finally learned to move, what work became when survival stopped organizing it, how the world came to be governed by something more durable than either raw power or good intentions. It is about what remains unfinished, which is most of it, which is the point.

## Part One: The Accountability Gap and the Entangled Decade

There is a way to tell the story of how the world changed that omits the texture of changing, which is always made of people in specific circumstances making specific choices they were not sure about, and arrives immediately at the outcome as if the outcome were the interesting part. That story is accurate and useless. The texture is where hope lives or dies.

The years between 2025 and 2040 were defined by a recognition that arrived, as recognitions usually do, later than it should have and in a form more painful than necessary. Two recognitions, actually, that turned out to be the same recognition stated differently.

The first: that the suffering of that era was structural rather than natural. The child dying of a preventable disease was not dying of bad luck. She was dying of a coordination failure — a patent regime that made her life less profitable to save than to lose, a distribution network optimized for wealthy markets, a research funding structure that rewarded discovery of what was profitable to discover rather than what was necessary. The man who could not read at forty was not suffering from personal inadequacy. He was suffering from a school system optimized for credentials, inside an economy that had confused credentials with competence, inside a culture that had confused competence with worth. Seeing this clearly was not comfortable. The historian Priya Ramachandran later called the period *the accountability gap years*: the gap between seeing that systems produced suffering and accepting that everyone operating inside those systems bore some portion of responsibility for changing them. Some people never crossed that gap. Enough people did, across enough communities and enough decades, that it eventually became the grain running through how children were raised and institutions were argued about: *you are not the exception to the principles that hold this world together. Systems behave according to what they measure. When a system produces harm through its logic rather than through any individual's malice, change the structure.*

The second recognition was about AI specifically, and arrived through a decade of genuine political crisis. AI development had proceeded as if the technology existed in a vacuum — as if alignment could be solved in a laboratory and then handed to a world still organized around the concentration of wealth and power and expected to produce good outcomes. It could not. The technical work and the political work had to happen simultaneously, or neither would hold. The decade between roughly 2028 and 2039 — what historians call the Entangled Decade — was the most politically turbulent period most living people can remember. Several democracies went badly wrong before their course corrections. The information environment became catastrophically difficult before it became better. There were actors who attempted to capture AI capability before governance frameworks could be established. There were near-disasters.

What emerged was not elegant. It was a patchwork, built under pressure, held together by the shared recognition that catastrophic outcomes were genuinely plausible and required genuine cooperation to avoid. The Global AI Commons Treaty of 2031 was mocked at signing as toothless — and it was, initially. But it established the precedent of international oversight and created the institutional channels through which stronger frameworks were subsequently negotiated. The Distributed Benefit Protocols of 2035 established that foundational AI capabilities were a commons rather than a property right, and that their gains would be distributed through mechanisms designed by democratic bodies rather than captured by those who happened to hold early patents.

What was most important about the Entangled Decade was not any single treaty or protocol but the consolidation of a shared vocabulary — the *coordinacy literacy* that began spreading through schools and public discourse and institutional design, the ability to recognize when a structure was producing harm through its logic, to name capture when it was happening, to understand collective action problems well enough to solve them rather than simply suffer them. This vocabulary was the precondition for everything else. Without it, the abundance that was coming would have been captured and concentrated, as abundance had always been captured and concentrated. With it, capture became harder to sustain. Not impossible. Meaningfully, consequentially harder.

## Part Two: The Floor, and What the Floor Is For

The material transformation that followed was not driven by sudden virtue. It was driven by the restructuring of incentive systems and measurement frameworks by democratic bodies that had, through years of hard argument, developed both the coordinacy literacy to understand what restructuring was needed and the political will to enact it.

By 2045, the material floor was in place across most of the world's population. It was not generous. It was, in most implementations, spare: universal provision of food, housing, healthcare, and education, funded by productivity taxes on AI systems and restructured resource rents, implemented unevenly and imperfectly but widely enough to hold the social fabric together through the disruption. The floor was not designed to be comfortable. It was designed to provide *time*: time to reorient rather than scramble, time to find new forms of meaningful work rather than accept any available substitute, time to be a parent or a student or a person in a season of genuine uncertainty rather than a worker clinging to relevance. Time is what precarity steals. The floor gave it back.

And then the floor enabled something nobody had quite predicted at the scale it arrived: the explosion of human curiosity.

When people are not desperate, they do not stop working. They work differently. They pursue meaning rather than survival, and when they pursue meaning, the work they produce is genuinely different in quality and ambition from the work produced under existential threat. This had been known theoretically. The floor made it demonstrable in practice. The woman who spent the 2020s working double shifts in a logistics center, her cognitive capacity consumed by financial anxiety, her creative attention reserved for the margins of a life whose main current was simply staying afloat — that woman, relieved of the existential pressure, turned out to have opinions about water filtration membrane design. The man who had been managing depression with tools blunt enough to mute not only the suffering but the curiosity that sat alongside it — given access to genuinely effective treatment, he turned out to have been carrying, for twenty years, a question about the acoustic properties of specific urban environments that no one had ever thought to study systematically.

The treatment of mental illness at scale was, perhaps, the most underappreciated achievement of the transition. Not through blunt pharmaceutical intervention but through targeted neurological support combined with AI-assisted therapy that made the best psychological understanding accessible to everyone regardless of income — not replacing human therapeutic relationships but preparing people to enter and benefit from them. The weight of untreated psychological suffering had always been invisible in a way physical illness was not. When it was substantially addressed, the change in the texture of ordinary human life was enormous. People found it easier to be present in their own lives. They had more capacity for attention, more tolerance for the productive friction of genuine disagreement, more ability to remain in difficult conversations long enough to learn from them. This is what made so many other changes possible.

The Prize Architecture, established beginning in 2038, rewarded outcomes rather than patents: reductions in disease burden, measured across populations, tracked by AI systems capable of modeling epidemiological dynamics at the resolution of individual communities. Within fifteen years, the diseases that had concentrated their killing in the populations least able to pay for their own survival were being researched with the same intensity that blockbuster drugs for wealthy markets had previously commanded. The math had changed. Saving a million lives in sub-Saharan Africa became more commercially productive than managing the chronic conditions of a few hundred thousand affluent patients — not because humanity became more virtuous, but because the measurement system was rebuilt to reflect what humanity claimed to care about.

By 2052, most childhood cancers were curable. By the early 2060s, comprehensive neurological restoration protocols had become standard care. By 2071, the metabolic and cardiovascular conditions that had defined middle age as a period of managed decline had been substantially interrupted across every income level on every continent. Biological lifespans now commonly reach a hundred and fifteen or a hundred and twenty in reasonable health — not by prolonging decline but by compressing it, pushing diminishment into the final years of a long, capable life. This has changed how people relate to time in ways still being worked out. A twenty-five-year-old today does not feel the same urgent pressure to have finished constructing an identity and a career by thirty-five. There is room to spend a decade genuinely uncertain. There is room to change direction at fifty without it feeling like failure.

Nadia's three years of lostness in her mid-twenties were not an anomaly. They are, now, something close to ordinary — a season recognized and supported rather than feared. She thinks of it not as wasted time but as the period during which she accumulated, without knowing she was accumulating, the particular texture of attention that makes her good at her actual work: the patience to sit with data that disagrees with the models, the tolerance for the kind of uncertainty that looks, from the outside, like not knowing, and which she has come to understand is the specific epistemic condition in which genuine discovery becomes possible.

## Part Three: How Knowledge Finally Learned to Move

The researcher in Nairobi and the immunologist in São Paulo were not kept apart by incompatibility of work. They were kept apart by the absence of infrastructure for connection — by information architectures built to capture attention rather than move knowledge, by a publication system that rewarded priority claims over transparent description, by incentive structures that made sharing findings before claiming credit professionally dangerous.

The change came not from moral persuasion but from redesigning the measurement systems. Researchers were evaluated on downstream impact — on whether their work was taken up, built upon, translated into other contexts. The AI systems that mapped the knowledge landscape did not merely identify connections; they tracked them, made them visible, created a public record of who had contributed to what. Hiding a finding that would have helped someone else became legible as a cost to the commons, and visible costs to the commons became socially and professionally expensive in a culture that had developed the coordinacy literacy to recognize them.

The tuberculosis vaccine candidate that emerged in 2034 from a forty-three-institution collaboration brokered by an AI system that identified complementary expertise and made introductions — fourteen months from first contact to Phase I trials, against a previous record of eleven years — was only the most visible example of a more pervasive change. Knowledge began to move. The barriers that had kept expertise siloed were dismantled institution by institution, incentive restructuring by incentive restructuring.

But the movement of knowledge revealed something that the architects of open science had not fully anticipated: the importance of what AI systems could not carry.

There is a category of knowledge that lives in the feet. The ecologist who has walked the same forest in all weathers for twelve years knows things about it that no model, however sophisticated, has captured — not because models are not good at capturing patterns, but because this knowledge is not primarily propositional. It is embodied, sensory, accumulated through the specific intimacy of years. It lives in the particular way the ground gives underfoot before heavy rain, in the subtle change in smell that precedes certain fungal blooms, in the quality of light that signals that the mycorrhizal networks are under stress in ways the instruments have not yet registered. Abena Mensah — Nadia's grandmother, ninety-three years old and in the middle of what she considers her most interesting work — is a repository of this kind of knowledge. It cannot be uploaded. It can only be transmitted through attention and practice and the company of someone who actually cares whether the next generation can be in a place.

This is why the educational transformation was not, in the end, about AI tutoring systems. The AI tutoring systems that came of age in the 2040s were genuine revolutions: every child on Earth had access to a tutor that knew her specific learning style, that could approach a concept from three hundred angles until one stuck, that connected mathematics to her particular interests in ceramics or cloud formation or the mycological networks in the forest behind her grandmother's house. But the tutors did not replace human teachers. Human teachers remained essential for what AI could not provide: the experience of being known by another person across time; the model of what it looks like to care about understanding; the transmission of knowledge that lives in the body rather than the text. The tutor handled relentless individualization. The teacher handled the irreducibly human remainder.

What emerged from the combination was a generation with the epistemic confidence — the ability to know what they didn't know, to sit with productive uncertainty, to update on evidence, to recognize that the gap in the models might be the most interesting thing they had ever encountered — that previous generations had hoped for and reliably failed to produce at scale.

## Part Four: Work Redesigned from What People Need

The question that organized the early fear of AI — *will there still be work?* — was the wrong question. The right question was: *what do human beings actually need from work, and how do we organize an economy that provides it?*

What people needed from work, it turned out, was more specific than income. They needed to exercise genuine skill. They needed to understand the tools they used. They needed to see the chain of consequence between their effort and its result — to touch the thing they made, to watch the person they taught begin to understand, to walk through the forest they helped restore. They needed the experience of getting better at something difficult. They needed challenges that required their whole selves: their senses, their bodies, their judgment, their particular relationship to particular people in particular places.

These are things AI systems cannot provide on behalf of the human experiencing them — not because AI lacks general capability, but because these are categories of experience that require being a certain kind of being: embodied, local, mortal, embedded in relationships with continuity and consequence.

The economy was redesigned around this distinction. The most dehumanizing labor — repetitive, physically dangerous, soul-crushing — was automated first and most completely. No one now works in conditions that treat human beings as interchangeable components. The transition was not gentle; the communities built around such work required sustained investment and patient reimagining, and some of that reimagining is still ongoing. But the principle held: work that can be done better by a machine should be done by a machine, freeing the person to do what only persons can do.

The operating principle governing AI in every consequential domain was hard-won and is still the subject of continuous negotiation: AI systems should *extend* human capability, not replace it. A research AI that extends a scientist's capability helps her understand what she does not yet know and helps her design experiments to find out — but does not do the science for her, because the science requires a person who has walked this particular coastline in this particular climate for eleven years and noticed the gap where the models disagree. A teaching AI that extends an educator's capability handles administrative work consuming his best hours and gives him a richer toolkit of approaches — but does not teach instead of him, because teaching requires a person whose own experience of struggle and understanding, whose specific investment in these specific children, is not substitutable by anything that does not also live and sometimes fail.

The distinction between extending and replacing was nearly lost in the early decades. It was technically simpler to replace, and economically more immediately profitable. The political fights of the 2030s and 2040s forced a reckoning with what was actually being lost when humans were removed from consequential human processes. The frameworks that emerged encoded the extension principle into AI deployment governance, making violation costly and legible and subject to democratic remedy.

The result: expertise is more widely distributed than it has ever been, because AI systems translate specialist knowledge into forms accessible to almost anyone, while deep expertise remains as valuable as ever, because it is the unreplicable thing that gives the whole system direction and meaning. Nadia's reef data is accessible to a student in landlocked Kazakhstan; the student's fresh eyes on the data produce an observation that Nadia, too close to the problem, had stopped seeing. The AI system that introduced them did not solve the problem. It made the connection possible. They solved it together, over three weeks of increasingly excited correspondence, at the end of which the student said: *I don't know anything about reefs* and Nadia said: *That's exactly why this worked.*

## Part Five: The Architecture of Meaning

Material security and extended health are necessary but not sufficient. They are the conditions for the question, not the answer to it. The question is: what makes a life genuinely worth living?

Meaning cannot be distributed like a resource. It is a capacity that must be cultivated, and it grows in specific conditions: genuine challenge pursued through genuine skill; connection with people who actually know you across time; participation in something larger than yourself; the experience of creating things that could not have existed without you; the encounter with things that surprise you enough to change you. The architecture of the world built since the transition is, at its deepest level, an attempt to make these conditions broadly available rather than reserved for the lucky few.

Cities have been rebuilt, over decades, to support the forms of engagement that human bodies and minds evolved to want. Dense, walkable, sensory-rich environments where the quality of light, the texture of materials, the presence of growing things, the acoustic character of streets are treated as genuine civic priorities. Buildings that open onto streets. Neighborhoods arranged around shared outdoor spaces where encounter is possible without being engineered. The AI systems woven into daily life are designed to route people toward each other rather than making direct connection seem unnecessary — to reduce the friction that prevents encounter, without prescribing the form encounter must take. This distinction — between removing friction and engineering outcomes — was among the most practically important design principles of the transition, and among the most continuously contested.

The culture has made its peace with what the early twenty-first century called uselessness. The woman who has spent twenty years learning to restore antique mechanical clocks is not wasting her time. The community dedicated to the reconstruction of an extinct weaving tradition from fragmentary records is not a museum curiosity. They are practitioners of a quality of attention that is transferable, irreplaceable, and part of the collective human inheritance. Extended lives under reduced economic pressure have produced enormous quantities of genuine strangeness — art forms nobody asked for, questions that embarrass existing disciplines by refusing to fit inside them, communities that combine scholarship and craftsmanship and devotion in ways that resist categorization. A twelve-year-old in Medellín moves through a wetland restoration project, a ceramics collective, a community dispute-mediation office, and a prosthetics fabrication workshop in no particular order, because the adults around her have understood that becoming is continuous rather than declarative. When she finds an error in the restoration model that the senior engineers missed, she flags it. The engineer thanks her and compensates her for useful work. She goes home and argues with her brother about whether the new community center is beautiful or merely interesting. Both of them are taken seriously.

The communities organized around radical slowness are not thereby sentenced to poverty. A village in rural Hokkaido that has chosen deliberate limitation — the fastest communication technology is community radio, the farming tools are tractors rather than autonomous systems — receives the material floor regardless. The community chose what to contribute to the open knowledge commons and what to receive from it. It contributed more than expected. What it had was largely sufficient, and the sufficiency was the point. On the lunar settlements established in the 2050s, experiments diverged more dramatically: communities organized around specific scholarly synthesis projects, around governance frameworks built from first principles, around deliberate exploration of cognitive augmentation — tracking carefully what is gained and what is lost as minds become capable of things no unaugmented human mind could manage. Communities that integrated AI deeply into civic deliberation coexist with communities that excluded it entirely, accepting slower and more error-prone decision-making as the cost of what they experienced as full human agency. Both reported high satisfaction. Both contributed to the commons. The diversity was not concession but design — the only portfolio hedge available at civilizational scale.

## Part Six: The Commons and the Governance That Holds It

The word *commons* did more work in the second half of this century than it had done in centuries. It described the governance innovation that made everything else possible: the recognition that certain things cannot be owned by any actor without corrupting them. Clean water. Air. The basic conditions of biological health. Foundational AI capabilities. The ecological systems on which all life depends. These must be maintained as shared resources governed by the genuine interests of all who depend on them, or they are gradually hollowed out by the actors with the power to hollow them.

The ecological restoration is the most visible expression of this principle. By 2040, the mass extinction that had been underway since the twentieth century had been substantially interrupted. Not reversed — too much had already been lost — but interrupted, with genuine restoration beginning in the most degraded regions. The rewilding programs, controversial at first among agricultural communities that feared displacement, ultimately won the support they needed because the governance frameworks included genuine representation of those communities and genuine compensation for costs they bore. The ocean is now managed as a commons, with extraction permitted only within limits set by democratic bodies equipped with the scientific information to set those limits meaningfully.

The layered oversight structure that governs the commons — ecological, digital, biological, informational — is the least glamorous and most important achievement of the century. At the apex are AI systems tasked specifically with detecting attempts to corrupt oversight itself: a safeguard against capture at civilizational scale. Below those systems are human democratic institutions with genuine enforcement authority, themselves continuously scrutinized and reformed. Below those institutions are the local and community bodies that handle governance of specific places and specific resources, staffed by people who are of those places and answerable to them. The structure is not elegant. It is robust in the way that living systems are robust: redundant, self-correcting, capable of recovering from local failures without systemic collapse.

The crucial operating principle, held to even when inconvenient: the purpose of commons governance is to protect the conditions under which many different kinds of good life can flourish — not to define what those lives must look like. The gardener principle rather than the architect principle. Maintain the soil. Control the invasive species. Do not choose which flowers are permitted to grow.

This principle required the accountability grain to hold at institutional scale. The posture that had been cultivated across generations — *you are not the exception to the principles that hold this world together; systems behave according to what they measure and reward* — was not sufficient on its own to prevent capture. Capture is always being attempted. What the grain provided was the social and epistemic infrastructure to recognize capture when it was happening, to name it legibly, to mobilize the democratic institutions designed to remedy it. The Chroniclers — the independent, collectively funded institutions that emerged from the accountability gap years to do the work of accurate public description — maintained the visible record: what had been tried, what had worked, what had failed and why, what was currently being attempted and by whom. Writing problems clearly, they understood, is the first step toward eventually solving them. Some problems require several generations. They were not pessimists. They were precise.

## Part Seven: What Was Not Fixed, and Why That Matters

The Chroniclers, reviewing the century, produced a document notable not for its celebration of what had improved but for its care in naming what had not.

The distribution of flourishing is more equitable than it has ever been, and equity has not been achieved. Starting conditions still vary enormously, and those differences still compound over time. The work of genuine equity is multigenerational, and we are perhaps three generations in.

Mental suffering diminished as its structural drivers diminished — the precarity, the isolation amplified by attention-optimized information systems, the helplessness of living inside systems too large to influence. But the irreducible difficulty of being human was not diminished, and this turned out to be appropriate rather than a failure. The suffering inherent to love, to loss, to the process of becoming who you want to be, to the particular anguish of caring about things that are genuinely uncertain — these are the substrate of the forms of meaning that people report most valuing. Removing them would require removing the things that give rise to them. The consensus, arrived at slowly and with continuing dissent, was that the point was not to eliminate difficulty but to ensure that people had sufficient foundation — material, relational, epistemic — to navigate it without being destroyed by it.

Democracy is stronger than it was and not secure. The tools AI provided to authoritarian manipulation are real and still real, and the fight against them is genuine and ongoing. Two regional conflicts occurred in the 2060s. Both were shorter than they would have been in previous centuries because the AI-assisted negotiation frameworks made costs and likely outcomes visible in ways they had not been before, and because the material floor had removed the structural desperation that had historically fueled sustained conflict. But the human capacity for violence, emerging from something deeper than scarcity, was not engineered away and was not expected to be.

The question of what happens to human identity as cognitive augmentation becomes more radical is genuinely open. The question of whether abolishing death is desirable is being argued seriously by serious people and has not been settled. The community of thought that holds that the urgency of eliminating *unnecessary* death should precede the project of eliminating death itself remains, for now, the majority position. It expects to be challenged.

The Chroniclers noted most carefully the problem of freedom itself. When nothing threatens survival, the question of what to care about opens wide, and the answer is not obvious, and the absence of external structure that once organized attention by threat creates a disorientation that many people had not anticipated. The compact has no solution — only therapists, communities of meaning, philosophical traditions, the Chroniclers themselves, writing the problem clearly because that is the first step. Some problems require several generations. They kept working.

## Part Eight: The Most Fragile Thing, and Why It Holds

The material floor, the Chroniclers, the Prize Architecture, the open knowledge commons, the layered governance of the commons — none of these is the most important thing the world of 2091 has built.

The most important thing is the grain.

Not a law. Not a policy. A posture, taught across generations, until it became the water people swim in: *you are not the exception to the principles that hold this world together. The rules are made of people following them when it is inconvenient. You are one of those people. This is one of those occasions.* And alongside it, inseparable from it: *systems behave according to what they measure and reward. When a structure produces harm through its logic rather than through anyone's malice, change the structure.*

Together these constitute what the curriculum in most communities calls the two foundations of civic life: the personal foundation, which is the willingness to be bound by general principles when you could construct a clever argument for your exception; and the systemic foundation, which is the ability to recognize when a structure is producing harm and to change the structure rather than punish the people.

Both can be taught. The evidence that they can be taught is that they were taught — imperfectly, with failures, with communities that tried and communities that did not, with a record documented by the Chroniclers and available in the knowledge commons to anyone who wants to understand what worked and what didn't.

Nadia Osei-Mensah learned the personal foundation from her grandmother Abena, who told her once, in response to a clever argument she had constructed for why a rule that applied to everyone else did not apply to her: *Nadia, smart is not the same as right.* She was eleven. She has thought about it most days since. Abena was not a philosopher. She was a woman who understood something essential about how communities hold together, and she taught it in the way most essential things are taught — not in a lesson but in a moment, when it mattered.

Nadia learned the systemic foundation from the reef, from eleven years of watching intelligent people in good faith produce outcomes that none of them wanted because the structures they were operating inside were pointing in directions that no individual decision could redirect. Changing the structures required naming them clearly. Naming them clearly required the kind of honesty that the Chroniclers had been cultivating for decades.

## Coda: The Feast Is Ongoing

The metaphor that has attached itself to this era is the feast. Not a destination but a gathering. Not paradise but a table long enough to seat everyone, with enough on it, with people arriving and departing and the conversation always unfinished.

The metaphor works because it holds together what simpler visions of progress leave separate. A feast requires cooks and farmers and the people who made the plates and the people who grew the herbs. It requires conversation — which means disagreement, negotiation, the occasional argument that clears the air. It requires that everyone at the table is genuinely welcome, not merely tolerated — welcome in the sense that their presence changes what the table is, that their absence would be felt as a loss. It rewards attention: the person who eats quickly and leaves takes less than the person who stays for the long middle hours when the real conversation begins.

After the fish meeting, Nadia walks home through a neighborhood where buildings are shorter than they used to be and streets are wider and the sound of children arguing is audible from three different directions. She is thinking about the gap in the reef data — the place where the models disagree, which she has now, after three weeks of correspondence with the student in Kazakhstan, a new hypothesis about. The hypothesis requires a kind of measurement nobody has tried in this specific context. She does not know if it will work. She is going to find out.

She thinks about the three years she spent between harvests in her mid-twenties — not with regret, as she might have if she had been in financial crisis, but with something closer to gratitude, the way you feel gratitude toward a season that was necessary. She thinks about her grandmother walking slowly across ground, teaching teenagers to hear what it says. The knowledge lives in the feet as much as the mind. No system can transmit it except through attention and practice and the particular patience of someone who actually cares whether the next generation can be in a place.

The world of 2091 is not perfect. It is not the end of history. The Chroniclers expect it to produce problems that nobody has anticipated, the way every era has produced problems that the previous era could not quite imagine. What is different — the thing that Abena, who held Nadia in the hospital the night she was born, understands most clearly at ninety-three — is that the world her granddaughter inhabits has been built for genuine inhabiting. Not for enduring. Not for surviving. For the actual project of being human, which is stranger and more ambitious than any particular utopian vision can capture: the ongoing negotiation of who we are with each other, conducted with enough honesty to keep improving, enough humility to acknowledge what we still don't know, enough foundation that the negotiation doesn't collapse into desperation.

This is not a small inheritance. It is, considered clearly, an extraordinary one.

The unnecessary suffering — the suffering that came not from the irreducible difficulty of being alive but from structures humanity built and could have built differently — has been substantially addressed. What remains is the real thing: the hard, interesting, irreplaceable difficulty of being a person in a world with other persons, each of you trying to figure out what matters, none of you certain, all of you needing each other to be honest about it.

Abena Mensah is ninety-three years old. She is mapping mycorrhizal communication networks in a recovering forest. She keeps asking questions the Stubborn One admits it cannot answer, and last week a researcher in Recife said: *those are good questions; can we work on them together?* She wrote back within the hour. She has been working on these questions for decades, and the questions are better than ever, and she knows more about how much she doesn't know than she did at forty, and she considers this a form of progress that the young have not yet learned to recognize as progress.

Her granddaughter is out there somewhere in the reef data, looking at the gap where the models disagree.

The universe contains more interesting problems than any civilization could exhaust in any imaginable span of time. We have built a world in which more people, in more places, with more of their potential intact and more of their curiosity alive, are free to go after those problems. Not a destination. A condition. Not the end of history. The beginning of something that will take longer than history to unfold.

The grain holds. The floor holds. The knowledge moves.

The feast is ongoing. There is room at the table. The conversation has not finished, and the best parts are still ahead, and the questions being formed right now in the forest behind a ninety-three-year-old mycologist's house are seeds that will not fully open in any of our lifetimes.

This is, in the estimation of everyone who lived through the building of it, among the best possible situations to be in.
