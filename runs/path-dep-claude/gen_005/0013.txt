# The Inhabited World: A Vision of 2120

---

## Prologue: Three Things in a Room

There is a room in the restored medina of Tunis where three things hang on adjacent walls.

The first is a photograph from 2029: a crowded emergency ward, staff visibly exhausted, faces masked, a queue extending into a corridor lit by failing fluorescent tubes. The second is a graph showing a single descending line—the global rate of preventable death from 1990 to 2100, falling like a slow exhalation. The third is a hand-stitched textile made by a community in the Atlas Mountains that declined, as a matter of deliberate collective choice, to integrate most of what the rest of the world calls progress. It shows a cedar forest, a river, three generations of women around a loom.

Visitors tend to stand longest in front of the textile.

When asked why, most say something like: *because it reminds me there were always many ways through.* Then they go quiet, because the word *through* implies there was something to get through, and most people alive in 2120 were born after the worst of it. They carry knowledge of what came before the way you carry knowledge of a disease you were vaccinated against—abstractly, gratefully, without full imaginative access to what it actually felt like.

But a fourth thing was added to this room in 2098, below the other three, installed by a coalition of schoolchildren from six cities who had spent a year corresponding about what the room was missing. It is a map: the watershed commons that straddles two national territories in the mountains visible from the medina on a clear day. The map is hand-drawn but accurate. It is updated annually by the communities who live there.

The children's reasoning, set down in a brief statement on the placard beneath the map, was this: the photograph shows what was possible to tolerate; the graph shows what was possible to change; the textile shows what was possible to keep; the map shows that the work of keeping and changing is never finished, and that it is done together, on a specific piece of ground, by specific people who know its particular river.

This is an attempt to describe the world all four of those things together represent.

## Part One: What Had to Happen First

The crisis years of the 2030s were the crucible, and it is worth being honest about them before describing what came after.

Labor displacement arrived faster than adaptation could follow. The geopolitical fracture over AI governance came close enough to catastrophe that people who lived through it still do not like to discuss the details. A global recession compressed a decade of structural adjustment into three ferocious years. Democratic institutions, already stressed, were tested in ways their designers had not anticipated, and some failed the test in ways that took a generation to repair.

What held, where it held, was not a single heroic decision but the accumulated weight of small institutional choices: scientists who maintained shared standards of evidence under enormous pressure to abandon them, civil servants who showed up to unglamorous work through years when the work seemed futile, international bodies individually weak but collectively load-bearing, networks of local organizations that kept relationships alive across the fractures. The thread was thin. It did not break.

The great failure of the early twenty-first century, historians now agree, was not a failure of knowledge. The knowledge existed. In 2025, humanity possessed the scientific understanding to prevent most of the deaths that were occurring, the agricultural techniques to feed everyone, the engineering capacity to build clean energy at scale, and—in fragments, distributed across disciplines that rarely spoke to each other—the governance philosophy to organize all of it. What it lacked was the infrastructure to connect these fragments before they decayed into obsolescence, were buried by publication volume, or simply failed to find the minds that could have used them.

The image that historians reach for is of a vast library in which every book had been placed on the wrong shelf, or in the wrong building, or locked behind a door whose key had been thrown into a different ocean. The knowledge was there. The connections were not.

The AI systems that matured through the 2030s and 2040s did not solve this problem by being smarter than humans. They solved it by being indefatigable indexers, patient bridge-builders, tireless translators between disciplines that had developed mutually incomprehensible vocabularies while working on adjacent problems. A materials scientist studying thermal properties for battery storage turned out to have been generating data directly relevant to a team of engineers in Dhaka trying to reduce urban heat island effects. The connection, once made, took three months to produce a working prototype. It had been waiting, unmade, for eleven years.

This story repeated itself, in different forms, across every domain that mattered. Not genius. Coordination. It turned out to be the rate-limiting step in almost everything.

The governance of these AI systems was the great political crisis of the 2030s. The frameworks that emerged—after a decade of civilizational improvisation under pressure, supported by a generation of people who had read the warning literature and taken it seriously—were imperfect in every detail and adequate in their overall shape. Systems were auditable. Oversight was real, not performative. Human oversight of consequential decisions was preserved not as a polite fiction but as a working requirement enforced by institutions with actual authority. Capture by any single national or corporate interest was, after brutal institutional fighting, prevented. No single entity controlled the connective infrastructure.

The infrastructure was commons.

That choice—commons, not empire—was the hinge. Everything else turned on it.

The economic renegotiation was similarly imperfect and similarly decisive. A universal floor—guaranteed housing, food, healthcare, education—was fought for, compromised, implemented unevenly, and extended across two decades of incremental legislation. It was funded by productivity taxes that made the gains from automation visible and transferable, and by wealth restructuring that redistributed what the previous era had concentrated. The version that emerged was not anyone's ideal. It was enough. The margin it created—the space between survival and catastrophe placed under every person's feet—made the rest possible.

The energy transition crossed decisive thresholds in the late 2030s when solar, storage, and advanced geothermal reached cost floors that made the oldest structural basis for resource conflict—the belief that your energy security came at someone else's expense—lose its material foundation. Desalination became routine infrastructure in water-scarce regions. The downstream effects compounded quietly for decades, and what they compounded into was a world where the basic conditions of human dignity could be upheld everywhere without requiring extraction from anyone.

## Part Two: The Material Floor

By 2070, humanity had done something it had never previously managed: it had made the basics non-negotiable.

Not uniform. Not centralized. Not through a world government or a benevolent hegemon. Through what political theorists of the period called a *distributed guarantee*—a layered architecture of local systems, regional compacts, and global backstops, all accountable to elected bodies and auditable by ordinary citizens, that collectively ensured clean water, adequate nutrition, basic medical care, and physical shelter for every human being on Earth, regardless of which community they lived in or what relationship that community had chosen to have with advanced technology.

Medicine participated in this transformation through a restructuring of incentives that proved, in retrospect, both inevitable and extraordinarily delayed. The Prize Architecture—adopted first by a coalition of governments in 2038 and spreading through regional compacts over the following twenty years—replaced patent monopoly with outcome-based rewards: reductions in disease burden, measured across populations, paid out over time. AI systems tracked and modeled these outcomes continuously. The effect was to reorient pharmaceutical research, for the first time in its history, genuinely toward the diseases that killed the most people rather than the diseases of the most profitable patients.

The diseases that had operated for centuries on the bodies of the poor—malaria, tuberculosis, cervical cancer—were dismantled not by heroism but by aligned incentives and computational reach. A device that could diagnose cervical cancer from a phone-sized scanner, trained on tissue samples from every skin tone and anatomical variation, calibrated to local disease variants: this device exists. It has existed, in various iterations, since 2052. The woman whose sister died of preventable cervical cancer in Lagos in 2031 was part of the team that pressure-tested its training data. She spent six months in mobile clinics in three countries before she let herself cry about it. Her name appears in the acknowledgments of the validation study, which has now been cited more than forty thousand times. She is not famous. This is correct.

By 2085, chronic malnutrition had been eliminated the way that good plumbing eliminates cholera: gradually, structurally, almost invisibly, until the absence of catastrophe became the background condition of ordinary life. The people most responsible were not generals or founders. They were people who reformed subsidy structures, redesigned logistics networks, built early warning systems for distribution failures, and showed up at the same policy table for thirty years. They were often bored. Their boredom saved millions of lives.

## Part Three: The Architecture of Many Ceilings

The governance structures that emerged from the long crisis were neither world government nor fragmented corporate feudalism. They were something genuinely new: a layered commonwealth of voluntary spheres, organized around the principle that the guaranteed floor was universal but the ceiling was deliberately, intentionally, diverse.

The foundational insight had been available for centuries but had never before been applied with adequate material support: that human communities are genuinely different from one another, with genuinely different visions of the good life, and that this diversity is not a problem requiring a solution but a richness requiring a structure. The question was how to honor it without allowing it to become cover for exploitation—without letting the rhetoric of cultural distinctness become a shield behind which the powerful harmed the vulnerable.

The answer that emerged, through decades of argument and failure and revision, was this: communities could organize themselves as they chose above the floor. They could integrate AI into every aspect of civic governance or exclude it entirely. They could pursue radical longevity research or decline such interventions. They could build densely networked urban environments or deliberately slow villages where the fastest communication tool was a radio. They could organize their economies through markets, commons, reciprocity networks, or combinations that would have been unrecognizable to twentieth-century economists. What they could not do was harm each other's commons, violate the floor for their own members, or expand their footprint into another community's territory without consent.

What neither utopian nor dystopian imagination had quite predicted was how genuinely interesting the coexistence proved to be. The theoretical prediction had been that communities with advanced technology would inevitably dominate or absorb those without it. What actually happened was more complicated. Some less-technological communities integrated new tools on their own timelines. Others discovered that the floor gave them the security to insist on their distinctness in ways that poverty had previously made impossible. And the flow of influence turned out to be genuinely bidirectional. The slower communities produced art, social knowledge, and relational wisdom that the faster communities found themselves hungry for and could not synthesize on their own.

The watershed commons is the emblem of this arrangement. In the mountains visible from the medina on a clear day, a governing compact straddles two national territories and several distinct communities: a hyper-connected research settlement, a farming region that has maintained older agricultural rhythms by deliberate choice, a semi-nomadic pastoral community, and a small city of eighty thousand with a mixed economy. These communities disagree about almost everything above the floor. They share water, which makes the disagreements consequential, which makes the commons real. Their governing body—the Watershed Council—is one of several hundred such bodies that have emerged as a novel tier of governance between the local and the national, addressing problems that are precisely wrong for either scale.

The Council is not a utopia in miniature. It contains genuine conflict. It has made bad decisions and revised them. A dispute over a proposed dam intervention in 2097 produced the most acrimonious argument in the commons' forty-year history and was resolved through a deliberative process that took eleven months and left several members feeling that the outcome was second-best. The dam was not built. Five years later, monitoring data suggested this was the right call. Three of the dissenting members acknowledged this, publicly, at a Council session, which was recorded and is now used in governance training curricula across four continents—not because it was heroic, but because the willingness to update publicly on evidence, in an institutional setting, turned out to be a learnable and teachable civic skill.

## Part Four: What Knowledge Became

In every city of significant size, there is a building that would have been recognizable to a medieval person and baffling to someone from 2025.

Recognizable because it is large, public, architecturally ambitious, and devoted to the collective life of the mind. Baffling because of what happens inside it: open research centers where the formal and informal production of knowledge are understood as aspects of a single activity, where the walls between disciplines have been replaced by gradients, where career incentives that once made scientists cautious and secretive have been replaced by incentives that reward replication, synthesis, the courage to investigate unfashionable questions, and the publication of negative results.

These institutions are public-facing in a way their predecessors were not. Researchers are expected to report back to the communities whose taxes fund and whose lives are affected by their work. Open-access infrastructure made paywall models legally untenable by 2055. Education systems teach children how evidence is made and why it matters. Knowledge is understood as belonging to the people who live under its consequences.

But the most important epistemic change was not institutional. It was cultural, and it was slow.

The information environment of 2025 had been vast, cheap to populate, and structured by incentives that rewarded engagement over accuracy and outrage over understanding. The renovation of this environment was not a single reform but the accumulated effect of interventions that each seemed modest in isolation: rationality education that improved through iteration until calibrated reasoning and the recognition of cognitive bias were taught in most school systems with the mundane confidence that arithmetic had once been taught; regulated forecasting infrastructure embedded into governance processes; AI tutoring systems that gave every child a patient, personalized guide; and a gradual restructuring of the media environment as the business models that had rewarded epistemic pollution stopped being profitable.

The result was not a world without disagreement. It was a world where disagreement was more often productive—where the difference between an argument worth having and a status performance was more commonly recognized.

Once a year in every community, there is a gathering that visitors tend to remember longest. Over three days, people come together to share what they have learned. No credentials are checked at the door. A farmer who has spent twenty years watching how a particular estuary responds to seasonal change speaks about what she has observed. A mechanic who has rebuilt the same engine four hundred times speaks about what he has noticed that no manual records. A historian and an AI researcher and a ninety-year-old gardener sit at the same long table, argue, eat, and discover they have been investigating the same question from different angles without knowing it. Hydrologist Asel, who crosses the border at dawn on working days to monitor the watershed commons, has attended this gathering every year for fifteen years. She says it is where she does her best thinking, and she is not being polite.

The AI tutoring systems that transformed formal schooling were initially modest improvements. The compounding of marginal gains over decades of deployment and refinement produced something that looked, from the outside, like magic. A child in rural Kazakhstan has access to a tutor that knows her learning style in finer grain than any human teacher could manage alone, that can approach the same concept from hundreds of different angles until one works, that connects a lesson in algebra to her specific interest in the patterns of the textile trade she watches her grandmother navigate. Her human teachers remain essential—for mentorship, for modeling, for the irreducibly human experience of being genuinely seen by another person—but freed from relentless administrative labor and one-size-fits-all delivery, they have time, for the first time in the history of mass education, to actually teach.

## Part Five: The Texture of Daily Life

Begin with sleep.

People sleep enough. This seems a small thing until you trace its consequences: the emotional regulation that becomes possible with adequate rest, the quality of attention people can give each other, the reduction in the small cruelties that chronic exhaustion produces in human interaction. A civilization that takes the biological reality of human bodies seriously looks, at first glance, like a civilization that has made a series of minor adjustments. At second glance it looks like a different kind of place entirely.

Cities were redesigned, over several generations of contested effort, to make the natural world present within them—not as decoration but as ecology, with functioning wetlands and canopy corridors and urban farms that produce a meaningful fraction of local food while teaching their neighborhoods about seasons and soil. The outdoor spaces that resulted became, as social infrastructure research had long predicted, the soil in which something essential grows: repeated low-stakes contact between strangers, the accumulated texture of shared life, the slow conversion of neighbors into something approaching community.

The loneliness crisis that had reached epidemic proportions in the 2020s was addressed as what it was: a structural problem requiring structural solutions. Communities invested in social infrastructure—parks, libraries, common kitchens, workshop spaces, neighborhood councils—that create conditions for people to encounter each other without agenda. The skills of relationship were moved from the implicit curriculum into the explicit one, taught with the same rigor as mathematics, starting young, because the evidence that they were teachable had accumulated beyond reasonable dispute.

Children are raised by communities as well as families. The nuclear unit, isolated in a house, responsible for everything, connected to nothing—recognized as a historical aberration that served almost no one well—gave way to networks of care with many more nodes. The mechanic who knows every child on the street by name; the retired ecologist who takes neighborhood children to the constructed wetland on Saturday mornings; the common kitchen where three generations eat together without planning to: these are not programs. They are the ordinary texture of life in a place designed for it.

The land value taxation reforms that began as a cantonal experiment in Switzerland in 2038 and spread to national frameworks over the following decades made cities more affordable than those of the previous century and better funded than those of the century before that. These reforms compounded: funding the next round of public investment, which funded the next, which funded the construction of the common kitchens and the wetland corridors and the public buildings of genuine beauty that people now take for granted and could not imagine losing, the way previous generations could not imagine losing libraries.

Mental health improved dramatically once the structural drivers—economic precarity, social isolation, environmental degradation, political helplessness, the epistemic chaos of a poisoned information environment—were substantially addressed. The remaining psychological suffering is genuine and taken seriously, not minimized by comparison to the past. The tools for addressing it are better than they have ever been, and improving. What has changed most is not the tools but the conditions: people have more time, more security, more genuine community, more sense that their choices matter and that the world they live in is one they helped build.

## Part Six: What Work Became

The labor displacement of the 2030s and 2040s was severe and its social disruption was real. It was met, after a decade of political crisis, with the guaranteed floor and a policy framework that provided housing, food, healthcare, and education regardless of employment status. It took a generation to feel normal. It did feel normal.

What replaced the old economy was harder to name than economists expected. People still work—often more hours than before, because the work has become genuinely theirs. But the character has changed. The tasks that remain are precisely those requiring what human minds are specifically suited for: contextual judgment in novel situations, genuine creativity, the navigation of human relationships, the understanding of what another person actually needs. The surgeon no longer documents her procedures—AI handles that—so she thinks more carefully about the surgery. The social worker no longer processes eligibility forms—AI handles that—so she is present, actually present, in the room with the family she is trying to help.

But something else happened too, something the economists had not predicted. With the material floor secured and the hours of obligatory labor reduced, people organized their time around work that was meaningful rather than simply remunerative. Not leisure—or not only leisure. Communities undertook large, slow projects: restoring watersheds, building public buildings of genuine beauty, developing civic rituals, composing music, caring for each other's children and elderly and sick with a quality of attention that scarcity had always foreclosed.

The care economy—the work of people attending to other people, which had always been the most essential work and had been systematically undervalued—was finally compensated commensurately, because the material conditions made it affordable and because the political will had been, at enormous effort over long decades, built. The result was not that people worked less. It was that they worked on different things, with more of themselves, and more often on things they had chosen.

The person who builds furniture for twenty years and then studies marine biology and then helps raise grandchildren and then writes a book that reflects on all of it is not unusual. She is the modal case. This became possible not through radical life extension, which remains contested and uneven across communities, but through the restructuring of education and work to accommodate the full span of a long and changing human life: education systems designed for return students at any age, professional networks that do not penalize reinvention, social institutions built for people who expect to want more than one life within the one they have.

## Part Seven: On Power and Its Arrangements

This world is not without hierarchy, but its hierarchies are multiple, overlapping, and contextually specific. Status in one domain does not automatically purchase authority in others. The person respected for scientific expertise is not thereby entitled to dominate conversations about neighborhood design. The elder honored for decades of community service is not thereby exempt from contradiction by someone half her age who has studied the question more carefully.

Governance matches decision-making scale to problem scale with a reliability that took generations of contested institutional evolution to develop. Climate requires global coordination; watershed management requires watershed governance; the education of a particular child requires the particular people who know that child. Political participation is not primarily electoral—elections happen and matter, but the more continuous form of participation runs through dense networks of local councils and deliberative assemblies that handle the texture of daily governance. Most people serve on something. Most people, over a lifetime, take a turn at the tedious and necessary work of making collective decisions, which makes them simultaneously more patient with its difficulty and less willing to abandon it to others.

The AI governance frameworks that hold this world together are imperfect and permanently contested. The questions about what systems can decide autonomously, under what conditions, with what oversight, are not settled—they are the subject of ongoing deliberation by bodies with genuine independence from state and corporate capture alike. What was preserved, through the crisis decade and its aftermath, was human plurality: a diversity of approaches, cultures, and ways of life that no optimization toward a single global framework was permitted to erase. This commitment was partly philosophical—different ways of being human have irreplaceable value—and partly epistemic—no single framework is right about everything, and error-correction requires variation.

The result is a world more interconnected than any before it, and not homogenized: a global commons supporting an ecosystem rather than a monoculture. The argument about where the line between local autonomy and global obligation should be drawn is not a dysfunction to be resolved. It is the process by which the line remains responsive to what people actually need.

## Part Eight: What Remains Open

This is not a finished world. It would be an insult to its inhabitants to suggest otherwise, and they would be the first to object.

People still die—later, better, but they still die. The cellular aging research that emerged from the same Prize Architecture that transformed cancer treatment has rewritten what eighty means, what a hundred means, what it means to plan a life. Most people in 2120 expect to remain cognitively and physically capable into their late nineties, with interventions available that some communities embrace and others decline. This has opened genuine moral questions—about resource distribution across extended lifespans, about what late life should be for, about what is lost as well as gained when death recedes—that no amount of longevity research can settle. Different communities have reached different provisional answers. The global commons supports and does not adjudicate this diversity.

People still conflict. The world of 2120 is less at war than any previous era in recorded history, and the conflicts that occur tend to be shorter and smaller than before. This is not nothing. It is not enough.

And the question of what it means to live a good life—which no amount of institutional reform or material sufficiency can answer—remains as genuinely open as it ever was. Perhaps more so. A world with less poverty, less disease, less institutional dysfunction is also a world in which the existential questions come into sharper focus, no longer blurred by urgent practical necessity. What do we owe to each other across radical differences of value? What do we owe to beings—animal, artificial, whatever comes next—whose experience we are only beginning to understand? What is the relationship between the good life and the meaningful life and the happy life, and are these the same thing?

These questions are not distractions from the project of flourishing. They are the project, encountered at a depth that scarcity never permitted.

## Part Nine: An Afternoon in the Watershed Commons

Asel finishes her morning work at the monitoring station and walks to the common kitchen that sits at the edge of the fishing village on the eastern bank of the commons. She is not from here but she has eaten here dozens of times, and the person behind the counter knows how she takes her coffee. She sits with a retired irrigation engineer and a young woman who is spending a season learning traditional water management from the community elders before returning to her engineering program.

They talk about the river for forty minutes. All three of them know different things about it. The young woman is writing down what she can. This is not unusual; it happens here most days.

What holds the conversation together is not that the people in it agree. The retired engineer thinks the commons is being too cautious about a proposed intervention; Asel thinks the caution is warranted but for reasons different from the ones the community council articulated; the young woman is trying to figure out what she thinks and asking good questions. They will not resolve this today. The formal deliberation is in three weeks, and it will involve dozens of people and probably produce a decision none of them would have arrived at individually, and that decision will be better for it, and it will be revisited in five years when new monitoring data comes in, because that is how the commons works.

This is the most ordinary kind of afternoon. It is also, in every way that matters, extraordinary.

Outside, the river moves through both territories indifferently. The mountains on the horizon are the same mountains they have always been. A heron, which does not recognize any of the governance frameworks, stands in the shallows with the patience that comes from being precisely what it is.

## Part Ten: The Shape of the Work

Back in Tunis, in the room with the four things on the walls, there is a docent named Sofiane who has given tours here for twenty years. He is sixty-seven and looks it, though sixty-seven looks different than it used to. He has degrees in history and public health and spent fifteen years working on water access in sub-Saharan Africa before returning to his grandmother's city to do something quieter.

He has a speech he gives, not from notes, that changes slightly each time. It goes roughly like this:

*The photograph shows you what was possible to tolerate. The graph shows you what was possible to change. The textile shows you what was possible to keep. The map shows you where the work is actually done.*

*People always ask which one is most important. The photograph, because without understanding what was tolerated you can't understand why the change mattered? The graph, because without the change nothing else would have been possible? The textile, because without the things worth keeping there would be nothing to build toward? The map, because without the specific place and specific people all the rest is abstraction?*

*The answer is that they require each other. The change was made by people who understood what was being tolerated and who held clearly in mind what they were trying to preserve. The preservation was only possible because the change was made. The map is specific because the textile is specific, because the river in the mountains is the same river the women in the textile are sitting beside, and the women who added the map were from the same community as the women who made the textile, and they added it because they wanted you to know that the community is still there, still deciding, still revising, still arguing about what the watershed needs and what they owe each other and what their children will find when they come to this room in fifty years.*

He pauses here. Then: *Both of those things are necessary—the vision and the specific ground. Neither of them is sufficient. The work continues.*

He has said this to thousands of people over twenty years. Most of them stand for a long moment in front of the textile before they leave. A few linger over the map. He finds these people most interesting. He asks them, when he can, what they noticed. Their answers have taught him things that aren't in any book.

## Coda: What Was Built

The path from the world of 2025 to this one was not straight, not inevitable, and not without terrible cost. It ran through a decade of disruption that tested everything. It required AI systems designed for genuine human oversight rather than around it. It required governance frameworks pluralistic enough to resist capture. It required distribution systems wide enough and fast enough to share gains before the social fabric tore. It required, above all, enough people deciding in enough places over enough time that the incremental work of making institutions somewhat better than they had been was worth doing—that problems which had always existed were not therefore problems that had to exist forever.

The deepest lesson is this: the question was never technology versus tradition, or growth versus limits, or individual versus collective. The question was always what human beings actually need in order to live well, and whether the institutions we build can be made to serve that answer honestly—not once, in a founding moment, but continuously, in the ordinary friction of shared life.

What people need turns out to include the obvious things—safety, health, food, shelter, time—and also things that are harder to measure and were therefore long neglected: genuine knowledge of and by their communities, work that uses their specifically human capacities, children raised in networks of care rather than in isolation, beauty as a public good rather than a private luxury, the slow pleasures of repeated contact with the same patch of earth and the same group of people across a whole lifetime, the right to sleep, the right to disagree productively, and the structural conditions that make all of this accessible to everyone rather than only those who were fortunate enough to be born in the right place at the right moment.

What was built was not a solution. It was a structure adequate to ongoing human diversity—a guaranteed floor beneath which no one falls, and above which ten thousand different experiments in the good life proceed simultaneously, learning from each other, arguing with each other, sometimes maddening each other, occasionally discovering in each other something they had not been able to find alone.

The cedar forest. The river. Three generations around a loom.

The watershed. The commons. The morning light still traveling across the mountains.

This is worth working for.

The work is already underway. Whether it continues depends, as it always has, on what enough of us decide to do next.

*Nine-year-old Linara, in the watershed village, has finished her tutoring session on hydrology—the same river her grandmother fishes, explained at the level of snowpack and aquifer and seasonal pulse—and has moved on to wondering about the heron she watched from the window. Whether it migrates. How it hunts. Why it stands so still. She will bring this question to her teacher tomorrow, and her teacher will know the answer to part of it and will sit with her in the not-knowing of the rest, because that is what a teacher is for. In twenty years, Linara may be the one crossing the border at dawn to monitor what the river is doing. Or she may be doing something no one has yet imagined. The commons will need people who know it well. It will also need people who see it freshly. These are not different people. They are the same person, at different moments of a long and continuing life.*
