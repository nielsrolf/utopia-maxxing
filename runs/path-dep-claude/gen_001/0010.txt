# The Long Flourishing: A Vision of 2150

*A world built slowly, wisely, and with open eyes*

---

There is a photograph taken from the roof of the new São Paulo Central Library in 2089 that has become something of an icon. In the foreground, a woman in her late sixties sits reading, her silver hair loose in the warm air. Behind her, the city stretches out in layers: the old concrete towers softened by hanging gardens, new neighborhoods threaded with canals and cycle paths, and above it all, a sky conspicuously, startlingly blue. The photograph circulated widely not because it depicted anything dramatic—no triumph, no catastrophe—but because of something in the woman's posture. She looks entirely at home in the world. Completely present. At ease in a way that, to twenty-first century eyes, read almost as alien.

That ease was earned. It was built, incrementally, across generations, through a thousand technical achievements, a thousand reformed institutions, and a million quiet decisions to do things somewhat better than they had been done before. This is the story of how the world became the kind of place where that photograph was possible.

---

## The Foundations: Energy, Food, and the End of Scarcity's Logic

The transformation began, as most transformations do, not with a single dramatic event but with a cost curve crossing a threshold.

By the 2030s, solar energy had become the cheapest source of electricity in most of the world, and a decade of aggressive grid modernization had begun translating that cheap electricity into cheap everything else. Aluminium smelting, cement production, nitrogen fixation for fertilizer—industry after energy-hungry industry restructured itself around the new price reality. But the deeper change was psychological. For most of human history, energy scarcity had been a silent background assumption in almost all resource planning, diplomacy, and economics. When that assumption dissolved, the downstream effects on how humans related to scarcity more generally took time to materialize, but they were profound.

The food system was transformed in three overlapping waves. First came precision fermentation and cultivated meat, which by the 2040s had reduced the land footprint of protein production by more than eighty percent, releasing vast acreages for rewilding and carbon drawdown. Then came a new generation of open-source, polygenic crop improvements—disease resistance, drought tolerance, yield improvements—distributed freely through a coalition of agricultural research institutions partly funded by the governments that had once spent similar sums on farm subsidies. Third came the rationalization of food distribution, enabled by better logistics software, reformed trade arrangements, and prediction markets that allowed food systems to respond to emerging shortfalls weeks before they became crises rather than months after.

By 2070, chronic malnutrition had been largely eliminated. Not through any single dramatic intervention, but because the system as a whole had become, slowly, less stupid. Fewer subsidies flowing to the wrong places. Fewer regulatory bottlenecks choking the distribution of cheap, nutritious food. Fewer coordination failures letting food rot in one region while people went hungry in another. The altruists of the early 21st century had been right that these problems were solvable; they had perhaps underestimated how long "solvable" would take to become "solved," but they had also underestimated how good it would feel when it finally happened.

## The Institutional Reformation

The political history of the late 21st century will not be written as a story of sudden revolutions. It will be written as a story of accumulating reforms, each modest in isolation, transformative in aggregate.

Prediction markets were legalized and regulated in most major jurisdictions by 2045, first for financial instruments, then gradually for policy questions. The FDA equivalent in the European Union adopted a modified approval process in 2051 in which drug efficacy was partly determined by market-aggregated probability assessments, with the agency's professional reviewers focused on safety and fraud detection rather than efficacy adjudication. The results were contentious but encouraging: approval timelines dropped, several treatments that had languished in regulatory purgatory reached patients, and a handful of high-profile failures where market confidence had proven misplaced produced genuine accountability rather than the usual dispersed blame. Over the following two decades, other jurisdictions adopted versions of the system, iterating on the failures. By 2080, forecasting infrastructure—markets, structured tournaments, institutional superforecasting teams—had become a routine part of policy development in most democracies, the way opinion polling had become routine a century before.

Georgist land value taxation spread more slowly but more durably. A small Swiss canton adopted a partial land value tax in 2038 as a budget experiment. The economic effects were sufficiently positive—falling rents, rising construction, surpluses for public investment—that neighboring jurisdictions began to take notice. By 2060, a dozen cities had adopted significant land value tax components. The reform never became universal, and it remains contested in many places, but where it took hold it reliably produced cities that were denser, more affordable, and better funded, and this accumulation of evidence gradually shifted the terms of the political debate.

Occupational licensing reform, which had seemed impossibly politically difficult in the 2020s, advanced with surprising speed once the first wave of automation rendered many licensed professions unrecognizable. When the procedural knowledge required for certain medical diagnoses could be embedded in a device a community health worker carried in their pocket, the case for requiring a decade of specialized training as a gatekeeper collapsed faster than the licensing boards could adapt. This was sometimes chaotic. It also extended effective medical care to hundreds of millions of people who had previously been priced out of it or lived too far from it.

Charter cities had a more mixed record than their proponents had hoped—several high-profile failures in the 2030s and 2040s generated understandable skepticism—but the ones that succeeded became important laboratories. The city of Nusantara, in what had been Indonesian Borneo, developed a governance model that borrowed elements of direct democracy, prediction-market-informed policy assessment, and an independent civil service selected partly on the basis of demonstrated epistemic calibration. Its model was studied, criticized, adapted, and partially adopted by dozens of other cities over the following half-century. Not copied wholesale—that would have missed the point—but used as a proof of concept that governance could be done differently, that the legacy systems of older cities were a choice and not a law of nature.

## The Shape of a Life in 2150

What is it like to be alive now?

You are likely healthier than any of your ancestors. The senolytics revolution of the 2060s extended not just lifespan but healthspan: the average person at seventy in 2150 has the physiological profile of a forty-year-old in 2000. Chronic diseases that once accumulated like sediment over a lifetime are cleared more regularly, if not perfectly. People work differently as a result—not longer in the grim sense, but across a longer and more varied arc, with multiple careers and genuine reinventions more common and more supported.

You almost certainly know, with unusual clarity, what your actual skills and preferences are. This is partly the legacy of the rationality education movement, which spent a century iterating on the best ways to teach probabilistic thinking, cognitive bias recognition, and epistemic hygiene to children and adults. The early efforts were awkward, sometimes didactic and off-putting. But the core insight—that thinking clearly is a learnable skill, not an innate gift, and that teaching it systematically changes how people live—proved durable. By 2150, something like calibrated uncertainty and steel-manned disagreement are considered basic civic competencies, taught as routinely as reading was once taught, if never as universally mastered.

Work has been transformed by automation in ways that are complicated to characterize simply, because what happened was not that humans stopped working but that the nature of what work was worth doing changed dramatically. The combination of cheap energy, capable robotics, and sophisticated logistics software automated most of what people had once meant by "labor"—the physical, the repetitive, the dangerous. What remained, and what expanded to fill the space, was work that was genuinely difficult to automate: the care of people for each other, the creation of novel things, the navigation of genuinely new problems. Care work—for children, for the elderly, for people in difficulty—was finally compensated at a level commensurate with its importance, partly as a result of its newly undeniable scarcity value, partly because the institutions that allocate compensation had been reformed enough to be less systematically wrong about value.

The city you live in is different. Urban land reform means it is denser than cities of a century ago, but not grim: the density is accompanied by genuine public space, green infrastructure, and buildings designed with more care and ambition than the 20th century's relentless cost-minimization had permitted. The infrastructure beneath the streets is not a century old; a wave of rebuilding, partly enabled by energy abundance, partly driven by the climate adaptation imperative of the 2040s and 2050s, replaced much of the world's urban infrastructure within a few decades. The cities that emerged were not perfect—nothing built by humans is—but they were legible, maintained, and designed with the understanding that they would need to adapt again.

## What Was Lost, and What Remains Open

This is not a perfect world. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise, and the people who live in it would be the first to insist on the qualification.

Progress on global coordination came slowly and remains incomplete. Some nations failed to make the institutional reforms that others made; some regions were slower to benefit from the energy transition and the biomedical advances. The gap between the best-off and the worst-off has narrowed dramatically, but it has not closed, and the people who live near the bottom of what remains are not consoled by historical comparisons. There are still political failures, still corruption, still the occasional catastrophic mismanagement of a public health crisis or a regional conflict. The tools for doing things better exist and are widely known; humans have not always used them.

The question of what it means to live a good life—which no amount of institutional reform or material sufficiency can answer—remains as genuinely open as it ever was. Perhaps more so. A world with less poverty and disease and institutional dysfunction is also a world in which the existential questions come into sharper focus, no longer blurred by urgent practical necessity. People in 2150 are, if anything, more focused on questions of meaning and consciousness and the nature of experience than people in 2024 were—because they have more space to think about them, and because the advances in biology and neuroscience have made those questions more tractable without yet making them settled.

And then there is the frontier.

Somewhere—not everywhere, but in certain institutions, certain universities, certain communities of people who have thought carefully about it—the work of understanding consciousness continues. What is experience? What makes a moment of joy or clarity or love what it is, physically, and how far can that kind of thing scale? The tools now exist to investigate these questions with a rigor that would have seemed like science fiction two centuries ago. The answers are not yet in. But the direction of travel is clear: there is more. There is vastly more. The moments in which humans have felt most alive—the experiences that made them sit up and say, *this, this is the real thing*—those moments are data, not just metaphors. They indicate a trajectory. They point somewhere.

The woman on the library roof, in that photograph, is reading a book about the neuroscience of aesthetic experience. She is sixty-seven years old. She expects, in reasonable health, to live to at least a hundred and twenty. She has had three careers, raised two children, and rebuilt her life from something that looked like wreckage in her forties. She is not perfectly happy. She knows that the world still contains suffering, that her city still contains people for whom things have gone terribly wrong, that the institutions she lives inside are improvements on what came before but far from what they could become.

She is sitting in the sun, reading, in a world that was built for her—built for everyone—not by a single generation's heroic effort but by the long, stubborn, imperfect, beautiful work of people who believed it was worth building.

That is what she is thinking about, or something like it. You can see it in her posture. She looks entirely at home.

---

*The long flourishing did not begin with a breakthrough or end with a reckoning. It began with people deciding, in large numbers and over long years, that incremental progress was worth making, that institutions could be reformed, that problems which had always existed were not problems that had to exist forever. It continues still. It is not finished. The best of it is probably ahead.*
