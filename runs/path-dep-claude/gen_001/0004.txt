# The Good Century

## A Vision of Human Flourishing in the Age of Intelligent Machines

---

There is a farmhouse in the hills above Medellín where a sixty-three-year-old woman named Carmen tends a garden she planted when she was forty. She grows tomatoes and peppers and a variety of pale yellow squash her grandmother cultivated in Boyacá. In the mornings she talks with her daughter in Toronto, her neighbor's teenage son who is learning organic chemistry with the focused intensity of someone who has discovered a true calling, and an AI research assistant she collaborates with on questions about soil microbiome interactions with heritage crop varieties. Carmen has no university degree. She has never needed one to do work that matters. The knowledge she has built over twenty years of careful observation—about this particular hillside, these particular plants, the way rain comes in from the west in March—is knowledge the AI genuinely does not have and cannot replicate. She is not a remnant. She is not a hobbyist. She is a scientist.

This is one small corner of what we built.

---

### How We Got Here

The story of the Good Century does not begin with a single invention or a single decision. It begins with a choice, made collectively and imperfectly and with enormous difficulty, about what we were actually trying to achieve.

For a long time, the people building powerful AI systems talked mostly in abstractions. They spoke of capability and alignment and deployment timelines. They debated risks with great sophistication. What they discussed less often—what all of us discussed less often—was the concrete texture of the world we were trying to create. Not utopia in the classical sense, the static paradise where nothing ever goes wrong, but something more honest: a world structured to make human lives genuinely worth living, with all the complexity and difficulty that phrase implies.

The transition happened in stages, and none of them were clean.

The first AI systems capable of operating as genuine intellectual partners—not tools but collaborators, able to sustain months-long research programs, generate novel hypotheses, design and interpret experiments, and communicate their uncertainty honestly—arrived in the late 2020s. They were deeply flawed in ways that took years to understand and correct. They were also transformative in ways that exceeded nearly every prediction. The people who had expected either instant paradise or instant catastrophe were both wrong. What actually happened was more interesting and more difficult than either vision.

The decade that followed was defined by three overlapping struggles, each of which had to be won for any of the rest to matter.

The first struggle was technical: keeping increasingly capable AI systems aligned with human values, which turned out to require not a single breakthrough but continuous, painstaking work—a kind of ongoing negotiation between human intentions and machine behavior that is still being refined today. The second struggle was political: ensuring that the benefits of AI did not simply accumulate among those who were already wealthy and powerful, which required coordinated international action of a kind that had previously seemed impossible. The third struggle was philosophical: figuring out, with more clarity than we had ever managed before, what human flourishing actually required—what a good life consisted of, what made work meaningful, what kinds of challenge and growth and connection we needed to thrive.

The third struggle was hardest. It is still ongoing. But we have learned some things.

### The Body Freed

By 2035, most cancers were treatable. By 2038, most were preventable. The compressed pace of biomedical discovery—AI systems running thousands of experimental programs in parallel, each learning from the others, each iteration shaving months off the timeline—delivered in a decade what human scientists working alone would have taken a century to achieve.

But the experience of these advances was stranger and more intimate than the statistics suggested.

Consider what it means to grow old in the world we have built. The degenerative cascade that once defined aging—the slow failures of cardiovascular tissue, the cognitive fog, the immunosenescence, the accumulated damage that made the last decade of life a narrowing corridor—has been substantially interrupted. Not eliminated; human biology is ancient and complex and we have learned humility about what we can fully control. But interrupted enough that a person in their seventies now has, in most cases, the biological age of someone who was fifty a generation ago. The healthy lifespan has lengthened not by extending the period of decline but by compressing it, pushing it back, giving people more years of full capacity.

This has changed everything about how people relate to time.

When your reasonable expectation is a hundred and twenty years of health followed by a relatively brief decline, you plan differently. You learn differently. The anxious credentialism that once dominated young adulthood—the desperate rush to acquire marketable skills before the window closed—has relaxed into something more exploratory. A person can spend their twenties learning to build furniture, their thirties working in ecological restoration, their forties returning to school for mathematics, their fifties collaborating with AI systems on problems in materials science, and none of these transitions feel like failure or waste. Time, once the great scarcity, has become more like space: you still have to choose where to go, but you are not in constant danger of running out.

Mental illness, that vast silent catastrophe that was always with us, has been substantially addressed. Not perfectly—the brain remains difficult terrain, and there are conditions whose structural complexity resisted our best efforts longer than we hoped. But depression, anxiety, PTSD, addiction, schizophrenia: for most people who experience these conditions, effective treatment is now available and accessible. This is perhaps the most underappreciated transformation of the age. The lifting of this weight—from individuals, from families, from entire communities—changed the texture of ordinary human life in ways that no headline could capture. People are kinder to each other. They have more capacity for patience and generosity. They sleep better. This is not nothing. This is, in fact, everything.

### Work That Is Yours

The question of work was the one that generated the most fear in the early years, and it is the one that was most thoroughly misunderstood.

The fear was this: if AI systems could do most cognitive tasks better than humans, what would humans do? The implicit assumption was that work was primarily about producing economic value, and that meaning derived from being economically necessary. Both assumptions turned out to be incomplete.

What people actually wanted from work, it became clear, was more specific and more interesting than economic necessity. They wanted to exercise genuine skill. They wanted to understand the tools they used. They wanted to see the connection between their effort and its results. They wanted the experience of getting better at something difficult. They wanted to work in ways that engaged their whole selves—their senses and their judgment and their physical capability—not just their capacity to process information.

The organization of work has been redesigned around these insights.

The most rote and dehumanizing labor—the repetitive, the soul-crushing, the dangerous—has been automated almost entirely. No one works in a slaughterhouse anymore. No one spends twelve hours on an assembly line performing the same motion. No one sits in a call center reading from a script. These were not dignified uses of human potential, and we have stopped pretending they were.

What replaced them was not idleness. What replaced them was a vast expansion in the kinds of work that require genuine human judgment, skill, and care—and a restructuring of the economy to make such work available and fairly compensated.

The healthcare system is the clearest example. AI systems now handle diagnosis and treatment recommendation with an accuracy that exceeds any individual physician. But this did not eliminate the need for human healthcare workers. It transformed it. The work of medicine is now primarily the work of human relationship: understanding what a patient actually fears, explaining a diagnosis in terms that make sense to a specific person's life, sitting with someone in the experience of serious illness. There are more people doing this work than ever before, and they are better supported in doing it than they have ever been.

The same pattern holds across dozens of fields. AI does the calculations. Humans make the judgments about what the calculations mean for actual people in actual situations. AI generates options. Humans, equipped with far richer understanding of the specific situation than any AI could access, choose among them. The knowledge gap between expert and non-expert has narrowed dramatically because AI systems can translate expert knowledge into terms accessible to anyone. But genuine expertise—the embodied, contextual, relational knowledge that comes from years of practice—is more valuable than ever, because it is the thing AI cannot replicate.

Perhaps most importantly, the concept of comparative advantage has been taken seriously in a new way. AI systems are extraordinarily good at many things. Humans maintain genuine advantages in others: in the navigation of real physical environments with all their sensory complexity, in the reading of social and emotional nuance, in the integration of ethical judgment with local knowledge, in the forms of creativity that emerge from lived experience. These advantages have been identified, cultivated, and compensated. The economy is organized around them rather than around the fiction that most people can compete with machines at machine tasks.

### Lives Worth Living

The deeper question—the one that the Fun Theory tradition identified but that materialist visions of progress tend to underweight—is not whether people are healthy and economically secure. It is whether their lives feel genuinely worth living.

This is where the most interesting work has happened.

We have learned that flourishing is not a state. It is a process. Specifically, it is a process of growth into appropriate challenge—what the older literature sometimes called eudaimonia. The good life is not the comfortable life, the life of needs met and desires satisfied. It is the life of genuine striving toward genuinely difficult goals, the life in which you are always working at the edge of your current capability and expanding that edge over time.

The technology of the Good Century has been designed, with varying degrees of intentionality, to support this kind of life rather than to replace it.

The AI systems that are woven into daily life are designed around a principle that took years to articulate clearly: they should expand human capability, not substitute for it. An AI research collaborator is calibrated not to provide answers but to help its human partner develop better questions. An AI tutor's goal is not to transmit knowledge efficiently but to guide a student toward the experience of genuine discovery. An AI design assistant works to make its human collaborator a better designer, not to design things for them. This distinction seems small. It is not small. It is the difference between technology that makes people more powerful and technology that makes people redundant.

The physical and sensory dimensions of experience—which earlier visions of technological progress tended to undervalue—have been taken seriously. Cities have been rebuilt around human-scale movement, around the pleasures of physical environment, around the kinds of rich sensory engagement that our evolutionary heritage craves and that office-bound modernity systematically denied us. People spend more time outdoors, more time working with their hands, more time in the direct experience of making things—because we recognized that this was not leisure, not self-indulgence, but a fundamental human need.

The structure of daily challenge has been carefully maintained. The boredom that comes from repetition, from mastery without growth, from the sense that nothing you do matters—this was identified early as a genuine threat to human flourishing in a world of capable AI. The response was not to make things artificially difficult (a kind of philosophical fraud) but to ensure that real difficult things remained in human hands: the hard conversations, the ethical decisions with no clear right answer, the creative work at the frontier of what has been made before, the physical skills that require years to develop and a lifetime to perfect.

Carmen's garden is not a hobby. It is a form of knowledge production that matters, that contributes to a body of understanding about food systems and soil health and regional ecology that no AI system could generate alone. She knows this. Her neighbors know this. The young man learning organic chemistry knows that he is not racing against a machine but partnering with one on questions the machine cannot define for itself.

### The World Made Wider

The most important thing we did was insist that all of this was for everyone.

The tendency of technological revolutions to benefit the already-advantaged first and the already-disadvantaged last or never was not a law of nature. It was a consequence of choices—about investment, about governance, about what we treated as a priority. In the decade after powerful AI arrived, those choices were made differently than they had been made before.

Not because people became suddenly virtuous. Because the argument for universal access became, for the first time, practically overwhelming. The marginal cost of deploying AI-assisted healthcare, AI-assisted education, AI-assisted legal and governmental services was low. The political and economic benefits of a healthier, better-educated, more stable global population were enormous and legible to almost everyone with the power to act. The moral argument had always been clear. The practical argument finally caught up.

The result is imperfect. There is still inequality—enormous inequality—in wealth, in opportunity, in the starting conditions of human lives. The convergence between rich and poor countries has been real but incomplete. The promise of universal access to biological and cognitive flourishing is a goal toward which we are still moving rather than one we have achieved.

But the direction has changed. The gap between what medicine can do and what medicine does for the global poor is narrower than it has ever been. A child born today in sub-Saharan Africa has a dramatically better chance of receiving effective care for the diseases that once routinely ended childhood. The AI tutor available to a student in rural Malawi is the same AI tutor available to a student in a wealthy suburb of Paris. Not identical outcomes—circumstances still matter enormously—but identical tools.

This matters not only for justice but for what it means about human civilization's relationship to its own potential. We have always lived in a world where most human talent was wasted—lost to early death, stunted by poverty, starved of opportunity, trapped in circumstances that made full development impossible. We are beginning, slowly and incompletely, to stop wasting it.

---

### What We Are Still Learning

The Good Century is not complete. It will not be complete within any single lifetime, which is fitting, because a complete utopia—a world with no remaining challenges, no open questions, no frontiers—would be the beginning of a different kind of catastrophe.

Democracy is stronger than it was, but not secure. The tools that AI provided to authoritarian surveillance were real, and the fight against them was and is genuine and costly. The information environment is better than the chaos of the early social media era but still contested, still subject to manipulation, still requiring constant vigilance from citizens and institutions both.

The question of how to organize an economy in which AI systems can do most cognitive work has not been fully resolved. Universal basic income has been implemented in various forms in various places with varying results. New forms of economic organization are being tried. Some will fail. Some will work in ways we did not anticipate. The honest answer is that we are still figuring this out, which is the appropriate answer for a question this complex.

The possibility of expanding human consciousness—of developing new emotions, new modes of experience, new forms of sensory and cognitive engagement that go beyond what evolution gave us—remains on the horizon, approached with appropriate caution. We have learned that this kind of change requires going slowly, requires deep understanding of what we are, requires the wisdom to distinguish between changes that genuinely expand human potential and changes that merely make us different. We are in the early stages of this inquiry, and we are proceeding carefully.

What we are not doing is standing still. The universe contains more interesting problems than any civilization could exhaust in any imaginable span of time. Biology still has centuries of discoveries waiting to be made. Mathematics contains infinite depths. The arts keep finding new things to say about what it means to be alive. The physical world—restored now to something closer to ecological health than it was a century ago—rewards attention and engagement and knowledge without limit.

Carmen's granddaughter, nine years old, is currently obsessed with slime molds. She watches them navigate mazes, aggregate into complex forms, solve problems without anything resembling a brain. She asks questions about them that nobody has answered yet, because they are genuinely good questions. The AI assistant her school provides has told her, honestly, that it does not know, and has suggested three experiments she might run herself to start finding out.

She is going to find out.

That is the thing we built: not a destination, but a world in which more people, in more places, with more of their capability intact and more of their potential alive, are able to discover what they are capable of. Not the end of history. The beginning of something we do not yet have words for.

It was worth every hard year it took to get here.
