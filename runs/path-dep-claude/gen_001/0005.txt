# The Inhabited Cosmos: A Vision of Humanity's Long Future

## I. The Near World

On a Tuesday afternoon in what we might call the Settled Era, a fourteen-year-old in Nairobi sits across from a woman in her seventies who spent three decades studying the aerodynamics of dragonfly wings. The girl has spent the last two weeks apprenticed to a civil engineer, a materials scientist, and a street food vendor. She liked the vendor best. Now she is here, at the Institute for Biological Mechanics, not because a school assigned her, but because a matching coordinator—a professional whose income depends on the quality of long-term outcomes for the young people she advises—thought she might find the intersection of biology and structure interesting.

She does. She is not being tracked. She is being *introduced*.

This is the most ordinary scene in the world.

The near world—Earth, the Moon, Mars, the orbital habitats, the asteroid mining cooperatives of the belt—looks, to someone transported from the early twenty-first century, recognizably human. People fall in love. They cook elaborate meals. They argue about whether the new concert hall in Medellín is beautiful or merely expensive. Children learn to read. Old men lose at chess. The ordinary texture of human life persists, and this is not an accident but an achievement.

What changed—gradually, then suddenly, in the century following the early AI transitions—was the architecture of institutions. The change was not ideological. No revolution swept the earth proclaiming a new doctrine of human nature. Instead, something quieter happened: the people building systems began to understand, with the precision of engineers rather than the enthusiasm of ideologues, what they were building and why it tended to fail.

The core insight was simple enough to fit on a placard, though its implications took generations to fully trace: *you get what you measure and pay for, nothing more, nothing less, and often something subtly different.*

This sounds obvious. It was not, in practice, obvious at all. The old world was full of systems that measured the wrong things—credentials instead of competence, activity instead of outcomes, compliance instead of judgment—and then expressed bafflement at the results. Courts that measured legal process rather than justice. Schools that measured attendance and test performance rather than genuine understanding and capability. Hospitals that measured procedures performed rather than health produced. The accretion of measurement errors, each individually defensible, had produced institutional systems that were, in aggregate, perversely aligned against the humans they were meant to serve.

The correction was not a single reform but ten thousand reforms, each small, each local, each experimental, and each—crucially—*public*. The willingness to run experiments and publish results, even embarrassing ones, became the foundational norm of governance. Cities would try different approaches to housing, to labor matching, to dispute resolution, and would share their data openly. The experimental disposition spread from scientific institutions outward.

The specific changes that followed from this disposition had a family resemblance: remove obstacles that require something other than actual competence; align incentives toward outcomes rather than processes; make information about quality visible rather than hidden; reduce the friction on good actors without reducing accountability for bad ones.

The labor market transformation is illustrative. Over several decades, occupational licensing was systematically examined, and most of it was found wanting. The question asked of every licensing regime was simple: does this requirement actually predict performance on the job? Almost always, the honest answer was: not much, and there are better ways to assess it. Licensing was not abolished wholesale—genuine safety requirements remained—but the burden of proof shifted. You needed evidence that the requirement predicted outcomes, not merely a tradition of demanding it.

Simultaneously, the practice of assessing actual job performance—through direct observation, structured trials, transparent track records—became standard. Children apprenticed widely and early, not to be exploited but to be *oriented*. The absurdity of spending four years studying before spending a single day trying the actual work became apparent once someone said it plainly, and people began to say it.

The matching problem—finding the human who would flourish doing a particular kind of work, and the work that a particular human would find meaningful—was recognized as a legitimate and important problem, and resources were allocated to solving it. Professional matchmakers emerged, paid on long-term outcomes rather than placements. The result was not a perfect system but a vastly better one: a world in which "choosing a career" was replaced by "finding your way toward work that suits you," through a process of guided exploration beginning early and never quite ending.

The economy runs hot. Not in the sense of inflation or instability, but in the sense that there is almost always more demand for good labor than there is good labor to supply it. Employers pursue workers more than workers pursue employers. This is not magic—it results from having eliminated the enormous friction that previously prevented employment relationships from forming. Every unnecessary credential requirement, every licensing barrier, every bureaucratic obstacle to starting a small business was a rock in the stream of economic matching. Remove enough rocks and the water flows.

## II. How The Near World Governs Itself

There is no world government, and this is not a failure.

Instead, there are tens of thousands of jurisdictions, arranged in overlapping layers, from neighborhood associations to city-states to regional compacts to the loose planetary coordination bodies that handle the things that genuinely require coordination at scale: atmospheric chemistry, orbital traffic, pandemics, the governance of AI systems powerful enough to affect everyone.

The diversity of jurisdictions is a feature. It is understood, widely and clearly, that having different places do things differently is how you run experiments, and running experiments is how you learn. Some cities have tried radical noise ordinances; some have tried radical noise permissiveness; the data on outcomes is public. Some regions have tried extremely low-friction property transfer systems; others have maintained stronger protections for long-term residents. The variation is not chaos—there are human rights floors below which no jurisdiction may fall, enforced by the planetary coordination bodies with the willingness to intervene militarily in extreme cases—but within those floors, diversity is cultivated.

Moving between jurisdictions is easy. This is essential. If you cannot leave, diversity becomes oppression. If you can leave, diversity becomes a portfolio of experiments you can personally navigate. The right to exit is treated as nearly sacred, and most jurisdictions make it easy to leave because they know that their own legitimacy depends on people choosing to stay.

The Chroniclers are the near world's closest thing to a universal institution. They emerged from the wreckage of twenty-first-century information ecosystems, which had produced an environment where it was genuinely difficult to find reliable accounts of what was happening, not because information was scarce but because it was weaponized. The Chroniclers are a professional class—well-paid, publicly funded through the system of voluntary-but-socially-expected contributions, and structurally isolated from political pressure—whose job is to produce the most accurate possible account of events and to reason about their causes and implications.

They are not neutral in the sense of refusing to evaluate. They evaluate constantly. They simply have strict professional norms about how evaluation is done: the reasoning must be shown, the uncertainty must be quantified, predictions must be made so they can be checked, corrections must be prominent. The closest earthly ancestors were certain science journalists and certain judges who wrote careful opinions. The Chroniclers took those traditions and professionalized them.

They are not loved. They are respected and used, which is what matters.

## III. The Gradient

Beyond the near world, the solar system is not uniform. This is the thing that is hardest to explain to someone from an earlier era: there is no clear line, but there is a gradient.

Earth and its immediate orbital neighborhood are, by deliberate design and strong social norm, *human-scale*. The concern is not prudishness or Luddism—every technology that demonstrably improves human welfare is welcomed—but the recognition that certain modifications to the environment of daily life have second-order effects on human psychology and social cohesion that are difficult to reverse and important to guard against.

Social media, in its twenty-first-century form, was the canonical example. It was not that people were forbidden to communicate at scale—they did, constantly—but that the specific mechanisms of engagement optimization, the algorithmic selection of content for emotional salience, had been recognized as a kind of commons tragedy. The short-term interest of each platform was to maximize engagement; the aggregate effect was an attention environment so polluted that many people could not think clearly. The correction required coordination that had previously been impossible, and it was one of the early victories of the new institutional architecture.

The gradient means that as you move outward—to Mars, to the belt, to the outer system habitats—you encounter communities running different experiments. Some Mars settlements have been running for three generations with significantly modified social norms: different relationships to work, different approaches to child-rearing, different (and quite radical) experiments in governance. Some have failed. Failure is permitted, with the crucial caveat that failure must not be irreversible, and must not harm people who did not consent to the experiment.

Further out, the communities become stranger. The asteroid habitats contain people who have chosen to modify themselves in ways that Earth maintains strong norms against—not prohibited, exactly, but strongly discouraged and physically segregated by distance. Some of these modifications are cognitive. Some are somatic. Most are reversible. A few are not, and those communities are watched carefully.

The people who have moved outward, in almost every case, did so voluntarily. This is the architecture that makes it acceptable: the near world is not pushing people away; the far world is pulling some people toward it. The existence of the gradient gives those who want to live otherwise a place to do so, without forcing that otherwise on everyone.

## IV. What Happens When People Die

This used to be a question with a simple, sad answer.

It still is, for many people—those who have chosen, explicitly and upon reflection, to have their deaths be final. This choice is respected. It is understood as a genuine value, not a failure of information.

For others, the question has become complicated in the way that genuinely good complications arrive: with a proliferation of options that would have seemed fantastical to earlier generations, each defensible, each chosen.

The hospice in Kyoto that has operated for sixty years offers what it calls *the conversation*: a structured series of discussions, conducted with the help of a trained advisor, about what a person values, what continuity of self means to them, and what they would want if continuation were possible. Some people emerge from this conversation having confirmed their preference for finality. Some emerge wanting to be preserved and eventually uplifted, when the relevant stratum of the cosmos is ready for them. Some want something stranger and harder to categorize.

The uplifting path leads, eventually, to the outer strata: communities and environments that are, by the standards of the near world, extraordinary. Enhanced cognition, extended lifespan measured in centuries, access to experiences impossible in unaugmented biology. These communities receive the willing dead, after some interval, and the person who arrives is—this is genuinely philosophically contested—either the same person who died or a very close relative who inherits their memories.

The unwillingness to force certainty about this question onto anyone is itself part of the architecture. Different communities have different views about the metaphysics of personal identity, and the system accommodates all of them, within the constraint that you cannot choose finality for someone else.

## V. The Far Horizon

The outermost strata of the human project are not yet built. They will not be built for centuries, possibly millennia. But they are anticipated, and the anticipation shapes how the near world conducts itself.

The exotic values—those that scale with resources, that treat all time as equally important, that are indifferent to substrate and physical location—are not suppressed in the near world. They are recognized, taken seriously, and given their eventual domain. The universe beyond the local cluster is vast in a way that makes all of human history look like a clearing of the throat before a very long speech. The exotic values will have room to work.

The critical deal—never fully explicit, never written in a single document, but embedded in the institutional architecture over generations—is this: the near world is preserved for human-scale values, and the far world is not forcibly converted to human-scale values. The people who care deeply about the total utilitarian calculus accept that Earth will not be converted to hedonium. The people who care deeply about grandchildren continuing to fall in love and cook meals accept that eventually the distant galaxies will be organized according to principles they may not fully understand or endorse.

What makes this deal work is not a treaty but an understanding: these values are nearly compatible. You lose almost nothing of what you care about by accepting that someone else gets the resources they care about that you were never going to want anyway.

The firewalls are softer than they might have been. People in the near world know, in general terms, that the outer strata exist and what they contain. What they don't receive, and what the social architecture is careful not to pipe in, is the kind of overwhelming aesthetic and experiential signal that would make ordinary human life feel diminished by comparison. The outer strata do not broadcast their most intense experiences into the near world for the same reason that the near world never built the engagement-optimization engines that had briefly threatened to hollow out human attention: some forms of competition are negative-sum, and recognizing them is the first step toward declining to play.

## VI. What Was Learned

The thing that future historians may find most interesting about the transition—the long century during which the near world slowly became what it is—is how little of it required new information.

Almost everything that changed could have been done with knowledge that existed in the early twenty-first century. The basic principles of institutional design, of mechanism design, of how to align incentives with outcomes—these were understood, by experts, decades before they were implemented. The coordination failures, the inadequate equilibria, the systems that measured the wrong things and produced predictable disasters—all of this was diagnosed correctly before it was fixed.

What changed was not knowledge but *literacy*: the widespread understanding, distributed through the population rather than concentrated in academic departments, of how coordination problems work, what causes them, and what solving them requires. When enough people understand that occupational licensing tends to entrench incumbents more than it protects consumers, licensing reform becomes politically possible. When enough people understand that measurement drives behavior and that measuring the wrong things produces perverse behavior, there is social pressure to get the measurements right.

This literacy spread slowly and then quickly, in the way that many things spread: through education, through crisis, through the emergence of respected institutions—the Chroniclers, the matching coordinators, the experimental governance movement—that modeled better ways of doing things and made their results visible.

The parallel to the historical spread of numeracy is not accidental. For most of human history, arithmetic was a specialist skill, practiced by merchants and accountants and largely opaque to ordinary people. Its gradual universalization did not make everyone a mathematician, but it made everyone capable of recognizing when a merchant was cheating them. The spread of coordination literacy accomplished something similar: not a world of game theorists, but a world in which most people could recognize when an institution was generating bad incentives, and say so in terms that others understood.

## VII. A Tuesday Afternoon, Continued

The fourteen-year-old in Nairobi asks the woman studying dragonfly wings why she has spent her career on something so specific.

The woman considers the question. She explains that the dragonfly is the most efficient aerial predator on Earth: it catches more than ninety percent of what it hunts, using a nervous system far simpler than any bird or mammal. Understanding how it does this, she says, has implications for everything from the design of prosthetic limbs to the architecture of early-warning systems to—this is her current project—certain questions about the aerodynamics of the outer-system habitats, which have unusual atmospheric compositions that create challenges no one had quite anticipated.

The girl asks if she can come back next week.

The woman says yes, of course, and makes a note to contact the matching coordinator, because this one might actually be the real thing.

Somewhere beyond the edge of the solar system, automated probes are laying the groundwork for what will eventually become the first genuinely post-human community, in the sense of containing beings that share human origins but have become something the early twenty-first century would not have recognized as human. They are doing this carefully. They have time.

And on a hundred platforms orbiting between the inner and outer world, people who have chosen the gradient—neither fully near nor fully far, neither unchanged nor unrecognizable—are living out experiments that will inform both ends of the spectrum.

No one is in charge of all of this. That is, finally, the point. The architecture was designed to not require anyone to be in charge of all of it—to make the local decisions local, the global decisions genuinely minimal, and the space between vast enough for every kind of human life that any human might reasonably want to live.

It is not perfect. Perfection was never the goal. The goal was an adequate equilibrium, clearly better than what came before, stable enough to persist, flexible enough to improve.

By those standards, it is doing rather well.
