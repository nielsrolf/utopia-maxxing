# The Competent Garden: A Vision of Human Flourishing

*An essay on the civilization we could build, and the tools we now have to build it*

---

## I. The Wager

Every generation inherits a civilization it did not design and cannot easily modify. The institutions we live inside—the way disputes are resolved, the way children are educated, the way diseases are fought, the way political power is organized—were assembled across centuries by people who lacked germ theory, game theory, behavioral economics, and the ability to sequence a genome. We have absorbed these systems so thoroughly that we mistake their accidents for necessities.

What follows is a description of a world that took seriously the possibility of doing better. It is not a fantasy. It is a wager: that the combination of institutional redesign and genuinely powerful scientific tools, navigated with sufficient care and collective wisdom, could produce a civilization worthy of the name.

The wager pays off. Here is what the world looks like when it does.

---

## II. How We Got Here: The Long Reform

No single event produced this world. It accumulated across two generations from a particular convergence of pressures and opportunities.

The first pressure was a series of coordination failures so catastrophic and so visible that they destroyed the assumption of institutional competence. The pandemic years of the early 2020s were the most instructive: vast scientific knowledge existed, the resources to deploy it existed, and nevertheless the institutional plumbing could not route them where they were needed. Millions died of failures that were not scientific but organizational—not a lack of knowledge but a lack of functioning systems for applying it. The embarrassment was productive. Reform coalitions found political surface area they had never previously had.

The second pressure was the arrival, in the mid-2030s, of AI systems capable of accelerating scientific discovery at a pace that made the stakes of institutional dysfunction undeniable. The question was no longer abstract: "could we arrange things better?" The question became urgent and concrete: "we now have the tools to compress a century of medical progress into a decade—what kind of civilization do we need to be to deploy those tools wisely rather than wastefully?"

The key intellectual innovation was diagnostic. It became broadly understood—in the same matter-of-fact way that everyone understands germs cause disease—that most of humanity's persistent suffering derived from two distinct but related failures. The first was coordination failure: the institutional plumbing was broken, and resources, knowledge, and effort could not reach the places they were needed. The second was discovery failure: there were things we simply did not yet know how to do, and the machinery for learning them was running far below its potential.

The great insight of the mid-21st century was that both failures were addressable, and that addressing them was an engineering problem rather than a utopian dream.

## III. The Biological Revolution: What We No Longer Fear

By the late 2040s, the category of "childhood death from infectious disease" had become historical. Children born in that decade grew up genuinely puzzled by references to smallpox, polio, and measles, the way earlier generations were puzzled by references to the Black Death—as something real but impossibly remote.

This did not happen by magic. It happened because AI systems operating as genuine research partners—not merely as tools for analyzing existing data, but as active participants in designing experiments, synthesizing biological literature, and directing laboratory work—compressed roughly a century of biological progress into fifteen years. The critical mechanism was the acceleration of the "platform discoveries": the generalized tools and techniques that, like CRISPR or mRNA vaccine technology before them, unlocked enormous downstream progress. Where human science had produced perhaps one of these foundational discoveries per year, AI-assisted science began producing several.

The results are concrete and enumerable. Cancer death rates, which had been declining around two percent per year for decades, collapsed. Treatment regimens adapted in real time to the specific genomic signature of a patient's tumor became standard within eight years of powerful AI's arrival; within twelve, most common cancers had become chronic manageable conditions rather than death sentences. The remaining difficult malignancies—rare, highly adaptive subtypes—continued to be worked on, and continued to improve.

Alzheimer's, which had resisted understanding for so long because its causes were tangled in an extraordinary complexity of interacting systems, yielded to the very thing it had most resisted: precise measurement tools that could isolate specific biological effects from the surrounding noise. The disease is now largely preventable if caught in its earliest biological stages, which it routinely is.

Mental illness followed a parallel trajectory, and its transformation has arguably changed the texture of daily life more than any other medical advance. Depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction—for decades these were managed but rarely cured, because the tools for understanding what was actually happening in the brain at the circuit level were too crude. Better measurement tools for living brains, new drugs that modulate neurotransmitter systems with far greater precision, and behavioral interventions refined by AI coaching systems that work with each person's particular patterns: the combination proved transformative. Not all mental illness has been eliminated—the most structurally rooted conditions remain difficult—but the hundreds of millions of people who previously lived in the gray fog of untreated or undertreated depression, anxiety, or addiction now mostly do not.

The subtler gains are equally significant. Most people now live with a baseline quality of physical and mental experience that previous generations would have considered extraordinary. The biological noise that made daily life difficult—the chronic pain, the persistent low-level anxiety, the cognitive fog that settled in middle age—has been substantially reduced. People have meaningful control over their biological trajectories: fertility, aging, cognitive function, and physical form are not fully malleable, but they are far more responsive to intervention than they were. "Biological freedom" is the ordinary phrase for this—the idea that your body's parameters are not simply handed to you at birth as fixed constraints, but are a domain in which you have genuine agency.

Human lifespan has extended substantially. Whether the current trajectory eventually reaches something like indefinite healthy life remains genuinely uncertain—the biology of aging is complex enough that careful scientists still hedge their predictions. But a healthy life of 120 to 150 years is now the realistic expectation for someone born today, rather than an outlier. The social implications of this are still being worked out. The economic implications were worked out faster: the projected insolvency of social support systems that assumed a fixed ratio of working to retired population never materialized, because the category of "retired due to age-related decline" has itself been substantially revised.

## IV. The Economy: Property Rights You Would Design on Purpose

The economic arrangements of a well-functioning civilization look, in retrospect, like the obvious things you would invent if you knew you were inventing property rights rather than discovering them as natural law.

The foundational reform was Georgist. Raw land and natural resources—which no one created and everyone depends on—are no longer simply owned. They are used under annual rent obligations, with the rents distributed equally to all residents of the relevant jurisdiction as a citizen's dividend. You keep everything you build. You pay rent on the location you occupy. The political difficulty of this reform, which economists had understood for over a century before it was implemented, was that the people who owned land also owned governments. The transition took thirty years of careful grandfathering, generous to existing landowners in the short term and transformative in the medium term. The result was an economy in which the primary path to wealth is producing things rather than sitting on resources other people need—a change that redirected an enormous quantity of human ingenuity toward genuine creation.

The citizen's dividend produced by land-value rents is not a trivial sum. It covers basic needs—food, shelter, healthcare—with enough margin to permit genuine economic experimentation. No one is economically conscripted into a job they find degrading because they cannot afford to leave. Labor markets, freed from the desperation that had previously characterized them, operate hot: employers must compete for workers, which means abusive bosses lose their people, gatekeeping credentials lose out to demonstrated competence, and the question "can you do this work?" consistently replaces "did you attend the right institution?"

This last change was enabled by something that runs deeper than economics: the collapse of the credential arms race. The old system required everyone to spend enormous resources acquiring signals that correlated weakly with job performance while providing employers with almost no genuine information about capability. It was a perfect lose-lose arrangement sustained only by collective action failure—everyone had to participate because everyone else was participating. When AI-assisted assessment tools made it genuinely cheap and accurate to test for actual competence rather than credentialed imitation of competence, the arms race dissolved within a single generation. People still learn deeply and extensively—knowledge remains essential—but the learning is tied to demonstrated capability rather than to the institutional stamp.

The intellectual property regime is radically simplified. Patents in the old sense—which created coordination nightmares by distributing veto power over valuable innovations across multiple rent-seeking parties—do not exist. Instead, there is a robust and publicly transparent norm that industries benefiting from foundational innovations owe a defined fraction of captured marginal value to the innovators. This is not enforced primarily by law but by public accountability: the books are open, the flows of benefit are tracked, and the question "what did you pay the people whose work you built on?" is a normal question asked at normal occasions. Copyright exists but expires after fourteen years, returning works to the public domain that was itself the necessary input to their creation.

## V. Governance: Bounded, Legible, and Competitive

The political arrangements of a well-functioning civilization look like what you would design if you understood that institutions need to be auditable and improvable rather than permanent.

The old bundling of nation-states—geographic monopolies on legitimate violence that happened to also provide roads, education, currency, cultural identity, military conscription, and a hundred other distinct functions—has been largely replaced by a nested system of overlapping jurisdictions with free entry and exit. Cities and regional federations provide public goods and charge residents for them. You can move. The result is something like competitive governance: jurisdictions that produce genuine value attract residents; jurisdictions that extract more than they provide lose them. The feedback loop is not fast or perfect, but it exists and has teeth.

Representation works through liquid delegation, which means that your representative on climate policy can be a different person than your representative on monetary policy, and both can be the same person as you if you choose to vote directly. The old system, in which a single territorial representative was expected to embody the views of a geographically defined population across every possible issue, produced systematic misrepresentation that everyone could see and no one could fix. The new system produces something much closer to actual representation, with the natural result that people are more engaged because they are more genuinely heard.

Courts produce publicly auditable reasoning. The logic of decisions is visible and criticizable, which means bad reasoning can be identified and corrected in a way that outcome-based accountability never permitted. The primary responses to rule violation are financial restitution and exclusion from jurisdictions rather than incarceration; cages are reserved for people who are actively and imminently dangerous. Arrest, prosecution, and incarceration are handled by separate institutions with separate incentives, so that no single actor benefits from moving a person through the entire pipeline.

There is a global layer for genuinely global problems: pandemic preparedness, the governance of catastrophic-risk technologies, the rules of trade. This layer has narrow scope and clear mandate, is not controlled by the largest military powers, and operates primarily through transparent information and credible coordination mechanisms. It was not easily built—the years of its construction involved genuine conflict between democratic coalitions and authoritarian states that had their own visions of what global AI governance should look like—but the coalition of democracies that led the development of advanced AI systems used both their technological advantage and a sincere commitment to distributing the benefits widely as the primary instruments of that construction.

The information architecture deserves particular attention because it shapes everything else. The old social media systems, optimized for engagement and therefore for outrage and tribalism, are understood in retrospect as a design failure that was obvious in advance and avoided only because the short-term incentives of their builders pointed the other way. The current platforms compete on different metrics: quality of reasoning, depth of updating, the ability to trace an argument to its foundations. Prediction markets are legally recognized and widely used. Forecasters have skin in the game; track records are public; confident expert predictions can be bet against; calibration is financially rewarded and miscalibration is financially costly. This does not eliminate poor analysis, but it applies continuous pressure toward accuracy that credentials and prestige alone never did.

A class of publicly funded "chroniclers"—somewhere between what journalists claimed to be, what Wikipedia editors tried to be, and what scientists should be—maintains a trusted repository of what has been established. Their work is transparent, cited, publicly updated when wrong, and funded from collectively determined charitable pools. They are not the only voices; polemicists and advocates exist and have their place. But the commons has a trusted baseline, and debates proceed from that baseline rather than from dueling sets of invented facts.

## VI. Growing Up in the Competent Garden

Children in this civilization grow up differently, and the difference is palpable.

The old front-loading of education—seventeen years of largely theoretical classroom instruction followed by sudden exposure to actual work—has been replaced by a continuous spiral. Children learn by doing alongside people who know how, with formal instruction woven through practice rather than separated from it. By adulthood, a person has tried perhaps fifty distinct kinds of work, not superficially but for real: enough time to encounter genuine difficulty and genuine satisfaction, enough time to develop a grounded sense of their actual interests and abilities rather than an abstract theory of what they imagine they might like.

The AI coaching systems that are now available to every child and adult provide something that was previously available only to the privileged few: a thoughtful, patient, knowledgeable presence that knows your specific patterns of learning and thinking, can identify where your reasoning goes wrong, can suggest the next step that is challenging without being crushing, and can celebrate genuine progress without the social distortions that affect human teachers. This is not a replacement for human mentorship and connection—the research on what makes learning stick is clear that human relationship is irreducible—but it is an amplifier, available to everyone, that has done more to equalize genuine educational outcomes than any policy intervention in the previous century.

Children grow up understanding, as a matter of ordinary literacy, how coordination works and where it fails. Nash equilibria, collective action problems, the tragedy of the commons, the logic of institutional design: these are taught alongside reading and arithmetic because they are equally fundamental to navigating a world with other people. A person who understands these things is a categorically different citizen than one who doesn't.

## VII. The Global Poor

The most important question about any improving civilization is: improving for whom?

The biological and cognitive advances described above reach the developing world faster than similar advances reached it in the previous century, for two related reasons. First, AI-assisted scientific progress produces tools—vaccines, treatments, preventive interventions—that are often structurally cheaper to distribute than the tools they replace. A single-dose vaccine that breaks the chain of transmission requires one contact per person; a treatment course requires ongoing supply chains. The new tools were deliberately designed, in many cases, with distribution in mind. Second, the global governance layer was negotiated with explicit distribution commitments as a condition of cooperation: the countries that agreed to support the democratic coalition's AI governance framework received, as part of the deal, access to the resulting technologies on terms they could afford.

This is not charity. It is a positive-sum arrangement that took serious effort to construct and that nearly fell apart several times during the negotiation. But it held.

Sub-Saharan Africa, which had a per-capita GDP of roughly two thousand dollars when this transformation began, has seen sustained economic growth rates that seemed impossible by the standards of previous decades. Eradicating malaria, AIDS, and parasitic worm diseases—which AI-assisted global health campaigns accomplished within the first decade—produced productivity gains that changed the economic trajectory of entire regions. AI-optimized agricultural systems produced a second Green Revolution. Markets, functioning better in economies that were now wealthier and more stable, brought down the cost of new technologies on the standard curves that markets reliably trace.

There are still gaps. The world is not equal. But the direction of travel is unambiguous, and the speed of convergence has surprised even the optimists.

## VIII. The Unresolved Questions

Honesty requires acknowledging what this civilization has not solved.

The question of meaning and economic purpose is genuinely open. When AI systems can do most cognitive work better than most humans, the old equation between labor and dignity requires renegotiation. The citizen's dividend provides a floor. But human beings need more than a floor—they need a sense that what they do matters, that they are agents in the world rather than passengers. The early evidence suggests that most people, given genuine security and genuine choice, find ways to be deeply engaged: in relationships, in creative work, in local governance, in raising children, in the endless complexity of caring for other people. The dire predictions of mass meaninglessness that attended the early AI transition have not materialized. But the new arrangements are still new, and the long-run cultural implications remain to be worked out.

The management of catastrophic risks—pandemics, weapons of mass destruction, the possibility of misaligned AI systems—requires ongoing vigilance at a global level that strains every institution charged with maintaining it. This is not a solved problem. It is a problem that is being worked on continuously, with more resources and more institutional seriousness than it received in the previous century, but without any guarantee of permanent success. The constraint that some technologies must be developed slowly, with extensive international oversight, and some research must be restricted until its implications are better understood, is genuinely costly and genuinely chafing. The cost is borne willingly by most people because the logic is clear: if the cost of excessive caution is some foregone growth, and the cost of insufficient caution is civilizational collapse, you accept a great deal of the former to avoid the latter.

The balance between democracy and autocracy is not permanently settled. Liberal democratic governance has structural advantages in this world that it did not previously have—the free flow of information is genuinely hard to block when AI systems can route around censorship, and the quality of life in societies organized around individual freedom and rule of law is visibly, undeniably better—but history does not move in straight lines, and the advocates of freedom cannot become complacent.

## IX. What It Feels Like

It feels, mostly, like competence.

When you encounter an institution—a hospital, a court, a school, a government service—your prior expectation is that it mostly does what it is supposed to do. The low-grade ambient anxiety of living in a civilization that seems to be continuously malfunctioning, that demands you navigate around its failures and construct workarounds for its broken systems, is largely absent. The chronic background hum of preventable suffering that previous generations accepted as simply the texture of existence—the cancer that didn't need to be fatal, the depression that didn't need to be untreated, the poverty that didn't need to persist—has quieted.

People argue. Values differ genuinely on things that genuinely admit of different values. Some local experiments fail. Some individuals behave badly. The constraints around catastrophic-risk technologies are sometimes frustrating and occasionally costly. The global governance negotiations are endless and exhausting. Progress is uneven and the gaps are not all closed.

But the default is competence rather than dysfunction. Diseases that should be treated are treated. Children who want to learn are taught. People who need help are helped. Disputes are settled by reasoning rather than by whoever has more lawyers. The media mostly tracks what is true. Work is tied to genuine value rather than to credential performance. Land and natural resources belong, in some meaningful sense, to everyone.

There is something else, harder to name. The experience of living in a civilization that is actually trying—that has genuine mechanisms for identifying its failures, correcting its errors, and improving its institutions—is qualitatively different from the experience of living in one that mostly isn't. It produces a kind of civic trust that previous generations, accustomed to systemic dysfunction, found difficult to imagine. Not trust that everything will be fine, because nothing guarantees that. But trust that when things go wrong, there are real mechanisms for making them better, and that those mechanisms are staffed by people who take the job seriously.

## X. The Path from Here

The distance from here to there is real but not infinite. Most of what needs to change is not technology—we have enough technology, and the technology is improving. What needs to change is *education*, specifically the addition of coordinacy as a genuine third pillar alongside literacy and numeracy. What needs to change is *epistemic infrastructure*, replacing engagement-optimized media with reasoning-quality-optimized media, institutionalizing prediction tracking, funding replication science properly. What needs to change is *economic plumbing*, primarily the Georgist shift. What needs to change is *institutional design*, the unbundling of state functions, the introduction of liquid democracy, the separation of prosecution from enforcement. And what needs to change is *the governance of transformative technology*, which requires democratic nations to cooperate in ways they find difficult and to make commitments they find costly.

None of this requires a revolution. It requires a long series of reforms, each individually defensible, collectively transformative, carried out by people who understand clearly what they are trying to build and why. The AI acceleration of biological and scientific progress provides an enormous tailwind, but also raises the stakes: the same tools that could compress a century of medical progress into a decade could be badly deployed in civilizations whose institutions are not ready for them.

The civilization described here is probably sixty to eighty years away, assuming the catastrophic risks are navigated successfully and the institutional reforms proceed. It is not inevitable. It is a choice—or rather, a long series of choices, each made by ordinary people in ordinary circumstances who understood the logic of what they were building and decided it was worth the effort.

The only question is whether enough people decide to make it.
