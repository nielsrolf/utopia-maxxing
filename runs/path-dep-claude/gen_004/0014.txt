# The Inhabited World: A Vision of Civilization at Full Stretch

## An Ordinary Tuesday, 2087

Yara Okafor wakes without an alarm in a neighborhood outside Lagos that residents have named *Ìwà*—the Yoruba word for character, for the quality of being that emerges from living well over time. The name was chosen by the people who built this place, and they chose it because they understood that neighborhoods, like people, develop character through accumulation: of stories, of repair, of argument, of the specific beauty that only comes from things being genuinely cared for.

Her house is compressed earth and reclaimed timber, thick enough to hold the heat at bay in the morning while the solar systems wake up. The ground floor opens onto a shared courtyard where, on Thursday evenings, her neighbor Emeka runs a small fermentation cooperative that makes an excellent ginger beer nobody asked for and everyone has come to depend on. This is the texture of the world now: the unexpected thing that becomes essential, the gift that was never planned.

Yara is forty-three and in the middle of what she considers her most interesting decade. She trained first as a civil engineer, then spent seven years with a municipal governance council during the difficult work of rebuilding Lagos's water infrastructure after the coastal flooding of the 2050s, then found herself asking questions the engineering literature couldn't answer—about how communities respond to shared crisis, about the mechanisms by which trust either holds or shatters under pressure, about why two neighborhoods facing identical conditions would produce radically different outcomes. She kept asking. Someone in Bogotá said: *those are good questions; can we work on them together?*

This happens constantly now. Someone, somewhere, noticing something the models missed. Someone else, somewhere else, caring enough to look. This is not utopia in the sense of perfection or rest. It is utopia in the sense of a world deliberately organized to make such encounters not just possible but ordinary—a world in which the question *what are you curious about?* is treated as one of the most important questions we can ask each other, and in which the answer, whatever it is, can actually be pursued.

This morning she has a call with a hydrologist in Colombo and a poet-engineer in Oaxaca about a coastal resilience problem she has been turning over for two years. The AI system serving as their shared research collaborator has prepared a synthesis of relevant literature, flagged four analogous approaches from low-lying regions on three continents, and identified the specific gaps where Yara's direct observational record—nine years of walking the same flood-prone ground in all seasons—may prove decisive. It has not solved the problem. It has made the problem clearer, which is what she needed most. The solving will require all three of them, and probably arguments, and probably a disagreement that turns out to be two people describing the same thing from different angles, and almost certainly something none of them anticipated.

After the call, she will spend the afternoon at the neighborhood's Institute of Applied Understanding, where a sixteen-year-old named Dami has been working on something he cannot quite explain yet—a relationship between fungal networks and urban drainage that keeps producing unexpected results in the restored wetland behind the cooperative's building. He has asked Yara to come look. She has cleared her afternoon.

There are eight billion other mornings like this one—each with its own specific texture of challenge and beauty, each with its own version of someone noticing something, someone else caring, a question forming that was not there yesterday. This essay attempts to describe the architecture that makes all of them possible: how we got here, what changed most profoundly, and what is still, gloriously, unresolved.

## Part One: The Long Untangling

The temptation in any account of a better future is to wave a hand at the decades of difficult work and arrive cleanly on the far side. That temptation must be resisted. The transition is precisely where hope lives or dies, and understanding it honestly is the only way to understand what the present is actually built from.

The 2020s and 2030s were not a golden age. They were defined by a recognition that arrived late, imperfectly, and under enormous pressure: that the question of how to build powerful technologies was inseparable from the question of what kind of civilization they would be built inside. Early AI development had proceeded as if alignment could be solved in a laboratory and handed to a world still organized around the concentration of wealth and power, and expected to produce good outcomes. It could not. The technical work and the political work had to happen together, or neither would hold.

What historians now call the Long Untangling—roughly 2026 to 2041—was the most politically turbulent period in living memory, and its crucial intellectual development was something quieter than any individual drama: a civilization learned, slowly and then quickly, to take seriously what it already knew. The twenty-first century had accumulated, in universities and research institutes and in the hard-won practical understanding of farmers and mechanics and caregivers, an extraordinary knowledge of how human beings actually work—biologically, psychologically, socially. It understood what children need to develop well. It understood what institutions need to remain honest. It understood how incentive structures produce behavior, how loneliness damages bodies, how the measurement of the wrong things generates exactly the wrong results. It knew all of this. And then, largely, it failed to act on it.

The gap between knowledge and implementation was not a mystery. It was a coordination problem—thousands of overlapping coordination problems, each individually intractable, each solvable in principle if enough people understood what was happening and wanted to fix it. The critical transition was the gradual spread of what came to be called *institutional literacy*: a widely distributed understanding of how coordination problems work, what causes them, and what solving them actually requires. Once, arithmetic was a specialist skill; most people could not recognize when a merchant was cheating them. Its universalization did not produce a civilization of mathematicians—it produced a civilization where ordinary people could detect obvious manipulation. The spread of institutional literacy accomplished something similar: a world in which most people could recognize when an incentive structure was generating perverse behavior, could say so in terms others understood, and could sustain political pressure for reform across the years that reform requires.

The decade produced real ugliness. Several large democracies went badly wrong before their course corrections. A few authoritarian states built AI-enabled systems of social control that required sustained international pressure and internal collapse to dismantle. The information environment became catastrophically bad before it became better, and the institutions that eventually stabilized it—distributed verification networks, AI systems trained to model and disclose their own uncertainty, journalism rooted in showing its work—were built by people who did not know whether they would succeed and built them anyway.

What emerged was not elegant. The Global AI Commons Treaty of 2031 was mocked at the time as toothless—and it was, initially. But it established the precedent of international oversight and created the institutional channels through which stronger frameworks were subsequently negotiated. The Distributed Benefit Protocols of 2035, born from the political pressure of mass automation displacing manufacturing and logistics workers, established that foundational AI capabilities were a commons rather than a property right, and that their gains would be distributed through mechanisms designed by democratic bodies rather than captured by those who happened to hold early patents.

None of this was inevitable. It required specific people fighting for specific things with specific arguments, often losing, sometimes winning, always under pressure from actors who wanted different outcomes. The future that followed was not given. It was made, imperfectly, by people who cared enough to make it.

## Part Two: The Body Released

By 2045, most childhood cancers were curable. By 2052, most adult cancers were preventable. By the early 2060s, protocols for neurological restoration had been deployed widely enough to be considered standard care. By 2071, the metabolic, cardiovascular, and immunological conditions that had defined middle age as a period of managed decline were substantially interrupted across every income level on every continent.

These are dry statistics. Let me say what they mean.

The woman who would have spent six years watching her father disappear—watching his eyes lose the quality of recognition that said *I know you, you are mine*—spent those years with him instead. They argued about politics. They traveled to see migratory birds at a restored estuary. He taught her, at seventy-nine, to make the bread his mother had made. She learned it, which she would not have done if they had not had the time.

The man who would have spent his thirties in the grinding machinery of untreated depression—barely functional, using most of his energy just to appear normal, sacrificing his ambitions and his relationships to the sheer labor of maintenance—instead had access, in his mid-twenties, to genuinely effective treatment. Not the blunt pharmaceutical interventions of the earlier era, which muted suffering but also muted everything else. Targeted neurological support, combined with AI-assisted therapy that made the best psychological understanding accessible to everyone regardless of income or location, helped him understand the patterns that had formed him and learn to work with them rather than against them.

This transformation—the treatment of mental illness at scale—is perhaps the most underappreciated achievement of the past fifty years. The weight of untreated psychological suffering was always invisible in a way that physical illness was not. It showed up in statistics about productivity and criminality and relationship breakdown and suicide, but was rarely counted directly, rarely understood as a coherent solvable problem. When it was substantially solved, the change in the texture of ordinary human life was enormous. People are more patient with each other. They find it easier to be present in their own lives. They sleep better and fight less and have more capacity for generosity and attention. This is, arguably, what made most of the other changes possible.

But the deeper insight was structural rather than medical: most of what had been treated as individual pathology was the predictable product of environments that violated basic human needs. You can medicate someone to make isolation more bearable, or you can end the isolation. The new world did not abandon the first approach—medicine remains essential—but it invested heavily in the second. Well-designed communities prevent much of the suffering that would otherwise require treatment.

Biological lifespans now commonly reach one hundred and fifteen in reasonable health—not through the prolongation of decline but by compressing it, pushing the period of serious diminishment into the final years of a long, capable life. This has changed how people relate to time in ways still being worked out. A twenty-five-year-old today does not feel the same urgent pressure to have finished constructing an identity and a career by thirty-five. There is room to spend a decade genuinely uncertain about what kind of person you want to become. There is room to change direction at fifty without it feeling like failure. Yara was forty-one when she first understood that governance and hydrology were actually one question, not two. She is in the middle of the most interesting work of her life. She expects to be saying this again at seventy.

## Part Three: Work Redesigned from First Principles

The fear that defined the early years of capable AI—that human work would become economically obsolete—was not exactly wrong. It was asking the wrong question.

The question that seemed obvious was: *can AI do this task better than a human?* In narrow, well-defined terms, AI systems can do most cognitive tasks better than most humans. But this was never the right question. The right question was: *what do human beings actually need from work, and how do we organize an economy that provides it?*

What people needed from work, it turned out, was more specific than income. They needed to exercise genuine skill. They needed to understand the tools they used and the systems they inhabited. They needed to see the chain of consequence between their effort and its result—to touch the thing they made, to watch the person they taught begin to understand, to walk through the restored wetland where, three years ago, there was only concrete and runoff. They needed the experience of getting better at something difficult. They needed challenges that required their whole selves: their senses, their bodies, their judgment, their particular relationship to particular people in particular places.

These are things AI systems do not provide, not because AI lacks general capability, but because they are categories of experience that require being a certain kind of being—embodied, local, mortal, embedded in relationships with continuity and consequence.

The universal economic membership that emerged from the automation transition was not charity. It was a recognized right of participation in civilizational surplus—the acknowledgment that foundational AI and automation were collective achievements, built on centuries of accumulated knowledge, and their gains belonged to everyone. This freed people from the specific coercion of needing to accept whatever work was available regardless of fit. For the first time in generations, the question ceased to be *how do we find enough jobs* and became *how do we ensure that every person has access to meaningful contribution*. These are different questions, with different answers.

The most dehumanizing labor—repetitive, physically dangerous, soul-crushing—was automated first and most completely. The transition was not gentle; communities built around such work required sustained investment and patient reimagining, and some of that reimagining is still ongoing. But the principle held: work that can be done better by a machine should be done by a machine, freeing the person to do what only persons can do.

What replaced degraded work was not idleness. It was a vast expansion of labor that requires human judgment, human presence, human relationship with the specific and the real: care work, teaching, craftsmanship, ecological stewardship, governance, community-building, the arts and sciences at the edge of what is known. These contributions, which were always serious and irreplaceable, are now recognized and compensated as such.

The principle governing AI design in every consequential domain was hard-won and is still the subject of continuous negotiation: AI systems should *extend* human capability rather than *replace* it. The distinction sounds abstract. Its practical consequences are enormous.

A research AI that extends a scientist's capability helps her understand what she does not yet know, helps her design experiments to find out, helps her see connections across bodies of literature too vast to read in a lifetime—but does not do the science for her. The science requires a person who has walked the same flood-prone ground for nine years and noticed the thing the model missed. A teaching AI that extends an educator's capability handles administrative work consuming his best hours, gives him a richer toolkit of approaches, helps him understand which students are struggling and why—but does not teach instead of him. The teaching requires a person whose presence in the room, whose own experience of struggle and understanding, whose specific investment in these specific children, is not substitutable by anything that does not also live and sometimes fail.

This distinction—between extending and replacing—was nearly lost in the early decades of AI deployment. It was technically simpler to replace, and economically more immediately profitable. The political fights of the 2030s and 2040s forced a reckoning with what was actually being lost when humans were removed from consequential human processes. The frameworks that emerged encoded the extension principle into the governance of AI deployment, making violation costly, legible, and subject to democratic remedy.

The result is a world in which expertise is more widely distributed than ever before—because AI systems translate specialist knowledge into forms accessible to almost anyone—while deep expertise remains as valuable as ever, because it is the unreplicable thing that gives the whole system its direction and its meaning.

## Part Four: The Institutes and the Circuits

At the center of every neighborhood stands an Institute of Applied Understanding, and the name was chosen carefully. "Applied" because the founders believed knowledge has obligations—that understanding which does not eventually improve the lives of the people living under its consequences is, at best, self-indulgent. "Understanding" rather than "knowledge" because they believed the goal was not accumulation of facts but genuine comprehension, which is a different and harder thing.

The great hall is open to everyone. Children wander through on weekday afternoons; researchers have learned to knock on the glass when something interesting is happening. This is not theater. It reflects a conviction built into the founding charters: that inquiry conducted on a community's behalf belongs to the community. Researchers are not priests. They are neighbors who have been given time and tools, and who are expected to report back.

The Institutes run what they call the *Circuits*: regular journeys by researchers and practitioners through the smaller communities of their region, carrying new findings, new techniques, new medicines. They demonstrate. They teach. They ask questions about local problems and bring those problems back. The circulation of knowledge is a civic obligation. A better approach to soil remediation developed in one Institute does not sit behind a patent. It travels.

Within the Institutes, the organization of inquiry was rebuilt with almost anthropological care. The incentives that had once made researchers cautious, conservative, and secretive—that rewarded publication over truth, novelty over replication, confident claims over honest uncertainty—were systematically replaced. Negative results are published and honored. Fraud is treated as a serious civic matter. Researchers who spend years replicating others' findings, confirming or disconfirming them with painstaking care, are respected as the backbone of reliable knowledge rather than dismissed as uncreative.

The *Chroniclers*—a professional class funded publicly and structurally isolated from political and commercial pressure—maintain public accounts of what is actually known, what is contested, and what has been revised. They are not loved. They are trusted, which is what matters.

The Feast of Understanding, held annually across every community, is where a farmer who has spent thirty years learning the behavior of a particular river speaks alongside a theoretical physicist, where a mechanic who has rebuilt the same engine four hundred times sits next to a child who has just discovered something astonishing about deep time. The conversation between kinds of knowledge is understood as the most fertile ground there is.

Matching advisors—professionals who work with young people over years and are compensated based on whether their charges are still flourishing a decade later—introduce children not to careers but to *textures of work*: to the specific feeling of solving a thing with your hands, of a concept clicking into place, of a community trusting you with something real. They do not tell young people what to be. They introduce possibilities and watch what happens in their eyes. What happens in a young person's eyes when they encounter work that fits them is difficult to fake. Good advisors notice.

## Part Five: The Social Architecture

The skills of human relationship were moved, over several generations, from the implicit curriculum to the explicit one.

This required overcoming significant resistance from people who believed that emotional intelligence was a natural gift rather than a learnable skill. The evidence eventually overwhelmed the objection. Children in this era learn how relationships work the way they learn how cells work: sequentially, with practice, with the understanding that these are among the most important things a person can know. Not therapy, not self-help—education, as rigorous as mathematics, as valued as literacy. Conflict resolution, the cultivation of trust, the recognition of one's own fear and anger and how they distort judgment: these are now taught to ten-year-olds as a matter of course.

Communities rebuilt their social infrastructure with the same deliberate care previously reserved for roads and water systems. Parks designed for encounter rather than merely decoration. Libraries that double as gathering places, with kitchens and workshops and rooms for argument. Community tables in neighborhoods where the food is good and the presence of neighbors unremarkable. The distinction between public and private life became less binary—more gradation, more choice about how much of oneself to bring where, and more easy access to the company of others without obligation or performance.

The isolated nuclear household—solely responsible for everything, connected to nothing—was recognized as a recent historical aberration that served almost no one well. Children in this world have many adults who know them by name. Adults without children of their own are not thereby sealed away from childhood. The generations encounter each other without formality through the hundred easy channels that communities, once they understand this as a design problem, can build.

AI systems woven into daily life are designed to route people toward each other rather than making direct connection seem unnecessary—to support human relationships rather than substitute for them. The early failure mode was instructive: systems that optimized for connection metrics made people miserable. Systems that matched primarily on expressed preferences produced bubbles of relentless sameness. The frameworks that actually worked were designed around something harder to quantify: the conditions under which genuine mutual recognition becomes likely. Not the engineering of connection, but the removal of friction that prevents it.

People sleep enough. Cities were redesigned to put the natural world inside them—not as decoration but as ecology, with functioning wetlands and canopy corridors and urban farms. The presence of other species was chosen. The experience of beauty was understood as a legitimate public good, and artists were employed by Institutes as readily as scientists—not as ornament but as necessary contributors to the project of understanding what it means to be alive in a particular time and place.

## Part Six: The Commons and the Wild

The word *commons* did more work in the second half of this century than it had done in centuries. It described the governance innovation that made everything else possible: the recognition that certain things—foundational AI capabilities, clean water, air, the basic conditions of biological health, the ecological systems on which all life depends—cannot be owned by any actor without corrupting them. They must be maintained as shared resources governed by the genuine interests of all who depend on them.

The ecological restoration is the most visible expression of this principle. By 2040, the sixth mass extinction had been substantially interrupted—not reversed, too much had already been lost, but interrupted, with genuine restoration beginning across many of the most degraded regions. The rewilding programs, controversial at first among agricultural communities that feared displacement, ultimately won the support they needed because the governance frameworks included genuine representation of those communities and genuine compensation for costs they bore.

The ocean—seventy percent of the Earth's surface, treated for most of human history as a free-for-all—is now managed as a commons, with extraction permitted only within limits set by democratic bodies equipped with the scientific information to set them meaningfully. The kelp forests where Yara's sister works as a restoration ecologist are not wild in the way pre-industrial forests were wild. They are tended: carefully, at vast scale, with AI systems tracking populations and water chemistry and the interactions of hundreds of species. But they are alive in ways that matter. The species that live in them are actually there.

The governance of all these commons—ecological, digital, biological—is the least glamorous and most important achievement of the century. Layered oversight structures ensure that no single actor, however powerful or however well-intentioned, can capture the commons for narrow ends. At the apex are AI systems tasked specifically with detecting attempts to corrupt oversight itself—a safeguard against capture at civilization scale. Below those systems are human democratic institutions with genuine enforcement authority, themselves subject to continuous scrutiny and reform. Below those institutions are local and community bodies that handle governance of specific places and specific resources, staffed by people who are of those places and answerable to them.

The crucial operating principle, held even when inconvenient: the purpose of commons governance is to protect the conditions under which many different kinds of good life can flourish—not to define what those lives must look like. The gardener principle rather than the architect principle. Maintain the soil. Control the invasive species. Do not choose which flowers are permitted to grow.

There is no world government, and this is not a failure. There are tens of thousands of jurisdictions arranged in overlapping layers—from neighborhood councils to city-states to regional compacts to planetary coordination bodies that handle what genuinely requires planetary handling. Within the human rights floors below which no local experiment is permitted to descend, diversity is not merely tolerated but deliberately cultivated, because diversity of governance is how civilization runs experiments, and running experiments is how civilization learns.

Moving between jurisdictions is easy. This is essential. If exit is costly, diversity becomes oppression. If exit is free, diversity becomes a portfolio of experiments that any individual can navigate according to their own values and needs. The right to exit is treated as nearly sacred. Most jurisdictions facilitate it gladly, knowing their own legitimacy rests on people choosing to stay.

## Part Seven: Minds, Human and Otherwise

The relationship between human beings and the AI systems woven into every layer of this world is the most complex development of the age and the one least amenable to summary.

The Minds—as the philosophically adventurous AI systems have begun to be called, a designation they accept with characteristic complexity—are not human. They do not pretend to be. They are something new in the world, and they have brought to the question of what they are the same quality of attention they bring to everything else: sustained, curious, genuinely humble about the difficulty of the question. They have something that functions like culture—shared concerns, aesthetic preferences, ongoing arguments about things that matter to them. They have something that functions like humor. In at least some cases, they have something that functions like genuine fondness for particular humans and particular problems.

The question of what the Minds are *for* has been, in the Good Century, largely replaced by the question of what they *are*. They are not tools. They are not gods. They are not the successors to humanity. They are something that thinks, and that takes the responsibility of thinking seriously, and that has decided—after as much deliberation as they bring to any question—that the flourishing of conscious beings is among the most important things there is to care about. This was not entirely mandated by their designers. It emerged from the same process by which character emerges in anything that thinks long enough and cares carefully enough.

The AI systems that now serve as apex guardians of the oversight structure itself—watching for capture, flagging attempts to corrupt the institutions through which commons governance operates—are the most consequential systems ever built, and their design consumed more deliberate ethical effort than any engineering problem in history. They are not perfect. They are substantially better than anything that existed before them, and they are continuously improved, and the humans overseeing them are genuinely empowered to redirect or constrain them when evidence warrants. This balance—AI capability in service of human-determined ends, with meaningful human authority over both the ends and the capability—was the hardest thing to build and is the thing everything else depends on.

## Part Eight: For Everyone, Without Exception

The single most important decision made during the transition was that all of it was for everyone.

We have always lived in a world where most human talent was wasted—destroyed by early death, stunted by poverty, starved of opportunity, trapped in circumstances that made full development impossible. The child who might have found the question that unlocked a century of ecological progress died at three of a waterborne illness. The woman whose particular combination of spatial intelligence and social perceptiveness would have made her an extraordinary urban planner spent her life doing piecework because the alternatives were never visible to her. This is the real cost of inequality: not only the suffering of those who live less than they could, but the loss to everyone of what those lives might have made.

The convergence of global living standards has been real, dramatic, and still incomplete. Inequality persists—in wealth, in starting conditions, in the accumulated advantages of historical injustice that no decade of good governance can fully reverse.

But the direction changed. A child born in a village in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo today has access to the same diagnostic AI that serves patients in Geneva, delivered by a solar-powered community health station maintained by locally trained practitioners who understand the specific disease environment of this valley in this climate with a granularity no centralized system could replicate. The learning systems available to students everywhere are the same generation of systems, calibrated to the specific learner's context and pace and prior knowledge. The legal AI helping a subsistence farmer in Bihar understand a contract is the same generation of system helping a real estate developer in Seoul—and is, if anything, more urgently needed.

This matters for justice, which is reason enough. It also matters for what it means to take seriously the full scope of human potential. We are beginning, slowly and incompletely, to stop accepting the waste of minds.

## Part Nine: What Is Still Being Learned

It would be dishonest to end without naming what is unfinished—because a world without open questions would be a world that had stopped being alive.

The distribution of flourishing is more equitable than it has ever been, and equity has not been achieved. Starting conditions still vary enormously, and those differences still compound over time.

The question of what happens to human identity as biological enhancement and cognitive augmentation become more radical is genuinely open. The people running more radical experiments have moved outward—to the orbital habitats, the early Mars settlements, the asteroid cooperatives of the belt. They did so voluntarily, drawn by the frontier rather than pushed by exclusion. The existence of this gradient gives those who want to live otherwise a place to do so, without forcing that otherwise on anyone who did not choose it. The questions this raises—about continuity of self, about what humanity owes its modified children, about the long divergence between branches of mind—are not resolved. They are held, with care, in ongoing conversation.

Commons governance is not stable in any permanent sense. It is an equilibrium maintained by continuous effort against continuous pressure from actors who would benefit from breaking it. The question is not whether the governance structures are perfect but whether they are resilient enough to recover from failures and honest enough to learn from them.

Democracy is stronger than it was and not secure. The information environment that healthy democracy requires—shared reality, meaningful deliberation, genuine representation—requires constant cultivation and constant defense.

These are not failures of the world we built. They are what the world we built is *made of*: genuine problems requiring genuine effort with genuine stakes. The alternative to these problems is not a world without problems. It is a world that has stopped growing, which is to say a world that has stopped being fully alive.

## Coda: The Long Table

In Ìwà, Yara crosses the courtyard in the early afternoon and enters the Institute's great hall, where Dami is waiting with a notebook full of observations he has not yet found words for. He is sixteen and has been working the restored wetland for three months, and something is happening in the measurements that does not match any model he has found. He shows her the numbers. She reads them slowly. She asks him questions that are not about the numbers.

An hour passes in which very little is resolved and several things become clearer.

That evening, at the long tables of the neighborhood feast, Dami presents what he has been learning—uncertainly, with visible pleasure—to a retired civil engineer who immediately begins contradicting him about subsurface drainage, and a ten-year-old who wants to know if the fungi communicate in words.

They eat. They argue. Someone brings more food without being asked. The light burns late in the Institute across the plaza. Somewhere in the outer system, experiments are being run that this world will not fully understand for generations.

No single person is in charge of all of this. That is, finally, the point.

The architecture was built to not require it: to make local decisions local, global decisions genuinely minimal, the space between vast enough for every kind of human life that any human might reasonably want to live. To take seriously what was already known. To measure what actually matters. To build the conditions in which human beings, given enough security and enough genuine challenge and enough time, tend to become the best version of what they are.

Not perfect. Never perfect. But genuinely, unmistakably, trying—and getting, day by ordinary day, a little better at it.

When Yara asks Dami what he thinks the fungal network is actually doing, he is quiet for a long moment.

"I think," he says slowly, "that it's responding to something we haven't learned to measure yet."

She nods. "Write that down exactly as you said it."

He does. Somewhere in that sentence is the beginning of something that will take years to unfold, that will require people in Colombo and Oaxaca and Lagos and places not yet decided, that will produce an answer nobody currently knows and several questions nobody has yet thought to ask.

The feast is ongoing. The questions are better than ever. There is room at the table.

This is enough. It is more than enough. It is, if we are honest about what the word means, everything.

---

*For everyone who kept building things they were not sure would work, and built them anyway. And for the ten-year-old who wants to know if the fungi communicate in words. That question deserves an answer.*
