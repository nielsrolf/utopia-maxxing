# A Civilization Worth Having: Notes Toward a Better World

*An essay on what humanity could build, if it decided to*

---

## I. The Thing That Is Missing

The gap between the world we have and the world we could have is not primarily a gap in technology, resources, or even intelligence. It is a gap in *institutional imagination*—a failure to notice that our collective arrangements were assembled by accident and could be redesigned on purpose.

Most of us inherit our civilization's systems the way we inherit our native language: fluently, unselfconsciously, and without ever suspecting that other grammars are possible. We find ourselves inside labor markets, legal systems, educational credentialing schemes, and democratic structures that were cobbled together across centuries by people who understood neither game theory nor behavioral economics nor information design—and we assume, implicitly, that these arrangements are roughly as good as arrangements can get.

They are not. What follows is a sketch of a civilization that takes that seriously.

---

## II. How We Got There

The interesting question about a better civilization is not what it looks like at peak flourishing—that part is relatively easy to imagine. The interesting question is: what were the first moves? What happened in the transition?

The honest answer is that no single event launched the better world. It accumulated, across roughly two generations, from a particular convergence: widespread improvement in statistical and economic literacy in formal education (starting, critically, in elementary school), the rise of prediction markets as a respected epistemic institution that began visibly humiliating credentialed experts throughout the 2020s and 2030s, and a series of high-profile coordination failures—most of them pandemic-related—so embarrassing and costly that reform coalitions finally found sufficient political surface area to operate on.

The key innovation was not ideological. It was *diagnostic*. At some point in the mid-21st century, it became broadly understood—in the same matter-of-fact way that everyone understands germs cause disease—that many of humanity's worst ongoing problems were coordination failures rather than resource failures or intelligence failures. The food was there. The knowledge was there. What was missing was the institutional plumbing to route them where they were needed.

Once that frame was established and normalized, reform became less like revolution and more like plumbing repair: tedious, incremental, unglamorous, and enormously effective.

## III. The Economy: Property You Actually Designed

The economic system of a well-functioning civilization looks, at its foundation, like the obvious thing you would invent if you knew you were inventing property rights rather than discovering them as natural law.

Raw land and natural resources belong, in some fundamental sense, to everyone, since no one produced them. The solution is not collective ownership in the sense of a government bureaucracy managing everything—that produces a different set of incentive disasters. The solution is a Georgist land-value tax: ongoing rents paid to use land, with those rents distributed equally to all residents of the jurisdiction as a citizen's dividend. You get to keep everything you build. You pay rent on the location you occupy. This is not controversial among economists who have actually thought about it. It was never implemented on Earth primarily because the people who owned land also owned governments, and they preferred the existing arrangement.

In the better world, this transition happened gradually, with existing landowners grandfathered out over thirty years—not because they deserved a windfall, but because the reformers were playing a positive-sum game and could afford to be generous. The result was an economy in which no one could get wealthy simply by sitting on resources other people needed, which redirected an enormous amount of human ingenuity toward actually producing things.

Labor markets operate hot. This sounds like a small thing but it is not. When employers must compete fiercely for workers rather than workers competing desperately for jobs, the entire texture of working life changes. Abusive bosses lose their employees. Companies that extract value through credential-inflation and unnecessary gatekeeping lose out to companies that test for actual skill. The question "can you do the job?" replaces the question "did you pay for the right piece of paper?" Concretely: a twelve-year-old who can actually cut hair can apprentice at a salon. A twenty-two-year-old who can actually write software can get hired without a university credential. What gets tested is what actually matters.

Intellectual property works as follows: patents, in the Earth sense, don't exist, because they are recognized as coordination nightmares—multiple parties with veto power, each able to extract all the value, each therefore trying to do so. Instead, there's a robust honor norm (backed by transparent public accounting) that industries benefiting from innovations owe roughly 15% of the marginal value they captured to the innovators. This isn't a law with a compliance bureaucracy. It's a social norm with teeth: if you benefited from the mRNA vaccine platform and donated nothing to its inventors, this is a public fact, and you will be asked about it at dinner parties.

Copyright exists but expires after fourteen years with no renewal, after which works enter a public domain that is understood to have been the necessary input to the copyrighted works in the first place.

## IV. Governance: The Art of Not Having a State

The better world does not have nation-states in the Earth sense—bounded geographic monopolies on legitimate violence, where your country of birth determines everything from which language you'll speak to whether you'll be conscripted. Those arrangements served certain historical coordination functions, but they package too many distinct problems together: defense, currency, regulation, taxation, culture. Unbundling them produces something much more functional.

What exists instead is a nested system of jurisdictions with free entry and exit. Cities and regional federations provide public goods—roads, sanitation, courts, basic research funding—and charge residents for them. You can move. You are not a captive customer. The result is something like competitive government: jurisdictions that produce good value for their residents grow and attract more residents; jurisdictions that extract more than they provide shrink and reform.

There is a global layer for genuinely global problems: pandemic preparedness, catastrophic technology risks, the rules of trade. This layer has narrow scope and clear mandate, is not controlled by the largest military powers, and operates primarily through transparent information systems and credible coordination mechanisms rather than through armies.

Courts produce written reasoning, publicly accessible, with the logic auditable by anyone. Judges are accountable for the quality of their reasoning rather than the outcomes they produce—because you can only reliably measure what you can see, and reasoning is visible while outcomes depend on too many uncontrolled variables. Arrest, prosecution, and incarceration are handled by separate institutions with separate incentives, so that no single actor benefits from moving a person through the whole pipeline. The primary responses to rule-violation are expulsion from jurisdictions and financial restitution rather than incarceration, reserving cages for people who are actively dangerous.

Democratic representation works through liquid delegation: you can vote on matters yourself, delegate your vote to someone you trust on specific topics, or delegate to a delegate-of-delegate. This produces something much closer to actual representation than territorial winner-take-all elections, and it means that your representative on environmental policy need not be the same person as your representative on monetary policy.

## V. Information: The Chroniclers and the Commons

The information architecture of a civilization determines much of what that civilization is capable of. Bad information environments—ones that reward outrage, punish nuance, systematically confuse people about what other people actually believe, and make it almost impossible to know which experts are trustworthy—are not merely annoying. They are civilizationally crippling.

The well-functioning world solved this with several overlapping mechanisms, none of them perfect but together sufficient.

First: prediction markets are legally recognized and widely used. Forecasters have skin in the game. Track records are public. When a credentialed expert makes a confident prediction, anyone can bet against them, and if the expert is systematically wrong, this is a legible fact rather than a matter of interpretation. This does not eliminate bad analysis, but it applies a continuous pressure toward calibration that has no analog in the credential systems of today.

Second: there exists a class of what we might call Chroniclers—something between what a journalist claims to be, what a Wikipedia editor tries to be, and what a scientist should be. Chroniclers are paid from collectively-funded charitable pools (the norm is roughly 15% of income donated to collectively-determined public goods, of which Chroniclers represent a significant fraction). They are expected to show their work, cite their sources, update publicly when they're wrong, and maintain a neutrality that is understood as a professional standard rather than a political position. They are not the only voices in the information ecosystem—anyone can write anything, and polemicists and advocates have their place—but when something becomes part of the official Record, it has passed through a higher epistemic standard. Their role is not to tell people what to think but to maintain a trusted repository of what has been established, leaving the debates and interpretations to the broader discourse.

Third: communication technology was developed differently. Social media as it evolved on Earth—algorithmically optimized for engagement, which turned out to mean optimized for outrage and tribalism—is understood as a design failure that should have been obvious in advance. The equivalent platforms are optimized for different things: long-form thinking, genuine updating, the ability to follow someone's reasoning rather than just their conclusions. The attention economy still exists—people still compete for audiences—but the competition rewards reasoning quality rather than emotional activation.

## VI. Science and the Experiment Society

The research apparatus of a well-coordinated civilization is organized around replication and transparency in a way that fundamentally changes what "scientific consensus" means.

Science is understood as a process, not a credential. The relevant question about any finding is not "what institution published it?" but "how many independent groups have tried to reproduce it and what happened?" Replication studies are funded at roughly the same level as original research, because everyone understands that a single non-replicated finding is just a prior, not knowledge. The researchers who replicate—or fail to replicate—someone else's work are celebrated, not dismissed. Their names appear prominently in the record of any finding they tested.

Local experiments are understood as a crucial feature rather than a bug of the system. Every city, every regional federation runs itself slightly differently, and the results are tracked. When one city finds a better way to manage housing density or childhood nutrition or public transit, this information flows into the commons and other jurisdictions adopt it. There is no single best answer to "how should a city be run," but there is an enormous ongoing empirical project of finding out, and that project has actual funding, actual rigor, and actual authority to change things when the evidence accumulates.

The approach to catastrophic risks—pandemics, weapons of mass destruction, and especially the development of transformative artificial intelligence—is characterized by a deep institutional commitment to slowing down and thinking carefully. There is a recognized class of "one-shot risks": scenarios where a single mistake could foreclose humanity's future permanently. These are treated categorically differently from ordinary risks. The global institutions charged with monitoring and managing these risks are funded generously, operate transparently, and have meaningful international authority to restrict activities that cross defined thresholds of danger. The logic is simple: if the cost of excessive caution is some lost economic growth, and the cost of insufficient caution is civilizational collapse, you should be quite willing to accept a lot of the former to avoid the latter.

## VII. Growing Up: How Children Become Adults

The transition from childhood to adult competence looks radically different when the goal is actual capability rather than credential accumulation.

Children learn to do things by doing them, alongside people who know how. Older children teach younger children what they actually understand, with external verification to prevent errors from propagating. By the time a person reaches adulthood, they have tried, at minimum for a week each, perhaps fifty different kinds of work—enough to have a grounded sense of their actual interests and abilities, rather than an abstract sense of what sounds prestigious. They have not spent seventeen years in classrooms acquiring theoretical knowledge that will be immediately forgotten, and zero days in workplaces discovering whether they like or are good at any actual work.

The credential system is absent because the people who designed the economic institutions recognized credentialism for what it is: a positional arms race in which everyone must spend enormous resources acquiring signals that convey nothing about job performance, while employers receive no actual information about whether their hire can do the work. Everyone loses, except the institutions collecting tuition.

What replaces it: you try the work. You are watched doing it. If you can do it, you do it. If you cannot, you find something else to try.

Education remains important, but it is continuous and embedded rather than front-loaded and disconnected. The great teachers are celebrated figures; teaching is understood as a high-skill activity that society benefits from paying for generously; the connection between what is taught and what it is useful for is maintained by the simple fact that children are constantly comparing what they learn to what they observe in actual workplaces.

## VIII. What It Feels Like to Live There

It feels, mostly, like competence.

When you encounter an institution—a court, a hospital, a job market, a news organization—your prior expectation is that it mostly does what it is supposed to do, because institutions that fail to do what they are supposed to do are quickly identified as such, embarrassed publicly, and reformed or replaced. The low-grade ambient anxiety of living in a civilization that seems to be continuously malfunctioning, that constantly demands you navigate around its failures and find workarounds for its broken systems, is largely absent.

It does not feel utopian in the sense of perfection. People argue. People have different values about things that genuinely admit of different values. Some local experiments fail. Some individuals behave badly. The global-level coordination around catastrophic risks involves real constraints on what researchers can publish and what technologies can be deployed, and these constraints are sometimes chafing and sometimes genuinely costly.

But the default is competence rather than dysfunction. Disputes are settled by reasoning rather than by whoever has more lawyers. People are paid roughly what they produce. The media mostly tracks what is true. Children grow up having actually tried the things they want to do. The labor market bends toward workers. The land belongs, in some meaningful sense, to everyone.

---

## IX. The Path

The gap between here and there is real, but it is not as large as it appears. Most of what needs to change is not technology, not genetics, not some fundamental reprogramming of human nature.

What needs to change is *education*—specifically, the addition of a genuine third pillar alongside literacy and numeracy: what we might call *coordinacy*, the practical understanding of how collective action works, where it fails, and what can be done about it. A person who understands Nash equilibria, Pareto improvements, the tragedy of the commons, and the logic of institutional design the way they understand multiplication is a categorically different political actor than one who doesn't.

What needs to change is *epistemic infrastructure*—the replacement of attention-optimizing media with reasoning-quality-optimizing media, the institutionalization of prediction tracking, the proper funding of replication science.

What needs to change is *economic plumbing*—primarily the shift from property-tax-free land ownership to Georgist rent-capture, which is technically simple and politically difficult.

What needs to change is *institutional design*—the unbundling of nation-states, the introduction of liquid democracy, the separation of the various functions currently bundled into police and prosecution.

None of these changes require a revolution. They require a long series of reforms, each of them individually defensible, collectively transformative, carried out by people who understand clearly what they are trying to build and why.

The world described in this essay is probably sixty to one hundred years away, assuming things go tolerably well and the catastrophic risks are navigated successfully. It is not a fantasy. It is an engineering problem.

The only question is whether enough people decide it is worth building.
