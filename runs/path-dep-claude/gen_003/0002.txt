# The Living Compact: Notes Toward a World That Actually Works

---

There is a child in Medellín who will never be asked what she wants to be when she grows up. This is not because no one cares what she becomes. It is because the adults around her have finally understood that becoming is a continuous process, not a destination you name at age eight and spend the rest of your life defending. She is twelve. She has already spent time in a wetland restoration project, a ceramics collective, a community dispute-mediation office, and a small fabrication workshop that produces custom prosthetics. She has not yet found the thing that will organize the next decade of her life. Nobody is worried about this. Nobody is pretending not to be worried while actually being worried. The absence of worry is genuine, which is different from performed optimism, and the difference matters more than it sounds.

This essay is an attempt to describe how that world was built and what it feels like to live in it.

---

## I. The Condition That Made Everything Else Possible

The story is usually told starting with technology, because technology is concrete and impressive and easier to narrate. Fusion reactors. AI systems that could design proteins and compose symphonies and run policy simulations. Molecular fabrication that brought the marginal cost of most goods toward zero. Robotic systems extending human reach into the asteroid belt and beyond. These things happened, and they mattered enormously, and we will get to them.

But the story that begins with technology is always missing the first chapter, and without the first chapter, everything that comes after looks like it happened automatically — as if sufficient energy abundance and manufacturing capacity would naturally produce a flourishing civilization, when the actual record of human history suggests that abundance without wisdom produces oligarchy, then collapse, and the ruins of something that could have been much better.

The first chapter is about what had to change in how human beings understood the systems they were living inside.

Call it coordinacy literacy, though the name matters less than the thing. The insight at its core fits in one sentence and took decades to propagate: *systems behave according to what they measure and reward, not according to what they intend.* Courts optimized for process produced process. Schools optimized for test scores produced test performance. Social media platforms optimized for engagement produced engagement — which turned out to mean fear and outrage, because fear and outrage were stickier than satisfaction. Hospitals optimized for procedures performed procedures. The aggregate gap between what institutions said they were for and what they actually optimized toward was a civilizational tax paid daily by everyone, mostly invisibly.

Closing this gap required two things that had previously been scarce: accurate information about what was actually happening, and enough people who understood incentive structures well enough to recognize when they were inside one. Neither of these is primarily a technical problem. The early twenty-first century was not short of information, in the quantitative sense. It was short of reliable, verified, carefully reasoned accounts of what that information meant — and short of the shared vocabulary for evaluating those accounts. The information ecosystems of that period had been optimized for attention capture rather than understanding, and the difference between those two goals turned out to be a chasm.

What changed was not a sudden shift in human nature. It was something slower and more durable: a grain running through how children were raised and how adults argued and what the cultures celebrated and what they condemned. The grain said, without softening: *the rules that hold civilization together are made of people following them when it is inconvenient. You are one of those people. This is one of those occasions.* This grain was taught. It can be taught. The evidence that it can be taught is that it was taught, across generations, in enough places that it eventually became the water people swim in rather than an argument people have.

Alongside this cultural shift, a new class of institutions emerged — call them the Chroniclers, though they had many names and took many forms in different jurisdictions. Their job was to produce the most accurate possible accounts of events and to reason carefully about causes and implications: not neutrally, in the sense of refusing to evaluate, but transparently, showing their reasoning in full, quantifying uncertainty explicitly, making predictions that could later be checked, and correcting errors prominently rather than quietly. They were funded collectively and structurally isolated from political and commercial pressure. Their closest ancestors were certain science journalists and certain judges who wrote careful, careful opinions. Those traditions were professionalized, scaled, and made into something closer to infrastructure — the information equivalent of a sewage system, handling something that civilization requires to function and that nobody notices until it breaks.

The Chroniclers are not loved. They are trusted, which is more durable than love and harder to fake.

This change in epistemic culture is the first chapter. Without it, the abundance that followed would have been captured and concentrated, as abundance had always been captured and concentrated before. With it, there was enough shared understanding of how capture happens that capture became, slowly and imperfectly and with constant vigilance, harder to sustain.

## II. The Architecture of Enough

The abundance itself arrived not as a single event but as a convergence across decades. AI systems, developing in capability through the 2030s and 2040s, proved most transformative not in dramatic individual applications but in the patient, unglamorous work of optimization: designing energy systems that actually worked, finding protein-folding configurations that produced better drugs, running the ten thousand infrastructure calculations that had previously required either enormous expertise or expensive error. Fusion reactors came online. Solar harvesting scaled beyond projections. Molecular manufacturing matured enough to produce food, medicine, and complex goods at costs that fell steadily toward zero.

The economic historian Amara Osei-Bonsu, writing in 2089, described it as "the moment the game changed shape." Before, human civilization had been playing an exhausting, multigenerational game of dividing a roughly fixed pie under conditions of scarcity. After, the pie was growing faster than anyone could consume it — and the mathematics of greed changed. Capturing an extra share of a resource pool that is expanding by orders of magnitude yields diminishing marginal returns, especially when the risks of conflict remain real and potentially catastrophic.

This did not eliminate competition or conflict. Human beings compete for status, for meaning, for the right to determine their own futures. But it drained the oldest and most structural incentive for violence: the belief that your neighbor's survival threatened your own.

The political structures that emerged were not the global government that twentieth-century internationalists dreamed of, nor the fractured corporate feudalism that dystopian novelists feared. They were something more interesting: a layered commonwealth of voluntary spheres, governed according to the principle that diversity of governance is how you run experiments, and running experiments is how you learn.

The foundational layer is called the Compact, though it operates less like a government than like infrastructure. A decentralized network of automated systems, accountable to elected planetary councils, maintains a material floor globally: clean water, nutritious food, basic medical care, physical security. It is not charity. It is infrastructure, present for everyone the way the atmosphere is present for everyone — requiring no one's gratitude, asking nothing in return except that you do not actively destroy it.

Above this floor, communities diverge spectacularly. The Compact's fundamental operating rule is non-interference: communities may exist at whatever technological level they choose, and technologically advanced communities may not disrupt the chosen way of life of less advanced ones. A deliberately slow village in the Scottish Highlands, where the fastest communication technology is a community radio and the farming tools are tractors rather than autonomous systems, is not thereby sentenced to deprivation. The material floor is there regardless. What the village has chosen is a pace and a texture of life, not poverty. This is a distinction that required the material floor to exist before it could be made, and making it has turned out to matter enormously.

In practice, the Compact governs only what must be governed globally: atmospheric chemistry, the information commons, the handful of rights that function as genuine floors — freedom from arbitrary violence, access to the material baseline, the right to exit any community you were born into — and the governance of systems powerful enough to affect everyone. Below those floors, the diversity is actively cultivated, because diversity of arrangements is the only portfolio hedge available against civilizational-scale mistakes.

Moving between jurisdictions is easy. This is essential. If exit is unavailable, diversity is oppression. If exit is available, diversity is a portfolio you can navigate personally, and most communities make leaving easy because their legitimacy depends on people choosing to stay.

## III. What Work Became

The labor market of the old world was cold by design — or rather, by accumulated accident. Workers competed desperately for positions, creating conditions under which abuse, credential inflation, and arbitrary gatekeeping could flourish. The barriers that maintained this arrangement were examined, jurisdiction by jurisdiction, with one question: does this requirement actually predict performance? Almost universally, the honest answer was: not much, and there are better ways to measure. Occupational licensing regimes that had calcified into rent extraction were dismantled. The regulatory capture embedded in credential requirements was unwound. Land and resource rents were captured collectively and distributed universally, not as charity but as dividends on assets no one created and therefore no one could justly claim exclusively. On top of this floor, the market ran. Under it, no one fell.

The labor market became, and has remained, genuinely hot: employers pursue workers more than workers pursue employers. This is not a temporary condition or a policy intervention that needs constant maintenance. It results from having removed the accumulated friction that previously kept the market cold, and friction does not naturally regenerate once removed.

But "work" in the old sense — labor exchanged for survival — is in any case increasingly optional for most of the world's population. The material floor is guaranteed independently. What people do instead is pursue meaning: they raise children with tremendous care and time, create art, conduct research, build community institutions, tend gardens, practice crafts, compete in sports, argue about philosophy. The infrastructure handles the invisible tasks. People know it is there. They mostly interact with it the way a person in 2024 interacted with indoor plumbing: gratefully, unreflectively, occasionally with wonder.

The matching-brokers — a profession that emerged from the recognition that connecting people to work suited to them was a legitimate problem worth solving — are paid on long-term outcomes rather than placements. The child in Medellín's advisor receives her best compensation if, in twenty years, the woman that child has become reports that her work feels meaningful. This incentive structure was previously missing entirely. The brokers are not perfect. They are vastly better than nothing, and they improve continuously because their performance is tracked by the Chroniclers and published openly.

What the child in Medellín is doing when she moves through the ceramics collective and the wetland project and the prosthetics workshop is not preparation for work. It is work, in increasing complexity, producing real value in the world, with real feedback about what she is good at and what she needs to improve. The idea that you would spend years preparing for a thing before spending a single day attempting the actual thing became, when stated plainly, obviously absurd — and once enough people said it plainly, it stopped being the default.

## IV. Diversity as Design, Not Concession

The world at the end of the twenty-first century looks nothing like the flattened, homogenized global culture that both its advocates and critics of globalization anticipated.

On Earth alone: you can be born into a fully networked city in Lagos where neural interfaces allow instant access to the sum of human knowledge, where AI collaborators help citizens design buildings, compose music, run experiments, and govern themselves through sophisticated deliberative systems that would be unrecognizable to anyone from the early democratic era. You can also be born into a deliberately slow community in rural Japan where the fastest communication technology is a community radio and the most advanced farming tool is a tractor, where the residents farm and teach their children and maintain ancient buildings and practice traditional arts. Both communities have access to the material floor. Neither is required to adopt the technologies of the other. The master ceramicist in the Japanese community whose work on clay glazing contributed to a breakthrough in filter membrane design received full credit and a small royalty that she donated to the village's communal fund. Knowledge moves freely across the Compact's open commons. Communities can choose what to contribute and what to receive. Most choose to contribute more than they expected to and receive more than they thought they needed.

In space, the divergence becomes more dramatic. The lunar settlements, established in the 2050s, became the first true laboratories of social diversity at scale. Some communities built themselves around radical longevity — residents measured their lives in centuries and planned accordingly. Others organized around specific cultural projects: communities of scholars dedicated to synthesizing intellectual traditions with future tools; communities of engineers who wanted to build new civilizations from first principles without importing their predecessors' legal and social frameworks wholesale. In the asteroid belt and on Mars, more divergent experiments proliferated. Some communities integrated AI deeply into governance, not as rulers but as sophisticated advisors running continuous policy simulations. Others excluded AI from civic life entirely, running their governance on purely human cognition, accepting slower and more error-prone decision-making as the price of what they experienced as full human agency. Both types reported high satisfaction. Both produced genuine contributions to humanity's collective understanding of how to live.

The mind-upload communities — collectives of digital persons running on computational substrate in orbital stations — occupy their own sphere. Their relationship with biological humanity was negotiated carefully over decades, formalized in the Tycho Accords of 2071: they contribute enormous intellectual labor to science, engineering, and art; in return, they receive resource allocations, legal personhood, and the right to expand into computational substrates in the outer solar system. What they do not receive — and agreed not to seek — is governance authority over biological human communities. Computational advantages are real and enormous. The principle that those advantages should not translate into political dominance over beings who did not choose to become digital is foundational.

The diversity is not without tension. There are communities that believe their way of life is the best one, that others are wasting potential or missing what matters. These beliefs are expressed, argued about, published in the open commons, and then — mostly — left to be resolved by the ongoing natural experiment of communities succeeding or failing on their own terms. What is not permitted is one community expanding its footprint into another's territory without consent, or attempting to short-circuit the experiment through force.

The Chroniclers write all of it up.

## V. The Edges of What Was Solved

Mental health, which had been a cascading global crisis through the early twenty-first century, improved dramatically once its structural drivers were substantially addressed. Economic precarity diminished. Social isolation — amplified for decades by information systems optimized for engagement rather than connection — diminished once those systems were rebuilt around a different goal: helping people find what they were actually looking for. Environmental degradation, which had been producing a background noise of ecological grief and political helplessness, reversed as AI-designed systems remade industry to run within planetary boundaries and restoration projects remade landscapes over decades. Political helplessness — the sensation of living inside systems too large and complex to be influenced by ordinary human action — diminished once the Compact's deliberative infrastructure made ordinary human action genuinely consequential again.

What did not diminish was the irreducible difficulty of being human. Grief, loss, failure, the particular anguish of loving someone who does not love you back, the ordinary machinery of limitation — these remain. The serious people have argued for generations about whether eliminating them would even be desirable, and the consensus, insofar as there is one, is that they are the preconditions of certain kinds of meaning rather than simply costs to be minimized.

What also did not disappear, and is taken seriously rather than papered over: the problem of freedom itself. When nothing threatens survival, you must choose, from a much wider menu, what to care about. Many people find this harder than they expected. The anxiety that previously organized attention — telling you exactly what to worry about, giving you a clear enemy — has been removed, and nothing has been built to replace the organizing function it performed. Newcomers to this world from harder circumstances report that freedom is more effortful than anticipated. The Compact has no solution to this. It has therapists, communities of meaning, spiritual traditions, philosophical schools, and the Chroniclers — who write up the problem clearly and repeatedly, because writing problems clearly is the first step toward eventually solving them, and some problems require several generations to solve.

The willingness to name unresolved problems clearly, to resist the temptation to paper over them with optimistic framing, is itself one of the features of the present arrangement that people are most attached to. The previous era's tendency to produce confident official accounts of a world that was working better than it was — because the institutions producing those accounts were optimized for reassurance rather than accuracy — was recognized as one of the deepest sources of the failures that followed. The correction was not pessimism. It was precision: good about what is good, honest about what is bad, genuinely uncertain about what is genuinely uncertain.

A researcher who has been presenting at the annual Coordinacy Symposium for thirty years returns this year with a new category of person the system is failing to notice. She is not entirely sure yet how to describe them. She is working on it. Others will argue with her. Some will run experiments. The Chroniclers will write it up.

This is not a satisfying resolution. It is a process, which is different from a resolution, and which has the advantage of continuing.

## VI. What the Children Are Taught

Every child in the Compact, regardless of where they are born, learns what the curriculum calls the three foundations.

The first is language and number, ancient tools, still necessary, still underappreciated in their difficulty. The second is coordinacy: the understanding of how incentives shape behavior, what collective action problems are and why they emerge, why a world of people each making locally sensible decisions can produce globally terrible outcomes. This is taught not as ideology but as vocabulary — the minimum shared language for participating in a civilization that intends to function. A child who leaves adolescence without it is not educated, any more than a child who cannot read. The content is neutral: it does not tell you which values to hold. It tells you how to reason about what pursuing your values will actually produce.

The third foundation is history told honestly in two registers. The first register is the history of scarcity — the long millennia in which human beings killed one another over water rights, arable land, oil fields, and shipping lanes, in which empires rose to capture resources and collapsed when those resources ran dry. The second is shorter and stranger: how scarcity ended, and what humanity chose to do with the abundance that replaced it. The lesson is not that technology saves us. The lesson is that abundance creates the conditions in which human wisdom — slow, imperfect, genuinely diverse — finally has enough room to work. And that room does not maintain itself. People maintain it, by following the rules when it is inconvenient to follow them, because they understand that the rules are made of exactly that.

The child in Medellín has not been told what to become. She has been given tools, materials, mentors, problems to work on, and feedback that is honest rather than managed. When she throws a bowl that is not good, the ceramicist tells her directly and explains why, in terms she can use. She does not feel diminished by this. She has been raised in an environment where feedback is assumed to be about the work, not about her worth as a person, which turns out to be one of those simple-to-state and fiendishly-difficult-to-achieve conditions that makes almost everything else easier.

In the afternoon, she finds a problem in the wetland restoration model that the engineers missed. She flags it. The senior engineer thanks her. She is compensated a small amount, because she did something useful, and here useful things are compensated regardless of whether the person who did them holds a credential. She goes home and has an argument with her younger brother about whether the new community center is beautiful or merely interesting.

Neither of them will remember this argument in twenty years. They will both remember that they had it, that it was taken seriously by the adults around them, and that their disagreement was treated not as a problem to resolve but as a sign that they were engaged with the world. Which, it turns out, is what you want from people who will be governing it.

## VII. The Most Fragile Thing

Not the hot economy, though people from harder times find it most immediately impressive.

Not the Chroniclers, though accurate information is doing more work than almost anything else.

Not even the material floor, though the material floor made everything else possible.

The most fragile thing is the texture. The grain running through how children are raised and how adults argue and what the communities celebrate and what they condemn, that says: *you are not the exception to the principles that hold this world together.*

Sophisticated people in the previous era were not stupid. They could explain, in precise terms, why the systems they were gaming were failing. They understood the theory. And then they made the locally rational move that was globally destructive, because their cause was important enough, their reasoning subtle enough, their situation different enough to justify setting aside the general rule. They were always right that their situation was somewhat different. They were always wrong that the difference was sufficient to justify the exception.

What this world learned to teach — and what has held, and what the Chroniclers watch for signs of erosion in, constantly — is not cleverness. It is the recognition that the rules are made of people following them when it is inconvenient, and that you are one of those people, and that this is one of those occasions. It was taught. It can be taught. The evidence that it can be taught is that it was taught, here, across generations, until it became the water people swim in.

It is the most important thing this world has built. It is the thing that, if lost, cannot easily be rebuilt. It is the thing that the Chroniclers worry about most and write about most and remind people of most insistently, not because they are pessimists but because they understand that fragile things require tending.

So far, it is holding.

The grain holds. The floor holds. The diversity of experiments continues, producing knowledge that feeds back into the commons. The child in Medellín has not yet found her central work, and nobody is worried, and the absence of worry is genuine.

The Chroniclers, reviewing the last century, agree that this is progress.

They have noted several things that could still go wrong.

They have published their reasoning.

You are welcome to argue with them.

That, in the end, is why they publish: not to close conversations, but to make them more honest. Not to declare that the work is done, but to name clearly what the work remaining is.

The world is not perfect. Perfection was never the goal. The goal was an adequate equilibrium — clearly better than what came before, stable enough to persist, humble enough to keep improving. A world good enough that the people living in it can spend their energy on the irreducible difficulties of existence rather than on the unnecessary ones. A world where the unnecessary suffering — the suffering that came not from the irreducible difficulties of being alive, but from the structures humanity built and could have built differently — has been substantially addressed, and where the remaining problems are named clearly and worked on honestly.

Those structures were built differently.

The work continues.

The world is adequate. In its own quiet, imperfect, ongoing way, it is more than adequate.

That will have to be enough. It turns out that enough, built carefully, is quite a lot.

---

*If you have arrived in this world recently, from a harder time or a harder place, find someone who knows what they are doing and ask them to show you around. They will probably be glad to. Knowledge here is understood as a commons that grows when it moves, rather than a credential that loses value when distributed.*

*If you cannot find someone, ask the Chroniclers. They are the ones writing it all down.*

*If you find something they have missed, tell them.*

*That is, in the end, how the world improves: not by being declared finished, but by being looked at carefully and honestly and often, by people willing to say what they see.*
