# The Coordinators' World: A Citizen's Account

## Being a Description of How Things Came to Work, and What Working Looks Like

---

Begin with something specific: a Tuesday in October, a neighborhood in what used to be called the industrial quarter of a medium-sized city on three continents simultaneously. The buildings are not identical—three different climates, three different traditions of shade and ventilation and stone—but they share a structure of purpose. The ground floors are open. People pass through them on the way to other things and stop because something interesting is happening. This was not accidental. The architects were given a brief: the building should create approximately forty-seven unplanned conversations per day per hundred residents. The number was derived from social network research on the correlation between weak-tie interaction and the two things the commissioning councils cared most about: individual wellbeing and collective problem-solving capacity. The architects thought the brief was strange. They built to it anyway. The forty-seven conversations happen.

Your daughter left for what she calls school an hour ago. It looks like a guild combined with a workshop: she spent the morning on mathematics calibrated precisely to the way her mind moves—intuitive, spatially oriented, drawn to pattern before abstraction—and this afternoon she will argue with a teacher who genuinely does not know the answer to the question under discussion and says so, because epistemic honesty about uncertainty is now explicitly a value the curriculum is designed to transmit. She will come home with dirt under her fingernails and three new questions she did not have when she woke up. You have learned to take the questions seriously, because the adults in her life who took questions seriously when she was small are now the people she most trusts, and trust is the load-bearing structure underneath everything else.

You have work to do. It is work you chose from among several options you actually wanted, because the economy of this world was redesigned—with enormous difficulty, over two decades of contested legislation and labor negotiation and genuine social upheaval—to ensure that the person who wants to do the work and the person who needs the work done find each other without either one being made desperate first. Desperation, it turned out, was expensive. Not just for the desperate person but for everyone who had to interact with them, and for every institution that had to manage the downstream effects of decisions made under duress. The Very Serious People who argued this case in the newspapers of the 2030s were not wrong that it was a solvable coordination problem. What they underestimated was how long solving it would take and how many times the solution would have to be rebuilt after being broken by people who benefited from the desperation.

By mid-morning you stop at the neighborhood's common kitchen for a second coffee and end up in a forty-five minute conversation you hadn't planned with a civil engineer and a retired schoolteacher and someone who turns out to have spent fifteen years studying the water table under the southern district. All three of them are right about different things. You go back to your work having thought about something you wouldn't have otherwise thought about. This happens most days. You have learned to recognize it as one of the mechanisms by which a society stays honest—the continuous, low-stakes collision of different kinds of knowledge, not in formal deliberation but in the ordinary friction of shared space. The common kitchen was deliberately designed to produce this friction. The design worked. Most of the good designs worked, once the institutions for testing them and learning from the tests were built.

This is not a perfect world. It is a world that has, with enormous difficulty and at considerable cost, comprehended itself as a coordination problem—and having comprehended this, has put the thought and experimentation into it that the comprehension demands.

## How the Comprehension Happened

The key insight arrived not as a revolution but as a slow literacy, the way arithmetic literacy arrived, the way reading arrived: unevenly, over generations, first among specialists and then among everyone, until the generation raised with it cannot imagine being without it.

The insight was this: coordination failures are failures of understanding first, and failures of will second. A world in which people drive into each other at intersections is not a world of people who want collisions; it is a world that has not yet invented traffic lights. A world in which workers and employers cannot find each other, or find each other only after burning enormous amounts of social and financial capital on the search, is not a world that wants unemployment; it is a world that has not yet invented the matching infrastructure adequate to the size of the problem. A world in which brilliant researchers in three cities are separately spending a decade each on the same problem that the three of them together would solve in two years is not a world that wants slow progress; it is a world that has not yet built the knowledge-sharing infrastructure that would let them find each other.

The literacy that grew over the course of the twenty-first century was the capacity to see these failures, name them with precision, and treat them as engineering problems with engineering solutions—social engineering, institutional engineering, the deliberately designed infrastructure of human cooperation.

This literacy was not invented from nothing. Game theory was already a century old. The science of institutions had been building for decades. Behavioral economics had been mapping the gap between how humans actually decide and how they were assumed to decide. What the crisis years of the 2030s added was urgency and, paradoxically, opportunity: enough existing systems broke badly enough that rebuilding them from better-understood principles became politically possible in ways it had not been before. And what the decade's new AI systems added was the capacity to hold more of the problem in view simultaneously than any previous generation of institution-builders had been able to.

The transition cost something real. Jobs disappeared faster than new ones arrived. Communities built around particular industries felt the ground shift under them. The distribution of the gains was unjust in ways that became a generation's central political argument. The argument was eventually resolved—partially, imperfectly, with significant regional variation—in the direction of wide distribution, because the alternative was a world in which the coordination gains were captured by a small fraction of the population, which is another way of saying a world that remained a coordination failure at the highest level.

The AI governance frameworks that held the transition together were not elegant. They were the product of bitter negotiation between incompatible positions, each of which was right about something, and they bear the marks of that origin. But they preserved three things that turned out to matter above all: human oversight of consequential decisions, real and enforced rather than theoretical and evaded; the diversity of human ways of life against optimization into uniformity; and the capacity for error correction, which requires that errors be visible and that the institutions capable of correcting them remain functional and independent. These were not guaranteed. They were chosen, repeatedly, by specific people making specific decisions, often against significant incentives pointing elsewhere.

## The Economy, Explained to a Visiting Child

If you explain the economic system of this world to a child from elsewhere, they ask why it wasn't done sooner. The answer involves a lot of things that are hard to explain to a child, including the concept of a coordination failure, which is the concept of a large group of people each making individually rational choices that together produce an outcome nobody wanted.

The land is held in a particular way: an ongoing auction-lease system captures the economic rent—the value that comes from location and from the social investment of the surrounding community—and returns it to the community that created it. If your land is valuable because you built something wonderful on it, you keep the value of what you built. If your land is valuable because you happen to be adjacent to a library and a transit hub and twenty years of neighborhood investment, the community captures that increase and reinvests it in the next library and the next transit hub. This was understood as elementary fairness by the second generation raised with it. To the generation that implemented it, it felt like a revolution.

Everyone receives a share of the planetary endowment—the resources that exist regardless of what anyone did, the raw materials and the spectrum and the airspace—as a baseline. This is not charity. It is the rent paid by those who use exclusive access to resources that belong to everyone, distributed to everyone. It is enough to keep the floor above subsistence for everyone not having children, which creates a particular incentive structure that the Very Serious People argued about for a generation before the current compromise was reached. It is not enough to live well. It is enough to survive badly, and to use the breathing room to find something better.

Jobs exist in abundance because the Very Serious People noticed that since medieval agricultural productivity had generated enough jobs for everyone in the medieval era, a society with a hundred times that productivity had no business struggling to employ its population. The observation sounds obvious. Removing the obstacles that prevented it from being obvious took two decades of painful institutional reform. Business licenses that served no function except revenue for the licensing authority were abolished. Credentialing requirements that demanded qualifications irrelevant to actual job performance were replaced by direct skill demonstration—you show that you can do the work, in the same way a programmer is hired, rather than showing a document that proves you paid tuition to an institution. The child apprenticeship system—older children teaching younger children, under external validation, in the things the older children have demonstrated they know—extended this logic down to the youngest workers and compressed the gap between learning and doing.

The economy runs hot enough that a hair salon wanting to expand has to work hard to find someone to hire. This is understood as the correct direction of the supply-demand asymmetry: employers compete for employees. Abusive workplaces cannot attract workers except by paying a premium that most abusive workplaces cannot afford. The premium required to retain staff at genuinely unpleasant jobs is high enough that the jobs tend to be automated if automation is possible, and if automation is not possible, the compensation is sufficient that people choose the work freely.

Innovation is compensated by an honor system with actual teeth: industries are expected to donate fifteen percent of the marginal value created by an invention to the people who invented it, verified by public audit. Nobody holds a patent veto. Anybody can build on any discovery. The discoverers are nonetheless compensated, because the honor obligation is enforced not by law but by something more reliable than law: the comprehensive public legibility of who paid and who didn't, combined with a culture that has internalized the connection between rewarding discovery and continuing to have discoveries to reward. Copyright lasts fourteen years. After that, what you made enters the common pool that made your making possible.

The pharmaceutical revolution that began in the late 2030s was primarily a restructuring of incentives: prizes paid for measured reductions in disease burden across real populations, replacing the patent monopoly system that had made diseases of the poor commercially uninteresting. The diseases that had killed the most people were no longer commercially uninteresting. Within fifteen years, the landscape of preventable death had changed beyond recognition. This was not a miracle. It was an alignment of incentives that any Very Serious Person could have described in 1990. The failure to implement it sooner is the kind of thing that the Chroniclers, when they write honestly, describe as a civilizational coordination failure of the first order.

## What the Institutions Look Like

The governing bodies of this world are multiple, overlapping, matched to scale. Climate governance requires coordination at planetary scale; watershed management at regional scale; school board decisions at neighborhood scale; the education of a particular child involves the people who know that particular child. The mistake of previous eras was to solve the wrong-scale problem with the wrong-scale institution: to make national law about things that require neighborhood judgment, or to leave planetary coordination to the goodwill of nations with incompatible incentives.

Everybody in this world knows what a Nash equilibrium is, the way everybody in a literate world knows what a sentence is. This is not because everyone has studied game theory—they haven't—but because the concept is now part of the civic curriculum from the age of eight, taught through the practice of actual coordination exercises, through examples from history and from daily life, through the experience of being in a classroom that collectively fails a coordination problem and then, with guidance, collectively solves it. The concept is available when it is needed. People notice when something looks like a coordination failure. They have language for it. They know what questions to ask.

The institutions for resolving disputes are separated carefully: the person who investigates is not the person who prosecutes, and neither is the person who adjudges, and none of these are the person who runs the correctional facility. Every decision is written down, with full reasoning, and reviewed by bodies with genuine independence. This produces slowness. The slowness is considered preferable to the alternative, which is a system optimized for speed that reliably produces the wrong answers. Every decision being visible means every decision is auditable, and auditability is the first line of defense against corruption, which is more reliable than trying to ensure that no person ever has enough power to be worth corrupting.

The Very Serious People—the visible intellectual class whose public debates function as the civilization's ongoing self-examination—are not rulers and do not have formal authority. Their authority is argumentative: they persuade. If they persuade wrongly, they are on record as having persuaded wrongly, and the record is kept and cited. Being publicly wrong about an important prediction is costly enough, reputationally, to make intellectual honesty more rational than tribal loyalty in most circumstances. Being privately wrong has no special cost over being publicly wrong, because the practice of writing everything down means there is very little that is only privately known.

The Chroniclers—somewhere between judge and journalist and scientist—receive fifteen percent of their funding from the commons they serve and the rest from voluntary contribution, in proportions that have been argued about since the institution was created and will continue to be argued about. They are expected to show their reasoning, cite their sources, and have their work reviewed by peers with the same standards they are expected to meet. They are also expected to describe difficult things in language that nonspecialists can understand, which is considered a skill that requires training rather than a concession to incompetence. The ones who do this best are among the most honored people in the civilization. The ones who cannot do it are considered to have failed at something important.

## The Texture of Daily Life

Cities smell of rain and bread and something green—the wetlands constructed in what used to be ring roads, the rooftop farms integrated into the building codes of three generations ago, the ash and poplar corridors that the urban ecology studies of the 2040s found to be the most cost-effective intervention for air quality and pedestrian comfort and biodiversity simultaneously. The presence of other species was chosen, by communities deciding what they wanted to live among. The choosing mattered as much as the outcome: people who have decided together what their city should be like have a different relationship to that city than people who inherited it.

Children move through environments calibrated to what their minds actually do. The tutoring systems know each child's learning style in finer grain than any human teacher could maintain across thirty students simultaneously—approaching the same concept from dozens of angles until one lands, connecting a lesson in arithmetic to the child's specific interest in whatever the child is specifically interested in. The human teachers become more important, not less: the irreducibly human work of mentorship, modeling, the experience of being genuinely seen by another person, cannot be automated, and the systems were designed to make these experiences more possible rather than to replace them. The children arrive at adulthood knowing the difference between what they know and what they merely believe. They can update on evidence. This does not mean they are always right. It means they can be wrong gracefully, which is the prerequisite for being less wrong over time.

People sleep eight hours. This seems minor until you trace the consequences through: the emotional regulation that adequate rest makes possible, the quality of attention available for other people, the reduction in the small cruelties that chronic exhaustion produces. A civilization that takes the biological reality of human bodies seriously looks, from the outside, like a civilization that has made a series of small adjustments. From the inside, it feels like a different world.

Mental health care is unremarkable—which is to say universal, unhurried, and largely ordinary. The transformation came from recognizing that most of what had been pathologized and treated individually was the predictable product of environments that violated basic human needs. You can medicate isolation into tolerability, or you can end the isolation. The Flourishing did not abandon pharmacology, but it invested heavily in structural causes, and the results were visible within a generation. The skills of relationship—genuine listening, conflict navigation, the cultivation of trust across difference—are now in the explicit curriculum, taught with the same rigor as mathematics, starting young, because they were found to be teachable and were found to be foundational.

People are not meant to find their mates alone in a world this large. Professional matchmakers—something between a real estate broker and a very well-trained therapist—know their clients more thoroughly than their clients' friends do and are compensated on long-term outcome measures rather than on volume. This is one example of the general principle: whenever there is a large allocation problem with high value on either side, the Very Serious People will have spent considerable effort building institutions to solve it. The assumption that individuals should navigate large and complex markets alone, without professional intermediation calibrated to their particular needs, is considered as strange as the assumption that you should try to diagnose your own illness from first principles.

## The Question of What Cannot Be Fixed

The oldest woman in the neighborhood is ninety-one. She was born in a city that no longer exists under that name, in a country whose borders have since been redrawn three times. She has outlived two husbands and one of her children. She will tell you, if you ask, that the world is incomparably better than the one she was born into, and also that the incomparably better world still contains her dead child, and that no amount of structural improvement changes what that is.

People still die. Later than before, and better—with more choice about how, with less pain, with more likely dignity—but they still die, and the people who loved them still suffer the suffering of that loss. The research into the aging process is serious and ongoing and has extended median healthy lifespan by more than two decades in this century and is nowhere near finished. The debates about how hard to push this research, what it means, what a world of dramatically longer lives requires in terms of social redesign—these debates are lively and unresolved. Most communities seem to have settled, provisionally, on this: the goal is not the elimination of the hard things but the provision of sufficient ground that people can navigate the hard things without also being destroyed by the unnecessary things. The unnecessary things—preventable death, avoidable suffering, pain that teaches nothing—are in steep decline. The inherent things are still there. The work continues.

People still conflict. The world of the middle twenty-first century is less at war than any previous era in recorded history, which is not the same as being at peace. The governance structures that contain conflict are strong enough to have prevented the civilizational catastrophes that earlier generations most feared, and imperfect enough to have failed in specific places and times in ways that are still being repaired. The failures are documented and studied rather than quietly absorbed into official optimism. The study of failure is one of the things the Chroniclers are explicitly charged with, and one of the things they are best at.

## Once a Year

In every neighborhood, once a year, there is a gathering that visitors tend to remember longest.

Over three days, people bring what they have learned. The structure is intentionally open: no credential required to speak, no hierarchy of legitimate knowledge enforced at the door. A farmer who has spent twenty years learning the behavior of a particular estuary speaks about that estuary. A mechanic who has rebuilt the same engine four hundred times describes what she has noticed that no manual records. A historian speaks. An AI researcher and the ninety-one-year-old woman sit at the same long table and argue about whether the grief we feel for people we have lost is one of the things worth preserving or one of the things worth eliminating. Neither of them wins. Both of them leave having thought something they hadn't thought before.

The gathering grew out of older traditions—the harvest feast, the salon, the town meeting—but was deliberately redesigned to serve a specific civic function: to remind people that knowledge does not live only in institutions, that every life contains genuine understanding, and that the conversation between different kinds of knowledge is where the unexpected discoveries happen. It also serves as an annual regrounding in the fact that the people you live among have inner lives as interesting and complicated as your own. The social research is unambiguous: this reduces the ease with which those people can be othered in subsequent months. It is, in both senses of the old word, an education.

The children run through it freely. The food is excellent because food has been understood for decades as a public good in the same category as parks and roads—something the community invests in together because everyone benefits and the investment pays for itself in the texture of daily life it enables. You are not late for anything. You have come to believe, slowly and with evidence, that the world is worth working on.

The morning light moves across three cities simultaneously, at different angles, through different air, finding everywhere the same construction: people in the act of figuring out, together, what kind of place this should be.

This is not finished.

All of it is possible.

The work is already underway.
