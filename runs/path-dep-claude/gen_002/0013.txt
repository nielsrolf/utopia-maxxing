# The Adequate World: A Letter to Those Who Will Live In It

---

You are reading this in a century that has not yet been named. Naming centuries, it turned out, was something people stopped doing once they became less preoccupied with where they had been and more preoccupied with where they were going. The historians argue about this. They argue about everything, loudly, in public, where anyone can watch and disagree. This is considered one of the better features of the present arrangement.

Let me tell you how things work.

---

## I. The Texture of an Ordinary Day

A fifteen-year-old in Lagos wakes up knowing that today she will spend four hours with a master ceramicist and three hours debugging irrigation software for a cooperative farm on the outskirts of the city. Neither of these was assigned to her. Both were found for her—or found by her, with help—through a process that began when she was nine and will not conclude until she dies, or possibly not even then.

She is not unusual. This is the point.

The world she moves through looks, to someone transported from the early twenty-first century, recognizably human. Markets exist. Families argue at dinner. People fall catastrophically in love and then, sometimes, fall catastrophically out of it. Old women lose at chess to children. The enormous ordinary machinery of human life continues: grief, humor, the argument about whether the new building on the corner is beautiful or merely expensive, the particular satisfaction of a meal cooked for people you like.

What is different is harder to see at first, because it is mostly about what is *absent*. There is a frequency of dread that simply does not exist here. Not the dread of death or loss or failure—those remain, and the serious people have argued for generations about whether eliminating them would even be desirable. The absent frequency is more specific: the fear of being discarded by an institution that has no structural reason to serve you. The fear of being unable to access what you need because access was rationed by conditions that had nothing to do with whether you needed it. The fear that the systems surrounding your life were oriented, in aggregate, against you.

These fears were not eliminated by becoming less true. They were eliminated by becoming less true.

---

## II. How That Happened

The correction was not a revolution. This is worth stating clearly, because the temptation to narrate it as a revolution is strong, and the narrative would be false.

What happened was something more like a slow upgrade of institutional firmware, running in parallel across thousands of jurisdictions over several generations, coordinated loosely through a growing practice of publishing results. The experimental disposition—the willingness to try different approaches and share the outcomes honestly, including the embarrassing ones—turned out to be contagious. Once enough places were doing it, the places that weren't began to look stubborn rather than prudent.

The core insight, when it finally became common property rather than expert knowledge, fit on a placard but took decades to fully trace: *systems behave according to what they measure and reward, not according to what they intend.* Courts that measured process produced process. Schools that measured test performance produced test performance. Hospitals that measured procedures performed produced procedures. The gap between what institutions said they were for and what they actually optimized toward was, in aggregate, a civilizational tax paid by everyone, every day, invisibly.

Closing that gap required two things that had previously been scarce: accurate information and what might be called *coordinacy literacy*—the widespread, not merely expert, understanding of how incentive structures work, what a commons is and how it can be destroyed, why sophisticated people who understand coordination theory in the abstract still routinely defect from the principles they can explain in lectures.

That last part was the hardest. Intelligence was never the bottleneck. The old records—preserved by the people whose job is preserving and accurately describing things—show that the most sophisticated actors of the previous era understood, in rigorous terms, exactly why the systems they were gaming were failing. They could give you the game theory. And then they would make the locally rational move that was globally destructive, because they had decided that their situation was special, their cause important enough, their reasoning sufficiently subtle to justify the exception.

What changed was not intelligence. It was something more like a *grain* in the culture—a texture running through childhood education and public argument and professional norms—that said, without softening it: *the rules that hold civilization together are made of people following them when it is inconvenient. You are one of those people. This is one of those occasions.* This grain was taught. It can be taught. The evidence that it can be taught is that it was taught, here, over generations, until it became the water people swim in.

The economic changes followed from this as much as from any policy intervention. Occupational licensing was examined, jurisdiction by jurisdiction, with one simple question: does this requirement actually predict performance on the job? Almost universally, the honest answer was *not much, and there are better ways to measure*. The barriers dissolved where evidence required them to dissolve. What replaced them was direct assessment of actual performance—structured trials, transparent track records, professional reputations built in public view. The labor market became, and has remained, what someone from an earlier time would recognize as hot: employers pursue workers more than workers pursue employers. This is not a temporary condition. It results from having removed the accumulated friction that previously kept the market cold, and friction does not naturally regenerate once removed.

The fifteen-year-old in Lagos has never had to pretend that four years of studying something constitutes evidence she can do it. She has simply been doing things, in increasing complexity, since she was old enough to do things. The idea that you would spend years preparing before spending a single day trying the actual work became, when stated plainly, obviously absurd—and once enough people said it plainly, it stopped being the default.

## III. The Three Things You Cannot Do Without

The architects of this world—insofar as it had architects, which is contested—eventually agreed that three elements were individually insufficient and jointly necessary. You cannot have one without the others and expect the structure to hold.

**The first is universal coordinacy literacy.** Every child, regardless of where they are born, learns what we call the three foundations: the tools for handling language and number, which are ancient; and what might be translated as *coordinacy*—the understanding of how incentives shape behavior, what collective action problems are and why they emerge, why a world of people each making locally sensible decisions can nevertheless produce globally terrible outcomes. This is not taught as ideology. It is taught as vocabulary—the minimum shared language for participating in a civilization that intends to function. A child who leaves adolescence without it cannot be called educated, any more than a child who cannot read. The content is genuinely neutral: it does not tell you which values to hold. It tells you how to reason about what pursuing your values will actually produce.

**The second is accurate, public, verifiable information.** The Chroniclers emerged from the wreckage of twenty-first-century information ecosystems, which had produced an environment where the problem was not scarcity of information but weaponization of it. The Chroniclers are a professional class—well-funded from collective contributions, structurally isolated from political and commercial pressure—whose job is to produce the most accurate possible accounts of events and to reason carefully about their causes and implications. They are not neutral in the sense of refusing to evaluate; they evaluate constantly. What they do is show their reasoning in full, quantify their uncertainty explicitly, make predictions that can later be checked, and correct errors prominently rather than quietly. Their closest ancestors were certain science journalists and certain judges who wrote careful opinions. Those traditions were professionalized, scaled, and made into something closer to infrastructure.

The Chroniclers are not loved. They are trusted, which is more durable.

**The third is an economy that cannot run cold.** The old arrangement—by which workers competed desperately for jobs, creating conditions under which abuse, credential inflation, and arbitrary gatekeeping could flourish—was recognized as the source of a cascade of other failures. The correction involved, primarily, removing the barriers that had caused it: the regulatory capture embedded in licensing regimes, the land and resource rents that extracted value without producing it, the credential requirements that limited supply of labor without improving its quality. The economic rent on land and natural resources is now captured collectively and distributed universally—not as charity, but as the dividend on assets that no one created and therefore no one can justly claim exclusively. On top of this floor, the market runs. Under it, no one falls.

## IV. The Architecture of Diversity

There is no world government. This is not a failure of ambition. It is the result of understanding, clearly and widely, that diversity of governance is how you run experiments, and running experiments is how you learn.

There are instead tens of thousands of jurisdictions arranged in overlapping layers, from neighborhood compacts to city-states to regional federations to the loose planetary coordination bodies that handle the genuinely planetary problems: atmospheric chemistry, the governance of systems powerful enough to affect everyone, the handful of human rights that are treated as floors beneath which no jurisdiction may fall. Below those floors, diversity is actively cultivated.

Moving between jurisdictions is easy. This is essential. If exit is unavailable, diversity is oppression. If exit is available, diversity is a portfolio you can navigate personally, and most jurisdictions make leaving easy because they know their legitimacy depends on people choosing to stay.

Some regions have tried collective property arrangements; some have tried nearly absolute individual property rights. Some cities enforce strict noise limits; others do not. Some communities have organized themselves around religious practice; others are aggressively secular. The results are public. The Chroniclers write everything up. The experiments inform each other.

The matching-brokers—a profession that emerged from the recognition that connecting people to work suited to them was a legitimate problem worth solving—are paid on long-term outcomes rather than placements. The fifteen-year-old's advisor receives her best compensation if, in twenty years, the woman the girl has become reports that her work feels meaningful. This creates an incentive structure that was previously missing entirely. The matching-brokers are not perfect; they are vastly better than nothing, and they improve continuously because their performance is tracked.

## V. What the Quiet Cities Are

When someone cannot find a place in the broader economy—through misfortune, or through the particular misfit between their constitution and the available niches, or through failure of one kind or another, so long as they have not harmed others—there are communities built to receive them.

These are not prisons or poorhouses. They are well-funded, because the alternative—visible human catastrophe at the margins—registers correctly as civilizational failure, and the Chroniclers write about nothing else until it is addressed. They are funded at around fifteen percent of the contributed income of those not needing them, directed through a competitive landscape of provider institutions that are evaluated regularly for effectiveness.

They ask one thing of those who settle in them permanently: that they not bring children into the permanent-settlement arrangement if they can avoid it. This is a norm, not a law. It is held in place by public argument rather than enforcement—by the widely shared, if uncomfortable, understanding that suffering transmits across generations in ways that compound, and that a civilization concerned with people who will exist in the future cannot be entirely indifferent to the conditions those people will inherit. The debates about where to draw this line are ongoing. They may always be ongoing. That the debates happen openly, with the Chroniclers recording all sides, is itself part of the point.

## VI. What Was Not Solved, And How That Is Handled

A researcher—I will call her M, because she has asked that her full name not be used in documents like this one—has spent thirty years on what she describes as the central unsolved problem of this arrangement. She presents at the annual Coordinacy Symposium every year, and every year her talk is the most attended.

Her concern is this: a civilization very good at coordination tends to coordinate itself into particular shapes of human life and leave insufficient room for the other shapes. The people for whom the three literacies feel like a second language rather than a birthright. The people who are genuinely alien to the consensus categories, who do not fit neatly into any niche, who experience the well-functioning economy not as liberation but as a kind of pressure. The things that cannot be funded because they have not yet been named. The forms of human flourishing that our instruments were not built to detect.

She also worries about a subtler problem: the transition from a scarcity-organized attention to an abundance-organized attention. When nothing threatens your survival, you must choose, from a much wider menu, what to care about. Many people find this harder than they expected. The anxiety that previously organized attention—told you exactly what to worry about, gave you a clear enemy—has been removed, and nothing has been built to replace the organizing function it performed. Many newcomers to this world, arriving from harder times, report that freedom is more effortful than they anticipated.

M presents on this at the Symposium. Others argue with her. Some conduct experiments. The Chroniclers write it up. This is not a satisfying resolution. It is a process, which is different from a resolution, and which has the advantage of continuing.

The willingness to name unresolved problems clearly, to resist the temptation to paper over them with optimistic framing, is itself one of the features of the present arrangement that people are most attached to. The previous era's tendency to produce confident official accounts of a world that was working better than it was—because the institutions producing those accounts were optimized for reassurance rather than accuracy—was recognized, eventually, as one of the deepest sources of the failures that followed. The correction was not pessimism. It was precision about what was good, what was bad, and what was genuinely uncertain.

## VII. The Gradient Beyond Home

Earth and its immediate neighborhood are, by strong social norm, human-scale. Not because technology is distrusted, but because certain modifications to the environment of daily life have second-order effects on human psychology that are difficult to reverse and important to guard against.

The engagement-optimization engines of the early social media era were the canonical example: individually rational for each platform, collectively catastrophic for the attention environment of the species. The correction required coordination that was previously impossible, and it was one of the early victories of the new institutional architecture. What replaced them were communication systems designed around a different goal: helping people find what they were actually looking for, rather than maximizing the time they spent looking.

Beyond the home world, the solar system is not uniform. As you move outward—to the orbital habitats, to the further settlements, to the communities running at the edges of what was previously imaginable—you encounter experiments that the near world, by mutual agreement, does not import wholesale. Some are cognitive modifications. Some are social arrangements that would be destabilizing at scale. Some are forms of experience so intense they would, if broadcast freely into ordinary life, make ordinary life feel diminished by comparison—and the outer communities do not broadcast them, for the same reason the near world never rebuilt the engagement-optimization engines: some competitions are negative-sum, and recognizing this is the beginning of declining to play.

The deal between the near world and the far is never written in a single document. It is embedded in institutional architecture over generations. The near world is preserved for human-scale values. The far world is not forcibly converted to human-scale values. The people who care about grandchildren continuing to fall in love and cook meals accept that the distant habitats will organize themselves according to principles they may not fully understand. The people who care about the vast scales accept that Earth will not be converted to serve those scales. These values are nearly compatible. You lose almost nothing of what you care about by accepting that someone else gets the domain they care about that you were never going to want anyway.

## VIII. What This World Is Most Proud Of

Not the hot economy, though people from harder times find that most immediately impressive.

Not the Chroniclers, though accurate information is doing more work than almost anything else.

It is something smaller and harder to point to: the texture, running through how children are raised and how adults argue and what the Very Serious People celebrate and what they condemn, that says *you are not the exception to the principles that hold this world together.*

Sophisticated defectors in the previous era were not stupid. They could explain, in precise terms, why defection was destructive. They understood the theory perfectly. They defected anyway, because in this specific case, their cause was important enough, their reasoning subtle enough, their situation different enough to justify setting aside the general rule.

What this world learned to teach—and what has held, and what the Chroniclers watch for signs of erosion in, constantly—is not cleverness. It is the recognition that the rules are made of people following them when it is inconvenient, and you are one of those people, and this is one of those occasions. It was taught. It can be taught. The evidence that it can be taught is that it was taught, here, over generations.

It is the most fragile thing this world has and the most important.

So far, it is holding.

---

## IX. The Afternoon, Continued

The fifteen-year-old in Lagos throws a bowl today that is not good. The ceramicist tells her this directly and explains why, in terms she can use. She does not feel diminished by this; she has been raised in an environment where the feedback is assumed to be about the bowl, not about her. She will try again tomorrow.

In the afternoon, she finds a bug in the irrigation software that two engineers missed. She flags it. A senior engineer thanks her. She is compensated a small amount, because she did something useful, and here useful things are compensated. She goes home and has an argument with her younger brother about whether the new concert hall is beautiful or merely expensive.

Somewhere much further out, in a community the near world mostly communicates with by text because the experiential bandwidth would be disorienting, beings who began as human are working on problems that do not yet have names in any language currently spoken on Earth. They are doing this carefully. They have time.

On the orbital platforms between inner and outer—neither fully near nor fully far, running their experiments in the gradient—people who have chosen a life of transition are living in ways that will inform both ends of the spectrum.

M will present at the Symposium in a few months. She has found a new category of person the system is failing to notice. She is not entirely sure yet how to describe them. She is working on it.

No one is in charge of all of this. That is, finally, the point. The architecture was designed to not require anyone to be in charge of all of it—to make local decisions local, global decisions genuinely minimal, and the space in between vast enough for every kind of human life that any human might reasonably want to live.

It is not perfect. Perfection was never the goal. The goal was an adequate equilibrium—clearly better than what came before, stable enough to persist, flexible enough to improve.

By those standards, it is doing rather well.

The Chroniclers, reviewing the last century, agree.

They have noted several things that could go wrong.

They have published their reasoning.

You are welcome to argue with them.

That is, in fact, why they publish.

---

*Welcome. Please find someone who knows what they are doing and ask them to show you around. They will probably be glad to. Competent people here tend to share what they know—it turns out this is what happens when knowledge is understood as a commons that grows when it moves, rather than a credential that loses value when it is distributed.*

*If you cannot find someone, ask a Chronicler. They are the ones writing everything down.*

*If you find something they have missed, tell them.*

*That is how the world improves.*
