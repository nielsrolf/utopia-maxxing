# The Weavers of Kael Dara

*Being an account of the world as it came to be, for those who wake into it*

---

You will have questions. That is good. Questions are how this world was built, and how it is maintained, and how—if we are lucky—it will be improved.

Let me tell you about the place you have arrived in.

---

## I. How the World Is Organized

The first thing visitors notice, coming from earlier times, is the silence where anxiety used to live. Not silence in the literal sense—Kael Dara is full of sound, children teaching other children in the learning-squares, the hum of fabricators producing whatever someone has decided they need today, laughter from the tea-houses where the *sharad* conduct their public debates. What is absent is the specific frequency of dread: the fear of being discarded by an economy, the fear of a medical bill, the fear that an institution you need has no reason to serve you.

These fears were recognized, some centuries ago, as coordination failures rather than facts of nature. The architects of this world spent a long time on the problem before accepting that the solution had three parts, and that you could not have any one without the others.

The first part: *Everyone learns the same foundations.* Every child in Kael Dara, regardless of where they are born, receives what we call the *three literacies*—letters and numbers, yes, but also what might be translated as *coordinacy*: the understanding of Nash equilibria, of Pareto frontiers, of what a commons is and how it can be destroyed and why you might not want to do that. A child who has not grasped these by adolescence cannot be called educated, any more than a child who cannot read. This is not indoctrination into any particular conclusion; it is simply the baseline vocabulary for participating in a civilization that intends to function.

The second part: *Information about how things are going must be accurate and public.* The *Chroniclers* are the closest thing we have to priests, and what they worship is precise description. They are paid, collectively, out of the fund that every working person contributes to; their compensation is highest when they are later proven to have been accurate, and there are formal penalties—not legal, but social, which here carries more weight—for Chroniclers who are caught shaping narratives toward preferred conclusions. They write everything out. All their reasoning. Anyone can inspect it. Superior Chroniclers can rebuke junior ones in public, and frequently do.

The third part—and this took the longest to get right: *The economy must be genuinely unable to run cold.* The old world's arrangement, by which workers competed for jobs rather than employers competing for workers, was recognized as the source of a cascade of other failures: the abuse that becomes possible when someone can be threatened with destitution, the credentials-as-barrier-to-entry that consumed years of human life and produced very little, the confusion of "has this piece of paper" with "can do this thing." We corrected this the way you correct any misalignment: by removing the barriers that had caused it. The economic rent on land and raw resources is captured and distributed. The licensing regimes that limited supply of labor without improving its quality were dissolved. The result is what a student of the old world might recognize as a hot labor market, except that it has been this way for so long that no one thinks of it as a condition. It is simply what an economy is.

## II. How Work Happens

There is no minimum age to work, because no one ever established a principled reason why minimum age would correlate with ability to do the specific thing being asked. A twelve-year-old who can teach arithmetic to nine-year-olds—and many can—is allowed to do so, supervised and supported, and is compensated. This is not exploitation; exploitation requires the possibility of refusal being too costly. Here, refusal is always possible. The twelve-year-old is doing it because it is satisfying and because someone thought to ask.

Older children teach younger ones as a matter of course. This is not merely efficient (though it is efficient; the act of teaching consolidates understanding in the teacher at a rate no other method matches). It is a recognition that the person who knows a thing can transmit it, and that demanding additional credentials beyond *knowing the thing* is the civilizational equivalent of rust: accumulating drag on everything it touches.

What the system watches for is ignorance iterating on itself—a bad teacher who produces bad students who teach others badly. This is why every teaching relationship has external validation, why the Chroniclers maintain public records of educational outcomes, why there are master-teachers whose role is oversight rather than direct instruction. Trust but measure. Pay for what you're actually trying to produce.

When a person cannot find a niche in Civilization—through no fault of their own, or even through fault of their own, so long as they have not harmed others—there are the Quiet Cities. These are not prisons or poorhouses. They are subsidized communities for people who need them, running on the collective donation of those who don't. The donation rate here is around fifteen percent of income, directed through a competitive landscape of institutions that the Chroniclers evaluate regularly for effectiveness. The Quiet Cities are well-funded because the alternative—a visible human catastrophe at the margins of society—would correctly register as a civilizational failure, and the Very Serious People would write about nothing else until it was fixed.

One condition attaches to the Quiet Cities, and it is the most controversial feature of this world to explain to newcomers: they ask that you not have children if you are settling there permanently. This is not enforced; there is no enforcement. It is a norm, held in place by the understanding—widely shared and publicly argued about—that inherited suffering compounds across generations, and that a world that cares about people who will exist in the future cannot be indifferent to the constitutions those people will carry. The debates about where to draw this line have not concluded. They may never conclude. The Very Serious People are still arguing loudly, and occasionally someone runs an experiment.

## III. How Decisions Get Made

There are no nations in the old sense. There are regions, and the regions have different rules, and you can move between them. The great coordination insight was that the value of having *different* places is exactly the ability to run experiments and house people with incompatible preferences. Somewhere there is a city that enforces strict noise-quiet hours. Somewhere else, there is one that does not. Somewhere there are communities with collectively managed property; somewhere there are communities where property rights are nearly absolute. The regions are not isolated—they are connected by trade and communication and the free movement of people—but they are genuinely different.

This produces a lot of messiness and a certain amount of friction at the borders between approaches. The Very Serious People write about this friction constantly. The friction is considered a reasonable price for the ability to discover which approaches work.

Governance within regions follows a principle of *radical visibility*. A decision-maker is not called legitimate because she holds an office; she is called legitimate in proportion to how clearly she has articulated the reasoning behind her decisions, how accurately those decisions can be checked against stated goals after the fact, and how explicitly the people affected have authorized her to speak for them. The phrase that gets translated into every regional language is roughly: "power proportional to demonstrated accuracy." You earn the right to make large decisions by being right about smaller ones in public, in ways that can be verified.

There are courts, but they are strange by old-world standards. The person who arrests is not the person who prosecutes is not the person who judges is not the person who runs the correctional facility. These separations were purchased dearly, after long argument, because it was recognized that combining them created terrible incentive alignments. The courts are paid to produce reliable reasoning, not favorable outcomes for any party. They write everything out. It can all be inspected.

## IV. What We Have Not Yet Solved

There is a researcher—I will call her Miri—who has spent the last twenty years working on what she describes as the central unsolved problem of Kael Dara. She presents at the annual Coordinacy Symposium every year, and every year her talk is the most attended, and every year she ends it by saying: *we still don't know if we're happy, or if we've just gotten very good at measuring things that correlate with happiness while the real thing slips away from us.*

Miri is not alone in this concern.

The world is, by most measures, working. People are fed and housed and occupied and connected. The Chroniclers' graphs mostly go in the right direction. The Quiet Cities are funded and not overcrowded. The labor market is hot. The courts are slow but not corrupt. The experiments in the regions keep producing information.

What Miri worries about is more subtle. She worries that a civilization very good at coordination might coordinate itself into a particular shape of human life and leave insufficient room for the other shapes. She worries about the people who cannot find a niche not because the economy excluded them, but because they are genuinely alien to the consensus categories—the ones for whom the *three literacies* feel like a second language, not a birthright. She worries that the fifteen-percent donation norm produces a certain kind of charitable institution and systematically fails to produce another kind, one that has not been named yet and so cannot be funded.

She worries, in short, that there are things this civilization is failing to notice because its instruments were built to notice other things.

The way this worry is handled is: Miri presents it at the Symposium. Others argue with her. Some conduct experiments. The Chroniclers write it up. It becomes part of the public account of where we are and what might be wrong.

This is not a satisfying resolution. It is a process, which is different.

## V. The One Thing We Are Most Proud Of

It is not the hot economy, though people from harder times find that most immediately impressive.

It is not the Chroniclers, though accurate information is doing more work than almost anything else.

It is, if we are honest, something smaller and harder to point to: the general internalization of the idea that *you are not the exception to the principles that run the world.*

In the old accounts—which the Chroniclers preserve, because the Chroniclers preserve everything—the most common failure mode of sophisticated people was not stupidity. Sophisticated people understood coordination theory. They could explain, in rigorous terms, why defection was destructive. They could give lectures on it. And then they would defect, because in this specific case, with these specific stakes, their situation was different, their reasoning was special, their cause was important enough to justify setting aside the general rule.

What changed was not intelligence. It was something closer to—the word is difficult to translate—a kind of *grain* in the culture, a texture that runs through childhood education and adult conversation and the way the Very Serious People write and the things that get celebrated. The grain says: *the rules that hold civilization together are made of people following them when it is inconvenient, and you are one of those people, and this is an occasion.*

This is taught. It can be taught. The evidence is that it was taught, here, over generations, until it became the water people swim in without noticing.

It is the most fragile thing we have and the most important. The Chroniclers watch for signs of its erosion constantly. So far, it is holding.

## VI. A Note for Those Just Arrived

You will find some things better than what you remember and some things worse and some things simply different in ways that are neither.

The matching-brokers who help people find partners are better than whatever you had before but still not good enough; Miri has a side project on this.

The music is more melodic than some of you will prefer. There are regions where the louder, more chaotic traditions are preserved, and if you want to find them, the Chroniclers' directories will point you toward them.

The food is excellent everywhere, because competition for labor applies to cooks as to anyone else, and cooks who are treated badly find other work immediately.

The thing you will miss most, coming from a world that ran on anxiety, is not anxiety itself. It is the *simplicity* of anxiety—the way it organized your attention, told you exactly what to worry about, gave you a clear enemy in the form of whatever threatened your survival. This world requires a more effortful kind of engagement. No one is threatening your survival, so you must choose, from a wider menu, what to care about. Many newcomers find this harder than they expected.

Miri says this is itself a coordination problem, that we have not yet built the right structures to help people navigate the transition from scarcity-organized attention to abundance-organized attention. She is probably right.

She will present on it at the Symposium next year.

You are welcome to attend. You are welcome to argue with her.

That is, in fact, why the Symposium exists.

*Welcome to Kael Dara. Please find someone who knows what they're doing and ask them to show you around. They will probably be glad to. Competent people here are often surprisingly eager to share what they know—it turns out this is what happens when knowledge is not hoarded as a credential but understood as a commons that grows when it moves.*

*If you cannot find someone, ask a Chronicler. They are the ones writing everything down.*
