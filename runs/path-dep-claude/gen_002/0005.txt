# The Flourishing Century: A Vision of Human Thriving

*Somewhere around 2047, a retired epidemiologist named Amara Osei gave a lecture at the University of Accra that began with a simple observation: her grandmother had died of malaria at forty-two, her mother had nearly died of it at thirty, and she herself had never once contracted it. Her students—most of them born after the great disease campaigns of the 2030s—looked at her with the particular polite incomprehension of people hearing about a vanished world. To them, malaria was what scurvy was to her: a word from history books, something that happened before.*

---

This essay is an attempt to describe a world that is genuinely within our reach—not the product of any single breakthrough or savior technology, but of sustained, compounding human effort across dozens of domains simultaneously. It is a world that arrives not with a bang but through the patient accumulation of better decisions, better institutions, better tools, and better understanding of ourselves. It is, in the language of the epigraph above, a world where children grow up finding the catastrophes of our era as remote and strange as we find the Black Death.

What follows is not prediction. It is a target.

---

## The Energy Foundation

Everything else in this essay rests on a single enabling condition: the world of 2045 runs on cheap, abundant, clean energy.

The story of how this happened is less dramatic than futurists once imagined. Solar costs continued their exponential decline long past the point where analysts said they would plateau. Fourth-generation fission reactors—smaller, modular, meltdown-proof by physical law rather than by engineered safeguard—gained regulatory approval first in Finland, then South Korea, then a cascade of other nations as the safety record accumulated and the economic case became overwhelming. Grid-scale storage, improved by materials science advances in sodium-sulfur and iron-air batteries, solved the intermittency problem that had once seemed intractable. By 2038, the marginal cost of electricity in most of the world had fallen below anything that seemed plausible a decade earlier.

The consequences rippled outward in ways that compounded. Desalination became economically trivial, relieving water stress across the Sahel, the Middle East, and the American Southwest. Vertical farming—long economically marginal—became competitive, reducing agriculture's land footprint and making fresh produce available in cities that had never had reliable access to it. Industrial processes that had seemed permanently carbon-intensive discovered electrified alternatives. The energy transition was not primarily a story of sacrifice and substitution. It was a story of getting something better and cheaper at the same time, which turned out to be a much easier political sell.

The geopolitical consequences were equally profound, if slower to materialize. Petrostates faced a structural reckoning. Some managed the transition thoughtfully, using hydrocarbon revenues during the final export years to build diversified economies. Others did not, and the instability that followed occupied international institutions for years. But the direction of travel was clear: the resource curse that had poisoned governance across much of the developing world for a century began, slowly, to lift. Nations that had been beholden to oil export revenues found themselves in a position to chart their own economic futures. This was not automatic—it required real political struggle—but the underlying constraint had been removed.

## The Biological Revolution

If energy was the foundation, biology was the cathedral built upon it.

The tools had been accumulating for decades: CRISPR gene editing in increasingly precise and targeted forms, mRNA vaccine platforms that could be reconfigured against a new pathogen in weeks, cell therapies that trained immune systems to identify and destroy cancer, optogenetic techniques that allowed researchers to probe neural circuits with unprecedented specificity. What changed in the 2030s was the velocity of discovery, driven by a new generation of research AI systems—not superintelligences, but extremely capable scientific collaborators that could synthesize the vast literature of biology, identify overlooked connections, design experiments, analyze results, and propose next steps at a pace no human team could match.

The crucial distinction, which early skeptics missed, was that these systems were not merely analyzing existing data. They were directing the generation of new data—running experiments through robotic laboratory systems, iterating on hypotheses in hours rather than months, and occasionally making the kind of lateral connection (the CRISPR moment, as biologists came to call any such discovery, after the original insight that a bacterial immune system could be repurposed for gene editing) that opened entirely new avenues of research.

The results, accumulated over fifteen years, transformed medicine:

Infectious disease largely became a solved problem in the developed world by the mid-2030s, and in the developing world by the early 2040s—a decade later, and harder-won, but achieved. The tool that made global eradication possible was not, in the end, a single miracle vaccine but a combination of rapidly deployable mRNA platforms, improved disease surveillance systems, and mosquito population modification that broke the transmission chains of malaria and dengue. Amara Osei's students could not get malaria because there were no longer enough Anopheles mosquitoes carrying the parasite to sustain transmission across most of sub-Saharan Africa. The mosquitoes were not extinct—ecologists had argued successfully against that—but gene-drive modified populations had replaced them in the regions where transmission had been endemic, carrying a payload that rendered them incapable of harboring the parasite.

Cancer death rates, which had been declining roughly two percent per year through the 2020s, accelerated dramatically once AI-assisted research began producing a new generation of targeted therapies. The key insight was individualization: cancers are not diseases but families of diseases, each patient's tumor genomically unique, and the computational tools to design patient-specific treatment regimens became both available and affordable by the late 2030s. Mortality from most common cancers fell by seventy to ninety percent. A residual tail of rare and aggressive malignancies persists, still being worked on, still killing people—but the disease that once killed one in four humans in wealthy nations became something closer to a manageable chronic condition for most who develop it, and a survivable acute illness for many more.

The picture in neuroscience is more complicated, and the progress harder to summarize, but its effects on human wellbeing may ultimately be the most important of all. Depression, PTSD, and addiction—conditions that affected hundreds of millions and caused incalculable suffering—yielded to a combination of new pharmacology, targeted neural interventions, and AI-assisted behavioral therapy that could be delivered at scale. The AI therapist did not replace human therapists; what it did was make evidence-based therapeutic support available to the billions of people who had never had access to any mental health care at all. A person in rural Mozambique with severe depression in 2045 has access to a quality of care that would have been unavailable to anyone, at any price, in 2020.

Healthy human lifespan has extended significantly—not yet doubled, but the trajectory suggests it. Drugs that demonstrably slow the biological aging process in humans have cleared clinical trials. People in their seventies who began treatment a decade ago show biomarkers of people a decade younger. The full implications of this are still working themselves out in demographic and social systems that were not designed for it.

## The Distribution Problem, Half-Solved

It would be dishonest to describe this world as uniformly better for everyone. The early benefits of the biological revolution accrued heavily to wealthy nations and wealthy individuals within those nations. The distributional struggle of the 2030s was real and contentious.

What shifted it was a combination of factors. Prediction markets for policy, long confined to niche applications, became mainstream tools for evaluating development interventions after a series of high-profile successes—an AI-assisted malaria eradication campaign that a prediction market had correctly identified as the most cost-effective deployment strategy, adopted over the objections of established institutions, and vindicated by the results. The markets did not make decisions. They provided epistemic structure that made it much harder to sustain the fictions that had historically protected ineffective programs from scrutiny.

Land value taxation, adopted first in a handful of jurisdictions as a reform experiment and then more widely as the evidence accumulated, proved to be genuinely transformative in the cities where it was implemented. Urban land that had been held idle for speculative purposes was put to productive use. Housing costs declined. The revenue funded public goods. The politics were always difficult—landowners organized, lobbied, litigated—but the pattern, once established in enough places, became self-reinforcing. Cities that had implemented the reform became magnets for the talented and ambitious, which increased their tax base and made the reform more politically durable.

Charter cities—new jurisdictions built from scratch on negotiated terms—became a significant vehicle for institutional experimentation. Not all succeeded. Several were embarrassing failures, either captured by their developers or abandoned when the promised investment did not materialize. But several became genuine success stories: places where regulatory innovation, freed from the accumulated legacy of systems built for different eras, produced strikingly better outcomes in housing, healthcare delivery, and economic development. The successes were studied, replicated, adapted. The concept normalized.

The developing world's growth trajectory in this period is genuinely remarkable even by the standards of the East Asian miracle. AI-assisted economic planning did not replace markets or democratic deliberation, but it did provide governments with something they had historically lacked: reliable, granular information about what was actually happening in their economies, and rigorous analysis of which interventions worked. Finance ministers who could run credible policy simulations before committing to programs made fewer catastrophically bad decisions. The institutional knowledge that wealthy-country governments had accumulated over centuries became, in compressed form, available to governments that had not had it.

Sub-Saharan Africa's average GDP per capita roughly tripled in real terms between 2025 and 2045. This is not the dream scenario of twenty percent annual growth that optimists once imagined. Corruption, weak institutions, and the genuine complexity of economic development proved more resistant than hoped. But tripling in twenty years is faster than any region has ever achieved over a sustained period. The children of Amara Osei's students will likely live in a sub-Saharan Africa that has, on most measures, caught up with where Europe was in the early 2000s.

## Governance in the Information Age

The political picture is the most mixed, and here honesty requires acknowledging that the world of 2045 is not a world of resolved ideological conflict.

Democracy did not automatically triumph. The authoritarian toolkit of surveillance, propaganda, and algorithmic manipulation of information proved genuinely dangerous, and several countries that had seemed to be on democratic trajectories slid backward during the turbulent years of the 2030s. The information environment remained contested and difficult.

What proved more durable than anyone expected was the spread of local and institutional capacity for what might be called epistemic hygiene—the practices, tools, and norms that allow communities and institutions to reason together more effectively. Prediction markets expanded beyond their early niche. Citizens' assemblies, first popularized for questions of constitutional reform in Ireland, became a standard tool for deliberating on complex policy questions in dozens of countries. AI systems that could aggregate preferences, model the consequences of different policy choices, and facilitate genuine dialogue across disagreement—without providing propaganda for any particular position—became infrastructure for democratic deliberation in the way that roads and communications systems had been infrastructure for previous eras.

The legal system, in jurisdictions that embraced it, became markedly more consistent and accessible. AI systems could not replace judges, but they could serve as a check on arbitrary or inconsistent reasoning, flag potential bias, make the law comprehensible to ordinary people navigating it without lawyers, and provide everyone with the kind of access to legal information and basic representation that had previously been available only to the wealthy. "Constitutional AI" came to mean not just alignment techniques for AI systems but AI systems designed to enforce constitutional rights—systems that monitored government action for rights violations in something closer to real time.

Occupational licensing reform, seemingly a minor issue, turned out to be a proxy war that mattered. State-level reforms in the United States, eventually spreading internationally, unlocked enormous amounts of human capital that had been trapped behind credential requirements with little relationship to actual competence. The nurse practitioner who can now prescribe in all fifty states. The dental therapist providing basic care in rural communities that previously had none. The teacher without a certification credential who had been tutoring privately but can now work in schools. None of these individually was transformative; collectively, the relaxation of credentialist barriers was.

## The Shape of a Human Life

What does a human life actually look like in this world?

A child born in 2045—in Accra, in São Paulo, in Seoul, in rural Montana—faces dramatically different odds than a child born twenty years earlier. She will almost certainly not die of an infectious disease. If she has a genetic condition that would once have been debilitating, there is a meaningful chance it has been treated in utero or in early childhood. The mental health care she will have access to if she needs it is vastly better than what was available to her parents. She will grow up in a city with cheaper, cleaner energy, with more affordable housing than the crisis years of the 2010s and 2020s, in an information environment that is still difficult and contested but where tools for navigating it are substantially better.

She will work differently. Automation has eliminated many jobs and created others, and the transition, as all such transitions have been, was painful for those caught in it. The social insurance systems that cushioned the transition were not universal; the suffering was not evenly distributed. But she will likely spend less of her life on tasks that feel degrading or pointless, and more on things that require genuine judgment, creativity, and human connection—not because this was inevitable, but because enough people fought for it to be the structure of the economy she inherits.

She will live, if the biology continues to go as it seems to be going, to something like 110 years in reasonable health. She will have access to tools for understanding her own biology, her own mental states, her own cognitive tendencies, that her grandparents could not have imagined. Whether she uses those tools wisely, whether the extended years are good ones, whether the freedom they represent is freedom toward something meaningful—those are questions she will have to answer herself. The technology does not answer them for her.

This is perhaps the most important thing to say about this world: it is not a world of solved problems, but of better-posed problems. The deep questions—about how to live, about justice between people, about what we owe each other—remain. They remain because they are not technical problems. They are human problems, and human problems are not solved by tools but by choices, made by people who have developed the wisdom and the institutional context to make them better.

The world of 2045 is not utopia. But it is, by any honest reckoning, dramatically better than the world that produced it. It got better not through a single breakthrough but through the patient, plural, sometimes fumbling work of people in dozens of fields who kept their attention on what actually mattered: the reduction of suffering, the expansion of possibility, the construction of institutions that let human beings coordinate without devouring each other.

That is, in the end, what thriving looks like. Not the elimination of difficulty, but the elevation of the difficulty we face.

*Amara Osei ended her lecture with a question for her students: "What will your grandchildren find incomprehensible about the suffering we still allow?" She told me, years later, that she asked it every year, and that the answers changed—which told her that the work was still going on. That seemed to her like a good sign.*
