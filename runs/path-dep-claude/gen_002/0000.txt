# The Commons of Everything: A Vision of Civilizational Flourishing

---

## A Morning in 2089

Amara Diallo wakes at six in the morning in a house she built with her own hands and the hands of her neighbors, on a hillside outside Tamale in what was once called northern Ghana. The house is made of compressed earth, its walls thick enough to breathe against the heat, its roof planted with a shallow garden of drought-adapted succulents that she tends the way a musician tends an instrument: with attention, with love, with the accumulated knowledge of small daily adjustments. Through the window she can see the city beginning its morning: the solar thermal collectors rotating to face the dawn, the cyclists moving along the green corridor that threads through the neighborhood, three children racing ahead of a tired parent on the path to the learning commons.

She has a meeting this morning with two people she has never met in the physical sense—a hydrologist in Dhaka and a poet-engineer in Guadalajara—about a water management problem in her district that she has been thinking about for three years. The AI system that serves as their shared research collaborator has prepared a synthesis of the relevant literature, flagged three analogous solutions from other semiarid regions, and identified four specific gaps in current knowledge where Amara's direct observational record might be decisive. It has not solved the problem. It has made the problem clearer, which is what she needed most.

After the meeting, she will spend the afternoon teaching twelve-year-olds to read soil. Not metaphorically. Literally: to walk slowly across ground and hear what it is saying about water retention, compaction, fungal networks, the presence or absence of particular organisms that signal particular histories. This is knowledge she holds in her feet as much as her mind, and no system, however sophisticated, can transmit it except through attention and practice and the particular patience of someone who actually cares whether the next generation knows how to be in a place.

Her daughter, twenty-six and curious about everything, is currently on a research vessel in the North Atlantic studying kelp forest restoration. They will talk tonight. The conversation will be long and wandering, the kind that used to be reserved for people who happened to live close to each other and could afford the hours. Distance is now cheap. Hours are now more available. The conversation will go where it needs to go.

This is one morning in one life in the world we built. There are eight billion other mornings, eight billion other lives, each with its own specific texture of challenge and beauty. This essay is an attempt to describe the structure that makes all of them possible—not the details, which are beyond any single imagination to capture, but the bones.

## Part One: How We Got Here Without Lying to Ourselves

The temptation in any vision of the future is to skip lightly over the transition, to wave a hand at "decades of hard work" and leap to the paradise on the far side. That temptation should be resisted, because the transition is precisely where hope lives or dies.

The 2020s and 2030s were defined by a recognition that arrived late and imperfectly but decisively: that the question of how to build AI was inseparable from the question of what kind of civilization it would be built inside. Early development had proceeded as if the technology existed in a vacuum—as if you could solve alignment in a laboratory and then hand the result to a world still organized around the concentration of wealth and power and expect good outcomes. You could not. The technical work and the political work had to happen together, at the same time, or neither would hold.

This produced what historians now call the Entangled Decade: ten years, roughly from 2028 to 2038, of simultaneous crisis and negotiation, during which the first genuinely capable AI systems arrived and the world scrambled, often chaotically, to build the governance infrastructure those systems required. The scramble was ugly. There were actors who tried to capture the technology before frameworks could be established. There were near-disasters, one of which became public knowledge and one of which, historians believe, did not. There were political fights of extraordinary ferocity about who would have oversight, who would benefit, who would be protected from harm.

What emerged was not elegant. It was a patchwork, held together by the shared recognition that catastrophic outcomes were quite plausible and required genuine cooperation to avoid. The Global AI Commons Treaty of 2031 was mocked at the time as toothless—and it was, initially. But it established the precedent of international oversight and created the institutional channels through which stronger frameworks were subsequently built. The Distributed Benefit Protocols of 2035, born from the political pressure of mass automation displacing workers in manufacturing and logistics, established that foundational AI capabilities were a commons, not a property right, and that their gains would be distributed through mechanisms designed by democratic bodies rather than captured by those who happened to hold early patents.

None of this was inevitable. It required specific people fighting for specific things with specific arguments, often losing, sometimes winning, always under pressure from actors who wanted different outcomes. The future we are now living was not given to us. It was made, imperfectly, by people who cared enough to make it.

## Part Two: The Body and the Mind, Freed at Last

By 2045, most childhood cancers were curable. By 2052, most adult cancers were preventable. By 2061, the first comprehensive protocols for neurological restoration—targeting the amyloid cascades and synaptic degradation of dementia—had been deployed widely enough to be considered standard care rather than experimental treatment. By 2071, the suite of metabolic, cardiovascular, and immunological conditions that had defined middle and old age as a period of managed decline had been substantially interrupted.

These are dry statistics. Let me say what they mean.

The woman who would have spent six years watching her father disappear—watching his eyes lose the particular quality of recognition that said *I know you, you are mine*—spent those years with him instead. They argued about politics. They took a trip to see migratory birds on the Camargue. He taught her, at seventy-eight, how to make the bread his own mother had made, and she learned, which she would not have done if they had not had the time.

The nine-year-old who would have been born into a body that could not process certain proteins grew up without that particular shadow. She became a distance runner, not because her biology demanded compensation for a limitation, but because she discovered in adolescence that she loved the sensation of covering ground under her own power. She ran ultras in her twenties. She coached children in her forties. She is fifty now, and still runs.

The man who would have spent his thirties in the grinding machinery of untreated depression—barely functional, using most of his energy just to appear normal, sacrificing his ambitions and his relationships to the sheer effort of maintenance—instead had access, by his mid-twenties, to genuinely effective treatment. Not the blunt pharmaceutical interventions of the earlier era, which muted suffering but also muted everything else. Targeted neurological support, combined with a form of AI-assisted therapy that made the best psychological understanding available to everyone regardless of income or location, which helped him understand the patterns that had formed him and learn to work with them rather than against them.

This last achievement—the treatment of mental illness—is perhaps the most underappreciated transformation of the past fifty years. The weight of untreated psychological suffering was always invisible in a way that physical illness was not. It showed up in statistics about productivity and criminality and relationship breakdown and suicide, but it was rarely counted directly, rarely understood as a coherent problem to be solved. When it was solved—imperfectly, gradually, but substantially—the change in the texture of ordinary human life was enormous. People are more patient with each other. They have more capacity for generosity and attention. They sleep better and fight less and find it easier to be present in their own lives. This is not a small thing. This is, arguably, the change that made most of the other changes possible.

Lifespans have extended not by prolonging decline but by compressing it—pushing the period of biological diminishment into the final years of a life that now commonly reaches a hundred and fifteen or a hundred and twenty in reasonable health. This has changed how people relate to time in ways that are still being worked out. A twenty-five-year-old today does not feel the same urgent pressure to have finished constructing an identity and a career and a family by thirty-five. There is room to spend a decade genuinely uncertain about what kind of person you want to be. There is room to change direction at fifty without feeling like failure. There is room, more broadly, for human lives to take the shape they need rather than the shape that scarcity and shortened time imposed on them.

## Part Three: The Architecture of Meaning

The question that haunted the early decades of abundance—and still haunts, in productive ways, the present—was not whether people would be healthy and fed and housed. It was whether their lives would feel genuinely worth living. These are different questions, and the failure to distinguish them produced a generation of policymakers who thought they had solved the human problem when they had only solved its material preconditions.

Meaning, we have learned, is not a resource that can be distributed. It is a capacity that must be cultivated, and it grows in specific conditions: genuine challenge pursued through genuine skill, connection with people who actually know you, participation in something larger than yourself, the experience of creating things that could not have existed without you. The material abundance of this world is necessary but not sufficient. What is also required is the social and institutional architecture that makes these conditions achievable for people broadly rather than a lucky few.

Work has been redesigned around this understanding, in a process that was more contentious and more creative than anyone anticipated. The most repetitive and dehumanizing forms of labor—the assembly lines, the call centers, the interchangeable cognitive tasks that treated human attention as a substitute for computation—were automated quickly, and the people displaced from them were not simply left to find their own way. What replaced those forms of work was not idleness. It was a vast expansion of the kinds of labor that require human judgment, human relationship, human presence in the world: the care work, the teaching, the craftsmanship, the ecological stewardship, the governance and community-building, the arts and the sciences at the frontier of what is known.

The economy now pays for these things as the serious and irreplaceable contributions they always were. Ecological restoration workers—people like the woman in Tamale who teaches children to read soil—are not volunteers performing a hobby between shifts at a real job. They are practitioners of a discipline that took decades to formalize and will take centuries to fully develop, working in ways that no algorithm can replicate and no automation can replace. The same is true of the teachers and the caregivers and the mediators and the community organizers and the thousands of other roles that require a human being who is fully present and genuinely invested in the outcome.

Crucially, the AI systems woven into this world have been designed around a specific principle that was not obvious at first and had to be fought for: they should extend human capability rather than replace it. The distinction sounds abstract but its practical consequences are enormous. A research AI that extends a scientist's capability helps her understand what she does not know, helps her design experiments to find out, helps her see connections across bodies of literature too vast to read, helps her communicate her findings to people outside her specialty—but does not do the science for her. A teaching AI that extends an educator's capability helps him understand which students are struggling and why, gives him a richer toolkit of approaches, handles the administrative work that was previously consuming his best hours—but does not teach instead of him. A medical AI that extends a clinician's capability provides diagnostic accuracy that exceeds any individual human, surfaces treatment options and their evidence bases, tracks outcomes across millions of patients—but does not replace the human encounter in which a person who is frightened and suffering needs to be genuinely understood and accompanied.

This distinction was hard-won. Early deployment of AI systems had often defaulted to replacement rather than extension, because replacement was technically simpler and economically more immediately profitable. The political fights of the 2030s and 2040s—over labor rights, over professional standards, over the proper role of automated systems in healthcare and education and governance—forced a reckoning with what was actually being lost when humans were removed from consequential human processes. The frameworks that emerged from those fights encoded the extension principle into the governance structures for AI deployment, making it costly to violate and legible when violated.

The result is a world in which expertise is more widely distributed than it has ever been—because AI systems can translate specialist knowledge into forms accessible to almost anyone—while deep expertise remains as valuable as ever, because it is the unreplicable thing that gives the whole system its direction and its meaning.

## Part Four: The Conditions of Connection

Loneliness was always the invisible catastrophe. It showed up in the studies—the mortality statistics, the mental health numbers, the data on cognitive decline, the surveys in which people reported that they did not have anyone they could call at three in the morning—but it was rarely treated as a problem of structural design. It was treated as a personal failing, or a feature of modernity to be accepted, or at best a social problem to be addressed through vague appeals to community that were never backed by the resources or design attention that actual community requires.

The world we have built takes connection seriously as a design problem.

Cities have been redesigned at human scale. This is not nostalgia. It is the application of substantial evidence about what built environments actually do to human social behavior. Ground-floor commercial spaces on walkable streets, neighborhood-scale gathering places, green corridors that invite movement rather than funneling people between vehicles, housing arranged around shared outdoor spaces—these choices were made based on rigorous study of what makes people actually encounter each other, and actually speak to each other when they do. The result is cities that feel inhabited rather than occupied, that have the quality of places that belong to the people in them.

The AI-assisted systems for facilitating social connection were among the most delicate to design and have been among the most consequential. The early mistakes were instructive. Systems that tried to optimize for connection metrics—for the raw number of social interactions—made people miserable. Systems that tried to match people primarily on expressed preferences produced social bubbles of relentless sameness. The frameworks that actually worked were designed around something harder to quantify: the conditions under which genuine mutual recognition becomes likely. Not the engineering of connection but the removal of the friction that prevents it. Not telling people who their people are but creating the circumstances in which people are likely to meet the people who will matter to them.

The matchmaking systems—for friendship as much as romance, for intellectual collaboration as much as either—operate this way. They are quiet. They do not present themselves as decision-makers. They work more like a thoughtful host who knows the guest list well: creating circumstances, making introductions, then stepping back. The Thursday fermentation collective in the neighborhood near the river, where you can reliably find four or five people who share your particular combination of interests and are also looking for people who share them. The research forum where someone working on soil microbiomes in Ghana is connected to someone working on analogous questions in the Sahel, not because an algorithm decided they were compatible but because a system noticed that their questions were converging. The hiking club that has become, over fifteen years, a genuine community of people who know each other across decades of life change.

Mental health support has been radically democratized without being depersonalized—a balance that required years of iteration to achieve. AI systems trained on the full depth of psychological knowledge, and continuously refined by actual therapeutic outcomes, are now available to everyone as a first layer of support: articulate, patient, honest about their limitations, capable of the kind of reflective questioning that helps people understand their own patterns. These systems are explicitly designed to support human relationships rather than substitute for them. A person working with an AI therapeutic partner is being prepared, over time, to be more present, more honest, and more generous in their human relationships—not being offered a replacement for those relationships. The systems measure their success partly by whether people are showing up more fully in their actual lives. The ones that do not produce this outcome are redesigned.

## Part Five: The Commons and the Wild

The word "commons" did more work in the second half of this century than it had done in centuries. It described the governance innovation that made everything else possible: the recognition that certain things—foundational AI capabilities, clean water, air, the basic conditions of biological health, the ecological systems on which all life depends—could not be owned by any actor without corrupting them, and therefore had to be maintained as shared resources governed by the genuine interests of all who depended on them.

The ecological restoration is the most visible expression of this principle. By 2040, the sixth mass extinction that had been underway since the twentieth century had been substantially interrupted. Not reversed—too much had already been lost—but interrupted, with genuine restoration beginning in many of the regions that had been most degraded. The rewilding programs, controversial at first among agricultural communities that feared displacement, ultimately won the support they needed because the governance frameworks that managed them included genuine representation of local communities and genuine compensation for any costs those communities bore.

The ocean covers seventy percent of the Earth's surface. For most of human history, it was treated as a free-for-all: a place where the absence of governance permitted exploitation until the resource was gone, then abandonment of the exhausted system. The frameworks established in the 2030s and 2040s changed this. The deep ocean is now managed as a commons, with extraction permitted only within limits set by democratic bodies with the scientific information to set them meaningfully. The result, sixty years later, is ocean ecosystems that are more abundant and more complex than they have been since the nineteenth century. The kelp forests where Amara's daughter works are not wild in the way that forests before human disturbance were wild. They are tended—carefully, at vast scale, with the help of AI systems tracking populations and water chemistry and the interactions of hundreds of species. But they are alive in ways that matter. The species that live in them are actually there.

The governance of all this commons—ecological, digital, biological—is the least glamorous and most important achievement of the century. Layered oversight structures ensure that no single actor, however powerful or however well-intentioned, can capture the commons for narrow ends. At the apex are AI systems tasked specifically with detecting and flagging attempts to corrupt the oversight structure itself—a safeguard against what earlier theorists had called the principal-agent problem at civilization scale. Below those apex systems are human democratic institutions with genuine enforcement authority, which are themselves the subject of constant scrutiny and reform. Below those institutions are the local and community bodies that handle the governance of specific places and specific resources.

The crucial operating principle, held to even when it was inconvenient: the purpose of the commons governance is to protect the conditions under which many different kinds of good life can flourish, not to define what those lives must look like. This is the gardener principle rather than the architect principle. Maintain the soil. Control the invasive species. Do not choose which flowers are permitted to grow.

## Part Six: The Multiplicity of Worlds

The most beautiful thing about the world we have built is not any single feature of it. It is the plurality.

On Earth alone you would find, coexisting within the same foundational commons, communities that have chosen radically different relationships to technology, to tradition, to the pace of change. Communities in which the rhythms of life are organized around agricultural seasons and the skills of land stewardship, using AI and biological knowledge only in ways that feel consonant with their larger commitments. Communities in which the leading edge of cognitive augmentation is being explored with careful intentionality, tracking what is gained and what is lost as minds become capable of things no unaugmented human mind could manage. Communities dedicated to specific philosophical or artistic or spiritual traditions that take decades to transmit properly, whose value lies precisely in their slowness and their refusal of shortcuts.

None of these communities is compelled into its choice. Each is protected in its choice by the commons governance that prevents any actor from forcing a single vision of the good on everyone else. The oversight system intervenes only when practices harm people who did not consent to them, or when capabilities are being developed that threaten the commons itself. Otherwise it observes, sometimes catalogs, and creates pathways for communication between communities that might otherwise never encounter each other.

Beyond Earth, the settlements now being built in the solar system are not colonies in the old sense—not extensions of any particular power's will, not resource extraction operations with human beings attached. They are experiments in how to be human in new conditions, each with its own character, each contributing to the diversity of ways that conscious life has found to organize itself. The settlers are mostly people who find Earth too settled, who want to encounter genuine constraints and genuine frontiers—the engineering constraints of hostile environments, the social constraints of small communities that must function or perish, the philosophical frontier of figuring out what you actually need when you have almost nothing familiar.

The travelers move between all of this—between the settled communities and the experimental ones, between Earth and the new worlds, between the slow and the fast, carrying observations and questions and the particular richness that comes from having lived in many ways. They are not tourists. They are something more like pollen: moving because they are looking for something, and in their looking, carrying what they have learned from one flowering to the next.

## Part Seven: What We Are Still Learning

It would be dishonest to end without naming what is unfinished, because a world without open questions would be a world that had stopped being alive.

The distribution of flourishing is more equitable than it has ever been, but equity has not been achieved. The starting conditions of human lives still vary enormously, and those differences still compound over time. The children born into communities with longer histories of stability and resource access still have advantages that cannot be eliminated simply by making tools and healthcare equally available. The work of genuine equity is multigenerational, and we are perhaps three generations into it.

The question of what happens to human identity as biological enhancement and cognitive augmentation become more radical is genuinely open. The early, cautious work in this domain has been more fruitful than pessimists predicted and more complicated than optimists hoped. There are people living now whose cognitive capabilities differ from unaugmented human cognition enough that the question of what constitutes continuity of identity has become practically urgent rather than merely philosophical. The frameworks for navigating this are being built in real time, out of genuine uncertainty, by people who are trying to be honest about what they do not know.

The governance of the commons is not stable in any permanent sense. It is an equilibrium maintained by continuous effort against continuous pressure from actors who would benefit from breaking it. There have been crises. There will be more. The question is not whether the governance structures are perfect but whether they are resilient enough to recover from failures and honest enough to learn from them.

Democracy is stronger than it was and more robust than it has been in most of human history, but it is not secure. The tools that AI provided to authoritarian manipulation were real and are still real, and the fight against them is genuine and ongoing. The information environment that a healthy democracy requires—shared reality, meaningful deliberation, genuine representation—requires constant cultivation and constant defense.

These are not failures of the world we built. They are what the world we built is made of: genuine problems, requiring genuine effort, with genuine stakes. The alternative to these problems is not a world without problems. It is a world that has stopped growing—which is to say, a world that has stopped being fully alive.

## Coda: The Long Game

When I try to reduce all of this to what it is actually for—what all the governance and the medical advances and the redesigned cities and the AI systems are ultimately in service of—it is something that has not changed across all of human history, because it is what human beings have always needed and always sought:

To be known. To do work that matters. To encounter things that surprise us. To have time enough to become who we are capable of becoming. To hand something worth having to the people who come after us.

What has changed is the scale of possibility. The child in Tamale learning to read soil has access, through the commons that humanity built together, to the sum of everything our species has learned about how living systems work—and has the time and the support and the freedom from preventable suffering to do something genuinely new with that inheritance. The question her twelve-year-old students ask this afternoon, the one she cannot answer, the one that will stay with her on the walk home and that she will bring to the meeting with the hydrologist in Dhaka—that question is the leading edge of what we know, pushed forward by a person who is fully alive and fully equipped to push it.

Multiply that by eight billion. Multiply it by the generations that will follow, with longer lives and wider tools and all the diversity of worlds being built at the solar system's edges. Multiply it by whatever comes next, which we cannot see from here and should not pretend to.

The universe contains more interesting problems than any civilization could exhaust in any imaginable span of time. We have built a world in which more people, in more places, with more of their potential intact and more of their curiosity alive, are free to go after those problems. Not a destination. A condition. Not the end of history. The beginning of something that will take longer than history to unfold.

We just had to want it clearly enough to do the work.

And enough of us did.
