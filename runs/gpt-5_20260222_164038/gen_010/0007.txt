The Lattice and the Loam

At dawn the shutters folded like elbows and the kitchen filled with the scent of rain on basil. Rafi set a kettle on a squat induction plate no thicker than a stanza and watched the canal below unknot itself from the night. On the sill lay his pocket steward, a cloth-wrapped rectangle with a rubberized switch you could feel without looking. Three soft ticks came from its motor—its way of asking to speak—and its single LED held steady until he thumbed it on.

Three things want your brain before noon, the steward said in a voice he’d chosen last year: gentle, brisk, disclaimers first.

- The floodgate actuator install by the north footbridge needs one more neighborhood key-sign to power on. Three keys required: maker, safety distrust, local users.

- Two new families arrive on the night line. The Arrivals House asks for a translator and two hands for bag repair.

- The Orchard Circle has a bat-sleeve trial at dusk; volunteers will drape specific fig clusters to count fruit share without harm. Consent papers ready.

“Gate, then Arrivals,” Rafi said. “Keep the snack ad-suppressor up for the festival; I don’t need to dream of caramel.”

Logged. I will not talk unless you call me.

He stepped past a café where a placard glowed soft green. Beside the bottle of oat milk, a square tag read: Nudge: stock at eye level. Paid by: Clinic Guild for lipid outcomes. See method. He took the goat milk anyway, read the method out of curiosity—comparative trials on processed fats—and smiled at the simple relief of a world that admitted when it was nudging and why.

Across the canal the House of Hands—part workshop, part clinic, part school, entirely public—had rolled up its doors. A welder tuned a seam on a wheelchair frame; a child peered into a lens at the scales of a moth. On one wall in carved cedar ran six short sentences. That was the city’s compact. You could say it while tying your shoes.

Three Guarantees, Three Guardrails

Guarantees:

1) The Floor You Don’t Have to Beg For. A room you can lock, staple foods, clean water, a basic clinic, transit that arrives, and baseline connectivity and compute—the “Watt & Word”—all arrive like electricity. A Common Share lands monthly, sized modest at first, then large enough to say no to bad deals. Decency does not hinge on performance.

2) Pace Districts With Real Doors. Neighborhoods declare their pace for change—fast-adopting, steady, or slower-and-skeptical—while keeping a slim set of shared law. Crossing between districts is easy and funded. If you need to leave a school, job, family, or platform quickly, Exit Escrow pays your first nights, carries your history in open formats, and puts a person in a red scarf between you and yesterday.

3) Trials With Tide-Backs. Anything new that could leave a mark comes with a stop date, measured outcomes, and a rollback path that is funded when the trial starts. Ending a trial is not an admission of failure. It is how grown‑ups learn in public.

Guardrails:

1) Reasons Before Orders. No rule that binds the public counts unless it posts its reasons to the Open Reasons Docket: assumptions, data, where a model assistant helped, and how to check or challenge it. A randomly selected mirror panel writes a parallel case for the biggest moves. Disagreement flags places to think harder.

2) Keys Before Dangerous Power. Systems that touch bodies, money, heavy gear, chemicals, votes, or borders live behind physical and social keys. Three keys must turn to run: the builder’s; a certified skeptic’s paid to distrust; and a local users’ cooperative that bears clear risk and can revoke. Every such “keyed operator” ships with a plain-language card: what it can and cannot do.

3) Consent You Can Hold. Each person is offered a pocket steward—hardware you can turn all the way off—that is barred from taking control and required to explain itself. For big changes to your body, home, work, or mind, it keeps “consent receipts”: you and your recent past self sign, with cooling-off built in. Influence is metered like water: nudges are tagged; hiding a tag is costly.

Money That Flows From the Commons

The Floor and the doors transact because the city remembers what value it creates and keeps a portion.

- Ground Leases, Not Feudal Deeds. The city owns its soil and auctions long leases—renewable—with rents pegged to location value the city creates: a new tram stop, a restored shoreline, a school that makes curious graduates. Improvements are yours to sell; the ground’s rising tide funds schools and clinics.

- Scarcity Leases. Curbs at rush hour, radio bands, air slots, berth time at the port, orbital shells, watershed carbon drawdown: none are owned outright. Exclusive use has a clock and a posted price. The receipts split three ways: the Common Share; Place Funds that keep value where it arose; and the Planet Ledger that pays for global basics—pathogen monitors, seed banks, open drought maps, a compute sentinel.

- Windfall Shares. Frontier compute, elite wet labs, and other rare rails sign windfall clauses: beyond a threshold, a defined share flows automatically to the Common Share and the oversight guilds that keep the rails safe. Skip the attestation, lose the discount power and fast pipes.

- Dormancy Rent. High‑value ground that sits empty by choice pays to rest. That is not a moral rule; it is a price. Speculators build, or they move on. Life fills the gaps.

- Pattern Dividends. When you ship an open fixture, a protocol that halves infections, or a teaching module kids everywhere use, a portion of Place Funds pays you. The pool is audited and global. Spoils hitch to use, not veto.

Measurement With Teeth

Rules are only as good as the habits that feed them. The city wrote a Measurement Constitution into its charter and then behaved like it meant it.

- Civic Stakes. Regulated markets post public questions with clear scoring: “Will the east busway carry 115,000 riders per weekday by March?” “Will the clinic bundle for heart failure cut 30‑day readmission by 12%?” Departments make forecasts and hold a piece of their own contracts. If they are right, they gain budget. If they are repeatedly wrong, audits and then leadership change follow. No one bets on cruelty, and questions clear like bonds.

- Truth Bonds. Leaders stake their claims with refundable bonds. Miss on cost, timeline, or outcomes beyond the bound you declared, and the bond pays out to the public and to those hurt. Hit or beat your range, the bond rolls to your next project.

- Open Reasons Docket. Agencies file decisions in machine‑readable form: the rule, the why, the data, the links, the model assists. The mirror panel’s case sits beside. High schoolers learn civics reading real dockets and writing friendly briefs. Some win; they earn stipends as “explainers” before they can drive.

The Guild of Works and Measures

The city has no single university atop a hill. It has a braided Guild: labs, shops, clinics, and farms that prize dull excellence and tools that survive five winters.

- Pattern Code and Planchecks. A shelf of pattern books—rowhouses, timber midrise with outward stairs, workshop courts—issued permits in three days if you stayed within their envelope. Deviate? Show your model checked by the city’s digital twin and a structural/fire engineer’s seal and you still moved in weeks. The code fit on eighty pages and made handsome buildings normal.

- Public Compute, Bounded On Purpose. The Civic Cloud ran a million expert agents for labs and schools and workshops, fenced by capability: they could plan experiments and check proofs; they could not synthesize DNA or write worms. Providers logged training and access. DNA houses screened all orders against a global no‑go list and flagged evasion patterns in aggregate. No single person could be unmasked simply for trying and failing.

- Replication Pools and Red Teams. Labs banked half their budget in replication. You pre‑registered methods; you published zeros. If a red team proved you wrong robustly, you paid them and got better. Prestige lived in the Hall of Durable Things: wrenches that fit twenty machines, how‑tos that strangers actually used, libraries still cited five years on. Children came on a Night of Hands when an elder retired and touched the tools that had stitched their world.

Health and Bodies

Clinics organized around outcomes people wanted, not reimbursements.

- Pathway Bundles. “Climb stairs without pain in six weeks.” “Bring A1c to target and hold for a year.” “Lift 50 pounds overhead post-rotator cuff.” The bundles were priced on data; insurers joined the Civic Stakes; clinics won by doing boring small things well. A gait‑tracker called when your stride shortened. A nurse came in the morning because that’s when you answered the door. Stairs tempted you up; posters were afterthoughts.

- Mental Health That Counts. Team care mixed sunlight, CPAPs, GLP‑1s when indicated, talk, and carefully fenced neural interventions. The pocket steward helped you scaffold habits, not sell you calm. Outcomes were measured, privacy was actually protected, and relapses were incidents, not identities.

- The Fertility Ledger. Eggs and sperm banked free. Counselors who sounded like counselors, not sales. Screening for single‑gene diseases and, where evidence was strong, for polygenic risk with humility. The ethics board posted minutes in plain language. Designer baby races never began because laughter and law met them at the door.

Tools With Teeth, Not Fangs

- Compute Passports. Chips above a threshold carried cryptographic IDs and sensors that tattled if you peeled them. Training runs that could alter code or hardware ecosystems registered with a treaty network of evaluators. If your model could make other models, coordinate cranes, or touch wet labs, you were hammered for deception, misuse, and autonomy. Some models shipped small with proofs of limits; others stayed sealed, running under fern‑filled rooms with three human keys.

- Bounded Objectives. Useful models declared their purpose and fences. Watchdogs—deliberately simple—looked for category errors like “pursued outside goal.” Logs bored and saved reputations. When a model found a hole, it flagged the hole and stopped.

- Bio as Fire. Community labs had glass walls and mentors. Equipment with bite had interlocks and audits. DNA screens caught the person trying to order 10,000 near‑harmless pieces that together spelled trouble. Wastewater genomics read the city’s cough. If a wave rose, corners sprouted test tents and texts arrived with invites, not shame.

- The Ward Net. Most compute with world‑shifting potential ran off‑planet or on sequestered islands under treaties. Links had literal knobs. Courts could pause streams; the cut was practiced; cooks froze mid‑chop the first time and then resumed. Boredom became safety. Innovation didn’t stop; it simply respected the places where error had too long a tail.

Attention, Consent, and Joy

- Nudge Tags. The shelves, feeds, and search slots wore their motives on their sleeves. Who paid, why you, the outcome they wanted—printed and tappable. You could cap categories. Hiding a tag cut your license.

- Analog Drift. At uneven hours, neighborhoods slowed. Feeds flattened; lights warmed; workshops clicked into hand‑tool mode; bakeries remembered flour. Emergencies cut through, and if your life broke in a drift, you were first in line.

- Pocket Steward. Your helper showed ranges, not oracles. It kept a visible file of your priors. It could comfort and role‑play if you asked. It could not be your only rope out of a pit. You chose a time ombud who asked once a year whether your life fit the shape you meant.

Work, Robots, and Time

- Competence Portals. Licenses faded. Exams rose. Wire a panel safely today, and you stamp jobs tomorrow. Cut a mortise clean, and your stamp transfers across any city that signs the reciprocity pact.

- Micro‑Apprenticeships. Rotate through weld, code, stagecraft, care, and clean in a year. Portfolios show work, not brand. Robots apprenticed beside you: arms that learned to pack peaches without bruising; trolleys that learned the night.

- Portable Benefits and Sabbaticals. One page carried your payroll, health, disability, retirement, and leave. Every seventh year you could claim a Sabbatical Year at half the average of your last three years’ income to learn, build, or care. No soul audits.

- Care Counts. Hours tending elders, children, and neighbors accrued credits exactly like hours slinging a wrench. Pensions saw both.

Law and Its Helpers

Police arrived with a toolkit: beds, detox, counselors, emergency grants that fixed the car that stood between a person and a job, mediators, jail when the harm was too large to hold. Courts used model assistants like librarians that could read a thousand cases and spot weird sentencing clusters; they never decided. Civil groups ran bias bounties; if a skew held up out of sample, the model went back to the shop and the finder got paid.

The budget was annotated like a garden fence. Before big moves, the office posted five falsifiable claims—cost, timeline, who was served, three top risks, and the triggers for a stop—to the Open Reasons Docket and Civic Stakes. Afterward, forecasts flipped to grades. Anyone could read; teenagers did; some of them out‑forecasted the city and got themselves hired.

Children’s Paper

Kids carried a cream‑paper card and a file on their steward that could not be revoked by any district:

- To learn to read, count, test a claim, and mend something real.

- To see how other districts live.

- To ask for an outside adult and meet them without permission.

- To leave, if they can explain what leaving means, with three nights of shelter.

- To be free of punishment by pain, humiliation, or isolation.

Teachers brought the numbers every year. The first line read, “We believe you.”

Rooms, Power, and Air

- Energy With Boring Glory. Rooftop solar led. Brownfield solar followed. Then two small reactors, licensed on a fast, transparent path with third‑party audits and published fault trees. HVDC trunks traded night wind for morning ovens. Storage came as habits and hardware: basement flow batteries, salt tanks that exhaled heat at dusk, a train that lifted rocks at noon and set them down like teacups. Outages under five minutes per person per year. Water that tasted like a mountain because teens made competitions out of leak hunts.

- Pattern Code. The build library made handsome the default. Corners housed small workshops that shared a loading court; ground floors refused to die. Spaces wore visible norms—quiet, dogs, stim‑friendly—and when needs clashed, another space appeared. Towers rose where tram met tram.

- Deliveries Bought Curb Slots. Box trucks booked time and paid open prices; robots minded the night; pedestrians owned the day. The map moved as rain, heat, and need moved.

Nature and Kin

- Trustees, Not Lords. Land and water carried rights for the not‑human. Stewardship paid enough to make care a calling. Rewilding corridors stitched songlines back together. Where suffering was relentless and fixable—parasites that blinded—vaccines dusted salt licks. Field ethicists argued under open skies.

- Sentience Atlas. Researchers kept a living map of minds across species. It argued with itself; it changed law; it taught humility.

Arrivals and Exits

Arrivals Houses flanked big stations. Two beds, a kitchen that never closed, a nurse, a steward with a scarf who knew the paperwork and the menace of an uncle. Exit Escrow made leaving safe: your data, credentials, and references left with you, signed and redacted appropriately, in formats any steward could read. Quiet rooms existed at borders. A phone got you a ride.

Attention Taxes and Right to Smallness

Entities above a size opened their interfaces and books or split. Big was fine; opaque was not. Platforms that thrived on micro‑targeting paid an attention tax proportional to precision unless they could show long‑horizon social benefit. Interoperability was law. Social graphs moved with you.

A Day in the Compact

Rafi reached the footbridge. The new floodgate actuator sat trussed in orange, gleaming like a well‑oiled sentence. The maker had turned their key. The distrust safety engineer—everyone called them “the second conscience”—had turned theirs. The users’ key was in his pocket.

He read the card. Capability: hold under 60 cm water rise; auto‑stop when debris density exceeds threshold; manual override requires two bodies. Limits: supplier delays in deep freeze; exfiltration risk minimal; last red‑team jammed the sensor with river fur and the device noticed within nineteen seconds. A small red paragraph directed aid: a co‑op of roofers had idle vans in February; a contract template favored apprentices.

He tapped his steward. “Sand the contract toward beginners.” The steward showed the new splits; pennies and days shifted; accountability remained. He turned the key. A small crowd clapped once, like a single drum.

At the Arrivals House, steam bicycles leaned in a row. A woman with a braided scarf and a tired smile stepped off a bus with a boy whose backpack zipper had surrendered sometime after midnight. Rafi translated a form about allergies. The bag fix took five stitches, one bold, four careful. No one demanded gratitude; hands moved where they needed to.

He ate late under the fig arbor behind the House of Hands. The Orchard Circle tied soft sleeves around specific clusters, part of a trial to learn how to share fruit with bats. A placard read: Nudge: fruit within reach; sugar high. Paid by: Health Collective & Growers’ Guild. Method: posted. He tapped and read and chose a low fruit anyway. Choice stayed his.

After dusk, the plaza filled for a dry run that refused to be grim. Stalls offered dumplings that dripped joy. On the far levee, lights flipped to signal a breach; the pumps hummed; a brass band found its second note. Phones breathed shallower: an Analog Drift slid through the blocks. A toddler clapped when the sluice latched; a steward wrote the postmortem before bed. Practice had become culture.

How We Walked Here

The Lattice and the Loam did not descend like a singular miracle. It accreted like a reef.

Years 1–3:

- A city passed a Reasons Act: agencies and courts posted decisions with data links and where they used model assistants. A mirror panel argued in public for a fraction of big cases. The files were open. Graduate students made hay.

- A tiny Civic Stakes market opened for three questions: bus ridership, clinic readmissions, tidal barrier. Regulators cleared questions carefully. A fraction of bonuses tied to accuracy. Nobody’s hair caught fire. Trust accreted.

- A pattern code for five safe building types passed with digital planchecks. Permits for in‑envelope builds hit three days. Rents twitched down; carpenters smiled.

- A pilot ground lease on a new tram line captured value that had previously evaporated. The first Common Share checks landed. They bought apples and time.

- A House of Hands opened a wing: measurement lab with calibrated sensors, a repair kitchen, and an explainers’ desk. The coffee was good, the lemon oil better.

Years 4–7:

- Ground leases expanded; dormancy rent started. Solar grew like weeds. A state let a small reactor fast‑track if audits were third‑party and fault trees posted. People held their breath. It worked.

- Frontier compute providers that wanted procurement contracts registered training runs, posted capability cards, and logged. A trading algorithm scandal burned a politically connected man and made “keys before power” sound as obvious as brakes.

- Arrivals Houses opened in two hubs. Red scarves became shorthand for decency. The Exit Escrow law passed. Shelters got calm, not chaos.

- The first Sabbatical Year cohort took time. Some learned. Some cared. Some just slept. The city learned to count patience as work.

- Outcome bundles launched for four conditions. Clinics that had been soft on coordination got good fast. The Civic Stakes pulled back two bundles that looked fancy and did nothing much. Nobody sued; they tried again.

Years 8–12:

- The Open Reasons Docket standardized across agencies. Data flowed. Teenagers argued with officials and sometimes won.

- The Guild published its first Hall of Durable Things: a low‑smoke kiln door, a wastewater sampler that ran on pocket power, a procedure that kept hands clean in real clinics. The replication pool paid two teams and humbled a third.

- Occupational licenses began to fall away in favor of competence portals. Reciprocity treaties linked three cities. People stopped moving for paperwork.

- A compute and chip alliance formed with other democracies. They secured fabrication at home, set export lines, and offered a bright carrot: the cure packages and logistics optimizers, free, in exchange for civil‑liberties baselines. Many said yes.

- The first Ward Net treaty signed. A data center at sea and a constellation overhead swallowed the risky runs. Courthouses installed the knobs. The first cut surprised people. Then it faded into the quiet competence of air traffic control.

Years 13–20:

- Common Shares grew as leases matured and the reactors hit their stride. Public options stayed good. Private pride sharpened above them.

- Auditor lotteries hit random keyed operators. Badges that read “Nosy For All” became adolescent currency. They earned what law firms once did.

- The attention tax bit: platforms paid if they targeted too precisely without good reason. The civic feed slowed by default on public devices; private feeds sprinted if you wanted. People lived with both.

- Rewilding corridors stitched across the map. A man who had shot wolves his whole life found himself counting pups and weeping. Field ethicists learned to argue against themselves.

- Pathogen watchers kept boredom alive. A cough flared; tents bloomed; texts invited; the city sighed and did what it had practiced. A microfactory rolled onto a mountain and printed vaccines sized to a valley.

- An Antitrust Launchpad law passed. When firms colluded, bonds unlocked capital and talent for a worker‑owned rival in sixty days. It was messy. It worked.

Years 21–30:

- The Pattern Dividend fund matured. Designers of unsexy miracles—door closers that never slammed; signage you could read with your fingers and your eyes—got paid.

- Computing rights (“Watt & Word”) turned on with the meter: baseline power and compute as easy as a light. Training runs that touched code or chemicals without attestations found their power throttled. Most complied because the rails led to procurement.

- Off‑world compute remained under treaties with practice‑heavy drills. No neon gods; only boredom and logs. Innovation hummed under ferns.

- The culture of calibration deepened. Schools played forecast leagues. Steelman debates became sports. Officials posted their error bars and held themselves to them.

Plausibility, In Plain Words

The parts exist. Ground leases and land value capture work where tried. Chip attestation has shipped. DNA order screening is normal and can be better. HVDC strings quietly under seas already. Fast, pattern‑book permitting and digital planchecks cut months. Public compute clouds exist; fencing them is choice, not science fiction. Real‑money forecasting applied to public projects exists in embryo. Outcome bundles are live in pockets. Interop mandates and attention taxes are law in some regions. Export controls and chip alliances are in motion. Off‑planet compute is a stretch, but its spirit—sequestered, attested, pauseable—has precedents in nuclear and aviation.

The social scaffolds are real jobs we can pay now: distrust engineers who are salaried to say “no”; explainers who show their work and get sued if sloppy; auditors with teenage fans; field ethicists; stewards in red scarves; teachers who leave pamphlets in the wrong places on purpose. Culture changed because we paid it to and because it worked once or twice and then again.

Why It’s Good

- You do not audition for dignity. You can have a bad season without falling through the floor.

- You can choose the pace of your neighborhood and switch when life asks you to be someone else.

- Dangerous tools operate under human hands and names with physical keys you can count.

- Kids carry rights nobody can pry loose, and a phone number that begins “We believe you.”

- You can know who is nudging you and why. You can turn it down.

- Trials end with grace. Failing to persist is a virtue when the world tells you fast that you were wrong.

- Work that cares for people counts like work that tightens bolts.

- Animals suffer less at our hands, and we argue in daylight about the places we still get it wrong.

- Machines coordinate. People decide. Boredom is a talent. Competence compounds. Joy remains.

A Walk Back

After the dry run, Rafi flicked the rubberized switch on his steward and heard the little motor sigh as it died. He strolled the long way home under ivy strung with ceramic spacers stamped with numbers where grapes would someday swell. A cork board near a fig trunk held two maps overlaid with thread: one traced a fox corridor that arced around schoolyards; the other wound along a grove elders liked to remember. A teenager and an old woman drew the solution in blue chalk that braided both. “So the fox feels wind, and I still walk through names,” the woman said, and they laughed.

In his kitchen the kettle had cooled. He poured anyway. On the page the House of Hands keeps for travelers, he wrote:

Turned a key at the bridge. Signed for a stranger’s bag. Counted figs for bats. The pump’s click sounded like a promise. We practiced stopping. It felt like a song.

Morning would bring the soft triple tick of the steward asking again for his brain. The list would be simple: a floodgate that needed hands, a clinic that wanted a gait test, a neighbor night with a chair that creaked and needed glue. The Lattice would hold. The Loam would feed it. And when storms came, as they always do, thermoses would arrive, and bored competence would run like a current, and the city would breathe together: a sound like patience, like abundance, like people who had agreed to keep the lights on for one another.
