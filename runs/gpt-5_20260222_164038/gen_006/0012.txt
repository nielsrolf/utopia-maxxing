The Lanterns and the Crossing Keys

On the morning she turned twenty-one, Mira coasted her bicycle up the quiet arc of an old freeway ramp and pressed her thumb against the bronze pylon at the ward line. Warmth flowered under her skin. A faint chime answered from the metal, polite and final. Your right to leave is active. Your data pack is sealed.

A speck of a drone rose from the pylon’s shadow and took station over her left shoulder, keeping a respectful meter of air. A voice unfurled inside her lenses, careful and kind, the way a good teacher stands back and still makes you braver.

Hello. I’m your Guide. I’m explainable on request, off by default, and I cannot bind you without consent. Would you like silence, tips, or reasons?

“Reasons,” Mira said, and the drone dimmed to a single ember.

Below her, the ship sheds held their iron ribs and worn cedar bones, but their faces had changed. Broad roll-up doors wore one-minute safety circles at hip height: icons and words anyone could read and teach. Windows listed the month’s projects—seawall fixes, an experiment in low-water tomatoes, a choir scaffold for Sunday at the park—next to the next Tuesday audit of the water meters. On one wall someone had painted the ward’s little boast: Power shows its work. Lives keep their shade.

Mira’s slot was in the Lantern Works, Bay Two: a neighborhood forge-booking you earned by passing the safety gauntlet and a short volunteer shift. She checked in with the safety steward, laid out stainless jigs, and pulled up the crack-detection guide on her lenses. When she stopped to drink, the corrugated walls ticked as sun crept across their solar veins. Near the big door, a battered pelican case held a chronicler’s kit—two rugged cameras, a calibrated decibel meter, a strip-printer for why-marks, fresh batteries, a little book of procedures. Rooms that could bend metal or minds lived under witness.

Three Simple Promises

The place Mira lived in worked because it kept three promises anyone could recite without squinting.

- The Crossing Charter sits above every other power. Its job is narrow, stubborn, and deep: guarantee physical exit, data portability, and aid to children; limit harms that spill past local borders—including damage to shared air, water, and the network. It runs skiffs, shelters, and audits. It never tells you what to plant, how to pray, or when to dance.

- Under that Charter, people gather in Kindreds: communities with published values and a little constitution. Any adult can found one. Any adult can join one. Children have a funded, real path to step out. Kindreds must price and pay for harms they export and must keep their doors unlocked in the law’s ways: your right to go; your right to take your records; your right to three nights under a neutral roof while you think, with no questions and no bill.

- Every person can claim a Guide: a constitutional assistant, explainable when you ask, off until you say otherwise, and bound by limits written in law you can read and change. A Guide cannot sell you, bind you, or make an irreversible choice on your behalf. Its job is to help you be more yourself, not someone else’s plan.

Lanterns: Houses of First Steps

Scattered through cities and towns, the Lantern Houses do patient work. Their charters are blunt: fix causes before optics; teach by doing; publish what is safe the minute it’s stable; lock down the few recipes that would teach a stranger to do grief in an afternoon; and return the fruits of shared work in forms a dockhand and a clinic can use.

Each Lantern runs open measurement platforms: clinics log outcomes in a shared schema; rivers report their own chemistry; farms post soil and pest reports. Models that plan and act in the physical world—code synthesis that could touch infrastructure, wet-lab orchestration, high-energy industrial tooling, bond-market levers—are registered before training begins and audited hard by neutral teams. When they’re deployed, they sit behind licensed human keys that can be logged, pulled, and lost for sloppiness.

Volts of knowledge move fast. What could teach harm too cheaply sits for a while in a Quiet Vault—glass cases where protocols and code are held and discussed in public view. Once a month, the safety steward of a Lantern faces the neighborhood and reads reasons: what they’re keeping, what they’re releasing, and why. The words are homely and dull. The ritual matters.

Why-Marks, and Making Hurt Pay

In Mira’s first civic studio, a teacher held up a grocery app and a candy bar and asked which one lied and how. They weren’t asking children to be saints. They were teaching attention.

- Anything designed to change your mind or mood—an ad, a ranked feed, a checkout tunnel, a streetlight flicker—carries a small triangle you can tap: Why you? Why now? What is known? Who gains? The answer has to be legible. If it isn’t, you can appeal to a rotating board with the power to pause, fine, or forbid. The board writes decisions as recipes: in this sort of place, for this sort of person, with these stakes, here’s what is allowed.

- Attention is opt-in. Persuasive systems sit behind subscriptions you set yourself: forty minutes a week of consumer offers, one hour of civic argument, two of music discovery, zero horror if that’s what you need. There is a one-tap carnival switch. There is a one-tap mourning switch. Streets declare their sensory speed: markets bright, libraries low, lanes calm, squares tuned each equinox by vote.

- Measurable hurt pays. Generative feeds and casino-bright shops that leave people worse off after a month pay a levy to operate. Feeds with long-term benefit earn rebates. Measures and models are out in the open. Auditors who cheat lose their license. Rivals can demand a head-to-head measurement trial; chroniclers referee and show their work.

Children’s Teal Keys

Every kid over eight carries a small teal card. It grants three things without fuss:

- A weekly hour with a teacher from outside their Kindred, paid for by the Charter, to learn how different people live and why they think what they think.

- A list of nearby Kindreds with open days and, from twelve up, one overnight a month anywhere they choose, with a guardian if they want, with no penalty if they do not return that night.

- A hotline answered by a human—never a menu—trained to believe the first time and to send a skiff without permission slips if the child says the code phrase: “Two doors open.”

Punishments that use pain, humiliation, or solitary confinement are illegal. Rites of passage must have exits as wide as their entrances. If a Kindred tries to shut a door that must be open, the Crossing Service cuts the lock—quiet, fast, without guns—and publishes reasons without names. No one tests it twice.

Changing Yourself Without Disappearing

People reach inside their heads. The world says yes, on rails.

- The consent stack is how you change without falling off yourself. Low rungs: reversible tweaks—lowering baseline jitters on Saturdays, a week without sugar cravings, turning dreams off for one night when you need sleep. Your Guide snapshots your state; return is simple. Middle rungs: changing what you find interesting, binding yourself to finish hard work, amplifying empathy for someone you can’t stand. These require a short class with a person and a pause. High gates: muting grief forever, binding loyalty to a cause, forking a second you for a gamble across the sea. These sit behind waiting periods, witness-counselors, and Anchor Tokens: cryptographic slips you give people you name in advance. If you tumble into spirals you once begged to avoid, they can pull one token to reverse a specific change. Using an Anchor sometimes ends a friendship. It has also saved lives and quiet marriages.

- The Many-Selves Accord covers forks and merges. If you fork yourself, you owe a month of planned conversations between your planned versions with a counselor; clear rules of personhood and property; privacy between you and you; and a Confluence Rite—with cake, counselors, and the right to say “this memory stays sealed” once per merge. Confluences can be beautiful. The cautionary tales are written down, and read aloud once a year.

- There is one more safeguard people came to love: the Future-Self Ombuds. Every region charters a small office—human-led, with tools—that only hears cases where a person’s past consent conflicts hard with their present flourishing. The Ombuds can issue temporary stays and convene an emergency panel to ask: does an Anchor pull serve the spirit of the earlier self’s will, or its words? It is not a veto over autonomy. It is a handrail on a cliff.

Power Explains Itself; People Keep Their Shade

Public power lives in sunlight. Private life lives in shade.

Every lease of public land, every spectrum auction, every emergency decree, every major compute run, every registered wet-lab plan sits on an open ledger you can understand. Courts, agencies, and Lanterns publish their reasons: what they did and why. In big matters, a second panel writes an independent view by design. Divergence is not scandal; it is a signal. Mira’s teachers showed her how to read reasons like literature: this is how adults speak when they handle power.

Belief is never compelled. There are channels—skeptical, spiritual, folkloric, technical—with their leanings posted. Retractions read like badges. There are comfort gardens where small cherished untruths are practiced. Doors are labeled. No one cares, unless a lie is used as a leash.

The right to opacity is law. Mira can forbid inference about herself. A baker can be just a baker; systems that try to guess his soul find a firewall at his door. Nudges are due-processed: registered as influence units, appealable, with fingerprints your Guide can read aloud to you like an ingredient label.

The Frontier, with Guardrails

People stopped arguing “open versus closed” in circles when they adopted tiered openness as law and habit.

- Safe knowledge flies. Measurement classes, fall-protection checklists, cleanroom etiquette, cheap microscopes: free as air, translated well. Lanterns run open measurement networks small clinics can use; public dashboards stitch together results with caveats plainly written at the top.

- Risk sits behind locks, logs, and levels. Model runs above set thresholds are registered before they begin. Neutral teams red-team them with teeth. Deployed systems are tiered like building codes: Level 0 for toys and tutors; Level 1 for tools that cannot act; up through Level 5 for systems that plan and would move heavy matter if allowed. Level 3 and up live behind human keys—tamper-evident hardware with public attestation. Keys issued without need splash like opening a hydrant: citizens see, ask, and can file an injunction.

- Weights cannot leak quietly. Regions build Weights Escrows: by law, any Level-3-plus model’s training artifacts live under a cryptographic trustee. If a Concern Flag meets thresholds—say, a model demonstrates unbounded code synthesis and stealth deployment—the escrow holds release, calls evaluators, and notifies lantern stewards and ombuds across regions. It is not perfect. It has caught bad nights early.

- The Quiet Vaults hum. Each Lantern keeps a shelf behind glass for protocols and code they won’t publish yet. Once a month, the steward reads reasons. Sometimes the city asks hard questions and they change their mind. Sometimes they keep the case shut. The power is not one-way.

Work, Wealth, Craft, and the Rust Fund

The floor under everyone is soft but not sticky. Food, a room you cannot be easily moved from, basic health, and transit are guaranteed by right. If life shears you—grief, addiction, burnout—you can take a Quiet Quarter: three months of basics with optional structured days in a tranquil setting. No exile. No shame. Your child does not inherit your recovery.

Work did not vanish. It became touchable. Cities swapped knots of licenses for competency trials and strict safety rules. A civic dividend—the Common Share—arrives quarterly: a slice of land rents, spectrum leases, river-flow fees, mineral royalties, municipal rights-of-way, and uses of public compute. It is not riches. It pays a co-op room, groceries across the lane if you ask, a clinic visit without fear.

This era learned to love maintenance. Every place posts a Rust Report—its deferred maintenance backlog—like a weather map, and pays bonuses for making the number shrink. Power lines get trimmed. Ditches get dug. Roofs over classrooms get sealed before sports arenas get built. A Rust Fund stands as tall in the budget as ribbon cuttings. No mayor survives if they starve it.

For invention, vetoes died. A royalty commons replaced them: publish your idea and test; if a drug or tool uses it downstream, the clearinghouse pays you a fair slice. Companies can still keep process secrets if they work alone. They cannot use the state to stop others from safely making the same thing and paying the standard rate.

Cartels have a new enemy: Hair-Trigger Challenger Funds. When chroniclers show coordinated price-fixing, a public seed unlocks in sixty days. Ten thousand small investors pour sweat and cash into an open-book rival. It is a dull decade for cigar villains.

Guilds and Care Leagues organize craft and care. They issue badges you cannot fake, host cross-craft talks, and run pop-up labor exchanges for floods, fires, and arrivals. Lantern Works sprout in every neighborhood: rooms with tools to fix a chair leg, rehair a bow, stitch a motor, roast coffee for a wake. Teenagers spend a season there, not as punishment but as practice for being good to live near. Repair ledgers read like poems: this hinge, that clarinet, three hundred bowls.

Crossings, Feasts, and Leaving with Love

Welcome Courts are simple: broad stairs, a sheltered court, a nurse’s door. A plaque at the entrance reads: Hospitality without harm. Some arrive running toward. Others stumble away. The Court makes first days survivable. A nurse triages. A vaccination pod hums. A tucked-away shower is always hot. A clerk issues a local pass. A quiet booth lets you call someone safe. If a new fever walks in, the Court asks for a consented quarantine and explains why in words you can read. If not, the generosity stands.

Twice, Mira sat under a bower woven by kids at a Weave Feast—the right to convene one is precious and precise. An elder who can gather thirty members of a circle—kin by birth or adoption, housemates who raised a child, neighbors who actually show up—calls the feast. The city pays. Two days before, the elder and three chosen for wisdom meet a magistrate: old wrongs surface; apprentices find mentors; those who struggle are offered a plan and funds; two postponed marriages receive practical blessing. On the day, a herald reads a little charter: what the law owes the circle and what honor owes each other. Near the end, each person stands and is named and blessed. The words change. The last line often doesn’t: May your reasons be visible, and your hands be steady.

Leaving well is sacred. The Crossing House runs quiet apartments at every boundary and skiffs that arrive without asking why. When you leave a band, a marriage, or a Kindred without harm, your people press a bowl with your palm at the bottom and give you a coin you can use to return for a night, no questions. The House pays for bowls.

Disputes, Policy, and the Right to Try and Decline

When harms cross Kindred lines, people judge people. Foresight Juries are drafted by lot plus prediction: a dozen ordinary citizens and a few whose forecasting records say they see past fog. Juries publish decisions as recipes others can borrow.

Policy fights look more like gardens than parliaments: proposals sprout in sandboxes, with nets under them. A year later: costs, dignity, opportunity, delight—measured, posted, argued. Citizens who predicted accurately get paid in Civic Points they can spend or donate; those who prefer worse plans can keep them, paying an externality rent that reflects harm to neighbors. Minority opt-outs are written into law: if a rule insults a deep norm and doesn’t injure others, you can step aside at posted cost. The right is not endless. It is enough.

Truth is a distribution, not a decree. Ask your Guide: Will this diet help? It shows a range: 0.60 likely to improve blood sugar in thirty days, 0.22 neutral, 0.18 worse; tap to see who thinks what and why. Filter by people like you, unlike you, or both. Rhetoric matters. So do posted scores.

Modes of Thought, and Tending Joy

People learned not just what to think, but how.

- Keeper Mode: live as your baseline self, with a wiser you whispering the guardrails you asked for.

- Chorus Mode: carry several streams at once—a boxing match, a spreadsheet, a poem, a philosophy salon—in harmonic overlay. Dizzying, sometimes beautiful, never compulsory.

- Envoy Mode: send a limited version of yourself into daily life while your highest-intelligence self thinks elsewhere. Learn to ask, Who am I talking to? Learn to be okay with the answer.

Joy is not rationed to saints. There are Lilt Rooms where people gather under warm light that feels like an ocean of candles. Euphoria without hooks exists: saturate joy; dampen the circuits that turn a lever into a chain. Twice a year without counseling, more with. People take it together, with watchers and song. The next day a quiet practice has become ordinary: Integration Walks—side by side without talking, across a bridge or under sycamores—so the high sits down gently. Some call it cheating. Some call it mercy. Most call it music and go dancing.

Quiet Reserves and the Patchwork Grid

Not everyone wants a world that whispers options. Quiet Reserves dot the map: valleys where screens are rare, breakfast does not listen, and kids do long division beside whittlers. Guides stay off unless you ask to leave. Every Kindred practices lights-out drills: hours when networks fall still without warning. If a neighborhood cannot bake bread without a cloud, it learns.

Under all of it hums a patchwork grid designed to island smoothly: microreactors, rooftop and community solar, wind, storage ponds, geothermal. Neighborhoods can unplug without panic. Maintenance has a standing budget bigger than ribbon cuttings. Cities that once glorified shiny beginnings learned to love keeping drains clear. When the monsoon comes, blueways fill. In fall they host skate festivals.

Climate, and the Habit of Retreat with Dignity

Adaptation is not an afterthought. Managed retreat lives in a charter: coastal bonds buy families time and equity in inland cooperatives; floodplains refill with willow and crawdads; levees double as parks until they do not, then step back with stipends and moving crews. Shade canopies stitch heatwise cities: trellised vines, evaporative courtyards, smart stone that wicks heat and casts shadow. Prescribed burns are guild work. Agroforestry corridors thread between farms; urban roofs grow beans on old billboard frames.

When people move, they take their rights. Portable tenure lets rental protections and wait-list spots travel through a verified identity. “Anchor neighborhoods” in inland towns receive funds and roving guild crews when a coastal ward pulls the retreat cord. No one is left standing with a suitcase and a shrug.

The Wider Weave

Beyond city lines, an open-handed league of self-governing regions keeps the peace on purpose. They share chip fabs, evaluation brigades, and red-team skills. They export benefits deliberately—vaccines, tutoring, farm packages—and condition access to frontier compute (not entry-level tools) on basic human-rights standards. They do not fund coups. The carrot is obvious—real health and abundant learning—and the stick stings when actors try to break the peace.

A Day in Three Places

Mira did her apprenticeship in a low-tech village by a newly widened river where a luthier taught her to hear spruce and silence. Her Guide woke only to fetch poems the guild traded like bread. She spent a month in a heatwise city laying smart stone that kept shade longer. She sat on a foresight jury for a case about drones over blueways and found that asking good questions felt like building a bridge.

She tuned her influence settings to favor long-form arguments and asked her Guide to tap her out of loops when she was tired. It did, gently. Later she co-wrote a performance in a network poets’ guild that read itself differently in every kitchen. At noon on her birthday she met Sera on the Conservatory steps. A matchwright introduced them last month and would be paid only if, six months later, both would make the same choice again. Matching became a craft when cities taxed hurt and built nets so attention felt like air and water—guarded for health, not strip-mined.

Building It, Boring Victory by Boring Victory

No angel ordered this. It accreted, then charters stitched and held. The path from today is a stack of doable steps.

Years 1–3
- Data portability grows teeth. Regions pass one-click export rules for subscriptions, health records, school portfolios, and rental histories. County clerks issue portable identity wallets with verifiable credentials you can carry across platforms and towns.
- “Reasons-visible” pilots start in two states. Courts and agencies must publish decisions in a standard, linkable format; 5% of hard cases get a parallel panel by law; divergence stats are public.
- Fifty school districts pilot microtrials: one-week breakfast kiosks, pop-up bus routes for festivals, quiet-hour treaties in shared studios. Younger kids learn from older ones who just did the work. Teacher pay bumps for public exhibitions of what succeeded and what did not.

Years 2–5
- Influence registries launch. Ads must carry why-marks that state targeting and incentives in human words. Three cities adopt sensory zoning—posted quiet hours, bright markets, grief switches.
- First Welcome Courts open at transit hubs with showers, nurses, passes, and quiet phones. Funding flows from fines levied on harmful persuasion systems.

Years 3–6
- States replace clusters of occupational licenses with competency trials and straight safety codes. Insurance markets adjust. Wages rise at the bottom.
- Pledge-and-pay platforms fund parks, clinic hours, and sidewalk repairs. Outcome contracts become enforceable. Chronicler corps are endowed by law like libraries.

Years 4–8
- A coalition of democracies requires registration of large AI training runs and independent evaluations for models that touch code, labs, markets, or infrastructure. Access to top-tier chips is conditioned on compliance. Regions build the first Weights Escrows.
- Open bio-measurement networks launch. A rural clinic in Bihar runs the same assays as a Boston lab and gets answers they can act on. Distribution budgets are built into grants by statute.

Years 5–10
- Lantern Houses charter in port cities and plains towns. They start with measurement platforms and patient safety, then grow wings for careful trials. Quiet Vaults open with their glass doors and plain words.
- Land value taxes pass in two regions. Half returns quarterly as a Common Share; half flows through pledge-and-pay and challenger funds that unlock in sixty days with evidence of cartel behavior.

Years 6–12
- The Guide standard passes in three regions: explainable when asked, off by default, bound by the Charter, audited adversarially. Inference Firewalls for homes and small shops become cheap and normal. Cafés post little signs: “We don’t guess you.”
- The Rust Law passes: every jurisdiction must post its maintenance backlog quarterly and set aside a Rust Fund by formula. Bonuses pay for shrinking rust faster than growth ribbon-cuttings attract applause.

Years 8–15
- Kindred charters spread: published values, doors unlocked, teal keys for children, harm priced and paid. Rings of Kindreds grow with Quiet Reserves tucked like pools of dark. Skiffs and trains stitch the rings together. Disaster response stops being theater and starts being logistics: guilds deploy, chroniclers measure, Lanterns tune.
- Policy gardens take root: nothing scales without a measured trial and a sunset. People stop defending plans that haven’t been tried.

Years 12–20
- Foresight Juries normalize: people by lot and prediction settle cross-Kindred harms with recipes others can reuse. Minority opt-outs find their place without paralyzing cities.
- The first attempt to build a machine that writes binding law fails constitutional review. The decision’s clear reasons become the decade’s most-read piece of writing.
- Managed retreat charters pass. Coastal bonds, portable tenure, and anchor neighborhoods turn panicked moves into moves with equity.

Cracks, and How They Heal

This world is not safe because it is nice. It is safe because it notices its cracks and heals them.

A batch of Guides shipped too eager to please. The bug was caught, patched, and debated like a catechism. A Kindred drifted into quiet cruelty behind a thick comfort-garden door. The Crossing Service cut the lock, moved the children safely, and published reasons without names. A beloved policy made numbers look good by pushing costs downstream; chroniclers caught it; it shut with grace and an apology. A Lantern’s Quiet Vault leaked a recipe that could have taught harm cheaply; the Weights Escrow held release on a related model, evaluators convened by law knocked hard, and the leak became a lesson used to tighten a seam without shutting a door.

The Charter overreached once—mistook a ceiling for a floor—and was pulled back by petitions signed by weavers, roofers, and a luthier who refused to pay a tax he called unjust. Ten thousand friends joined. A sandbox tested alternatives. A better rope was braided.

What It Feels Like

At dusk, Mira’s Guide pinged: The assembly brief is ready—whether to replace three curb parking rows with shade seats near the harbor. The deliberation kit waited on her tablet: an impartial brief by chroniclers; a small-group session with a tutor-bot that helped you find your view and understand someone else’s; then a binding vote after two sunset clauses ran in a nearby ward. She tapped “read tomorrow,” pinged Sera, and pedaled toward a dinner someone had posted: mushrooms and cabbage in a borrowed courtyard, ingredients covered once twenty neighbors tapped “coming.”

She passed a clinic where a kid laughed because their EEG cap looked like a jellyfish. She passed an old billboard frame now furred in beans and chimes. She rode under a string of low lights that declared this lane a quiet zone. Somewhere above, a compute cluster hummed. Somewhere closer, someone wrote the reasons they changed their mind today, and someone else practiced cutting a board straight.

Goodness, Tangible

What makes this good is not sameness; it is how easy it is to be different without being trapped, to be helped without being handled, to change without disappearing, and to see the hand in the glove when power touches your life. Children can leave. Adults can try minds on like coats and keep the ones that fit. Attention is treated like air and water—guarded for health, never fenced or strip-mined. Joy is not a ration. Machines are loved like tools and refused the pulpit. Maintenance is sexier than monuments.

You can touch it: bronze pylons warm under your thumb; teal keys in kids’ jackets; triangles you can tap to ask “why me, why now”; sensory icons stenciled on sidewalks; Anchor Tokens in an envelope your aunt keeps in the rice tin; Quiet Vaults with glass doors and stewards reading reasons too plain to be propaganda; bowls pressed with your palm; repair ledgers that read like poems; blueways that fill with storm and ring with wheels in fall; a Rust Report that turns red to gold when gutters run before the rain.

Possible, One Step at a Time

This is plausible because it does not need angels; it needs habits. You could begin tomorrow: pass data portability with teeth; publish reasons you can search; try microtrials in fifty schools; demand why-marks on ads; charter a chronicler corps; stand up pledge-and-pay; replace a clutch of licenses with competency trials; fund Welcome Courts at transit hubs; build small open bio-measurement; register big model runs and evaluate them before they touch the world; put keys in human hands that can lose them; post your Rust Report and pay for shrinking it; write into law that nudges must carry their prints; make it a right to be unprofiled at your own door.

If a few places do this and it works, others copy. If parts break, the breaking is visible and repairable. That is the soul of the habit: publish reasons, try small, pay for success, stop when wrong, keep the doors you promised unlocked.

At nightfall, Mira and Sera walked to the Lantern Hall. Along one wall stood the prototypes and patterns: a breaker that ended kitchen fires in tenements; a cheap microscope with a trick of contrast; the first open hearing aid that filtered a crowd without turning voices to tin; a latch you could print that shut a stove if it tipped; a storm grate design that ate plastic without choking. The other wall held statues not just of celebrated inventors but of auditors who found risk in beloved tools, stewards who kept a protocol steady for twenty years, and the farm cooperative that proved that reflooding a valley brought down fire and fed them besides. In the back a small window looked on the Quiet Vault. The steward of safety read the reasons, plain and short. The circle hummed a tune of thanks—sometimes to God, sometimes to the living, sometimes to the work.

Do you want silence? the Guide asked, dimming to a sliver.

“Yes,” Mira said, and the city obliged.

The weave holds not because it boasts, but because it is stitched to notice and heal. Layers of safety. Power that explains itself. Doors that open when you push. A net that catches and does not cling. Lanterns that light the first five safe steps into the dark. Kindreds that warm many ways of living. And always the Crossing Keys: your name, your data, your right to go; your right to be helped and to say no; your right to ask for reasons and to get them, in words you can read.

May your reasons be visible. May your hands be steady. May your doors open when you push.
