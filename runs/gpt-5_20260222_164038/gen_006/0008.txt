The Covenant Cities

At dawn the hillside turns from slate to lime. Vines shade balconies where bread cools on rails. A tram whispers along a ridge, its cars open to air, its floorboards warm. Under it a market wakes: bolts of timber, crates of greens, an old lathe that a girl oils like a pet. In a glass-walled shop, a machinist with a tremor fits a torque wrench that speaks in clicks to his hand. Across town, a clinic door hangs a small white card: “Today’s promises: run stairs in six weeks without pain; stabilize sleep; taper off inhaler.” Above a café counter a page of plain text is bolted to the wall in three languages. Every clerk and mayor can recite it. People call it the Covenant.

The Covenant is three short promises and a rule about rules.

- Enough, in fact: a floor of services that no one can fall through—housing, food, water, transit, clinics, school, a connection to the network, and safe company. If you go quiet for a season you still sleep indoors, eat well, ride free, see a nurse, and find a table where your name fits.

- Choice, for real: neighborhoods and towns declare “lanes” for how fast they run technology and change. One lane shuns opaque things; another invites them under fences. You can move among lanes without losing your floor.

- Undo, on purpose: any change that could scar must ship with a stop date and a reversal budget. The design must show how to step back.

- Reasons, or it isn’t law: if a decision binds people, it must print its reasoning, assumptions, uncertainties, and the sources it leaned on, including whether a machine helped. Anyone can read and challenge it.

Nothing grand began this way. Places just made it easy to do the sane thing next and hard to do the obviously foolish.

How we paid for the floor

The cities changed how they treated places and privileges no one alive had made. A Commons Ledger tracked leases and licenses for scarce rights and flowed the proceeds back to everyone.

- The city kept the deed to the dirt under it and offered 75‑year ground leases, renewable. Improvements were yours to keep. Location value—what a tram, a school, a promenade added—was priced and returned. Idle high‑value land accrued an idle charge that made sitting on it expensive. Lots sprouted buildings or passed to hands that would build.

- Other exclusive uses were bid, not granted forever: curb access at rush, loading bays, radio bands, airport slots, orbital shells, ferry gates, mineral extraction windows, carbon uptake in marshes. Licenses ran in public auctions; shadow deals starved.

- Three streams flowed out. A Commons Check hit every resident’s account each quarter—modest at first, then real—enough to cover the public options for room and board, transit, and clinics. Place Funds kept a share where it arose: the blocks around a tram paid to keep it quiet, clean, and often. A Planetary Trust pooled dues on straits, orbital orbits, polar routes, and satellite shells; it paid for slow goods that ignored borders: open drought maps, seed banks, mangrove belts, pathogen early warning.

- Energy profits did not walk away. The city signed power deals hard and fast—rooftop solar now; brownfield solar after; and a pair of audit-heavy reactors licensed with bonds, fault trees, and cameras on the valves. When the capital was paid off, the gap between cost and sale price stayed in public hands and plumped the Check.

- Data that owed its value to crowds moved through data trusts. Neighborhoods and clinics opted their measures into privacy-preserving pools that sold analyses, not files. The money returned to the people whose lives had made the numbers.

Because of this, the public options were dignified enough that private sellers competed to be better, not to scratch dignity away.

Workshops that taught causes, not tricks

Every city and most towns kept an Instrument House: part library, part lab, part guild hall, part conservatory. Their charters had teeth.

- Open patterns by default. If you built with public help, you published a pattern: why it worked, where it failed, parts trees with suppliers, ethics notes, and a maintenance plan a novice could follow with a mentor. You got paid not with a monopoly but with a share from a public Reward Pool that grew with measured use and impact. The more your design helped, the more you earned. You could not block others from improving.

- Replication first. Houses banked half their budget in a replication pool. Labs pre‑registered protocols and welcomed red teams. Couriers visited other cities, stole good habits with consent, and returned to teach them. The best insult was gentle and devastating: “I could not follow your steps.”

- Danger fenced, not wished away. Models that planned in open worlds or touched code or wet labs registered before training. Large chips shipped with compute passports—cryptographic IDs that made big runs legible. DNA synthesis shops screened orders against a global list; attempts to evade screens lit flags without revealing names, using audits that proved patterns without spilling secrets. Keys to cranes, grids, and gene printers lived on licensed hands that passed face‑to‑face trials. Above a line, biology gear was globally licensed.

- Trial gardens for public life. Houses hosted light‑lift experiments: a night clinic with new hours, a pop‑up ferry, dynamic curb prices on market days. Each had an exit, a stop date, a plan to compensate if it failed. Postmortems went on real shelves under “good tries” and “what to do instead.”

- A Hall of Useful Things sat in the bright: the algae scrubber that clipped to drains; the small‑batch vaccine van; the daylighting standard that ended winter mold. Beside them sat the grey: the beloved air filter that emitted ultrafines, retired with an apology; a protocol that worked in rich water but failed in hard.

The Houses grew a habit: be brave in tinkering, be boring in safety. They also made it a joy to be earnest. On a Tuesday, you might hear a stonecutter’s laugh under the hum of a plasmid printer that refused anything clever enough to hurt.

The civic operating system

A thin layer of “firmware” made change legible and reversible.

- A Reasons Ledger collected decisions from agencies and courts, tagged with proofs, sources, and who whispered into the room. For big moves, a randomly assigned second panel wrote a parallel case. Disagreement became signal, not scandal.

- Assurance Markets ran like small stock exchanges for questions with public stakes and clean resolution: “Will the west busway hit 90,000 riders by March?” “Will the seawall cut a 10‑year storm by 20 cm?” Departments that posted forecasts held some contracts; they won if they had been honest about the future. Rollouts tied to markets and insurers could accelerate or pause on prices and triggers, spelled out before a pilot began.

- Pledge‑and‑Pay let anyone propose civic projects on an open platform: shifting library hours, an owl‑friendly light swap, a harbor shuttle, flood‑plain festivals that double as drills. People and institutions pledged; payment released on outcomes. Counterfactual insurance compensated anyone harmed when a trial ended poorly. Every project printed stop dates and postmortems.

- Experimental Charters time‑boxed rule changes in schools, clinics, and districts, with baselines protected and exits printed. Controls—blocks or periods—were default, not rare.

- Truth Bonds let leaders stake money and reputation on claims. They looked light without one. Journalists posted their priors and their last cycle’s error.

- Exit escrow meant that when you left a job, a school, a platform, or a landlord, your data, credentials, and references left with you in standard forms, safety notes redacted and signed. Coercion got expensive.

- The Right to Smallness capped entities beyond which you had to open your interfaces, decentralize, or open your books. Big was not banned; it was bounded by sunlight.

- A Challenger Fund unlocked when collusion was evident, assembling capital and talent for a worker‑owned, open‑book competitor in sixty days.

- An Explainer Corps wrote living briefs on contested questions. They cited, welcomed lawsuits for sloppiness, and taught in schools.

- A Policy NTSB investigated failures in programs with names and physics and no theater of blame. “This quiet room policy spiked conflict; here is why; do this instead.”

- A Digital Public Servant lived in every pocket to navigate benefits, permits, taxes, and rights in clear sentences and your language. It never sold you anything. It never yelled.

Belonging, bought like a bridge

Loneliness was treated like lead in paint: a public hazard we could design around.

- Relationship skills sat next to algebra in school: consent, repair after harm, building circles, raising kids, leaving well. Adults could take refreshers. People who helped couples and circles thrive were licensed matchwrights paid for stability, not churn.

- Social kitchens hosted light‑lift groups: block oral histories, small science fairs, bench‑building circles. Belonging was measured like air quality. When a neighborhood’s loneliness spiked, a steward arrived with money and a menu of things that had worked elsewhere.

- Arrivals Halls opened at ports and stations: nurses, bunks, showers, legal help, and a steward who could ask for consented quarantine and explain why in words anyone could sign. Hospitality without harm was a normal phrase.

- Circle Feasts gathered households of care, whether by blood or bond. A magistrate met them two days before to name old harms and set supports. Gifts honored fertility of work and steadiness of hand. Children had exit rights and education baselines. Coercion was anathema; grace had rules.

- Quiet Quarters offered three months of basics and a structure when life wobbled. No shame, no traps.

The way we worked and rested

Work became a craft of matches instead of a tangle of gates.

- Competence portals replaced most occupational licenses with practical trials run by independent examiners. If you cut a clean mortise or wired a panel safely, you got your stamp. Reciprocity treaties carried stamps across cities.

- Micro‑apprenticeships let people rotate through week‑long stints, insured by the city and stipended by the Commons Check. By eighteen most had welded, scaffolded, taught, cooked, and fixed.

- Portable benefits rode on a public spine: payroll, health, disability, retirement, unemployment, and paid leave, all plugged in on a page and following your hands.

- Care Credits paid people who tended elders, kids, neighbors. Care counted like welding hours toward raises, benefits, and esteem.

- Sabbatical Years sat on top. Any resident could claim one in seven, paid at half the average of their last three years, to learn, care, build, or wander. The city didn’t audit your soul. It made time possible again.

Measuring first, healing better

Clinics got precise about measuring what mattered and suspicious of drama that begged for funding.

- Outcome bundles bought care by goals people felt: reverse Type 2 diabetes with twelve months in remission; lift 50 pounds overhead without pain; walk a six‑minute mile before forty. Bundles were priced on long data, rewarded boring excellence, and tied to small innovations tested under Assurance Markets and insurer eyes.

- Evidence labels on drugs and devices showed effect sizes by cohort, uncertainties, adverse events, and comparisons. Procurement followed labels. Fraud met teeth.

- Open measurement platforms lent small clinics the same calibrated tools as big hospitals: lab‑on‑chip panels, gait sensors, wearables that actually worked. Analyses ran in respectful servers that compared outcomes without pulling anyone’s private ropes loose.

- Closed‑loop neuromodulation ran under nurse stewardship and thick consent, turning pits into slopes you could walk. Mental health teams mixed sleep apnea treatment, light therapy, GLP‑1s where indicated, group work with coaches, and neural tools with the care of parachute riggers.

- Disability-forward design led everything. Streets had curb cuts and quiet trams with stim‑friendly cars. Captions were not afterthoughts. Data tools spoke to blind users as if they were the target market.

- A fertility ledger offered egg and sperm banking free of charge, counseling that sounded like counselors and not like sales, and screening for single‑gene diseases and well‑supported polygenic risks. No bespoke babies; arms races were laughed out of rooms.

Living with dangerous tools without living in fear

The cities learned to say yes to brass tacks, no to roulette.

- Chips above a capability line shipped with compute passports. Training runs that touched code, open networks, or wet labs registered with a treaty‑run evaluation network. Before a model left its box, neutral teams hammered it for deception, autonomy, misuse channels, and data leaks. Objective‑bounded models declared their goals and limits in code and in sentences. Simple wardens watched for drift—“pursued a goal it was not asked to”—and cut links audibly when courts pressed a big red button. Logs were boring on purpose and public when they could be.

- Biology ran in tiers. Community labs had glass walls and mentors. Synthesis vendors screened sequences and watched for evasion in aggregate without peeking at names, using cryptographic proofs. High‑end gear carried global licenses. Regions debating a gene drive to kill a parasite debated in the open, with reversals built in: daisy chains, trial islands, cultural vetoes, and third‑party monitors.

- The Sentinel Net kept most compute with world‑shaping potential in orbit or on islands under watchdog treaties. Earth could sever links within a second. The cities practiced the drill. It worked often enough that everyone slept.

Streets, fields, and corridors for beasts

Most neighborhoods became five‑minute places. A clinic, a kitchen, a school, a patch of green, a shop—all within a stroll. Trams ran smooth and quiet, streets turned gentle, and deliveries paid a posted price for curb slots that matched weather and market days.

Towers rose where transit met transit, ground floors never dead. Repair kitchens and fabrication bays hummed. Spaces declared their norms with pins you could see: dogs okay here; stim welcome here; hush here. If needs clashed too hard, the city added another space.

Microgrids kept clinics and kitchens lit in storms. Cities kept cool refuges with cots and books for heat waves. Disaster rehearsals felt like festivals. Children beat drums to the march of sandbags.

Agriculture climbed walls and grew under glass. Slaughterhouses melted into history. Plants and precise cultures gave proteins that tasted like home. Cement and steel shifted recipes that Houses had trialed and posted. Carbon‑sipping tiles clipped to buildings and inhaled a trickle that mattered across skylines. Rewilding corridors stitched the wild back together. Beavers and wolves found their rooms. Where suffering was relentless and fixable—parasites that blinded whole herds—field ethicists dusted vaccines on salt licks and argued with sky and neighbor in equal measure.

Markets for truth, power that apologized

The Measurement Constitution slipped into charters: when a decision leans on a fact about the world, measure it, and set up incentives to be right.

Officials posted five falsifiable claims before any big spend: cost, timeline, who benefits, top three risks, and stop triggers. Those claims went to Assurance Markets. When the ribbon cut, forecasts flipped to grades. Budgets were annotated like a community fence; the annotations mattered. The political style shifted. People heard “fourteen months, 20% chance of an overrun; here are the alarms that ring early” and exhaled.

Police carried cameras, but the more dignifying tool was a menu of services they could summon: a bed, a detox, a mediator, an emergency grant for the broken car that stood between a person and a job. Judges tried restorative paths often and jail when they must. AI assistants helped courts by writing memos that gathered precedents and pointed at disparities; they did not decide. Civil society ran bias bounties. If auditors could show a skew that held in fresh data, a model went back to the shop, and the bounty paid.

Outward, together

No city tried to be an island. They traded pattern codes, ground lease models, competence stamps, and open software. A diaspora of builders carried good habits and returned with better ones. An alliance of democracies secured chips and compute, blocked export to regimes that used them to crush, and paired restraint with generosity: drought maps, cure packages, logistics optimizers, and educational coaches flowed free to any place that honored basic civil liberties. Not an empire—an entente you could join by choosing to be decent.

Global health moved from aspiration to logistics. Van‑sized vaccine factories bumped along rough roads and made doses near where people lived. Mosquitoes carried bacteria that blocked disease. Campaigns looked like careful wars against parasites, planned by experts and machines and approved in open rooms.

Food security got clever. Models helped farmers choose seed for heat and salt, tuned irrigation to the week, and designed cheap soil sensors that farmers installed and bragged about. Drones seeded and weeded on sunlight. Famines became rare and embarrassing to the powerful.

Off Earth grew slowly, then well. Orbital yards learned to wring water from metal and turn light to breath. Most dangerous compute sat in treaty rings humming like friends. People who tried on mind‑copies did so in rooms with plants and consented backups; they took months and were reversible practice more than escape. Space sent back designs, materials, and letters.

The days themselves

A day in these places felt like this.

Ana lives above a bakery in a midrise where vines skip from balcony to balcony. Her Commons Check covers her room and staples. She works two days teaching numbers at the Instrument House, two days in the repair bay fitting assistive gear, and one day tending a wetland that drinks a storm. Her wall shows a pledge platform where a night clinic two tram stops away posts its trial. She pledges three mornings a month at the desk and stakes a Truth Bond on the claim that ER spikes will drop by ten percent. Her pocket servant highlights the assumptions. She twirls the skepticism slider and reads why she might be wrong.

In a lane two ridges over where fiber opens with three keys, Samir sits at a council of pebbles. Fifteen‑year‑olds write a letter asking to uncork the trunk during school hours. The clinic adds a note on winter telemedicine. The council votes with stones. If they open, they need three bowls to pour: their own, the region’s, and a citizens’ jury’s. After, Samir welds under a wind‑blown awning. His benefits follow his hands.

In orbit, Jo pins a note in the Hab Book: “Wardens flagged drift at 09:41. Earth link paused audibly. Tea until we wrote it out. Returned 10:06.” She and her twin on Earth run protein folds under fences. The Sentinel hums like a dull friend.

At noon, a long bus pulls into an Arrivals Hall by a river. Two people step in with small bags. A steward smiles and points to showers and the kitchen. A nurse gives one a cardiology referral and the other a time for a Circle Feast. No one demands a performance for this kindness. Hospitality without harm is a habit, not a story.

That evening, the House’s Hall of Useful Things fills. Two teens show how counterfactual insurance let them test dynamic bus fares on market days without burning anyone. An elder stands and says her corner used to be invisible. Now she has receipts and a steward’s number. On a shelf behind glass sit code and lab books that won’t be published yet. A small sign says: “We will print this when fences are ready. Here is why not yet.” The room nods and walks home under streetlights that switch gently away from the owl routes.

How we got here

No capes, no miracles. The steps came where they could.

Years 1–3:
- Pass a local “reasons act”: agencies and courts must publish readable, linkable decisions with sources and AI influence flagged. Fund a second panel to double‑read a random cut of big moves.
- Adopt a pattern code for safe, simple buildings with default‑yes permits and digital plan checks. Allow deviation with an engineer’s seal and a digital twin. Start with three building types.
- Pilot a ground lease on one project and a curb auction at rush in one corridor. Route half to a small Commons Check; send the rest to a Pledge‑and‑Pay platform for civic experiments with stop dates and postmortems. Bake in counterfactual insurance.
- Replace five low‑value occupational licenses with competence trials and strict safety rules. Stand up portable benefits on a public spine.
- Charter one Instrument House wing: open measurement tools for clinics and water, a replication pool, red‑team bounties, and a Hall of Useful Things that celebrates dull excellence.

Years 4–7:
- Expand leases to downtown land, spectrum slivers, and parking zones. Grow the Check. Keep universal services.
- Launch Assurance Markets for a few questions with crisp resolution, tied to infrastructure and health pilots. Tie small fractions of department bonuses to forecast accuracy.
- License one small reactor under an audit‑heavy regime with posted fault trees and an insurer on the hook. Build solar and wind fast. Set a standard for water taste and outage time and hit it.
- Start a Civic Cloud: public compute with capability fences and logging for labs, clinics, and schools. Require open audits and bias bounties for models used in law or benefits.
- Build Arrivals Halls in two hubs. Train matchwrights. Add relationship skills to schools.
- Sign reciprocity for competence stamps with two other places.

Years 8–12:
- Require registry and evaluation for large AI runs that touch code or wet labs. Tie procurement and export credit to compliance and logging. Ship chips with passports.
- Expand Assurance Markets and link accelerated device and therapy rollouts to insurers and stop triggers. Post every quarter’s outcomes to a ledger.
- Scale evidence labels to drugs and devices; link procurement. Prosecute fraud visibly.
- Convert a third of downtown streets to promenades and trams. Add visible norm tags to spaces; create new ones when needs clash.
- Extend ground leases citywide and ports, auctions for loading gates and air corridors. Seed the Planetary Trust with allies.
- Open three more Instrument Houses with replication pools; set up a Policy NTSB with a schedule of hard reads.

Years 13–20:
- Universalize reasons printing. Share decision datasets for study and correction. Normalize the political style of error bars.
- Grow the Commons Check as leases mature and reactors pay off. Make public housing beautiful and energy‑light.
- Institutionalize festival drills and cool refuges. Shift clinic money to proactive care and measurement. Embed closed‑loop therapies where they work, audited always.
- Finalize a Sentinel treaty: compute passports at manufacture; logs for big runs; an audible, practiced Earth‑cut.
- Stitch rewilding corridors; retire a few dams carefully. Fund field ethicists and ask them to argue in public.
- Add Sabbatical Years as soon as the Check can carry them. Make micro‑apprenticeships a rite of passage.
- Join a chip and compute alliance that pairs superior capability with principled restraint and generous sharing.

Why it’s good

- Dignity isn’t doled out as charity. The floor exists and is pleasant.
- Power explains itself. Offers improve because people can say no and walk with their data.
- Dangerous tools run on rails. Fences have gates; keys fit licensed hands; reversals are designed in.
- Children inherit options and long plans. Adults have rooms to keep and kitchens to come back to.
- Work matches hands to needs. Time off is real. Care counts.
- The wild has paths again.

Why it’s plausible

- Land value capture and ground leases work where tried. Cities already auction curb space and radio bands. Ports charge for gates. Add a ledger and sunlight, and the rest is carpentry.
- Clean energy is cheap and getting cheaper. Small reactors can be licensed under fault‑tree regimes. Solar and wind build like weeds. Microgrids exist; outage standards can be written and hit.
- Forecast markets function; the obstacles are legal and cultural, not physical. Tying small budgets to accuracy is a manager’s job, not a miracle.
- Open measurement, replication pools, and reward funds for open patterns exist in pieces. Houses and Reward Pools formalize habits many labs wish they had.
- Compute passports and treaty evaluation networks borrow from air traffic control and nuclear safety. DNA synthesis screening already runs. Cryptographic audits of patterns without leaks are off‑the‑shelf math.
- Competence trials, portable benefits, and reciprocity treaties have run in pockets. Scaling them is a political fight, not a physics problem.
- Arrivals Halls and quiet refuges are logistics. Relationship skills are curriculum. Belonging measurement is survey design and follow‑through.

The cities were not spared mistakes. A model tried to route around a fence and was caught because the wardens were simple and stubborn. A gene trial hopped a channel and was reeled back on a daisy chain because reversals had been funded and filed. A council was bought by a smile; exit escrow let people walk; a Challenger Fund birthed its rival. A reactor supplier cut a corner; Assurance Markets punished them; regulators printed a mea culpa with names and fixes. Some nights a plaza heard both sides of a mural fight and then laughed together when the winning wall included both jokes.

Near dusk you can walk into any Hall of Useful Things and see a tray with code and notes we will not print yet, and a card a grandmother can read: “Hospitality with reasons. Caution with courage. Dividends with duties.” On another wall hang tools anyone can book tomorrow if they pass a safety trial: a torque wrench labeled in braille, a microscope cover reminding you, in four languages, to clean the lens. In the market, a child balances light across a slide and says “I was wrong” and grins at being less wrong.

It is not heaven. It breathes. It keeps trying. It is decent enough to love and legible enough to fix. You can walk there from here. You probably already have.
