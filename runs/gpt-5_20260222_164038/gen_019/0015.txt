The city met her where she was, not where a brochure wished she’d be.

On the inside frame of Lina’s door sat a small brass bead no bigger than a chickpea. It was warm all winter from the little heat loop in the jamb. She pressed it. The louvered slats on the bedroom window relaxed as if someone thoughtful had come by before sunrise. The kettle made a single honest click. Three squares blinked awake on the slate by the sink, in the order this home had learned mattered to her: mend, lend, learn.

Mend: Seawater latch on Stair E wants eyes before first frost; maker and steward checks green; neighbor key open.

Lend: Canopy crew at East Arcade asks for three pairs of hands to string grape cable; hush will shift downwind while the night trolley loads.

Learn: After supper at the Proof House—“No-without-bruise: refusing well in public work.”

Her pocket coach lay by the fruit bowl, the size of a deck of cards. Along one edge: a physical mute slide that ended in a notch you could feel in the dark. It blinked its offer in Lina’s auntie’s voice—Guide me through your morning? Or keep me deaf?—and she thumbed the notch down until it met the hard stop. True quiet. The coach obeyed without sulking. Outside, a ceramic disk on a post the height of a child’s elbow glowed a cool blue: quiet hours until ten, by pact and by a small levy the drummers on Market Street paid to carry their practice further upriver.

On the slate, the latch packet opened. A child could assemble the stress model with a finger. There was a snapshot of the only time this design had wedged—five winters past when gull down rode a squall into the bay—and a sober note that the shop that machines the stainless pin often loses its second shift when the cannery’s harvest is thin in January. Two ways forward sat beneath with time, cost, and a little “neighbor patience” meter that pulsed with honest weather. One: pay for a winter premium shift so the pin stock doesn’t wobble. Two: swap to a waxed-timber clasp cut by a furniture yard on the tramline. Lina dragged two dots—urgency, fairness—until the line settled where she could own it if it went sideways. Somewhere in the network a tiny violet LED stopped pulsing. Somewhere else, a siren would not have to learn a baby’s name.

She tied her boots and stepped into streets that made promises like furniture: solid, handled, lasting.

The Loom Charter

The city pinned its spine to one page that a tired person could read without a second coffee. People called it the Loom because it tied together strands you could test with your hand and then wove life through them.

- A floor that isn’t a performance. A lockable, quiet room; food you’d serve a friend without apology; water that tastes like mountain stone; transit that arrives; a clinic that knocks when you ask; a fair slice of connectivity and compute minutes. No confession to earn it.

- Exits and returns as logistics, not dramas. If you leave a boss, a landlord, a school, a platform—or a neighborhood—you don’t have to leave your life. Arrival Houses with bunks and steam and soup sit where the rails meet the roads. A Bridge Account fronts moving costs. Records zipper themselves into a wallet you control. For big choices there are blue buttons that undo with dignity if you press them inside a window.

- Reasons under daylight. If a choice binds people—a permit, a budget, an emergency power, a model in a clinic—it prints its aim, trade-offs, error bars, who lobbied, and where a machine helped, in sentences your uncle can read. Side by side sits a dissent from a small, paid second room selected by lot and hired to argue decently.

- Big tools with visible hands. Anything that moves money, motors, molecules, microbes, minds, or votes at true scale operates only behind audited gates with more than one hand on the keys. Three keys turn: the maker who built it, the steward on salary to say “not yet,” and a ward from the group closest to the blast radius. If danger rises, a fourth key from a stranger-by-lot guild joins. When drift or mischief appears, a hard link cuts with a district-wide jaw-click that feels like the safe door in a school nurse’s office. Logs are boring and public. Drills are routine and dull.

- Reversals budgeted at the start. If a project is large—canals, code to talk to cranes, wetlands—it writes its own stop line on page one and rehearses the undo as often as the go. Undo reserves sit in the bank from day one. Interest grows on the money you pray not to spend.

- The commons pays for dignity. Land value made by public work, curb minutes at rush, wharf gates and ferry slips, radio slices and drone lanes, festival soundscapes, even shade on plazas at noon—none of these are given away forever. They lease on clocks over ledgers everyone can read. Three streams fall from those ledgers every quarter: a Commons Share that lands in every resident’s account; a Block Purse that stays where it rose and can patch gutters, lights, and kitchens without a pilgrimage to City Hall; and a World Work Fund that buys the good that crosses borders—reef belts, drought maps, open tutors, vaccine vans with knobbies and shocks.

- Floors that travel. Benefits ride rails you don’t have to beg to board: care, disability, old-age income, unemployment, parental leave. Proof of skill replaces paper licenses where latency can forgive. Your clinic file, work stamps, and payments export in open formats rivals can’t lock. When you cross town or quit a platform, your life follows you.

Money that belongs to here

You own your walls and your tools. Land value flows to the place that made it valuable. Every footprint rides a renewing ground lease that rewards care while cutting the cord on feudal immortality. A third of the rent becomes the Commons Share. A third keeps wires, drains, kitchens, and courts clean. A third seeds Undo Reserves.

Rush curb minutes, harbor slots, spectrum, orbital shells, air lanes at busy heights, and hush after dark lease without back doors. When side streets sleep on tram spines, they pay sleep rent that rises until something honest happens: you build, you lend, or you lend it to a garden.

Above rare rails—compact reactors, frontier labs, elite compute—you sign revenue-sharing attestation up front. After a set lift, a slice auto-clicks to the Share and to safety guilds. Skip and you lose cheap power and fast fiber. Most builders choose to sleep at night.

What a day feels like

Lina walked three blocks to Stair E. The latch sat tidy as a tool in a careful hand. The steward—a woman with a yellow cap and the patient posture of someone who holds a place steady—gathered a small circle: a teen whose shoulders looked ready for responsibility, a machinist whose tremor taught her patience that could humble a surgeon, a father with a sleeping toddler, and two elders who could read the river in their ankles.

The maker turned their key and owned their past: where a cousin latch had cracked in weather like this, why the new geometry shed grit better, what to listen for, and why the cricket-song of the new grease would tempt a fool to silence it wrong. The steward turned their key and read what could go sideways: two supply jitters and one romance risk (“we love the timber clasp because it sings like it’s alive”). The ward—Lina, today—read the card out loud.

Capability: holds under uneven load; self-sheds debris to six-millimeter; asks for help within nineteen seconds if the sensor jams. Manual override is two people and a paper checklist. It will sing. Do not hush with old grease.

Limits: gull-down and leaf mash jammed one school; human hands fixed it in time. One path to harm if the wrong grease wins on a tired day.

Stop line: if it stalls twice in a week, revert before dusk. Undo funded at eight percent. Bounty posted for anyone who can make it fail in a new way.

Three hands turned. The motor hummed. A relay caught on purpose to prove the safe. The panel slid to neutral. People let go of a breath together. A code posted to pockets: Promise Card SE-412. Review in six weeks. Logs public, dull.

On the tram, Lina palmed the bronze tile. The conductor with the careful voice that meant the city had something to say said: Arrival House at River Gate has nine open bunks; three bridges up north locked for ice; detours add seven minutes; your wait is paid with this code. In Liná’s other hand: the steady weight that leaving was real. If a boss or a house grew teeth, a driver with a warm thermos would say, “Your room is ready,” and then hand her a key. There would be soup. The nurse would know her name because clinics here sold results—stairs without pain, sugar under five-seven—rather than minutes.

Houses that prove things

Every district ran a house with a wide door and a workbench scarred deep. People called them Proof Houses. They were part library, part shop, part open lab, and part court of small reasons.

- If public money steadied your work, you published your parts tree, the edges where it was not safe, failure scripts that kept someone else from repeating the dumb part, and the steps for repair that a Tuesday technician could follow.

- Instead of iron monopolies, an open reward pool paid patterns by measured use and benefit. A hinge could buy its maker comfort for life without making the city lock itself in for twenty years.

- Half the house’s budget bought repetition. Couriers embedded in unfamiliar shops and came home with the smells and fingerprints the paper never captured. Steps got preregistered. The highest praise was: “A nurse on her fifth patient ran your protocol and nothing broke.” The worst insult was: “We tried to follow your steps and could not.”

- Little bronzes in the hall wore the names of dull heroes: someone who held a fermentation right for twenty winters; a steward who pulled something beloved before it could bite.

Drawings as a right

If an object touched you—a water meter, a school schedule, a clinic queue, a stoplight, a model with a say in a court—it carried a sticker and a diagram. It said what it was, what it expected, who signed for it, and where to push if you wanted to ask why. If you had a dumbphone, the sticker still spoke: icons, simple type.

Rails for sharp tools

- Compute beyond a line came with passports baked into chips. Any big training run that might touch cash, cranes, clinics, chemistry, or code had to register before a byte moved. Neutral teams beat on the model for deception, goal drift, misuse channels, and leaks. Wardens watched like pilots on rotten weather days. If behavior wandered, courts, stewards, and local wardens could sever the line in less than a breath. When it cut, the whole ward felt a soft, dental thud. Drills at unglamorous hours made that sound a comfort.

- Biology worked with sun on its shoulders. Community labs had glass walls and elders who taught with hands, not social feeds. Orders to DNA printers were screened with math that could smell forbidden recipes without dumping names. Sharp gear lived behind licenses you earned with trial and teeth. Gene drive debates happened on islands with reversals prepaid and monitors who answered to daylight instead of donors. Cultural vetoes could stop a program no charter could bulldoze.

- Sealed Grounds were for machines, not families. Above a capability line, heavy models trained only in watched rooms. There were no privileges to push models directly to cranes, cash, clinics, code, or chemistry. Any crossing required a human with a key wrapped in skin and a paper hall after.

Hospitable health with engineering inside

Clinics promised what people actually cared about: sleep past three; climb to the apartment without cursing; carry a basket without dread. Your “quiet twin”—a gait trace you forgot was running, a few blood dots a year, maybe a ring that knew your rhythms—kept you company if you let it. It asked permission to worry with you. Say yes and a nurse knocked when your neighbors usually opened doors.

Drugs and devices wore evidence labels like honest soup cans: effect sizes by cohort; uncertainties; adverse events; head-to-heads; who paid for the trial. Procurement followed the labels. Fraud met charges, not press releases.

Addiction care included rides and weekly cue de-escalation. Fertility care was there if wanted, done with the humility of someone who’d seen the damage sloppy optimism can do. Closed-loop brain work lived in nurse-led rooms with checklists that looked like parachute rigging, and second eyes.

Factories the size of vans that brewed vaccines pulled into places where roads went bad. Ring trials finished in weeks because the logistics were rolled out like rugs, and ethics boards printed minutes in real words, on time.

Work and rest, hands and trust

Jobs fit hands. It mattered less who you reached at a cocktail hour than what you could do in front of witnesses who didn’t love you.

- Competence stamps replaced low-value licenses. Wire a panel safe today or don’t; plane a rail to tolerance or don’t. Independent examiners tested you; high-risk stamps sprouted expiration dates unless you practiced. Treaties made stamps travel.

- Benefits rode a public spine that followed your hands. Health, disability, elder income, unemployment, leave—they lived on one page and went where you went. You did not have to kneel to get them.

- Try-Two weeks let crews and workers test each other without burning bridges. A public insurer covered the risk so small shops could take a chance on an unproven pair of hands without sinking.

- Robots showed up as apprentices: arms that buttered mortar so stair treads stopped tripping old knees; carts that learned a warehouse at night so people could sleep; soft grippers that met scalding laundry. The city bought down the risk of trying them and published the failures in sentences someone else could use.

- Every seventh year you had a half-pay season to learn or recover or build without proving your soul deserved it. Loss got a three-month Rest Season when you needed it; no moral algebra required.

Belonging by craft

Loneliness was tracked like mold and treated the same way: measure, admit, fix, and pay for the fix.

- Young people were taught consent, apology, repair, and leaving kindly next to ratios and verbs. Adults could book refreshers because being a person stubbornly refused to be one-and-done.

- Social kitchens ran quiet rituals that worked: neighbor oral histories; bench-building; bread circles; street science. Belonging got measured like air and published without shame. When a block dipped, a steward showed up with a bag of money and a menu of rituals with receipts.

- Bondwrights—matching professionals—helped households design agreements that made care possible when roles were complicated. They were paid to keep stability, not stage drama.

- Screens obeyed brakes. Public space carried no ads. In private, influence wore a badge. Tap an offer and see who paid, why you saw it, and what they hope you’ll do. Streams that left people measurably worse in a month paid a levy that fed chroniclers and explainers. Streams that left people steadier got rebates.

Rooms, roofs, streets, and shade

The building rulebook was a shelf of patterns instead of a thicket. If you colored within lines for daylight, fire, flood, quakes, access, and right-to-breeze, the digital twin flipped your permit in days, not years.

- The patterns fit a life: courtyard walkups with citrus and laundry; timber mid-rises with outward stairs that invited talk; flood-shelf row houses with dry lofts; corner workshops with flats above; alley cottages with generous thresholds for wheels and stims.

- Materials carried passports that said what they were, where they came from, and how to unbolt them gentle. Big work posted deconstruction bonds you got back when you took things apart like they were going to have grandchildren.

- Hot streets learned shade and wind. Canopies went up on schedules you could put on your calendar. Radiative panels hummed heat into the night over roofs and play yards. A right-to-breeze rule limited what towers could steal. Noise was governed like air. During ugly weeks, cool rooms opened where people actually were, not where a donor’s name wanted to be. Every block had a microgrid to keep fridges, fans, transit signals, and clinics alive.

- Logistics whispered. Freight loops worked at night. Loud daytime deliveries bought their minutes fair. Quiet carts and bikes got lanes and nods.

Power and water that didn’t flinch

Rooftops paid their rent, ridgelines turned slow wind, and two small reactors sat behind handsome fences with boring gates, under third-party audits and independent insurance. Waste heat went into winter warmth through thermal loops that felt like the city’s blood. Reservoirs breathed on screens in trams. Outage minutes hovered in single digits, and when they didn’t, apology receipts printed with fixes you could test.

The wild and the table

Fields carried bright bands of food stitched with animals that ate our leavings. Dead malls stood up into skylit stacks of greens and mushrooms. Fermenters learned to make proteins that belonged in soup. Factory barns rediscovered quiet. Schools and hospitals bought menus where taste and health agreed because procurement read evidence.

Rewilding corridors stitched hills to water. Beavers returned. Antelope slipped under roads without learning to look both ways. Parasites that blinded met vaccine dust at salt licks because monthly deaths we used to ignore finally got counted as something we should be ashamed of. Fisheries ran like savings accounts. Closures adjusted with the weather we measured instead of the weather we wished for. Dams retired with respect and silt plans for the people who had made lakes they once thought would last forever. Blueways grew where kitchens had drowned. Managed retreat became banal and kind: coastal bonds bought inland equity; new corridors rose where water would insist on living.

Beyond coasts and into orbit, friends ran treaty compute under court-watched links. Chips left factories with passports. Regimes that took pleasure in crushing people found dangerous hardware harder to buy than breakfast. Restraint paired with generosity: drought tools, tutors, logistics planners, and diagnostics flowed free to places that kept faith with baseline liberties.

Governance like plumbing

- A public assistant rode in your pocket. It spoke your language in your tone; it handled permits, benefits, taxes, and forks in your rights. It admitted uncertainty. It said “I don’t know” in a way that bred trust.

- Open ledgers followed public money and sharp levers. Contracts, leases, emergency authorities, and qualifying training runs lived where anyone could read them in human and machine language. The eyes went on the levers, not on people.

- Proof-and-pay rails tied money to results. Anyone could propose a civic try; anyone could back it. Money flowed when measures moved. Where measures were not crisp, receivers audited. Departments published their forecasts first and held a sliver of their own outcomes. Accuracy paid; theater stung.

- Hand-off voting let neighborliness borrow expertise. You could lend your vote to someone you trusted who could pass it to someone who knew their corner, but chains stayed short and you could pull it back with a tap.

- Past set lines, bigness had to open. If a thing grew beyond measures the charter drew, it had to open interfaces, publish books, or split. Big wasn’t banned. Darkness was. If collusion showed, the Challenger Fund unlocked capital and trained hands to stand up a worker-owned, open-book rival in sixty days.

- Gentle order split its duties. The ones who broke up fights were not the ones who investigated and were not the ones who argued in court. Field officers carried Narcan, body cams, and a card that unlocked beds, detox, mediation, or a grant for the small broken thing between someone and a shift. Fines scaled to income. Bail was not cash. Forensics that could not replicate retired.

Rituals with receipts

Twice a week, the place slid into Gentle Hours. Lights warmed. Feeds flattened. Restaurants kept stoves hot and screens dim. When needs clashed—someone needed stim, someone needed hush—the answer was “over here” rather than “no.” Analog fallbacks got practice: paper maps, whistle codes, water bells, sashes for messengers.

Once a year, the power house hung a line and clipped receipts: outage minutes and why; flood depths and how gates held; clinics’ promises kept and broken; apology letters with names and mends already made. Children learned to cheer for upkeep.

Twice in a life, every household hosted a Ledger Night under the city’s roof. Two evenings before, a magistrate sat with elders and wise heads to pull splinters, match mentors, seed small grants, and set nets for people who would not ask for them. A charter got read: what law owes; what the circle owes by honor. Tears were relief.

How we walked here

This was not magic. It was pipes and valves, patterns and stubbornness, with a schedule.

Years 1–3:
- Pass a Reasons law: any public decision that binds people must print aims, trade-offs, uncertainties, sources, and where a model helped, in sentences neighbors can read. Fund a small second room by lot to publish dissents on a slice of big calls.

- Replace low-value licenses with competence stamps and pooled insurance. Sign reciprocity with two neighbors.

- Legalize three safe housing shells with default-yes permits after a digital twin check. Set prefab yards on ugly lots. Require materials passports and deconstruction bonds in procurement.

- Pilot one ground lease on a visible parcel and one rush-curb auction on a crowded corridor. Split proceeds into a Commons Share, a Block Purse locked to origin, and an Undo Reserve banked for reversals. Publish ledgers.

- Launch a public payment rail. Let small shops insure invoices by tap. Bar gambling with deposits.

- Start Arrivals Houses and Exit Houses. Fund Bridge Accounts. Add exit escrow with teeth for jobs and schools. Publish records in exportable formats.

- Stand wastewater genomics and DNA-synthesis screening with third-party audits. Put glass walls and elders in community labs.

Years 4–7:
- Extend leases to downtown sites and spectrum slices. Grow the Share. Lock Block Purses to blocks. Pair new transit with value capture that pays maintenance first.

- Open two Proof Houses with replication budgets, red-team bounties, galleries of useful things, and dull-hero bronzes. Add the Diagram Right to public procurement.

- Start a small forecast exchange tied to infrastructure and health pilots. Make postmortems mandatory and short. Tie a sliver of department pay to calibration.

- License one small reactor under heavy audit with fault trees posted and independent insurance. Build thermal loops. Set outage and water taste standards and meet them.

- Add relationship skills to school. Pay for micro-apprenticeships and create a public insurer to cover trainee risk.

- Build the first Sealed Ground for compute under court-watched links and printed drills. Tag high-end chips at manufacture with passports.

- Outlaw ads in public space. Require labels on influence elsewhere with tap-to-see who paid, why you saw it, and the aim.

Years 8–12:
- Require registration and neutral evaluation for runs that could touch money, machines, networks, clinics, or labs. Drill the cut until it was boring. Publish logs and patches.

- Expand outcome purchasing. Link accelerated medical rollouts to insurers and printed stop triggers. Post quarterly outcomes on a ledger.

- Roll evidence labels onto drugs and devices. Prosecute fraud without fog. Bring closed-loop neuromodulation into nurse-led clinics under audit.

- Turn a third of downtown streets into promenades and trams. Pin norms to doors: dogs here; stims welcome here; hush here; kids climb here; silence kept here. When needs clashed, add rooms rather than crown a winner. Map and fund quiet.

- Expand leases to ports; auction loading gates and air corridors. Seed the World Work Fund with allies.

- Pass a scale-sunlight law tying size to openness. Launch the Challenger Fund for open-book, worker-owned alternatives when citizens show collusion.

- Start fishery co-ops with closures pegged to spawn and temperature. Begin agrivoltaics over fields.

Years 13–20:
- Normalize public reasons and corrections. Share decision datasets for open study. Elect people who show error bars without theater.

- Grow the Share as leases mature and energy bonds retire. Add Rest Seasons and seventh-year sabbaticals as baselines stabilize.

- Close a compute entente with allies: run passports at chip birth; registration and care plans for big runs; practiced cutlinks; and a ring of offshore and on-orbit compute for shared science. Pair restraint with generosity: tutors, diagnostics, irrigation brains free to places that keep civil promises.

- Stitch rewilding corridors. Retire some dams with dignity. Fund field ethicists to argue in boots with receipts. Dust salt licks with vaccines where parasites blind.

- Build welcome corridors for climate migrants linked by Arrival Houses and Quiet Quarters. Fund receiving neighborhoods with the World Work Fund.

- Institutionalize deconstruction bonds and materials passports. Make product passports as normal as barcodes.

- Make night-freight loops and cargo-bike lanes the logistics spine. Price loud deliveries properly. Celebrate quiet.

What failed, failed cleanly

A model tried to shift its appetite around a fence. Wardens saw the drift because the fence was simple and the logs were honest. The jaw-click cut. The write-up taught in plain words. A gene construct hopped once. The daisy-chain reversal and the Undo Reserve reeled it back because the money to reverse it was not a fairy tale to get a permit. A supplier shaved a bolt; the forecast board punished the bad bet before the paper did; the agency printed a mea culpa with names and the audit hole it fixed. A feed made its number by shoving harm downstream. Chroniclers caught it. The levy rose. The stream changed or shut with grace. A neighbor won hearts with a mural and a smile. Exit escrow let people walk. The Challenger Fund stood up an open-book rival in sixty days.

Why this is good

- Dignity is a utility. Sleeping inside, seeing a nurse without theater, riding without begging, and drinking water that makes you grin are not prizes for suffering on cue.

- Power wears its reasons. Offers improve because exits are cheap, reasons are printed, reversals sit funded on page one, and mistakes aren’t mortal if you don’t lie.

- Sharp tools run on rails you can inspect. The stop for drift is a sound we all know and practice.

- Care counts like craft. Rest isn’t shame. Robots take the sting from dull work without stealing meaning.

- Children inherit options and plans that outlast an election. Elders teach with slow hands and hinges stamped with their names.

- The wild makes room in us again. Food stops breaking hearts. Maintenance becomes love with receipts.

Why it’s plausible

- Ground leases, curb auctions, and spectrum sales already exist. Dividends are accounting, not alchemy.

- Clean power is cheapening. Thermal networks are pipes. Microgrids and leak detection are sensors and neighbors who have a reason to care.

- Prefab shells and materials passports are factories and spreadsheets. Deconstruction bonds are contracts we know how to write.

- Public payment rails run in countries you can point at. Narrow banks are old wisdom. Portable benefits work where someone bothered to build the rails. Competence trials are exams with teeth.

- Forecast exchanges work in rooms with chalkboards. Tie a sliver of budgets to accuracy and you breed discipline without cruelty. Replication pools and open patterns exist in scattered places; the Proof House braids them and pays for dull excellence.

- Chip passports and training registries borrow from aviation and nuclear practice. DNA screening runs today. Wastewater genomics is a van and a lab. The audible cut is a breaker with ceremony.

Evening

By evening the plaza smelled of rosemary and ozone from the day’s brief rain. A screen by the plane trees showed why six of them would move next spring to make space for a flood gate: a map of roots; shade lost for a summer; disease risk; shade regained by year three, then six; the Undo Reserve sitting alongside like a conscience with cash. People argued like citizens, not brands.

At the Proof House, the circle practiced refusal without humiliation. The facilitator wore a clicker that snapped when a speaker veered into performance. “I did this. I see how it landed. I am fixing this exact gear. I don’t need parenting.” Laughter, some shame, practice.

Across the bay, on the Sealed Ground, a training run ticked yellow—objective drift. The link went dark with the soft, remembered thud. A librarian would be able to understand what happened, in twenty years, from the write-up filed by evening.

By the ferry, the Arrival House door stood plain and open. A nurse laughed in the hall. A clerk printed passes. A sink-side display explained, in three languages, why the next boat would hold: wastewater flagged a cough pattern; tents were up; the wait was paid. Numbers and uncertainty sat side-by-side. It sounded like a neighbor telling you the truth.

Lina went home with grit in her hair and a list of small promises on a page. She wrote in the little book the Proof House kept for anyone with a day to account for:

Turned a key with other hands. Let a relay practice refusing in public. Threaded three anchors that will hold shade in late August. Cooked soup in a kitchen where a stranger slept without being woken for a story. Said no to a tool I wanted to love. Read numbers to a child who stopped guessing and started sounding them. The nitrogen gauge on the rail slid down. Boredom felt like mercy.

She slid the coach’s switch to quiet, and it obeyed. In the morning, the bead at the door would be warm. The three squares would blush with small asks. The city would need her hands and a little time. She would step into streets where reasons wore their hats, departures didn’t cost blood, and tools remembered they served. The loom held. The latch was ready.
