The Plenty Compact: A Utopia Built from Abundance and Attention

We did not reach a good future by becoming angels. We reached it by getting the incentives right, then building social technology that turned raw abundance into durable flourishing.

Two ideas unlocked the door. First, that the biggest risks of conflict emerge in worlds where resources feel fixed. Second, that human thriving depends as much on the quality of our relationships and institutions as on the quality of our gadgets. Once these ideas were widely understood—and not just by experts, but by mayors, teenagers, ministers of finance, and film directors—the path forward became visible. We set out to make resources grow fast enough to change the game, and to organize ourselves to turn “more” into “better.”

The result is the Plenty Compact: a way of life in which most of what we want is not scarce, most of what remains scarce is not zero‑sum, and most of what is still zero‑sum is channeled into games people consent to play.

How we got here

In the early 2030s, it became obvious that the largest economic surges in history would be driven by AI-enabled automation, synthetic biology, and offworld industry. On an ordinary growth curve, the temptation to fight for advantage would have been irresistible. But growth expectations were no longer ordinary: credible projections showed that within a single strategic planning window, total human productive power could expand by orders of magnitude. This wasn’t wishful thinking—it followed from concrete pipelines: fully automated factories that built more factories; software that wrote better software; orbital photovoltaics delivering continuous power; autonomous mining and refining; precise gene-editing platforms bringing down the costs of health and materials.

This changed the bargaining landscape. When actors genuinely expect the pie to grow a thousandfold, the difference between a large slice and a slightly larger slice becomes trivial compared to the value of keeping the ovens running and not burning down the kitchen. That shift—amplified by sobering risk models showing how “take it all” strategies end in catastrophic conflict—made space for a set of institutions that coordinated self-interest without demanding moral heroism.

The early slate of institutions looked like this:

- Windfall trusts: Jurisdictions controlling advanced compute and critical biomanufacturing licenses adopted “windfall clauses” that automatically diverted a fixed fraction of extraordinary gains (defined by pre-agreed thresholds of capability or profitability) into a sovereign global trust. The trust paid universal dividends and funded planetary public goods. Because these clauses attached to access to licensed chips, fabs, and markets, enforcement was realistic.

- Defensive stability pacts: Rival blocs negotiated verifiable limits on offensive capabilities that most threatened first-strike instability (e.g., covert autonomous biomanufacturing, untraceable fleet swarms). Verification combined on-site inspection, cryptographic attestation from secure hardware, and continuous sensing from shared sky-and-sea monitoring. Violations were costly because defection jeopardized access to windfall platforms.

- Open blue commons: Non-rivalrous knowledge—software, scientific data, designs—was published by default to a shared commons under licenses that allowed free personal and non-profit use while requiring revenue-sharing for for-profit deployment. This shifted innovation races from hoarding to building reputation and claim on the windfall pool.

- Frontier dividends and exit rights: A portion of new abundance was paid out as unconditional income, reducing zero-sum labor competition and enabling people to leave coercive arrangements. Exit rights included relocation insurance and legal aid across allied jurisdictions, lowering the cost of saying “no.”

- Peace via plenty doctrine: Defense white papers began describing the strategic objective not as relative supremacy but as locking in credible access to a fast-growing base of shared resources. This reframing turned investment toward “growth multipliers” (energy, materials, automation) and “risk dampers” (safety research, monitoring, rapid response), while deprioritizing weapons systems that made everyone nervous.

Within a decade, the doctrine was vindicated: more actors earned more by cooperating than by grabbing. Conflicts still flared, but the expensive, identity-binding fights lost their financial sponsors because sponsors had better ways to win.

Abundance on the ground

Abundance is a word that asks to be made concrete. Here is a day in the life of a typical person under the Plenty Compact.

You wake to light that has learned your preferences over years—pink at the edges, quiet warmth without glare. Your home adjusts for your sleep and metabolism, not because you asked for gadgets that do this, but because the building joined the cooperative that updates its own firmware from a trusted commons. Your food is delicious because the bioreactors in the basement co-op grow proteins that match the textures you love. The cost is low and falling; the subscription is bundled into your housing, which is cheaper than your grandparents’ rent ever was because materials are abundant and energy is near‑free.

Work looks different. On your wall, your Mission Board lists the projects you care about: restoring a salt marsh nearby, training new mediators in your covenant, contributing to an open photonics toolkit. You’re paid a base dividend from the windfall trust. You also receive mission bounties funded by municipal and global pools that match citizen preferences and public needs. When you do work for a for-profit venture, your open contributions confer “claim-credit”: a small, durable revenue share, tracked on public ledgers that everyone can audit but no one can rewrite.

Your personal AI helps you plan your day, but it is not your boss. It is bound by your “preference constitution”—a living document you trained with support from counselors—which encodes your deeper values and hard consent boundaries. Changing that constitution requires waiting periods and cross-checks from future-you letters you left yourself. Your AI can simulate how choices ripple through your relationships and goals; when you are tempted to do something you’ve told yourself you would regret, it asks you to talk it through with a human.

Loneliness is rare, not because people are forced into togetherness, but because we built infrastructure for belonging.

- Relationship studios exist on most blocks: places where people learn the practical arts of friendship, parenting, intimacy, and conflict repair. Attendance is normal and subsidized, like vaccinations once were. The vibe is not clinical; it’s more like music lessons for connection.

- Mutual family networks offer modern kinship. Groups of 15 to 30 households covenant to be “alloparents” and “allograndparents” to each other’s children, with schedules and budgets visible to members. The networks are voluntary, opt‑in, and easy to leave; they provide redundancy and continuity, so no one is ever raising children—or aging—alone unless they want to.

- Conversation plazas—public squares sheltered from weather and noise—are designed the way old cathedrals were, for awe and gathering. People spend time there because they are delightful: adaptive acoustic canopies, low-cost coffee, gardens scaled for human senses, and interludes of street performance drawn from a universal talent exchange.

We took status seriously. Humans compete; the trick is to make competition safe and meaningful without letting it poison everything else.

- Challenge arenas exist for high-intensity pursuits—strategy, athletics, speed‑building, art. They are consensual spaces with clear rules and trained referees; they satisfy the itch to win without mortgaging your life to an arms race.

- Recognition is many‑dimensional by design. Communities maintain “honor rotations” that cyclically spotlight caretaking, mentoring, craft, discovery, humor, and grace under pressure. These rotations aren’t empty theater; they are linked to real awards like extra sabbatical time or priority use of studios.

- Most rankings are private by default. Your AI can show you if you are improving at the things you said you care about. Public leaderboards exist, but they are opt‑in and contextual.

Health, physical and mental, is the first dividend of abundance. We learned to treat the nervous system like a partner, not an enemy to be subdued.

- Continuity care teams pair human clinicians with models fine‑tuned on population‑scale data and your own physiological patterns. Diagnostics are continuous and noninvasive; your wristband can detect the earliest signatures of infection, inflammation, or mood shifts. Interventions are gentler because they are early: light exposure, nutrition, neural entrainment, targeted microdosing when needed.

- Deep suffering—the howling kinds that made so many lives intolerable—has become rare. Not because we sedated it away, but because we changed upstream conditions: poverty and isolation were reduced; trauma was treated; addiction was met with massive support and nonjudgmental care; and when chemistry was needed, we used it with consent, context, and long‑term follow‑up.

- Aging slowed. Not to mythic immortality, but to a long, healthy runway: centenarian vitality is common, and many plan their lives in arcs. People choose sabbaticals for radical reinvention—learning a new craft, moving to a different covenant, living a quiet season. Your great‑grandmother’s best friend from school may reappear in your city after fifty years, and it is normal and joyous.

We did not “connect our minds” into a hive. We did, however, widen the pipes between them where that made sense. High‑bandwidth interfaces enable shared attention within consented windows. Couples sometimes share a “thinking loop” while cooking; research teams enter synchronized states during discovery sprints. These are not permanent mergers; they are like chamber music, where distinct instruments briefly become one sound.

Education is play with purpose.

- Every child has a constellation of mentors: humans and AIs that know how to make numbers, language, and the natural world irresistibly interesting. Field trips include hillside wind farms and safari parks rewilded after protein fermentation made ranching obsolete. Children spend serious time outdoors and serious time tinkering indoors.

- Adolescents learn “civic kung fu,” the moves that make communities work: budgeting, mediation, safety protocols, ecological literacy, how to test a claim. Before graduation, most have served on a mini‑council, shipped a public good, and changed their minds about something important.

- Adults learn in cycles. When you take a year to study ceramics or Cambodian, society expects it, budgets for it, and applauds it. Boredom is a sign to rotate, not a sentence to serve.

Economics without a zero-sum spine

Abundance did not eliminate markets; it changed what they are good for. Markets now excel at rapid coordination around variety and taste, while basics are handled by cheaper, steadier systems.

- Baseline goods—health care, housing standards, clean water, energy, staple foods, connectivity—are rights and are met through a mix of universal provision and vouchers. Because energy and materials are cheap, providing these at high quality is affordable.

- Markets shine in curation and expression: restaurants, fashion, personal habitats, exploratory tech. Starting something new is easy because infrastructure is commoditized and modular; the cost of failure is low; the upside of success is still meaningful but muted by the windfall sharebacks.

- The Blue Commons, a global shared library of designs, code, models, and data, is a basic layer like air. You can take from it without limit for non-profit personal and community use. To profit from it commercially, you agree to a small automatic tithe to the commons. Lawyers once called this impossible; they were wrong because the levers of compute, cloud platforms, and distribution were aligned with enforcing the rules.

- The Grey Finance layer (public-private partnerships) directs enormous patient capital to infrastructure with public spillovers: carbon drawdown, biodiversity corridors, water systems, earthquake retrofits, space tethers, and orbital habitats. Returns are capped; the point is smooth, predictable funding and global benefits.

Security without paranoia

The Plenty Compact made security boring in the best way. The gravest risks—rogue capabilities, runaway weapons races—are handled jointly. This feels strange to people who grew up with rivalry, but it works because the mechanisms of trust are technical as well as diplomatic.

- Shared sensing: The skies and seas are instrumented by a publicly governed network of sensors. The raw feeds are encrypted and split among custodians. Analytics are open; whistleblowing tools are powerful. If a fleet lifts off from some dark harbor, everyone sees it.

- Compute attestation: Advanced chips are sealed with hardware that can prove, without revealing secrets, what models are running and with what constraints. Facilities operating at the frontier publish daily attestations; failure to publish locks them out of premium energy pricing and international markets.

- Bioguard rails: Biomanufacturing above specific capacity requires registry, monitoring, and layered physical and organizational safety. Community biology thrives under safe guidelines; the gates close only where they must.

- Dispute scaffolding: Conflicts within and among covenants escalate through well-known ladders: mediation, restorative circles, adjudication, arbitration, and finally, if necessary, defensive intervention. No one pretends coercion has vanished; instead, its triggers and consequences are so predictable that its use is rare.

Pluralism, with real exit and real dignity

Not everyone wants the same life. Under the Compact, you live in a covenant polity: a voluntary association (geographic or virtual-physical) with declared norms. Examples include quiet monastic communes, loud art collectives, science‑first mini‑cities, low‑tech agrarian towns, and hybrid mosaics. Two principles keep this pluralism from curdling into oppression.

- Strong exit rights: Any adult can leave a covenant, taking their portable identity, savings, access to the Blue Commons, and their dividend. Neutral agencies provide relocation help and temporary housing. Children’s moves are reviewed by cross‑covenant child advocates.

- Transparency of norms: Every covenant publishes a comprehensible “rules of life,” with red lines, grievance processes, audit trails, and history of how it has handled dissent. Your AI can model likely fit, show you daily rhythms, and flag where your preference constitution would clash.

Nature and more-than-human life

Animal suffering from industrial farming dwindled swiftly once fermentation and cell-cultured proteins became cheaper and better than slaughtered meat. We preserved heritage farming as culture, not as cruelty. Wild places recovered, not to an untouched myth, but to resilient biodiversity corridors managed with humility.

- Rewilding levees replaced old floodwalls so rivers could breathe again. Species moved along shaded highways across continents, guided by ecological models that improved with every season.

- Invasive species management and humane population control used genetic tools conservatively, with sunset clauses, international oversight, and local consent. We did not try to tune entire ecosystems to human taste; we learned to stabilize and protect.

- More-than-human intelligences—dolphins, octopuses, corvids—were given protected domains and channels of contact designed on their terms. The goal wasn’t to make them like us, but to respect that they are who they are.

Suffering in non‑biological minds was never permitted. If a system could plausibly have experiences, its designers had to prove either that those experiences were not painful or that the system could opt out. This was non‑negotiable.

Art, adventure, and the long now

Culture is thick. We invented new ways to play and make meaning.

- Epochal games are cooperatively authored adventures that can last months, with dance, theater, puzzles, sports, music, and science woven together. They culminate in local and global festivals where communities tell their stories with light and movement, then archive them in “memory orchards”—gardens whose plants and paths encode collective histories and personal milestones.

- Aesthletics—the blending of athletic rigor and artistic expression—became a primary school of life. One day you learn capoeira calligraphy. Another, you slow‑cook a poem on a potter’s wheel. These are not mere hobbies; they are taken as seriously as factory work once was.

- Spiritual life shed compulsory belief and found energy in practiced attention. Silent retreats are normal. Secular monasteries teach contemplation like a craft. Some people experience mind states once called mystical; the society does not demand that others follow them there.

- Great projects exist for those who crave scale. Restoring a sea. Building a city on the steppe. Tying a space elevator ribbon skyward. Seeding a ring of orbital parks where gravity is light and children can learn to fly in greenhouses backlit by Earth. Participation is elective; you can spend a century on one, take a decade in a quiet valley, then return.

Guardrails that make freedom durable

We kept the best of human unruliness, and we tamed what would have destroyed us.

- Anti‑wireheading norms are encoded into platforms. Systems that do nothing but pump reward without relation to chosen goals are considered defective. Getting lost in pleasure is permitted; redesigning your motivation to care about nothing else triggers deliberate friction: waiting periods, counselor check‑ins, feedback from future‑you letters.

- Right to cognitive opacity: You can decline to share your thoughts. Brain‑computer interfaces are opt‑in and reversible. High‑bandwidth links require pre‑session consent rituals and leave hashed audit trails that only you can open later.

- Change with continuity: Deep self‑modification is staged. You rehearse in safe “near‑you” simulations, consult with people who made similar changes years ago, and keep a key that lets you step back within a time window if you and your future self agree. Society glamorizes not constant self-hacking but skillful self‑authorship.

- Everyone gets a time advocate. At least once a year, you sit with a person whose only job is to represent your interests across time: the child you were, the elder you will be, the friends and future children who will live with your choices. This is not bureaucratic; it is intimate. It keeps us connected to who we are.

Plausible steps from today

If this feels too far, consider the pieces already on the table:

- Cheap energy is arriving through the global roll‑out of solar, wind, storage, and next‑generation fission, with fusion moving from lab to pilot. Support policies can lock in learning curves and grid integration.

- Alternative proteins and precision fermentation are scaling. Public procurement (schools, hospitals, militaries) can swing markets away from industrial animal suffering within a decade.

- AI tutors and care copilots are in their infancy and could deliver major improvements in education and mental health if regulated with clear safety standards and funding for public models.

- Dividend mechanisms already exist in Alaska and Norway. A global windfall trust can start among a coalition of willing jurisdictions with large compute and market access, then grow by attraction.

- Compute attestation and safety licensing are tractable: require that frontier models be trained and deployed on sealed systems capable of verifiable reporting; tie access to subsidies and market privileges.

- Relationship studios can be piloted by cities as part of public health. Mutual family networks can be seeded by nonprofits with light legal scaffolding and microgrants.

- Open commons can be funded like open science, with licensing that ensures commercial beneficiaries contribute back. Governments can require that publicly funded code ships to the commons.

- Sensor networks for shared monitoring can begin with ocean and airspace. International consortia can build and govern them like we govern satellite navigation: as neutral infrastructure.

- Exit rights can be embedded in domestic policy—mobility grants, rental guarantees, legal aid—and expanded through regional agreements.

None of this requires genius. It requires choosing abundance as a lever for peace, then building platforms and norms that turn abundance into better lives.

What we did better than our ancestors

Past visions of a good life often failed for predictable reasons: they centralized too much power, demanded ideological unanimity, ignored incentives, or treated people as cogs in a lovely machine. We learned from that.

- We aligned greed with greatness by making it more profitable to grow and share than to capture and hoard.

- We designed for grounded pluralism: difference without domination, autonomy without anomie.

- We invested as much in care infrastructure as in factories—relationship studios as critical infrastructure, like roads and clinics.

- We made it easy to be good and hard to do irreversible harm, using technical guardrails that don’t rely on constant moral willpower.

- We honored the animal and the spirit. We gave the body beautiful food and movement; we gave the mind challenges and rest; we gave the soul places to be quiet.

The future remains open

This is not an end state. It is a practice: a way of meeting tomorrow that does not mistake wealth for worth, or difference for danger. New problems will come. We are not done with grief. But we now have deeper buffers against disaster and deeper wells of meaning to draw from.

Children growing up under the Plenty Compact take certain things for granted: that they will never starve, that their home will not be stolen by illness, that they can leave a bad situation without becoming destitute, that their friendships and loves can be tended like gardens with tools everyone knows how to use, that if war threatens, enough powerful people would lose enough by fighting that they will likely find another way.

The door we unlocked was always there. It opens whenever humans expect that the pie will grow more than enough for all, and expect that together we will decide what “enough” is for the parts of life where more is not better. It opens wider when we remember that the best things people say about their lives are almost never about what they owned, but about who showed up for them, what they learned to make together, and where they were brave.

We stepped through. We can keep walking.
