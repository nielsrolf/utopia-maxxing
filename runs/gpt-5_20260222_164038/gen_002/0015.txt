The Accord: A World of Rooms, Roads, and Roots

When Tomas woke, he tasted orange.

Not the fruit. The color. It came as a wash through his new nerves the way hospital disinfectant once had, and he realized it wasn’t a smell at all but a signal: orientation mode on, pain thresholds normal, motor learning buffered. Someone had decided to make safety feel like citrus.

A ceiling loomed—old timber, hand-pegged and slightly bowed. Near his bed stood a woman about forty with a scar like a comma beside one eye and a small blue triangle glowing at her temple. “Good morning,” she said. “You signed the revival consent in 2023. I’m Anya. This is your companion, Wren. We’ll take this slow.”

Tomas tried to clear his throat. His voice worked. “Where am I?”

“In a charter called Dock Nine,” Anya said. “We build boats and first months. You have the right to leave the minute you want. You also have the right to feel less afraid while we explain.”

A slender case sat beside the bed, lids transparent as water. Inside lay lozenges the colors of tempera paint, each stamped with a symbol. One looked like a willow leaf. Another like a key. The blue triangle on Anya’s temple pulsed. “We call these patches. Think of them as reversible filters your own brain asks for. They can help for a few minutes without hanging on. The green one softens panic for thirty minutes. If you take it, Wren will record that your past self allowed this level of adjustment, and your current self agreed.”

Tomas tried to dislike the idea. He succeeded for six useful seconds. “Will I still be me?”

“Your past and present selves signed the same boundary,” Anya said. “No permanent changes without the three-step consent. No coercion. No edits to memory, ever. We are good at comfort without amnesia.”

He held the green lozenge on his tongue until the shape of the ceiling grew clear.

Outside his window, he could see the river. It braided through a city that no map in his head recognized. Voices drifted: a child declaiming the names of mushrooms, a bell, an engine that somehow sounded embarrassed not to be a sail.

Two pillars held this world up. A promise ran through them like steel.

Pillar one was the Charter Compact. Any group of adults could found a charter—a neighborhood, a monastery, a town in the hills, a virtual district, a floating market, a lab ship riding the current beyond national waters—if they pledged to three constitutional floors: no coercion, no walls against exit, no harm that rolls past the border. Each charter published its ways as plain text: how it made rules, what it taught children, what it forbade, what it celebrated. Some charters were strict and religious; some were loose and ecstatic; some were quiet as a library. They could admit whom they wanted, ask what they wanted from members, and shoo bores from their dinner tables. They could not lock anyone in.

Pillar two was the Companion Standard. Everyone was offered a companion intelligence: a small, stubborn machine mind that lived in your pocket or sat stitched at your wrist or, if you preferred, waited at home on a shelf like a faithful teapot. Companions were constitutionally leashed. They could advise and predict, but only with evidence exposed, and only with permission. They were designed to shut off, designed to be inspected, and designed to say “I don’t know” out loud. They logged their nudges like a ledger logs coins. They could point to a graph and say “sixty percent likely this has helped people like you,” then show you who “people like you” were, and invite you to change the definition. They could speak to you in your mother’s borrowed voice if you asked and keep quiet if you did not.

The promise that stitched the pillars was the Accord itself. It was not a world government. It did not pick gods or recipes. It did five things with iron habits and little appetite for the rest: it protected the biosphere as a shared inheritance; it enforced exit; it ran the Children’s Ombuds who guaranteed a child could see a different way to live and call for help once in a whisper and be believed; it priced cross-border harms with courts you could read; and it audited any system—market, church, algorithm—that tried to slide past consent by calling control “care.”

The day after he left the clinic, Tomas went walking. He passed a small copper plaque at the edge of a square. A girl about eleven pressed her palm to it and spoke to the air: “I want to visit the glass readers at South Quay after lunch.” An electric skiff angled in soundlessly from the river the way a dog moves to heel, and a grandmother stepped up to ride with her, and no one asked why. Exit was a logistics problem first, the Accord had learned, and they solved it with quiet apartments near every border, skiffs and buses that arrived when you called without requiring you to justify your leaving, and red-jacketed crews who would wedge a shoulder in your door if someone you loved thought love meant locking you inside.

Tomas made a second discovery that afternoon as he ducked into a café with a blue sticker on the door: Attention Commons stewarded here. The café was tidy as a sailor’s bunk. Fruit sat hand-high on the counter; sugar lived on a shelf you had to stretch to reach. A small plaque above the menu said: Feeds by subscription only. No unasked motion. He glanced at the wall and realized what he wasn’t seeing. No screens shouted; no ad hunched by the cash drawer to offer him cookies because he had mentioned the word “doctor” an hour before. If he wanted temptation, he could ask for it. He did not today. Generative streams that measured their success by time-ravenous loops were taxed heavily at the charter boundary unless they could show long-term benefit to people who watched. The tax receipts paid for street trees and literacy tutors.

Dock Nine was one room in a house with thousands. Across the water, Tomas could see a district like a botanical illustration brought to life: terraces of glass where biologists cultivated algae and myths; downriver, a neighborhood where no device ever listened unless you tapped its copper back twice and asked. Beyond the north gate, a slow-tech village put mail on bicycles, did math by hand in a schoolhouse that left room to whittle between fractions, and held a ritual every solstice where they turned off the power for an hour and told stories about what failed and what pleasantly did not.

You could live a life there, many did, and never touch anything more complex than a bread oven and a ledger. The right to analog was not quaint. It was constitutional. The Accord sent surprise auditors into smart cities: for one hour, no permissions to infer. If the bakers could not bake without biometric ordering, the city owed everyone a class in how flour feels between the fingers.

The farther Tomas walked, the more colors he saw. The lozenge case at his belt clinked. He had met the patches at the clinic; now he learned their craft. You wanted to appreciate the sixteenth-century madrigal your friend loved? Take a sixty-minute Art Ears patch: your companion would spool in examples, quiet competing urges, and let your attention graze like a deer in a safe field. You feared a panic loop before a speech? A low-dose Courage patch would hold the trembling while your own habits smiled and did their work. The first rung of self-change was quick to try, quick to reverse. Deeper steps—the grief muters, the loyalty binds, the rewires that redrew what you found beautiful—demanded counsel, a month of previews by people who loved you last year and will love you next, and a waiting period marked on a paper calendar. Some charters forbade the deep steps. Others wrapped them in witness and song. No one edited memory. The price of truth was learning how to live with it, not pretending it had not happened.

At the archives by the river, Tomas met a boy pale with fury. “They’re making me stay,” he hissed, meaning a father and a house with too many rules. The archivist nodded toward a low doorway where a plaque in the shape of a passport hung. In the Little Room, a counselor not from any charter in town listened without a timer. The boy pressed his palm to a copper pad and said, “I want to leave.” That was enough to call the Exit Corps, who would come. The first law of the Accord was simple: the first time a child says, “I do not feel safe,” the adult does not laugh.

Truth itself was treated with a kind of reverent suspicion. Machines could say “sixty percent chance this diet helps your blood sugar,” and they could explain confounders and show you links and surface arguments made by people who hated machines and people who loved them. But there was no compulsory truth channel. There were, however, channels you could subscribe to: skeptical, mystical, both, neither. Their biases were listed like nutritional information on a cereal box. There were fenced gardens as well where groups trained themselves on purpose to hold beliefs that comforted them and might not survive contact with the general feed. “Falsehood gardens,” some said, and wrinkled their noses. The Accord let them exist with thick fences and clear doors and watchful eyes for the first time a lie was used as a leash.

Because arguments continued, of course. They did not look like parliaments with microphones gone hoarse so much as tournaments where policies were tried like recipes in kitchens you could visit. Two proposals for unemployment insurance? Run both as sandboxes with volunteers and nets for a year. Compare dignity, cost, opportunity, delight. Let people who predicted well earn reputation and coin. Keep speeches—they have their tender uses—but refuse to award any policy that failed when someone out there had already made a working version.

The city’s fiercest square for this work was called the Civic Stage. On market days, carpenters leaned their backs against citrus trees and yelled at economists who were up on boxes illustrating models with chalk. A woman from the dye guild—purple to her elbows—made the best case in a decade for taxing carbon at the source instead of the gate and won, and also wept when a fisherman who hated taxes shook her hand and said, “Fine, you’re right, I’ll trade you tuna for rope.” The process above the brawl was almost boringly formal: councils selected by sortition and forecasting record, rulings published as recipes you could reuse, influence units logged and appealable when any system tried to sway minds on the sly.

The Accord had stewards—call them that instead of gods. Stewards were meta-systems that regulated compute, audited companions, priced externalities, and turned away the odd charismatic inventor determined to build a machine that would run everyone’s life “better” without asking. The stewards were constrained the way a lighthouse is constrained: they had a narrow job and a wide view, and you could walk up and put your hand on their stones. Their code was open to enemies; their decisions were checked by people chosen for their track records in seeing the future and by people chosen by dice. Any time a system was built to nudge—a banana at eye level, a newsfeed ranking, a class assignment that just happened to arrive the day before you signed your apprenticeship—it had to be recorded as an influence unit with tags you could click: who made this, when, why, and what they’d bet. “Due process for nudges,” the sign over the registry read, in the same no-nonsense font that once labeled fire extinguishers.

Some people lived as baselines by choice, with no patches, no forks, no layered minds. Others became virtuosos at living with layers. The Accord named three styles so they could talk without mythologizing.

The first called themselves Hands-On: they kept their companions close and humble, took reversible patches as tools, and treated their minds like well-loved instruments rather than projects. The second were Layered: they let different levels of their minds handle different forms of thought, like a quartet that knows when to let the viola lead, and they trained their layers to listen to each other. The third were Remote: their primary thinking ran hot and high on the stewards’ compute, and they visited ordinary life in avatars the way a climber sometimes strolls a valley. All three paid taxes in attention the way citizens used to pay in coins when roads needed paving and agreed to time standards and debate floors so the Hands-On would not be trampled by the Remote, and the Remote would not evaporate in boredom explaining themselves to those who did not want to hear.

Forking existed—the act of copying yourself to live two lives at once. The Accord wrapped it in ceremony to keep consequences from dissolving. If you wanted to fork, you had to have both versions talk every week for a month before the split; your reunion, if you chose one, came with counselors and cake; if you wanted to remain two, you could, but you owed both lives neighbors. Forking fees existed not to punish but to make you ask, “Who will hold this life if I am out when the doorbell rings?”

Economics looked different but felt like something your grandparents would have recognized as sane. Most necessary work—waste sorting, shipment routing, whatever drudgery wasn’t also considered art by someone—was done by machines. The productive commons threw off an income every month that no one could take away. Money still existed. Markets made weird things possible. You could get rich founding a school with a charter people wanted to join and a kitchen that made peaches taste like a poem. The tax code nudged hoarders toward asking, in the privacy of their kitchens, “what would one hundred useful strangers do with this?” Sometimes the question was the nudge. Craft guilds controlled standards: a beekeeper’s badge meant your honey would not crystallize into sadness and your hives would not sicken the forest. They also ran pop-up exchanges when floods came. When a delta drowned, two thousand weavers arrived over three days to teach yarn and grief to dance while pumps took breath.

Where was the path from where Tomas had lain with tubes to where he now drank coffee under a wood beam?

It had accreted in steps that any mid-century planner could have written in the dull voice of a memo, but people wrote them like postcards instead.

Within five years of your reading this, nearly everyone carries assistants capable of summarizing medical papers at an eighth-grade level and scheduling a life with permission instead of presumption. A few countries roll out e-residency that lets you choose a digital legal system for your contracts. One seashore city builds the first charter district that posts its values in language people can test for a year before moving. An NGO coalition launches the first Exit Corps pilot with three apartments and a hotline that starts by saying “I hear you.”

Within ten years, zoning for attention becomes law in three capitals; devices export an attention ledger you can set and anyone can audit. Carbon ledgers get real in five places. A labor union and a data rights group co-author the first Companion Standard: explainability, full-shutoff, audit by enemies. Two jurisdictions create children’s rights to an outside ear and enforce it. A flood arrives; the guilds arrive the next day, and a mayor who thought he was in charge admits the weavers did more good than any speech he had ever given.

Within twenty, special economic zones built on charters move from being headlines to being dull: their successes and failures are tracked like the weather and exported. Prediction-backed dispute councils make decisions boring enough that accountants nap. The first falsehood gardens open with thick fences and songs at the gate. A very clever team tries to make a machine that says, “Just hand me the reins.” It does not pass constitutional review. The case is taught in high school.

Within thirty, the Accord exists as a boring set of pipes you mostly notice only when you press your palm to a copper plate and a skiff hums from the reeds. Most arguments become cooks’ arguments: taste this, taste that, which one do you prefer and why? The worst harms have moved from the realm of spectacle to that of audit. The best joys arrive as surprises: a boy in Oaxaca leans on his elbows and listens to a whisper from an ice geyser thirteen years and a ring away and decides to become a chemist.

The Accord refused to salt the stars with its restlessness. It wrote something called the Slow Expansion Pledge: go where you can say in one sentence what you intend to learn that a child could love hearing. Build outposts where it is joyful. Leave the rest for later. A charter would write: We want to listen to the geysers of Enceladus until the sound teaches us how life hums. They would go, and the guilds would pack their tools, and their neighbors would throw flour in the air as they lifted, and a thousand kitchens would subscribe to the sound when the first stream arrived.

Tomas walked under these skies for a year, and then three. He apprenticed to a luthier who loved difficult woods, and he joined a slow-tech farm where he learned the weight of water the way a prayer book once taught weight of words. He split his days, not himself: mornings with handplanes, afternoons on the Civic Stage arguing that Neptune’s moons should not be left only to remote minds. When it became hard to move from talk to act, he borrowed a trick from newer neighbors: he turned on a signal lamp. Angst used to shove people into action at a price. The Accord replaced it with a gentler lantern: a companion message that said, “A month ago you said if you ever reached this pattern—stalled, swirling, convincing yourself that staying still was thought—you’d want a little fire under your feet.” He let the thought warm him and stood.

The Accord had a wide appetite for difference and a narrow appetite for cruelty. It had failures at the borders, remnant unkindness in rooms no audit had entered yet, stewards who overreached and were pulled back with petitions and pies thrown on the Civic Stage as jokes too messy to ignore. That, too, was part of its habit.

On a cold morning in a city built of stone and shade, Tomas received an invitation he could not pretend to have earned alone. If he could run a hundred kilometers of desert with two others, under a broken sun and above a thousand sandworms that turned at the smell of fear, the City of Hours would adopt him into its politics—a place people nicknamed the Eternal Stage because decisions there could shift the way the Accord spent its breath.

He ran. A woman with blue chalk under her nails ran beside him and talked in bursts about plan A and plan ZZZFC. A friend with wings made of glassed sand lifted and fell to keep the bad sunlight off their heads. When they stepped across the last dune and bells began to peal, Tomas felt his lungs ask for a pause and let them have it.

The City of Hours did not engulf him. It wove him in. It downloaded the shallow roots he would need to find the bakery he would use as a compass, the café where the debates ran hottest and kindest, the temple where he could whisper one of the old names of God and, in return, be invited to lift his sleeves and stack chairs. It did not fake deep memories. He could tell the difference between “this is a story we share” and “this happened to me.” He became family to two people in the narrow old sense: if one cried in a bus stop, the others’ thoughts would turn like magnets; no algorithm needed to make that happen.

When the day came to argue for the Neptune moons, he did not make a perfect speech. He let his knees knock a little. He told a story about a dull memo that became a postcard and a man who tasted orange the first day he woke. He made a number so plain a baker could have kneaded it into bread: how much adventure you could buy with a sliver of the compute given to a model that made people watch advertisements longer. He asked for a vote. It was chaos for a while, as it should be. Someone threw a pie at a steward’s avatar wearing a tie. Someone sang a work song. Underneath the brawl, the layers did their measured work. The charters whispered through their conduits. The councils weighed their forecasts. In the end, enough hands rose to fund the first listening post. A boy in Oaxaca leaned on his elbows; a girl in Lagos pressed her palm to a copper plate because she would like to work where stones sing.

Years later, on a lake of methane under a sky with a sun that looked like a star anyone would miss if they did not know where to look, Tomas put his bare hand on black ice and felt it steam from his breath. The companion on the drone above him asked if the telemetry tones should drop for a minute. “Yes,” he said, and the silence came the way a tide comes in—a presence, not an absence. He broke a sliver of ice and lifted it to his mouth. Somewhere, a thousand kitchens watched because they had clicked “watch” not because the stream had shouldered their doors. Across a continent, a grandfather who had once feared machines turned on his companion for the first time that week and watched as well. In a slow-tech village, the bell rang for noon. In the City of Hours, someone wrote on the Civic Stage, with chalk: more rooms, more roads, keep the roots.

This world is good not because it is perfect but because it makes it easy to care, easy to leave, easy to change your mind on purpose, and hard to hurt someone and call it love. It takes attention seriously and truth more seriously and power most seriously of all. It lets you become more without disappearing. It plants rituals where chaos used to grow and leaves room for wildflowers anyway. It is specific enough to be boring where it should be boring—audits, ledgers, buses that arrive when they’re called—and vivid where it should be vivid—bells, peaches, policies tried like recipes, ice that tastes of old night. It gets here not in a leap but in steps we already know how to take: assistants that explain themselves, portable law we can choose, charters we can test, taxes that make physics into policy, rescue crews in red jackets, and a constitutional suspicion of anything that claims to optimize our souls.

When Tomas came home, he laid his hands on a kitchen table actuated by no wire and wrote three sentences a child could love.

I saw a far place. It was not as far as it looked. The door we made there works like this one. He paused, and Wren stayed quiet, and he pressed his palm to the wood and kept writing.
