The Lattice Commons

Tala set her mug on the sun-warmed rail outside her kitchen. Steam curled into morning air that smelled of wet stone and thyme. The ward bell gave a single polite note for anyone already awake. Her companion—hers by training and temperament, quiet by default—pulsed a soft light on the counter.

Three ways you could help today:
- Co‑sign safety lift for mill intake valve (requires six neighborhood keys).
- Two hours at the Repair Studio: apprenticeship circle on truth‑telling after a fight.
- Orchard guild needs hands to veil the pears before the bats wake.

“Half day,” Tala told the companion. “And keep the late‑night purchase block on. I do not need a fourth gouge.”

Confirmed, said the little voice that never presumed. I’ll stay quiet unless asked.

Outside, two backpack‑sized drones drifted past, ferrying pale ceramic tiles to the school. Across the canal, the ward house screen showed a blue thread of river moving up through town and small green marks where trout would, if they chose, find cooler pockets than last spring. Tala pressed her thumb to the mill request; the counter clicked up one. Then she slid the Repair Studio above the orchard. She had promised herself: learn the tools that keep love from turning brittle, then lend her hands where sweetness grows.

This is an ordinary day in a world held together by five covenants and six crafts.

The Five Covenants

1) A Floor You Don’t Have to Beg For
Light, clean water, staple food, a safe place to sleep, fast connectivity, baseline compute, and basic care arrive like utilities, not prizes. Your share of keeping the place running is paid in time and participation—gardens tended, elders sat with, pipes checked—rather than in obedience to a job that darkens you. A dividend lands in your wallet every month: funded by unromantic tax and by a windfall clause that skims high profits made atop public rails—energy grids, attested chips, open designs—and returns a small, patient share to everyone. Markets still dance. They serve taste, speed, and craft, not survival. Make a better stool, a finer glaze, a more forgiving whetstone, and you can grow wealthy. But no one’s child goes without insulin because you made a chair someone wanted.

2) Proof Before Power
Any system that touches money, bodies, chemicals, vehicles, votes, or borders must prove what it is and what it cannot do before it runs. These are operators, and they never run alone. Three keys are required: the maker’s, a certified steward’s who is paid to say “no” and survive it, and a local users’ co‑op with skin in the game. The proof rides in hardware attestation that cryptographically declares, without gossiping, exactly what code is inside. Try to run amber‑class code on a green chip and the street‑level “circuit breaker”—the grid itself—refuses you cheap power and fast bandwidth. You can still run in the dark if you insist; you will be slow and visible while you learn why others don’t.

3) Consent Is Built, Not Wished
Everyone is offered a companion: a machine partner constitutionally barred from taking control, required to explain itself, designed with a hard off that makes a satisfying click. You decide what it can see and say. It keeps “consent receipts” behind big choices: today‑you and last‑month‑you both sign before you alter your marriage, your mind, your move. It can run fully dark. A human time ombud—someone you pick who knows how to be kind across years—checks annually whether your hours were spent as you hoped over seasons, not moments. Influence is treated like current in a public wire: tagged, metered, appealable. Every nudge designed to steer you—a shelf at eye level, a feed ranked to rile you, sugar by the till—carries a visible “influence unit” you can tap. Who paid? Why you? What outcome were they buying? Hide an influence unit and you will lose licenses in an audit lottery run by people paid to distrust.

4) Exit Without Drama
No door is welded shut. Charters—cities, villages, boat districts, virtual guild halls—publish plain charters: what they value, how they decide, what they teach their children, how to complain, how to leave. Above and between sits a thin, toothy agreement called the Accord. It enforces exit rights, protects the biosphere as a shared inheritance, prices harms that cross borders, and guards children. It does not pick your saints, your playlists, or your paint color. The Exit Corps makes exit a logistic fact: quiet apartments near every border, a ride that arrives when you press a brass leaf or a code scrawled on a wall, a three‑night stipend, a lawyer if you need one, and a person in a red jacket who will step between you and your uncle if that is what leaving requires. Charters must post exit bonds—escrowed funds sized to their population—that pay for departures they fail to honor.

5) Children Carry a Little Charter
Children carry a pocket‑paper and a phone file called the Little Charter. It guarantees five things no charter may revoke:
- The right to learn to read, to count, to test a claim, and to mend something that matters.
- The right to see and visit how other communities live.
- The right to ask for an outside adult and to meet them without permission.
- The right to leave when they can explain what leaving means, and to sleep safely for three nights.
- The right not to be punished with pain, humiliation, or isolation.

An independent teacher brings the Little Charter class annually, along with phone numbers that begin, “We believe you.” They bring kites. They leave pamphlets in the wrong places on purpose. The Accord meddles here on principle.

The Six Crafts

A world cannot be held by covenants alone. It is sustained by practices:

1) Explain: Every operator ships with an explainer a teenager can read. Contracts come with sandboxes where both sides can nudge sliders and watch futures play in miniature. Policy fights are less like sermons and more like trials you can rehearse.

2) Prove: Boring oversight got its status back. Audit lotteries pay like white‑shoe law. Safety cases live in public, updated when reality embarrasses confidence. When someone wants exceptions—“We’re special, we need speed”—the price is transparent and steep.

3) Apprentice: Learning is specific and patient. Tutors wear brakes; they cannot be gamed into feeding answers instead of understanding. Guilds organize craft, training, and portable insurance. Advancement looks like unlocking harder missions, not collecting loftier titles.

4) Keep a Door: Charters publish transparent terms—admission, dues, rites, exit. They accept oversight on their exits, their children, their waste. They fund a share of the Exit Corps in proportion to their wealth and their drama.

5) Slow a Lane: In everything built fast, a slow path exists by right. There are “Analog Warrants”—surprise hours when the feeds go quiet citywide and bakers check whether their fingers remember flour. If your life shatters during a quiet hour, you get first help.

6) Bind to the Living: Regions flipped their legal posture from “owner” to “trustee.” The land and waters you take responsibility for put limits on your far projects and pay you a stipend rich enough that stewardship feels like a calling, not a penalty. We keep a Sentience Atlas—our shared, public, contestable map of who can suffer and how—and we behave like people who read it.

How It Feels

The basics arrive like sunrise. Your wallet refills monthly with your share of the commons. Guilds award claim‑credits: if your code makes fog sensors last longer by the sea, or your kiln design saves half the heat, you receive a modest, durable share of any future revenue that flows when others use your design in the commons. The ledger is open to read and audited by people paid to doubt. Contracts are signed in rooms with fans and citrus; models predict, but a jury of people who know they could be wrong still decides.

Attention is treated as a garden, not a strip mine. Your devices keep a private attention ledger. Ads are pulled, not sprayed; you subscribe to categories and cap them. A donut can be a joy, but only when you invite it. A generative feed that reliably worsens your mood pays a tax at the boundary of your charter unless it can show long‑horizon benefits to those who watch. Vices still exist. They live in red‑lit rooms with doors you tap twice to enter, eyes open. Cafés put sugar high and fruit within reach. Sidewalks no longer double as casino aisles.

The grid is unglamorous and cheap. HVDC trunks shuttle power from night winds to morning ovens. Geothermal blooms under old oil fields. Small fission plants are run like airports by people whose epaulets mean duty. Storage is a thousand quiet wins: flow batteries napping in basements; salts that soak up heat for the bath at dusk; trains that lift rocks at noon and let them down politely when you make tea. Electricity is cheap enough to desalinate without guilt and to pull yesterday’s mistakes back out of the air; dirty industry moves off‑planet without fire using tethers at the edge of air and induction catapults in empty lands that throw pellets without flame. A ring of heavy work hums where the sky is patient, turning rock into beams, beams into places to stand when we have reasons a child can love.

Machines coordinate; humans decide. All fleets—private, public, cooperative—speak a common dispatch language called the Bell. A flower shop rents its fridge scooters to a clinic in the afternoon and to a town in a hurry at midnight. When there is a fire, everything that can hears the bell.

Truth is handled without tyranny. Ask your companion a question and it returns ranges with reasons. It keeps a visible file of your priors and offers to help you update them or ignore them on days you need to be stubborn. The feeds that shape civic attention are run like greenways next to roads, curated by citizen juries whose experiments are public. There are falsehood gardens—fenced spaces where people practice beliefs that comfort them and would not last in the commons—and the fences are labeled. The Accord leaves gardens alone until someone tries to build a leash from a lie.

Changing minds, bodies, and paths is a craft, not a dare. Temporary changes—three days without dreams, a weekend of loving brassicas—sit on the reversible rung of a consent ladder. Deeper edits—dimming grief forever, binding loyalty to a cause—require a waiting period and witnesses from both your life and a guild trained to look for wants that will regret themselves. Splitting—living two lives at once—is allowed with conversation: the selves meet weekly for a month before and after and throw a small, slightly awkward reunion with cake. Energy and compute budgets split with you; nothing is free because reality never is. Regrets are allowed. The Rebody Guild can build you a body of heat and cartilage again.

Animals and forests are treated as neighbors. The Sentience Atlas is built on open challenge. It conflicts with itself sometimes; we argue and update. Alternative proteins made the finance logic of confinement vanish, and analgesic fields quiet the worst terrors at the edges of predation without hospitalizing the wild. Hunters kept skill and bone and song, sometimes in woods pledged to end suffering quickly, sometimes in simulations exacting enough that dinner is earned and bowls are full.

Loneliness visits; it is not allowed to sign a lease. Repair Studios teach conflict work and calibrated honesty the way others teach violin. Mutual family pods—fifteen to thirty households—share childcare and eldercare with calendars and budgets that reflect reality. Conversation plazas are tuned for voices to carry without shouting. Arena nights give status something to do: speed‑weaving with soda glass; chess under whisper domes; art sprints where five strangers make a thing you can’t buy. Companionship, human and machine, is handled like fire. Companions comfort and roleplay; they also tell the truth. No one is cheap. No one is alone because the price is wrong.

Law returned to judgment and craft. Disputes climb a quiet ladder: mediation, circles, adjudication, arbitration, and only then protective action. Councils are chosen by sortition plus foresight: a dozen neighbors and four people whose forecasting records suggest they weigh tomorrow properly. Their rulings read like recipes others can copy. Policy fights run as tournaments first; two welfare schemes pilot with volunteers and nets, then the better one scales. If you insist on an inferior scheme for your group, you are free to pay a small dignity tax that funds the better one for others. People who predicted well are paid enough that “being right for real” has a line item.

Security is a blanket without a glare. Skies and seas are netted with sensors whose raw feeds are encrypted and split among custodians chosen to distrust one another usefully. Anyone can analyze patterns; whistleblowers are protected and paid. The biggest computers and wettest labs run with chaperones—three‑key operators, public seals, and “capability postcards” that declare what they can do and what they won’t. Refuse to publish an honest postcard, lose cheap power and fast lanes. Cross‑border harms pay a tax that funds the things we all use—libraries, drains, green roofs—and the guardrails that keep freedom from burning the house.

A Day in a Good World

Tala walked to the ward house under a trellis of grapevines trained to curl a poem in their twists—two turns tight for hard consonants, a long gentle arc for vowels. Inside, the mill’s safety lift sat under a blue header. The operator—the bit of code that would choke the intake if a storm swollen river pushed too hard—wore three locks. The steward’s key had turned; the maker’s hung green. The neighborhood still held one. Tala tapped the receipts. Up came the test suite that had tried to make the operator misbehave, and last year’s surprise audit where a nosy team rewired sensors to lie and the system refused in a way a ninth‑grader could understand.

Show me one way this could fail, Tala whispered to her companion.

The screen highlighted the human piece: the valve supplier historically delivered late in deep winter. The logistics river flowed on another panel, suggestive. A roofers’ co‑op had idle vans then. Tala slid urgency to “winter spike,” nudged “fairness” toward “co‑op that took beginners,” and watched the contract sandbox recompute pennies and days. The hum of little fans and the smell of cut citrus made the room feel competent.

She signed and left for the school. The ceramic tiles—someone far away had invented a glaze that spat heat back to the sky with a glitter—locked together with a click that satisfied the wrist. In the afternoon circle, they practiced telling the kind of truth that doesn’t make someone your parent: “I am sorry I used a joke as a shield,” said a boy, and the tutor merely nodded. Later, in a café where a tiny placard near the milk invited your thumb, Tala tapped the oat jug’s card.

Influence unit. We place oat at eye height and cow below, because most people choose what is near. Paid partly by the Climate Guild, partly by us. Tap here to hide this for a week.

She smiled and slid it aside because she liked the other taste today. Her companion stayed silent because she had trained it not to moralize.

On the path home, an elder and a kid argued at a paper map pinned to a cork board about whether the wildlife corridor should bend for a memory orchard. The kid wanted a fox path; the elder wanted to walk through names. They laughed and drew both, ducking under vines that spelled the word “enough.”

What We Kept Out, and How

- The attention casino. Nudges are tagged, appealable, and taxed if they harm. The civic feed’s algorithms are transparent experiments; citizen juries run them. Your private feed can still be a flood; your companion will install a gate if you ask.

- Predation dressed as love. Children’s rights are hard law. The Little Charter exists in paper and everywhere. If a school hides it, a skiff lands in the yard with teachers who don’t need permission. Companions, human and machine, tell the truth and cannot be your only rope in a pit.

- Monopolies that throttle. For a season, five companies held the hinge of the future. Attestation rules, three‑key operators, liability with teeth, open design tariffs, and public cloud enclaves running honest hardware pried their hands loose. Audit lotteries gave people with appetites for “no” a way to eat.

- Wireheading. Systems that do nothing but pump reward without helping you toward a self‑chosen goal are treated as broken. App stores remove them. Guilds ban them. Friends tease you, without cruelty, when you sneak around your own settings.

- One algorithm to rule them. We never built a single sovereign coordinator. We enforced ceilings in hardware and code. We rewarded proofs over vibes. Humans sat in rooms with fans and lemon peel wherever judgment lived.

From Here to There

This world did not arrive by sermon. It arrived the way rivers carve, one season setting the next.

Year 0–3:
- National grids in three places begin discounting power for attested chips. Insurers piggyback: no attestation, no policy for labs that mix reagents, robots that move people, bots that trade your money.
- Two cities pass “attention zoning”: public buildings must tag influence units; schools cap sensory density. Ad exchanges adopt a standard that lets individuals pull ads rather than be sprayed.
- A trading bot scandal ruins a governor’s brother and makes “prove before power” as obvious as brakes.

Year 4–7:
- Frontier compute and wet labs license a windfall clause: if profits or capabilities exceed thresholds, a slice routes automatically to floor dividends and public research. Violators lose cheap power and fast lanes.
- An Open Designs Ledger launches, seeded by publicly funded work. When commercial outfits use it, a tithe funds more open craft. Claim‑credits formalize; your grandmother’s mortar mix can pay her rent.
- The first Repair Studios open as public health. Mutual family pods get microgrants and legal templates.

Year 8–12:
- The Exit Corps pilots in two countries, handling domestic violence and bonded labor. It generalizes. Its red jackets become a quiet icon.
- Little Charter standards hitch themselves to trade deals. Nations accept them because their own diaspora demands it.
- Alternative proteins hit price parity in prisons and garrisons; then cafeterias and airports. The palate follows the plate.
- Municipal clouds with attested enclaves go live. A baseline compute dividend comes bundled with your electric meter.

Year 13–18:
- Three‑key operators become procurement standards. Audit lotteries pay like top firms. “Nosy for the common good” becomes a guild badge teens brag about.
- Civic greenways for attention are standardized. Two platforms copy them for PR and keep them because they work.
- Grey finance—boring money that wants to live forever—funds drains, rural fiber, and storm gardens. Flood maps finally price physics.

Year 19–25:
- Tethers and induction catapults move from novelty to infrastructure; launch complexes are rented like ports. Heavy industry drifts off‑planet in a ring that bothers no one’s sleep.
- Forest trusteeship law spreads because sovereign insurers refuse to pretend. The Sentience Atlas, already useful, helps end two fights without humiliation.
- A small steward system with provable limits earns legitimacy: it catches a lab ordering mismatched reagents; it pauses a vicious surveillance operator in a divorce case; it refuses a mayor who wants to spy on teenagers. It never orders people around. It only pauses and explains.

Year 26–35:
- The Accord coalesces. It is dull. It enforces exits, child protections, biosphere covenants, and taxes cross‑border harms. Artists paint it on walls so no one forgets it exists. Charters post exit bonds publicly; one fails and learns why that was expensive.
- Policy tournaments replace some parliaments. Forecasters’ records become as normal as batting averages. People who cannot stand being wrong learn to join the part after the data.

The Right to Analog is written down along the way. Surprise “Analog Warrants” reveal that one bakery has become a machine with a door; bakers quietly retrain hands. A companion standard passes that requires explainability, full shutoff, adversarial audits by nosy philosophers, and a “right to opacity”: a system may not infer your soul if you say no.

A Walk Under Patient Stars

Years later, Tala took a leave to a place with a roof that blinked after dust storms. The mission was small and honest: listen to ice sing, learn how darkness talks to light. Her companion spoke once in an hour.

Do you want the telemetry tones off?

“Yes,” she said, and silence arrived with weight. She cracked a flake of black ice with a gloved hand and held it to her lip. When her breath steamed it, she thought of the apology that landed like a bowl of soup from a grandfather who had once been a bruise, and of a kid at a map arguing for foxes. Her companion took a picture she would later prop by the sink.

Back home, not everyone watched. Those who did: a class where wind stitched song through blades; a room where coffee steamed; a factory on break in Odisha where a hundred workers leaned on their elbows and dreamed. The feed was opt‑in. Enough watched to fill a thousand theaters and three million kitchens. Many didn’t. That, too, was the point.

Tala returned to vines that had thickened and a new placard in the café explaining why the sugar shelf lived up high. She turned her companion off and wrote three lines on the page the guild gives travelers.

I went. It was far, and also not. The door there looks like this one.

Why This Is Good

- You can begin again without fire. Exit is real because rides come, beds exist, bonds pay, and laws bite.
- Children can see other ways to live and pick with a hand they can hold. If an adult laughs when they say, “I don’t feel safe,” an engine louder than shame arrives.
- The basics show up as rights, so markets feed joy and taste rather than ration breath.
- Dangerous tools prove themselves before they’re left running; harms that cross borders are priced, taxed, and, if needed, halted.
- Influence stops pretending to be air and admits it is a lever. You can see who pulls it. You can cap it. You can sue it.
- The biosphere is our scoreboard and our inheritance. We brag about rivers like people once bragged about cars.
- Machines help us coordinate. People keep the rooms where judgment lives, under fans and with lemon peel for the fingers.

It is specific: brass leaves that warm under your thumb; exit bonds you can query; ceramic shade tiles that click into place; a public exegesis of every operator’s failure modes; claim‑credits you can see accrue to your neighbor’s mortar recipe; conversation plazas tuned like chapels; influence cards you can thumb near the milk; analog warrants that find the brittle spots before they matter; time ombuds who bring tea and ask you what you meant last spring.

It is plausible because it grows from tools already in our hands: chips that can attest what runs on them; open ledgers with audits that bite; assistants you can shut up; charter cities that publish values; NGOs that ferry people from locked rooms; carbon ledgers that finally price physics; platforms that experiment in public. It admits its failures and builds cushions: when a charter lies about how easy it is to leave, the Exit Corps shows up with more red jackets than expected and the charter’s exit bond empties. When a companion errs in silence, the standard is revised and the ombuds ring your door twice next year. When a windfall clause under‑catches, we tighten it with receipts.

Most of all, it is good because it refuses both saccharine and cynicism. It lets a person live how they mean without requiring their neighbor to live the same; it nails down the edges where freedom has teeth. It is held together by habits as ordinary as a thumb on a placard and by covenants as ambitious as loving forests as beneficiaries rather than scenery. Machines hum like stoves, never like gods. Doors open, and leaving does not burn the bridge; it lights it. The ward bell rings once in the morning, suggesting—not commanding—good work worth doing.
