The Accord Network

The good future arrived not as a revolution, but as a firmware update for civic life. It appeared first as a building permit that took three days instead of nine months, then as an electricity bill that edged down and stayed down, then as a clinic that called you because your stride had shortened by two centimeters and it mattered. One city after another adopted the same set of habits. Within a decade the map showed a constellation of places calling themselves the Accord Network. They were not a federation so much as a recipe. Anyone could fork it.

Each Accord city made two promises and wrote them down:

- We will make the important things easy and the dangerous things hard.
- We will show our work.

Those lines turned out to be surprisingly load-bearing. They shaped housing, energy, health, law, news, work, and the way strangers trusted one another enough to build.

Ground, power, and the right to build

Accord cities began by separating land from buildings. The city held the ground in public trust and offered renewable 60–90 year ground leases through regular auctions. If the city made an area more valuable—by extending a tram, daylighting a river, or opening a remarkable school—part of that uplift returned automatically through rising lease payments. Private parties owned what they built, improved, and maintained; the location value flowed back to help pay for schools, clean water, clinics, and cash dividends.

Permits lived in a pattern book. There were standard designs for rowhouses, midrise mixed-use buildings, small workshops with shared courtyards, timber towers, and corner groceries with upstairs flats. If you stayed within the envelope—height, structural systems, fire egress, setbacks, daylight, seismic detail—the permit auto-issued in 72 hours from the city’s plan checker. Departures were welcome and reviewed on a timeline measured in weeks, not years, if you brought a structural and fire seal plus a simulation check against the city’s digital twin. The code fit on eighty-odd pages and three posters in the permit hall. It was written for a competent builder, not a lawyer.

The lights stayed on because cities treated electrons as the base layer. Rooftop solar arrived first; then solar arrays on brownfields and parking canopies; then a pair of advanced fission units licensed under a fast, transparent regime with independent auditors and posted fault trees. Cities wrote down a dignity standard: average power interruptions under five minutes per person per year; drinking water that tasted like it came from a mountain; indoor air below a PM2.5 threshold you could monitor in your kitchen. Desalination plants and aquifer recharge drank sunlight and electrons; high-resolution metering turned leak-hunting into a paid teenage sport with leaderboards.

When kilowatt-hours got cheap and steady, other bottlenecks softened. Induction ovens let microbakeries occupy storefronts without grease hoods. Quiet motors changed the shape of nights. Microfactories took root in places where the rent had fallen just enough: one staffed by humans on day shift, the other two by robots that were still learning to peel peaches without bruising them.

A constitution for being right

Every Accord city adopted a short charter clause called the Measurement Compact. It said that when public decisions depended on facts about the world, those facts would be forecast in advance, recorded in a way that encouraged courage and punished overconfidence, and graded afterward in public.

To make the clause real, cities stood up regulated forecast exchanges—Assurance Boards—where any major claim with civic stakes could be listed with precise resolution criteria. You could not list cruelty; you could list whether the new busway would hit 120,000 daily riders by March; whether a bridge retrofit would cut failure probability under a 500-year storm by half; whether a primary-care redesign would reduce 30-day readmissions after heart failure; whether floodproofing the substation would keep the next hurricane’s blackout under 12 hours.

Departments that made claims took a small position on their own questions. Vendors could opt into accelerated procurement if they posted conditional claims about outcomes and carried an insurer willing to take the other side; if the market priced high confidence and a monitor signed off on the rollout plan, a pilot began. Every quarter, scores posted to a public ledger. If reality moved against a claim, the rollout paused and the insurer called back capital. People learned to speak with error bars. It turned out to be a relief to hear, “We think fourteen months; there’s a one-in-five chance of an overrun; these are the three signs we’ll be wrong early.”

Representation as a network, not a pyramid

Voting stayed, but decisions traveled through a different nervous system. The Accord protocol for civic representation was a layered delegation network that people used like they use calendars. You could vote directly on issues if you wanted, you could delegate your vote on transit to Julia who had built two tram lines, your vote on parks to Marlon who had restored a river, and your vote on housing to anyone you trusted. Delegations were transitive and revocable. The interface showed whose judgment you were “standing on” and who was “standing on you.” When you changed your mind, your weight moved next week, not next decade.

Committees shrank because the city could see what most people trusted by domain, not just by geography. “Liquid” councils met in rooms with glass walls and microphones that belonged to the public. They published not just conclusions, but their notes: the tradeoffs considered, the forecasts cited, the confidence intervals. Influence was not a robe; it was a ledger of where people had chosen to place their trust.

News that deserved the name

Accord cities invested in institutions that told the truth without a sneer. The Chroniclers were a civic guild funded partly by the land dividend and partly by small public pledges—one percent here, half a percent there—visible on a public roll. Chroniclers were paid well and selected by a process that combined open peer review with a jury of senior practitioners. Their job was to reconstruct what happened in terms anyone could follow, to write timelines and causal graphs, to interview the people who were wrong without humiliating them, and to stamp a few stories each week as “clean history.”

Advertisers still existed, but everyone understood most advertising as a transfer game. Companies who wanted to claim a better product paid independent test houses and Chroniclers to try it and publish likelihood curves. “Here is how much better it is, with error bars.” Jingles didn’t decide anything. It was a strange relief.

Work, skill, and the mission exchange

The economy ran hot because cities removed the thickest frictions and made it honorable to do useful things. Occupational licensing receded into competence portals: independent testing houses where you could book an exam that looked like the job. If you could wire a panel safely, nobody made you sit through 400 hours of stale curriculum. If you could cut a clean mortise on camera and on site, your stamp traveled across Accord cities by reciprocity.

For teenagers and adults who didn’t want a classroom, and for those who did but wanted more, micro-apprenticeships arrived like summer jobs had once arrived. Starting around twelve, if you and your guardians agreed, you could sign up for week-long “try-days” in kitchens, clinics, assembly floors, archives, farms. The safety rails were heavy: no high-energy machines without dual consent and interlocks; insurance baked into the city; a supervisor whose score moved if they let you do something foolish. Older kids taught younger kids the things they had just learned to do; they got paid to teach, and their students’ later success bumped their teacher score.

Between the private job boards and the public sector sat a Mission Exchange: a catalogue of work that mattered but didn’t fit neatly inside a firm yet. “Make a parts tree for this school lathe and translate it into three languages.” “Refactor the city’s food-inspection routes to cut travel time by 12%.” “Pair with our street team for two weeks to test a high-touch insulin coaching model.” The missions posted price, acceptance criteria, and a score of how many times similar missions had paid and matched. Some missions were slices of R&D; some were pure civic housekeeping; some were competitions to design a better gripper for a peach-packing robot. Anyone could bid; the testing was in the outcome.

Machines didn’t steal dignity; they took the night shift. Robots arrived as apprentices that did dull loops and left the weird, high-judgment work to people. When a line of work shrank, the city did not pretend it was a surprise. The commons dividend and Sabbatical Years meant that a layoff was a change, not a cliff. You could claim one in seven years at half your recent income to learn, care for someone, build a thing, or stare out a window. The city didn’t audit your soul. The city gave you back time.

Paying for it, and paying people back

Money for the new normal came from where value pooled: ground leases, power margins after the bonds were paid, port fees, rights to use the city’s data commons under strict privacy rules, licenses to traverse the curb, and the surplus from the Civic Cloud (a public compute co-op that sold cycles to labs, clinics, and schools under capability limits that auditors could see). After the boring bills were paid—trash, trees, teachers—what remained went half to capital projects and half to people as a commons check. At first it was coffee money. Then groceries. Then rent. It didn’t replace work; it made change bearable.

Invention was not fenced off behind patents that blocked everyone. Instead, cities signed onto a “royalty commons” for industry-changing ideas: If your factory, drug, or algorithm made new money because of an invention, you were obliged—by contract in some industries, by reputational gravity in all—to flow a standard share of the marginal gain back to the inventor or inventor fund for a time. Third-party auditors published the flows. Chroniclers wrote down who thanked whom. If you stiffed the people who made your money possible, your customers and peers saw it, and your brand shed trust like a tree sheds leaves in wind.

Health that bought outcomes and kept receipts

Clinics in Accord cities were paid for what people wanted, not for how many times a billing code printed. Bundles had names instead of ICD numbers. “Walk the stairs without pain in six weeks.” “Remit Type 2 diabetes and keep it there for twelve months.” “Lift your grandchild.” The bundles paid for boring excellence—sleep studies, wound care, counseling—and dramatic things where they made sense. The point was that someone was on the hook for an outcome that mattered. The data anonymized and flowed. If a clinic hit the marks, it grew; if not, it fixed what it could and referred you somewhere that did.

Powerful models accelerated medicine without letting it run wild. The Assurance Boards let a consortium of oncologists roll out a therapy to a thousand patients in months, with a standing plan to stop if harm appeared. Microfactories printed small-batch vaccines validated in ring trials. The Guild of Instruments—the Network’s distributed research shop—fixed its attention on biomarkers that didn’t lie and open tools that made replication boring. Every lab banked part of its budget in a replication pool. Red teams got paid to bruise egos. Success felt like your protocol, run by a stranger halfway across the world, working on the first try.

Safety as a practice, not a mood

Cities treated biological risk like flame in a busy kitchen. Community labs had glass walls and mentors. Equipment that could cause harm had physical interlocks and audit trails. DNA synthesis providers screened orders against a global database. Wastewater genomics read the city’s pulse; if a cough pattern crept upward, nurses appeared on corners with friendly instructions and rapid tests, and the EMS schedule flexed.

Other risks got the same calm. Flood maps moved under your feet; drains had QR codes that told you when they’d last been cleared. When a crane failed, a clock started counting down to the preliminary report. The final write-up read like a story with names, physics, decisions, and moments where someone could have asked a better question. Watching hurt. People watched anyway.

Cognition hygiene mattered. The Civic Cloud enforced capability limits on general-purpose models used for law, benefits, and care. Anyone could inspect the safety case for a public model: what it was allowed to do, what it could talk to, what logs existed, who would be notified if it misbehaved. It was boring by design. The most addictive forms of social media—engagement-optimized feeds without friction—never got a foothold inside schools or civic interfaces. Phones shipped with a “consentful affordance” mode on by default: no autoplay, no infinite scroll, no bad surprises. People could turn it off; most didn’t.

Law, courts, and the habit of reasons

Police stopped being a single tool for layered problems. The responder on your block could bring you a bed, a detox, a mediator, or a ride to a day labor gig. Cameras rolled, but the more interesting object was not the footage. It was the menu of guaranteed services that officers could summon, and the rule that someone had to follow each case until it landed somewhere decent.

Courts wrote down their reasoning. Judges were paid to produce decisions that matched the law and the values cities had written into their charters; they published the logic. Appeals addressed logic, not just outcome. Two courts sometimes ran in parallel on the same issue when the stakes were monumental. Models wrote memos that pointed out precedents and disparities; they were assistants, not deciders. Civil society ran bias bounties. If an advocate showed a systematic skew that held out of sample, a public fix followed, and the bounty paid.

Civic services stopped being a labyrinth. Everyone had a digital public servant that spoke their language, told them what they qualified for, filled the forms, and put the rules into sentences. It did not sell anything. It did not yell. If it went down, the mayor’s phone buzzed.

Culture, joy, and the right to be left alone

Accord cities made room for both noisy plazas and quiet lives. On certain nights, courtyards filled. Someone read the list: new arrivals, babies, the grieving, people looking for a hand, people starting a thing. Tables were dragged together. Appointments were made. A neighbor caught you by the elbow if you missed what you had promised and said, “We noticed.”

On other days, the doors along a side street had a small emblem: a fern in a circle. The Quiet Houses were places where people could live gently when life had gotten too loud, or too sharp, or just wrong-sized. There was no sermon attached. The commons dividend covered a room and meals; there was counseling if wanted; small tasks if wanted; an expectation of kindness in both directions. Some people stayed a month, some a year, some longer. The price of admission was simple: be decent while you are here. The Quiet Houses never became warehouses of despair because the rest of the city took them seriously and because the path back into work or school ran a few doors down, not across town.

There were matchmakers the way there are real estate agents. If you wanted help finding someone to build a life with—or a team to build a company with—you paid on results. They saw you at your best and worst, and you saw that everyone else, too, had a lot going on. It removed some of the theater. It made falling in love less like a market and more like an honest negotiation.

Kids learned to read and count and argue without turning it into a feud. They trained on calibration boards, making hundreds of small predictions about the weather, sports, crops, and their own moods. They learned how it felt to be less wrong. Lathes hummed next to microscopes. The janitor showed you how to fix the machine he used.

Commerce without overlords

Cartels had a hard time breathing because the public could see them form. The Chroniclers and the Assurance Boards were a bad habitat for quiet collusion. If a reputational puddle of “villain respect” started to shimmer somewhere, consumer co-ops and investors knew how to build a competitor, fund it overnight, and climb aboard. Boycotts had due process: a civic panel published a case; the accused got a response window; the recommendation was time-limited and reviewed. This made boycotts rare and powerful.

Intellectual property was narrower. Copyright lasted long enough to get a return, not long enough to fence in culture. Patents existed as shall-license contracts in a few industries—pharma, materials—where a flood of me-too goods could otherwise strangle the lab that did the long, slow thing. Everywhere else, the royalty commons and reputational accounting carried the load. You were expected to pay people who made your profits possible. Most people did.

Global and outward

The Accord pattern didn’t try to be an island. Ports resized their cranes and fees together. A compute and chip alliance made sure advanced fabs existed inside polities with due process and free speech, and that public models used in other countries came with audits, capability limits, and train logs. The alliance said no to exporting surveillance kernels and yes to exporting drought maps, cold-chain optimizers, ring trial kits, and small mRNA microfactories in vans. The price of entry was small and high: don’t jail people for speaking. Don’t run secret blacklists. Keep clean courts. Many places said yes. More came later.

Outbreaks stopped being moral theater and turned into logistics. An eradication team in Ghana shared a supply chain with a team in Louisiana trying to stamp out a different parasite, each making use of ring trials and wastewater reads and microfactories that could bounce along a rough road. The Guild’s “Hall of Useful Things” put a star next to tools that were still widely used five years later. It kept the star or took it down, visible to all.

No miracles, just margins that moved

The Accord cities did not abolish tragedy. A reactor somewhere had a scare. It was contained. The supplier’s stock price fell until they learned to be humble. A lab lead tried to walk around a rule and lost her job; the court wrote a long opinion about why. A neighborhood fought about a mural for a month and then laughed together when the artist painted both sides’ jokes.

What changed was leverage. Ordinary virtues—truthfulness, competence, neighborliness—reached further. Forecasts went on ledgers before action and came back graded after. Budgets posted falsifiable promises. People who moved a system to a better equilibrium got outsized thanks and tangible rewards. People who tried to defect at scale discovered that a city could, if it had to, coordinate as quickly as one firm.

Getting from here to there

None of this required a messiah. It needed a civic appetite for grown-up tools. The first cities did eight things.

- Passed a pattern code for a handful of safe, handsome building types, with default-yes permits and fast digital checks. They measured outcomes and adjusted the code annually.
- Moved toward ground leases or land value capture where they could; when they couldn’t, they piloted it around new infrastructure and directed the uplift to public goods and dividends.
- Committed to cheap, clean power with a two-track plan: solar and wind everywhere they fit; one or two advanced fission units licensed under an auditable, time-bound regime with third-party safety cases. They set and published reliability standards.
- Stood up a legal, small-stakes forecast exchange tied to a few early projects. They started with three questions and one department bonus linked to forecast accuracy; they grew from there.
- Built a Civic Cloud—public compute with audited safety limits—that labs, clinics, and schools could use. They required audits and bias bounties for any model used in law, benefits, or health.
- Created a Guild of Instruments, with replication pools, red-team bounties, and a simple prestige: how often was your protocol still used and did a stranger succeed on the first run.
- Replaced occupational licensing with competence exams in three trades, then ten, then most. They signed reciprocity treaties with any city that copied them.
- Launched a small commons dividend the day they had the smallest margin to pay it. They kept paying it, publicly, even when it was more hassle than help. They added Sabbatical Years as soon as the math permitted.

The rest followed from momentum. The first Assurance Board question paid out on the day a busway hit its ridership and also missed its promised opening by three weeks. The mayor apologized and meant it. The dividend bought diapers. An apprentice baker who had never liked school found she loved ovens and control charts. The Chroniclers wrote a long, beautiful history of a failed flood wall that saved a neighborhood three years later when the fix held. The police chief started carrying a laminated card that listed every service they could summon to a scene. She was most proud of the one that paid for a cab to the warehouse that would rehire a man if his car worked tomorrow.

In the evenings, under lights that felt gentle on purpose, people sat on stoops and argued without hating each other. They were not saints; they were not perfect; they were not even especially unusual. They had just agreed to make good equilibria visible, to pay for outcomes, to thank with money and honor the people who made useful things, and to keep the lights on.

If you wanted to taste one of these cities for the first time, you would notice small things first. The bus arrived when the sign said. The clerk at the permit counter didn’t treat you like a suspect. A teenager pointed at a storm drain and showed you the app that said it had been cleared two days ago; she’d done it and logged it; the city owed her twenty dollars. You would notice how often grown-ups said, “We were wrong.” You might not notice at all that the city had quietly, steadily made it harder to set your life on fire with a tap. You would feel, instead, a wide permission: to start a small hard thing, to stop and rest, to change your mind, to be brave and then be wrong and then be better.

The Accord Network did not arrive all at once. It arrived as jurisdictions copied one another’s best commits. It arrived because we already knew how to build houses that didn’t burn and clinics that called you first and courts that could show their work. It arrived because we decided to act as if incentives worked on us, too, and as if our neighbors might be as decent as we are on our best days. It is hard to imagine, from within a bad equilibrium, how quickly a good one can compound. The only hard part is the first promise: we will make the important things easy and the dangerous things hard. The second promise—show your work—follows, and with it a city that breathes like a living thing, steady, generous, alert.
