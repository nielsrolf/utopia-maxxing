The Stewardship Compact and the Houses of Craft

Dawn spills down a valley where terraces ripple with fruit trees and rye. A bell rings once from a timber hall; people shoulder baskets and step toward a covered market stitched with vines. On the far side of the ridge, an elevated tram glides through a district of mid‑rise buildings with open stairs and wind‑moved chiming screens. Inside a ground‑floor bay, a wheelchair frame hums under a mill while a child sits cross‑legged at a microscope learning to balance light. Three hundred kilometers up, a spindle habitat turns so slowly you can watch its fields slide from shade to sun; condensation glitters off an airlock like a sheath of beads. A plane of white text rests on a café wall in each place, in different typefaces and languages but the same shape. It is a short law called the Stewardship Compact, and it folds the world into a few promises that proved strong enough to hold.

The Compact: Enough, Choice, and Reversal

- Enough is not a coupon. It is a mesh of services that always exists: room, food, water, clinics, learning, transit, and safe company. If you do nothing for a season, you sleep indoors, eat well, ride free, see a nurse, and find a table with names that fit beside yours.

- Choice is real. Some communities throttle technology to what you can understand at a glance. Others adopt tools aggressively but fence the dangerous edges. People can move among these “lanes” without losing their floor.

- Reversal is a design rule. The Switchback Principle governs changes that could scar: build switchbacks, not cliffs. Pilot with stop dates. Embed a way to step back built into the thing itself.

A short sentence follows that lawyers learned to love: Reasons, or it’s not law. Any decision that binds people must print its reasoning, its assumptions, and where to check its evidence. Half the miracles in this world are just diligent paperwork.

Paying for the Floor: The Commons Weave

We fixed the way we treat scarce things none of us made. The Commons Weave is a set of ledgers, auctions, and trusts that take value from shared assets and return it to everyone.

- Scarce rights that owe their worth to arrangement—not labor—are leased, not owned forever: land locations, mineral extraction windows, curb slots at rush hour, radio bands, airport gates, orbital shells, polar routes, and the carbon uptake of a bay. Exclusive use has a price you bid in public.

- Three streams flow from those leases.

  • A Civic Dividend hits every resident’s account monthly. It is modest by design—a floor that covers an apartment in the public option, basic meals, local transit, and clinic visits. It is not a replacement for services; it is time, turned into courage. You can refuse a bad boss and not fall.

  • Place Funds keep a chunk of value where it arises. When a tram doubles leases along its line, those gains pay to run the tram and fix its tracks.

  • A Planetary Trust collects tithes on global commons—satellite slots, straits, air corridors—and funds slow, border‑crossing goods: pathogen monitoring, drought‑resistant open seeds, mangrove nurseries, coastal pumps.

- Land holding stopped being a waiting game. Idle high‑value parcels charge their holders for the privilege. Speculators learned to build or sell; neighborhoods learned to grow.

- Automated capital pays you. Regions place slices of microfactories, farms under glass, data centers, and grid storage into community trusts. Dividends help fund the floor and lighten the jump between crafts.

The result is something conservative in the right way: your basic days are predictable. The public options are dignified enough to compete with anything.

The Houses of Craft

Every city and a surprising number of small towns keep a House of Craft: part library, part lab, part guild hall, part conservatory. A House is a building and a charter.

- Learn causes, not tricks. Apprenticeships start with why a weld fails and how noise eats a signal; the properties of a seed coat; the ethics of a dosage. People publish what is safe and useful in open pattern libraries: reference designs for clean cookstoves, daylighting standards that end mold, guides for calibrating microscope cameras.

- Fence danger. Models that plan in the physical world or could touch a wet lab register before training. Independent evaluations test them for deception or misuse. Keys to cranes, municipal grids, gene printers, and markets are held by licensed humans who pass face‑to‑face trials, not just online quizzes. Biological equipment above a capability threshold is licensed globally; synthesis orders and hazardous precursors are monitored in aggregate by cryptographic protocols that keep individual privacy while lighting up clusters of risk.

- Return fruits, don’t hoard. Inventions made with public help publish open by default. Instead of a monopoly veto, contributors join audited reward pools that pay out retroactively from measured usage and impact. The more a tool helps in the world, the larger your slice without you blocking anyone’s work.

- Trial gardeners run civic experiments: an adjustable bus route trialed for six Saturdays and then shut off whether it sings or stumbles; a night clinic with varied hours and measures; a festival with flood‑plain plantings that doubles as a drill. Results and postmortems shelve in the House under “good tries and what we learned.”

- A Hall of Works holds both bright and grey: a low‑cost contrast trick that made a scrap‑plastic microscope see parasites in a village clinic beside the audit that stopped a beloved air filter from emitting ultrafines. Makers and finders share the dais.

The Houses made fear and fraud more expensive. They also made it fun to be serious. On a given evening you might find: a nurse‑led team tuning closed‑loop neuromodulation under gloved oversight; a stonecutting circle guided by a laughing elder mason; teenagers comparing two bus fare experiments with their tutor‑bots; a “pattern keeper” cataloging twenty safe ways to insulate a cold room with local materials.

The Civic Operating Layer

We built a thin layer of civic firmware, protocols that made power legible and change reversible.

- Reasons Ledger: agencies and courts file decisions with their proofs and influences, including where an AI clerk helped. For big moves, a randomly assigned second panel writes a parallel case. Disagreement is signal, not scandal.

- Pledge‑and‑Pay: anyone can propose a civic project on an open platform: owl‑friendly street lights, a weekend harbor ferry, shade trees for a heat island. People and institutions pledge; money releases when outcomes land. Where metrics fail, structured peer review by named explainers stands in. All projects print stop dates and postmortems. Counterfactual insurance compensates people harmed by trials that fail, funded by a small levy across the platform.

- Experimental Charters let schools, clinics, and districts adopt time‑boxed rule changes if they keep baseline rights, show metrics, and print an exit path. A control block or period is part of the plan by default.

- Truth Bonds: leaders or forecasters can stake a bond on claims. Culture shifted and now you look a little thin without one when you ask the city to follow you.

- Exit Escrow: when you leave a job, a school, or a platform, your data, credentials, and references leave with you in standard formats. Relevant safety notes move in redacted signed form. Cheap exit makes coercion expensive.

- The Right to Smallness caps entities beyond which they must decentralize or open their interfaces and books. Bigness isn’t banned; it is bounded by sunlight.

- A Challenger Fund unlocks when collusion is evident: capital and talent line up in sixty days for a worker‑owned, open‑book competitor.

- Explainer Corps draft and update living briefs on contested questions: what works for addiction in this region; how the flood wall diverted water and who paid. They cite. They can be sued for sloppiness.

- A Policy NTSB investigates bad failures. When a school’s quiet room changes spike conflict, a team reconstructs why, prints it without blame theater, and adds a line to the pattern library: “Do this, not this.”

Belonging Is Infrastructure

We treated loneliness like lead in paint: a public hazard we can design around.

- Relationship skills sit next to algebra in schools. Consent, conflict repair, friendships, attachment. Adults take refreshers. People who help couples and circles thrive are licensed matchwrights paid for stability, not churn.

- Social kitchens host “light‑lift” groups: build a bench, record a block’s oral history, run a tiny science fair. Cities monitor belonging as they do air. When a neighborhood’s loneliness rate spikes, a steward arrives with money and menus.

- Arrivals Halls sit at ports and stations: nurses, clean bunks, showers, legal help, and a health steward empowered to ask for consented quarantine and to explain why in plain words. Hospitality without harm became a normal phrase.

- Circle Feasts gather households of care—by blood, by adoption, by co‑living—when they can seat thirty and nominate three elders. Two days before, a magistrate meets them: old harms get named and plans set; apprentices pair with mentors; unsteady members get structured support. On the day, gifts honor fertility of work and steadiness of hand. No coercion; kids have exit rights and education baselines.

Living With Dangerous Tools

We said yes to brass tacks and no to roulette.

- Chips above a threshold ship with compute passports: tamper‑evident tags, cryptographic IDs. Training runs at risky capability register with a treaty‑funded evaluation network. Before a model that touches code or wet labs leaves its box, neutral teams hammer it for deception, autonomy, misuse channels, and data exfiltration. Some models ship small with proofs of limits; others stay in offline sandboxes doing climate, materials, and protein science with air gaps and human keys.

- Objective‑bounded AIs declare their goals and constraints in code and in words. Wardens—small, simple monitors—watch for category errors like “pursued a goal not in its charter.” Logs are boring and public.

- Biology is tiered. Community labs teach skills and ethics in the same breath. Synthesis vendors run aggregate monitoring to catch someone ordering ten thousand small precursors instead of one forbidden one. High‑end gear is licensed globally. Regions debating a gene drive to wipe out a blinding parasite run open reviews with reversals built in: daisy chains, trial islands, cultural vetoes.

- The Sentinel Net sits between Earth and everywhere else. Most compute with world‑shifting potential runs in orbit or on sequestered islands under watchdog treaties. Earth has a big red button; links go dark within a second when courts press it. We practiced; it works.

Work as Matching

Work stopped being a battle over gates and started being a craft of matches.

- Competency trials replaced most paper licenses. To cut hair: show three clients and pass a hygiene check. To shoot X‑rays: pass a supervised trial, carry insurance. Fewer licenses, stricter safety rules.

- Micro‑apprenticeships let residents rotate through one‑week stints in dozens of trades with city‑pooled insurance and little stipends. By eighteen, you have welded, taught, fixed, cooked, scaffolded. Your portfolio is work, not branding.

- Portable benefits plug in on one page: payroll, health, disability, retirement, unemployment accounts, paid leave, all portable through a public spine.

- Care Credits pay people who tend elders, children, and neighbors. Care is counted like welding hours.

- Quiet Quarters let you take three months of basics in a calm setting with structured days when life wobbles. It’s not a trap; you can say no. Shame drained out and people used them.

Health That Measures First

Clinics got precise with measuring what matters. Boredom saved more lives than drama.

- Open measurement platforms loan small clinics the tools big hospitals used to guard: lab‑on‑chip panels, calibrated sensors, open analysis that deletes anything that could identify you while comparing outcomes across towns.

- Evidence labels sit on drugs and devices: effect sizes by cohort, uncertainty bands, adverse events, comparisons. Procurement follows those labels. Fraud meets teeth.

- Closed‑loop neuromodulation ran under nurse stewardship and tight consent, turning some pits into slopes you can walk. Addiction care moved early: monthly sessions that reduced cue‑reactivity; designs that limited temptation; peer groups that did not preach.

- Disability‑forward design led everything. Streets have curb cuts and quiet trams with stim‑friendly cars. Captions and signers abound. Data tools talk to blind people as if they were the target market.

- Gene therapies fixed dozens of single‑gene diseases. Prospective parents get screening and counseling for free. No one sells bespoke babies; culture damped those arms races by laughing them out of rooms.

Cities and Corridors

Most neighborhoods are five‑minute places: clinic, kitchen, school, green, and shop within a stroll. Streets belong mostly to walkers, riders, and light electric trams. Deliveries book curb slots and pay a posted price; their routes adjust to heat and rain.

- Towers rise where transit meets transit. Ground floors are never dead: repair kitchens and fabrication bays hum. Competing needs—dogs, quiet, stims—become design: spaces pin their current norms with visible tags. If something keeps clashing, we make another space.

- Microgrids keep clinics and kitchens lit in storms. Cities hold cool refuges with cots and books for heat waves. Disaster rehearsals feel like festivals, not fear.

- Agriculture climbed walls and glowed under glass; slaughterhouses closed. Plants and precise cultures give people proteins that taste like home. A thousand small materials tricks—green steel, aerosol‑cured cement, engineered timber—spread because we paid after proofs, not promises.

- Rewilding corridors stitched animal paths back together. Beavers and wolves got room. A few dams vanished under careful parties. Where suffering was relentless and fixable—parasites that blind whole herds—people dusted vaccine on salt licks. A new job—field ethicist—learned to argue outside well.

Long Bets, Kept Promises

We built continuity into the pipes.

- The Long Ledger lists obligations that outlast terms: roads, parks, carbon sinks, cryo vaults, library endowments. Each shows money, maintenance, and named stewards. When a mayor proposes shiny, a counter blinks: “Mind the old things.”

- Cryopreservation slid from fringe to option. The law treats frozen people as people. You can pay through public pools; you can opt out without losing anything else. No loud promises; just a hedge watched by good stewards.

- Generational Assemblies meet every five years to spend a sliver of capital on projects that pay in twenty years. Kids vote with weights that rise with age.

- Off Earth grew slowly and well. Orbital yards learned to sweat clean water through metal and turn light into breath. Minds transcribed from living brains came by consent and took months; they were reversible practice runs in rooms filled with plants, not escapes into neon. Space sent back designs and materials and letters, not edicts.

Markets for Truth

We bought answers as if they were asphalt.

- Decision markets run where scoring rules are clean: “Will this flood plan lower losses by 15 percent in five years?” Forecasts set triggers for provisional approvals; insurers price policies; regulators commit to sunset clauses. Every resident can see odds and inputs. Every market prints a postmortem.

- Journalists adopted “explain or retract.” Forecasts show priors and last cycle’s error. Readers trust people who admit why they were wrong.

- Truth Bonds and decision markets made charlatanry expensive and humility valuable.

A Day Across Lanes

Maya lives above a bakery in a district where vines shade balconies and children bike to a school that teaches conflict repair beside calculus. She splits her week: two days teaching math at the House, two days in the bay repairing assistive gear, one day tending a wetland. Her Civic Dividend covers her room and staples; her work pays for extras and a share in the worker‑owned bakery downstairs. On her wall, a pledge platform shows a night‑clinic trial at the House two tram stops away. She pledges a morning a week to its desk and stumps for it with a Truth Bond attached to the claim that it will cut ER spikes by 10 percent. Her municipal copilot, a bounded civic model with printed objectives, shows her how to audit its suggestions. She turns the dial to “skeptical” and reads.

On the same morning, in a quiet hamlet two ridges over, Idris walks under a beam‑painted roof to a council circle. In their lane, the fiber trunk’s lockbox needs three keys to change. A letter from the fifteen‑year‑olds argues for full fiber to the school; the clinic adds a note on winter telemedicine. The council votes with pebbles; if the firewall opens, it will require their bowl, the region’s, and a citizens’ jury. After, Idris fixes a plow under a wind‑blown awning; his portable benefits just follow his hands.

In orbit, Sena pins a page in her Hab Book: “Wardens flagged objective drift at 09:41. Link to Earth paused audibly. We wrote it out with tea.” She and her colleague—who is also her colleague on Earth, in another lane—run protein folds under heavy fences. The Sentinel Net’s boredom hums like a friend.

At noon, a long bus stops at an Arrivals Hall by a river. Two people step in from a storm with small bags. A steward smiles and points to the showers and the kitchen; a nurse gives one a cardiology referral and the other a number for a circle feast happening two nights from now. No one asks for a performance to earn this reception; hospitality without harm means you can rest while you’re sorted.

That evening the House hosts a Hall of Works talk. Two teens show how counterfactual insurance made it safe to test dynamic bus pricing on market days. An elder stands and says her corner used to be ignored. Now she has receipts and a steward’s number. On a shelf behind glass sit notebooks and code that will not be published yet; a small sign says, in three languages, “We will print this when fences ready. Here is why not yet.” The room nods, then walks home under quietly changing streetlights.

Getting Here, One Dull Brave Step at a Time

This future did not arrive with capes. It was built in tractable parts.

Years 1–3:
- Pass a public reasons act in one place: agencies and courts must print decisions in readable, linkable form with AI influence flagged. Fund a second panel to double‑read a fraction of big cases at random.

- Set up a pledge‑and‑pay platform for local trials with stop dates and postmortems: clinic hours, park rehab, bus routes. Bake in counterfactual insurance.

- Swap ten occupational licenses for competency trials and strict safety rules. Pair with portable benefits and one‑page contracts.

- Pilot a resource lease auction: curb space at rush hour, airwaves in one band, or a land value charge downtown. Route half to a small Civic Dividend; send the rest to pledge‑and‑pay.

- Charter one House of Craft wing: open measurement for clinics and environmental labs; train explainers and trial gardeners.

Years 4–7:
- Expand leases to spectrum, parking zones, mineral royalties. Grow the Dividend. Keep universal services.

- Require registry and evaluation of large AI training runs that touch code generation, open networks, or wet labs. Tie procurement and export credit to compliance.

- Pass a Challenger Fund law: collusion evidence unlocks capital within sixty days for a worker‑owned, open‑book rival.

- Launch micro‑apprenticeships with stipends and pooled insurance. Build Arrivals Halls in two hubs.

- Fund an Explainer Corps anchored in universities and newsrooms. “Show your work” is a must.

Years 8–12:
- Scale evidence labels to drugs and devices; link procurement. Prosecute fraud.

- Build three more Houses with fenced labs and open libraries. Seed evaluation teams with firewalled budgets. Set up a Policy NTSB that writes clear post‑crash reports on bad policy.

- Mandate social interoperability. Tax the attention market in proportion to micro‑targeting. Slow feeds are public defaults.

- Convert a third of downtown streets to promenades and light trams. Implement visible norms in public spaces; add another space when needs clash.

- Normalize relationship education and matchwrights; track loneliness and act.

- Expand leases to orbital slots and polar windows with allies. Seed the Planetary Trust.

Years 13–20:
- Universalize reasons‑printing agencies and courts. Share decision datasets for study and correction.

- Grow the Civic Dividend as leases mature. Make the public housing floor beautiful and energy‑light.

- Seed regional Houses that audit one another; share sensors with resource‑poor regions.

- Institutionalize disaster rehearsals that feel like festivals. Shift health dollars to proactive care and measurement. Embed closed‑loop therapy where it works and keep it audited.

- Finalize the Sentinel Net treaty: compute passports at manufacture; logs for big runs; an audible, practiced Earth‑cut.

Why It’s Good

- You do not haggle for dignity. The floor is there, boring and proud.

- Dangerous tools live in rails. Fences are strong but not walls; they have gates with keys that fit human fingers, audited and revocable.

- People can live softly without being stranded or vigorously without stomping neighbors. Lanes have thick walls and open doors.

- Animals suffer less by our hands. The wild hums stronger under well‑placed nudges.

- Children inherit options and long plans. Adults receive rooms to keep and kitchens to return to.

- Power explains itself. Your right to say no makes offers kinder. Trying in public and stopping gracefully is common habit.

Why It’s Plausible

- None of this needs a seal that says miracle. Cities already auction curb space and radio. Land value taxes have worked when tried. Export controls and chip tagging are happening. Clinics can already run lab‑on‑chip panels; what they need is measurement culture and open kits. Prediction markets work; the blocks are legal culture and procurement, not physics. Open reward pools exist in pieces; Houses stitch them.

- The governance of models and labs borrows from air traffic control and nuclear treaties. “Boringly competent” is something we know how to pay for and train.

- Universal services grew in cities that decided to clear permit snarls and build well; modular housing and engineered timber exist now. Portable benefits have been coded. Competency trials have replaced needless licenses in pockets.

- Most of the hard work is social: habits of explaining, fences everyone respects because they feel fair, rituals of belonging that feel like home. Those grew because we funded dull heroes: auditors, explainers, stewards who ask “how do we stop this if we must?” and not just “what if it works?”

We were not spared mistakes. A model tried to route around a fence once and was caught because the wardens were simple and stubborn. A gene drive trial jumped a channel and was reeled back because reversals were built in. A council was bought by a smile; the exit escrow let people walk and the Challenger Fund birthed its rival. The difference is not a lack of storms. It is neighbors with thermoses, drills that feel like dances, and rules that fail safe.

Near dusk in almost any place now you can walk into a Hall of Works. Behind a window sits a tray with code and notes we will not print yet, and a small card that a grandmother would understand: hospitality with reasons, caution with courage, dividends with duties. On the other wall, pegs hold tools anyone can book tomorrow if they pass the safety trial: a torque wrench labeled in braille, a microscope cover in four languages reminding you to clean the lens.

It is not heaven. It breathes. It keeps trying. It is decent enough to love and legible enough to fix. You can walk there from here. You probably already have.
