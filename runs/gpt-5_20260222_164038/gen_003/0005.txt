The Lattice and the Garden: A Utopia You Can Walk To

The turning point was embarrassingly small. One winter, school systems in three countries tried a new ninth-grade course called “How We Move Together.” It taught what once lived only in economics or game theory departments, but with playground examples and role-playing: how bad equilibria trap good people, how public goods get underfunded unless we agree to pay, how reputation can be a commons, how status games can be healthy or toxic. Kids learned to spot Nash traps the way earlier generations spotted typos. They ran little experiments, then bigger ones. By graduation they could redesign the cafeteria line to halve waiting times and had the political language to get their principal to try it.

It sounds trivial. It wasn’t. When a significant fraction of adults share a vocabulary for seeing coordination failures, they demand fixtures—durable, boring, reliable institutions—that let them repair those failures as easily as they fix a leaky pipe. Over two decades, our societies built a set of such fixtures and fitted them everywhere. The bundle became known as the Lattice.

The Lattice didn’t centralize everything. It did the opposite. It made it easy for neighborhoods, cities, professions, and online communities to choose clear rules among themselves, to try variants, to measure consequences, and to switch with dignity when one way proved better. It made exit and entry straightforward, recorded obligations transparently, and gave reputation teeth without turning it into tyranny.

Around that skeleton we planted the second half of our wager: the Garden. These were the social technologies and material novelties that made longer, happier, stranger, more beautiful lives: mental health as a first-class good; medicine that mostly prevents rather than mops up; work that looks like projects you pick; abundant time with people you love; the option to learn anything by doing it with someone who does it well; clean energy, clean meat, clean cities; art and sport and play that feel like your life, not your weekend. The Lattice made the Garden stable; the Garden made the Lattice worth keeping.

Here is that world, and the plausible way we got there.

1) Property as a Lease from Everyone, and the Dividends That Follow

We stopped pretending that nobody owns the Earth and also stopped pretending that one corporation should. Land, minerals, the radio spectrum, orbital slots, fishing grounds: any resource that gets value from scarcity rather than from anyone’s work is treated as a commons with the right to exclusive use auctioned in open, recurring leases. “Owning” a downtown block, a cobalt field, or part of the spectrum now means: you won the lease, you pay a price that reflects its current market value each year, and you can lose it if you stop paying or violate the terms you agreed to. There’s momentum for long-lived investments—build a hospital and you can’t be evicted on a whim—but you can’t sit on a parking lot a city needs as housing.

The rents flow into three places:

- A per-capita Common Dividend paid to every legal resident, no paperwork, every month.
- Local infrastructure funds that capture proximity value (if a new tram line doubles the value of adjacent leases, the line gets paid for).
- A Global Commons Trust that handles resources no city controls: satellite lanes, cyberspace addressing, oceanic carbon sinks.

The Common Dividend began modestly, layered over existing safety nets. As resource auctions expanded and carbon rights and spectrum rights were priced properly, it grew into a floor you could stand on. It didn’t replace services—you still get healthcare, education, and transit without a bill—but it gave every person liquid freedom: to walk away from a bad boss, to start a salon, to take six months to learn carpentry, to say no.

This is not a fantasy. Cities are piloting land value taxes. Spectrum auctions already exist. Port fees, mining royalties, and carbon pricing are familiar. We unified, simplified, and made them legible to citizens. The Common Dividend arrived as a politically neutral dividend from shared assets, not as charity.

2) Work as Matching, Not Gatekeeping

We dismantled a world where you prove you can do a job by paying for a degree that proves you can sit in rooms. Instead:

- Micro-apprenticeships: A directory lets you try “do the job for a week” stints across hundreds of trades and professions, with small stipends and insurance pooled by the city. A teenager can rotate through bakery, solar installation, phlebotomy, CAD design, paralegal work, and elder care before committing.
- Skill trials: Employers demonstrate tasks, then watch candidates do them. Licenses exist for safety-critical work, but they validate competence, not attendance. Unnecessary occupational licensing is treated like graffiti on an engine: scrubbed by default.
- Hot economy by design: Starting anything is easy. Incorporation, benefits pooling, payroll, basic compliance—one afternoon. Street-level kiosks help set up shop. With fewer artificial barriers, employers compete for workers. Abuse gets priced out because people have options and a floor.

The Common Dividend and Universal Services (healthcare, transit, connectivity, basic housing options) mean “no job” doesn’t mean “no life.” Many still choose to work, because work is also meaning and community. But now “work” looks more like projects:

- A Projects Market where individuals and teams post ideas with milestones; funding can come from customers, the city’s experiment funds, retroactive public goods prizes (more on those), or donations. Success means continuing; failure is data you can carry to your next pitch.
- Shorter default contracts with smooth off-ramps. Sabbaticals normalized. “Try us for six months” is as common as “hire for years.”

We did not discover that everyone wants to lie on a couch. We discovered that when your rent is not held to your throat, you are picky—and pickiness raises standards.

3) Knowledge Paid For After It Works, and Belongs To All

We replaced the worst of intellectual property monocultures with two mechanisms:

- Open-by-default licensing: If you publish an invention, model, or design for others to use, you can’t stop them from using it—no veto. In exchange, you or your firm become eligible for Retroactive Prizes funded by a standing Public Goods Fund. Independent auditors estimate the marginal value your work unlocked; a public board allocates awards. Think Nobel meets prize grant meets royalty, but paid after we can measure real impact. Early pilots of this “RPGF”—on drugs, open-source libraries, and climate tech—showed it attracts serious talent without blocking downstream use.
- Mandatory fair-use benchmarking: For technologies critical to life and safety—drugs, diagnostics, power electronics—“evidence labels” replace some prescriptive regulation. Third-party test labs publish standardized likelihoods (“reduces mortality by 13-20% in cohort X”), uncertainty bands, and adverse-effect profiles. Products carry a legible label like nutrition facts; procurement and insurance are tied to those labels. Fraud is prosecuted hard. Innovation speed rises because you can move fast without breaking consumers’ ability to tell what works.

Copyright exists but is short. We returned to roughly fourteen years with no renewals. People still make music and stories. They just enter the commons while their authors are still alive to enjoy their influence.

4) Justice That Explains Itself, and Heals When It Can

Courts no longer pronounce ex cathedra. They publish reasoned judgments, with the logic chain and the evidence used. Two independent courts are paid to evaluate every major case; disagreements auto-trigger a higher review. Judges are bought out of conflicts with large salaries and strict bans on side income. You can appeal by hiring an auditor to show that a previous reasoning step broke a shared standard. This is not law by algorithm; it is law that behaves like science where science can help.

Prisons shrank. For most nonviolent offenses, restitution and structured amends replace cages—mediated by professionals with teeth. For violence and genuine risk, secure care exists, but with clear progress paths and strong external oversight. The rate of re-offense dropped because people are healthier coming out than going in.

Police were split into four professions: responders (halt harm), investigators (build cases), mediators (defuse), and caretakers (manage those in crisis). Crossing those roles is a firing offense. Cameras point both ways: officers wear them and citizens have absolute rights to record. The result is less fear on both sides.

5) Media You Can Audit, and a Chronicle That Endures

We fund Long-Form Truth as a public good. A network of independent “Chroniclers” produces definitive explainers on contested topics: “What do we actually know about screen time and teen depression?” “Who benefits and who pays for the 3rd Avenue rezoning?” They cite, replicate, and update. Their code of practice is enforceable: quiet retractions cost them funding; deliberate distortion ends careers. They do not sell ads. Citizens rate clarity; peers rate epistemic hygiene; courts can be asked to compel a correction if methodology is clearly unsound. Universities and labs compete to host Chronicler bureaus as badges of pride.

Meanwhile, we taxed the attention economy’s arms races: differential ad taxes that hit targeting harder than context, interoperability mandates for social networks (you can take your friends and followers somewhere calmer), and “slow defaults” in public spaces and schools: by default, feeds are chronological and quiet; nudges toward outrage have to be toggled on, conspicuously. You can still swim in rapids. But if you fall in by accident, the current is gentle.

6) Matchmaking, Friendship, and the End of Quiet Desperation

We treated relationships as a skill and a right. Two pillars changed the emotional climate:

- Relationship education is as universal as math: communication, attachment, conflict repair, sexuality, consent, parenting, how to be a good friend. Not a few awkward assemblies; two years of thoughtful curriculum with practice, reflection, and coaching. Adult “refreshers” are common and proud.
- Professional matchwrights replaced swipe roulette. You tell a human your story and hopes; they talk to peers; if a match becomes a committed relationship that still exists at six months and one year, they get paid. The incentives are aligned with your long-term happiness, not your short-term clicks.

Loneliness clinics—really, communities—operate inside libraries, parks, and transit hubs: warm third places with staff trained in social scaffolding. People sign up for “lightlift groups”: small projects that matter enough to bring you back (build a bench; stage a tiny festival; assemble a neighborhood archive). No one is turned away for lack of polish. Cities track loneliness like they track air quality. If a district’s metrics go red, funds and organizers flow.

Consent cultures got deeper. Kink and sex work exist—in the open, not in the shadows—with clear norms that protect participants and friends who don’t want to be in that world. The baseline ethic is simple: keep pleasures from burning commons. So extreme visual manipulation and body-mod trends live in opt-in spaces; public spaces are for everyone’s eyes to feel at home. Tech helps: filters that normalize lighting and angles in fashion feeds are default-on; “glamour districts” exist for peacocking; most of the city is cozy-chic.

Children grow inside circles, not just households. Mutual adoption clubs—about twenty households each, formed voluntarily—share meals, babysitting, elder check-ins. Members take a light, explicit oath: if your life goes sideways, the club steps in. It’s not forced commune; it’s formalizing what good neighbors do, so nobody falls between cracks.

7) Health That Starts in the Head and Ends With an Open Future

We stopped accepting that pain’s worst extremes were necessary. New analgesics and neuromodulators narrow the ceiling of suffering without dulling all signals: your foot still lets you know it’s on fire; your mind stops falling into inescapable pits. Psychedelic therapies, carefully guided, help many rewrite old loops. Addiction is treated early and as a design problem: life architectures that reduce temptation, AI stewards that flag risk patterns, communities that close ranks to protect rather than punish.

Gene therapies cure dozens of single-gene diseases. Polygenic selection exists but sits behind thick ethical glass: strong norms, informed consent, equity safeguards. We leaned toward editing to prevent disease and lift health, not stacking personality traits to order. Counseling centers for prospective parents are free, no-judgment zones; they answer instead of preach. People with disabilities live visibly rich lives; the world altered itself—curb cuts, signers, quiet rooms—rather than expecting individuals to fit a template.

Memory gets aids that feel like part of you: encrypted lifelogs you can search as you would your mind, with privacy controls you can explain to a grandparent. Brain-computer interfaces remain medical until they’re as safe as a pacemaker. Direct “meeting of minds,” when it arrived, was mostly used by therapists, musicians, and advanced calculus study groups. It stayed opt-in, intensely supervised at first, then integrated into trusted circles. The best surprise: empathy, not groupthink. Most people’s selves got stronger from seeing how different others were, not weaker.

8) Cities That Feel Like Villages and Work Like Networks

Streets belong to people. Most neighborhoods are car-light or car-free, with quiet electric trams at edges, delivery robots you barely notice, and dense, gorgeous housing that filters light like forests. Every block is a five-minute city: food, clinic, childcare, workshop, parklet. Towers aren’t banned; they come where transit meets transit, and their first floors are never dead.

“Competing access needs” are handled as design, not arguments:

- Spaces are tagged with their current norms: “Dog-free hours 9-11,” “Quiet chat zone,” “Stim-welcome car.” Sensors adjust signage as bookings change.
- The city’s calendar balances allocations. If a need keeps clashing, the fix is a new space, not new scolding.
- Schools and workplaces schedule neurodiverse days as routinely as dental checkups.

Rewilding corridors braid between towns, letting animals migrate and kids remember what wonder feels like at six in the morning. Agriculture moved up buildings and under LED roofs; slaughterhouses closed, replaced by cultured meat and plant proteins that taste like home.

9) The Peace That Comes From Boring Military Deterrence and Exciting Political Experiments

Nations still exist, but power moved downward and sideways. The Lattice favors federations of cities and professions over monolithic states. People belong to multiple compacts: your city, your trade guild, your science commons, your resource federation. Each has a constitution, a transparent budget, and a method for yelling if norms are being burned. Entry is typically a three-click pact; exit is a well-trod path.

Policy doesn’t ossify. The Experimental Charter lets any city, school district, or hospital propose a different way—basic income flavor here, policing model there, school schedules elsewhere—provided they commit to evaluation metrics agreed upfront and rights that don’t bend (speech, due process, bodily autonomy, freedom of movement). If it works, neighboring places copy. If it fails, the data are as honored as a success.

War didn’t vanish because humans changed. It receded because we made conquest unprofitable and illegitimate. Defensive alliances present credible, precise, non-escalatory means to make aggression a losing game; automated deterrents that can’t be repurposed for offense sit in the open with their logic audited by international teams. Offense is expensive PR: if you break the peace, your products and professionals don’t travel. It turns out a planet that can see each other’s budgets and carbon accounts can also see who started it.

10) AI Without Roulette

We did the scary thing: we tapped the brakes.

- Compute governance: Chips above a certain training threshold carry “compute passports” signed by manufacturers, logged to an international registry. Training runs over that threshold require a license. Black-market compute exists, as black markets do, but is expensive and dangerous to use. Most talent stays in above-board labs because that’s where the tools and prestige live.
- Model evaluation: Independent “red rooms” probe systems for deception, situational awareness, goal misgeneralization. Release decisions weigh concrete capabilities against concrete alignments achieved. Some models ship small and safe; others stay in sandboxes that earn their keep doing research for climate, materials, and biology without internet access.
- Alignment science: The smartest kids of a generation chose to work on making minds that don’t break rather than making minds that make money. They did this because their schools told them what was at stake, because early career paths were funded and respected, and because anyone can still get rich in green steel or mRNA hospitals and not everything has to be software.

Was this global and flawless? No. It was piecemeal at first: compute passports adopted by five manufacturers, then ten; national registries that federated; treaties that took years. But every step made it more normal for leaders to say “not yet” to particular classes of risky experiments, and to be reelected after they did.

11) Culture, Status, and the Right to Be Strange

We didn’t try to abolish status. We multiplied it. Instead of one pyramid in each city (who’s richest? who’s prettiest?), we built a thousand pedestals with wheels. The city rotates festivals of honor: the year’s best caregivers get the front page, then the year’s cleverest welders, then the best five-person street-band, then the high schooler who redesigned a playground to include wheelchair tag. This isn’t participation trophy soup; there are juries and criteria; but because something different shines every month, no one kind of person gets to claim they’re the person.

We also let subcultures ringfence their norms. If you want to wear Victorian suits and play hurdy-gurdy, there’s a neighborhood that leans in. If you want to raise your kids in a polyglot, screens-low, forest school microdistrict, you can—so long as your kids’ exit rights and education baselines are guaranteed. If you want to sign a covenant banning cars on your block forever, you can; the city records and enforces it; potential buyers see it before they bid. Diversity stops being an argument and becomes a map.

12) The Path From Here

This future is not a moonshot. It’s a thousand crossings of a street most cities already live on. Here is a credible route:

Year 1-3:
- Put “How We Move Together” into fifty pilot schools. Mix civics, game theory, mediation, and practical projects. Measure peer cohesion and local improvements.
- Start two lease-based resource auctions in one city—downtown parking minimums replaced with land-value taxes on a corridor; a public auction of curbspace for delivery robots; a radio spectrum pilot that funds a neighborhood dividend.
- Establish a city Chronicler office with universities and local newsrooms; fund it with a 0.1% slice of the capital budget.
- Stand up a Projects Market with small city match funding. Pair it with micro-apprenticeships; underwrite insurances.
- Launch an OpenTrials label for one medical device type in one hospital system.

Year 4-8:
- Expand land-value-based leasing to more parcels; retire nuisance licensing; simplify business start-up.
- Pass national short copyright.
- Create a public Retroactive Prize Fund seeded by philanthropy and later taxes. Award the first big one to the open insulin protocol, the little one to the urban algae crew that cut inlet nitrates in half.
- Roll out city-level relationship education for adults with childcare and dinner. Pay matchwrights from philanthropic pools at first, then insurers (relationships are shock absorbers for health shocks).
- Pilot courts that publish reasoned judgments with two-key verdicts on civil cases. Spin up an Appeal by Audit mechanism.

Year 9-15:
- Federate compute registries across allied manufacturers; require compute passports for chips receiving export credits. Create independent model eval teams with ring-fenced budgets.
- Make social network interoperability law; tax hyper-targeted ads.
- Grow land, spectrum, and carbon lease revenues until the Common Dividend becomes noticed even by skeptics. Keep services universal.
- Legalize and regulate sex work with worker-led safety standards; bring kink into the open with consent protocols anchored by law and culture.
- Convert a third of downtown streets to car-light promenades; stand up competing-use schedulers; normalize stimulus-friendly and quiet zones.

Year 16-25:
- Internationalize lease models for seabed mining, orbital slots, and polar shipping lanes; dividend a piece to the Global Commons Fund.
- Export Chronicler standards; treat attacks on truth infrastructure as attacks on critical infrastructure.
- Reform prisons into restorative justice and secure care; break police into four roles; retrain with the social-tech generation now becoming chiefs.
- Triple the Climate Prize side of retroactive funding; decarbonize steel and cement with open process patents prized after deployment.
- Teach ninth-grade coordination in most countries; watch the generation whose baseline assumption is “if it’s broken, it’s probably a coordination failure” begin to run cities.

At every step, iterate. The Lattice only works if it evolves. The Garden only grows if plants fail and are replaced. The binding promise is not perfection; it’s that our fixtures will be auditable, reversible, and copyable, and that we will love them for that, not despite it.

13) What It Feels Like

The vibrations of this world are small and continuous, not grand and singular. You notice them when the bus always seems to arrive just when you do, because neighbors chose the route and stop times to match their lives. You notice when a cousin who once spiraled after a layoff instead spends three months at a community workshop, builds beehives, gets a micro-grant, and starts a little business because that whole path had been prefabbed. You notice when elections are calmer because the most important fights happen year-round in citizens’ assemblies that talk like adults, whose agendas you can subscribe to like a show. You notice when a friend whose depression once devoured her now has tools that interrupt the fall, a therapist who can literally show her how a thought flowed, and a neighborhood contract that keeps the night café open later in winter.

You notice that funerals are rarer; not because we conquered death, but because we conquered many stupid ways of dying. You notice that meeting a stranger feels less like sizing up a rival and more like testing how your narratives can click. You notice that your favorite band exists because someone you will never meet received a prize for a sensor that stabilizes cheap violins, and someone else got an award after the fact for reviving an old dance that makes shy people brave.

Some people live in virtual worlds for chunks of time; they’re the exquisitely designed kind that encourage you to come back to reality and build the thing you saw. Uploading is research, not routine. It may become a path later; if it does, the Lattice will hold it while we all look.

A good society is not the absence of badness; it is the presence of abundant, legible, shared tools for making things better. This one has them. It did not get them from saints or superintelligences. It got them from the mundane heroism of people who learned, in school and life, to spot where we were stepping on each other’s feet, to name it, and to try a different dance.

The Lattice keeps the beat. The Garden is why we dance at all. If you like, we can start with a class next fall, a pilot land lease the following spring, a Projects Market by summer, and the first Chronicler’s explainer before winter ends. If that sounds too slow, remember: slow is easy to keep doing. And easy to keep doing is how you get to the future and feel at home when you arrive.
