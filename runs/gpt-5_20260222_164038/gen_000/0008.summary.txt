## Type of utopia described: “Paretotopian” (Pareto-improving) abundance-enabled cooperation

### Core defining feature
- A future where **AI-enabled capability drives extremely large increases in available resources** (up to orders of magnitude, e.g., **1000×**), transforming many social and geopolitical interactions from **zero-sum rivalry** into **positive-sum, mutually beneficial bargaining**.
- It is “utopian” in the specific sense that it aims for **Pareto-like improvements**: **almost everyone becomes better off and almost no one is worse off**, relative to today.

### Resource environment
- **From fixed to expanding “resource pie”**: The classic assumption that resources are fixed (so one party’s gain is another’s loss) becomes less relevant if total resources rapidly expand.
- **Abundance on a decision-relevant timescale**: Not slow historical growth (~3.5%/year) but **transformative expansion within the horizon of current decision-makers**, making cooperation rational now.
- **Diminishing marginal value**: Because additional resources matter less once basic and many luxury needs are met, the **utility difference between “a big share” and “a bigger share” shrinks** under conditions of vast abundance.

### How conflict declines (incentive structure)
- **Reduced payoff to “greed”**: When the total future pie is enormous, the incremental advantage of capturing 90% instead of 50% becomes relatively less valuable compared to:
  - the benefit of ensuring the expansion happens at all, and
  - securing any substantial stake in it.
- **Bigger “cooperation space”**: Massive expansion creates many outcomes where **both sides can gain simultaneously**, so negotiation has more mutually acceptable endpoints.
- **Conflict becomes irrational due to risk**: Attempts to grab disproportionate shares increase the chance of resistance and escalation. If conflict risks catastrophic losses, then:
  - the **expected value of aggressive strategies drops**, because the marginal gain from extra dominance is outweighed by downside risk.

### Social/political character of the utopia
- **Not equality-first, but “everyone much better off”**:
  - It does not require equal shares.
  - It claims that even with persistent inequality, **absolute living standards can rise for everyone**, reducing the stakes of distributional fights.
- **“Fair-enough” coordination**:
  - Cooperation is stabilized by a practical notion of fairness: deals perceived as reasonably fair are more likely to succeed and avoid conflict.
  - Because the pie is huge, **disputes over the exact fairness criterion matter less**.
- **Strategic alignment among competitors**:
  - Actors (individuals, firms, states) remain distinct and self-interested, but their **best strategies increasingly overlap**: support expansion, manage shared risks, and avoid destabilizing grabs.

### Economic and technological substrate
- **AI as a general resource multiplier**: The premise is that AI-driven capabilities can unlock vast new production, automation, discovery, and coordination capacity.
- **Greater role for non-rivalrous goods**:
  - As information goods and services become more central (software, knowledge, designs), many valuable outputs can be shared at near-zero marginal cost.
  - This further reduces direct competition over scarce physical resources.

### Psychological/behavioral assumptions
- **Satiation effects**:
  - Many wants are satiable, so after reaching very high consumption levels, additional gains yield less motivation for conflict.
- **Perspective matters over wealth scales**:
  - The text emphasizes that standard “log utility” models may miss how people evaluate life-changing vs. absurdly large gains; still, the core claim remains that **relative valuation compresses** under extreme abundance.

### Remaining tensions acknowledged (utopia is conditional, not absolute)
- **Positional/status goods**:
  - People may still care about relative standing, which can preserve rivalry.
  - However, the argument is that high absolute abundance can make status competition less harmful because baseline wellbeing is universally high.
- **Power-as-a-goal remains dangerous**:
  - Desire for control over others is not automatically solved by abundance and links to deeper security dilemmas.
- **Security and defensive stability are crucial**:
  - For the utopia to hold, it must be true that even if one actor has far more resources, this does not inherently threaten others (i.e., **defensive stability can exist despite asymmetry**).
  - The text treats this as essential and requiring further work.

### Governance and policy implications (what institutions in this utopia aim to do)
- **Shift strategy from division to expansion**:
  - Prioritize policies that increase total capability and shared prosperity rather than fight over slices.
- **Risk-management cooperation**:
  - Since conflict could destroy the gains, actors have aligned incentives to cooperate on preventing catastrophe (war, destabilization).
- **Option-awareness as a stabilizer**:
  - Simply understanding that “Paretotopian” outcomes are possible can change perceived interests and drive more cooperative planning.

### Overall “utopian” endpoint described
- A world where **AI-enabled abundance makes many traditional conflicts over resources no longer worth fighting**, because:
  - there is enough growth that most actors can be better off simultaneously,
  - greed yields diminishing returns,
  - and conflict risk makes aggressive capture strategies unattractive.
- The utopia is therefore best characterized as a **cooperation-through-abundance equilibrium**: a stable alignment of incentives toward broadly shared gains rather than zero-sum competition.
