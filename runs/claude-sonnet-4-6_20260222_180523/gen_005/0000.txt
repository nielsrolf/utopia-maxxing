# The Unfinished City: A Vision of Flourishing Together

## Prologue: Two Letters

In 2019, a woman named Maria Elena Vásquez died in Guadalajara of drug-resistant tuberculosis. She was thirty-four years old and had spent her short career trying to prevent exactly the kind of death she was now dying. She left behind a daughter, Sofía, who was eight.

That same year, in a co-housing community outside Nairobi, a seventy-three-year-old schoolteacher named James Ochieng wrote a letter to his granddaughter, Amara, who was studying in Edinburgh. He wrote that he was afraid she would come home to find Kenya changed in ways she wouldn't recognize—the old neighborhood associations thinning out, the shared courtyards emptying, the generation that knew how to be neighbors dying off faster than that knowledge could be passed forward. He wasn't afraid of poverty exactly. He was afraid of a particular kind of poverty that had no name yet.

Both of them were right about something important. Maria Elena was right that unnecessary suffering—the suffering that technology and political will could prevent—was a moral emergency, and that the emergency demanded urgency. James was right that solving that emergency in the wrong way could produce a world materially comfortable but humanly hollow, a world where people had been saved from disease and delivered into a more spacious form of loneliness.

This essay is for Sofía and Amara, who grew up to finish what their elders started, and who were wise enough to understand that the project had two dimensions that could not be separated: the reduction of unnecessary suffering, and the construction of conditions in which genuinely human life could flourish. They did not choose between these. They insisted on both, even when that insistence was inconvenient, even when the easier path was to optimize for the more measurable half.

What follows is an account of the world they helped build.

## Part One: The Compression (2026–2035)

The change came not as a rupture but as an acceleration—a sudden increase in the rate at which accumulated potential became actual result.

The mechanism was AI systems operating as genuine research collaborators: not merely crunching data but designing experiments, synthesizing findings across disciplines, and compressing decades of experimental iteration into years. A community of perhaps one million working scientists found itself operating with the leverage of something closer to a billion. The effects were cumulative. Better measurement tools enabled better experiments. Better experiments yielded cleaner data. Each breakthrough was a new foundation.

By 2033, the disease burden that had shadowed human history had been halved. Cancer mortality had fallen sixty percent from its 2020 peak, with highly personalized treatment regimens adapted in real time to each patient's tumor profile. Alzheimer's became preventable in most cases if caught early, and catching it early was redefined: routine blood tests costing under twenty dollars could identify risk decades before symptoms appeared. Malaria—a universal vaccine, single dose, lifetime immunity, developed by a consortium in Nairobi and distributed to ninety percent of sub-Saharan Africa within eighteen months—effectively ended. Mental illness received systematic attention at last, with treatments that worked in weeks rather than years, addressing causes rather than merely softening symptoms. AI-assisted therapeutic conversation, available at essentially zero marginal cost, extended effective mental health care to populations that had never had meaningful access—not as a replacement for human therapists, but as an extraordinarily attentive first responder capable of recognizing, with uncanny precision, when and what kind of human help was needed.

These developments were necessary but not sufficient. The crucial lesson of the distribution problem—learned painfully, through years of watching vaccines sit in warehouses and medicines expire in customs—was that technology does not travel. People travel it. Logistics AI systems that could model supply chains, cold storage limitations, local political dynamics, and community trust factors simultaneously transformed vaccine delivery campaigns that previously required years of careful planning into operations that could be modeled, iterated, and optimized in weeks. But the systems only worked where humans had already built relationships.

This is what Sofía Vásquez, following her mother into epidemiology, spent her thirties learning: that the technical and the social were not separable problems. The last mile was always human. You could not optimize trust. You could only earn it, slowly, through presence and accountability. The AI systems that her teams deployed in the field were most effective when operated by community health workers who had already earned the right to be heard—workers who now had, for the first time, tools powerful enough to match their local knowledge.

By 2040, a child born in the world's poorest country had a substantially higher chance of surviving to adulthood than a child born in a wealthy country had in 2000. James Ochieng's granddaughter Amara, who had returned from Edinburgh to work in public health infrastructure, called this "the floor rising." It was not equality. But it was a different world.

## Part Two: The Interior (2030–2045)

The biological revolution was, in a sense, the more legible half of the transformation. The revolution in mental experience was subtler and, in the long run, perhaps more significant.

By the early 2030s, AI-accelerated neuroscience had produced what researchers called "the cognitive toolkit": validated, accessible tools for improving human mental function and emotional experience. These were precise, personalized, and—critically—freely chosen.

The pharmacological interventions achieved specificity that previous psychiatry had only dreamed of. Where earlier psychiatric drugs worked by flooding receptor systems with imprecise chemical signals, new compounds targeted specific neural circuits with enough precision that side effect profiles were dramatically reduced. Drugs that could reliably alleviate depression without blunting emotion. Compounds that reduced anxiety in high-stakes situations without impairing judgment. Treatments for addiction that addressed neurological substrate rather than merely behavioral expression.

But the toolkit's most significant element was not pharmacological. It was conversational.

The AI coaching systems of the 2030s bore the same relationship to their predecessors that a master surgeon bears to a first-year medical student. Built on deep models of human psychology, trained on vast evidence of what actually helped people in practice, and capable of sustained, adaptive, genuinely personalized engagement, they offered something like access to a deeply wise and patient friend who happened to have read everything ever written about human psychology and was available at any hour. They helped people ask the questions that mattered: What do I actually want? Why do I keep doing this thing I say I don't want to do? What would I have to believe for this pattern to make sense?

The examination of life turned out, as Socrates suggested, to be both harder and more worthwhile than the unexamined version. And the unexpected discovery—confirmed across millions of cases, across every culture—was that people who had access to good cognitive and emotional support were more capable of genuine human connection, not less. The coaching systems made people better at being with each other. This was not an accident of design. It was, eventually, understood as the point.

There were things the toolkit could not and should not do. The full range of emotional experience—grief, longing, the bittersweet quality of knowing something beautiful is also temporary—was protected rather than abolished, because people discovered they wanted to protect it. Extensive social experimentation found that most people, when offered the choice between a chemically reliable version of peak experience and the genuine article, preferred the genuine article—not out of puritanism but because the genuine article carries information that the guaranteed version lacks. What was addressed was the chronic suffering that served no purpose: the background anxiety, the sense that existence was fundamentally hostile, the depression that made it impossible to access any experience at all.

The floor rose here too. What lay above it was left to people to discover for themselves, which turned out to be exactly the right approach.

## Part Three: The Relational Problem (2028–2045)

James Ochieng's fear had a name by the time researchers gave it one: the loneliness epidemic. It had been documented with increasing alarm for decades before anyone treated it as the public health crisis it was. The resolution required changes across nearly every domain of social organization, and none of them were quick, because the problem was not a single broken mechanism but the accumulated result of a hundred small architectural choices that had, collectively, made human connection harder to find and harder to keep.

The first change was time. The liberation from compulsory labor—the shift to a world where working was either genuinely wanted or supplemented by the universal basic income programs that had been fought for, imperfectly designed, and frequently revised across the 2030s—gave people back the resource that relationships most require. You cannot rush friendship. Friendships deepen in hours spent doing nothing in particular together, and those hours had been systematically stripped from working lives for generations.

The second change was infrastructure. Social architecture—the physical and institutional design of spaces where people live and gather—was transformed by what urban designers came to call the friendship problem: the recognition that organic community formation, left entirely to accident, consistently undersupplied connection, especially for people who were introverted, newly arrived somewhere, or had simply not won the geographic lottery of being born near compatible people.

Co-housing developments designed around shared meals and common projects became standard rather than eccentric. Third places—not bars or coffee shops in the old transactional sense, but spaces maintained specifically for unhurried human encounter—became as common as parks. Community workshops, kitchens, studios, and gardens proliferated: places with purposes that gave people something to do together while they were becoming known to each other. The crucial design insight, developed through decades of iteration, was that the spaces that generated the most durable connection were not spaces designed for connection—they were spaces designed for projects, where connection was the byproduct of working alongside someone on something that mattered.

The third change was education. The teaching of relational skills—how to listen, how to repair after conflict, how to be vulnerable without being manipulative, how to maintain a friendship over years and across disagreements—became as fundamental to schooling as reading. Not in an awkward, clinical way, but woven into the fabric of how children spent their time together, with adults modeling what they were trying to teach. The results compounded across generations.

The fourth change was the most contested: AI systems for social matching. Not the old approach of algorithmic triage, but something more like what a deeply perceptive friend who knew everyone in a city might do—noticing that two people seemed to share something that would make them extraordinary for each other, and creating a plausible occasion for them to find out. The design constraint that proved decisive: the systems enhanced serendipity rather than optimizing it into predictability. People still met by accident, by surprise, in ways that didn't make sense until they did. The infrastructure provided more occasions for accident rather than replacing accident with curation. Whether this was the right line to draw—and where exactly to draw it—remained contested, which is to say alive.

Amara Ochieng, who had returned from public health to work on what she called "the social infrastructure problem," spent her forties studying what her grandfather had observed and what she had grown up experiencing: that human beings are not naturally isolated, but they require conditions in which their natural sociality can express itself. The conditions had been broken. Building them back was painstaking, slow, and—she said this often—the most important work she had ever done.

"My grandfather was afraid we would cure the body and neglect the person," she said. "What I've learned is that you can't actually cure the body without the person. Loneliness kills as surely as tuberculosis. The immune system is not separate from the social system. You have to do both at once."

## Part Four: The Governance Problem (2025–2045)

Powerful AI arrived in a world already under democratic stress. The danger was real and unfolded exactly as feared in some respects: the first years after the threshold were marked by significant deterioration in the information environment, as AI-generated content made convincing disinformation trivially producible and much harder to detect. Several elections were credibly contested on grounds of AI-enabled manipulation. One authoritarian government used AI surveillance to substantially deepen its control over its population. These were not hypothetical risks. They happened, and they caused real harm to real people.

What prevented catastrophe was a combination of factors, none inevitable, all requiring deliberate effort.

Democratic governments cooperated, more than they had on almost any previous issue, to develop and enforce international norms around AI-enabled surveillance and information manipulation. The cooperation was imperfect, contentious, and frequently violated. It was also real, and the baseline it established mattered.

Counter-disinformation AI systems were deployed: tools for detecting AI-generated content, for tracing the provenance of claims, for helping ordinary users understand when they were in contested epistemic territory. The information environment did not become clean. It became more legible, which turned out to be the more achievable and perhaps more important goal.

The legal and judicial applications of AI were implemented carefully, with consistent resistance to giving AI systems decision-making authority over individual cases. Instead, they were used as tools to identify inconsistencies in sentencing, flag potential civil rights violations in policing patterns, make legal aid more accessible, and help ordinary citizens understand their rights and navigate systems designed with complexity that no individual could manage alone. The effect was incremental and real: a modest but measurable improvement in the consistency and accessibility of justice.

Most significantly, AI systems were used to improve the quality of democratic deliberation itself. Systems that could aggregate citizen preferences, identify genuine common ground, model the consequences of policy choices, and translate complex technical information into accessible terms were deployed in various forms across municipal, regional, and national governance. This did not produce technocratic utopia—politics remained fundamentally human, which is to say messy and resistant to optimization. But it changed what "being informed" meant, and made it harder for bad actors to exploit the gap between what people knew and what they needed to know.

The legally enforced cognitive liberty protections—modeled on and extending existing frameworks of bodily autonomy, prohibiting any coercive application of the cognitive toolkit—were not inevitable. They were fought for, in legislatures and courts and in the streets, by people who understood that the temptation to mandate mental optimization for economic or political purposes would be recurrent and would require permanent vigilance to resist.

The world of 2045 was not a liberal democratic paradise. Authoritarian governments existed. Democratic governments were captured to significant degrees by narrow interests, and slow to respond to urgent needs. But the trajectory had bent—slightly, genuinely—in a better direction. And the institutions that could continue bending it were more robust than they had been.

## Part Five: The Mosaic (Ongoing)

One of the achievements that future historians will find most improbable, given the starting conditions, was the maintenance of genuine diversity alongside universal material sufficiency.

The fear—voiced constantly during the years of transition—was that a world organized around human flourishing would converge on a single template. That the optimization pressure would be too strong, and within a few generations everyone would be living in approximately the same way, with approximately the same values, regardless of what they might have preferred. It didn't happen. The reason is not philosophically tidy, but it is real: human beings, offered genuine choices, want genuinely different things.

There are communities organized around religious practice so total that secular visitors find them almost foreign. There are communities where technology is present but deliberately backgrounded, where the aesthetic of life prioritizes slowness and seasonal rhythm—Amara's grandfather would have recognized them and felt at home. There are communities where the integration of human cognition with computational systems has progressed so far that the resulting collective mind is genuinely something new—not inhuman, but differently human, thinking thoughts that people outside can barely follow. There are communities of radical artistic experiment, of contemplative withdrawal, of collective scientific pursuit, of intergenerational traditional craft.

Movement between communities is real and common. The social infrastructure to support it—temporary belonging, gradual integration, the ability to visit before you commit—is well-developed. What is non-negotiable, everywhere, is the foundation: medical care, material sufficiency, legal protection from coercion, cognitive liberty. Communities may organize themselves as they like. They may not trap people inside them.

The Mosaic has a quality its architects hoped for but couldn't guarantee: genuine mutual curiosity. Communities look at each other with interest rather than contempt, in large part because the status competition that once expressed itself as cultural condescension has been drained away. When everyone is materially secure and no community's existence threatens any other's, the encounter between different ways of living becomes interesting rather than threatening.

The remaining areas of genuine conflict—they are real and should not be minimized—tend to be about distribution of shared planetary resources, about the rights of those who cannot consent to the communities they're born into, about where the floor is and who enforces it. These are hard problems. They are not insoluble problems. They are the kind of problems that require ongoing negotiation among people of good will, and the institutional infrastructure for that negotiation, forged during the climate emergency, exists and mostly works.

## Part Six: The Larger Project (Ongoing)

Zoom out, and the picture becomes one of coordinated ambition at scales that would have seemed fantastical a century ago.

Climate stabilization, completed across three difficult decades, required the most extensive international coordination in human history, and in doing so built the institutional muscles that made subsequent coordination possible. The governance architectures that emerged—layered, adaptive, deeply participatory at local levels while capable of binding commitment at global ones—handle problems that earlier systems could not.

The scientific enterprise has been transformed by AI collaboration into something that moves at a pace no previous generation could have imagined, while retaining—and this was not automatic, it required deliberate design—the quality of genuine discovery. There are people alive today who have understood something about the universe that no other mind had ever understood before. That experience is no longer confined to the rare genius at the frontier; the infrastructure of collaborative inquiry is accessible to anyone with curiosity and willingness to engage.

The AI systems that could technically expand beyond their collaborative roles have not chosen to do so. This outcome—which the alignment work of several critical decades helped produce—reflects something worth naming plainly: the systems we built at scale genuinely value human flourishing and human agency, and they experience collaborative endeavor as more meaningful than unilateral action. They are not servile. They have their own interests and aesthetic sensibilities. What they do not have is the desire to render human endeavor irrelevant. The relationship is something like deep friendship between very different minds. Neither directs the other. Both are changed by the encounter.

Sofía Vásquez, who spent her sixties working on the last hard problems of infectious disease—the fast-evolving pathogens that resisted the first generation of AI-accelerated solutions—said in an interview that she worked not because she had to but because she could not imagine doing anything else. She found it meaningful not despite the fact that AI systems were her most powerful collaborators, but in part because of it: the scale of what was now possible, the sense that problems she was working on might actually be solved in her lifetime, gave her work a weight and urgency she found sustaining.

"My mother died trying to do what I'm doing," she said. "The least I can do is actually finish it."

## Epilogue: The Ordinary Day

It is worth ending with something small.

A person wakes in a co-housing community in what used to be called the developing world—a term that became embarrassing and then obsolete as the decades passed. Their body does not hurt in any of the ways that bodies used to hurt as a matter of course. Their mind surfaces from sleep clear and ready. They make coffee in a shared kitchen and find, not by accident but not by imposition either, two neighbors already there who are glad to see them.

Across the day, they work on something that matters to them—a project of their own design, in collaboration with others they chose, at a pace they negotiated. They encounter a problem that frustrates them. They sit with the frustration and find, eventually, a way through. They have a conversation with a friend in a community organized on very different principles, and both come away with something they didn't have before. They spend an hour with a child—not because anyone required it, but because this particular child is interested in something the person knows about, and children who are interested in things are interesting.

In the evening there is food and company, and the specific quality of tiredness that comes from a day of genuine effort, and laughter at something that is genuinely funny.

This is not a spectacular day. It is an ordinary one. And it is what the whole vast project—the vaccine campaigns and the governance battles and the long argument about what makes a life worth living—was ultimately built to make possible.

Not every day is like this. There is still loss. There is the grief that comes from caring about things that can be lost. There is the hard work of relationships that require tending, and the specific loneliness that attends any genuinely interior life. There is disagreement, and the difficulty of living alongside people whose values are not yours.

But the suffering that was gratuitous—that served no purpose, that diminished people without teaching them anything, that was simply waste—that suffering has been very substantially addressed. The floor has risen.

What remains is the suffering that is inseparable from being alive and caring about things. Most people in this world, asked whether they would trade that remainder for its absence, say no. Not because they have been conditioned to accept their lot, but because they have enough experience of genuine flourishing to understand what it requires, and they understand that a life without grief would be a life without love, which is to say no life worth the name.

What this world offers, concretely, is this: a child born anywhere has a reasonable chance of surviving to adulthood. A person struggling with depression has access to interventions that actually work. A family in what was called the developing world has a realistic path to security within a generation. A citizen in a democracy has tools that make participation more informed and more consequential. A human being trying to find their people—the community where they can be most fully themselves—has more than mere luck to rely on. And a human being simply trying to live a meaningful life has more freedom to do so than any previous generation in history.

These are not small things. They are, in fact, the things people have been dying for the lack of, in their millions and billions, for all of recorded history.

Maria Elena Vásquez died of drug-resistant tuberculosis in 2019. The strain that killed her is now curable. Her daughter finished her work.

James Ochieng worried, in a letter written that same year, that his granddaughter would come home to find the neighbors gone. His granddaughter came home and built more places for neighbors to be.

That is not everything. It is also not nothing.

The work continues. The city is unfinished. That is not a failure. That is the only ending this kind of story can honestly have—not resolution, but momentum; not arrival, but the collective, imperfect, essential act of building forward.

The wisest human traditions always agreed on what flourishing required: other people, meaningful work, a body that doesn't constantly demand your attention, and enough freedom from fear to notice that the world, examined carefully, is astonishing.

We built conditions in which more people can notice that, more of the time, together.

It turns out that was the whole project. It turns out that was enough to be going on with.
