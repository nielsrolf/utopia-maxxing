# A World We Made Ourselves

## A Utopia for the Long Human Future

---

There was no single morning when humanity woke up and realized things had become genuinely good. The shift was gradual, uneven, and—like most real things—messier than anyone had planned. But there was a decade, somewhere in the middle of the twenty-first century, that later historians would mark as a hinge: the period when enough pieces fell into place that progress began to compound rather than stall. When the good news stopped being the exception and started being the rule.

This is a description of the world that eventually emerged from that hinge—not a blueprint handed down from above, but an account of what human ingenuity and accumulated wisdom actually produced, given enough time and enough willingness to take the right problems seriously.

---

### The Material Foundation

The first thing to understand about this world is that it is genuinely, materially abundant. Not wealthy in the way that meant some people had yachts while others scraped by, but abundant in the way that meant the baseline—available to virtually everyone—was extraordinary by any historical measure.

The energy transition happened faster than almost anyone predicted, and not because of a single breakthrough. Solar costs fell below any reasonable floor, then kept falling. A new generation of modular fission reactors—smaller, safer, manufacturable in factories rather than constructed on-site—came online across three continents through the 2040s, first in the countries with the least regulatory inertia, then spreading as their safety record accumulated. The combination produced something no prior civilization had ever had: energy so cheap that it ceased to be a meaningful constraint on most human activity. Desalination became trivial. Vertical farming became competitive with traditional agriculture for most crops. The phrase "energy poverty" entered the past tense.

This energy abundance cascaded outward in ways that were both predictable and surprising. Manufacturing became dramatically cheaper. Distribution systems that had depended on fossil fuel logistics reorganized themselves. The cost of building—housing, infrastructure, hospitals, schools—fell substantially, driven partly by new materials and partly by the automation of construction processes that had remained stubbornly manual for a century. The global housing shortage that had plagued cities for decades became, city by city, a solved problem. Not because anyone discovered a clever financial instrument or defeated all the political opposition at once, but because the underlying economics finally shifted enough that building became the path of least resistance.

This material abundance did not solve everything. It did not automatically make people kinder, or wiser, or better at maintaining relationships. But it removed a vast category of suffering that had been so endemic it was almost invisible: the suffering of scarcity, of precarity, of lives spent in anxious calculation about whether there would be enough. When those calculations became unnecessary—not for everyone simultaneously, but progressively, region by region—it turned out that human beings had remarkable reserves of attention and creativity that had simply been occupied elsewhere.

### The Institutional Substrate

The second thing to understand is that the institutions got better. Not all of them, not all at once, but in aggregate, over time, measurably.

Prediction markets went mainstream in the 2030s, after a combination of regulatory reform and several high-profile demonstrations of their accuracy made the political case undeniable. The first large-scale policy prediction markets launched in three countries nearly simultaneously; within a decade, they had spread to most democratic governments. They did not replace democratic deliberation—the old worry that they would short-circuit politics proved largely unfounded—but they provided something genuinely new: a mechanism for aggregating distributed knowledge about what would happen, visible to anyone who wanted to see it. Officials who ignored market-flagged warnings faced questions they could no longer brush away. Policies that the markets consistently rated as likely to fail were subjected to scrutiny they had previously avoided. The epistemic environment of governance improved, imperfectly and incompletely, but genuinely.

Charter cities—initially dismissed as a libertarian fantasy—found their footing in a handful of jurisdictions willing to experiment. The lesson learned was not that old cities were obsolete, but that competition between governance models produced pressure to improve. The most successful charter experiments were not ideologically pure laboratories but practical demonstrations that particular regulatory tangles could be cut: construction codes modernized, occupational licensing rationalized, permitting processes streamlined. The wins were rarely dramatic, but they accumulated. And the traditional cities, watching, borrowed what worked.

Occupational licensing reform spread across most of the United States by the late 2030s, driven by a coalition that proved surprisingly durable: libertarian-leaning economists, progressive advocates for low-income workers locked out of licensed professions, and fiscal conservatives alarmed by the costs of artificial scarcity in healthcare and education. The Shoshana Weissmans of the world turned out to be right: most of what occupational licensing actually protected was incumbent market position, not public safety. Once the evidence was laid out clearly enough, and repeated often enough, reform became politically available. Healthcare delivery changed substantially, as a range of services that had required physicians could be safely performed by nurse practitioners and other trained providers. Costs fell. Access expanded.

Land value taxation—an idea Georgists had been advocating for a century and a half—found its first significant adoption in a handful of cities and regions desperate for housing. The results were favorable enough that adoption spread. Speculative land-holding became less profitable; productive development became more so. The perverse incentive that had made it rational to sit on vacant urban land for decades began to dissolve. This was not a revolution. It was a slow, jurisdictional-level correction of a misaligned incentive, but corrections of that kind compound.

None of these institutional improvements was utopian on its own. Each was partial, contested, sometimes reversed, often slower than its advocates hoped. But the aggregate effect, over several decades, was a world with governance that was more responsive, more epistemically humble, and less captured by incumbent interests than the governance of the early twenty-first century. Not ideal government. Better government.

### The Medical Revolution

The extension and enhancement of human health proceeded along two tracks simultaneously.

The first track was deployment: getting existing tools to the people who lacked them. This proved, as always, harder than the purely technical problems but not intractable. Global health organizations, increasingly well-funded by the accumulated wealth of the abundance era, made dramatic progress on the diseases that had never been prioritized because their victims were poor. Malaria was finally brought to heel, partly through gene drives that reduced mosquito populations in high-transmission zones, partly through new prophylactics, partly through sheer logistical commitment. Deaths from preventable infectious disease fell to a fraction of their early-century levels.

The second track was discovery: genuinely new medicine. The 2030s and 2040s saw the maturation of approaches to aging that had been in development for decades. Senolytic therapies—drugs that cleared accumulated senescent cells from aging tissue—moved from promising animal results to human trials to clinical use. The effects were not immortality, and the early advocates who had promised dramatic life extension delivered something more modest: measurable improvements in healthspan, the period of life spent in good health. People in their seventies and eighties remained functional, vigorous, and cognitively sharp at rates that were simply higher than before. The burden of late-life disease and disability, which had threatened to overwhelm healthcare systems worldwide, began to lighten.

Mental health treatment underwent a quieter revolution. The combination of better pharmacology (including, eventually, carefully regulated therapeutic protocols using compounds that had been criminalized for decades) and genuinely scalable talk therapy—delivered through trained human therapists at lower cost, and through digital tools that proved more effective than skeptics expected—reduced the burden of depression, anxiety, and trauma substantially. Loneliness, recognized finally as the public health crisis it had always been, became the object of serious intervention: community design, social prescribing, programs that connected isolated individuals with others in deliberate and structured ways. The results were imperfect. Loneliness did not disappear. But it became less normal, less default, less accepted as simply the condition of modern life.

### The Texture of Daily Life

But statistics are the wrong frame for understanding what changed about the actual experience of living in this world. The texture of daily life is better.

People have more time. The automation of tedious, dangerous, and repetitive work—in factories, in logistics, in construction, in food preparation—did not produce mass unemployment, but it did produce something that turned out to be more valuable: discretionary time, distributed more broadly than in any prior era. The four-day workweek, once a radical proposal, became the standard in most wealthy economies by the 2040s and spread from there. This is not leisure in the passive, consumption-oriented sense. It is time for the things that people, when asked what makes their lives meaningful, consistently name: relationships, creative work, learning, the unhurried raising of children, the maintenance of communities.

Communities are stronger, partly by design. The lesson that social infrastructure requires active maintenance—that it does not persist automatically, as a byproduct of economic activity—was absorbed slowly and painfully, but it was absorbed. Cities were built and rebuilt with gathering in mind: third places, walkable neighborhoods, common spaces that invited lingering. Schools recognized that knowing how to be a good friend, how to sustain a relationship through difficulty, how to repair ruptures and maintain trust, was at least as important as any academic subject, and began teaching it explicitly and well. The deep loneliness that had quietly accompanied the prosperity of the early twenty-first century did not vanish—the human condition retains its difficulties—but it became less culturally invisible and less individually unavoidable.

Children grow up better. Every child in this world has access to what any reasonable person would consider a good start: adequate nutrition, healthcare, education, and—crucially—adults who are present and attentive. This did not require eliminating the family or replacing it with collective arrangements; it required taking seriously the insight that children benefit from knowing many adults, not only their parents, and that communities which structure themselves to provide this produce better outcomes than communities that treat childrearing as entirely private. Children move more freely, know their neighbors, understand that they belong to something larger than their household. The wonder of childhood, which had been compressed into shorter and shorter windows by the anxious competitiveness of the early century, is given more room.

### The Inner Life

None of the above addresses what is perhaps the most fundamental question: what is happening inside the people living in this world? Is the inner life of the average person genuinely richer, or merely better-provisioned?

The honest answer is: both, and the distinction matters less than it might seem.

Material security changes the inner life profoundly. When the background hum of financial anxiety—where will the money come from, what happens if I get sick, can we afford for the children to—falls silent, the mind occupies itself differently. People in this world are not free from difficulty or suffering; the human sources of those things (loss, disappointment, conflict, mortality) remain. But they are free from a particular kind of grinding, compounding, preventable suffering that had been so common it had come to seem natural.

The cultivation of virtue—a phrase that would have sounded archaic to most early twenty-first century ears—has become less strange. Not as a moralistic project imposed from outside, but as a broadly shared recognition that the quality of a life is substantially shaped by the character of the person living it: their capacity for honesty, for courage, for genuine care toward others, for equanimity in the face of what cannot be changed. The traditions that had always carried this wisdom—philosophical, religious, contemplative—find themselves taken more seriously, and in conversation with each other and with the scientific understanding of human psychology in ways that are genuinely generative rather than mutually dismissive.

People have richer interior lives partly because they have more room for them. The attention economy of the early twenty-first century had produced something close to an epidemic of shallowness—not stupidity, but the particular fragmentation of consciousness that comes from constant interruption and the substitution of passive consumption for active engagement. The world that emerged from the hinge decade did not solve this cleanly or completely, but it shifted: partly through regulation of the most predatory attentional practices, partly through cultural change, partly through design choices that made depth more available and distraction less relentless. People read more. People make more things. The boundary between consuming culture and participating in it became more permeable.

Learning became genuinely pleasurable for more people. Not for everyone—the range of human preferences is too wide for that—but the notion that curiosity was something that education systematically extinguished, rather than cultivated, became recognized as a design failure subject to correction. Curricula changed. Pedagogy changed. The experience of understanding something for the first time—really understanding it, not just being able to recite it—became something that more people had more often. Science was presented, successfully in some places, as what it actually is: a collective adventure, a structured form of finding out, something that can be participated in rather than merely received.

### The Shape of Meaning

What do people in this world devote themselves to?

The range is enormous, and that range is itself a good. Some people organize their lives around deep creative work: the making of things—music, visual art, writing, architecture, craft—that did not exist before and that express something about what it is to be human. Some people structure their lives around relationships: they are parents, grandparents, friends, partners, community members, and they find in those roles sufficient richness to fill many decades. Some people pursue mastery: they want to become the best at something, whether that is a traditional sport, a musical instrument, a craft that takes years to learn. Some people organize their lives around understanding: they want to know how things work, why things are as they are, what the universe is made of. Some people simply want the texture of a good ordinary day, repeated: good food, good company, physical health, the changing of seasons.

The crucial difference from prior eras is that these are genuinely choices rather than consolations. The person who chooses a life of craft is not settling because they couldn't succeed in a more prestigious career; they are pursuing what they actually value. The person who makes relationships the center of their life is not failing to be ambitious; they are recognizing, correctly, that relationships are among the most valuable things available.

This requires—and the world has developed—a culture that can hold diverse definitions of a successful life simultaneously, without constant pressure to compare them on a single scale. Status hierarchies did not disappear; they are probably too deeply wired for that. But the multiplication of communities with distinct internal standards, and the reduced dependence of material security on positional success, made it genuinely possible for more people to find the domain in which they could feel recognized and respected—and then to stay there, building something, rather than being perpetually pulled toward some universal summit.

### The Long Horizon

The people living in this world are living longer than any generation before them. Not yet indefinitely—the deeper biology of aging remains incompletely understood—but substantially longer in good health, and with the expectation of further extension. This changes the experience of time.

Long lives are structured differently. The early decades are given to learning and exploration—not the anxious, credentialist race of the early century, but a genuinely exploratory period in which it is acceptable, even expected, to try things and change direction. The middle decades are often the period of deepest work and contribution, when accumulated skill and experience combine with still-substantial energy. The later decades—extending further than they once did—carry their own gifts: depth of perspective, freedom from the urgencies that organize younger lives, the particular richness of relationships maintained across many years.

The phrase "I don't have time" has not disappeared, but it means something different. People still feel the pressure of limited time—the day still has twenty-four hours, and attention is still finite—but the pressure of the whole life being too short, too compressed, too rushed for everything to find its place, has eased. There is more room. Room for a second act, a third, a long deepening of commitments made early and a willingness to make new ones late.

### What Made It Possible

Looking back at the hinge decade and what came after, several things stand out as having mattered most.

The first was epistemic: people got better at knowing what they knew and what they didn't. Prediction markets, better science communication, the spread of probabilistic thinking, the gradual cultural absorption of the insight that overconfidence is costly—these things improved the quality of collective decision-making enough to matter. Not dramatically, not overnight, but measurably. The worst policy mistakes of the early century had often been predictable; the institutional reforms that followed made them harder to ignore.

The second was the willingness to solve problems that were politically inconvenient rather than technically intractable. Energy abundance was always technically achievable; the barriers were regulatory and political. Housing was always buildable; the constraints were zoning and finance and the interests of existing owners. Healthcare was always improvable; the obstacles were licensing and insurance structures that protected incumbents. The progress made was not primarily scientific. It was the application of sufficient political will and sufficient good arguments to problems where the solutions were known.

The third was the recovery of the long view. The early twenty-first century had been marked by a peculiar temporal compression—attention foreshortened, political horizons limited to the next election or the next quarter, difficulty even imagining consequences that would unfold over decades. The recovery of the long view was cultural as much as institutional: a gradual re-expansion of the time horizon over which people and organizations were willing to think and act. Forests were planted whose shade those who planted them would not sit in, and this came to seem normal again.

---

The world described here is not perfect. It contains unresolved arguments, persistent injustices, losses that cannot be recovered, sorrows that cannot be prevented. Human beings, given abundance and security and time, still sometimes fail each other, fail themselves, fail to be as good as they could have been. The future remains open and uncertain; nothing described here forecloses it.

But it is genuinely, substantially better. Not better in the way that means the things that made people suffer have been pushed aside and ignored. Better in the way that means they were taken seriously, addressed carefully, solved where solving was possible, and mourned honestly where it was not.

Humanity got its act together. Not in one moment, not through any single mechanism, not because anyone finally discovered the correct utopian architecture. Through accumulation: of good decisions, good institutions, good values quietly cultivated across many generations, and the stubborn refusal to accept that the preventable suffering of human beings was simply the price of being human.

That refusal, sustained long enough, turned out to be enough.
