# The Flourishing: A Vision for the World We Could Build

## Prologue: What We Are Aiming At

Somewhere between the world we have and the world we deserve sits a door. On one side: coordination failures piled like geological strata, each century's bad equilibria compressing the ones below into permanent bedrock. On the other side: something that has never existed before, a civilization that is genuinely, concretely, measurably good at being a civilization.

This essay is about the world on the other side of that door. Not a paradise handed down by benevolent machines, and not a return to some imagined pastoral simplicity. A world built by people who finally got serious about building it—who treated the design of their institutions with the same rigor they once reserved for bridges and vaccines, who understood that "good enough" is an answer that compounds into catastrophe over centuries.

This is a description of where we could be by 2150, if we are neither catastrophically unlucky nor catastrophically stupid. It is not a prediction. It is a target.

## I. The Transition Nobody Called a Revolution

History rarely announces its inflection points. The industrial revolution was named long after the smoke had cleared. The great transition of the twenty-first and twenty-second centuries—which people living through it called, variously, the Adjustment, the Great Untangling, the Long Dawn—was similarly invisible to those inside it.

It did not begin with any single invention. It began with a slowly accumulating disgust.

The early twenty-first century was a period of extraordinary productive capacity paired with extraordinary institutional failure. The world could manufacture vaccines against novel pathogens in under a year, then distribute them according to the logic of passport color. It could model climate decades into the future with reasonable accuracy, then fail to coordinate any serious response for forty years. It could identify the cognitive biases that wrecked individual decision-making in laboratory conditions, then watch those same biases wreck civilizational decision-making in real time.

The disgust accumulated among a critical mass of people who were, crucially, not merely angry but *curious*. They wanted to know why. They read the economists and the political scientists and the game theorists and the historians, and they understood—really understood, not just as an abstract proposition but as a felt reality—that the failures were not accidental. They were structural. They were the predictable outputs of systems optimized for the wrong things, measuring the wrong things, rewarding the wrong things.

This cohort did not seize power. Power-seizing was part of the old logic they were trying to escape. Instead, they built.

They built small, first. Pilot cities with reformed zoning. Regional experiments in deliberative democracy. Prediction markets embedded in government agencies, feeding probabilistic estimates into policy decisions that had previously been made on vibes and faction. They built transparency tools that made the reasoning behind institutional decisions legible for the first time—not just the conclusions, but the actual chain of inference, auditable and contestable.

Most experiments failed. The ones that failed well were written up honestly and the information was shared. The ones that succeeded were copied, adapted, copied again.

Then AI arrived, and the slope of the learning curve changed.

## II. What AI Actually Did

The story of AI told by its most anxious observers was a story about replacement—human workers displaced, human connection hollowed out, human judgment superseded. The story told by its most credulous enthusiasts was a story about transcendence—a god born in a data center, ascending to solve everything.

Both stories were wrong, in the ways that dramatic stories about technology tend to be wrong.

What AI actually did, over the course of several decades, was *lower the cost of coordination and raise the quality of information*. This sounds modest. It was not modest. It was, in its cumulative effects, the most significant structural change to human society since the printing press—possibly since writing.

Consider what coordination actually requires. It requires that the relevant parties can find each other. It requires that they can communicate accurately about their preferences and constraints. It requires that they can model the likely consequences of various agreements. It requires that agreements, once reached, can be monitored and enforced. And it requires that all of this happen at a cost low enough that the effort is worth making in the first place.

For most of human history, most potential coordination was never attempted because the transaction costs were prohibitive. You could only negotiate with people you could physically reach. You could only monitor agreements you could personally observe. You could only enforce contracts through social structures that were themselves expensive to maintain.

AI systems—first narrow, then increasingly capable, always carefully constrained by a generation that had read the cautionary literature and took it seriously—compressed these transaction costs toward something approaching zero.

The effect on daily life was enormous in aggregate and nearly invisible in any single instance. Job matching became genuinely mutual: an AI interlocutor representing a potential employee could have a substantive negotiation with an AI interlocutor representing a potential employer, exploring the full space of possible arrangements—compensation, hours, responsibilities, career development, exit conditions—in the time it once took to write a cover letter. Both parties brought more information to the table. Fewer matches were bad matches. Fewer good matches went unmade.

Property disputes that once required years of litigation were resolved through structured negotiation processes where each party's AI could model the full range of possible outcomes, including adjudicated ones, with enough accuracy that the rational move became obvious. Courts still existed—humans still wanted humans to make the final call on contested moral questions—but they heard dramatically fewer cases.

At the geopolitical scale, the effect was subtler but no less real. AI systems capable of simulating conflict outcomes with reasonable fidelity changed the logic of brinkmanship. When two powers disagreed about who would prevail in a hypothetical confrontation, they had historically resolved that disagreement through an actual confrontation. When both sides' simulations converged on similar answers—and when the cost of running those simulations was lower than the cost of a single casualty—the incentive structure changed. Not perfectly. Not everywhere at once. But the direction was clear, and the direction was away from violence.

## III. The Shape of the Economy

The economy of the flourishing world looks, at first glance, like a market economy. There are prices. There is competition. People trade things for other things. Private businesses exist. People earn incomes and spend them.

What is different is the understanding that saturates every participant—the understanding that markets are coordination tools, not sacred objects, and that the design of the rules within which markets operate is itself a choice with consequences.

The foundational insight, which took embarrassingly long to operationalize, was something like: *land and natural resources are the collective inheritance of humanity; labor and ingenuity are not*. This is not a controversial proposition once stated clearly. The planet's arable land existed before any human worked it. Its mineral deposits were laid down by geological processes no one organized. Its favorable locations—harbors, river confluences, positions within thriving cities—derive their value from the presence of other humans, not from the efforts of whoever happens to hold title.

The transition to capturing economic rents from location value and natural resources, while leaving the fruits of labor and capital investment with those who created them, was politically brutal and took decades. But it happened, in stages, in jurisdiction after jurisdiction, and its effects were large.

Land hoarding became economically pointless. Speculative withholding of prime urban real estate—perhaps the most reliable mechanism for concentrating wealth in a previous era—was taxed into oblivion. Cities densified. Housing became affordable in a way that nothing short of this structural change could have accomplished. The people who had previously competed to acquire location value now competed to create productive value instead, which is a much better thing to compete about.

On top of this foundation, a genuine universal basic income became not a utopian aspiration but an accountable fiscal policy: the dividends of the shared inheritance, distributed to all shareholders. It was not lavish. It was enough that no one starved, no one was coerced into abusive labor relationships by desperation, and children were never conscripted into adult work to keep their families alive.

The economy ran hot. Not in the inflationary sense—monetary policy had, with the help of better forecasting tools, become significantly more competent—but in the sense that labor was scarce relative to the demand for it. Employers competed for workers. Workers chose among opportunities. The power gradient that had defined employment relations in previous centuries flattened dramatically.

Where it did not flatten—where genuine monopsonies or monopolies emerged—the transparency infrastructure made them visible, and the tools for responding to them (competing cooperatives, regulatory intervention, public investment in alternatives) were deployed quickly. The key was that these were understood as coordination failures requiring structural responses, not as natural features of the economic landscape.

Intellectual contribution was handled through a system that would have seemed alien to earlier eras: a combination of open publication, mandatory licensing, and community-determined attribution. The basic principle was that knowledge, once created, should flow freely, and that the creators of knowledge should be recognized and supported by the communities that benefited from their work. This worked imperfectly, as everything works imperfectly. But it worked better than a system where crucial vaccines sat behind patent walls while people died, or where the most valuable scientific insights were locked in journals that most scientists couldn't access.

## IV. Growing Up in the Flourishing World

A child born in 2130 encounters a strikingly different educational environment than her ancestors.

She is not sorted into a grade by age and marched through a standardized curriculum toward a credential that will be presented to employers as a proxy for competence. The people who designed that system, in retrospect, were solving the wrong problem. They were trying to produce standardized outputs at industrial scale. The flourishing world is not trying to produce standardized outputs.

She learns to read, to count, to reason about probability and evidence, to understand the basic logic of social coordination—these are the genuine basics, the literacy of her civilization—through a combination of AI-assisted tutoring and peer learning. The AI tutoring is extraordinarily good. It models her specific confusions, identifies gaps, explains the same concept through twelve different framings until one clicks. The two-sigma improvement in learning outcomes that researchers once called Bloom's problem—the chasm between what one-on-one tutoring achieved and what classroom instruction achieved—has been substantially closed.

But the most important parts of her education are not delivered by AI. They are delivered by older children who have already learned the skills she is learning, by adults who practice the trades she is curious about, by actual extended engagement with actual problems in the actual world. She spends two weeks at twelve years old apprenticed to a civil engineer overseeing a flood barrier project, and it is more educational than a year of classroom instruction would have been.

There is no minimum age to work, and no credential required to begin. What is required is demonstrated competence in the specific task, assessed by people who actually do the task. The notion of attending four years of university to acquire a credential before spending a single day attempting the actual job of your intended profession has been recognized as the extraordinary waste it always was.

There is also no credential required to learn. Curiosity is sufficient permission. She follows hers.

## V. Information and Trust

Perhaps the most important—and the hardest-won—transformation in the flourishing world is in its epistemic infrastructure.

The early twenty-first century had abundant information and collapsed trust. People swam in data and couldn't agree on basic facts. This wasn't primarily a technological problem; it was an incentive problem. The systems that distributed information were optimized for engagement, which meant they were optimized for outrage and novelty, which meant they were optimized for distortion. The people who created information were operating in an ecosystem that rewarded confident wrongness over careful uncertainty.

The response was not to restrict information—that path led only to authoritarian capture. The response was to change the incentive structures around information production, and to invest in institutions for establishing shared ground truth.

The Chroniclers—funded through a combination of public endowment and voluntary subscription, governed by explicit charters requiring transparent methodology and correction processes—became the closest thing to a shared epistemic substrate the world had ever had. They were not infallible. They were not without institutional biases. But they were auditable, correctable, and committed in a visible way to the practice of showing their work. They published their reasoning, not just their conclusions. They updated publicly when they were wrong. They distinguished clearly between what was established, what was probable, and what was contested.

Alongside the Chroniclers, a robust culture of calibration developed. Prediction markets—not merely for financial instruments but for questions of genuine public importance—gave people skin in the game for being right rather than for sounding confident. Forecasters with good track records earned attention and consulting arrangements. Forecasters with bad track records lost both. The culture began, slowly, to treat epistemic accuracy as a genuine virtue rather than merely a performance of virtue.

AI assisted here too—in pattern-matching new claims against established knowledge, in flagging statistical irregularities in published research, in making the full reasoning chain of complex analyses legible to non-experts. But the core solution was cultural and institutional: people had decided that being wrong about important things was costly enough to try harder to be right.

## VI. Governance Without Leviathan

The question of governance in the flourishing world is best understood not as a question about who has power but as a question about what decisions belong at what scale.

The old nation-state model concentrated an extraordinary range of decisions at a single level: local ordinances and nuclear deterrence, food safety standards and immigration quotas, all managed by the same apparatus, using the same blunt democratic instruments. This was not designed. It accreted. And its accretion produced institutions that were simultaneously too large for granular local decisions and too small for genuinely global ones.

The flourishing world has, imperfectly, sorted decisions by their actual scope. Genuinely global challenges—the risks that could end the experiment of human civilization entirely—are handled at the global level, through institutions with genuine teeth and genuine accountability. Climate, pandemic preparedness, the management of catastrophic technologies: these are not left to the coordination of sovereign states pursuing narrow national interests.

At the other end of the scale, a vast proliferation of smaller polities has emerged—experimental communities, specialized cities, regional arrangements of various kinds—each handling the decisions that genuinely belong to the people most affected by them. You can move. Entry is not easy, and taking on the obligations of a community has real costs, but the option exists. The knowledge that you can leave makes staying meaningful.

Between the global and the local, the intermediate scales have largely lost the semi-sacred character they once had. Nations still exist, culturally and historically. They host Olympics and produce literature and maintain cuisines. Their capacity to extract and coerce has diminished substantially.

The key innovation in governance was a simple and humiliating one: the requirement that reasoning be public and auditable. Officials at every level are required to publish not just their decisions but the chains of inference that produced them. These chains are monitored by independent auditors, by journalists, by academic institutions, by the AI systems that have become genuinely useful at detecting inconsistency and motivated reasoning. It does not eliminate bad decisions. Nothing eliminates bad decisions. But it raises their cost dramatically, and it makes learning from them possible in a way that opaque decision-making never was.

## VII. The Good Life

What do people actually do with their days, in a world where their basic needs are met, where administrative friction has been drastically reduced, where no one is coerced into degrading work by desperation?

They do, mostly, what humans have always done when not consumed by survival: they form relationships, pursue projects, raise children, engage in arguments, make things, break things, fix them again. The content of human life has not changed beyond recognition. The texture of it has.

Work, in the flourishing world, is something closer to vocation than to coercion. Not everyone has found their vocation—that would be too much to promise. But the structures that prevented people from even looking have been largely removed. You are not locked into a credential track at eighteen that determines your opportunities for decades. You are not trapped in a job by the fear that losing it means losing your health care and your housing. You can try things, fail at them, try something else.

The Maslow pyramid is not a hierarchy that most people have climbed to its apex. Human life contains suffering, loss, disappointment, mundane frustration. But the broad baseline is higher. Malnutrition is rare. Preventable childhood death is nearly eliminated. The experience of being at the bottom of a coercive hierarchy—needing the approval of someone who has power over your survival—is rare enough that children who encounter it in historical literature find it difficult to fully imagine.

Self-actualization is not universal. But it is, for the first time, available to a large fraction of humanity—not just to the lucky few who were born into the right family in the right country at the right time. Artists who would have spent their lives in administrative work are making art. Scientists who would never have gained access to a research institution are asking and answering questions. Parents are present for their children in a way that exhausted desperation previously made impossible.

The joy in this is not the joy of paradise. It is the quieter and more durable joy of a world that is mostly, with important exceptions, trying to work correctly—a world that, when it fails, has the tools and the culture to understand its failures and correct them.

## Epilogue: The Path to the Door

None of this arrives by wishing. The path from here to there runs through decades of hard, specific, unglamorous work: reforming zoning laws in individual cities, building and testing and iterating on governance experiments, developing and deploying AI systems carefully enough that they accumulate trust rather than destroying it, winning the political fights required to capture resource rents, investing in educational reform, building the epistemic institutions that make shared reality possible.

The work is not sexy. It does not lend itself to dramatic narratives. It is the work of treating civilization as a design problem—as something that was built, can be rebuilt, and should be rebuilt with more care and more knowledge than was available when it was first constructed.

The people who will do this work are alive now. Many of them are young. They will need to be stubborn, to care about the right things, to be willing to look foolish in the defense of ideas that are correct before they are popular.

What they are building toward is a world that a medieval peasant and a twenty-first-century middle-class worker would both recognize, immediately and without needing explanation, as better. Not better in some abstract or philosophical sense. Better in the sense that more people wake up in the morning looking forward to the day. Better in the sense that fewer people lie awake afraid. Better in the sense that children are more likely to grow into themselves, rather than into the shape that economic necessity carved for them.

The door is real. The other side is accessible. The only question is whether we will be the generation that opens it, or the generation that handed that task—one more time—to someone else.
