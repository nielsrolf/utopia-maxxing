# The Commonwealth of Unfinished Things

*A Vision of the World Three Centuries Hence*

---

We tend to imagine utopia as a destination—a place finally, perfectly arrived at. But the best futures are not places of arrival. They are places of departure. What follows is an account of one such world, as best as it can be rendered from within the impoverished vocabulary of this earlier age.

---

## I. First Encounter

Imagine you sail toward it—not across an ocean, but across decades of accumulated catastrophe and incremental repair. The approach is not dramatic. There is no pillar of light. What you notice first, arriving in any of its great cities, is a quality of attention in the people you meet. They are not hurried. They are not glazed. They seem to be somewhere, fully, which is rarer than it sounds.

The official name for this civilization varies by region. Some call it the Renovatio. Others, the Long Work. Most people simply call it *here*, which tells you something important: they do not feel themselves to be living in the anteroom of somewhere better. This is not a waiting room civilization. People are not managing time until the good part starts.

How did it begin? In the way that most lasting things begin—not with a revolution, but with a long, unglamorous accumulation of better decisions, better institutions, and better habits of mind. The transition from our own era took roughly a century and a half. It was not smooth. There were reverses, catastrophes, decades where the whole project seemed to be collapsing back into its starting material. But each time, something held. A set of practices. A culture of rebuilding.

What held, ultimately, was a particular understanding: that a world worth living in is not a problem to be solved once and then preserved in amber, but a kind of craft—endlessly practiced, endlessly refined.

---

## II. The Structure of Knowledge

At the center of every major settlement—and the Renovatio has both dense cities of three million and intentional communities of forty—there is a building called the *Institute of Works*. The name is Baconian in spirit and deliberate in its reference. These are not universities in the old sense, though they contain everything universities contained and considerably more.

The Institutes exist to do one thing: advance the knowledge of causes, so that human capability over the conditions of life continues to grow. Each Institute has several hundred permanent researchers and a rotating population of thousands—citizens who come to learn, to contribute, to be changed. The internal organization would be recognizable to any historian of science: collectors of existing knowledge, experimenters, theorists, synthesizers, translators into practice. What would be unrecognizable is the relationship between this institution and the rest of society.

In our era, the laboratory and the lived world are separated by an enormous distance, crossed only by the occasional product or policy. In the Renovatio, this distance has been deliberately collapsed. The Institutes are physically embedded in the cities they serve. Their researchers are expected to spend a third of their time in what is called *contact practice*—working alongside farmers, builders, physicians, teachers, cooks, caregivers, children. The flow of knowledge runs in both directions, and the practical wisdom embedded in ordinary work is treated as data of the highest order.

The result is a science that is at once more rigorous and more human. Researchers are not rewarded for the accumulation of publications but for the quality of understanding they produce and transmit. The measurement of this is itself a sophisticated practice—one of the more interesting institutional inventions of the Renovatio is the *Interpreter's Review*, in which researchers must explain their work to a rotating panel of non-specialists, and are judged partly on the quality of that explanation. The idea is old but had never been implemented with real consequences attached.

Every Institute maintains two great halls. In the first, the current state of every major domain of knowledge is displayed—not in the form of dusty databases but as living exhibitions, regularly updated, designed to be comprehensible and beautiful and genuinely inviting to any visitor regardless of background. In the second hall hang the portraits and brief biographies of every person whose contribution to that domain of knowledge has been judged significant: not just the geniuses, but the careful observers, the instrument-makers, the translators, the patients who volunteered for difficult studies, the farmers who noticed something odd about their soil. The intent is to make visible the full social texture of discovery, which had always been present but had long been obscured by the cult of the solitary genius.

## III. The Shape of a Life

The citizens of the Renovatio live longer than we do—not dramatically so, as of the period I am describing, but meaningfully. The average life now extends to a hundred and twenty years, with the final decades being, for most people, a period of continued engagement rather than managed decline. This was achieved not through any single breakthrough but through a century of steady work on the biology of aging, improvements in nutrition and environment, and—perhaps most importantly—the elimination of the chronic stressors that had silently shortened billions of lives.

But length of life is a secondary achievement. The primary achievement is *shape*.

A life in the Renovatio is expected to have multiple phases, each with its own character, its own demands, its own pleasures. Formal education, in the old sense of structured instruction by specialists, occupies roughly the first twenty years—but it is education organized around the cultivation of *capability* rather than the transmission of content. Children learn to observe, to reason, to make things with their hands, to express themselves precisely in multiple modes, to collaborate, to disagree productively, to tolerate uncertainty without being paralyzed by it. Content—history, science, mathematics, literature—arrives in service of these capacities rather than as an end in itself.

The next thirty years are typically years of what the Renovatio calls *deep engagement*: periods of intensive focus on particular crafts, problems, relationships, or projects. These are not careers in the old sense, though they often look like careers. The difference is orientation: the goal is mastery and contribution, not income and status, though both tend to follow as byproducts.

The middle decades are often years of *breadth*—citizens who have spent years becoming genuinely expert in one domain are encouraged, through a system of sabbaticals and supported transitions, to spend time in entirely different fields. A physicist becomes a cheesemaker. A physician studies architecture. A farmer spends two years learning the mathematics of networks. This is not dilettantism; it is a deliberate social technology for keeping knowledge fresh and preventing the calcification that comes from too many decades in a single groove. The cross-pollination of ideas that results is one of the Renovatio's great engines of innovation.

The later decades are often years of *transmission*—the systematic effort to pass on what has been learned. This is considered one of the most demanding and most honored forms of work. The best teachers in the Renovatio are not those who remember being beginners and explain accordingly, but those who have recently re-experienced beginner's mind through their own transitions into unfamiliar domains, and can map that territory with unusual precision.

At no point in this scheme is a person a passive recipient. The Renovatio has a near-total allergy to arrangements that require people to simply *receive* things—to consume, to sit back, to have things done for them. There is an aesthetic embedded in the culture, absorbed from childhood, that values *living by one's own strength*. This does not mean rejecting help or collaboration. It means that the ideal relationship with any capability—cooking, navigation, music, medicine—is active rather than passive, participatory rather than receptive.

## IV. The Texture of Daily Life

A morning in the Renovatio does not begin with the consumption of algorithmically selected stimulation. This was one of the more difficult habits to break, and the history of how it was broken is long and often uncomfortable. What replaced it is harder to describe: a kind of orientation toward the actual, the local, the present.

People cook. Not exclusively, but most people, most days, prepare at least part of what they eat, and this is considered neither a chore nor a performance but simply a form of engagement with the material world that most humans find satisfying when they are not too depleted to do it. One of the great practical achievements of the Renovatio is the organization of work and rest in ways that do not leave people too depleted for the things that matter—a problem that had been accumulating for centuries and whose solution required simultaneous changes in labor arrangements, urban design, social expectations, and the concept of productivity itself.

The cities are built for encounter. This sounds like a small thing and is in fact enormous. The physical layout of settlements in the Renovatio—the distribution of markets, workshops, parks, gathering places, Institutes—is designed to maximize the likelihood that a person moving through daily life will run into something unexpected and interesting, will overhear a conversation that reframes a problem they've been stuck on, will meet someone whose work intersects with their own in ways neither could have predicted. Serendipity is treated as a social technology and engineered with some care.

The family, in the Renovatio, takes many forms, but the celebration of intergenerational connection has become—in a form Bacon would have recognized—one of the culture's central rituals. There are feasts, not unlike his Feast of the Family, at which three or four living generations gather around those who have helped build what exists. These are not sentimental occasions but genuinely serious ones: the elders are expected to give honest account of what they have learned and failed to learn, what they would do differently, what they are proud of. The young are expected to ask hard questions and to bring news from domains the old have not visited. The exchange is reciprocal in a way that would surprise those who imagine such rituals as mere veneration of age.

## V. What Has Been Kept

The designers of the Renovatio—insofar as it was designed, which was only partially and imperfectly—made a series of deliberate choices about what *not* to smooth away.

They kept difficulty. Not pointless difficulty, not cruelty, not the grinding exhaustion of poverty and disease and preventable death. But the difficulty that is intrinsic to genuine achievement: the difficulty of learning a craft to the point of mastery, of sustaining a relationship through genuine conflict, of creating something original, of understanding something previously mysterious. The Renovatio operates on the principle that a life made uniformly easy is a life progressively drained of meaning, and that the greatest disservice you can do to a person is to protect them so thoroughly from challenge that they never discover what they are capable of.

They kept stakes. Choices in the Renovatio have real consequences. Not catastrophic, arbitrary, irreversible consequences—the kind that had crushed billions in earlier eras for reasons having nothing to do with their choices. But consequences of the kind that allow a person to know, and to be known, through their decisions. A world in which no choice matters is not a comfortable heaven; it is an exquisite prison.

They kept surprise. This required active effort, because there are forces—some technological, some institutional, some psychological—that tend toward the elimination of surprise, toward the optimization of experience into a smooth and predictable trajectory. The Renovatio resists these forces in a dozen ways, some formal and some simply cultural. There is a value placed on the unexpected, the unplanned encounter, the discovery that overturns a previous understanding. Citizens are positively encouraged to maintain what are called *regions of ignorance*—domains in which they have deliberately not accumulated expertise, so that genuine novice experience remains available to them throughout their lives.

They kept loss. This is the most counterintuitive element. Death remains. Grief remains. Failure remains. The argument for this is not sentimental; it is structural. A world from which the possibility of loss has been engineered away is a world in which the texture of care has been subtly altered, in which commitment means something less, in which love is a different and lesser thing. The Renovatio has abolished much *unnecessary* suffering—the suffering that comes from disease, poverty, violence, ignorance, and systematic injustice. But it has not attempted to abolish suffering itself, recognizing that the attempt would destroy something more valuable than what it preserved.

---

## VI. What Has Not Been Achieved

The Renovatio is not a finished place. This is perhaps its most important feature.

There are problems that remain unsolved and are known to be unsolved. There are questions in physics, in biology, in the understanding of consciousness, that have resisted three centuries of serious effort. There are social arrangements that remain imperfect—ways in which the distribution of meaningful work is still inequitable, in which the transition from old life-phases to new ones is still more painful than anyone would wish, in which the relationship between large communities and small ones generates genuine and unresolved tensions.

There are also entirely new problems that have emerged from the solutions to old ones—problems that could not have been anticipated and that require the invention of new conceptual frameworks to even describe. Citizens of the Renovatio encounter these problems with something that looks, from the outside, like eagerness. They have been raised in a culture that treats unsolved problems not as evidence of failure but as the primary invitation to a life well spent.

The great hall of every Institute contains, alongside its portraits of contributors and its exhibitions of current knowledge, a third exhibit: a curated collection of the most beautiful open questions in every domain. These are presented not as shameful gaps but as gifts—as the places where the next generation will do its best work. Children visit these exhibits on school trips. The questions are periodically updated as old ones are resolved and new ones are discovered.

The motto carved above the entrance of the first Institute—the one built in what had been Geneva, during the difficult decades of reconstruction—reads, in the original: *The end of our foundation is the knowledge of causes and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire.* This they borrowed, without apology, from a man who had seen it but never lived to build it.

Underneath, a later generation added a second line: *We will not finish. Begin.*

---

The sailors who stumbled onto Bacon's Bensalem were told they had found a land of angels. What they had actually found was a land of careful, ambitious, generous humans—people who had organized themselves around the pursuit of knowledge and the cultivation of good life with unusual seriousness, and had achieved, as a result, something that looked miraculous only because it was so much better than what the visitors had known.

The Renovatio is not a land of angels either. It is a land of people who have decided, collectively and over a long and difficult time, to take seriously the question of what a life worth living requires. They have found that the answer is complicated—more complicated than any single ideology or religion or philosophy had predicted. They have found that it requires difficulty and novelty and connection and purpose and surprise and loss and mastery and love. They have found that it cannot be given to people but must be built by them, continuously, each generation inheriting not a finished thing but an ongoing project whose terms they are expected to contest and revise.

They are, most of them, most of the time, genuinely glad to be alive.

That is the whole of the miracle. It is enough.
