# The Drawer in the Attic

There is a moment that most people alive today can probably name. Not a grand historical turning point, but a personal one — the kind that arrives sideways, when you aren't looking. Maybe you were watching light move through leaves, or holding someone's hand in a hospital, or hearing a piece of music that reached through your sternum and rearranged something inside you. And for a second — or ten seconds, or a minute — you sat bolt upright and thought: *Oh. This. This is what it's actually like to be alive. I didn't know it could be this.*

And then it faded.

That fading is one of the great cruelties of being human in 2025. Not just that our best moments pass, but that they leave so little residue. We return to our inboxes, our anxieties, our dull chronic aches of body and spirit, and we can barely remember what the good thing felt like — only that it was real, and that it was more real than the ordinary substrate we swim through every day. Philosophers call the ordinary substrate various names. Soot. Fog. The hum.

What I want to describe is a world in which the hum is gone.

Not because we have solved the problem of consciousness with a pill, or burned away all pain with some clean surgical light. But because we did, over a long and difficult and frequently embarrassing period of several centuries, the patient and unglamorous work of actually building the conditions under which human beings — all human beings — can find their way to those moments more often, stay in them longer, and carry them forward into the rest of their lives like a coal that doesn't go out.

Let me be precise about what I mean, because precision matters here.

---

The world I am describing did not arrive through a singularity. There was no decade of rupture, no glorious upload, no benevolent superintelligence descending from the servers to hand down enlightenment. What happened was both more boring and more remarkable: people kept working. Generation after generation, in the ordinary human way — with arguments and setbacks and wasted effort and occasional spectacular success — they accumulated knowledge, adjusted institutions, changed norms, and passed things down.

The first century of this was mostly about removing catastrophic risk. A handful of international agreements, imperfect but durable, constrained the development of the most dangerous technologies long enough for aligned alternatives to emerge. Climate stabilized at levels that were punishing but survivable, and the survivors — chastened, practical, newly possessed of a long-term thinking that previous generations had mostly theorized about — rebuilt supply chains and coastal cities with the grim competence of people who understood, finally, what was actually at stake.

The second century was about abundance. Not luxury, not decadence — abundance in the literal sense: enough food, clean water, shelter, medical care, and basic education for everyone alive. This turned out to be technically achievable far earlier than most people expected; the bottleneck, as it had always been, was political. Once the question of survival was settled enough that long-horizon cooperation became possible, the question of distribution turned out to be less a technical problem than a cultural one — a matter of what stories people told about whose lives counted and how much.

The stories changed.

I won't pretend this change was simple or that it came without violence and regression. But it came.

By the third century — which is where I want to linger — the material foundations were largely in place, and something stranger and more interesting began to happen. Humanity, freed from immediate existential threat and immediate material desperation, turned its attention to what it actually wanted. This sounds simple. It was not. Discovering what you actually want, as opposed to what you have been trained by circumstance and incentive to want, is one of the most difficult things any person can do — and the difficulty scales up unpredictably when you ask it of a civilization.

There were wrong turns. There were centuries of wrong turns. There were movements that mistook conformity for harmony, and periods of such radical individual autonomy that the word "community" lost all meaning. There were technologies of pleasure that some people disappeared into, and there were backlashes against those technologies so severe that genuine goods got discarded along with genuine harms. The history of this period is not a clean arc. It is a mess of experiments, most of them partial failures, all of them instructive.

But something was accumulating.

---

Here is what life is like in the world I am describing, which I will put in the present tense, because that is the tense of inhabitation.

You wake up in a body that does not hurt. This alone is a revolution almost impossible to convey to someone living in 2025, when chronic pain is so ordinary that most people have simply integrated it into their sense of who they are. The medical infrastructure of this world has made pain — not discomfort, not the productive ache of exertion, but the grinding pointless suffering of disease and injury and aging — largely optional. Not through any single breakthrough, but through the slow accumulation of regenerative medicine, precise pharmacology, genetic therapies, and, most importantly, the political will to distribute all of these things as public goods.

You wake up in a body that does not hurt, and the first thing you notice is that your mind is clear.

This is the second revolution. Not intelligence enhancement in the science-fiction sense — not brains the size of planets, not uploaded consciousness, not any of the dramatic transformations that were imagined and debated and feared in the early twenty-first century. Most people in this world are cognitively similar to people today. What is different is the *quality of attention* available to a mind that is not chronically stressed, not economically desperate, not algorithmically managed by systems designed to exploit its weaknesses, not sleep-deprived, not malnourished, not in pain.

It turns out that the ordinary human brain, in genuinely good conditions, is extraordinary. The people of this world are not smarter than people of 2025 in the way that a calculator is smarter than an abacus. They are smarter the way a person is smarter when they have slept well, eaten real food, spent time in nature, been genuinely loved, had interesting work, and been given enough unstructured time to let their minds wander and settle and find their own shape. If you want to know what most people in this world feel like most of the time, take the best afternoon you have ever had — one of those long, golden, unhurried afternoons when everything was interesting and you were fully present and the world seemed both larger and more intimate than usual — and imagine that as your baseline.

That is what the absence of the hum feels like from the inside.

---

The question people in 2025 most often ask, when someone describes this possibility, is: *But what do they do?*

The question reveals something. It reveals that we have trouble imagining a life in which the doing is not the point; in which the activities that fill a day are not primarily the means to some justification, some productive end that earns a person the right to exist. But let me try to answer it concretely anyway.

Some people do what we would recognize as work, in the sense of sustained effort toward some shared external goal. There are engineers and doctors and scientists and builders, and the nature of their work is recognizable, though the conditions under which they pursue it are different: no economic coercion, no manufactured urgency, projects chosen because they are genuinely interesting or genuinely needed, and enough time to do them right. Science in this world is slower in some ways — less frantic, less driven by publication pressure — and vastly more productive in others, because scientists can follow their actual curiosity and can build on each other's work without the incentive structures that used to bury null results and reward flashy overclaiming.

Some people do what we would recognize as art. There is more of it than there has ever been, because the barrier to making things is lower and there is no economic penalty for being an artist who isn't also commercially viable. The art of this world is strange and abundant and uneven and alive.

Some people spend long periods of time — years, sometimes — doing what looks from the outside like very little: walking in mountains, caring for gardens, learning to play instruments badly and then gradually less badly, raising children, cooking elaborate meals, sitting with elderly parents or friends, reading. These activities are not residual. They are not what people do when they have failed to find something more important. They are recognized, in this world, as among the most important things a person can do with a life — because the people of this world have learned, through collective and painful experience, that the texture of daily life is not the wrapping around the real content. It *is* the content.

And some people — more than you might expect — choose to work on the hard problems. The problems that remain, because there are always problems that remain. How to distribute resources fairly when preferences are genuinely incommensurable. How to raise children well when there are so many competing theories of flourishing. How to maintain the conditions that make this world possible against entropy, both physical and political. How to extend the circle of consideration — to animals, to whatever forms of mind are just beginning to emerge from the machines, to distant people and distant futures. These problems are the intellectual and moral frontier of this world, and they attract some of its best minds, not because those minds are compelled, but because the problems are genuinely hard and genuinely matter.

---

I want to say something about consciousness, because I think it is the most important thing and the most difficult to describe.

In the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, there was a widespread if rarely articulated assumption that the kinds of experience available to a human being were more or less fixed — that you got what you got, and the variation you might expect across a lifetime was modest. Peak experiences were treated as precious partly because they were rare; they arrived unbidden, stayed briefly, and left, and there was no real practice for inviting them or prolonging them, no shared understanding of what they were or what they were evidence of.

This assumption has been revised.

It has been revised not through any single discovery but through the slow accumulation of evidence from neuroscience, from clinical practice, from the careful documentation of contemplative traditions that had been circling these questions for centuries, and from the careful and cautious and much-debated development of technologies that can, in the right hands and the right conditions, give a person temporary access to states of consciousness that they had not previously known were available to them.

I want to be careful here, because this is where the vision of utopia is most easily misunderstood. This is not a world of permanent bliss. Permanent bliss is not actually a coherent goal; it would require the elimination of the contrast that makes experience meaningful, and also of the struggle and uncertainty and occasional grief that seem to be constitutive of what humans recognize as a good life. The people of this world suffer. They lose people they love. They fail at things that matter to them. They sit with uncertainty and regret.

But the baseline is different. And more importantly, the *range* is different. The ceiling of what is available to an ordinary person, in an ordinary week, in the way of genuine beauty and genuine aliveness and genuine connection, is higher than anything that was normal or expected in 2025.

And there is a practice for approaching that ceiling. Not a formula — the people of this world have learned to be deeply suspicious of formulas — but a set of accumulated wisdoms, personal and communal, for how to live in a way that keeps the door open to those moments. How to notice them when they come. How to share them with other people. How to let them change you.

In the attic of our minds, we have always kept a drawer for the notion of a higher state of being. In this world, people open that drawer regularly. They take out what's inside and look at it in the light. They talk about it with each other. They treat it not as a private mystery but as a shared project — the project of figuring out, together, what it actually means for a human life to go well.

---

The path from here to there is not a single road. It is a thousand overlapping paths, most of them undramatic: a piece of legislation that makes a medication affordable, a school curriculum that teaches children how to pay attention, a scientific paper that overturns a harmful assumption, a community that figures out how to disagree well, a technology that is deployed with more caution than was strictly necessary because the people deploying it had learned, from history, to be afraid of their own overconfidence.

None of these steps, taken individually, looks like utopia-building. Most of them look like ordinary life. But that is the point. The world I am describing is not a world that was built all at once by people who knew what they were building. It is a world that accumulated, the way a coral reef accumulates: slowly, through the death and deposit of countless small organisms, each doing only what it can do, none of them aware of the structure they are together creating.

The structure is not finished. It will never be finished. The people of this world are clear-eyed about this, and it does not depress them — it interests them. There is still so much to understand about what minds are capable of, so much of the ocean that remains unswum, so many symphonies that have not yet been written in the key that evolution, for all its ingenuity, never quite reached.

They are working on it.

So can we.

What's needed is not a blueprint, and not a revolution, and not a messiah, and not a god from the machine. What's needed is for enough people to take seriously — with the same matter-of-fact seriousness they bring to the question of whether they will get ice cream by the end of the week — the possibility that all of this is real; that the best moment you have ever had is not a fluke or a gift or an accident, but evidence; that the universe is actually this good, and that we have done very little yet to find out just how good it gets.

Keep the ember.

The bonfire is possible.
