# The World That Learns to Want More

## A Vision of the Near and Far Future

---

There is a moment, familiar to anyone who has ever been truly absorbed in something they love, when the ordinary texture of the world falls away. The problem you're working on, the music you're playing, the conversation you're having—it becomes *real* in a way the rest of life often isn't. The clock means nothing. Your boundaries with the task dissolve. Later, when you try to describe it, the words come out wrong. "Flow state," psychologists call it, a term clinical enough to sand off the experience's edges. What it actually feels like is closer to: *oh, so this is what I'm for.*

Most people touch this feeling rarely. The conditions are hard to arrange—you need the right problem, the right skill level, enough uninterrupted time, freedom from anxiety about money or status or the thousand administrative burdens of modern life. The tragedy of our present world is not primarily that it is cruel, though it is sometimes cruel. It is that it is *wasteful*. Billions of minds equipped for depth, consigned to shallows. What follows is an account of a future in which that waste is largely repaired—not through any single revolution, but through a long sequence of practical changes whose cumulative effect reshapes what a human life can be.

This future is not guaranteed. It is not inevitable. But it is genuinely available to us, if we don't destroy it first—and knowing that it's available is itself a reason to fight for it.

---

## The First Generation: 2025–2045

### The End of Administrative Life

Begin with something small but not trivial: the death of the inbox.

By the 2030s, AI systems competent enough to handle most routine coordination tasks have become ubiquitous. This sounds modest. It isn't. Consider how much of a typical adult's cognitive bandwidth is consumed not by thinking but by *routing*—scheduling appointments, filing forms, navigating insurance claims, managing returns, translating between bureaucratic systems that were never designed to speak to each other. Economists have estimated that the average American spends roughly a quarter of their working life on tasks that produce no output they would recognize as meaningful. For people in lower-income brackets, who cannot outsource these burdens to accountants and personal assistants, the proportion is higher.

By 2040, an AI agent handles most of this. You specify what you want in natural language—"I need to resolve a billing dispute with my health insurer, and I want them to cover the procedure my doctor recommended"—and the system negotiates, documents, follows up, escalates when necessary, and reports back. It doesn't get tired. It doesn't get put on hold for forty-five minutes and then accidentally hung up on. This isn't science fiction; it is a straightforward extrapolation of systems already beginning to function in 2025, and it clears roughly ten hours per week from the average person's life.

Those ten hours compound.

The person who previously spent Sunday afternoons battling health insurance now uses that time for something else. Some read. Some learn an instrument. Some spend more time with their children. Some do nothing in particular, and find—to their surprise—that "nothing in particular" means watching their thoughts without agenda, which is its own kind of valuable. The philosopher Simone Weil wrote that attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. When you stop hemorrhaging it into administrative friction, you discover you have more than you knew.

### The Democratization of Expertise

Simultaneously, a quieter revolution: expertise becomes abundant.

The 2 sigma problem in education—Benjamin Bloom's 1984 finding that individual tutoring produces achievement gains two standard deviations above classroom instruction—was one of the best-documented and least-acted-upon findings in educational research. The reason for the inaction was obvious: individual tutors are expensive. You can't staff every child with one. By the mid-2030s, AI tutoring systems competent enough to approximate the gains Bloom measured are available for free to anyone with an internet connection.

The effects are measurable within a decade. In countries that implement AI-assisted education aggressively, the gap between children from wealthy and poor families narrows significantly on every academic metric. More importantly—and harder to measure—the gap in *confidence* narrows. The experience of having a patient, infinitely available tutor who adjusts to your pace and never makes you feel stupid for asking a question twice turns out to matter enormously. Children who grew up feeling that school was a place where their inadequacy was revealed weekly now experience learning as a place where their capacity expands daily.

The same dynamics apply outside childhood. The 45-year-old who wants to understand the legal implications of starting a small business can now have a conversation with an AI advisor as sophisticated as any human lawyer, at no cost. The farmer in rural Bangladesh who wants to optimize her crop yield for changing weather patterns has access to agronomic expertise that would previously have required an agricultural university. The aspiring writer in rural Appalachia gets detailed, calibrated feedback on her work. Not all of them produce masterpieces. But the constraint on what's possible for any individual person loosens in ways that accumulate across a civilization.

There is an important thing to say here about what expertise *is* in this world. The AI advisor is not infallible. It makes mistakes. People learn, reasonably quickly, to treat it as a very good first opinion rather than a final authority—the way a sensible person treats a knowledgeable friend, rather than the way a credulous person treats a sacred text. This turns out to be fine. The friction of mild skepticism keeps humans in the loop without sacrificing most of the benefits.

### The Coordination Revolution

A subtler change unfolds in parallel: the cost of coordination drops by an order of magnitude.

This matters because most large human problems are, at their root, coordination problems. Climate change is not primarily a technology problem—we have had the technology to decarbonize for decades. It is a coordination problem, a collective action problem, a problem of aligning incentives across nations and corporations and individuals who all have private reasons to defect. The same is true of pandemic preparedness, nuclear nonproliferation, antibiotic resistance, the governance of emerging technologies.

AI systems that can negotiate, translate between interests, model the second-order effects of proposed agreements, and monitor compliance don't solve these problems automatically. But they dramatically reduce their difficulty. International climate negotiations that previously required years of painful shuttle diplomacy can be aided by systems that continuously model the policy space, identify solutions that most parties would accept, and flag when proposed agreements are likely to be violated before anyone has committed to them.

This doesn't eliminate geopolitical conflict. It doesn't make nations trust each other. But it changes the cost-benefit calculation of cooperation. When the AI can show you, with reasonable confidence, that the proposed trade agreement benefits both sides more than the alternative, and when the AI can monitor compliance automatically, the space of achievable agreements expands. Over twenty years, this compounds into a measurably more cooperative world—not peaceful in some utopian sense, but less prone to the specific failure mode of wars fought because both sides genuinely disagreed about who would win.

## The Middle Generation: 2045–2080

### The Medical Revolution

By mid-century, the thing that matters most in human life—its length and quality—has changed dramatically.

AI-accelerated drug discovery is not a metaphor. The process of developing a new therapeutic has historically taken twelve to fifteen years and cost over a billion dollars, with a failure rate above ninety percent. AI systems capable of modeling protein folding, simulating drug interactions at scale, and designing clinical trials compress this timeline drastically. By 2050, the pace of medical progress has roughly tripled. Diseases that were death sentences in 2025—certain pancreatic cancers, most forms of ALS, the majority of antibiotic-resistant infections—have effective treatments.

More importantly, the AI systems are beginning to crack the fundamental biology of aging itself. This is not the province of breathless futurism; it is the direction that geroscience has been pointing for thirty years, delayed primarily by the slowness of biological research cycles rather than by any principled obstacle. By 2060, the first generation of aging-modulation therapies enters widespread use. They do not grant immortality. They extend healthy function. A person who reaches sixty in 2065 can expect, with reasonable confidence, to remain in full cognitive and physical health until ninety-five or beyond.

What this does to the texture of a human life is hard to overstate. The urgency that pervades modern existence—the feeling that you must achieve things *now*, before your body fails you, before your mind slows, before time runs out—relaxes. Not into complacency, but into something more like the attitude of a person who knows they have enough. More time for depth. More time to repair relationships you'd thought were beyond repair. More time to become, with patience, the person you always intended to be.

Death is not eliminated in this world. It is rarer and, when it comes, less often a theft. The grief when someone dies at ninety-eight still real, but different in character from the grief when someone dies at forty-three of a disease that, a generation later, would have been curable.

### The Post-Scarcity Transition

By 2060, in most wealthy countries and an increasing number of middle-income ones, the basic material conditions of life are secured for essentially everyone.

This comes through a combination of channels: AI-driven productivity growth that makes goods and services dramatically cheaper; political choices to distribute those gains through universal basic services and guaranteed income floors; and a cultural shift that happens slowly and then suddenly, in which the experience of genuine poverty becomes recognized as the anomaly it actually is rather than the regrettable inevitability it was previously treated as.

The outcome doesn't look like the French bourgeoisie or Burning Man, though it has elements of both. It looks like a world where the question "what are you going to do with your life?" has genuinely changed character. It is no longer primarily a question about economic survival. It is a question about meaning, contribution, and identity—which are harder questions, but better ones to be faced with.

Some people, freed from economic necessity, do nothing much in particular for a while. They watch too much television, they sleep late, they feel purposeless. This is a transition problem, not a terminal condition. The same happened to retirees in the twentieth century—the first years of retirement are often disorienting before people find their footing. What comes after, for most people, is a gradual migration toward what they actually care about.

What people actually care about, it turns out, is neither as selfish as cynics predicted nor as altruistic as idealists hoped. Most people want to be good at something. They want to contribute to something larger than themselves. They want close relationships, meaningful work, beauty in their lives, and the experience of growing. These wants don't require much philosophical sophistication to satisfy; they require primarily the time and stability to pursue them. Post-scarcity provides the stability. The time, people largely use well.

The social competitions and status hierarchies that worried the pessimists don't disappear. Humans will always find something to compete over. But the thing being competed over shifts, gradually, from positional goods—money, rank, the outward markers of success—toward intrinsic goods: craft, mastery, contribution, the esteem of people you actually respect. The artist who earns less than a surgeon but whose work moves people to tears is no longer making a heroic sacrifice; they are simply living their life as they chose to live it, which is unremarkable and fine.

## The Far Horizon: 2080 and Beyond

### Minds We Can Barely Imagine

Here is where honesty requires a kind of double vision.

On one hand: everything described above is concrete, achievable, and represents genuine, enormous improvement in human life. A world of ten more years of healthy life for the average person, AI tutors that close educational inequality, coordination systems that make international cooperation dramatically easier, post-material abundance for most of humanity—this is already a world worth fighting for. This alone would justify any amount of effort.

On the other hand: it is almost certainly not where the story ends.

The history of human cognition is a history of boundary dissolution. We have repeatedly discovered that what we thought were limits were actually only current conditions. The printing press revealed that literacy was not an elite capacity. Public education revealed that sustained abstract reasoning was not a rare gift. It is a reasonable guess that we have not yet found the actual limits of what a human mind, properly supported and developed, can achieve.

By the end of the twenty-first century, if we navigate the transition well, the question of what a mind can be has opened up in ways that are genuinely hard to describe from 2025. Not because the possibilities are mystical or supernatural, but because they are as far from our current experience as our current experience is from that of our ancestors who first developed written language. The tools for extending and enhancing cognition—brain-computer interfaces, AI augmentation, better pharmacology, better understanding of how to train and develop minds—will have been available for decades. The people who grew up with those tools will not think the way we do, in the same way that we do not think the way people did before mass literacy.

What they will think about, and what their lives will feel like, is beyond confident prediction. What we can say is this: the peak experiences that humans have occasionally and with effort—the moments of total absorption, profound connection, overwhelming beauty, the sense of being fully alive and fully present—those experiences point in a direction. They are evidence that human minds can go further than they usually go. A civilization that has solved the problems of material poverty and poor health and poor education, and that has a century to work on developing what minds can do, will have traveled considerably further in that direction than we have.

This is not a promise of perfection. This future will have its own problems, its own conflicts, its own forms of loss and failure and grief. No world eliminates those. What it won't have, in anything like current quantities, is the specific suffering that comes from unnecessary poverty, preventable disease, wasted potential, and lives cut short before they found their shape.

## The Path from Here to There

The distance from 2025 to this future is real, and it runs through genuine dangers.

The most serious is also the most obvious: the AI systems that make this future possible could, if developed carelessly, produce catastrophes instead. The path to beneficial AI requires sustained, serious work on alignment—ensuring that increasingly capable AI systems remain oriented toward human flourishing rather than instrumental goals that diverge from it. This is not a given. It requires choices.

The second danger is political. The gains from AI could flow to a small class of people rather than broadly. They probably will, if the only forces shaping distribution are market incentives without political counterweight. The history of the industrial revolution offers both warning and encouragement here: industrialization created vast inequality before it created prosperity, and the mechanism by which its gains became broadly shared was conscious political effort—labor movements, welfare states, public education, progressive taxation. The same effort will be required with AI, and there is no guarantee it will succeed. But it is not a difficult thing to want; it is only sometimes a difficult thing to achieve.

The third danger is subtler: we might get the material improvements without the deeper ones. A world where everyone has enough food and a pleasant AI companion and a long healthy life, but where nobody develops depth, relationships lack genuine intimacy, and creative work is mostly consumed rather than done—this world would be comfortable and hollow. It is achievable, and it would represent a kind of failure.

Against this failure, the best protection is clarity about what we're actually trying to achieve. Not comfort. Not even happiness, exactly, though happiness matters. What we're trying to achieve is the full expression of what human beings are capable of—the art and the science and the love and the discovery and the hard-won wisdom—in as many people, for as long as possible. That goal is worth holding onto carefully, because the path to it runs close to the path to the hollow version, and it's easy to take the wrong fork without noticing.

---

## What This Requires of Us

The last thing to say is about the present moment, and what it asks of us.

We are living in the period before the transition—close enough to see it clearly, early enough to shape it. The choices made in the next twenty years will constrain what's possible for centuries. This is not a comfortable position, but it is an interesting one. It means that the work done now—on AI safety, on AI governance, on the political economy of distribution, on preserving and strengthening the institutions that make democratic accountability possible—is unusually important.

It also means keeping the positive vision in clear view. Not as a fantasy that exempts us from present difficulties, but as a genuine image of what we're working toward. People work better when they know what winning looks like. They make wiser tradeoffs when they understand what's at stake on both sides—not just what's to be lost if things go badly, but what's to be gained if things go well.

There are oceans we haven't yet swum. There are forms of human flourishing we haven't yet conceived. There are relationships of depth and quality that the busiest, most exhausted, most materially constrained people of our era have had neither the time nor the stability to develop. There are minds—right now, distributed across the world in bodies that don't have enough food or access to good teachers or freedom from the anxiety of poverty—that would, given the chance, produce things of great beauty and usefulness and meaning.

The future described here gives them that chance. It gives all of us that chance. It is available. It is not guaranteed. And the distance between available and guaranteed is exactly the space where human choice and effort live.

That seems like a good place to direct them.
