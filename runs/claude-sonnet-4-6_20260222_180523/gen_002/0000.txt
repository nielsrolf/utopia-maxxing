# The Coordination Dividend: A Vision of the World We Could Build

## Prologue: Two Failure Modes

The twentieth century gave us two great cautionary tales about utopian thinking. The first was the utopia that tried to *force* outcomes—collectivizing farms at gunpoint, purging dissenters, bending reality to ideology until millions died. The second was subtler: the utopia that never tried at all, that mistook the current equilibrium for the natural order, that watched coordination failures compound into climate catastrophe and institutional decay while insisting that any intervention would be worse.

The world described here threads between these failures. It did not emerge from a revolution or a strongman or a sudden technological rupture. It emerged from a long, unglamorous, deeply human process: people gradually getting better at noticing when they were in a bad equilibrium, building the tools and institutions to escape it, and then—crucially—teaching their children what they'd learned.

This is not the story of a perfect world. It is the story of a world that got serious.

---

## I. The Great Realization (2020s–2060s)

The turning point, historians would later argue, was not a single invention or a single crisis. It was the slow accumulation of a new kind of literacy.

In the early twenty-first century, a small but growing fraction of the population—economists, game theorists, rationalists, effective altruists, policy researchers—had developed precise language for a class of problems that had always existed but had never been clearly named. They could distinguish a coordination failure from a market failure from a commons tragedy from a principal-agent problem. They understood Goodhart's Law not as a clever academic observation but as a live danger that infected every institution they cared about. They could look at a bad equilibrium and ask: what would it take for everyone to simultaneously prefer to jump?

For decades, this knowledge lived mostly in universities and research papers. Then something changed.

The change was partly technological. The collapse of several major social media platforms in the late 2020s—not from regulation but from a coordinated mass exodus by users who had finally, viscerally understood what attention-harvesting was doing to them—created a brief, chaotic period of experimentation in how humans communicated at scale. The platforms that survived and thrived were the ones that had been designed with different incentives: forums where you could only post if you cited a source, prediction markets integrated into news feeds, reputation systems that tracked whether your confident claims turned out to be correct.

These tools were imperfect. They were gamed. They were frustrating. But they began to make something visible that had always been invisible: the difference between *performing* good reasoning and *doing* it.

The change was also educational. A generation of teachers, radicalized by watching their students become perfect targets for manipulation, began doing something simple and radical: teaching the names of things. Confirmation bias. Sunk cost fallacy. The prisoner's dilemma. Asymmetric information. The tragedy of the commons. Not as abstract curiosities but as *here is the thing that is happening to you right now, here is why it feels so natural, here is what the trap looks like from the inside*.

By mid-century, this kind of thinking—let's call it coordinacy, the literacy of collective action—was roughly where basic statistical reasoning had been in 1990: still rare, still unevenly distributed, but no longer exotic. Perhaps fifteen percent of adults in wealthy countries could, when prompted, correctly identify a coordination problem and describe what a resolution would require. Among people under thirty, the number was closer to thirty percent.

This was enough. Not to solve everything—not even close—but to create a critical mass of citizens who could recognize when their institutions were failing and articulate why. Crucially, they could also recognize when proposed "solutions" were themselves coordination traps in disguise.

## II. The Economy, Redesigned from the Ground Up

The economic system that emerged over the following century was not capitalism, and it was not socialism. It was something that people in the early twenty-first century had glimpsed in fragments—in Henry George's writings on land value, in Ostrom's work on commons governance, in the mechanism design literature, in the scattered experiments with prediction markets and quadratic voting—but had never assembled into a coherent whole.

The foundation was a principle that eventually seemed obvious: *you should capture the value you create, and you should pay for the value you consume that you didn't create*.

Raw land and natural resources were auctioned on continuous leases. You could bid for the right to use a piece of land or extract a mineral, but you paid ongoing rent calibrated to what someone else would bid for it today. This wasn't punitive—it simply prevented the accumulation of unearned windfall from holding resources others needed. The revenue flowed into a common fund, distributed as a citizen's dividend: not quite enough to live on in comfort, but enough to prevent the most brutal forms of desperation.

Labor markets ran hot. This was not an accident; the Very Serious People (as they came to be called, with affection and mild irony) understood early that a labor market where employees held bargaining power was a precondition for almost everything else they wanted. The combination of the citizen's dividend, low barriers to starting small enterprises, and aggressive antitrust enforcement against monopsony meant that by 2100, the concept of being "grateful to have a job" was as archaic as being grateful the feudal lord had allowed you to farm his land. Employers competed intensely for workers. Abusive workplaces bled talent. The question was not whether you could find work but which work suited you.

Intellectual property had been rebuilt from scratch. Patents as they existed in the twentieth century—monopoly rights that blocked anyone from using an idea—were recognized as exactly the kind of coordination trap that earlier thinkers had described: multiple parties each with veto power, each trying to extract all the value, the result being that useful things didn't get made or got made much more slowly than they should have. The replacement was a mandatory licensing system with a strong honor norm: if your invention generated value for an industry, fifteen percent of that marginal value flowed back to you. Accounting agencies verified the numbers publicly. Your neighbors and colleagues could see whether you had paid what you owed to the people whose shoulders you stood on. Most people paid. Not because they were angels, but because the social cost of being known as someone who didn't was real and lasting.

Copyright was simple: fourteen years, then public domain, no exceptions.

## III. Governance Without Leviathan

The political structure that emerged was harder to describe to someone from the early twenty-first century than almost anything else about this world, because it required letting go of the assumption that "governance" meant "a monopoly on legitimate force concentrated in a nation-state."

Nations still existed, in the sense that there were large regions with shared legal infrastructure. But they had shed most of their identity-claims. You did not belong to your country the way you once had; you belonged to a complex web of organizations, cities, professional associations, and regional experiments, each of which provided some public goods in exchange for some taxes, and from which you could exit if you chose. The result was something like a marketplace for governance, except that the goods being provided—roads, courts, schools, basic security—were actually public goods rather than commodities, and the coordination problems involved in providing them were handled by institutions that had been explicitly designed to handle coordination problems.

Courts were radically transparent. Every decision came with a written explanation of the reasoning. Senior judges reviewed the reasoning, not just the outcome. If two courts hearing similar cases reached different conclusions, this was not quietly papered over; it was treated as a significant public event, an invitation for the Very Serious People to have the Very Serious Debate about which reasoning was better.

The closest thing to a legislature in most regions was a multilevel delegation system: you chose someone to represent your interests in a particular domain, and that person could in turn delegate to specialists in subdomains, and you could revoke your delegation at any time. The result was not perfect—nothing was perfect—but it made visible what had always been invisible: exactly who was speaking for whom, with what mandate, on what topic.

Elections for positions with real executive power still existed. But the descriptions attached to those positions were ruthlessly precise. A mayor was not "the leader of the city." A mayor was "the executive officer of the municipal corporation, serving at the discretion of the delegate assembly, with specific authority over X, Y, and Z, subject to recall by petition of fifteen percent of registered residents." This probably sounds tedious. It was. It was also surprisingly effective at preventing people from accumulating authority they hadn't been granted.

There was no standing army in the traditional sense. There were treaty-bound mutual defense arrangements, rapid-response forces governed by multilateral oversight, and—not discussed openly but understood by everyone who needed to understand it—carefully maintained capabilities for responding to existential threats. These capabilities were managed by people who understood that *actually using them* represented a catastrophic failure of every other system, and were designed accordingly.

## IV. The Joy of the Thing

Let it not be thought that this was a world of gray efficiency, of earnest people solving coordination problems and carefully tracking their reputational scores.

Art flourished. The collapse of the attention economy had been wrenching, but what grew in its place was stranger and wilder and more interesting. Without the pressure of maximizing engagement, musicians made music for people who would sit and listen. Novelists wrote books meant to be read twice, built around foreshadowing so deep that only a second reading revealed it. Theater was everywhere, because theater is cheap to produce and expensive to broadcast, which turned out to be the right trade-off.

Food had become an obsession. There was something about a world that had finally gotten serious about coordination that produced an almost childlike delight in the things that didn't require coordination—a bowl of soup, a piece of bread, the particular pleasure of eating outside in good weather. Farmers' markets had not survived as an alternative to industrial agriculture; they had become industrial agriculture, or rather, industrial agriculture had learned to produce what farmers' markets had always promised.

Cities were beautiful. Not uniformly beautiful—there were cities that prized density and cities that prized space and cities that prized silence, and people sorted among them according to their preferences. But the persistent ugliness of the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries—the strip malls, the parking lots, the deliberate aesthetic nihilism of development that had no interest in the people who would have to live with it—had been addressed by the simple mechanism of requiring that the economic rents generated by urban land be reinvested in the urban commons. If your building made the neighborhood worse, you were not capturing all of that cost; if your building made it better, you were not capturing all of that value. Over time, the incentives aligned.

Children grew up with enormous freedom and enormous responsibility. The transition from childhood to adult work was not a cliff crossed at eighteen or twenty-two but a long slope, beginning with older children teaching younger ones, continuing through apprenticeships and short trials, managed by a professional matching class who thought about nothing but helping people find work that suited them. The notion that you would spend four years studying something before spending a single day trying to actually do it had been recognized as obviously absurd sometime in the 2040s and quietly abandoned.

## V. The Expansion Ahead

In the early twenty-second century, the most consequential decisions were not about how to divide up existing wealth. They were about whether to take the expansion seriously.

The possibility had been understood in abstract for generations: that AI-enabled automation could increase the total productive capacity of human civilization by factors that made all previous economic growth look trivial. The question was not whether this was possible but whether it could be managed in a way that preserved everything worth preserving about human life while eliminating everything worth eliminating.

The decision to manage the development of artificial general intelligence slowly—not suppressing it, but deliberately constraining the rate of capability gain while alignment research caught up—was the most consequential act of coordination in human history. It did not feel like a single decision because it wasn't; it was thousands of decisions, made by researchers and funders and institutions and governments, all of whom had been shaped by a century of practice at identifying coordination problems and building mechanisms to solve them. They understood what they were doing. They understood the cost—accepting slower growth for decades—and they accepted it, because they understood what the alternative looked like.

By the time the expansion began in earnest, in the latter half of the twenty-second century, something important had happened. The incentive landscape had shifted so dramatically that the old zero-sum logic no longer applied. When the total available resource base increases by a factor of a thousand, the difference between receiving a "fair" share and receiving a "greedy" share—viewed from the original position—is genuinely small. The Very Serious People had been explaining this for a long time. People had come to believe it.

This did not mean conflict disappeared. It meant that conflict had shrunk to its proper scale: arguments about which experiments to run, which values to pursue, which version of the good life to try. Arguments worth having. Arguments that left the combatants better informed at the end.

## VI. What Was Lost, Honestly

Honesty requires acknowledgment of what this world surrendered.

Some kinds of spontaneity were harder. When everyone around you has thought carefully about the second-order effects of their choices, the first-order pleasures become slightly more self-conscious. People noticed this and argued about it constantly.

Some forms of beauty that had emerged from conflict and suffering were rarer. The blues required something to be blue about. The traditions that grew from necessity—blues music from oppression, certain religious practices from persecution—could be studied and appreciated and even continued, but not regenerated from the conditions that produced them.

The world was more legible. This was mostly good. But legibility meant that certain kinds of mystery—the mystery of institutions whose logic no one understood, the mystery of customs whose origins were forgotten, the mystery of living inside a system too large to think about—had diminished. People found this loss real and sometimes grieved it.

And there were people for whom this world was simply not their world: people whose joy came from transgression, from fighting an unjust system, from the particular pleasure of being misunderstood. A just and well-coordinated world was not easy on the romantic rebel. It provided other pleasures, but not that one.

---

## Epilogue: The Point

The point was never efficiency. That was a persistent misunderstanding.

The point was that a hundred million people did not need to die of preventable disease this decade. The point was that the child born into poverty did not need to stay there. The point was that the species did not need to cook its own planet. The point was that the next century did not need to look like the last one.

The people who built this world were not saints. They were people who had learned—slowly, imperfectly, with enormous backsliding and failure—to see coordination problems clearly and take them seriously. They were people who understood that "this is how things are" and "this is how things must be" are different claims, and that confusing them is the most expensive mistake a civilization can make.

They built institutions that were accountable and transparent and designed to fail gracefully rather than catastrophically. They taught their children not what to think but how to notice when their thinking was going wrong. They expanded the resource base until the most vicious zero-sum conflicts dissolved into positive-sum abundance. They did all this while remaining, recognizably, human: petty and funny and grief-stricken and in love and confused and occasionally brilliant.

The world they built was not paradise. It was something better: a world that knew the difference between what it was and what it could be, and kept working on the gap.
