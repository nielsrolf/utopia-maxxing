# The World That Learned to Want the Right Things

## A Dispatch from 2147

---

The first thing visitors notice about Porto Experimentale is the smell of bread.

Not because bread is particularly remarkable, but because the bakery on the Via Della Coordinazione is owned by a seventy-three-year-old former macroeconomist named Chiara who decided, fifteen years ago, that she wanted to understand fermentation from the inside. She spent eight months apprenticing with older bakers before her first solo loaf. This is not unusual. What is unusual, compared to the world of two centuries ago, is that nobody told her this was a waste of her expertise. Nobody made her justify the transition to a credentials board. Nobody denied her a license. The economy was running hot enough—and the safety net was woven tight enough—that she simply did the thing.

The bread is extraordinary.

---

Let me describe the world as it is, not as it was promised.

In 2147, approximately four billion people live on Earth, distributed across what are called *tesseres*—interlocking regional communities that range in population from a few thousand to several million, each with its own elected character but all operating within a shared constitutional framework. The number four billion often surprises newcomers to history who remember the projections of twelve billion. The decline was not catastrophic. It was, in the end, chosen—slowly, unevenly, imperfectly, but chosen. As prosperity spread and women gained full economic standing, birth rates fell. The demographic transition that demographers had predicted for centuries finally completed itself, once the structural barriers that had trapped so many families in poverty were systematically dissolved.

The planet is measurably greener than it was in 2024. About forty percent of Earth's land surface is now rewilded or lightly managed, threaded through with human settlements rather than encircling them. This happened because the Georgist land-rent reforms of the late twenty-first century made land hoarding economically irrational—you pay rent on land in proportion to its productive value, whether you use it or not—which meant vast tracts of speculative holdings were released and, in many cases, allowed to return to forest. The forests are not museums. People live at their edges, harvest from them carefully, walk through them daily. Children in the tesseres learn the names of trees the way children in earlier centuries learned multiplication tables: as basic literacy.

The atmosphere stabilized. Not without cost, not without loss. The transition cost generations of political struggle and two catastrophic decades of managed retreat from coastlines. The grief of those losses is part of the cultural inheritance now—woven into architecture, memorial practice, and the design of civic spaces in ways that are neither fetishized nor forgotten. In Porto Experimentale, the central square is built forty centimeters lower than the surrounding streets, a deliberate inverted bowl that fills with water during heavy rains. Children wade in it. It is called *la memoria*. Everyone knows why.

---

To understand how the world coordinates, you have to understand that it now teaches coordination the way it once taught reading.

This is the single most important change, and it happened slowly enough that no one can point to a moment when it began. Sometime in the middle of the twenty-first century, a critical mass of educators, backed by a critical mass of evidence, concluded that the failure to teach basic economics, game theory, and social epistemology in primary schools was not a minor curricular gap but a civilizational hazard—roughly equivalent to teaching children to speak but not to read. Children who understood what a Nash equilibrium was, who had played cooperative games that failed because of defection and succeeded because of trust, who had seen with their own eyes how rational individuals could produce collectively irrational outcomes—these children became adults who could *see* coordination problems rather than merely suffering them.

The curriculum is not ideological. It does not tell children what to think about redistribution or property or markets. It teaches them to recognize the shape of a problem before debating its solution. It teaches them that "everyone would benefit if everyone did X, but everyone has an incentive to do Y" is a recognizable category of problem with recognizable categories of solution, and that defaulting to outrage or blame or the assumption of individual malice is usually a slower path to fixing it than asking who needs to move first, and what would make it worth their while.

The consequences of this reform are still reverberating. Adults who grew up with it are notably harder to manipulate with simple us-versus-them framings. They are also, on average, better at *actually helping* people they care about, because they have some model of how incentive structures affect behavior even when intentions are good. They are not uniformly wise. They are not incorruptible. But the baseline has shifted, and the shift is measurable in the quality of public deliberation, in the half-life of bad policies, and in the speed with which obvious improvements to civic systems now get implemented.

The Very Serious People of this era—the Chroniclers, the regional delegates, the senior economists who write for the networks—are not smarter than their predecessors. They are operating in an information environment that is less aggressively stupid. The institutions that fund them have figured out, after a century of trying, how to pay for reliable reasoning rather than for confident-sounding conclusions. Prediction markets, calibration tracking, and adversarial review are now as routine in policy deliberation as footnotes are in academic papers. Not perfect. Nothing is perfect. But when a Chronicler publishes an analysis, it comes with a track record. The track record is public. People read it.

---

The tessere system emerged from the collapse of the old nation-state framework, which did not happen all at once but eroded over the course of a century as trade barriers fell, as climate governance required genuinely transnational institutions, and as the slow recognition spread that you could not actually govern well at a scale where you could not know your representatives and they could not know you.

A tessere is not a city and not a commune. It is more like a civic organism with a metabolic rate. Each tessere controls its own land use, its own public institutions, its own local customs. Some tesseres are loud and dense and experimental; Porto Experimentale, as its name suggests, is one of these—it has tried at various times a four-day work week, a six-day work week, universal childcare funded entirely by land rents, and a radical street design program that eliminated private automobiles from most of its area. Some tesseres are quiet and traditional, organized around religious communities or craft traditions or deliberate simplicity. What they share is a common constitutional layer—a set of rights and obligations that are not negotiable at the local level—and open borders between them.

The freedom to move between tesseres is considered fundamental. It is not unlimited in practice—moving costs money, moving costs social capital, moving means leaving people you love—but the legal right is absolute. This freedom is what makes the tessere system function. A tessere that treats its members badly loses them. A tessere that cannot attract new members slowly ages out. The competition is not economic competition in the sense of a race to the bottom on wages; the constitutional layer prevents that. It is more like an ongoing civilizational experiment in what kinds of communities people actually want to live in, run simultaneously across several thousand different test cases.

The results have been informative, surprising, and in some cases humbling to theorists of all persuasions. It turns out that a significant fraction of people, given genuine freedom to choose, choose communities organized around religious practice—but the religions that thrive tend to be the ones that have modernized their relationship to doubt and to science. It turns out that experimental communities that adopt radical governance structures often fail within a generation, but occasionally produce innovations that spread system-wide. It turns out that the majority of people, offered the full menu of human social organization, choose something that would look fairly ordinary to a person from 2024, but with better architecture, more green space, shorter working hours, and a stronger sense that the people around them are actually paying attention to each other.

The ordinary life of this world is, in many respects, genuinely ordinary. People fall in love and lose each other. They care for aging parents and raise complicated children. They have careers that matter to them and jobs that bore them and hobbies that consume them entirely. They disagree about politics. They eat dinner. They worry about whether they are living well enough, and they frequently conclude that they are not, and they sometimes take steps to fix this and sometimes simply continue worrying.

What is different is the quality of the infrastructure within which this ordinary life is lived.

---

The matching problem—who does what work, for whom, at what price, with what support—was recognized early in this era as a civilizational allocation challenge of the first order, and it received civilizational attention. The result is something that would look strange to a person from the early twenty-first century, when a young person was expected to choose a field of study before they had spent a single day working in it, accumulate credentials for years at great expense, and then spend the rest of their life in the field they had chosen at eighteen, or suffer the social stigma of being a person who had "failed to launch."

In the current system, the early years of a young person's development are explicitly organized around encounter. Children and adolescents cycle through a wide variety of real work environments—not simulations, not classroom units, but actual supervised participation in actual skilled labor—long before any commitment is required. This is not romanticized apprenticeship. It is structured, assessed, and supported by both human mentors and powerful matching algorithms that track demonstrated aptitudes and interests over time. The commitment, when it comes, is to a first sustained focus, not to a permanent identity. Moving between fields is normal, expected, and supported. Chiara the baker is not an eccentric; she is a person living in a system that was designed with enough flexibility to accommodate her.

The labor market runs hot by design. The Georgist reforms, combined with progressive consumption taxes and a universal baseline income funded by natural resource rents, mean that no one is compelled to work by hunger alone. This changes the negotiating position of workers fundamentally. Employers compete for labor rather than the reverse. Abusive workplaces lose workers quickly and find it difficult to attract replacements. Good workplaces grow. The feedback loop works imperfectly, but it works, and the direction of its pressure is legible.

---

Among the things this world got wrong that are still being corrected:

The early tessere experiments badly underestimated how much social friction would be generated by communities with strong shared identities living in proximity to communities with incompatible values. The constitutional layer holds, mostly, but the work of what is now called *boundary diplomacy* is unending, subtle, and genuinely hard. There are tesseres that have made choices their neighbors find abhorrent, and the mechanisms for intervention—when does a community's internal practice become a violation of the constitutional layer?—are contested in the courts every decade, redrawn, contested again.

The matching algorithms, for all their sophistication, have a documented tendency to channel people toward roles they are likely to succeed at rather than roles they most want to inhabit. This is not sinister; it is structural. The people who work on fixing it are called vocational ethicists, and they are among the most philosophically sophisticated practitioners in the system.

The Chroniclers are not, despite their training and their track records, immune to groupthink. There have been notable failures of civilizational attention—things that were quietly happening while everyone was looking at the monitored metrics, and which were obvious in retrospect, and which the Chroniclers reproach themselves for missing. Some of these failures were small. One was not small. It is now called the Slow Emergency, and it lasted twenty years, and the full accounting of it is still in progress.

The world has not solved pain. It has not solved grief. It has not solved the deep discomfort that accompanies genuine moral progress—the recurring discovery that things that seemed fine were not fine, and that the burden of fixing them falls on people who did not cause them. The removal of easily preventable suffering has made the suffering that remains harder to ignore. This is progress. It does not feel like only progress, from the inside.

---

What the world has learned to want, slowly and imperfectly, is something that philosophers spent centuries arguing was too complicated to specify: lives that are both free and entangled, both self-authored and genuinely responsive to others, both challenging enough to be worth living and supported enough to be livable.

This is not a solved problem. It is a direction. The direction has been held for long enough now, through enough failed experiments and successful ones, that it has begun to feel like ground rather than a path—a place you can build from, rather than a place you are always in danger of leaving.

The bread at Chiara's bakery costs four tessere-credits. She bakes two hundred loaves a day, sells them all, and is happy in a way that she describes, when asked, as *piena*—full.

She is aware that her happiness is not the whole story. She is aware that the system that enables her happiness has costs and failures she does not fully see. She is also aware that this awareness is not an argument against the happiness, but an argument for staying attentive, for participating in the civic life of the tessere, for reading the Chroniclers even when they are inconvenient, for showing up.

She shows up. Most people, in this world, show up. Not out of obligation, exactly, and not out of joy, exactly, but out of something that sits between the two—a sense, cultivated over generations and still fragile, that the world they are living in is worth tending.

They tend it. The bread rises. The square fills with water after rain, and children wade in it, and someone always explains to them what it means, and they always, eventually, understand.
