# The Legible World

## On Building a Future Worth Inheriting

---

There is a moment that almost everyone has had, and almost no one talks about directly, because it seems too small to mention and too important to reduce to words.

You are somewhere unremarkable—a kitchen, a garden, a street corner in the early evening—and something happens to the quality of your attention. The usual film of preoccupation thins. You see what is actually in front of you: the specific color of light through a window, the precise weight of what you are carrying, another person's face in its actual particularity rather than as a familiar projection. For a few seconds, or a few minutes, you are not managing your life from a slight distance. You are inside it. Existence reveals itself not as a problem requiring solution but as a place you actually inhabit, strange and specific and sufficient.

Then it closes. The phone. The pain. The arithmetic of obligation. You get back to it.

What I want to describe is a world in which that moment is not the exception. Not because suffering has been abolished or human nature re-engineered into permanent serenity, but because several generations of people—unglamorous, frequently wrong, stubbornly serious—did the patient work of removing what should never have been there, and then, having cleared the ground, began with hard-won humility to build something worthy of it.

I will try to be specific. Vagueness is how futures die.

---

## I. How It Accumulated

The history books of this era do not record a founding moment. There was no rupture, no singularity, no dramatic turn. What they record is something harder to narrate: the gradual improvement of humanity's collective ability to recognize its own mistakes and correct them before they compounded beyond recovery.

The first critical shift was epistemic. Beginning in the mid-twenty-first century, the institutions through which societies made collective decisions became measurably better at knowing what they knew and what they didn't. Probabilistic reasoning spread from academic economics into policy, journalism, and eventually into ordinary cognitive habits. Prediction markets moved from novelty to norm. The change was not dramatic on any given day. But overconfidence, over decades, became less cheap. Leaders who ignored measurable warning signals faced questions that were harder to deflect. Policies rated consistently as likely to fail attracted scrutiny they had previously avoided.

This epistemic improvement had one consequence that turned out to matter most: it made the long view legible. The early twenty-first century had suffered a strange temporal compression—political horizons foreshortened to the next election, corporate horizons collapsed to the next quarter, individual lives oriented around immediate obligation in ways that made the future feel abstract. Better collective forecasting made future consequences visible in the present, which made people willing to pay present costs for future goods. Forests were planted whose shade the planters would not sit in. This came to seem normal again.

The second shift was material, and it cascaded.

The energy transition arrived faster than predicted, driven not by a single breakthrough but by the continued fall of solar costs combined with modular fission reactors—smaller and safer, manufactured in factories, deployed across three continents through the 2040s. Together they produced something no prior civilization had possessed: energy so cheap it ceased to be a meaningful constraint on most human activity. Desalination became trivial. Vertical farming became competitive. The phrase "energy poverty" entered the past tense.

This abundance cascaded outward. Manufacturing costs fell. Automation of construction made building dramatically cheaper. The global housing shortage that had defined urban life for a generation became, city by city, solvable. Not solved overnight, not without political friction—but solved, because the underlying economics shifted enough that building became the path of least resistance. Land value taxation, an idea advocated for a century and a half, found its first significant adoptions in cities desperate enough to try it. The results were favorable enough to spread. Occupational licensing reform freed healthcare delivery from restrictions that had protected incumbent market position far more effectively than public safety. The accumulated legal barnacles of a century were scraped away, one jurisdiction at a time, by the improbable coalition of people who had simply grown tired of what was obviously broken.

None of these changes was utopian in isolation. Each was partial, contested, sometimes reversed, frequently slower than its advocates had hoped. But their aggregate effect was a world whose institutions were more responsive, more epistemically humble, and less captured by incumbent interests than those of the early twenty-first century. Not ideal governance. Substantially better governance. Better in ways that accumulated, the way interest compounds, the way a small course correction early in a voyage means a completely different landfall.

The second century was stranger and harder. With existential catastrophe reduced and material desperation addressed, humanity discovered that it did not know what it actually wanted—which sounds like a luxury problem, and is, but is also genuinely difficult. There were wrong turns: movements that mistook conformity for harmony, movements that mistook atomization for freedom, technologies of pleasure that some people disappeared into. Backlashes so severe that genuine goods were discarded with genuine harms. The historians of this period describe it not as a dark age but as a prolonged and necessary argument—civilization working out, through expensive experiment, what it had been reaching toward all along.

What accumulated across both centuries—slowly, disputedly, never completely—was a particular understanding: that a world worth living in is not a problem to be solved once and then preserved, but a craft. Endlessly practiced, endlessly refined, always unfinished. The greatest achievement of this period was not a scientific discovery or a political institution but an epistemological shift: people became, collectively, better at learning from their own experiments. Better at recognizing when a plausible idea was producing results that were clearly wrong. Better at holding their certainties loosely enough to update them without abandoning them entirely.

This sounds like a small thing. It was not a small thing.

## II. The Body

You wake up in a body that does not hurt.

I want to stay with this, because it is easy to understand intellectually and difficult to feel from inside a time when chronic pain is so ordinary that most people have incorporated it into their sense of who they are. The grinding, pointless suffering of disease and injury and aging became, in the early twenty-first century, a kind of background condition—experienced as personal bad luck rather than political failure, mourned privately rather than addressed collectively.

This changed through two parallel tracks. The first was deployment: getting existing tools to the people who lacked them. Global health organizations, increasingly well-funded by the material abundance of the new era, made dramatic progress on diseases that had never been prioritized because their victims were poor. Malaria was brought to heel through gene drives and new prophylactics and sheer logistical commitment. Deaths from preventable infectious disease fell to a fraction of their early-century levels.

The second track was discovery. The 2030s and 2040s saw the maturation of approaches to aging that had been developing for decades: senolytic therapies that cleared accumulated senescent cells, precision pharmacology that addressed the specific failure modes of specific bodies rather than the statistical average patient, genetic interventions that had moved—carefully, with a rigor born of hard lessons—from theoretical to clinical. The effects were not immortality. They were something more modest and more lasting: measurable improvements in healthspan, the period of life actually spent in good health. People in their eighties remained functional, vigorous, and cognitively sharp at rates simply higher than before.

Pain—not the productive ache of exertion, but the grinding pointless variety—became largely optional for people who wanted to address it. The political will to distribute these tools as public goods, which had lagged behind technical capacity for most of human history, finally caught up. The question of why lifesaving treatments should be rationed by wealth rather than need had always had a clear answer; the political structures that sustained that rationing eroded as their justifications became harder to maintain against the evidence of what was possible.

The effect on consciousness is difficult to overstate. The ordinary human brain, in genuinely good conditions—not chronically stressed, not economically desperate, not sleep-deprived, not in pain—turns out to be extraordinary. Not smarter in the way a faster computer is smarter, but smarter the way a person is smarter when they have slept well, eaten real food, spent time in nature, been genuinely loved, and been given enough unstructured time to let their minds wander and settle and find their own shape.

This alone—the mere absence of something so ordinary that most people would barely name it as suffering—turns out to change the quality of an entire life.

## III. The Shape of a Life

People in this world live longer—typically well past a hundred, with the later decades being for most a period of continued engagement rather than managed decline. But length is a secondary achievement. The primary achievement is shape.

A life here is expected to have distinct phases, each with its own character and demands and pleasures. The first decades are organized around the cultivation of capability. Children learn to observe, to reason, to make things with their hands, to express themselves in multiple modes, to collaborate and disagree productively, to tolerate uncertainty without being paralyzed by it. Content arrives in service of these capacities—a child who has learned to really look at something, really think about it, and clearly say what they found can learn anything. Schools recognize that knowing how to be a good friend—how to sustain a relationship through difficulty, how to repair ruptures, how to be genuinely present with another person—is at least as important as any academic subject, and teach it explicitly, not as a separate class but as the medium through which everything else is taught. Children grow up understanding they belong to something larger than their household. They know their neighbors. They move more freely through their communities, known and cared for by more adults than their parents alone.

The next several decades are typically years of deep engagement—intensive focus on particular crafts, problems, relationships, projects. These are not careers in the old sense because the orientation is different. Scientists follow their actual curiosity. Artists make what interests them. Builders and farmers and physicians and engineers do their work with the unhurried attention that comes from not being in permanent economic terror. Status and income follow as byproducts rather than organizing purposes, which changes the texture of the work entirely.

The middle decades are often years of deliberate breadth. Citizens who have spent years becoming genuinely expert in one domain are encouraged—through a system of supported transitions and what are called sabbaticals of ignorance—to spend extended time in entirely different fields. A physicist becomes a cheesemaker. A physician studies urban design. A farmer learns the mathematics of ecological systems. This is not dilettantism. It is a social technology, carefully developed over generations, for preventing the calcification that comes from too many decades in a single groove—and for generating the collisions between domains that produce the most interesting new knowledge.

There are also regions of ignorance that people maintain deliberately throughout their lives: domains in which they have chosen not to accumulate expertise, so that genuine novice experience remains available. Being a real beginner at something—fumbling, uncertain, dependent on others, suddenly aware of how much there is to understand—is treated as one of the most valuable states an adult mind can occupy.

The later decades are often years of transmission. Not passive retirement but the systematic and demanding effort to pass on what has been learned in ways that are genuinely useful rather than merely nostalgic. The best teachers in this world are valued not as repositories of content but as people who have recently re-experienced what it is to not know something, and can therefore map the territory of learning with unusual precision. The gatherings that bring three or four living generations together are not sentimental occasions. The eldest are expected to give honest account of what they learned and failed to learn, what they would do differently, what they got wrong. The young are expected to bring news from domains the old have not visited, and to ask hard questions. The exchange is genuinely reciprocal in a way that earlier cultures—which practiced either pure veneration of age or its opposite—rarely managed.

A life of a hundred and thirty years, spent mostly in good health, lived in genuine community, with real work and real leisure and real relationships, is a different kind of thing from a life of seventy-five years spent largely in anxious productivity. It has room for more than one full arc, more than one deep commitment, more than one period of transformation. The question of what you want to do with your life becomes less terrifying when the answer doesn't have to be singular and permanent. People in this world change directions in their sixties in ways that might have seemed like starting over; they don't experience it that way, because they have already lived a complete earlier chapter and they know that another is possible.

## IV. The Mind and the Attention Commons

The more consequential change is what happened to attention.

The ordinary human brain, in genuinely good conditions, is extraordinary. Most people in 2025 have experienced this in glimpses—on a long vacation, during an afternoon when, for no clear reason, everything was interesting and the mind felt spacious and curious rather than battered and distracted. People know the feeling. Most don't expect it to last, and it doesn't.

What the world described here discovered, through the accumulation of neuroscience and clinical practice and the careful documentation of contemplative traditions, is that the conditions for that kind of mind are not mysterious. They are mostly the absence of things: chronic stress, economic precarity, sleep deprivation, poor nutrition, pain, and the algorithmic management of attention by systems designed to exploit specific vulnerabilities of the human nervous system for commercial ends.

The elimination of economic precarity was the work described above. The reform of attention economies was harder in some ways because the enemy was less easy to name. There was no single decision that solved it. There was regulatory pressure on the most predatory attentional practices, which came slowly and incompletely. There was cultural change, which came even more slowly. There was the design of alternatives: technologies built to support depth rather than exploit distraction, public digital infrastructure that treated attention as a commons worth protecting rather than a resource to extract.

And there was, eventually, a broad cultural absorption of what had been, in 2025, still a minority view: that the quality of a person's attention is among the most important facts about their life, and that allowing it to be systematically degraded for others' profit is not an acceptable arrangement. The reformers who made this argument faced the same accusation that every generation of reformers faces—naivety, utopianism, failure to understand how things actually work—and made the argument anyway, and over decades won enough of it to matter.

The result, at the level of lived experience, is a quality of mind that most people in 2025 would recognize—because they have felt it, briefly, in their best moments—but would not expect as a baseline. Minds that are not chronically fragmented. That can follow a thought to its end. That can sit with a difficult question long enough for something to emerge. That can be genuinely present with another person rather than partially present while distributing attention across a dozen ambient demands.

## V. The Institutes and the Hall of Open Questions

At the center of every major settlement there is a building called the Institute of Works. These are not universities in the old sense, though they contain everything universities contained and considerably more. They exist to advance the knowledge of causes, to enlarge what is possible for human beings in the conditions of human life—a purpose borrowed and adapted from a much older formulation, worn smooth by three centuries of use.

What would be unrecognizable to someone from our era is not their internal structure, which contains recognizable researchers, experimenters, theorists, and synthesizers, but their relationship to the rest of the world. Researchers spend a third of their time in what is called contact practice: working alongside farmers, builders, physicians, teachers, cooks, caregivers, children. The practical wisdom embedded in ordinary work is treated as data of the highest order. The result is a science that is at once more rigorous and more human: slower in some ways, vastly more productive in others, because it builds on the full range of what is actually known rather than only the subset that has been published.

Science in this world has been reformed in another way that matters as much: there is no publication pressure that rewards flashy overclaiming. No manufactured urgency. Projects are chosen because they are genuinely interesting or genuinely needed. Scientists have enough time to do them right, and build honestly on each other's work. The history of science is full of crucial insights delayed by decades because no one could afford, professionally, to pursue them seriously; in this world, that delay is much shorter.

Every Institute maintains what they call the Hall of Open Questions: a curated collection of the most beautiful unsolved problems in every domain, presented not as shameful gaps but as gifts. Children visit on school trips. The questions are updated as old ones yield and new ones emerge. This is perhaps the most important single cultural artifact of this civilization: the public, celebrated, beautiful display of what is not yet understood. It signals, in the most concrete possible way, that the appropriate response to the unknown is not anxiety or denial but curiosity and the willingness to begin.

Above the entrance of the first Institute are carved two lines. The first: *The end of our foundation is the knowledge of causes and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire.* Underneath, added by a later generation: *We will not finish. Begin.*

Adults return to the Hall. The most distinguished researchers sit in front of it for hours. It is considered one of the best places to think.

## VI. Community and the Built Environment

Human beings are not individuals who occasionally encounter other individuals. They are social organisms constituted, in large part, by the quality of their relationships. A person with a healthy body and a clear mind who is deeply lonely is not flourishing.

The deep loneliness that had quietly accompanied the prosperity of the early twenty-first century was invisible for a long time—invisible partly because it was so widespread that it seemed natural, and partly because the cultural emphasis on individual self-sufficiency made it difficult to name without shame. When it was finally recognized as a public health crisis comparable in its effects on mortality and wellbeing to smoking or obesity, the response was imperfect but genuine.

Community, it turned out, could be designed for. Not imposed—every attempt to impose community from above produced resentment rather than belonging. But the built environment could make gathering easy or difficult, and for most of the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries it had been making it difficult.

Cities were rebuilt with encounter in mind: walkable neighborhoods, third places, common spaces that invited lingering, markets and workshops and gardens distributed to maximize the likelihood that a person moving through daily life would run into something unexpected—overhear a conversation that reframes a problem, meet someone whose work intersects with theirs in ways neither anticipated, find a strange beautiful thing they had no reason to expect. Serendipity was treated as a social technology and engineered with some care, because it was discovered—through painful experience with the alternative—that optimization toward the predictable produces a subtle atrophy of the capacity for genuine surprise, and that this atrophy is more costly than it appears.

This is not nostalgia for a pre-industrial village. The communities of this world are diverse and cosmopolitan in ways that actual pre-industrial villages were not, and that diversity is understood as a feature. But the insight that physical design shapes social connection, and that the privatization of space comes at a social cost, was finally absorbed and acted on.

People cook. Not exclusively, and not as performance, but most people, most days, prepare at least part of what they eat—not because cooking has been fetishized but because it is a form of engagement with the material world that most humans find genuinely satisfying when they are not too depleted to do it. One of the great practical achievements of this civilization is the organization of work and rest in ways that do not leave people too depleted for the things that matter. This required simultaneous changes in labor arrangements, urban design, social expectations, and the concept of productivity itself—it could not be solved in any one of these domains alone, which is partly why it took so long.

## VII. What People Do

The question people in 2025 most often ask, when someone describes this possibility, is: *But what do they do?* The question reveals an assumption worth examining—that a life needs to be justified by its productive output, that a person earns the right to exist through contribution to some external goal.

This assumption has not been eliminated in the world described here, but it has been substantially loosened—and not by declaring that work doesn't matter, but by expanding what counts as real work and by creating the conditions under which work becomes genuinely chosen rather than frantically necessary.

The automation of tedious, repetitive, and dangerous labor produced not mass unemployment but something more valuable: discretionary time, distributed more broadly than in any previous era. The four-day workweek became standard in most wealthy economies by the 2040s and spread from there. This is not leisure in the passive, consumption-oriented sense. It is time for what people, when asked what makes their lives meaningful, consistently name: relationships, creative work, learning, the unhurried raising of children, the maintenance of communities.

The art of this world is abundant and strange and alive. The barrier to making things has fallen, while the cultural value placed on making things—as opposed to merely consuming them—has risen. The boundary between creating culture and participating in it has become more permeable. People who in a prior era would have spent their evenings as passive recipients of professional entertainment instead spend them making things: music, food, furniture, garments, stories, gardens, games. The things they make are not always good. The point is not that they are good. The point is that the act of making them is a form of vitality—the particular satisfaction that comes from being a cause rather than an effect.

There is, deeply embedded in the culture, an allergy to arrangements that require people to simply receive. The ideal relationship with any capability—cooking, navigation, music, medicine—is active rather than passive. This doesn't mean rejecting help or technology or the genuine gifts that specialization provides. It means that the good life here is not a life of well-managed consumption but a life of sustained making, learning, and encounter with things that are genuinely difficult.

Some people spend years doing what looks, from the outside, like very little: walking in mountains, tending gardens, learning to play instruments badly and then gradually less badly, sitting with elderly friends, cooking elaborate meals, raising children with the unhurried attentiveness that children actually require. These activities are not residual. They are not what people do when they have failed to find something more important. They are recognized—and this recognition required cultural work, not just material change—as among the most valuable things a person can do with a life, because the texture of daily experience is not the wrapping around the real content. It *is* the content.

## VIII. What Has Deliberately Not Been Solved

The designers of this world—insofar as it was designed, which was only partially and imperfectly—made a series of deliberate choices about what not to smooth away.

They kept difficulty. Not the pointless difficulty of poverty and preventable illness, which had crushed billions for reasons having nothing to do with their choices. But the difficulty intrinsic to genuine achievement: learning a craft to the point of mastery, sustaining a relationship through real conflict, creating something original, understanding something previously mysterious. A life made uniformly easy is progressively drained of meaning—and the greatest disservice you can do to a person is to protect them so thoroughly from challenge that they never discover what they are capable of.

They kept stakes. Choices in this world have real consequences—not the catastrophic, arbitrary, irreversible consequences that had crushed billions for reasons unrelated to what they chose, but consequences of the kind that allow a person to be known through their decisions. A world in which no choice matters is not a comfortable heaven. It is an exquisite prison.

They kept surprise, and worked actively to maintain it against the natural drift toward optimization and the predictable.

They kept the open questions. New problems have emerged from the solutions to old ones, as they always do—problems that could not have been anticipated, that require the invention of new frameworks even to be described clearly. Citizens encounter these with something that looks, from outside, like eagerness. They have been raised in a culture that treats unsolved problems not as evidence of failure but as the primary invitation to a life well spent.

## IX. The Interior

The contemplative traditions of the world—Buddhist, Stoic, Sufi, and the many others that had been circling the same territory from different directions for centuries—find themselves taken seriously in this era, in genuine conversation with the scientific understanding of human psychology in ways that are generative rather than mutually dismissive.

The accumulated wisdom of these traditions about the structure of human suffering and the conditions for genuine peace was never a secret. It was available. What was different, in 2025, was that very few people had the conditions under which that wisdom was practically applicable. When the material conditions changed, when time became available and pain became manageable and anxiety became less chronic, people discovered that the old wisdom worked. Not as magic, not as escape, but as a set of practices for inhabiting a life fully rather than enduring it.

Meditation, or its many functional analogues—the deliberate cultivation of present attention, of equanimity, of genuine engagement with what is rather than anxious anticipation of what might be—spread not as a wellness trend but as a recognized and valuable form of human development, as ordinary as physical fitness. The question of what kind of person you were becoming—not just what you were accomplishing, but the character taking shape through your choices and habits—became a serious and common topic of reflection rather than an embarrassing one.

The development of carefully regulated protocols for therapeutic use of compounds that had been criminalized through most of the twentieth and early twenty-first century contributed something particular. Not a mass experience of artificial enlightenment—something more modest and more lasting: for people who needed it, genuine access to a broader range of their own experience. The therapeutic benefit was substantial. But the deeper cultural effect was a renewed understanding, distributed more widely than before, that ordinary consciousness is not the ceiling of what is available to a human mind. That the moments of heightened perception most people have experienced at least once, usually by accident, are not flukes but evidence of what is actually there, waiting.

This is the hardest thing to describe and the most important. The world described here is not a world of permanent bliss, and it would be worse if it were. Permanent bliss is not a coherent goal. The people of this world suffer. They lose people they love. They fail at things that matter to them. They sit with uncertainty and grief and regret.

But the baseline is different. And the range is different. The ceiling of what is available to an ordinary person, in an ordinary week, in the way of genuine aliveness—genuine presence, genuine beauty, genuine contact with other people and with the strangeness of existence—is higher than anything that was normal or expected in 2025. And there is now a shared practice for approaching that ceiling. Not a formula—the people of this world have learned to be deeply suspicious of formulas—but an accumulation of wisdom, personal and communal, for how to keep the door open to those moments. How to notice them. How to share them. How to carry them forward into the ordinary substrate of a day like a coal that doesn't go out.

## X. What Made It Possible

Looking back at the hinge decades, several things stand out as having mattered most.

The first was the recovery of seriousness about preventable harm. The early twenty-first century had sustained an extraordinary collective fiction: that the suffering caused by poverty, inadequate healthcare, loneliness, the degradation of attention, the chronic pain of undertreated bodies, the neglect of children—that all of this was natural, the unavoidable price of being human, rather than the predictable result of specific political choices that could have been made differently. When this fiction became difficult to maintain, progress accelerated. Not because the solutions were newly discovered. The solutions had been largely available. The bottleneck had always been the will to apply them.

The second was the willingness to work at multiple scales simultaneously. The great temptation of people who care about human flourishing is to find the single lever: the policy change, the technological breakthrough, the cultural shift, the correct theory of everything, that will unlock the rest. This world was not built through any single lever. It was built through the simultaneous and overlapping application of many: energy transition and institutional reform and medical advance and educational change and community design and the slow cultural rehabilitation of the interior life. These things reinforced each other. Material security made contemplative practice more available. Better institutions made long-term investment possible. Longer healthy lives made community investment worthwhile. No one of them was sufficient; together, they were.

The third was the stubborn refusal to accept that human suffering is simply what being human costs. This refusal is less obvious than it sounds. Every generation in human history has inherited a vast load of preventable suffering and has been told, by the people who benefited from it, that this is just how things are. Every generation that pushed back was told they were naive. Every generation that pushed back anyway made progress that seemed impossible before it happened.

The world described here was made by people who pushed back. Not in one dramatic moment of collective awakening, but persistently, incrementally, in a thousand overlapping domains, with the unglamorous seriousness of people who had decided to take the possibility of a genuinely good human life as a real project rather than a consoling daydream.

## XI. The Shape of Time

The world is not finished. It is not perfect. It contains unresolved arguments, persistent injustices, losses that cannot be recovered, sorrows that cannot be prevented. It faces hard problems: how to extend the circle of moral consideration to animals and to whatever new forms of mind are beginning to emerge, how to maintain the conditions of abundance against entropy both physical and political, how to distribute resources fairly when preferences are genuinely incommensurable, how to raise children well when the definition of flourishing is still being worked out.

These problems interest people. They are not crushed by them. They approach them with the equanimity of those who have seen difficult things become less difficult, who have inherited not the answer to every question but something more useful: the demonstrated knowledge that questions can be worked on, that progress is real, that the world is responsive to sustained effort, and that the effort is worth it.

The Hall of Open Questions is very full. Adults return to it. The most distinguished researchers sit in front of it for hours. Children visit and are not frightened by what they find there—they have been raised to understand that the unknown is not a reproach but an invitation.

We have not yet found out how good it gets. We have barely started looking.

---

The morning is Tuesday. You wake up in a body that is simply working—not negotiating with its own limitations, not spending the first twenty minutes assembling itself against resistance. The light in the room is the right color. Your mind is clear, turning slowly, finding nothing to flinch from.

You have things to do today that you care about. People to see who know you and love you imperfectly and truly. Work that interests you, and a question at the edge of it that you haven't yet solved. Later there will be a meal made by hands, eaten with people who will argue about something that matters, and laughter, and perhaps a moment in which the quality of your attention shifts and the world reveals itself, briefly, as the strange and specific and sufficient place it has always been.

You are not the best version of yourself that it is possible to imagine. The world is not the best version of itself. But both of you are genuinely here, and genuinely trying, and the gap between what is and what could be is a place people work rather than a wound they carry.

The hall of open questions is full.

You get up and walk toward it.
