# The Lived World

## A Vision of What We Could Become

---

There is a particular quality to the last moment before you understand something. You can feel the understanding assembling itself just beneath the surface of consciousness—the pieces arriving, rotating into alignment, not yet locked—and there is a specific pleasure to that moment that is entirely distinct from the pleasure of comprehension itself. The threshold is its own country.

Most people visit it rarely. The conditions require the right problem, the right preparation, enough uninterrupted quiet, freedom from the anxiety of an inbox that is never empty and a body that is slowly failing and a world that seems to demand constant routing of attention toward tasks that produce nothing anyone would recognize as meaningful. The tragedy of our present era is not primarily cruelty, though cruelty persists. It is waste. Billions of minds built for depth, conscigned by circumstance to shallows.

What follows is a description of a future in which that waste is largely repaired. Not through any single revolution, but through a long accumulation of practical changes whose sum reshapes what a human life can be—and what a human mind can become.

This future is not guaranteed. It is available. The difference between those two words is where we live.

## The First Generation: 2025–2050

### The Death of Administrative Friction

Begin with something that sounds small.

By the 2030s, AI systems competent enough to handle most routine coordination have become ubiquitous. You specify what you want in natural language—*resolve my insurance dispute, my doctor says the procedure is necessary*—and an AI agent negotiates, documents, follows up, escalates when needed, and reports back. It doesn't get tired. It doesn't get put on hold for forty-five minutes and accidentally disconnected.

This clears roughly ten hours per week from the average person's life.

Those hours compound. The person who previously spent Sunday afternoons battling health bureaucracies now has time for something else. Some read. Some learn instruments. Some spend those hours with their children, actually present rather than exhausted and half-absent. Some do nothing in particular, and discover—to their genuine surprise—that "nothing in particular" turns out to mean watching their own thoughts without agenda, which is its own valuable country that most modern people never visit long enough to settle in.

Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. When you stop bleeding it into administrative friction, you discover you have more than you knew.

### Expertise Becomes Abundant

Benjamin Bloom demonstrated in 1984 that individual tutoring produces achievement gains two standard deviations above classroom instruction. This finding was thoroughly documented and almost entirely ignored, for the obvious reason that individual tutors are expensive and you cannot staff every child with one.

By the mid-2030s, you can. AI tutoring systems available for free to anyone with a device approximate Bloom's gains across every subject, at every age, in every language with enough speakers to generate training data. The patient, infinitely available tutor who adjusts to your pace and never makes you feel stupid for asking a question a third time—this existence turns out to matter enormously, in ways that go beyond test scores. Children who grew up experiencing school as a weekly demonstration of their inadequacy now experience learning as a daily expansion of their capacity. This is not a minor change in mood. It is a structural change in how people understand themselves in relation to knowledge.

The same dynamic applies outside childhood. The forty-five-year-old considering a career change has access to vocational guidance as sophisticated as any human counselor. The farmer in rural Bangladesh optimizing crops for shifting weather has agronomic expertise that previously required a university. The writer in a small town far from any literary community receives calibrated, detailed feedback on her work from a reader that is always available and never bored.

Not all of them produce masterpieces. The point is not masterpieces. The point is that the constraint on what's possible for any particular person has loosened, and the loosening accumulates across a civilization into something genuinely different.

People learn, reasonably quickly, to treat AI advisors as very good first opinions rather than final authorities—the way a sensible person treats a knowledgeable friend, rather than a sacred text. The mild friction of this skepticism keeps humans in the loop. This turns out to be fine. Better than fine: it produces a population with good judgment about when to trust a source, which is a capacity we badly needed anyway.

### The Coordination Unlocking

Most large human problems are, at root, coordination problems. Climate change is not primarily a technology problem; we have had the technology to meaningfully decarbonize for decades. It is a coordination problem, a collective action problem, a problem of aligning incentives across entities with private reasons to defect.

AI systems that can model the policy space, identify solutions most parties would accept, and monitor compliance automatically don't solve these problems by themselves. But they dramatically reduce their difficulty. When the system can show you, with reasonable confidence, that a proposed agreement benefits both sides more than the alternative—and monitor compliance in real time—the space of achievable agreements expands. Over twenty years, this compounds into a measurably more cooperative world. Not peaceful in some utopian sense. Not free of conflict or mistrust or competing interests. But less prone to the specific failure mode of wars fought because both sides misread who would win, or treaties abandoned because neither side could verify the other's compliance.

The result is not harmony. It is a world where the cost of cooperation has dropped enough that cooperation is, more often, the rational choice.

## The Middle Generation: 2050–2090

### What Medicine Becomes

By mid-century, the thing that matters most in human life—its length and quality—has changed.

AI-accelerated drug discovery is not a metaphor. The historical process of developing a new therapeutic took twelve to fifteen years and cost over a billion dollars, with failure rates above ninety percent. AI systems capable of modeling protein interactions, simulating drug behavior at scale, and designing adaptive clinical trials compress this dramatically. By 2055, the pace of medical progress has roughly tripled. Diseases that were death sentences in 2025—certain pancreatic cancers, most forms of ALS, the majority of antibiotic-resistant infections—have effective treatments.

By 2070, the first generation of aging-modulation therapies is in widespread use. They do not grant immortality. What they grant is something quieter and more profound: a longer plateau of full function. A person who reaches sixty in 2075 can expect, with reasonable confidence, to remain in full cognitive and physical health until ninety-five or beyond. The decade of gradual decline that previously swallowed the last years of life—the fog descending, the body betraying its inhabitant, the slow diminishment of capability—this is no longer the default.

What this does to the texture of a life is hard to overstate. The urgency that pervades modern existence—the feeling that you must achieve things *now*, before your body fails, before time runs out—relaxes. Not into complacency. Into something more like the attitude of a person who knows they have enough to work with. More time for depth. More time to repair relationships you thought were beyond repair. More time to become, slowly and with patience, the person you always intended to be.

Death is not eliminated. When it comes, it is rarer and less often a theft. The grief when someone dies at ninety-eight is real, but different in character from the grief when someone dies at forty-three of a disease that, a generation later, would have been curable.

### The Post-Scarcity Transition

By 2065, in most wealthy countries and a growing number of middle-income ones, the basic material conditions of life are secured for essentially everyone.

This arrives through several channels simultaneously: AI-driven productivity growth that makes goods and services dramatically cheaper; political choices—contested, hard-won, requiring sustained effort—to distribute those gains through universal basic services and guaranteed income floors; and a cultural shift that happens slowly, then suddenly, in which genuine material poverty is recognized as the anomaly it actually is rather than the regrettable inevitability it was previously treated as.

The outcome doesn't look like any prior utopian fantasy, because those were all imagined by people who couldn't see past their own era's constraints. It looks like a world where the question *what are you going to do with your life?* has genuinely changed character. It is no longer primarily a question about economic survival. It is a question about meaning, contribution, and identity.

These are harder questions. They are also better ones to be faced with.

Some people, freed from economic necessity, feel purposeless for a while. This is a transition problem, not a terminal condition. What comes after, for most people, is a gradual migration toward what they actually care about. What people actually care about—when you remove the distortions imposed by scarcity and anxiety—turns out to be neither as selfish as cynics predicted nor as altruistic as idealists hoped. Most people want to be good at something. They want to contribute to something larger than themselves. They want close relationships, meaningful work, beauty in their lives, the experience of growing. These wants don't require much philosophical sophistication; they require primarily the time and stability to pursue them.

Status competition doesn't disappear. Humans will always find something to compete over. But what's competed over shifts, gradually, from positional goods—money, rank, the outward markers of success—toward intrinsic goods: craft, mastery, contribution, the esteem of people you actually respect. The artist who earns less than a surgeon but whose work moves people to tears is no longer making a heroic sacrifice. She is simply living the life she chose, which is unremarkable and fine.

### The Shape of a Day

It is worth stopping here to describe, concretely, what daily life actually looks like in this world. Abstract claims about flourishing can be substituted for lived texture, and texture is what makes the difference real.

A person in 2075—call him Marcus, fifty-three years old, born in Lagos, living in a city that exists partly as built infrastructure and partly as layered digital space overlaid on that infrastructure—wakes without an alarm because he no longer needs to be anywhere at a specific time unless he chooses to be. His body is in the condition of someone who, in 2025, would have been thirty-eight. This is not because he has pursued any heroic regimen; it is simply what medicine has made the default.

He makes coffee the way he has made it for twenty years, with a manual pour-over and beans he buys from a cooperative two streets away, because the ritual pleases him and pleasure in small rituals compounds into a life that feels inhabited rather than endured. While the water heats he checks in with his AI coordinator—not an inbox, not a list of demands, but something closer to a trusted colleague who has already handled everything that didn't require his actual judgment and is now prepared to brief him on the two things that do.

One of those things is a decision about whether to accept an invitation to contribute to a research collaboration on the acoustic properties of ancient West African musical instruments. This is not his profession, exactly. In this world, "profession" is a less rigid category. He spent his twenties as a structural engineer, his thirties raising children and doing engineering part-time, his forties increasingly absorbed by the intersection of physics and music that had always interested him peripherally. His AI tutor had helped him move into this space without the credential gatekeeping that would have blocked it in 2025. He is not the world's leading expert in this area. He is a serious amateur who has done work that serious professionals have found useful, and the invitation reflects that.

He accepts it.

The morning he spends in a state that he has learned, with practice and some intentional psychological calibration, to reliably enter. Not flow, exactly—that word was always too frictionless, implying an ease that isn't quite accurate. Something more like *engaged struggle*: the problem is hard enough to require his full attention, the tools available are good enough that full attention is rewarded, and there is enough time that he doesn't feel the artificial urgency of a deadline cutting into the quality of his thinking. He makes progress. He reaches a dead end. He finds a different way around. By midday he has produced three hours of work that he would not be ashamed to show anyone.

He eats lunch with a friend who lives nearby. They have been friends for twenty-two years, long enough that the friendship has gone through multiple phases—competitive in their thirties, distant when Marcus moved cities, close again when his friend's marriage ended and Marcus showed up repeatedly to help him through it. This history is the texture of the friendship in a way that no amount of shared interest or compatible personality could substitute for. They argue about something minor. They agree about something important. Neither of them is performing.

The afternoon splits. Part of it he spends with his youngest daughter, who is twelve and at the age where she sometimes wants his company and more often doesn't, and navigating which is which remains one of the genuinely difficult and unreplaced human skills. Part of it he spends in a community workshop three blocks away, where a group of twenty people across a range of ages and backgrounds share tools, skills, and projects in a space that has the specific quality of places where people make things—sawdust and the smell of flux and someone's music playing from a corner and the comfortable noise of productive work. He is building a chair. He is not very good at building chairs yet. He will be.

In the evening he reads. The book is a history of the Songhai Empire, and it is very good, and when he puts it down at eleven he has the feeling of having been somewhere and returned, which is what the best reading always produces.

He goes to sleep. The problems he was working on in the morning are somewhere in the back of his mind, turning slowly in the dark, and he trusts—from experience—that they will look different in the morning. They always do.

This is not a perfect day. It is not presented as such. It is a *normal* day for someone whose baseline has been lifted by the accumulation of changes described in these pages. He did not do anything extraordinary. He was simply, throughout it, largely present in his own life.

## The Question of the Mind

Here the account must acknowledge a complexity, because the most significant feature of this future is also the hardest to describe from where we stand.

The history of human cognition is a history of boundary dissolution. We have repeatedly discovered that what we took to be limits were only current conditions. Mass literacy revealed that sustained abstract reasoning was not a rare gift. Public education revealed that abstract mathematics was teachable to almost everyone, given adequate time and instruction. The Gaussian shape of cognitive distributions—the bell curve whose existence we treat as a fact of nature—is, in part, an artifact of how poorly we have historically taught and supported developing minds.

By the late twenty-first century, the tools for developing what a mind can do have been available for decades: brain-computer interfaces that extend working memory and attentional control; AI systems that function not merely as tutors but as *thinking partners*, entities capable of holding the parts of a problem you can't hold simultaneously and surfacing them at exactly the moment you need them; a vastly more sophisticated understanding of the neuroscience of learning, attention, creativity, and wisdom; pharmacology that enhances cognitive function without the side effects that made earlier generations of enhancement drugs undesirable; and perhaps most importantly, a cultural understanding that the development of mind is a lifelong project to be actively pursued rather than a fixed endowment to be accepted.

The people who grow up with these tools from birth do not think the way we do, in the same way that we do not think the way people did before mass literacy. Not because they are a different species. Because they were given better tools earlier and used them longer.

What they think *about*—what occupies their attention, what they find interesting, what problems seem important to them—this is not fully predictable from our vantage point. But we can observe something: the peak experiences that humans occasionally access—total absorption in a hard problem, the experience of profound understanding arriving whole, the quality of attention that makes you feel you've finally met someone rather than merely exchanged information with them, the aesthetic experience that reorganizes your sense of what's possible in a form—these experiences point in a direction. They are evidence that human minds can go further than they usually go.

A civilization that has solved material poverty and preventable disease and wasted educational potential, and that has had a century to seriously work on what minds can do, will have traveled considerably further in that direction than we have. What they encounter there, we cannot fully say. But it will almost certainly include forms of understanding, forms of relationship, forms of creative work, that we currently lack the concepts to name.

This is not mysticism. It is a straightforward extrapolation of a trajectory. The trajectory is genuine. Whether we follow it depends on choices not yet made.

## The Necessary Difficulties

This future has problems. It would be dishonest to omit them.

The first and most serious: the AI systems that make this future possible could, developed carelessly, produce catastrophic outcomes instead. This is not a theoretical risk. The same capability that enables beneficial AI enables AI whose goals have diverged from human flourishing in ways that are subtle until they are not. The path to the future described here runs through serious, sustained work on ensuring that increasingly capable AI systems remain oriented toward human benefit—and through the political and institutional structures that enforce this requirement when market incentives alone would not. This work is not guaranteed to succeed. It requires choices, and the choices are available now.

The second: the gains from the transition could accrue to a small class of people rather than broadly. History offers both warning and encouragement here. Industrialization created enormous inequality before it created broad prosperity, and the mechanism by which its gains became widely shared was conscious political effort—labor movements, welfare states, public education, progressive taxation. None of this happened automatically. All of it was opposed at the time by people with strong incentives to oppose it. The same dynamic will play out with AI, and the same kind of sustained, organized political effort will be required.

The third is subtler and requires saying carefully: we might get the material improvements without the deeper ones. A world where everyone has enough food and reliable medical care and a pleasant AI companion and a long healthy life, but where relationships lack genuine intimacy and creative work is mostly consumed rather than done and the development of mind is treated as optional rather than central—this world would be comfortable in a way that represents a profound failure of imagination. It is achievable, and we should be vigilant against it.

Against this failure, the best protection is clarity about what we're actually trying to achieve. Not comfort. Not even happiness, exactly, though happiness matters. What we're trying to achieve is the full expression of what human beings are capable of—the art and the science and the love and the discovery and the hard-won wisdom—in as many people, for as long as possible. The good life, understood not as ease but as the experience of being fully alive and fully present and fully engaged with something real.

That goal is worth holding carefully, because the path to it runs close to the path to the hollow version, and it's possible to take the wrong fork without noticing.

## What This Requires of Us

We are living in the period before the transition. Close enough to see it, early enough to shape it. The choices made in the next two decades will constrain what's possible for centuries.

This is not a comfortable position. But it is an interesting one. It means that work done now—on AI safety, on AI governance, on the political economy of distribution, on the institutions that make democratic accountability possible, on understanding what human flourishing actually requires and being honest about that understanding—is unusually important. Rare moments when individual choices matter at civilizational scale; this is one.

It also means keeping the positive vision in clear view. Not as a fantasy that exempts us from present difficulty, but as a genuine image of what we're working toward. People make better decisions when they know what winning looks like. They make wiser tradeoffs when they understand what's at stake on both sides—not just what's to be lost if things go badly, but what's to be gained if things go well.

There are minds alive right now—distributed across the world in bodies that don't have enough food, or access to good teachers, or freedom from the corrosive anxiety of poverty—that would, given the chance, produce things of great beauty and usefulness and meaning. There are relationships of depth and quality that the busiest, most exhausted, most materially constrained people of our era have had neither the time nor the stability to develop. There are problems of real importance that are going unsolved because the people equipped to solve them are spending their cognitive resources on administrative friction and economic survival and the management of preventable illness.

The future described here clears the way for those minds, those relationships, those solutions. It is not a world without conflict or loss or the particular grief that comes from being a finite creature in a large universe. It is a world where the unnecessary suffering—the suffering that comes from poverty and preventable disease and wasted potential and lives cut short before they found their shape—has been substantially reduced, and where the freed resources of human attention and care and creativity have been turned toward living more fully.

We know what the threshold feels like—the moment before understanding arrives, when the pieces are almost locked. An entire civilization has been waiting, for a very long time, at that threshold.

The question is whether we will choose to step through.
