# The Inhabited World: On Building a Life Worth Living In

## A Letter from the Near Future

---

Begin with a doorway, because this world is defined by its doors.

The doorway belongs to a community hall in the Atacama highlands of northern Chile, where a woman named Valentina is teaching a group of twelve-year-olds to identify the stars by their Kunza names—the language of the Atacameño people, reconstructed and returned to daily use over the course of forty years by a community that decided its language was load-bearing, not decorative. The children repeat the names back to her in the dark, their breath visible in the cold air, the Milky Way sprawling above them with the intensity it achieves only at altitude and only when you've earned it by going somewhere difficult.

Valentina is sixty-one years old and has lived in four different communities across three continents. She was born in this one. She left at twenty-two, restless in the way of people who need to know what else exists. She spent nine years in a rationalist cooperative in Scotland, eight years in a craft-focused collective in coastal Vietnam, three years in a research station on the edge of the Greenland ice sheet, where she fell briefly in love with someone who preferred the cold and permanently in love with the practice of patient attention. Then she came home, because home had grown into something she wanted to be part of, and because the woman who had taught her the Kunza names for stars was dying and wanted to pass them on, and Valentina was the right person to receive them.

None of these moves required her to abandon what mattered. The healthcare followed her. The education records followed her. The baseline income—derived from a global commons fund built on resource rents, platform revenues, and automation dividends—followed her. She never had to stay anywhere out of fear of what leaving would cost her. She stayed, when she stayed, because she wanted to. She left, when she left, because she wanted to. These are ordinary facts now, unremarkable to the children listening to the stars' names. To Valentina, who is old enough to remember a different world, they remain something close to miraculous.

This is where the story starts: with the simple, radical insistence that freedom from coercion and freedom to belong are not enemies, but conditions for each other.

## What We Finally Understood About Human Nature

For most of recorded history, two great camps fought over how to organize collective life. One camp believed that clear, correct answers existed—the right religion, the optimal economic system, the proper family structure—and that the role of governance was to impose these answers universally. The other camp, reacting to the obvious horrors this produced, retreated to procedural neutrality: the state should not favor any vision of the good life, only protect the rights of individuals to choose their own.

Both camps were partly right and catastrophically wrong in the same way. They misidentified the unit of human flourishing.

The imposers were wrong that there is one correct answer. Human beings vary in what they need, what they love, what gives their lives texture and meaning. A world designed for a single type of person will impoverish everyone who doesn't fit the template, and most people will not fit the template, because the template was always an abstraction.

But the proceduralists were wrong too, in a subtler way. Their model assumed the autonomous individual as the natural atom of humanity—a free-floating chooser who, given sufficient rights and information, would construct a flourishing life. What the evidence from psychology, anthropology, and the lived experience of millions of lonely people demonstrated, with relentless consistency, was that humans are not built to flourish alone. We are communal creatures, constituted by our relationships, shaped by our belonging, made whole by being known and needed. "Freedom" without community is not liberation. For most people, it is a specific kind of suffering.

The question, it turned out, was never *freedom or belonging*. It was: what structures allow both to be real at once?

The answer—still being refined, still being contested, still being tested in ten thousand variations across the inhabitable world—is something like this: a high, portable floor that no community can take away; genuine freedom to leave and to arrive; communities diverse enough to actually contain the variety of human ways of being; and the civic muscle to coordinate across differences without requiring that the differences disappear.

## The Foundation That Travels With You

Nothing else in this world works without the foundation, so it must be described carefully.

The biological transformations of the mid-century made the baseline for healthcare genuinely extraordinary. Engineered therapies that clear amyloid and prevent cognitive decline, immunological treatments that render most cancers manageable rather than fatal, genetic corrections that eliminate hundreds of inherited conditions—these are available to every human being on Earth, regardless of community membership, regardless of income, regardless of geography. There is no community wealthy enough to offer better care than the baseline, because the baseline is now extraordinary. Some communities choose, for religious or philosophical reasons, to decline certain treatments collectively. Individual members of those communities may still access them. The community's right to govern itself does not extend to trapping its members in suffering they would choose to escape.

The economic foundation is equally concrete. The Global Commons Fund—assembled, over the course of four contentious decades, from natural resource rents, land-value taxes, platform-economy revenues, and automation dividends—provides every person on Earth with enough to live a dignified life. Not a luxurious one. Not identical across contexts; regional cost variations mean the baseline looks different in different places. But real. The floor is real and high. No child's future is foreclosed by the address where they were born. No one chooses between medicine and food. No one stays in a community that harms them because leaving would mean destitution.

What took longest to build, and what is perhaps most important, was what practitioners came to call *civic infrastructure for the self*: the combination of educational practices, AI-assisted self-knowledge tools, and human mentorship structures that help people understand what they actually want. The AI systems involved are not companions—that application was tried early and found wanting, producing dependency rather than flourishing—but something closer to skilled mirrors. They are extraordinarily good at helping people recognize the gap between the values they claim to hold and the choices they observe themselves making, and at connecting people to human relationships and communities better suited to who they actually are. Their explicit design goal is to make themselves unnecessary as quickly as possible: to help you find the room where the people you're looking for are waiting, not to become the relationship.

## How the Communities Work

The tessera—the basic unit of organized community life, a word borrowed from the mosaic tile and chosen deliberately—ranges in scale from a few hundred to several million people. Each tessera operates within a shared constitutional framework and maintains its own character: its practices, its economy, its relationship to technology, its internal governance, its particular theory of what makes life worth living.

The diversity in practice is genuine and startling. There are religious communities, some ancient in their traditions and some syncretic in ways that would astonish their founders, where daily life is shaped by prayer and observance and the moral architecture of a specific cosmology. There are craft-revival communities—Valentina's home tessera is one of these—where the deliberate recovery of traditional skills is itself a form of resistance to amnesia and a theory of what the hands are for. There are communities where neural integration with AI systems is so deep that the boundary between individual and collective cognition is the most interesting philosophical question at dinner. There are secular rationalist communities with public prediction logs and formal accountability ceremonies. There are ecological communities whose members are among the most technically sophisticated people in the world and also the people most likely to know, on a given Tuesday afternoon, exactly which tree is flowering and why.

The Georgist land-rent reforms that spread through the mid-twenty-first century made the physical conditions for this diversity possible. You pay rent on land in proportion to its productive value, whether you use it or not, which meant that vast speculative holdings became economically irrational to maintain. Land flowed toward use. Communities that wanted to exist—that were willing to actually inhabit a place, tend it, build on it—could access land in ways that had been impossible when it was locked in speculative portfolios. This is why roughly forty percent of Earth's land surface is now rewilded or lightly managed: not because humans were expelled from it, but because the economic incentive to warehouse it as investment disappeared.

What no tessera charter may specify is kept deliberately short, and enforced with seriousness:

Communities cannot prevent members from leaving. They cannot restrict access to the external world beyond what members have voluntarily requested. They cannot harm members physically. They cannot prevent children from receiving civic education delivered by the regional authority—education designed not to undermine any community's values but to ensure every child understands that a world exists outside and that they may always choose to reach it. They cannot externalize ecological costs onto the commons without compensation.

And every tessera must maintain an embassy.

## The Embassy System

The embassies are small. They look like neighborhood spaces—a comfortable room, a kitchen, a place to sleep—rather than government offices, because the research was unambiguous that official-looking spaces deterred the people who needed them most.

Walk in and say you need to leave, and you will not be handed forms. You will be given somewhere safe to sleep if you need it, and in the morning you will meet a resettlement coordinator: a human being trained in trauma-informed practice, prohibited from wearing any markers of community affiliation, and prohibited from steering you toward any particular destination.

The coordinator's job is to understand what kind of life you want and to help you find it. They do not evaluate whether the community you are leaving was good or bad by their own lights. They are not recruiters. They are, in practice, among the most important people in the world—individuals with deep working knowledge of hundreds of communities across every inhabited region, who have watched people move from evangelical communities to queer collectives to rationalist cooperatives to silent monasteries to no formal community at all, and who have learned to understand all of these movements as authentic expressions of human searching.

The child protection provisions are treated with special seriousness. Every child in every tessera, regardless of the community's philosophy, attends regular sessions with a civic educator employed by the regional authority. These sessions are not designed to undermine local values. They are designed to make exit real—to ensure every child understands, in terms appropriate to their age, that they are not defined by where they were born, that there is a world outside, that people live beautifully in many different ways, and that there is always somewhere to go.

## Learning to See What We Were Doing

The single most consequential reform of the past century was one of the quietest: the decision to teach coordination literacy in schools.

Sometime in the middle of the twenty-first century, educators backed by mounting evidence concluded that the failure to teach basic game theory, social epistemology, and institutional economics to children was not a minor curricular gap but something closer to a civilizational hazard—roughly equivalent to teaching children to speak but not to read. Children who understood what a collective action problem looked like, who had played games that failed because of defection and succeeded because of trust-building, who had seen with their own eyes how individually rational choices produce collectively catastrophic outcomes—these children became adults who could *see* coordination failures rather than merely suffering them and attributing them to malice.

The curriculum does not tell children what to think about redistribution or markets or governance structures. It teaches them to recognize the shape of a problem. It teaches them that "everyone would benefit if everyone did X, but everyone has an incentive to do Y" is a recognizable category of situation with recognizable categories of solution—and that defaulting to outrage and blame, while understandable, is usually a slower path to improvement than asking who needs to move first, what would make it worth their while, and what institution could make the commitment credible.

Adults who grew up with this curriculum are measurably harder to manipulate with simple enemy framings. They are better, on average, at helping people they love, because they have models of how incentive structures affect behavior even when intentions are good. They are not incorruptible or uniformly wise. But the baseline quality of public deliberation has shifted, and the shift is visible in the speed with which obviously bad policies now collapse and obviously useful reforms now spread.

The prediction markets, calibration tracking, and adversarial review that now attend every significant policy deliberation are the institutional expression of the same insight: what you measure, you can improve; what you track, you can hold accountable; confidence that comes with a public track record is confidence that earns its place.

## The Matching Question

For most of human history, the way societies answered the question of who does what work was a combination of coercion, accident, and market pressure—none of which reliably connected people to labor that used what was distinctive in them. The result was an enormous and largely invisible waste: millions of people spending their working lives in roles their capabilities had nothing to do with, or trapped in work they were performing out of desperation rather than fit.

The current system treats the matching problem as what it is: one of the central allocation challenges of a complex civilization, deserving commensurate attention.

Young people now encounter real work—not simulations, not classroom units, but actual supervised participation in actual skilled environments—long before any commitment is required. They cycle through a wide range of vocations in adolescence, supported by both human mentors and sophisticated tracking of demonstrated aptitude and interest over time. The commitment, when it comes, is to a first sustained focus, not a permanent identity. Moving between fields is normal, supported, and unencumbered by the credential structures that once made such movement economically ruinous.

Because the baseline income is real and the healthcare is portable, no one is compelled into work by hunger. Employers compete for workers rather than the reverse. Abusive workplaces lose their people quickly and find replacement difficult. Good workplaces grow. The feedback loop is imperfect—everything is imperfect—but the direction of its pressure is legible and largely correct.

What emerges is a labor market organized less around the extraction of productivity from captive workers and more around the human need to find work that means something. Not everyone finds this immediately or easily, and not every job can be transcendent. But the share of people who spend their working lives in conditions that are actively bad—dangerous, degrading, mismatched to every ability they have—has fallen dramatically. The habitat stewards who tend the rewilded corridors between tesseras consider theirs one of the most meaningful vocations in the world. The macroeconomist who decides at sixty to apprentice to a baker and spend her mornings learning fermentation is not an eccentric. She is a person living in a system flexible enough to contain her.

## The Deliberative Machinery

Community governance fails in predictable ways: loud voices dominate, patient bad actors outlast good-faith participants, information asymmetries let confident wrongness defeat tentative correctness. The AI systems that now assist with governance do not vote and do not decide. They do four specific things.

They surface information—aggregating sources that community members would otherwise lack time to synthesize. They model consequences with calibrated uncertainty and track, publicly, the accuracy of previous models, which has produced a healthy humility about predictive confidence. They flag gaps between stated values and proposed policies—not as judgment but as information; the community that says it values human dignity and proposes a policy that a respected ethical framework identifies as dignity-violating will be shown this, may proceed anyway, but cannot proceed in ignorance. And they maintain the prediction log: a public record of significant claims made in governance deliberations, with outcomes tracked over time, which has done more to reduce confident wrongness than any other single institutional innovation.

These tools serve communities; they do not govern them. Designing them to be the former and not the latter required decades of work, several serious failures, and institutional corrections that are still ongoing. The failures are documented and public. The corrections are required precisely because the system was designed to learn from them.

## The Ecological Compact

The atmosphere stabilized—not without cost, not without loss. The transition required two catastrophic decades of managed retreat from coastlines and a generation of political struggle of sufficient intensity that its cultural residue is still visible in architecture, civic ritual, and the names of public spaces.

In Valentina's tessera, the central gathering space is built forty centimeters lower than the surrounding streets: a deliberate hollow that fills with water during heavy rains. Children wade in it. It is called *la memoria*. Everyone knows why. The grief of the losses is not fetishized and not forgotten. It is treated as load-bearing—a real weight that the present is obligated to carry, because forgetting it would make the mistake possible again.

The rewilding that followed the land-rent reforms has produced landscapes that would have seemed impossible to planners of the early twenty-first century: vast corridors of recovering forest and grassland and wetland threaded through and around human settlements, maintained not as untouched preserves but as living systems in which humans are participants rather than invaders. The habitat stewards who tend these corridors are a vocation that emerged when ecological tending became understood as skilled work worthy of status and compensation—people who know, in intimate practical detail, which species are establishing, which hydrological processes are recovering, what the seasonal rhythms of a particular valley look like when they are healthy.

Children in the tesseras learn the names of trees the way earlier generations learned multiplication tables: as basic literacy. Not romantically—with full knowledge of ecology's brutality and complexity—but as the foundation of a relationship with the living world that cannot be outsourced to someone else's expertise.

## The Things That Remain Hard

This world has not abolished suffering. The statement must be made plainly, because the alternative is a description of a world that does not exist, and false descriptions breed cynicism when reality declines to match them.

Consciousness capable of joy is consciousness capable of grief. The death of someone you love remains devastating, even in a world where many deaths have been made optional by medicine, because some people still choose death, and because some grief is not about death at all but about the ordinary irreversibility of time. The longing to be fully known by another person is not solved by any matching algorithm. The difficulty of genuine moral progress—the recurring discovery that things that seemed acceptable were not acceptable, and that the burden of correction falls on people who did not cause the problem—is not made easier by prosperity. If anything, the removal of easily preventable suffering makes the suffering that remains harder to look away from.

The tessera system has been exploited. Communities have interpreted their charters in ways that required legal challenges and forced revisions. The boundary diplomacy between communities with incompatible values is unending, genuinely difficult, and occasionally fails badly. The embassy system has documented failures in which people who needed it did not reach it in time. The AI governance tools have exhibited biases that caused harm before they were identified and corrected; the log of these corrections is long and publicly available.

The matching algorithms, for all their sophistication, have a documented tendency to channel people toward roles they are likely to succeed in rather than roles they most want to inhabit. This is not malice. It is a structural feature that requires ongoing attention from people called vocational ethicists—among the most philosophically serious practitioners in the system.

There was a period called the Slow Emergency, lasting nearly two decades, in which a failure of collective attention allowed a problem to develop that was obvious in retrospect and should have been caught earlier. The full accounting of what went wrong is still in progress. It is discussed in schools.

None of these failures are arguments against the direction. They are arguments for the specific kind of politics this world requires: engaged, locally embedded, attentive to evidence, humble about predictions, and stubborn about the foundation.

## How It Feels From the Inside

Return to the Atacama highlands. The children have learned the Kunza names for seven stars, which is enough for one night. They're heading back toward the lit windows of the community hall, their voices overlapping in the dark, already arguing about which name is hardest to pronounce.

Valentina watches them go and feels the particular contentment of someone who has made enough different choices to know what choosing feels like. She has been lonely in this life, in specific ways and at specific times, and she has been more deeply known by more different people than she would have thought possible. She has changed her mind about important things—about what work is for, about what silence is for, about what the obligation to the past actually requires of you. She has made errors she is still carrying.

What she does not feel, and what she sometimes stops to register the absence of, is the constant low frequency of coercion—the sense that every decision was constrained not by what she wanted or valued but by what she could survive economically, by what her community would permit, by what her credentials allowed, by what her accident of birth had made available to her. That particular vibration, which she remembers from her twenties as a kind of background noise she had simply accepted as the texture of adulthood, is gone.

In its place is something that doesn't have a clean name. *Responsibility* is close. *Mattering* is close. The sense that the community she is part of is genuinely dependent on her attention and presence, and that this dependence is not a trap but an honor; and the sense that she is there by choice, which makes the dependence feel different than it would if she had no door.

In a piazza three tesseras away, adults from communities with incompatible cosmologies are eating together. They will not resolve their disagreements tonight. They are not trying to. They are trying to understand each other, which is different and more interesting, and which requires—as they have each, in their different traditions, learned—that you begin by assuming the other person's experience is as real and as complex as yours.

The girl who will go toward the difficult and strange thing is being born in every generation. The question that matters is what world she finds when she arrives—whether there are structures that can use her gifts, protect her when she needs protection, and release her when she is ready. Whether the door she needs is real and opens when she pushes.

This world built the door. Many doors, in many places, opening onto many different kinds of morning.

Not easily. Not without serious error. Not by agreeing on the destination, because agreement on the destination was never available—the destinations are plural, as plural as the genuine variety of human ways of being alive. By agreeing on the foundation: that everyone deserves the floor, that no one should be trapped, that the extraordinary diversity of human ways of making meaning is not a problem requiring a solution but the most valuable thing we have.

And by staying attentive. By tending what has been built. By showing up to the ordinary work of maintenance, which is most of what any civilization actually is: not the founding moments or the crises or the dramatic choices, but the thousand daily decisions to repair rather than abandon, to engage rather than withdraw, to keep the door open.

The stars have Kunza names again.

The bread rises.

The children argue happily in the dark, learning the names of what is above them, belonging to a world that was made to hold them.

This is not everything. It is enough to work from.

That, in the end, is all any foundation needs to be.
