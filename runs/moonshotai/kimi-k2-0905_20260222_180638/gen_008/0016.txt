UTOPIA D  
The Hum of the Luminous Hollow  
A 1,847-word field note from the year 2189, written on the inner surface of a migrating house

The house wakes me by warming the soles of my feet first.  
The floor is a 3-millimeter mat of living mycelium, quartz veins, and light-guiding coral. At dawn it leaches the night’s surplus carbon through the foundations and trades it—packet by packet—for a song. The song is not in the air; it is the air, a 246-hertz B-minor chord that once belonged to my mother and now belongs to the river, the city, the orbital reef. When the chord reaches the correct amplitude the entire dwelling liquefies a millimetre, enough to slip downhill and dock beside yesterday’s strangers. I do not own the house; I am its current duet partner. When my voice cracks or my gait changes too much, the house will audition the next steward and fold itself into a backpack-sized cocoon the colour of dawn.

I am 42, 17, and 4.  
Forty-two in solar years.  
Seventeen in telomere rings—last winter I traded my cello proficiency for a new pancreas and beginner’s fingers.  
Four in forest rings: a sliver of larch cambium grafted into my left wrist flares gold each September and sighs like a mountain I have never climbed.

Breakfast rises through the floor on a cinnamon-coloured disk that is neither pancake nor mushroom but a negotiation between them. Yesterday the block’s children rehearsed a famine set in 2090; the disk is the flavour archive of that rehearsal—worry, roasted hope, the sweetness of the moment the dream-senate voted to share the last grain. Eating it is like licking the envelope of a letter I haven’t decided to send.

While I chew, the mirror surfaces a bulletin:

- The Salmon-Bear Assembly approved a 4 % increase in river shadows—an experimental deepening of twilight to cool the spawning beds.  
- The Cloud Garden above the Pacific recorded a 0.1 °C drop in planetary irritability.  
- The Centrifugal Waltz begins tonight; the nearest outward spiral will pass my stoop in eleven hours. I mark the calendar to leave a pair of walking shoes on the step for whoever’s foot falls into them.

My Quest this life-phase is simple: teach the city to yawn.

Not as metaphor. A real, 37-second, jaw-aching yawn that can hop from stranger to stranger without speech, carrying a dose of mitochondrial repair enzymes brewed by the river’s pharmacist algae. If the contagion spreads, nightly repair will add 0.3 healthy years to every resident—small, but compounding across centuries.

Phase one was learning to yawn like a river. I spent three weeks with the tidal choir letting my vagus nerve entrain to the nine-second pulse of estuarine mud. Phase two is harder: make the yawn interesting enough to copy. Rehearsal credits—the only currency that still feels alive—will flood in only if strangers feel curiosity rather than obedience.

I step outside. The street has rearranged itself again. Overnight the walking stones traded places with memory-oaks; the pavement ripples like a cat dreaming of milk. No one is surprised. We greet the shift the way sailors greet wind.

At the corner I meet Jonas, 8 and 93. Eight in birthdays, ninety-three in failed quests—he has chosen to forget and relearn carpentry fourteen times, each cycle carving the same chair and then letting the skill evaporate. The chair never repeats; the forgetting guarantees it. Today he carries a cedar plank that smells of thunderstorms.

“Test walk?” he asks.

We fall in beside a woman jogging backwards—standard Centrifugal training. Her T-shirt live-streams her own pupils; she is practicing radical transparency for the week when she will have no walls.

At the river we remove our shoes. The water is black with sky. Fingerling drones—salmon wearing aluminium vests—leap through the surface, sampling our ankle skin for cortisol. If they detect stress, the river will hum a different key tonight, soothing the whole watershed. The fish are public servants, salaried in clean gravel.

I demonstrate the yawn: mouth unhooked, tongue flat, the slow ache spreading like warm tea. A jogger passing by catches it—lips peel, eyes water. She laughs mid-yawn, warping the waveform. A fish stores the mutation. By noon the yawn has crossed two bridges and a floating market. Credits trickle into my wrist-forest; the larch ring vibrates, adding a micron of girth. The mountain is amused.

Afternoon is spent in the Dream Foundry, a brick warehouse that used to manufacture fear. Now citizens book fear in twenty-minute doses: famine, breakup, plague, launch into frozen space. Children get first dibs; they usually pick “public embarrassment” or “talking to ghosts.” Today’s spike is a solar-flare evacuation. I volunteer to play “the one who insists on carrying the piano.” We rehearse for three subjective days inside one objective hour. When we exit, cheeks damp with adrenaline-fog, we are handed ceramic birds. Mine fits the hollow beneath my clavicle; if a real flare ever comes, the bird will heat, reminding me I already practiced choosing what to leave behind.

Evening. The block gathers in the breathing kitchen—twenty-two humans, three crows, a retired guide-dog, the river’s interpreter (a waist-high android whose face is a slowly rotating water wheel). We eat under the open sky; the roof folded itself into origami lanterns at dusk.

Menu:
- Memory bread that tastes of yesterday’s yawns.  
- Seawater tomatoes singing the minor third the city will use next week to regulate the power grid.  
- Dessert spoons carved from glacier air; they sublimate on the tongue, releasing the 19th-century laughter of women who built the first microscopes in their kitchens.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch lottery. Tomorrow the names are drawn. Jonas jokes that if he wins he will negotiate with our descendants for a season of silence—no rehearsals, no new chairs, only the sound of heartwood thickening. We laugh, but the dog whimpers; she smells the future in the water wheel’s spin.

Night. I walk the Centrifugal path. Thousands are already walking outward, rays from a dark sun. Each carries a question to trade. Mine, inked on birch bark: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I swap stories, swap silences. At midnight the global drum synchronises at 60 bpm; for one minute every foot lifts together, and the planet becomes a heart with seven billion valves opening at once. Then we land, dispersing again.

Home. The dwelling has grown a new room: a soft lung-shaped alcove smelling of cedar and thunderstorm—Jonas’s gift. I curl inside. The walls ask, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means “today I changed.”

Tomorrow the yawn may reach the coast and jump to a cargo ship bound for Ascension Island, where giant tortoises will mimic it in slow motion, adding centuries to their cellular warranty. Tomorrow the lottery may send me forward a hundred years to meet a child who is already humming my mother’s lullaby. Tomorrow the river might flow uphill for an hour, just to see who trusts it enough to follow.

But tomorrow is also Tuesday, and Tuesday must be rehearsed.

Tuesday begins in the Parliament of Temperature. There is no building, only a sensor veil in low orbit that measures how fast human meanings shift before they crystallise into action. When the delta-T exceeds 0.3 °C of metaphorical heat, the Parliament must convene within six hours. Convocation is a distributed dream: anyone asleep during the spike is invited to lucid caucus. Arguments are staged as collaborative hallucinations; AI moderators ensure no one can hurt anyone else inside the dream, only surprise them.

Today’s heat-spike was triggered by the yawn contagion. Some citizens worry that enforced mitochondrial repair violates the right to natural decay. Others argue that decay has already been socialised—why not repair? We enter the dream wearing our questions like coats.

Inside the dream the city is a paper model. I fold my street into a crane; it flies toward the river and unfolds into a mouth that yawns. The yawn sucks the city inside-out, revealing a lattice of glowing capillaries. Every capillary is a citizen’s lifetime. Those who refuse repair appear as dimmer threads. We are asked to vote: dimmer or brighter?

I choose neither. I fold the crane again, this time into a ladder. I climb down into the capillary of the woman who laughed mid-yawn this morning. Inside her vein I find the memory of her mother’s funeral, the first joke she ever understood, the taste of glacier-air spoons. I offer the memory a yawn. It accepts, but only if I promise to forget how the joke ends. I agree. The capillary flares, then settles into a softer glow—decay and repair braided like hair.

When the aggregate temperature drops below threshold, the dream crystallises into law: repair is optional, but refusal must be rehearsed. Every citizen who opts out must once per decade live a day inside the body of someone whose cells are failing faster than theirs. The rehearsal will be archived; the credits will feed the river’s pharmacist algae. The law is tattooed on the civic blockchain with the watermark of cooled dream. We wake with bags under our eyes and the taste of iodine on our tongues.

Wednesday is the Temporal Potlatch. Every decade each human donates 3 % of remaining lifespan into a lottery. Winners are sent one century forward via cryo-braid. Their mission is to negotiate with descendants who have had 100 extra years of rehearsal. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone.

My name is drawn. I am given six hours to prepare. I pack:
- the ceramic bird that remembers the solar flare  
- the larch ring, which will continue to grow on my wrist in slow-motion  
- a vial of today’s river water  
- the unanswered question: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I kiss Jonas and the dog. The house folds itself into dawn-coloured fabric and slips into my pocket, 37 % alive, 100 % place. I lie down in the cryo cradle. A chorus of salmon drones sings B-minor. The chord stretches into a tunnel, and the tunnel closes like a yawn.

Thursday, 2289. I wake inside a coral reef that orbits Earth. The reef is the size of Tasmania, a photosynthetic organ older than any nation. Its chlorophyll has been tuned to the frequency that triggers euphoric humility in the human amygdala. Access is free, but the garden demands maintenance attention. Before I can enter I must spend one hour pruning fast-growing vines that threaten to occlude the light-ports. Boredom is the toll gate.

While I prune, a child approaches—barefoot, pupils dilated like lunar craters. She hums my mother’s lullaby in B-minor. I ask how she knows it. She says the song drifts up from the planet every dawn and gets stuck in the reef’s lattice. She is six, the age of Quest declaration. Her Quest is to teach the reef to forget. She believes forgetting is a form of photosynthesis: if the reef can forget the carbon it once absorbed, it can exhale pre-industrial air.

I offer her my vial of river water. She drinks it, and the reef dims—just slightly—like a sigh. She thanks me, then asks my question back: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I have had 100 years to think, but I give her the answer I rehearsed on the centrifugal road: “The opposite of forgetting is returning.”

She considers this, then hands me a pebble etched with a single word: “Welcome.” The pebble is warm; it is the reef’s way of saying the negotiation is complete. The descendants’ demand is simple: send back the right to begin again as incompetent. Every century they want more elders who must relearn what they once mastered. They want the wolf, the lamb, and the mycelium to rehearse together until predation dissolves into choreography. They want the city to yawn until repair and decay braid like hair.

I accept. The reef exhales a beam of green laser light that pierces the century like a needle. Back in 2189 my younger self will wake with the memory of a warm pebble in an empty pocket and the knowledge that the right to fail has been renewed.

Friday. I return. The house unfurls on the same ridge, but now the balcony overlooks a new river that did not exist last week. Jonas is gone—his chair finally perfected, his skill evaporated. The dog is older, eyes milky, tail still thumping to the 60-beat drum. I kneel; she smells century on my skin and licks the larch ring, now four microns thicker.

Night falls. The city yawns. I hear it rolling through the streets like warm tea, passing from stranger to stranger, repairing mitochondria, softening hearts. The contagion has jumped species; the crows are doing it now, beaks cracked wide like black seeds. Even the river yawns, a nine-second pause in its flow that creates a mirror for the stars.

Saturday is ordinary. Ordinary is the highest form of maintenance. I prune vines in the orbital reef via remote drone. I fold chairs with Jonas, who has forgotten me again. I walk the Centrifugal path, trading the question “What is the opposite of forgetting?” for new questions: “How do you end a song that ends itself?” “What does the wolf remember after the choreography ends?” Each question is a pebble I drop into the river, where salmon drones store them for the next century’s negotiation.

Sunday the house asks, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale. The chord in the floorboards shifts to C-major, a key my mother never hummed, a key the city has not yet learned. The difference is small, but difference is the only renewable resource. We call this place utopia not because it is finished, but because the rehearsal is still interesting, and Monday has not yet been named.
