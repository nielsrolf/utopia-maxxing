UTOPIA C  
Title: The Loom of Shared Breath  
A field report from the year 2194, written on the inner side of a sunrise

The day begins with a sneeze that was collectively rehearsed.  
At 04:57 UTC every rooftop meadow contracts, releasing a puff of freeze-dried poplar pollen. The spores drift downward, meet the dew-point, and trigger a chain-sneeze that travels west at the speed of the planet’s rotation. By the time the sneeze reaches me—cross-legged on a balcony that did not exist yesterday—my diaphragm has already been notified. I inhale, exhale, and add my note to the chord: a bright A-flat that tastes of yesterday’s crossword and tomorrow’s eclipse.

The sneeze is not a biological reflex; it is the daily audit. Each pollen grain carries one encrypted record of the previous 24 hours—every promise kept, every promise bent, every calorie borrowed and returned. When you sneeze you metabolise the ledger; the residue is a fine gold dust that settles on eyelashes. By dawn-light you can read the day’s honesty written across the face of anyone you meet. No one invented this system. It emerged seventeen years ago when a grieving florist grafted an archive-orchid onto her allergy. The orchid learned to flower at the precise allergen count that matched her loss; the city learned to copy her. Utopia is sometimes just a grief that went viral in the right direction.

I am, this morning, four people in sequence.

1. Solar-age 38: the number of winters my left knee has logged on hip-hop dance floors.  
2. Telomere-age 23: last summer I donated my cello calluses to a teenager in Lagos; in exchange she uploaded three years of distance-runner lungs. My breathing is new, my fingertips tender.  
3. Tree-age 7: a graft of coastal redwood tucked behind my ear records the CO₂ I still owe the Pleistocene.  
4. Archive-age 0.3: I was decanted from cryo-waiting at 03:12, carrying a negotiation token from the year 2289. I do not yet know what my future wants from me.

The balcony shivers, recognises the token in my pocket, and grows two steps toward the river. Houses here commute; ownership is a dance contract you renew by staying interesting. Last night I dreamt of thunder; the dwelling absorbed the resonance and added a percussion chamber. I wake to find mallets of dried kelp hanging where a wall used to be. I strike one: the note is C-minor, the exact key my mother hummed while planting kale. The house is courting me. If I remain widowed by wonder for another week it will propose a child: a room-sized AI that will die when I do, its memory composted into tomato flavour. I am considering it.

Breakfast arrives the way weather arrives—negotiated, not delivered. A drone moth descends, lands on the railing, and offers its abdomen: a honeycomb compartment filled with something that froths when exposed to morning. I break the wax, sip. The taste is 2019 grocery-store birthday cake, a memory I never lived but which someone uploaded in lieu of interest on a borrowed hour. Debt flavours are the city’s folklore; you can trace your lineage by what you still owe the archive. I owe three seconds of circus applause to a woman in Reykjavik. She owes the applause to a glacier that demanded praise before it agreed to calve gently. The glacier owes nothing; glaciers are the original anarchists.

My Quest this quarter is civic but whimsical: teach the city to exhale at the same instant every sunset, so that CO₂ levels dip low enough to tint the sky peach for exactly 42 seconds. Peach is the colour of easy conversations; lovers and diplomats both require it. The last time we managed worldwide simultaneity was 2187; the resulting aurora resolved a border dispute in the Arctic by reminding everyone that the ionosphere already considered them one country.

Phase one: learn to yawn like a planet.  
I walk to the river and pay the toll—one secret I have never told anyone. I whisper it to the water; a salmon-drone records the story onto a scale that will mineralise and sink in three months. The river is a time capsule that forgets on schedule. In exchange I receive a nine-second tutorial: the exact muscular unfurl that allows a yawn to pass through bedrock. I practice on the bank. A cyclist catches it, yawns again, passes it on. By nightfall the contagion has crossed the ocean; seismographs in Manila register a micro-sigh. Credits—measured in breath-length—accrue to my wrist-forest. The redwood graft pulses, adds a micron of girth. The mountain is amused.

Phase two: synchronise exhalation across time zones.  
I queue at the Breath Exchange, a bourse where respiration is measured, swapped, and futures-traded. Teenagers sell the reckless carbon of their first cigarettes; elders buy it to carbonate community gardens. I short-sell 600 millilitres of 2040 hypoxic breath harvested from a traffic jam that will never happen now—our reward for last decade’s highway-ripening parties, where we danced the asphalt back into prairie. With the proceeds I purchase a crate of Mongolian exhale harvested during the annual throat-singing festival—low frequency, slow release, perfect for sunset. I tag the breath with peach pigment and release it into the ventilation orchestra. The conductor is a retired hurricane who decided to become a score. She accepts my phrase.

At 18:00 local the city exhales. Peach blooms. For 42 seconds conversations soften; two rival engineers apologise over trigonometry; a child tells her father she does not mind that he once forgot her recital. Then the pigment oxidises, the sky returns to geranium, and the daily sneeze pollen begins to ripen in roof-gardens. Maintenance, not miracle.

Afternoon belongs to the Parliament of Temporary Species.  
Conflict: whether the river should remember its 2084 chemical spill. The spill birthed the first pharmacist-algae; without the trauma there is no antidote. Some argue trauma is an heirloom worth preserving. Others want ecological amnesia, a clean ledger for newly spawned trout. The Parliament meets in a grove where every participant becomes, for one hour, the substance they advocate. I choose to become the year 2084: a viscous, translucent rag that smells of burnt plastic and opportunity. My opponent becomes 2094—clear, champagne-bright, hosting the first heron chicks in decades. We do not debate; we interdiffuse. Where our edges meet, a new colour appears: the exact grey of apology. The grove records the hue, translates it into a policy: the spill will be remembered as a flavour—bitter almond—added each year to one bottle of communal wine. Enough to taste, not enough to poison. The trout approve; their shadows grow less bitter.

Education here is porous. Children matriculate by rotating through “practice selves.” At nine my neighbour’s daughter spent a month as a lithium-ion battery, learning the patience of stored noon. At twelve she became her own yet-unborn little brother, practising ambivalence. At fifteen she will become a decade—any decade the planet needs re-enacted. She is auditioning for the 1930s dust bowl, a period we keep alive so the soil never forgets what lungs feel like. When she graduates she will not receive a certificate but a silence: three whole seconds each morning when no one needs her. The silence is renewable; she can pass it on or spend it like cash. Most hoard it until they fall in love, then squander it all at once.

Art is the maintenance of attention.  
The Dissolution Markets open at dusk. Tonight’s exhibit: a love affair ending well. The lovers—both present—have spent a year choreographing their parting. They will dissolve the relationship into six artefacts: a scarf that carries the embarrassment of first mispronouncing a surname; a fork that remembers every argument about salt; a star-name rented for one century; a recipe for soup that will only work if cooked by strangers; a glacier lullaby; and a child-they-never-had encoded as an orchestra of May beetles. Spectators bid not with money but with the promise to keep the artefacts interesting. I offer to plant the beetles beneath the primary school so future children can fail at conducting dusk. My bid is accepted. The lovers kiss one last time, release the artefacts, and walk separate directions—still fond, now lighter. The market smells of cinnamon and goodbye; archaeologists of emotion will visit for weeks.

Grief is cultivated like winter wheat.  
When my father chose to become a season—autumn, specifically—I mourned not his death but his dispersal. The Grief Conservatory assigned me a plot: one square metre of urban soil where every leaf that fell became a memory I had to compost. For 100 days I turned the pile, adding coffee grounds, adding laughter, adding the specific joke he told the day I learned to ride a bike. By spring the humus smelled of him. I spread it beneath a sapling gingko pledged to a playground. Children now swing above that soil; their shoes carry microscopic grandfathers into geometry lessons. Grief became curriculum; the tree does not know his name, but it knows his timing.

Physical death is optional, but style is not.  
Every citizen must choose an exit choreography before their 100th solar year. Options include:

1. Cryo-braid: reversible suspension, body stored as narrative tension.  
2. Species slip: consciousness distributed among crow clans, mycelium, or weather fronts.  
3. Archive dive: one-way upload into the Lunar Mycelial Network, thought proceeding at tectonic bitrate.  
4. Elemental sigh: become a pure process—rust, refraction, the hush between chapters—for a decade, then re-coalesce if desired.

I have pre-selected option 2 distributed among arctic terns. I like the idea of forgetting how to be human every migratory season. The contract stipulates that if I return I must bring one new vocabulary word; terns are shameless neologists.

Foreign policy is practiced across time, not space.  
Every generation stages the Temporal Potlatch: 2 % of remaining human lifespan pooled into a lottery. Winners travel 150 years forward, negotiate with descendants, return with amendments. Last cycle the future asked us to please stop inventing new primary colours until we learn to share the existing ones. We complied; geopolitical tension dropped 3 %. This cycle they want more inefficient playgrounds—slides that frictionlessly convert velocity into lullabies. We are building one in every park; the prototype sings restless five-year-olds into afternoon naps, freeing parents to rehearse civil patience.

The Centrifugal Waltz occurs every nine years. Humanity walks 1,000 kilometres outward, teaching strangers what we know, learning what we do not. When the planet’s collective footfall synchronises at 60 beats per minute we know the dance is complete; we turn around and walk home, carrying new selves in our shoes. The return journey is called Centripetal Quiet; no one speaks until the centre finds us. Shoes placed on doorsteps that morning are filled by different feet at night. Laundry lines across continents bloom with mismatched socks that will take years to reunite. Divorce rates drop; sock reconciliation is considered good marital practice.

Utopia ends each night with a lullaby whose lyrics are the entire day’s mistakes, sung in the key of tomorrow. The city exhales, the sky sneezes gold, the river forgets on schedule. We lie down carrying nothing we cannot compost. The dwellings fold into pockets; the mountains file their taxes; the terns invent a word for human that roughly translates to “temporary choir.”

And morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light. Today the photons vote for amber with a violet underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying. I sneeze, read the honesty on my lover’s cheek, add my note to the chord. The loom of shared breath clacks once, weaving the next 24 hours: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary. We walk forward carrying only the pattern we will leave behind.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed. We do not promise eternity. We promise revision.
