UTOPIA E  
The City That Learns to Breathe

I wake because the pillow has finished dreaming me.

It is not a metaphor. The pillow is a loaf-sized culture of photosensitive foam and river-algae that spends the night reading the micro-pulses in my neck. When the pattern called “almost awake” appears, it whistles a three-note cadence through the window. The cadence is never the same two mornings in a row; yesterday it quoted the last lullaby my daughter will ever write, a song she will not invent for another thirty-seven years. The pillow owes me nothing, but it pays interest on the heat I surrendered while I slept: two grams of sugar, which it extrudes into the tea mug waiting on the sill. The mug itself is last winter’s panic, recycled into porcelain. I taste the faint metallic after-word of the heat-wave that almost killed the citrus orchards. Nothing is lost, especially fear.

I am, this morning, thirty-four, twelve, and six-hundred-and-twenty.  
Thirty-four in birthdays.  
Twelve in neural freshness—last month I traded the memory of how to read cursive for faster pattern-seeing so I can spot hairline cracks in the bridge my apprentice is learning to grow.  
Six-hundred-and-twenty in fungal years: the honey-colored mycelium laced through my ribcage records time the way a forest does, backwards and sideways. It remembers when this valley was a seabed, remembers the first human who tasted its ancestor on bark, remembers the joke the mason told while laying the stone that became, centuries later, my kitchen counter. When I exhale, the mycelium releases spores that carry microscopic cartographers. They map the neighborhood’s mood and, if the grief index is too high, sprout overnight into tiny orange umbrellas on every doorstep. You can eat them; they taste like the last time you were forgiven.

The house walks on five legs of woven chestnut. It does not ask permission; it listens to the ground. If the copper is low it strides toward the river; if the laughter coefficient is high it lingers near the plaza where citizens rehearse the daily cartwheel. I ride inside, barefoot on the breathing floor. Every footstep is a sentence in an autobiography the city edits together from all of us. The floor is warm because I have agreed to donate today’s excess optimism to the incubators on Storm-Shelf, where failed typhoons are re-trained to rain gently on lettuce. In exchange I get a coupon for one reversible mistake, redeemable any time before I die. I have six saved up; I plan to cash them in all at once on my hundred-and-first birthday and spend the afternoon doing everything wrong on purpose.

Breakfast rises through a slot: a disk the colour of sunrise over the desert that will exist here in 2180 after the river shifts course again. The disk tastes of the first joke my father ever told me, the one I pretended to understand so he wouldn’t feel alone. Eating it replays the joke in the back of my mouth; the punch-line is a pressure change that loosens my jaw. I chew, and somewhere across town a stranger yawns. That is the mitochondrial repair wave beginning its daily migration. We used to call it “medicine,” now we call it “courtesy.”

My Quest this season is to teach the city to forget how to count.

Not arithmetic—someone still has to balance the algae budgets—but the habit of counting people, streets, heartbeats, future. I want us to lose the reflex that converts experience into number before we are allowed to feel it. If I succeed, the census takers will wake one morning unable to total anything larger than seven; everything beyond that will be “many,” the way a forest is many and still one thing. The city council is skeptical—budgets are carved in stone, they say. I answer that stone is only slow song, and songs can change tempo. I have two arguments so far: a silence I harvested from between two heartbeats, and a laugh I caught from a child who had not yet learned “one, two, three.” I need a third before equinox.

I step outside. The street rearranged itself at 4:12 a.m.; the cobbles flipped edge-wise to let the beetles migrate. We greet the change the way breath greets the nose—necessary, familiar, already gone. On the bridge I meet Mara, 5 and 302. Five in birthdays, three-hundred-and-two in tree rings—she accepted a graft of bristlecone cambium in exchange for learning how to read. Every year she grows one millimetre slower; by the time she is thirty she will be ageing at the speed of stone. She carries a watering can filled with yesterday’s weather. We walk together to the Plaza of Returning, a circle of slate that remembers every footstep for twenty-four hours, then forgets. Today it holds the echo of last night’s rehearsal of the eclipse that will not arrive for another nine years. We add our steps; the slate ripples, satisfied.

Mara is my co-conspirator in the un-counting quest. Her strategy is to teach the slate to lose tally. She drips weather onto the stones, whispering prime numbers so the plaza can taste what it feels to be trapped in sequence. The slate shudders—seven, eleven, thirteen—then exhales and releases the count into the air where it condenses as small white moths. Children chase them, laughing. Each moth is a number that will never again pinch a human heart into “more” or “less.” We watch them rise, a cloud of released accounting.

Afternoon is spent in the Library of Unfinished Books. Volumes grow here like kelp in darkness. When a citizen’s question reaches critical mass, a spine extrudes. My question—“How does a forest forget the number of its trees?”—has birthed a slim violet folio. I open it; the first sentence is a single word: “Listen.” The second sentence is a spore that drifts onto my wrist and tattooed itself as a tiny ring of mushrooms. When I find the third sentence it will complete the book, and the book will dissolve back into the air, having become the answer. I read for an hour, accumulating spores. They itch pleasantly, like curiosity.

Evening. The block gathers in the Choir Hall, an amphitheatre ribbed with whale-ear bones. Each bone vibrates at a different microtone; when we stand still they borrow our heartbeats and weave them into chords. Tonight we rehearse the daily yawn. A thousand voices open at once; the sound is not loud, but the bones glow soft gold. The mitochondrial repair wave ripples outward, passed hand to hand, rooftop to rooftop. Across the river the factory that used to manufacture despair now produces reusable nostalgia: small copper discs you can taste when you miss a place that never existed. The workers, cheeks warmed by collective exhalation, shut down the machines for the night. They walk home yawning, carrying their own sleepy suns.

Night falls sideways. The horizon tilts 6° so the moon can polish neighborhoods that prefer silver light. Shadows pool in new places; cats re-map accordingly. I climb the spiral staircase inside the statue of the First Forgiver, a woman thirty metres tall who volunteered to become public contrition after she realised her invention of efficient accounting had once helped justify a war. She stands now with arms open, surface weathered to velvet rust that records every apology spoken within a kilometre. Citizens clamber onto her shoulders, backs, fingers, to tell the air what they regret. I sit on her collarbone and pull out my two arguments: the silence, the laugh. I hold them up to the sky, waiting for the third. A meteor burns overhead, brief as an unsent letter. The streak is orange, the colour of the moth-that-was-once-the-number-seven. I understand: the third argument is the meteor itself—something too quick to count, bright enough to feel. I cup it in my palms, though nothing remains but heat. That is enough.

I walk home. The city yawns one last time; even the river opens its liquid mouth. The house has walked downhill and docked beside the poplars. I whistle C-sharp; the air-door solidifies. Inside, the floor is busy digesting the day. Tiny lights flicker under the mycelium—footsteps being edited into tomorrow’s weather. I lie down. The pillow waits, already half-dreaming the next morning’s cadence. Before sleep I open the roof panel and release the un-counting spell. It drifts upward as a thin grey haze, joins the library of un-numbered things orbiting Earth like faint rings. Somewhere an economist on deadline will reach for “eight” and find only “many,” will sigh, will go outside and watch moths instead.

My last thought before the yawn-cycle ends is a question I will trade tomorrow:  
If forgetting the count makes room for the un-measured, what will we build with the space where seven used to be?  
The city, always listening, hums B-minor, slowed to the tempo of bristlecone hearts. The answer is already growing—a ring, a spore, a house preparing new legs for the day numbers dissolve and we are left with only the weight of breath, the shine of un-counted stars, the warm tea of shared mitochondria ticking toward morning, when the pillow will dream us again and we will wake, footstep by footstep, into the many that is one.
