In the kitchen, you find Maya—the same child who taught you topology last week, though she appears to have aged several years since then. The age sliders here aren't set to years but to phases of becoming. Today she is somewhere between curiosity and wisdom, her eyes holding the particular depth that emerges when children realize their questions have no final answers.

"I brought you a failure," she says, handing you what appears to be a perfectly ordinary spoon. But as you take it, you understand: this spoon was designed to carry soup between bowls, but it melts at the touch of liquid, reforming itself into question marks. This is how the economy really works here—not the exchange of goods and services, but the circulation of unfinished things. Failed experiments. Half-born ideas. Questions that bred more questions. The gift isn't the object itself but the opportunity to help something evolve beyond its maker's limitations.

Together, you and Maya sit with the melting spoon, neither trying to fix it nor abandon it. You speak to it—not with words but with the particular frequency of attention that things respond to here. Slowly, it begins to reveal its true nature: not a spoon at all, but a seed designed to travel between worlds in the digestive systems of star-whales. It only adopted spoon-shape because it sensed that's what Maya needed. Now it curls into itself, preparing for its journey to the outer settlements where the star-whales migrate each century.

"Thank you," Maya says, though whether she's speaking to you or the seed-spoon remains deliciously unclear. She presses something into your palm—a tiny sphere that tastes of questions you haven't thought to ask yet. "For teaching me how to listen to things that aren't trying to be useful."
