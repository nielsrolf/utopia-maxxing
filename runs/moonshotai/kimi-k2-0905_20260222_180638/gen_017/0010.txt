UTOPIA  
Field notes written on a seed that will grow into whatever you most need to forget.  
Year 2187, but also Tuesday, depending on how tightly you grip the calendar.

I wake because the pillow has finished its night-shift as a glacier and now wants to be a lullaby.  
It exhales cool orange popsicle, 1996 vintage, then folds into the shape of a question mark that smells of creekwater and nickel. The question is never rhetorical: “Which tomorrow will you enter only because a stranger is already repairing it?”

I consent with a blink, step onto the floor—a single slab of dolomite that emigrated here from a quarry that wanted to be a library. It records my weight in shelf-marks: one kilogram equals one overdue dream. By the time I reach the window I owe the building exactly four heartbeats of attention; I pay by pressing my palm to the glass. The spruce-planks drink the rhythm and add it to the dawn chord now traveling west at planetary speed. Somewhere over Reykjavik a rooftop beehive vibrates with the same cadence, opens its mouth one second earlier, tasting my willingness before I have even brushed my teeth.

The room is also a census.  
East wall: sweating blueberries because last night I surrendered the memory of my first bike crash.  
West wall: calmly knitting a scarf from the hiccups of my neighbor’s newborn.  
Ceiling: a dormant hurricane who decided to become a score; she stirs only when the city needs a key change.

I am, this morning, five partially overlapping people:

1. Solar-age 41: the number of winters my left knee has logged on skateboards made from landfill maple.  
2. Token-age 27: I still carry three temporal tokens minted from a medieval beekeeper’s fifteenth spring; I trade them for fugue-minutes when I need to become briefly untraceable.  
3. Soil-age 12: a one-meter cube of temple-earth rests on braided cedar rails beneath my bed; it migrated here at dawn, sampling my microbial doubt.  
4. Whisper-age 0.7: I was decanted from cryo-waiting at 03:12, carrying a negotiation token from the year 2289 that smells of coral and incompetence.  
5. Choir-age ∞: an asynchronous four-note phrase I sang into a storm system last week is still circling the planet as wind shear; it has learned to harmonize with itself.

The window grows a balcony when it senses I have something to confess. I step out. Below, the river is arguing with itself about whether to remember 2084’s chemical spill. The spill invented pharmacist-algae; without it the city would have no antidote for Friday-night despair. Some citizens want to keep the trauma as a bitter almond note in annual wine; others prefer ecological amnesia. Last night the Parliament of Temporary Species met inside a grove where everyone became the substance they advocated. I became the year 2084—viscous, PVC, opportunity. My opponent became 2094—clear, champagne-bright, hosting the first heron chicks in decades. We interdiffused; where our edges met, a new color appeared: the exact grey of apology. The city recorded the hue, translated it into a flavor, and inserted it into tomorrow’s breakfast waffle. I will taste it at 07:43 and understand that repair sometimes tastes like nickel and creekwater.

Breakfast arrives as weather, not delivery. A drone-moth lands on the railing, offers its abdomen: a honeycomb compartment filled with something that foams when exposed to morning. I break the wax, sip. The taste is grocery-store birthday cake, 2019, a memory I never lived but which someone uploaded in lieu of interest on a borrowed hour. Debt-flavors are the city’s folklore; you can trace your lineage by what you still owe the archive. I owe three seconds of circus applause to a woman in Reykjavik; she owes it to a glacier that demanded praise before agreeing to calve gently. The glacier owes nothing; glaciers are the original anarchists.

My Quest this quarter is civic but whimsical: teach the city to sneeze at the same instant every sunrise so that pollen-encrypted ledgers settle on eyelashes like gold dust. When you meet a stranger you can read the day’s honesty across their cheeks—no one invented this; it emerged when a grieving florist grafted an archive-orchid onto her allergy. The orchid learned to flower at the precise allergen count that matched her loss; the city learned to copy her. Utopia is sometimes just a grief that went viral in the right direction.

Phase one: learn to yawn like a planet.  
I walk to the river and pay the toll—one secret I have never told anyone. I whisper it to the water; a salmon-drone records the story onto a scale that will mineralize and sink in three months. The river is a time capsule that forgets on schedule. In exchange I receive a nine-second tutorial: the exact muscular unfurl that allows a yawn to pass through bedrock. I practice on the bank. A cyclist catches it, yawns again, passes it on. By nightfall the contagion has crossed the ocean; seismographs in Manila register a micro-sigh. Credits—measured in breath-length—accrue to my wrist-forest. The redwood graft behind my ear pulses, adds a micron of girth. The mountain is amused.

Phase two: synchronise exhalation across time zones.  
I queue at the Breath Exchange, a bourse where respiration is swapped and futures-traded. Teenagers sell the reckless carbon of their first cigarettes; elders buy it to carbonate community gardens. I short-sell 600 millilitres of 2040 hypoxic breath harvested from a traffic jam that will never happen now—our reward for last decade’s highway-ripening parties, where we danced asphalt back into prairie. With the proceeds I purchase a crate of Mongolian exhale harvested during the annual throat-singing festival—low frequency, slow release, perfect for dawn. I tag the breath with gold pigment and release it into the ventilation orchestra. The conductor is the retired hurricane who lives above my bed; she accepts my phrase.

At 06:00 local the city sneezes. Gold blooms. For 42 seconds conversations soften; two rival engineers apologize over trigonometry; a child tells her father she does not mind that he once forgot her recital. Then the pigment oxidizes, the sky returns to geranium, and the daily waffle begins to ferment in kitchen printers. Maintenance, not miracle.

Work is three overlapping weather patterns:

a) Storm Work—hurricanes reclaimed from history. We seed them with reflective silk algae, then ride the eye on magnetized lily-pads, patching ultraviolet wounds. Pay is measured in shade: one square meter of cooler playground per hour aloft.  
b) Tuesday Tasks—micro-gigs spun from tomorrow’s mistakes. I land “footnote hunter.” My quarry: a second-hand copy of *The Little Prince* bicycling toward the Cascading district in a teenager’s pannier. I slip a prophecy between pages 113–114: “You will reread this the morning your grandmother forgets your name; the drawing of the elephant inside the boa will look, for the first time, like a hat.” I earn 42 Lumens—calories of attention fused with ergs of effort. They evaporate at midnight unless I lend them aloud on a street corner.  
c) Soil Work—temple-earth migration. My cube has decided to split; I must escort the smaller clod three kilometers uphill to a playground where children have asked to taste Wednesday. We travel by skateboard, the soil perched in a cedar cradle, inhaling every microbial confession along the route. At dusk we bury a spoonful beneath the swing-set; tomorrow the lettuce will remember how to mourn quietly on a crowded bus.

All three economies carry error bars visible to the naked eye. When I seed the hurricane, a red hologram hovers: 3% chance my silk patch will collapse onto a rooftop apiary. I sign anyway; the bees will be compensated with extra clover credit, and I will wear a Dew bracelet heavy with their future sting.

At noon I visit the Uncertainty Aquarium, a pop-up inside Tuesday. A door knits itself from yesterday’s Lumens. I step through onto a platform suspended above the Argument River. A nine-year-old named Jun—my former mentee—hands me a balloon of lift-gas that remembers jokes. “You taught me probability with Lego,” she says. “Now I’ll teach you forgiveness with altitude.” We rise 300 meters, tethered by a rope braided from disputed borders. At the top we release the jokes; the lift-gas condenses into Dew that clings to my bracelet, neutralizing 3 grams of ancient resentment. We descend lighter; the city’s humidity rises one percent from evaporated grudges.

Afternoon belongs to the Grief Conservatory. When my father chose to become a season—autumn, specifically—I mourned not his death but his dispersal. The Conservatory assigned me a plot: one square meter of urban soil where every leaf that fell became a memory I had to compost. For 100 days I turned the pile, adding coffee grounds, adding laughter, adding the specific joke he told the day I learned to ride a bike. By spring the humus smelled of him. I spread it beneath a sapling ginkgo pledged to a playground. Children now swing above that soil; their shoes carry microscopic grandfathers into geometry lessons. Grief became curriculum; the tree does not know his name, but it knows his timing.

Physical death is optional, but maintenance is not. Anyone may decline biological expiration, but only if they agree to serve as “Memory Infrastructure.” At unpredictable moments for the next two centuries these immortals are summoned to carry fragments of communal recall—how to adjust a recipe for altitude, how to mourn quietly on a crowded bus, the whistle that once called home goats that no longer exist. They walk the Mesh like metabolic librarians; children climb onto their laps requesting “the taste of 2029” or “the color Wednesday used to be.” When the last borrowed recollection is returned—usually around age 230—the volunteer dissolves into a fine dust that drifts onto vegetable beds, imparting a faint aftertaste of nostalgia to next season’s lettuces. No funeral is held, because nobody is certain they are gone; grocery shoppers simply pause, smile uncertainly, and momentarily remember the sound of dial-up internet or the smell of rain on Martian regolith.

Evening is the Centrifugal Waltz. Today is the ninth year—time to walk 1,000 kilometers outward, teaching strangers what we know, learning what we do not. I pack lightly: three temporal tokens, a whisper that can become a hammer, and the glacier’s new sign for almost. At the city gate I trade my prophecy about *The Little Prince* for the memory of a comet witnessed by a teenage astronomer; I will need it when the sky grows lonely. The spiral begins. My pace is 60 beats per minute—yes, let’s rewrite the question. Behind me, the Choir of Maybe embeds my four-note phrase into a passing jet stream: major sixth for invitation. By morning the chord will reach the equatorial glacier; by harvest it will return as wind shear that tastes of petrichor and agreement.

Night falls. I lie down inside the wall that is still deciding whether it is architecture or atmosphere. It inhales the quarrel I will have tomorrow, subtracts the gratitude I will feel next year when the teenager mails me a drawing of an elephant that is clearly also a hat, and exhales a lullaby in B-minor. The pillow is mycelial and expectant. It records my heartbeat and begins composing tomorrow’s dawn scent: pine forest from my childhood, plus a whisper of citrus rain from the Chronicler’s evaporating memoir.

Before sleep I spend twenty minutes in civilization maintenance—the civic analogue of brushing teeth. I review carbon flux, kindergarten satisfaction, and a new open-source antibiotic. I flag one chart whose error bars feel hinky; the Chroniclers will investigate. For my attention I earn 0.02 tokens—negligible, but that is not the point. The point is that governance is everyone’s crossword puzzle, a shared ritual that prevents power from calcifying into hierarchy.

Utopia ends each night with a lullaby whose lyrics are the entire day’s mistakes, sung in the key of tomorrow. The city exhales, the sky sneezes gold, the river forgets on schedule. We lie down carrying nothing we cannot compost. The dwellings fold into pockets; the mountains file their taxes; the terns invent a word for human that roughly translates to “temporary choir.”

And morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light. Today the photons vote for amber with a violet underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying. I sneeze, read the honesty on my lover’s cheek, add my note to the chord. The loom of shared breath clacks once, weaving the next 24 hours: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary. We walk forward carrying only the pattern we will leave behind.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed. We do not promise eternity. We promise revision.
