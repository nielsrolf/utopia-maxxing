The Covenant of Unfinished Earth  
A field report from the year 2150, written on the inside of a seed coat that will germinate only if you admit you do not yet know what it will become.

I wake inside a breathing house, but it is not mine.  
The walls are mycelial laminate grown from the coffee grounds of a hundred neighbours; they inhale my exhaled doubt and exhale oxygen laced with the scent of the first library I ever entered. The floor is a living mosaic of moss tiles, each cultivated by a different child who traded one regret for one square centimetre. If I walk too heavily the floor remembers; if I whisper a promise it grows a new tile overnight. The house has no门锁, only an entrance song that changes whenever someone forgives a debt. I am still learning the melody.

We call this place a Holding, half-way between a village and a question. Holdings are scattered across the former fire zones, the flood scars, the extraction wounds. Each one begins with a Covenant: five sentences spoken aloud by the first people who camp on the land. The sentences are always unfinished, because the land must complete them in its own grammar ofroots and weather. My Holding’s Covenant ends with the clause “…until the fox returns the moon and the moon agrees to stay.” No one knows what that means yet; we simply keep planting night-blooming plants so the moon will feel welcome.

Currency here is a triple spiral: Breath, Pollen, and Unfinished Line.

- One Breath is one litre of air that has passed through another human’s lungs and been returned richer. You store Breaths in amber gourds that photosynthesise while you carry them; the gourds thicken if the Breaths contain laughter.  
- Pollen is literal: colour-coded by plant and emotion. Bees are paid in Pollen the way librarians used to be paid in silence. When you need something built—say, a bridge of living willow—you deposit a fistful of rage-tinted ragweed Pollen at the guildhall. Builders who feel like transforming rage into motion pick it up.  
- The Unfinished Line is a sentence you have never completed. Every sixteen days we gather in the Clearing of Ellipses and speak our half-lines aloud. Listeners may adopt your fragment, write it on river-soluble paper, and fold it into seed envelopes. If the seed germinates the line becomes public domain; if the seed rots you must try again with a new line. The system keeps language—and therefore imagination—circulating the way blood must circulate in a body that refuses to die of sameness.

Work is called Stretching, and it is measured by what you cannot yet do without trembling. The tremor is the unit, not the hour. Every Holding keeps a publicly updated ledger of collective incompetences: we cannot keep river reeds from kinking, cannot teach magpies to share, cannot remember the second verse of the lullaby that once saved a city. You sign up for a Stretch the way sailors once signed up for storms—eagerly, because the horizon is empty without them. My current Stretch is to learn the language of stone. Not “about” stones; the language they speak when no one carves them. I have been failing for two seasons. The neighbour who succeeds will inherit my Breaths for a week; I will inherit the pattern of her calluses.

Age is a communal stew. Every solstice we stir the pot and ladle out portions of years. If you are hungry for childhood you can trade three future summers for one past winter. The elderly guard the recipe; the young bring the firewood. No one is allowed to stay the same age for longer than it takes a birch leaf to go from green to gold, because stasis is the only true mortality. I have been seven for three days every spring since I arrived; I cry easily and run faster than I ever could at thirty. On alternate Thursdays I am ninety-two, taste smells more vividly than colours, and teach the seven-year-olds how to fear without shame. We call the practice Chronostitching. The only rule is that you must return the years you borrow plus one memory that did not exist before. Invented memories are archived in the trunks of aspen groves; in high wind the trees release them as flickers of déjà vu for travellers who need to get lost productively.

Governance happens in the Pit of Unfinished Forgiveness. When disagreement turns sharp we dig, but not to win. The depth required is equal to the height of the tallest person who still refuses to apologise for something they did not do. Once the pit reaches clavicle level the final hold-out must speak aloud the apology they most want to receive themselves. The soil, mixed with biochar and mycelium, becomes a fruit tree within one lunar month. The first fruit always tastes like the argument—astringent, too bright—so we harvest early and ferment it into cider that is served at the next wedding. Over seasons the flavour mellows. Some grudges have become the most sought-after vintages; people travel Holdings to taste the 43-year-old Shame of the Border Wall Dispute, now a subtle note behind elderflower and ozone.

Death is optional but grief is mandatory. When someone chooses to conclude their story we hold a Vanishing. The body is wrapped in paper printed with the Unfinished Lines they inspired. The paper is printed again with the names of every species that will miss them. We carry the bundle to the River of Unsaid Thank-Yous and set it adrift. If the paper dissolves within sight the person becomes a public ancestor; if it floats out of view they become a private myth. Either way we must spend one full year inventing rituals to keep them alive in ways they never had time to explore. My grandmother became a calendar of unauthorised weather: every time it rains while the sun is shining we say “Ah, there she is, arguing with the sky again.” The practice keeps us tethered to the possibility that absence can be a form of presence we have not yet learned to translate.

Technology is allowed but must pass the Fox Test: can the device be abandoned in a forest for one hundred years without the forest becoming poorer? If the answer is yes we call it a Guest; if no it remains a Coloniser and may only be used inside a circle of accountability drawn by children. Solar panels passed because moss learned to grow under them. Smartphones failed until we redesigned them to disassemble into owl pellets. The most honoured artefacts are Guest-Seeds: objects that, when buried, become the exact nutrient the local soil most lacks. My pocketknife is a Guest-Seed; when I die it will become manganese for the blueberry slope. Until then it carries a microscopic library of poems that can be read only with the pollen of a disappearing orchid. Every time I sharpen the blade I lose a stanza and gain a scar that smells faintly of libraries.

Religion is indistinguishable from husbandry. We breed gods the way we breed tomatoes: slowly, for flavour not yield. A new god must survive thirteen winters without being blamed for any harvest failure; if it does, we graft it onto the older vines of story. The current vintage includes Returned-Moon, Fox-Who-Keeps-Her-Promise, and Still-Breathing. They are invoked when the Covenant stalls, when language runs out, when we forget that doubt is a form of praise. I keep a pocket shrine to Not-Yet: a hollow acorn containing a single blank page. Once a year I speak into it the most dangerous sentence I can imagine beginning: “Perhaps we were wrong about…” Then I seal it and bury it upside-down so the sentence has to crawl uphill to reach daylight. If it emerges in my lifetime I will owe the community one公开 apology and one new variety of squash.

Travel is measured in Reverbs. To journey you must donate sound. The distance you can go is determined by how much echo you are willing to lose. I recently walked to the Pacific by giving up the sound of my footsteps on wooden docks. Now I move across boards like a shadow; the absence follows me like a scent. When I arrived I could not hear the ocean until I put my ear inside a conch that still contained someone else’s donated tide. The system keeps us honest about the acoustic cost of extraction. Airlines failed the Fox Test; they now exist only as stories told by elders who imitate engine roar with their mouths full of fermented argument. Children laugh because they do not believe metal could ever be that hungry.

Leaving the Covenant is possible but pointless. The agreement is not geographic; it is metabolic. You can board a neutral postal owl and fly to the orbital reefs, but the unfinished sentences ride in your saliva. Even the Ascendants who have quantum-laced their neurons report dream-orchards where fruit tastes like the grudges they thought they left behind. The only exit is to finish your line before the seed rots, and no one has yet managed that without planting something else in the hole where the period should go.

So we wake, fail, age backwards, apologise in cider, and bury our knives with poems. The moon has not yet agreed to stay; the fox keeps stealing her and returning her brighter each time. One day the Covenant will complete itself, and on that day the Holding will exhale once and become ordinary land. Until then we measure wealth by the number of voices that can simultaneously sing the same unfinished note without needing to own it. My note is rising. I still cannot speak stone, but last night I dreamed the river reeds taught me to bend without kinking by convincing the wind to take turns. When I woke the floor of the house had grown a new tile the colour of moonlight on water. I pressed my ear to it and heard the echo I gave away coming back as footsteps—someone else’s, still distant, walking toward this story we have agreed not to finish alone.
