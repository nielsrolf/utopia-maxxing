UTOPIA C  
The Hum & the Hollow  

I wake because the room has begun to hum in B-minor—the key my mother hummed while folding laundry, the key the city’s river uses when it thinks no one is listening. The sound is not in the air; it is the air. Ten thousand fungal hyphae under the floorboards vibrate at 246 hertz, reporting that last night’s carbon ledger is balanced, the neighborhood has stored 3.7 kilos more CO₂ than it exhaled, and the surplus has been converted into a single stanza of coral poetry now drifting south on the under-sea jet.  

The hyphae ask, without words, whether I want to hear the stanza. I tap my toe once—yes—and the eastern wall blossoms into a slow-moving projection of turquoise syllables. I cannot read coral script, but I feel it: the way you feel a childhood lullaby when the actual words are gone. It lingers for seven breaths, then dissolves, leaving the faint taste of iodine on my tongue.  

I do not own the room.  
I am its current steward, elected by lottery last spring when the previous steward’s Quest-cycle ended. My term lasts 1,000 days. On day 999 the dwelling will interview the block’s six-year-olds to see which of them carries the next compatible microbial choir. I will pack one bag, leave the coffee mug that remembers my fingerprints, and walk on. The house itself—mycelium, quartz ribs, algae glass—will stay, because it is 37% alive and 100% place.  

Breakfast arrives through the floor: a cinnamon-colored disk that is not a pancake and not a mushroom but a negotiation between them. Yesterday the block’s kids rehearsed a famine scenario set in 2090; the disk is a flavor archive of that rehearsal—notes of worry, roasted hope, and the precise sweetness of the moment the dream-senate voted to share the last grain. Eating it is like licking the envelope of a letter you haven’t decided to send.  

While I chew, the dwelling’s mirror surfaces a morning bulletin:  
- The Salmon-Bear Assembly approved a 4% increase in river shadows—an experimental deepening of twilight hours to cool the spawning beds.  
- The Cloud Garden above the Pacific reported a 0.1 °C drop in planetary irritability.  
- The Centrifugal Waltz has begun; the nearest outward spiral path will pass through my street in eleven hours. I mark my calendar to leave a pair of walking shoes on the stoop for whoever’s foot falls into them.  

I am 42, 17, and 4.  
Forty-two in solar years.  
Seventeen in telomere count (I reset last winter, trading my cello proficiency for a new pancreas and the right to beginner’s fingers).  
Four in forest rings—because four years ago I grafted a sliver of larch cambium into my wrist. Every autumn the skin there flares gold and I feel the mountain behind my eyes sigh.  

My Quest for this life-phase is simple: teach the city to yawn.  

Not metaphor. A real, 37-second, jaw-aching yawn that can be passed from person to person without speech, carrying in it a dose of mitochondrial repair enzymes woven by the river’s pharmacist algae. The contagion must spread through muscle memory alone; if we succeed, the nightly repair will add an extra 0.3 years of healthy life to every resident—small, but cumulative across centuries.  

Phase one was learning to yawn like a river. I spent three weeks with the tidal choir, letting my vagus nerve entrain to the nine-second pulse of estuarine mud. Phase two is harder: make the yawn interesting enough to copy. Rehearsal credits will only flood in if strangers feel the yawn as curiosity rather than obedience.  

I step outside. The street has rearranged itself again. Overnight the moving stones traded places with the memory-oaks; the pavement now ripples gently, like a cat dreaming of milk. No one is surprised. We greet the shift the way sailors greet wind.  

At the corner I meet Jonas, who is 8 and 93. Eight in birthdays, ninety-three in failed quests—he has chosen to forget and relearn carpentry fourteen times, each cycle carving the same chair and then letting the skill evaporate. The chair never repeats; the forgetting guarantees it. Today he carries a cedar plank that smells of thunderstorms.  

“Test walk?” he asks.  

We fall into step beside a woman jogging backwards—standard practice for Centrifugal season, training to meet strangers face-first. Her T-shirt displays a live feed of her own pupils dilating; she is practicing radical transparency for the week when she will have no walls.  

At the river we remove our shoes. The water is black with sky. Tiny silver drones—salmon fingerlings wearing aluminum vests—leap through the surface, sampling our ankle skin for stress hormones. If they detect cortisol, the river will hum a different key tonight, soothing the whole watershed. The fish are government employees, salaried in clean gravel beds.  

Jonas and I rehearse the yawn. I demonstrate: mouth unhooked, tongue flat, the slow ache spreading like warm tea. The river listens; a single drone fish records the EM signature of my facial muscles.  

A jogger passing by catches the yawn—lips peel, eyes water. Success. She laughs in the middle of it, which warps the waveform. The fish stores the mutated version. Already the contagion is drifting.  

By noon the yawn has crossed two bridges and a floating market. Rehearsal credits trickle into my wrist-forest; the larch ring vibrates, adding a micron of girth. I feel the sap rise and know the mountain is amused.  

Afternoon is spent in the Dream Foundry, a brick warehouse that used to manufacture fear. Now citizens book fear in twenty-minute doses: rehearse a famine, a breakup, a plague, a launch into frozen space. Children get first dibs; they usually pick “public embarrassment” or “talking to ghosts.” The foundry’s most popular session is merely one minute of total silence while anonymous strangers stare at you—an exquisite lesson in being seen.  

Today’s spike is a solar-flare evacuation. I volunteer to play “the one who insists on carrying the piano.” We rehearse for three subjective days inside one objective hour. When we exit, cheeks damp with adrenaline-fog, we are each handed a small ceramic bird. Mine fits the hollow beneath my clavicle; if a real flare ever comes, the bird will heat, reminding me that I already practiced choosing what to leave behind.  

Evening. The block gathers for supper in the breathing kitchen—twenty-two humans, three crows, a retired guide-dog, and the river’s interpreter (a waist-high android whose face is a slowly rotating water wheel). We eat under the open sky; the roof folded itself into origami lanterns at dusk.  

Menu:  
- Memory bread that tastes of yesterday’s yawns.  
- Seawater tomatoes singing the minor third the city will use next week for power-grid regulation.  
- Dessert spoons carved from glacier air; they sublimate on the tongue, releasing the 19th-century laughter of women who invented the first microscopes in their kitchens.  

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch lottery. Tomorrow the names are drawn. Jonas jokes that if he wins he will negotiate with our descendants for a season of silence—no rehearsals, no new chairs, only the sound of heartwood thickening. We laugh, but the dog whimpers; she smells the future in the water wheel’s spin.  

Night. I walk the Centrifugal path. Thousands are already walking outward, radiating like rays from a dark sun. Each carries a question they have promised to trade. Mine, written on birch bark: *“What is the opposite of forgetting?”*  

I pass strangers, swap stories, swap silences. At midnight the global drumbeat synchronizes at 60 bpm; I feel it in the tendons of my knees. For one minute every foot lifts at the same instant, and the planet becomes a heart with seven billion valves opening together. Then we land, dispersing again.  

Home. The dwelling has grown a new room: a soft, lung-shaped alcove smelling of cedar and thunderstorm—Jonas’s gift left behind. I curl inside. The walls ask, *“Still you?”* I exhale once more than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means *“today I changed.”*  

Tomorrow the yawn may reach the coast and jump to a cargo ship bound for Ascension Island, where giant tortoises will mimic it in slow motion, adding centuries to their cellular warranty. Tomorrow the lottery may send me forward a hundred years to meet a child who is already humming my mother’s lullaby. Tomorrow the river might decide to flow uphill for an hour, just to see who trusts it enough to follow.  

Utopia is not a place where the wolf lies down with the lamb; it is a place where the wolf, the lamb, and the mycelium rehearse together until the idea of predation dissolves into a choreography of carbon exchange. It is not the absence of death but the presence of reversible practice—every ending grown, tasted, failed, and replanted before the real irreversible dawn.  

I close my eyes. The house inhales my day, exhales lullaby-B-minor. Somewhere a coral stanza drifts south; somewhere a cedar chair forgets itself; somewhere a tortoise yawns for the first time, its beak cracking open like a seed.  

The morning will request me again. I will answer, not because I must, but because the question is still interesting, and I have rehearsed the art of being surprised by what I already know.
