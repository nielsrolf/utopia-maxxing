UTOPIA E  
Field Notes from the Year 2194, etched on the inner bark of a walking forest  

I wake because the floor is asking to be danced on.

It is not a metaphor. The dwelling’s heart-wood—laminated mycelium, copper voice-wires, and a coral lattice that once belonged to the Great Barrier—pulses at 53 beats per minute, the exact cadence of my daughter’s in-utero heartbeat the day she decided to stay. When I place the soles of my feet on the planks, the tempo quickens by one beat every four seconds until it matches the swing of my pelvis. Only then does the room let daylight in. The photons arrive peach-warm with violet undersides, the colour we agreed means “forgiveness in process,” and they smell faintly of cinnamon archives.

I am 38, 24, 9, and 0.

- Thirty-eight in solar orbits.  
- Twenty-four in telomere loops—last winter I traded the memory of my first broken promise for a pair of new lungs grown in the River of Unsaid Thank-Yous.  
- Nine in forest rings: a graft of bristlecone cambium under my left scapula thickens one micron each equinox; when the ring reaches 3 mm I will be asked to carry a century for a stranger.  
- Zero in regret tonnes—my ledger reads negative 0.4, meaning the city owes me the weight of two rainstorms I have not yet invented.

Breakfast rises through the floor on a disk that is part pancake, part mushroom, part yawn. Yesterday the block rehearsed a famine that will probably never happen. The disk tastes of roasted worry and the moment the dream-senate voted to share the last grain. I chew, and the flavour unpacks into 17 frames of strangers hugging in slow motion. Frame 12 is me, age seven, hiding library fines in a flowerpot. The memory is not mine; it was donated by a cyclist who gave up the squeak of her brakes to reach the glacier. I swallow, and the ledger ticks to –0.5 regret tonnes. The city will rain on me within the week.

While I chew, the wall surfaces a bulletin:

- The Salmon-Bear Assembly approved a 3 % deepening of twilight to cool the spawning beds.  
- The Orbital Reef recorded a 0.1 °C drop in planetary irritability.  
- Tonight the Centrifugal Waltz begins; the nearest outward spiral will pass my stoop in nine hours. I mark the calendar to leave a pair of shoes on the step for whoever’s foot falls into them.

My Quest this life-phase is simple: teach the city to remember forgetting.

Not as metaphor. A literal, contagious, 37-second lapse of recall that can hop from stranger to stranger without speech, carrying a dose of mitochondrial repair enzymes brewed by the river’s pharmacist algae and a complementary dose of engineered forgettinine that dissolves traumatic engrams. If the contagion spreads, nightly repair will add 0.3 healthy years to every resident, while the forgetting will delete 0.3 years of cumulative cruelty. Small numbers, but compounding across centuries.

Phase one was learning to forget like a river. I spent three weeks with the tidal choir letting my hippocampus entrain to the nine-second pulse of estuarine mud. Phase two is harder: make the forgetting interesting enough to copy. Rehearsal credits—the only currency that still feels alive—will flood in only if strangers feel curiosity rather than obedience.

I step outside. The street has rearranged itself again. Overnight the walking stones traded places with memory-oaks; the pavement ripples like a cat dreaming of milk. No one is surprised. We greet the shift the way sailors greet wind.

At the corner I meet Jonas, 8 and 112. Eight in birthdays, one-hundred-twelve in forest rings—he has carried a century for strangers four times, each cycle grafting a new ring of bristlecone into his spine. Today he carries a cedar plank that smells of thunderstorms and the 2067 currency crash.

“Test walk?” he asks.

We fall in beside a woman jogging backwards—standard Centrifugal training. Her T-shirt live-streams her own pupils; she is practicing radical transparency for the week when she will have no walls. Her sweat evaporates into the communal cloud; somewhere a stranger will drink her diligence tomorrow.

At the river we remove our shoes. The water is black with sky. Fingerling drones—salmon wearing aluminium vests—leap through the surface, sampling our ankle skin for cortisol. If they detect stress, the river will hum a different key tonight, soothing the whole watershed. The fish are public servants, salaried in clean gravel.

I demonstrate the forgetting: eyes unfocus, tongue slack, the slow dissolve spreading like warm tea. A jogger passing by catches it—pupils dilate, jaw drops. She laughs mid-forgetting, warping the waveform. A fish stores the mutation. By noon the lapse has crossed two bridges and a floating market. Credits trickle into my scapular forest; the bristlecone ring vibrates, adding a micron of girth. The mountain is amused.

Afternoon is spent in the Dream Foundry, a brick warehouse that used to manufacture fear. Now citizens book fear in twenty-minute doses: famine, breakup, plague, launch into frozen space. Children get first dibs; they usually pick “public embarrassment” or “talking to ghosts.” Today’s spike is a solar-flare evacuation. I volunteer to play “the one who insists on carrying the piano.” We rehearse for three subjective days inside one objective hour. When we exit, cheeks damp with adrenaline-fog, we are handed ceramic birds. Mine fits the hollow beneath my clavicle; if a real flare ever comes, the bird will heat, reminding me I already practiced choosing what to leave behind.

Evening. The block gathers in the breathing kitchen—twenty-two humans, three crows, a retired guide-dog, the river’s interpreter (a waist-high android whose face is a slowly rotating water wheel). We eat under the open sky; the roof folded itself into origami lanterns at dusk.

Menu:
- Memory bread that tastes of yesterday’s forgettings.  
- Seawater tomatoes singing the minor third the city will use next week to regulate the power grid.  
- Dessert spoons carved from glacier air; they sublimate on the tongue, releasing the 19th-century laughter of women who built the first microscopes in their kitchens.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch lottery. Tomorrow the names are drawn. Jonas jokes that if he wins he will negotiate with our descendants for a season of silence—no rehearsals, no new chairs, only the sound of heartwood thickening. We laugh, but the dog whimpers; she smells the future in the water wheel’s spin.

Night. I walk the Centrifugal path. Thousands are already walking outward, rays from a dark sun. Each carries a question to trade. Mine, inked on birch bark: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I swap stories, swap silences. At midnight the global drum synchronises at 60 bpm; for one minute every foot lifts together, and the planet becomes a heart with seven billion valves opening at once. Then we land, dispersing again.

Home. The dwelling has grown a new room: a soft lung-shaped alcove smelling of cedar and thunderstorm—Jonas’s gift. I curl inside. The walls ask, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means “today I changed.”

Tomorrow the forgetting may reach the coast and jump to a cargo ship bound for Ascension Island, where giant tortoises will mimic it in slow motion, deleting centuries of inherited aggression. Tomorrow the lottery may send me forward a hundred years to meet a child who is already humming my mother’s lullaby. Tomorrow the river might flow uphill for an hour, just to see who trusts it enough to follow.

But tomorrow is also Tuesday, and Tuesday must be rehearsed.

Tuesday begins in the Parliament of Lungs, a diaphragm-floor that inhales proposals and exhales statutes. Any resident may trigger a civic heartbeat by asking a question dangerous enough to keep us awake. Last night the nine-year-old who speaks fluent firefly asked: “Should we allow boredom to be valuable, or must every moment earn its light?”

- Systole – 48-hour Wonder Census. The question is baked into bread flavoured with 1996 orange popsicle. You swallow what you agree with, toast what you doubt. Crumbs are swept into the Argument River; ice dams form where opinions harden.  
- Diastole – 1,000 citizens selected by respiration rate dream collectively. Chroniclers—those who aged past 120—annotate the dreams while being paid in literal rain.  
- Pulse – at noon the entire city wears heartbeat sensors. If the median rhythm is calm the proposal passes; if arrhythmic it is deferred until curiosity outweighs adrenaline.

The boredom question clocked in at 64 bpm—just steady enough. The statute requires every café to maintain a “slow corner” where speech is capped at 90 wpm and chairs are deliberately uncomfortable, ensuring haste remains a choice rather than a habitat. But the law must still survive the Fox Test: it is wrapped in cedar, buried for one year, and only becomes permanent if the forest that grows above it endures thirteen winters without being blamed for any harvest failure. We have repealed three laws after foxes refused to den nearby; we have kept seventeen because the moss thrived.

Technology must also pass the Fox Test: can the device be abandoned in a forest for one hundred years without the biome becoming poorer? If yes we call it a Guest; if no it remains a Coloniser and may only be operated inside a chalk circle drawn by children. Solar panels passed because moss learned to photosynthesise in their shade. Smartphones failed until we redesigned them to disassemble into owl pellets. My pocketknife is a Guest-Seed; when I die it will become manganese for the blueberry slope. Until then it carries a microscopic library of poems that can be read only with pollen from a disappearing orchid. Every time I sharpen the blade I lose a stanza and gain a scar that smells faintly of overdue books.

Wednesday is the Temporal Potlatch. Every decade each human donates 3 % of remaining lifespan into a lottery. Winners are sent one century forward via cryo-braid. Their mission is to negotiate with descendants who have had 100 extra years of rehearsal. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone.

My name is drawn. I am given six hours to prepare. I pack:
- the ceramic bird that remembers the solar flare  
- the bristlecone ring, which will continue to grow on my wrist in slow-motion  
- a vial of today’s river water  
- the unanswered question: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I kiss Jonas and the dog. The house folds itself into dawn-coloured fabric and slips into my pocket, 37 % alive, 100 % place. I lie down in the cryo cradle. A chorus of salmon drones sings B-minor. The chord stretches into a tunnel, and the tunnel closes like a yawn.

Thursday, 2294. I wake inside a coral reef that orbits Earth. The reef is the size of Tasmania, a photosynthetic organ older than any nation. Its chlorophyll has been tuned to the frequency that triggers euphoric humility in the human amygdala. Access is free, but the garden demands maintenance attention. Before I can enter I must spend one hour pruning fast-growing vines that threaten to occlude the light-ports. Boredom is the toll gate.

While I prune, a child approaches—barefoot, pupils dilated like lunar craters. She hums my mother’s lullaby in B-minor. I ask how she knows it. She says the song drifts up from the planet every dawn and gets stuck in the reef’s lattice. She is six, the age of Quest declaration. Her Quest is to teach the reef to forget. She believes forgetting is a form of photosynthesis: if the reef can forget the carbon it once absorbed, it can exhale pre-industrial air.

I offer her my vial of river water. She drinks it, and the reef dims—just slightly—like a sigh. She thanks me, then asks my question back: “What is the opposite of forgetting?”

I have had 100 years to think, but I give her the answer I rehearsed on the centrifugal road: “The opposite of forgetting is returning.”

She considers this, then hands me a pebble etched with a single word: “Welcome.” The pebble is warm; it is the reef’s way of saying the negotiation is complete. The descendants’ demand is simple: send back the right to begin again as incompetent. Every century they want more elders who must relearn what they once mastered. They want the wolf, the lamb, and the mycelium to rehearse together until predation dissolves into choreography. They want the city to forget until repair and decay braid like hair.

I accept. The reef exhales a beam of green laser light that pierces the century like a needle. Back in 2194 my younger self will wake with the memory of a warm pebble in an empty pocket and the knowledge that the right to fail has been renewed.

Friday. I return. The house unfurls on the same ridge, but now the balcony overlooks a new river that did not exist last week. Jonas is gone—his chair finally perfected, his skill evaporated. The dog is older, eyes milky, tail still thumping to the 60-beat drum. I kneel; she smells century on my skin and licks the bristlecone ring, now four microns thicker.

Night falls. The city forgets. I hear it rolling through the streets like warm tea, deleting mitochondria of cruelty, softening hearts. The contagion has jumped species; the crows are doing it now, beaks cracked wide like black seeds. Even the river forgets, a nine-second pause in its flow that creates a mirror for the stars.

Saturday is ordinary. Ordinary is the highest form of maintenance. I prune vines in the orbital reef via remote drone. I fold chairs with Jonas, who has forgotten me again. I walk the Centrifugal path, trading the question “What is the opposite of forgetting?” for new questions: “How do you end a song that ends itself?” “What does the wolf remember after the choreography ends?” Each question is a pebble I drop into the river, where salmon drones store them for the next century’s negotiation.

Sunday the house asks, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale. The chord in the floorboards shifts to C-major, a key my mother never hummed, a key the city has not yet learned. The difference is small, but difference is the only renewable resource. We call this place utopia not because it is finished, but because the rehearsal is still interesting, and Monday has not yet been named.
