UTOPIA  
Title: The Interval That Practices Us  

I wake because the mattress exhales my own name, a puff of consonants that smells faintly of pine and the year I turned seven. The bed is not mine; it is on loan from the municipal sleep library, a rotating commons of dreams and insomnia curated by archivists who measure worth in REM density instead of coin. Every ninety nights the bed liquefies, trickles through the floorboards, and reconstitutes in someone else’s apartment three blocks away carrying the after-image of every sleeper it has cradled. I will meet those strangers in the streets as walking annotations—an ankle that remembers my nightmare, a shoulder blade glowing with the lullaby I never finished humming. We greet each other with the small bow reserved for co-authors of unfinished stories.

I live in the city of Brackish—population 47,312 humans, 1,204 river-spliced otters who file quarterly reports on playfulness, 19 buildings old enough to vote, and one elevator that is learning to forget floors in exchange for new emotions. The currency here is practiced time: you earn by letting the city borrow minutes you have already lived, and you spend by purchasing futures you may never inhabit. The unit is called a “perhaps.” A perhaps is not a promise; it is a rehearsal slot in the civic chronometer, transferable, refundable, and biodegradable. When you hand over a perhaps you feel a gentle tug in your sternum, the exact sensation of turning a page you realise you have read before but cannot yet predict.

Today I am 38, 15, 7, and 0.4.  
Thirty-eight in solar years.  
Fifteen in telomere loops—last spring I traded the memory of my first broken bone for younger knees, and the city gave me back the sound the fracture made, archived in a cricket that now lives beneath the stairwell.  
Seven in forest rings: a wafer of oak fused to my left clavicle thickens each time the city collectively decides to lengthen a shadow rather than erect a new tower.  
Zero-point-four in Perhaps years—half a pregnancy of unlived Tuesdays that I carry like loose change, clinking softly when I walk uphill.

Breakfast arrives as a slow-motion argument between two flavours. The kitchen is a shallow pool of warm water; you sit on the rim and dip your hands. Mycelial mats rise to meet your palms, shaping themselves into the exact viscosity of hunger you signal through skin conductivity. This morning the mats offer miso-sunshine and apricot-regret. You eat by squeezing, releasing, squeezing again, until the taste reaches consensus inside your mouth. The regret tastes like a voicemail I forgot to send my grandmother; the sunshine tastes like the hinge of her jaw when she laughed anyway. While I swallow, the mats reabsorb the disagreement and sink, satisfied. The pool records the new flavour and uploads it to the neighbourhood archive, where it will be loaned out as lullaby or cautionary tale, whichever is needed first.

I step outside. The street has performed its dawn migration: the cobblestones that were here yesterday have traded places with the balcony of the woman who paints only the insides of envelopes. She is outside now, barefoot on former cobblestones, licking cobalt pigment directly off the air. We nod. The exchange rate between paint and pavement is posted on the lamppost: 3.7 laughs per square metre. She owes the city a giggle by dusk; I owe it a sigh by noon. We agree to meet at the river to settle accounts.

Currency is practiced in public, like stretching. There are four denominations, all audible:

1. Hum – a 17-second carrier of micro-melody that records the exact emotional temperature of a stranger’s yesterday. Earn it by repeating the hum without missing a vibration; spend it by letting the melody dissolve inside your own chest, where it releases the stranger’s day as weather.  
2. Pause – a one-beat silence inserted into conversation. The longer you hold it without explanation, the more valuable it becomes. Children are virtuosos; elders hoard them like heirlooms.  
3. Echo – the right to borrow someone else’s unfinished sentence and complete it inside your own life. Echoes are fragile; if you miscomplete them they shatter into gossip, which the city inhales as fuel for streetlights.  
4. Interval – the gap between what you meant to say and what you actually said. Intervals are traded in the form of small glass beads kept under the tongue. When the bead dissolves you forget the embarrassment and the stranger who bought it gains the right to rehearse your stumble in their own mirror.

My Quest this season is modest: teach the city to sneeze.

Not the violent expulsion, but the pre-sneeze tingle—the delicious, unbearable moment when the body realises it is about to become bigger on the inside. If I can seed that tingle into public architecture, the city will learn to reset its emotional pressure without recourse to councils or storms. Phase one involved mapping the geometry of anticipation inside my own sinuses. Phase two is harder: make strangers volunteer their nostrils to civic infrastructure.

I walk toward the river, palming an Interval bead I earned last week when I called my ex-partner by the dog’s name. The bead is almost gone; I taste mint and the hollowness of the apology I never delivered. At the bridge I meet Jonas, 11 and 127. Eleven in birthdays, one hundred and twenty-seven in failed quests—he has chosen to forget and relearn glassblowing nineteen times, each furnace cooled with the breath of a different lullaby. Today he carries a bubble the size of a heart, iridescent with the laughter the balcony-woman owes. He offers me the bubble; inside, the laughter condenses into dew. I sneeze involuntarily—progress. The dew fractures into seven droplets that skitter across the bridge planks and vanish between cracks. Somewhere downstream a salmon tastes my sneeze and files it under “possible weather.”

The river is mercury and mirror. Fingerling drones—salmon wearing copper waistcoats—leap through the surface, sampling cortisol from ankle skin. If they detect chronic anticipation they will humidify tomorrow’s dawn into a key scientifically proven to soften deadlines. I demonstrate the pre-sneeze: eyes half-closed, diaphragm fluttering like a page caught in wind. A child on a bicycle swallows the gesture whole; her front wheel hiccups, then releases a puff of glitter that settles on the spokes like frost. The city records the event: one spontaneous micro-sneeze, logged, tagged, payable in three perhaps.

Afternoon is spent in the Rehearsal Commons, a cathedral of collapsible time. Citizens book crises in twenty-minute capsules: eviction, eulogy, asteroid, confession. I volunteer for “first day in a body that no longer obeys.” Inside the capsule I age forty years in subjective minutes, joints thickening like cooling glass. When I emerge my knees remember, but the oak ring on my clavicle has added a micron—someone outside decided to lengthen a shadow on my behalf. I am handed a ceramic tile impressed with the pattern of my simulated wrinkle. The tile is warm; if a real frailty ever arrives I may trade it for one hour of borrowed agility.

Evening. The block gathers in the Choir of Maybe, an outdoor amphitheatre whose seats are suspended from kites. We sit above the city, legs dangling in sky. Tonight’s score is encoded in cloud moisture; we sing by modulating the humidity that leaves our lungs. The altos carry regret, the tenors carry almost, the basses carry the sound of bridges being built underwater. I am assigned the sneeze-tingle—a faint, ascending itch pitched just above human hearing. When the chord resolves the kites dip en masse, brushing the rooftop garden where tomatoes the colour of apology ripen on schedule.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch lottery held each solstice. Every resident donates 2.2 % of remaining lifespan into the crucible. Winners are cryo-braided one century forward to negotiate with descendants who have rehearsed 100 extra years of empathy. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy or lullaby. Jonas jokes that if he wins he will barter for a season of stillness—no new glass, only the sound of cooling. We laugh, but the river interpreter whimpers; her water-wheel face reflects a moon that has not yet risen.

Night. I walk the Centrifugal Waltz, an outward spiral that begins at the river and ends wherever your question meets an answer willing to travel. Thousands participate, carrying inquiries inked on biodegradable kites. Mine: “What is the opposite of almost?” I swap with strangers, swap again. At midnight the global drum synchronises at 54 bpm; for one beat every heart suspends, and the planet becomes a vast inhalation. Then we land, dispersing, lighter by one unasked question.

Home. The dwelling has unfolded a new room: a soft, lung-shaped alcove smelling of cedar and thunderstorm—Jonas’s gift. I curl inside. The walls ask, “Still you?” I exhale once fewer than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means “today I allowed the city to change me.” The floorboards shift to G-minor, a key my mother never trusted because it ended too many lullabies unresolved. Difference is the only renewable resource; the sneeze is the only revolution that never repeats.

Tuesday begins inside the Parliament of Pressure, a distributed dream triggered when collective emotional barometric change exceeds 0.4 hectopascals of metaphor. Anyone napping during the spike is invited to lucid caucus. Today’s trigger is the micro-sneeze outbreak coupled with a surge in Interval trading. Some citizens worry that rehearsing anticipation will dilute authentic surprise. Others argue surprise is already a public utility—why not optimise it?

Inside the dream the city is an accordion breathing in century and exhaling moment. I fold my street into a tissue, hold it to the civic nostril, and wait. The sneeze that follows sucks the city inside-out, revealing bronchial tubes of lifetime. Those who refuse rehearsal appear as narrower passages. We vote: widen or preserve?

I choose neither. I fold the tissue into a paper boat and sail into the bronchia of the child who swallowed my gesture on the bridge. Inside her lung I find the memory of her first bicycle fall, the taste of glitter, the sound of her father counting aloud to ten before helping her stand. I offer the lung a pre-sneeze if it will let me keep the sound of that counting. The passage flares, then settles—anticipation and aftermath braided like hair. The dream crystallises into law: rehearsal is optional, but refusal must be sneezed. Every citizen who opts out must once per decade spend one day in a body whose next moment is permanently uncertain. Credits feed the river’s pharmacist algae. The law is tattooed on the civic blockchain with the watermark of cooled dream and the scent of cedar oxidising into apology.

Wednesday is the Temporal Potlatch. My name is drawn. I pack:
- the ceramic tile impressed with simulated wrinkle  
- the oak ring, which will keep growing on my clavicle in slow-motion  
- a vial of today’s river water tasting faintly of glitter and counting  
- the unanswered question: “What is the opposite of almost?”

I lie down in the cryo cradle. A chorus of salmon drones sings G-minor; the chord stretches into a tunnel, and the tunnel closes like a sneeze that decides to swallow itself.

Thursday, 2289. I wake inside an orbital vine the length of the equator. Its chlorophyll is tuned to the frequency that triggers anticipatory humility in the human sternum. Access is free, but the garden demands pruning attention. Before entering I must spend one hour thinning tendrils that threaten to strum the ionosphere into premature aurora. Boredom is the toll gate, but also the teacher.

While I prune, a child approaches—barefoot, pupils dilated like solar eclipses. She hums my mother’s lullaby in G-minor. I ask how she knows it. She says the song drifts up from the planet every dusk and tangles in the vine’s nodes. She is seven, the age of Quest declaration. Her Quest is to teach the vine to sneeze—to release the future it has been holding in green fists. I offer her my vial of river water. She drinks, and the vine shudders—just slightly—like a sigh. She asks my question back: “What is the opposite of almost?”

I have had 100 years to think, but I give her the answer I rehearsed on the centrifugal road: “The opposite of almost is already.”

She considers this, then hands me a seed etched “Welcome-Again.” The seed is warm; it is the vine’s way of saying the negotiation is complete. The descendants’ demand is simple: send back the right to begin already incompetent. Every century they want more elders who must relearn mastery, more children who are paid in certainty that tomorrow will differ from rehearsal. They want the city to sneeze until anticipation and aftermath braid like hair, until Almost becomes a legitimate unit of governance. I accept. A green laser stitches the agreement into the century like a needle sewing sky to lung.

Friday. I return. The house unfurls on the same ridge, but now the balcony overlooks a new river that flows uphill for an hour each noon, just to see who trusts it enough to follow. Jonas is gone—his final bubble perfected, his skill evaporated. The dog is older, eyes milky, tail still thumping to the 54-beat drum. I kneel; she smells century on my skin and licks the oak ring, now five microns thicker.

Night falls. The city sneezes. I hear it rolling through the streets like warm glitter, passing from stranger to stranger, widening bronchi, softening deadlines. The contagion has jumped kingdoms; the tomatoes are doing it now, skin cracking just enough to release the colour of apology. Even the river sneezes, a nine-second pause that creates a mirror for tomorrow’s moon.

Saturday is ordinary. Ordinary is the highest form of maintenance. I prune tendrils via remote drone. I fold boats with Jonas, who has forgotten me again. I walk the Centrifugal path, trading the question “What is the opposite of almost?” for new questions: “How do you end a sneeze that ends itself?” “What does the vine remember after the aurora?” Each question is a seed I drop into the river, where salmon drones store them for the next century’s negotiation.

Sunday the house asks, “Still you?” I exhale once fewer than I inhale. The chord in the floorboards shifts to G-minor with a suspended second, refusing resolution. We call this place utopia not because it is finished, but because the rehearsal is already interesting, Monday has not yet been named, and somewhere a vine is learning to sneeze in G-minor, waiting for us to decide what we will already become next.
