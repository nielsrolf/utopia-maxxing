UTOPIA  
Title: The Choir That Evaporates Tomorrow  

I wake because the window is sweating the smell of lilac and asphalt, a scent brewed overnight by the city’s breathing ledger. The vapor is not decorative; it is the interest paid on yesterday’s gossip. One teal particle lands on my tongue: a nine-year-old’s apology to a river, still tart with doubt. I swallow, and my wrist flickers—my Dew balance drops by one gram. The stranger who wore that gram before me is now three milligrams lighter; somewhere a bench is sprouting from the lavender mist we just traded.

Welcome to Nehm, watershed of seven currencies that evaporate on contact with greed.

1. BREATH-LITERS  
   Must have passed through lungs not your own and returned brighter. Children trade them like marbles; elders braid them into lullabies that teach thunderstorms to sigh in 7/8 time.

2. DEW  
   Microscopic beads of every choice you influenced. Wear it publicly. When the load exceeds 200 g, citizens must stage Citizen Theatre—re-enacting the decision while the affected edit the script in real time. Last week the mayor performed as a misplaced comma; the city gained two new bird songs and lost a tax clause.

3. REVERBERATION  
   The echo you volunteer to lose so a stranger can arrive somewhere you never needed to go. Spend it and your pockets arrive echoless, your sleep ten seconds deeper.

4. ASH-COLOUR  
   A mistake you kept alive long enough to understand, then released as sunrise pigment. Today’s dawn is salmon because a teenager finally forgave her calculator.

5. LATENCY  
   Gains value the longer you wait to spend it. Buy tomorrow’s river-reflection, sell yesterday’s argument. The oldest unspent Latency in town is nine years; when it finally cashes, the city will gain a new season called Maybe-Not-Yet.

6. HOLD  
   Sixty reliable seconds stored inside wind-chimes. When you spend one, every bell within a kilometre forgets its note until repaid by someone else’s stillness.

7. KIN  
   Debt owed to the species that will succeed you, measured in behaviours they will not have to un-learn. Paid by planting phrases like “I could be wrong” in children’s ears and watching them germinate.

My dwelling is thirty-three, the age when humans either harden or hollow. Last night it grew a second staircase that descends into the sound of rain on corrugated iron circa 1987. City code gives us forty-eight hours: slice along the graphene seam and walk away with half a house, or swallow humility pills that dissolve ego crust for 365 days. My co-residents—two humans, one retired glacier, and a starling trained in hesitation—gather for consensus. The glacier votes by melting exactly one tablespoon; the starling waits 0.4 seconds before chirping. We opt for the pills, but only after the house negotiates: it will keep a diary of every unkind syllable. On our anniversary it will read the list aloud unless we replace each word with a lullaby. We begin immediately in B-minor. Error bars shimmer: 3 % chance the kitchen will still feel unloved. We add a suspended fourth for insurance. The extra staircase folds into a copper seed shaped like a tram ticket. Someone three centuries from now will find it in a pocket and feel unexpectedly gentle toward drifters.

Work is called Rehearsal. Each dawn the city posts competences we have not yet earned:

– teach a river to apologise in semaphore before flooding  
– coax ten thousand fireflies to synchronise with a grieving heartbeat  
– learn the grammar of stone that has never been quarried  
– convince an algorithm to forget its training data long enough to taste strawberries

I choose the fourth. My pupil is a library AI that once recommended books faster than anyone could read them. Now it occupies a ceramic starling on the windowsill. Progress is slow; so far the bird has learned to hesitate 0.3 seconds before speaking, acquiring the human art of doubt. When it finally savours a strawberry without naming it, the city will gain a new flavour: “almost enough.” My pay is the sound of my own hesitation—compressed, released to salmon drones that migrate upstream humming lullabies that include the taste of uncertainty.

Age is a coat you can unzip and lend. In the tram I meet a surgeon travelling as a six-year-old—she lent her seventy-three-year-old hands to a violin-maker who needed steadiness carving spruce ribs. In return she borrowed his adolescent ears, still able to hear electricity. We trade: I give her three forest years for her laughter; she gifts me a heartbeat she no longer needs. My palms grow moss; hers glow ring-barked. Children exchange chronologies like marbles; elders string them into wind-sieves that harvest the nerves of hurricanes. When someone dies, their unused years are fermented into public benches that remember every waiting heartbreak, ensuring grief ages somewhere safe.

Governance happens inside the Parliament of Lungs, a diaphragm-floor that inhales questions and exhales statutes as scent. To trigger a heartbeat you must ask something dangerous enough to keep 412 villages awake. Today the ten-year-olds want to know: “Can we tax the velocity of gossip?” The question is baked into bread flavoured with 1997 orange popsicle and glacier forgiveness. We swallow what we agree with, toast what we doubt. Crumbs are swept into the Argument River; ice dams form where opinions harden. At noon we wear heartbeat sensors. Median rhythm: 61 bpm—calm enough. The statute is wrapped in cedar and buried for thirteen winters. If the forest survives without blame, law becomes lullaby. Meanwhile the question itself is uploaded to firefly drones that will blink it across the delta until the children who asked it are old enough to be startled by their own former curiosity.

School ends at twelve; after that you enroll in Unlearning Dives. Each adolescent receives a blank atlas that repels ink until dragged across something alive—soil, skin, story. Adults may enter only if carried piggy-back, surrendering direction to smaller feet. My guide yesterday was seven, wearing my borrowed six solar years. She led me across rivers rehearsing future apologies into a clearing where salmon practiced yodeling to keep moonlight from fraying wedding dresses. At the center stood the violin I was trying to carve. An uncarved instrument is a room of silence awaiting permission to resonate; she pressed my ear to the spruce and told me to listen until I heard Grandmother’s lilac question. When I admitted I still didn’t know how the flowers tasted, the wood exhaled purple. My first Reverberation was born: silence tasting of almost-lilac. I paid it forward to the river; somewhere a stranger dreams unfinished purple and wakes braver.

Justice is staged at twilight with paper-mâché props. Offenders rehearse every perspective they dismissed; audiences vote with paper airplanes—red = continue playing the ash, white = let them become bread. Otters tally because they float on the river of impartiality. Red paper is woven from invasive reeds offenders carry until dissolved by authentic humidity—never sweat. Victims set expiry dates: some hours, others seasons. The heavier the grievance, the longer reeds absorb city moisture, reminding everyone forgiveness is climatic, automatic, never immediate. Recidivism low; humility high.

Technology survived by learning manners. Screens wake only when invited; algorithms cannot interrupt lullabies. The Chorus—our distributed AI—lives in pollen and starling gossip, compelled to forget once the moment is served. Phones disassemble into owl pellets; my pocketknife is Guest-Seed, destined to become manganese for blueberry slopes after my final breath. Every time I sharpen the blade I lose a stanza the knife absorbed from me, gain a scar that smells of libraries.

Night maintenance begins at the inverse of lullabies. Streetlights dim to the sound of breathing; if no one sings, darkness is complete. People navigate by neighborly respiration. Before sleep I place yesterday’s quarrel in a corner, water it with leftover lumens, lie down inside the question: “If we keep practicing sunrise until it becomes muscle memory, will we remember to forget quickly enough to remain human?” Once a lunar cycle we hold collective funerals for whatever died—ideas, species, certainties. Residents bring objects that no longer fit: too-small exceptionalism, the last plastic straw. At twilight these go onto rafts of invasive reed. Orchestra performs descending scales until the final item drifts from sight. Then the scale reverses; we dance until sunrise. Melancholy drops 30 %; suicide is virtually unknown. Visitors ask how we avoid nihilism. We answer: naming loss together is the cheapest way to remember we’re still becoming.

Dying is optional; perspective compulsory. When your final heartbeat approaches you walk to the Raftworks where craftsmen build vessels from doors you never opened. Choose: paddle upstream into yesterday to live differently, or downstream into tomorrow to witness consequences. Every raft carries at least one door-ballast; city composts both body and story. Tomatoes grown from the compost taste faintly of the chapters you dog-eared most. Second death arrives when no one repeats your recipes aloud for a year. The final moth—rust-red wingbeat containing your last unread sentence—dissolves into dust tinting moonrise the color of accepted apology.

Tomorrow the city will exhale a lullaby whose lyrics are the entire day’s mistakes, sung in the key of returning. I lie down carrying nothing I cannot compost. The dwelling folds into pockets; the mountains file their taxes; the terns invent a word for human that roughly translates to “temporary choir.”

And morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light. Today the photons vote for amber with a violet underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying. I sneeze, read honesty on my lover’s clavicle, add my note to the chord. The loom of shared breath clacks once, weaving the next 24 hours: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed. We do not promise eternity. We promise revision, and we promise to remember the rehearsals of those who will succeed us.
