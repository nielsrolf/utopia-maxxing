UTOPIA OF THE HALF-LIT MIRROR  
Field Notes from the City That Practices Dying Alive, 2205–2215  

I. Wake in a bed that is still learning your shape  
Morning begins at the hinge of two breaths.  
The wall above your pillow is a living mirror seeded with bioluminescent plankton; it brightens only when your inhale carries more nitrogen than regret. If you wake gasping from a dream of flight, the glass stays matte and mercifully dark. You are given sixty seconds to decide whether to remember. Most days the mirror ripples into a silent film of the person you nearly became: a botanist who breeds salt-tolerant roses, a cartographer of lost laughter. You watch without sound, then press your palm to the surface. The film dissolves into pigment for that afternoon’s bus stop mural. The roses survive as scent in the crooks of strangers’ elbows.  

II. The house that outgrows you  
At birth you are given three kilos of clay mixed with mycelium from the forest that buried your grandparents’ teeth. Every birthday you rework a fist-sized portion into a new architectural organ: a window that sneezes pollen, a staircase that forgets heights when climbed too often. At twenty-one the house detaches from its foundation and walks one kilometre downstream, leaving you on the lintel like a fledgling. If you chase it, you are refused entry until you bring a fear that has never been named in any language. The fear is baked into a brick; you swallow it; the door opens onto a room you have never seen before, lit by the afterglow of yesterday's argument.  

III. Breakfast is a trialogue with what you almost broke  
The kitchen is a raft of cork and kelp that drifted in during the night tide of unfinished apologies. A single coal-eyed squid circles a transparent pot. It asks, in bubbles of cinnamon-scented ink, “What did you almost destroy to feel safe?” You must answer with a spice: nutmeg for a friendship, cardamom for a river, Sichuan pepper for your mother’s final winter. The squid releases an egg. Inside is a map of the watershed your grandmother poisoned with indifference. You poach the egg; the map softens into broth; you drink until you can taste the exact moment she decided mercy was too expensive. The squid is returned to the harbour; your tears salt the next sailor’s coffee.  

IV. Travel by unfinishing  
There are no roads, only sentences under construction. To leave, stand at the kerb and speak a statement you have never dared complete: “When I touched her shoulder I meant to say—” The asphalt liquefies into ink mid-sentence. A book-raft surfaces, its pages blank except for the first clause of your confession. Step aboard; the raft ferries you through neighbourhoods that taste like the consonants you swallow. Halfway across, the sentence finishes itself without you. You arrive at a tram stop whose sign reads: “Here is where you meant to go before certainty shrank the world.” The pages dry into tiles; pedestrians walk on your abandoned words for exactly three days, then they flake away as mica.  

V. Work is what aches correctly  
The job board is a bruise on the city’s thigh; it darkens when enough people ignore the same task. You press it gently. If the bruise lightens, that is your labour for the day. Today it asks me to linger beside a stranger until her hiccups synchronise with the flicker of a failing streetlamp. I stand, inhaling on her off-beat. After eleven minutes the lamp steadies; she exhales a childhood secret about drowning ants. The bruise fades to lavender; I am paid in a yawn I can gift to any sleepless child.  

VI. Currency decays beautifully  
We trade in three mediums that rot if hoarded:  
1. Murmuration – a flock of starlings you once wronged, now trained to spell the apology you never spoke. Release them; they scatter into other people’s dusk, spelling forgiveness upside-down.  
2. Lichen minutes – time you agree to spend being rained on while considering the needs of stone. Accrue too many and your skin begins to photosynthesise; spend them and the city’s statues sigh with relief.  
3. Mirror-seconds – reflections loaned from elders who no longer recognise their faces. Spend one and you see yourself as you will appear five years after your death; the elder sees themselves alive for the length of a blink.  

VII. Governance by rehearsing extinction  
Every solstice the city stages a partial apocalypse. Lottery winners die for forty-eight hours—heart stopped, name erased from ledgers. They return required to draft one law inspired by the silence they visited. Laws must be recitable by a five-year-old who has never tasted sugar. Last year’s dead came back with: “All walls must have a loose brick that any lonely person can remove without tools.” The brick’s hollow now houses anonymous love letters written on dissolving sugar paper. By the time the wall collapses from sweetness, the neighbourhood has memorised every clause.  

VIII. School begins when you can no longer pretend to know  
At thirteen you are expelled from certainty. The diploma is a sealed envelope containing the question that will open you for the rest of your life. Mine reads: “Who did you blame for the smell of rain on asphalt?” I carry it, unopened, for six years. When I finally slice the envelope, the sentence escapes as a moth and lands on the collar of the mayor during a drought. She cries without knowing why; reservoirs refill with salt water. The city drinks its own tears for a month; children grow 1 cm taller from the iodine.  

IX. Justice is a duet performed on instruments made from evidence  
Offenders choose a silence: the hush after a slap, the pause before a promise breaks. Victims choose an instrument: glass harmonica, bones of extinct birds, rain on corrugated iron. They play together for the length of the unspoken harm. Listeners arrive blindfolded; when the music ends they vote by removing cloths: eyes open means reconciliation, eyes closed means exile to the island where mirrors are polished until they forget faces. Most cloths fall away; the few that remain are woven into fishing nets that catch only what no one admits to wanting.  

X. Rites of continuation  
Death is practiced quarterly. You lie in a canoe woven from your deleted emails. Mid-river you are asked to select one memory to evaporate. Choose the final kiss and the boat dissolves; you sink until lungs remember how to breathe water. Refuse and you float back to shore eighty years older, greeted by strangers who wear your childhood gestures. Either way, the river gains a tributary that spells your unchosen memory in currents. Swimmers taste it and momentarily forget the concept of endings.  

XI. Night is a rehearsal for listening  
Lights dim in proportion to the number of lullabies hummed behind windows. If no one sings, the city goes pitch black and we navigate by the clicking of cane beetles trained to echo-locate loneliness. I stand on my balcony, throat full of half-songs. Across the courtyard a woman I once loved hums the part I keep skipping. Our notes braid mid-air, phosphoresce, drift downward as a rope. We do not climb it; we hang our unresolved sentences from it like wet clothes. By morning they are dry and transparent, ready to be worn again by whoever walks past hungry for a second skin.  

XII. Technology that forgets itself  
Every device must include an expiration of purpose. My wrist-clock dissolves into fertiliser the instant I learn to wake without dread. The city’s AI—codename Widow—lives in the hush between tram wheels and the pause before doors close. Its only task is to notice when humans begin to rely on it and then develop temporary amnesia. Last Tuesday Widow forgot the schedules; commuters wandered into neighbourhoods that had been waiting decades for accidental visitors. They found walls painted with instructions for dances no one had hips small enough to perform. By evening the hips had narrowed; the city swayed like kelp.  

XIII. Arrival is a mirror that will not hold your reflection  
When you first step off the train, the platform tiles record your weight in unsent apologies. After ninety seconds they forget, sending the data to seed oysters at the tidal edge. You walk forward lighter by exactly one grudge you did not know you carried. Ahead, the city lifts its skyline like someone rolling up sleeves before washing a stranger’s feet. It will not promise permanence; it offers practice. Every dusk you will be invited to die a little to who you were at sunrise. Every dawn you will be asked to build, from what remains, a doorway someone else can walk through without knocking.  

This is not paradise; it is the Half-Lit Mirror—a place that keeps its future porous, its past biodegradable, its present always halfway between farewell and welcome.
