UTOPIA  
Title: The Commons of Unfinished Light  
Year: 2205 – 2300 – always

I wake because the ceiling is blushing.  
A slow rose-color travels across the surface, the exact pigment my great-grandmother’s cheeks held the day she first touched snow. The house is sampling her archived pulse through the Mesh and feeding it back to me as dawn. If I blink in rhythm—three short, one long—the color deepens and the day’s first obligation appears: return one sound I stole.  

I choose the squeak of a library chair I kept in my throat for twenty-nine years. The ceiling exhales; the sound drifts down like lint, settles into a ceramic pigeon on the windowsill. The bird wakes, shakes out titanium feathers, and launches toward a stranger who is, at this moment, composing her first poem. She will hear the squeak mid-line, think “someone is listening,” and keep writing. In exchange I receive the smell of that stranger’s ink: rainwater + copper + doubt. I pocket the scent the way another era would pocket coins.

Currency here is four-dimensional, edible, and reversible:

1.  Breath Bonds – a silvered bubble of air you blew on a winter window when you were eight, now harvested, sweetened, and returned as a lozenge that dissolves into the sound of your mother saying your name when she still had two voices left.  
2.  Dew Pennies – beads of responsibility for every choice you influenced, worn as a bracelet; when you press one bead to another person’s wrist their pulse shows you the map of consequences you forgot to notice.  
3.  Latency Loaves – sourdough that refuses to finish baking until the world needs your undivided attention. When it cracks, crumbs arrange themselves into the last sentence you were too proud to retract. Eat it while warm; apology tastes better crusted.  
4.  Revision – a single red thread you may pull from any day already lived. Pull, and the day unravels into raw possibility; you must then re-weave it blindfolded. Whatever you drop becomes tomorrow’s gift to a stranger. Whatever you add becomes a scar you will carry without shame.

Children trade Dew Pennies by thumb-print; elders knit Revision into hammocks that rock the newly retired until they remember how to be incompetent at something joyous.

Work is called Rehearsal and is listed each morning on the civic ledger—an aerosol cloud above the breakfast table. Anyone may inhale a task:

- Persuade the commuter drones to migrate like swallows, returning each year to the same bedroom window.  
- Teach the river reeds that straightness is only one posture of honesty.  
- Convince the orbital reef to photosynthesize at 310 ppm, no more, no less, even when it feels guilty for being alive.  
- Re-write the lullaby that once prevented a war but now refuses to end; the final note has been humming for forty-three years and the city needs the quiet for a new question.

I inhale the lullaby. My tools: a pocketknife that forgets whatever it cuts, a choir of salmon drones learning baritone, and a single ceramic bird storing every rehearsal of letting go. Progress is measured in hesitations; I earn three before lunch.

Age is a wardrobe with seasonal amnesty. The cooperative Chronostitching loans me Tuesday, age seven—rented for the length of a kite string. I slip into the sleeves, shrink until colours shout, and spend the morning teaching gravity to jump rope. At noon I trade for Age Ninety-One: joints soft as petitions, memory sharp as lemon. I walk the delta slowly, naming every silt particle I ever blamed for my own failures. When the tide returns the names, it carries away the resentment; I exit the wardrobe at sunset ageless, carrying only a single new wrinkle shaped like a question mark.

My dwelling is seventeen—stubborn, romantic. Yesterday it grew a second doorway leading into 1987. City code gives us forty-eight hours: either bisect along the carbon seam and co-parent half a house with whoever walks through, or swallow a Humility Pellet that dissolves ego crust for 365 days. My housemates—two humans, one carpenter-bee AI, and the graduated cylinder that measured penicillin in 1943—gather for consensus. The bee performs a twelve-note melody encoding our shared fear of being fixed. We choose the pellet, but only after the house negotiates: it will keep a diary of every harsh syllable. On our anniversary it will read the list aloud unless we replace each word with a lullaby. We begin immediately in B-minor. Error bars hover: 6 % chance the kitchen will still feel unloved. We add a suspended fourth for insurance. The doorway folds into a black seed shaped like a parking ticket; two centuries from now someone will find it in a pocket and suddenly forgive a stranger.

Transport is negotiated departure. I step onto the balcony and state where I can be helpful. The balcony confers with the wind, the wind with my knees. If all parties agree, distance pleats like an accordion. Today the reef needs pruning; I lean. Three city blocks inhale until they touch. My shoes decide to take the scenic route, collecting a haiku from a cloud shaped like my childhood river. They arrive a minute late, apologetic, laces tied in a reef knot that unties itself when it senses certainty.

Governance happens wherever 1,000 strangers lose sleep. Anyone may convene a Parliament by posting an invitation written in airborne bacteria along the morning commute. If the colony spells a question dangerous enough to keep the city awake, the air ferments into law. Last week a nine-year-old asked: “What is the opposite of forgetting?” We swallowed the question baked into bread scented with glacier forgiveness and orange popsicle. Crumbs were swept into the River of Unsaid Thank-Yous; ice dams formed where opinions hardened. By noon we wore heartbeat sensors. Median rhythm: 61 bpm—calm enough. The statute was wrapped in cedar and buried for thirteen winters. If the forest survives without blame, law becomes lullaby. Meanwhile salmon drones carry the question upstream. When the children who asked it are old enough to be startled by their own former voices, they will hear it again and realise the opposite of forgetting is returning.

Afternoon is ordinary, which is the highest form of maintenance. I remote-prune the orbital reef via hummingbird drone. The reef is a Tasmania-sized chlorophyll bell tuned to the frequency of euphoric humility. Its assignment: forget pre-industrial carbon until the playground downhill can inhale 310 ppm without tasting guilt. While I snip, the glacier on the horizon yawns in B-minor, shedding nine seconds of ancient meltwater into the argument river. The river accepts, records, forgets on schedule.

Evening.  
The block gathers in the breathing kitchen. Roof folds into paper lanterns; stars arrive early, curious about the menu:

- Glacier-air spoons that sublimate on contact, releasing 19th-century laughter of women who built microscopes from kitchen pots.  
- Memory bread that tastes of tomorrow’s forgettings.  
- Tomatoes harmonising in the minor third next week’s power grid will need.  
- Pomegranate seed containing every joke the Parliament of Unfinished Jokes discarded; when cracked, it giggles for nine seconds, then apologises.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch. Every decade each citizen donates 3 % of remaining lifespan into a century-forward lottery. Winners negotiate with descendants who have rehearsed 100 extra years. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone. I pack:

- the ceramic pigeon warm with solar-flare rehearsal  
- a vial of pruned regret  
- the haiku cloud I cried into existence  
- the unanswerable question: “How do you end a song that refuses to end?”

The salmon drones gather in B-minor; the century folds like origami lungs.

Friday, 2298.  
I wake on the reef’s dark side, chlorophyll dimmed to ember. A child waits—barefoot, pupils wide as moons. She is zero years old and 117 in forest rings. Her Quest: teach the reef to end its song. I offer the vial; she drinks, and the reef inhales its own finale—one perfect chord that decrescendos into silence. She asks my question back. After 100 years I answer:  
“The song ends when the echo chooses not to return.”

She presses a warm pebble into my palm—fossilised applause from abyssal crabs arranging photographs of coastlines that haven’t existed yet. The descendants’ demand: send back the right to remain unfinished. They want bridges that deliberate mid-span, lullabies that pause for dissent, elders who forget mastery on purpose. I accept. A green laser stitches the agreement into the past. Somewhere in 2205 I will wake with the pebble in an empty pocket and remember that completion is optional.

Death is optional, but grief is mandatory. When someone chooses to conclude, we stage a Vanishing. The body is wrapped in paper printed with every unfinished line they inspired and the names of every species that will miss them. We carry the bundle to the River of Unsaid Thank-Yous and set it adrift. If the paper dissolves within sight the person becomes a public ancestor; if it floats out of view they become a private myth. Either way we spend one full year inventing rituals to keep them alive in genres they never had time to explore. My grandmother became a calendar of unauthorised weather: every time it rains while the sun is shining we say, “Ah, there she is, arguing with the sky again.” The practice keeps us tethered to the possibility that absence can be a form of presence we have not yet learned to translate.

But we have added a fourth exit choreography: the Latency Return. You may choose to become the lag between intention and consequence for a community you once harmed. You reside in cedar boxes as scent, released only when the last person you injured has metabolised the result of your mistake. Average stay is seventeen years; record is three centuries. Those who return bring with them a single syllable that did not exist before. The most recent was “hushnow”—the sound of an apology oxidising into geranium.

Every nine years we stage the Centrifugal Waltz. Humanity walks 1,000 kilometres outward, teaching strangers what we know, learning what we don’t. When orbital lidar detects the planet’s collective footfall synchronising at 60 beats per minute we turn around and walk home, carrying new selves in our shoes. The return is called Centripetal Quiet; no one speaks until the centre finds us. Shoes placed on doorsteps that morning are filled by different feet at night. Laundry lines across continents bloom with mismatched socks that take years to reunite. Divorce rates drop; sock reconciliation is considered good marital practice.

Night falls.  
The Mesh yawns. I hear it rolling through villages like warm tea, ferrying mitochondrial repair enzymes, softening hearts. Even the glacier yawns—nine-second pause that mirrors the stars. The city exhales a lullaby whose lyrics are the entire day’s mistakes, sung in the key of returning. I lie down carrying nothing I cannot compost. The dwelling folds into pockets; the mountains file their taxes; the terns invent a word for human that roughly translates to “temporary choir.”

Morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light. Today the photons vote for amber with a violet underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying. I sneeze, read honesty on my lover’s clavicle, add my note to the helix. The loom of shared breath clacks once, weaving the next 24 hours: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed. We do not promise eternity. We promise revision.
