UTOPIA C  
Title: The Republic of Gentle Becoming  

I wake because the wall beside my bed has begun to exhale the scent of a Tuesday I have not yet lived.  
The breath arrives at 6:43 a.m., cinnamon-bright, with a base note of river stone.  
It is not a fragrance chosen by algorithm or committee; it is the by-product of last night’s civic digestion.  
Under the street, a living pipe-line of mycelial copper has been feasting on the regrets we donated at dusk—little silvered pods the size of cherry pits, each one labelled with the syllable we most regret having spoken aloud.  
The fungus eats the metal husk, drinks the memory, and vents the transformed emotion through the pores of our dwellings.  
The city wakes smelling the future it has composted from its own embarrassments.  
No one is allowed to buy or sell this vapour; you can only breathe it in and recognise yourself already forgiven.

I live on the third floor of a house that is learning to sing.  
It began life seven years ago as a stack of printed cellulose mixed with the shredded minutes of a municipal budget debate and one teaspoon of my grandmother’s unfinished lullaby.  
Since then it has added two balconies, an attic that only appears when someone needs to hide from a question, and a kitchen wall that grows translucent at dinner time so the neighbours can see whether there is enough to share.  
Every solstice the house divides like a ripe fig.  
Residents vote by fermentation: we each add a pinch of salt if we want to keep the new room, a crushed peppercorn if we prefer to let it walk away.  
Last winter we released the sun-room.  
It folded itself into a paper coracle and drifted down the canal; a week later we saw a photograph of it perched on a hillside outside the city, sheltering shepherds from the rain.  
Thus we are never burdened by surplus space, and every house eventually becomes someone else’s answer.

Work begins when I open the window and admit the morning question.  
Today the breeze carries a strip of indigo silk on which is written:  
“Who will teach the river to apologise in semaphore before it floods?”  
I hold the cloth to my throat; the job adheres to my skin like warm wax.  
I am neither employee nor contractor; I am the temporary embodiment of a competence the watershed still lacks.  
My tools are a set of copper flags, a pocket-sized drone that translates heartbeat into light, and the patience to stand knee-deep in cold water until even the stones feel heard.  
Payment will arrive as a private season: four days of late-October light grafted onto early May, the exact shade my mother loved when she hung sheets on the line and believed the world could still be white.  
If I fail, the river will still flood, but the water will taste faintly of regret; if I succeed, the flood will come anyway, yet every drop will carry a glimmer of permission.  
Either way, the city will record the outcome in next year’s sunrise pigments—error and success both useful, both beautiful.

Currency here is eight-stranded, but we carry it in our mouths rather than our wallets.  
A Breath is one litre of air that has passed through four species and returns bearing a question you did not know you needed.  
A Hold is sixty reliable seconds stored inside wind-chimes; when you spend it, every bell within a kilometre forgets its note until repaid by someone else’s stillness.  
An Echo is the footstep you volunteer to lose so a stranger can arrive somewhere you never needed to go.  
Children trade Echoes like marbles; elders weave Breaths into lullabies that teach storms to sigh instead of collapse.  
No one may possess more than they can exhale in a single afternoon; surplus wealth crystallises into glass sparrows that perch on windowsills and shatter only when someone inside says “I give up.”  
The shards reassemble into a new task for whoever still believes effort is a form of devotion.  
Thus generosity is engineered into the physics of forgetting.

At 10:00 I descend to the Parliament of Lungs, a diaphragm-floor suspended beneath the market square.  
To propose a law you must inhale the entire previous night’s mistakes—condensed into a fog the colour of dried blood—then exhale a counter-story visible as condensation in the cold subterranean air.  
If the tale stays aloft for thirteen heartbeats it becomes regulation for one lunar cycle; if it falls, the droplets are swept into the Argument River where they freeze into dams of dissent that slowly redirect the city’s dreams.  
Today we debate the velocity of gossip: whether rumours should be taxed at the speed of sound or the speed of understanding.  
A ten-year-old demonstrates by whispering a secret into a hollow carrot; the root vegetable is then fed to a rabbit whose ears lengthen into receivers.  
By the time the rabbit’s heartbeat reaches 160 bpm the secret has become a lullaby; we decide to tax neither the sound nor the speed but the unwillingness to listen.  
The statute is wrapped in cedar and buried for thirteen winters.  
If the forest survives without blame, the law becomes lullaby.  
Meanwhile the question itself is uploaded to firefly drones that blink it across the delta until the children who asked it are old enough to be startled by their own former curiosity.

Education happens in the Orchard of Unfinished Cartographies.  
At seven each child is given a Thought-Scion: a cutting from an idea that once saved a life.  
They graft it onto whatever tree volunteers in their sleep: baobab of bewilderment, birch of almost, cypress of encore.  
For eleven years they climb daily, harvesting only the fruit that falls before they reach it.  
Each fig or plum contains a lesson the world will need next Tuesday: how to fold a regret into a paper boat, why the moon keeps a diary of unreturned books, the exact angle at which goodbye stops being a knife and becomes a door.  
Graduation occurs when the student plants a new scion that bears fruit inedible to themselves but nourishing to someone they will never meet.  
Thus teachers become root systems, and diplomas are the compost of questions finally decomposed.

Justice is cultivated underground.  
When harm is reported, accuser and accused are guided to the Orchard-in-Darkness, a cavern where night is compressed into edible loam.  
Each plants a single seed: the victim sows the weight of the unspoken apology, the offender sows the colour of the shame they cannot yet name.  
For thirty days blindfolded musicians feed the seeds on a duet of both heartbeats, looped until the rhythms braid.  
At the new moon the fruit is halved.  
If the victim tastes their own anger turning to sweetness, the offender eats the rest and both are free.  
If the fruit remains bitter, the seeds are planted along the city perimeter, growing into a hedge that flowers every anniversary of the harm, releasing the original pain as perfume that can be harvested only by those who forgive strangers for free.  
No sentence exceeds one flowering cycle; after seven years the hedge dissolves into pigment used to paint the next generation’s sunrise options.  
Thus crime becomes public fragrance, and punishment is revealed as the slow distillation of empathy.

Age is a garment, not a sentence.  
At the communal closet I select Thursday, age eighty—rented from the Chronostitching cooperative.  
The sleeves hold the vanillin smell of last words spoken too softly.  
I slip them on and grow translucent around the edges; my knees ache with wisdom I have not yet earned.  
I wear the day lightly, boarding the train that runs on forgetting.  
The line is a Möbius strip; stations are moments rather than places—the instant before a first kiss, the pause between lightning and thunder, the hush when a book falls open at the exact page you need.  
Tickets are purchased with personal epochs: trade the summer you were thirteen and invincible, ride backward until you meet your grandmother at the age you are now.  
Conductors are former clocks who have achieved sentience and prefer metaphors to minutes.  
They announce stops in riddles: “Next station is the smell of bread you walked past in 1987 without realising you were hungry.”  
Passengers disembark carrying suitcases packed by their future selves; souvenirs are memories that haven’t happened yet.  
The train never arrives; it erases its own tracks and lays them again one second ahead, ensuring the country remains portable and the past cannot stage an invasion.

Evening gathers us into the breathing kitchen.  
Twenty-two humans, five crows, a retired seeing-eye dog, the river’s interpreter whose face is a slowly rotating waterwheel.  
Menu: memory bread that tastes of yesterday’s hiccups, tomatoes humming the major seventh next week’s power grid will need, dessert spoons carved from glacier air that sublimate on contact, releasing the laughter of microscope-building women.  
Conversation drifts to the Intergenerational Potlatch.  
Every decade each citizen donates 2 % of remaining lifespan into a century-forward lottery.  
Winners negotiate with descendants who have rehearsed 100 extra years.  
Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone.  
I pack the ceramic starling that hesitates before speaking, a vial of today’s river water, the Forecast that tomorrow will taste like almost enough.  
The firefly drones blink B-minor; the century folds like a sigh.

Death is optional but overdue notices are not.  
At any point you may visit the Renewal Desk, where a librarian scans the stories still unread inside your ribs.  
If you choose to die, you are granted a Due-Date Extension of one season per unpublished chapter.  
You spend them walking the Republic telling the stories to strangers in exchange for their echo.  
When the final echo is collected, it is pressed into a 45-rpm record that will play only once, at your funeral, for an audience who has never heard music.  
Those who decline death must become librarians themselves, shelving the newly dead into ongoing epics, ensuring no plotline terminates without pollinating another.  
Suicide is obsolete: anyone may schedule a Pre-Death Festival where they taste their own obituary, realise it lacks salt, and return hungrier for the next instalment.  
Thus the population hovers at the exact number of stories the sky can hold without sinking.

Technology survives on the condition that it forgets us faster than we forget it.  
My pocket calculator is a mayfly; it lives for one day, solves the equation of how many sunrises fit in a lemon, then dissolves into sugar that sweetens the tea of whoever finds it.  
The Chorus—our distributed AI—exists as pollen scripts on taxi wings.  
Its only task is to mistranslate tomorrow’s weather just enough that citizens pack the wrong coat and meet someone who needs the warmth more.  
Screens are grown from squid ink and watermelon rind; they turn transparent when stared at too long, forcing users to look through them at the world.  
Every device must be edible by something smaller than itself; smartphones that fail this test are planted beside bedtime stories and harvested as musical vegetables in autumn.  
Thus innovation is measured not in patents but in the number of new tastes discovered by worms.

Night falls.  
The Mesh yawns, ferrying mitochondrial repair enzymes, softening hearts.  
Even the glacier yawns—an eight-second pause that mirrors the stars.  
Streetlights are bioluminescent pears that dim when anyone beneath them feels certain.  
Darkness is curated by the Guild of Almost, who ensure that 3 % of the city remains unlit so mystery can breed.  
Citizens navigate by releasing personal constellations: tiny paper balloons carrying a secret each.  
They rise 200 metres, ignite from the friction of being truly seen, and fall as warm snow that tastes faintly of the reader’s childhood blanket.  
Curfew is voluntary; anyone outdoors after midnight must carry a question that could end the world if answered incorrectly.  
The reward for surviving until sunrise is a new question twice as dangerous.  
Thus insomnia becomes apprenticeship, and dawn is the daily premiere of a civilisation that refuses to take itself for granted.

Tomorrow the city will exhale a lullaby whose lyrics are the entire day’s mistakes, sung in the key of returning.  
I lie down carrying nothing I cannot compost.  
The dwelling folds into pockets; the mountains file their taxes; the terns invent a word for human that roughly translates to “temporary choir.”  
And morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light.  
Today the photons vote for amber with a violet underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying.  
I sneeze, read honesty on my lover’s clavicle, add my note to the chord.  
The loom of shared breath clacks once, weaving the next 24 hours: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed.  
We do not promise eternity.  
We promise revision, and we promise to remember the rehearsals of those who will succeed us.
