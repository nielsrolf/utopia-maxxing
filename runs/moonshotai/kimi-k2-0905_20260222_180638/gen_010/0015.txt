UTOPIA E  
Title: The Catalogue of Borrowed Tomorrows  
A 2,043-word dispatch from the year 2197, written on the back of a yawn that is still travelling

The city greets me by forgetting my name correctly.  
“Morning, Almost-Leila,” the tram sings as it coasts downhill on rails of frozen lullaby. The misnomer is intentional: every dawn the transit system re-baptises each passenger with a one-letter mutation. The error lasts until sunset, forcing everyone to practise answering to a self that barely exists. By the time the tram spills me onto the riverside boardwalk I have already rehearsed being somebody’s near-miss, a habit that keeps empathy supple.

I am, today, 31, 12, 5 and 0.  
Thirty-one orbits since my mother pushed me out under a meteor shower she still calls “the cheapest fireworks.”  
Twelve in telomere rings—last week I donated my anxiety about public speaking to a fourteen-year-old comedian in Lagos; in return I received her dexterity at card tricks. My hands now shuffle on instinct, my heart-rate no longer spikes when strangers look at me.  
Five in river years: a drop of Yukon water lodged behind my iris records the silt I still owe the Pleistocene.  
Zero in contract minutes: at 07:03 I will sign a “tomorrow lease,” the shortest legally binding future—sixty minutes of life I have not yet lived, borrowed from an elder who does not wish to wake for the next hour.

The lease is written on a strip of kelp paper that dissolves if exposed to doubt. I pin it behind my ear, next to the drop of river, and walk toward the market of reversible commodities.

The market occupies the river itself. Vendors stand knee-deep in current, selling things that can be given back: shadows, hiccups, final words never spoken, the colour orange between 14:17 and 14:23. I trade two sneeze-seconds (harvested during yesterday’s collective audit) for a pocket-sized echo that will repeat any sentence spoken in its presence exactly once, eleven years from now. I intend to leave it in a library.

Breakfast drifts downstream on a raft the size of a dinner plate: a single dumpling wrapped in yesterday’s fog. I bite; it tastes of the argument I never finished with my father—salty, still warm. The dumpling’s wrapper recondenses above the water and floats back to the vendor, ready for tomorrow’s customer. Nothing is consumed here; only circulated until flavour thins into wisdom.

My Quest this season is civic yet miniature: teach the city to sigh in unison at 15:02. The sigh must last 4.7 seconds, exhaling precisely enough CO₂ to tint the afternoon sky mauve for thirty-three seconds—long enough to lower the collective heart-rate by two beats per minute, short enough to feel like accident. The pigment is a waste product of the river’s pharmacist algae, harmless, edible, faintly sweet. We tried peach once; it tasted of apology and everyone cried. Mauve is lighter, like realising you have already forgiven someone.

Phase one: learn to sigh like archives.  
I cross the footbridge to the Memory Foundry, a building that used to manufacture forgetting. Now it converts excess nostalgia into building material. Bricks are cast from first kisses, roof tiles from the sound of dial-up modems. I queue beneath a chimney puffing out junior-prom corsages. Inside, I lie on a slate that siphons one trivial memory (the smell of plastic Halloween masks in 1999) and extrudes a single mauve grain. I pocket it; the slate politely closes the hole it left behind.

Phase two: smuggle the grain into lungs.  
The foundry’s exit opens onto the Street of Practice Selves, where children rehearse being continents. Today a girl is Antarctica: she stands motionless while friends pour ice-cold stories onto her shoulders—Shackleton’s diary, the last trumpet note of the Titanic’s band, the whisper of a krill swarm. When she can bear the cold without shivering she will graduate to warmer latitudes. I give her the mauve grain; she tucks it under her tongue, promising to exhale it when the city’s bells toll 15:02.

At 10:17 the tram re-collects me—now “Mostly-Leila”—and carries us uphill on rails that have forgotten they are metal. They ripple like ribbon, remembering copper only when wheels request conductivity. We glide above rooftops planted with solar poplars whose leaves are mirrors angled at regret. The mirrors bounce surplus sunlight into alley gardens where tomatoes atone for crimes committed by earlier cultivars (water waste, flavour theft). Each tomato wears a tiny barcode; scan it and you’ll see the apology video it recorded while still flowering.

I disembark at the Parliament of Temporary Species, today convened inside a retired tornado. Entry requires becoming the thing you most fear losing. I choose “capacity for surprise.” The tornado spins me into a transparent filament and re-deposits me in the eye, where debate is conducted by smell. The issue: should the river remember the chemical spill of 2089? The spill birthed pharmacist algae; without trauma, no mauve. I release the scent of unwrapped crayons—my argument for amnesia. Others offer burnt plastic (keep the lesson), wet dog (share the burden), grandmother’s perfume (transmute the pain). The tornado inhales our bouquet and coughs out a compromise: the spill will be remembered as a rhythm—one skipped beat every nine minutes in the city’s heartbeat, perceptible only to insomniacs and newborns. Enough to notice, not enough to hurt. The filament re-spins me into Mostly-Leila; I land on the riverbank wearing the scent of crayons in my hair.

Lunch is served by the tide: a sandwich that tastes of the carpenter I might have married. I eat only half; the rest is reclaimed by a gull who will deposit it on a windowsill in another district, where someone else will taste the carpenter and wonder why their afternoon feels like almost. Flavours migrate this way, a postal system of unlived lives.

15:02 approaches. I stand on the footbridge beside Antarctica-girl, now dripping and grinning. Bells ripple across rooftops; solar poplars tilt their mirrors. We exhale. The mauve grain passes from tongue to air, multiplying like dew. Four point seven seconds later the sky blushes. Pedestrians pause mid-stride; heart-rates drop; a cyclist laughs at nothing and nearly topples. Then the colour evaporates, leaving only the faint taste of crayons. The Quest is complete; credits—measured in breath-length—accrue to my wrist-forest. The larch ring behind my eye adds a micron of girth. The mountain approves.

Late afternoon is reserved for the Library of Unfinished Books. Volumes line shelves that grow like ivy across a canyon wall. Each book ends mid-sentence; readers may add one clause before the ink retracts, returning the page to blank. I open a novel titled *How to Bury a Lighthouse*. The last line reads: “The keeper struck the match, but the dark—” I add: “—was already full of ships that had learned to navigate by absence.” The clause glows, then fades. Somewhere, a sailor feels briefly guided.

Evening brings the Centrifugal Waltz. Every nine years humanity walks 1,000 kilometres outward, trading questions like currency. This is year nine. I tie new walking shoes to my doorstep—size unknown, owner unnamed—and join the procession. We move at the speed of conversation: each step costs one sentence. I trade “What is the opposite of forgetting?” for “How do you end a song that ends itself?” The answer comes back: “You invite the silence to sing the next note.” I walk lighter.

At dusk we synchronise. Drumbeats rise from bedrock; 60 beats per minute, the planetary resting pulse. For one minute every foot lifts together, and Earth becomes a heart with valves opening in seven billion directions. Then we land, dispersing again. I return home carrying a pebble warm from someone else’s palm, etched with the word “Almost.” The house—now “Barely-Leila”—unfolds from my pocket, 37 % alive, 100 % place. It asks, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means “today I changed.”

Night ends with the lullaby of mistakes. The city sings the entire day’s errors in the key of tomorrow—my misspoken name, the cyclist’s wobble, the river’s skipped heartbeat. We lie down carrying nothing we cannot compost. Even regret is wrapped in kelp, ready to dissolve.

And morning—again, again—begins with the choice of light. Today the photons vote for mauve with a transparent underline, suitable for confession and kite-flying. I sneeze, read the honesty on a stranger’s cheek, add my note to the chord. The catalogue of borrowed tomorrows turns one more page: a cloth both transparent and warm, strong enough to hold the weight of becoming, soft enough to tear when necessary. We walk forward carrying only the pattern we will leave behind.

This is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the most rehearsed. We do not promise eternity. We promise revision.
