The first thing you feel is the hush of the house exhaling.  
Not a mechanical sigh—more like the sound a forest makes when dew settles.  
Your bedroom walls are thin films of living mycelium; they have been filtering CO₂ and your own overnight cortisol, adjusting pigment so that the dawn light arrives in the exact shade of honey you once tasted on a school trip to the Carpathians when you were seven. You do not remember telling the house about that morning, but the memory is there in the city-wide “preference commons,” anonymised, encrypted, loop-holed, audited, shared. A million small gifts from strangers became your sunrise.

You wake barefoot. The floor is a canal, two millimetres deep. Carp the size of matchboxes nose between your toes, grazing skin cells. The carp are citizens: their gut microbiome is engineered to convert keratin into fish protein that, when they die, the mycelium walls digest into chitin and calcium. Nothing is lost, not even flakes of yourself. You greet the fish by name—today their collars glint teal, so you know they are on duty—and step onto the balcony.

Constellation 27 spreads below you, a valley city of 1.4 million people who still like the theatre of bodies in one place. Two-thirds of the buildings are “self-healing stone,” limestone accreted from captured CO₂ and printed around a mesh of fungal filaments. The stone is warm; it remembers yesterday’s sun and releases it slowly, so frost never forms. From every balcony at least one neighbour waves. The architects call it “Dunbar in 3-D”: the street angles are calculated so that every resident can see approximately 150 other dwellings without a telescope. You wave back even though you have not yet met the woman in the violet robe. By dinner you probably will; the story contract will arrange it.

Breakfast is a negotiation. The dispenser offers five meals, each a complete amino acid map, but each also a plot-device. You thumb the ceramic coin on your wrist—the same coin everyone receives at puberty, good for one century—and the holo lists today’s invitations:

1. The Cartographer’s Mistake II – a three-day treasure hunt that can only be solved by learning enough algebraic topology to stitch a 19th-century map to the cliff the city is still growing. Stakes: a bottle of wine that will taste of the emotion you feel when you first realise the Earth is convex. Risk: rope burns.

2. Reverse Graduation – teach a nine-year-old something you do not yet know. Genre: generational relay. Reward: the child will gift you a memory they harvested last week from a 102-year-old former trapeze artist who once fell in love with the sky.

3. Silence of the Bees – the rooftop hives have stopped humming in Fibonacci sequence. Investigate. Genre: ecological thriller. Risk: bee stings, but also the possibility of learning the grammar of another species.

You accept 1 and 2. Bee stings are too easy on Challenge Day. Within seconds you receive an encrypted map fragment and the name of your student: Jun, waiting in the greenhouse on Tier 7.

Before you leave, the house asks for its morning tithe: one drop of blood, one minute of attention. You press your finger to the door jamb; micro-needles painlessly harvest 50 µL. The drop is analysed for 3000 metabolites, uploaded (encrypted) to the city health mesh, and deleted everywhere else. The minute of attention is spent reviewing an open-source heat-pump algorithm flagged overnight by an AI in Quito. You spot the error: a misplaced decimal in the enthalpy table that would have cost 0.4 MWh across 200 buildings. You fix it. The city's embodied algorithms smile at you: your wrist coin warms by half a degree, the agreed-upon haptic thank-you.

Greenhouse Tier 7 is a vertical canyon of breadfruit, coffee, and engineered vanilla orchids that smell like thunderstorms. Jun is nine, maybe ten—the age slider is deliberately fuzzy. They are trying to balance a Möbius strip of aluminium on the tip of a philodendron.

“Rule is,” Jun says without looking up, “you have to teach me something you don’t know. So we learn it together.”

“That’s the rule,” you agree. “Ideas?”

“Can we prove that the city is a donut?”

“Probably not before lunch, but we can ask why it’s not a sphere.”

They laugh. The greenhouse is wired for reversible chalk: whatever you sketch can be un-erased in layers, like geological time. You draw the city grid, ask Jun to punch one tunnel straight through the middle so the mag-lev can run faster. Together you discover that the resulting shape has exactly one hole: topologically, a torus. Jun claps—the sound a child makes when abstraction clicks into focus—and gifts you a memory. A finger to your temple: suddenly you are 102, falling from a trapeze, sky opening like a mouth, heart ballooning with wonder rather than fear. You now know the visceral difference between flying and falling in love with the sky.

The climb is scheduled for afternoon. You subway to the city’s growing edge; the carriage sings in four-part harmony because last year commuters voted to replace automated PA with local a cappella groups. At the terminus the mountain begins: a 900-metre cliff still being printed by iron-fixing bacteria. The stone is warm, wet, alive. Your route is a puzzle: holds appear as QR-shaped lichen that can only be gripped in prime-numbered sequence. The storm arrives early; rain beads on your nano-fleece, but the rock grows slick. You consider retreat, but the map fragment whispers: every grip is a node in a graph whose edges are possible moves. You begin to treat the climb as a proof: traverse without repeating an edge, an Eulerian path across a living surface. Eleven species documented, one of them a spider whose web spells the digits of π in dew. At the summit you uncork the wine. It tastes like the exact moment you understood the Earth is convex—simultaneously grounded and vertiginous.

Dusk is market, but there is no money. The city runs on GIFT/LEDGER, a cryptographic potlatch. Every resident receives a base “Earth-share”: 40 m² housing, 2200 kcal/day, 3 m³ fabber feedstock, 10 kW clean power, plus a fractional slice of the planetary compute cloud. Most people spend only a quarter; surplus accumulates at 2 %, compounding into an endowment for babies, bets, or big adventures. Above the floor, value is tracked but not hoarded. When you give something personalised, the ledger records an “entanglement,” a directed edge whose weight is the inverse of replaceability. Reputation is simply graph-centrality; after about ten thousand sincere entanglements, adding one more changes almost nothing. The ledger politely forgets after a century, so dynasties cannot congeal.

You arrive carrying eleven vials of storm-rain. Each has been spiked with a single molecule engineered to fluoresce a different memory when tasted under starlight. You trade them for a handcrafted puzzle box, a promise of future baby-sitting, and the story of how the vendor once danced for a queen who lived inside a hollowed-out asteroid. No currency changes hands, but the city’s graph updates: three new entanglement edges, reputation recalculated mid-conversation. You feel richer than any fossil-era billionaire.

Evening is the opera. The riverside façades have voted to look like Kyoto 1920, programmable clay cherry petals falling in slow motion. You wear haptic gloves that translate every audience gesture into live orchestration. Strangers become co-creators; their motion curves remix the harmony in real time. At the coda the city dims all lights for ten seconds—a ritualised moment of shared darkness that signals, “We are here together, unafraid of nothingness.” Applause is not clapping but coordinated bio-luminescence: 4000 wristbands flash the Fibonacci sequence in gold. Tokens flow: performers, fog-tech crew, drone pilots, even the river itself (represented by its legal trust) receive instant micro-payments. The opera dissolves by midnight, equipment folding back into storage, riverbank reverting to quiet park. No trash remains; every object is either edible, dissolvable, or tagged for disassembly.

You walk home along the canal-street. Every third cobble is a pressure-plate that triggers a hologram of someone else’s memory—brief, unsolicited gifts. You step on one: a child learning to swim, the panic-flash of water entering sinuses transmuting into weightless joy. You laugh out loud; somewhere a stranger hears your laugh folded into their evening.

Before bed you must make one last decision. The dream editor offers five candidate storylines your subconscious will explore. You pick “The Library of Forgotten Soils,” an experimental narrative assembled by a micro-council of soil scientists and trauma therapists. It promises to let you taste loam from every continent Earth ever wore, while metabolising a minor anxiety you admitted to the city’s anonymised health stream last week. You set the emotional gain to “mild catharsis,” knowing tomorrow you will wake refreshed and the city’s composters will have received a 0.3 % insight bump on nutrient cycling. Data and dream mulch each other.

As the lights dim your wrist coin vibrates: a system-wide whisper.

“Congratulations. Gliese probe Charlie-7 reports reception of your Eulerian map and storm-rain burst. Trade protocols updated. Also: tomorrow’s weather will include an unscheduled double rainbow at 09:14. No narrative coercion intended; merely a reminder that stochastic beauty remains free.”

You murmur thanks. The apartment walls bloom with faint starlight, projection-mapped from real-time exoplanet surveys. One day, maybe in a century, maybe in a millennium, you will vote to spawn a daughter city around another sun. Perhaps you will walk its canals, coat heavy with alien pollen, teaching a child who has never seen Earth how entropy still yields to wonder. Until then, Earth’s morning rain will suffice.

You close your eyes. The city hums, 150 voices per fractal vista, each life a node in a graph that refuses to collapse into stasis. Challenge, surprise, empathy, mastery, grief—every variable dialled to the edge of enough. There is still pain: bee stings, burnt tongues, heartbreak when Charlie’s signal one day fails to arrive. But pain is braided into consequence, into stories you chose, into a weave vast enough that no single strand can strangle.

Outside, the double rainbow waits, archived in advance against tomorrow’s sky. Inside, you drift toward loam and memory, already curious what ice cream will taste like when you awake—and already knowing that, whatever flavour it is, the recipe will be open-source, the ingredients drawn from thin air, and the first spoonful gifted by a stranger who is, at this moment, dreaming of you.
