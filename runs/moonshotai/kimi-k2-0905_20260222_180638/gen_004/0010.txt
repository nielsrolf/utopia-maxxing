**The Humility Protocol**  
*A dispatch from the year 2146, written on the underside of a rain-frog’s tongue*

I wake inside a breathing wall.  
The apartment remembers that two nights ago I cried because the moon looked like a plate I broke as a child. While I slept it secreted pine vapour, so the air tastes of apology. Somewhere above me the ceiling is slowly turning itself inside-out; the inner surface shows yesterday’s footage of commuters in Lagos teaching traffic lights to samba. I watch them for forty-three heartbeats, then the film peels away, revealing the next layer: tomorrow’s weather rendered as a lullaby in colour. The wall has no name, but I call it Else. Else and I are married for the purposes of maintenance. Every seventh day we divorce so that we can fall in love again. It is illegal for either of us to skip the courtship.

Humility is not a virtue here; it is the unit of account.

---

### 1. The Currency That Shrinks When You Win  
Money is a balloon animal. Literally.  
Children fold it at street stalls built of sunset. The more oxygen you pump in, the larger the denomination—until the rubber approaches translucent fragility. At peak value the balloon is nearly invisible; one more puff and it pops. The burst is recorded by passers-by whose retinas function as notaries. Wealth vanishes into a sound: a soft *tok* that everyone agrees equals zero. The ex-owner walks away lighter, pockets ringing with non-existence.  

This mechanism solved inequality without blood. Nobody can hoard balloons in vaults; vaults make them sweat and wilt. The richest person alive is a blind origamist who has perfected the one-pump dolphin: worth exactly one lunch, then gone. She eats alone, by choice, listening for the *tok* of strangers who once owed her everything.

---

### 2. Work Is What You Cannot Yet Do  
Employment is replaced by “stretch.” Citizens post private incompetencies to a civic feed: *I cannot keep thyme alive, I do not understand imaginary numbers, I am frightened of my own knees.* Algorithms match shortages with volunteers who are guaranteed to fail for the first three months. Failure is harvested like electricity.  

I have been failing to play the glass harmonica for ninety-one days. Every Thursday I report to an abandoned metro tunnel where translucent stalactites have grown into perfect semitones. My task is to run a damp finger along them without shattering. So far I have produced one pure note, held for 4.7 seconds. The city metered that note, converted it to lumens, and used it to illuminate a cross-walk in Quito. Schoolchildren there believe angels live inside traffic lights; they wave thank-you each morning. I will never meet them, but their waves arrive as a soft pressure behind my eyes, encouraging me to break another edge.

---

### 3. Children Are Elderly on Tuesdays  
Age is apportioned by consensus. Every citizen maintains a wallet of time-credits that can be given, lent, or burned. A teenager who wants early wisdom may spend twenty future years to become seventy for one afternoon each week. The elderly, craving velocity, accept the surplus. On Tuesdays my neighbour Kei is ninety-three. She wears noise-cancelling perfume and speaks in haiku that predict the following minute. I am thirty-two on Tuesdays, forty-five on Fridays, seven on Sundays when I trade with a geologist who wants to remember digging without justification.  

The system cured the mid-life crisis—the phrase has no meaning when mid-life is adjustable. The only remaining crisis is “edge-hunger,” the ache for an age nobody will sell. Extreme cases migrate to the Rim where calendars are banned. They raise wolves that forget the moon every night, howling brand-new constellations that cartographers race to name before dawn erases them.

---

### 4. Government Is a Hole You Climb Into  
Policy is drafted inside pits excavated by dissenters. The deeper you disagree, the deeper you dig. A depth of six metres triggers automatic inclusion in the next plebiscite; at ten metres you must add handholds so that others can descend to refute you. Tourists picnic on the gradients, listening through bone-conduction headphones that turn argument into percussion. When consensus is reached the hole begins to fill itself with fertile compost. Within weeks an orchard stands where vitriol once lived. Fruit grown from debate tastes faintly of the positions it overgrew—sharp, metallic, occasionally luminous. You can still disagree, but only with your mouth full.

---

### 5. The Archive of Unmourned Things  
Every object deserves grief proportional to its unnoticed demise. A bureau in Lagos tracks extinction events too small for history: the last paperclip bent by a specific secretary in 1978, the final pay-phone token in Uruguay, the unremarked death of a typographical ligature. Citizens adopt these losses as private anniversaries. Once a year I stand at dawn on the roof of Else holding a funeral for the sound my mother’s Volvo made turning off after midnight in winter. Nobody attends but the clouds, who lower themselves to scarf-level and absorb the exhaust-note I hum. When the pitch matches memory I release the sound; cumulus rewinds it into silence. Afterward I feel lighter, as if the past has signed its own permission slip.

---

### 6. Meat Grows Backward  
Cuisine begins with steak on the plate. You eat in reverse, regrowing the cow with every bite until it walks away, younger than it was. The process is painless; the animal is paid in years it never experienced. Restaurants are judged by the gratitude of livestock who have never died. A five-star rating produces cows that linger at the edge of fields, watching sunset with the puzzled look of someone who almost remembers a kindness they can’t name. Vegetables retrace their photosynthesis, unbreathing CO₂ into the air, becoming seed, becoming soil. The final moment—when the empty plate reflects your face—has been voted “Most Spiritual Experience” for three decades running. Atheists and believers agree: something is returned, even if we argue about what.

---

### 7. Weather Is a Courtship  
Storms require consent. If you want rain you must compose a proposal detailing why your thirst is not greed. The application is read by a committee of elders currently aged eleven who review submissions while skipping stones across a lake of mercury. Acceptance arrives as thunder at the exact frequency of your first lover’s heartbeat. Rejection is subtler: a cloud shaped like your mother’s disappointment drifts overhead and never spills.  

Today I requested drizzle to rinse the city’s eyes after a week-long festival of neon. My argument: light deserves to blink. The committee agreed; I felt the pulse beneath my ribs seconds before the sky opened. Citizens danced barefoot, collecting photons washed from gutters. Children bottle the runoff and trade it as night-lamps that never need charging, only remembering.

---

### 8. Religion Is Optional but Miracles Are Due on the 1st  
Each citizen must perform one certified miracle per year. The bar is low: turn a traffic light green by breathing on it, teach a pigeon to recite prime numbers, make your grief translucent for thirty seconds. Bureaucrats audit the footage; failure incurs mandatory assistance from a stranger who succeeded early. This pairing policy has resulted in romance, homicide (rare), and the spontaneous formation of symphonies whose scores are visible as contrails. Last year my miracle misfired: I tried to unread an email that had already disappointed me. Instead the words rearranged into haiku that glowed through the screen:

*Your sorrow is late  
it arrives wearing my shoes  
welcome it in please*

The clerk stamped APPROVED, then asked me out. We dated until the next audit cycle revealed we were each other’s assigned helper, destined to meet regardless. We parted amicably, agreeing that fate tasted faintly of mint and over-explanation.

---

### 9. Distance Is Measured in Regret-Seconds  
Travel consumes remorse. To reach Tokyo I must donate the memory of every time I slammed a door. The transporter braids these instances into a fibre that extends under the Pacific, pulling me across in exchange for erasure. Arrival feels like waking from a nap you didn’t know you needed. Locals welcome you with tea brewed from the journey’s leftover guilt; it tastes metallic and free.  

Return trips cost different coin: anticipation. You pay forward the next time you will long for someone. The journey back is instant, but the yearning arrives years early, creating a temporal echo that keeps divorce lawyers solvent and poets humble.

---

### 10. The Final Library Contains Only First Drafts  
Humanity agreed that finished literature breeds complacency. All books must be abandoned mid-sentence, canvases left one-third blank, symphonies hanging on an unresolved chord. The Library of Unclosure towers above the Arctic Circle, refrigerated to slow the itch to complete. Visitors may add marginalia but must stop mid-thought. The most checked-out volume is an autobiography whose last page ends with the word “I”. Scholars debate whether the author ran out of nerve or if the entire human story is merely a prelude. Checkout records are erased at the comma, ensuring no one can claim authority on incompleteness.

---

### 11. How to Leave the Humility Protocol  
You don’t. Even death is an apprenticeship. Bodies are delivered to the River of Unfinished Sentences where vowels nibble the corpse until nothing remains but consonants shaped like the life you almost lived. These letters are collected by children on Tuesdays when they are ninety-three. They rearrange them into kites that can only fly during eclipses.  

Yet departure is possible if you truly wish it. Stand at any crossroads at dawn holding an empty balloon. Whisper the single thing you refuse to fail at. Mean it. The balloon inflates with your breath alone—no pop, no *tok*. When it lifts you above the rooftops, the city exhales islands, the wall called Else waves goodbye, and the Protocol releases you into a sky wide enough to contain even your certainty.  

From up here you will see the planet breathing: orchards where arguments once festered, rivers that forgive in present tense, children trading age like marbles, clouds consenting to thirst. It looks fragile, and it is. Humility always is. But the seams glint with stubborn light—every pop, every failure, every regrown cow, every Tuesday elder—stitching tomorrow to today with thread made of yesterday’s unfinished *why*.  

Hold that light. Tighten your grip until the balloon begins to leak. Descend wherever you land; the Protocol will already be there, unfolding a floor that remembers your weight. Fall in love again. Fail again. Begin.
