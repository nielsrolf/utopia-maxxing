UTOPIA C  
Field Notes etched into a single bamboo shoot that will ripen into tonight’s flute, 2198

I wake because my heartbeat is being borrowed.

The bed—part loquat-wood, part river-mist—doesn’t alarm me; it simply lends today’s pulse to a stranger in Quito whose own heart has paused to admire an eclipse. My wrist glows aquamarine, the color we agreed means “temporarily infinite.” The loan lasts forty-three seconds. In exchange I receive the smell of Ecuadorian cinnamon carried backward through the Mesh. We call this small asymmetry “interest,” but it feels like breathing someone else’s wonder and finding it fits.

Age is a playlist, not a prison.  
Today I cue up:  
7 years old—barefoot on warm slate,  
29—first public failure,  
101—learning to yawn in four dialects,  
0—whatever I haven’t invented yet.  
The slider is wooden, smells of pear. I drag it until the proportions feel aerodynamic, then step into the day.

My room is a petition in progress.  
North wall: a slab of glacier learning to apologise in sign language; its Morse-code drips spell “sorry” in 14 fonts.  
South wall: a collective dream of 312 strangers who last night rehearsed losing their keys together; the dream hardened into translucent bricks, now stacking themselves into a doorway that opens only if you admit you might be wrong.  
Floor: oyster-carpet filtering yesterday’s arguments into pearl buttons. I tread softly; each footstep donates one syllable of tomorrow’s lullaby to the city’s treasury.

Currency is three-dimensional and edible:

1. **Breath Bonds** – a cubic litre of air that once passed through lungs you love; when chewed it releases the sound of their laughter at the exact moment they forgave you.  
2. **Dew Pennies** – microscopic beads of responsibility you can spend only by placing them on the tongue of someone who has never tasted your climate.  
3. **Latency Loaves** – bread that stays doughy until the exact minute the world needs your undivided attention; when it cracks open, crumbs spell the question you’ve been avoiding.  

Children trade Dew Pennies like stickers; elders bake Latency Loaves the size of hills and leave them in public squares to rise on strangers’ hesitation.

Breakfast arrives as choreography. A drone-moth lands, abdomen vibrating in 7/8 time. I must match the rhythm with spoon-taps on the bowl’s rim; only then does the moth uncurl a tongue of honey that tastes of the drought that didn’t happen because 400 villages learned to yawn in synchrony. I fumble the fourth beat, so the honey adds a note of my own 2019 embarrassment. Sweet with aftertaste of humility—perfect.

Work is called “tuning the almost.”  
The civic ledger floats above the breakfast table like heat-haze:

- The ravens have agreed to share, but only in minor keys.  
- River reeds still believe straightness is moral; we need 3 bent volunteers.  
- Someone must teach the concept of “enough” to the orbital reef before it photosynthesises gratitude into narcotic humility.

I sign up for the reef. My tools: a pocketknife that becomes manganese when I die, a choir of salmon drones learning baritone, and a single question I’m forbidden to answer until 2284: “How do you end a song that refuses to end?” Pay is one memory of weightlessness per hour; I collect them like stamps.

Governance happens in the Parliament of Lungs, but also in the Parliament of Ladders, the Parliament of Forgotten Smells, and the Parliament of Second Thoughts. Anyone can convene a parliament by posting an invitation written in bacteria on a public pear; if the colony spells something dangerous enough to keep 1,000 strangers awake, the fruit ferments into law. Last week a thirteen-year-old convened the Parliament of Unfinished Jokes; the resulting statute requires every elevator to pause between floors long enough for passengers to invent a punchline. Compliance is measured by collective exhale; if the car arrives before the joke lands, the building loses one floor for a day.

Transport is a promise, not a vehicle.  
I step onto the balcony and state where I’d like to be helpful. The balcony negotiates with the wind, the wind with the street, the street with my joints. If all parties agree, I lean forward and the distance contracts like inhaled accordion. Today the reef needs pruning; I lean. Three blocks dissolve into a single elongated second. My shoes arrive a minute later, apologetic—they took the scenic route to pick up a haiku from a passing cloud.

The reef orbits at 310 km, a chlorophyll continent tuned to the frequency of euphoric humility. I access it through a vine that grows straight out of the pavement, corkscrewing skyward. The commute is 17 heartbeats; the vine harvests the lift from my nostalgia and pays itself in panoramic dread—fear of heights is the toll, composted into petals at journey’s end.

Inside the reef I meet my supervisor: a six-year-old who was old yesterday. She wear overalls stitched from her grandparents’ lullabies and carries a watering can full of enforced boredom. Her Quest is to teach the reef when to stop photosynthesising; too much humility and the planet will apologise itself into paralysis. She assigns me the eastern quadrant where the leaves are tipping into self-reproach. My job: prune regret without wounding gratitude. The pocketknife is perfect—its blade forgets what it cuts, so the wound can decide whether it wants to be scar or sunrise.

While I work, the salmon drones loop in figure-eights, singing B-minor with modified lyrics: “Return is just forgetting in reverse.” Their aluminium vests reflect Earth like a marble in a sling. Each reflection is a photograph mailed to the ocean floor, where abyssal crabs collect them into mosaics of coastlines that haven’t existed yet.

Lunch is delivered by gravity. A ripened cloud drifts overhead, darkens exactly above me, and releases one raindrop the size of a plum. Inside: seawater tomatoes, bread that tastes of last night’s dreams, and a single question suspended in gel: “What did you almost learn too late?” I swallow the question whole; the cloud thanks me by shifting shape into a perfect cartogram of my childhood river. I cry precisely 3 ml—payment for the image—then pocket the remaining tears as Dew Pennies.

Afternoon is spent rehearsing the almost-famine of 2104. The city stages failure the way other places stage festivals. Citizens queue to practice scarcity, heartbreak, evacuation. Today’s scenario: a solar flare knocks out the Mesh for nine minutes. Volunteers scramble to remember phone numbers, barter songs for antibiotics, decide which memory to upload into the last pigeon. I play “the one who insists on carrying the piano.” We sweat inside borrowed adrenaline; when we exit, cheeks stinging with phantom ash, we receive ceramic birds able to store one rehearsal each. Mine warms whenever real danger approaches, chirping the piano’s middle C—reminder that I already practiced letting go.

Evening.  
The block gathers in the breathing kitchen: twenty-two humans, three crows, a retired guide-dog, the river’s interpreter (a waist-high android whose face is a slowly rotating water wheel). The roof folds itself into paper lanterns; stars arrive early, curious about the menu.

- Appetiser: glacier air spoons that sublimate on contact, releasing 19th-century laughter of women who built microscopes from kitchen pots.  
- Main: memory bread that tastes of tomorrow’s forgettings.  
- Side: tomatoes harmonising in the minor third next week’s power grid will need.  
- Dessert: a single pomegranate seed containing every joke the Parliament of Unfinished Jokes discarded; when cracked, it giggles for nine seconds, then apologises.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch. Every decade we each donate 3 % of remaining lifespan into a century-forward lottery. Winners negotiate with descendants who have rehearsed 100 extra years. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone.

I’ve won twice before—once to request slower rain, once to ask for the right to age backwards on Tuesdays. Tonight my name is drawn again. I pack:

- the ceramic pigeon warm with solar-flare rehearsal  
- a vial of today’s pruned regret  
- the haiku cloud I cried into existence  
- the unanswerable question: “How do you end a song that refuses to end?”

The salmon drones gather in B-minor; the century folds like origami lungs.

Friday, 2298.  
I wake on the reef’s dark side, chlorophyll dimmed to ember. A child waits—barefoot, pupils wide as moons. She is zero years old and 117 in forest rings; she has carried three centuries for strangers and today chooses to be newborn. Her Quest: teach the reef to end its song. I offer the vial of pruned regret; she drinks, and the reef inhales its own finale—one perfect chord that decrescendos into silence. She asks my question back. After 100 years I answer:  
“The song ends when the echo chooses not to return.”

She presses a warm pebble into my palm—fossilised applause from the abyssal crabs. The descendants’ demand: send back the right to remain unfinished. They want bridges that deliberate mid-span, lullabies that pause for dissent, elders who forget mastery on purpose. I accept. A green laser stitches the agreement into the past. Somewhere in 2198 I will wake with the pebble in an empty pocket and remember thatcompletion is optional.

Saturday is ordinary.  
I prune vines that have learned to forgive straight lines. The reef exhales 310 ppm air; schoolchildren inhale and argue about whether clouds should be allowed to vote. On the ground, the walking stones trade places with memory-oaks; no one is surprised. I glide home on an exhalation of shared wonder, shoes arriving just before I need them, laces tied in a knot that unties itself when it senses certainty.

Night.  
The city forgets cruelty in 37-second batches. Crows practise the lapse mid-flight, wings forgetting the down-stroke until they almost fall, then remembering laughter instead. The river pauses for nine seconds, creating a mirror wide enough for the galaxy to check its posture. I stand on the balcony, warm pebble against pulse. The house asks, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale—the small asymmetry that means the rehearsal is still interesting, and Monday has not yet been named.
