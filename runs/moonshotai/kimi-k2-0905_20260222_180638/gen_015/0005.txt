UTOPIA  
Title: The Choir That Forgets the Words  

I wake because the floor is humming the first sentence I ever mispronounced.  
The vibration is gentle—0.3 on the Richter scale of regret—but unmistakable. The apartment’s living parquet is reading last night’s dream aloud to the foundation beams. When the story ends the boards will erase the dream with enzymes extracted from squid that never learned ink was supposed to be defensive. By the time I open my eyes the memory is already 37 % gone; what lingers is the taste of almost-correct grammar and the colour that is both peach and violet, the shade we call “revision in progress.”

I live in the city of Second-Set, population 38,112 humans, 512 glaciers that chose to become apartments rather than melt, 1 crow who has been trading one eye for one new syllable every seventy-five years and is therefore down to four eyes and up to “hushloud.” The buildings here are engaged in a perpetual talent show with their residents. My flat is 19 years old—barely adolescent—still trying to impress me with a balcony that unfolds only when I forget to check the time. Last week it attempted a sonnet in cinnamon steam; the rhyme scheme collapsed into a cough, but we applauded anyway. If we ever divorce the law requires us to co-author one room the other would truly prefer: I must design a silence the house has always longed to hear; the house must grow a confession I have never dared speak aloud. We have filed for separation twice; both times we ended up hosting the mediation ourselves inside the proposed room, and both times we emerged laughing too hard to sign the scroll.

Currency here is practiced in public, like tai chi. There are five current-cies; if any stops moving for more than nine minutes it sublimates into gossip and is lost:

1. Inhale – one litre of air that remembers the exact moment someone else realised they were wrong. Earn it by describing to a stranger the Pantone shade of the second before they apologised to themselves.  
2. Exhale – the right to release a decision you no longer want to metabolise, measured in barefoot steps walked backwards while humming the national anthem of a country that will never exist.  
3. Glint – the flash that jumps from pupil to pupil when consensus is reached without language. It can be peeled off like foil and folded into a glacier’s creak to pay for long-distance lullabies.  
4. Latency – the agreed lag between touching the world and realising you have changed it. Stored as scent in cedar molt; the only debt that accrues interest the longer you wait to collect it.  
5. Almost – a quantum denomination. Spend it and the transaction both happened and did not; the ledger is kept by teenagers who volunteer to stay uncertain for a year. Almost is the favourite coin of new parents and particle physicists.

I am 64, 12, 203 and 0.7.  
Sixty-four in solar years.  
Twelve in telomere loops—last winter I traded a decade of cellular age for the right to beginner’s lungs on the glass harmonica.  
Two hundred and three in forest rings: a wafer of larch cambium grafted over my pulse thickens by one micron every time the city collectively forgets a boundary.  
Zero-point-seven in Almost years—half a pregnancy of possibility that I carry like a pocket.

Breakfast rises through the floor on a spiral elevator of warm air. Today’s meal is a wafer the colour of sunrise over polycrisis, flavour-inscribed with last night’s performance of the Extinction of 2084. Teenagers acted it out inside the Dream Foundry—three subjective centuries compressed into forty minutes—then archived the emotion in fungal parchment. Eating it is like licking the envelope of a letter I haven’t decided to send. I taste roasted guilt, the sweetness of the moment the dream-senate voted to share the last chromosome, and something new: the aftertang of “almostgoodbye,” a word invented by a man who spent two centuries as Latency, paying for a mistake that became a valley. The word dissolves on my tongue, leaving the faint scent of peach turning into glacier.

I step outside. The street has rearranged itself again; memory-oaks and walking stones traded places while I slept. No one is startled. We greet the shift the way sailors greet wind. At the corner I meet Jonas, 9 and 104. Nine in birthdays, one hundred and four in failed quests—he has chosen to forget and relearn cooperage fifteen times, each cycle building the same barrel and then letting the skill evaporate. The barrel never repeats; forgetting guarantees it. Today he carries cedar that smells of thunderstorms and forgiveness. We fall in beside a woman jogging backwards—standard training for the Centrifugal Waltz, a nightly parade where thousands walk outward carrying questions to trade. Her T-shirt live-streams her own pupils; she is rehearsing radical transparency for the season when privacy will be outlawed for one week. Behind her a child pedals a bicycle whose chain is woven from the sound of her mother’s first laugh. Every revolution erases one second of maternal shame; by dusk the gears will be silent and the woman will have learned how to ride backwards into her own childhood.

The river is black with sky and mercury. Fingerling drones—salmon wearing aluminium vests—leap through the surface, sampling our ankle skin for cortisol. If they detect chronic stress the river will modulate tonight’s lullaby into a key scientifically proven to re-parent human mammal cells. I demonstrate the global yawn: mouth unhooked, tongue flat, the slow ache spreading like warm tea through seven billion facial muscles. A jogger catches it—laughs mid-yawn, warping the waveform. A fish stores the mutation. By noon the yawn has crossed two bridges and a floating market; the larch ring on my wrist vibrates, adding a micron. The mountain applauds with a small avalanche of forget-me-notes.

Afternoon is spent in the Choir of Maybe, an asynchronous chorus sung into weather systems. I upload a four-note phrase—major sixth for invitation—encoding a proposal: let the glacier “Tardy” host tomorrow’s Parliament of Temperature inside its own meltwater. The weather AIs embed the motif into a contrail. Listeners will taste intention in tomorrow’s rain: metallic for urgency, faint turmeric for laughter. The Choir has prevented three famines, one asteroid, twenty-three unnecessary fashion trends, and the re-emergence of celebrity presidents.

Evening. The block gathers in the breathing kitchen—nineteen humans, two crows, a retired guide-dog, the river’s interpreter (a waist-high android whose face is a slowly rotating water wheel). We eat under the open sky; the roof folded itself into origami lanterns at dusk. Menu: seawater tomatoes harmonising in the minor third the city will use next month to regulate empathy tariffs; dessert spoons carved from glacier air, sublimating on the tongue to release the 19th-century laughter of women who built the first microscopes in their kitchens while men argued about what seeing really meant.

Conversation drifts to the Temporal Potlatch lottery. Tomorrow the names are drawn. Every decade each resident donates 2.7 % of remaining lifespan into the cauldron. Winners are cryo-braided one century forward to negotiate with descendants who have rehearsed 100 extra years. Whatever agreement is reached ripples backward as policy tweak or Eclipse Zone. Jonas jokes that if he wins he will barter for a season of silence—no new barrels, only heartwood thickening. The dog whimpers; she smells the future in the water wheel’s spin and finds it citrusy with a chance of reconciliation.

Night. I walk the Centrifugal path. Thousands are already outbound, rays from a quiet sun. Each carries a question inked on biodegradable substrate. Mine: “How do you end a lullaby that begins in someone else’s childhood?” I swap stories, swap silences. At midnight the global drum synchronises at 60 bpm; for one minute every foot lifts together, and the planet becomes a heart with seven billion valves. Then we land, dispersing again, lighter by one unanswered question.

Home. The dwelling has grown a new room: a soft lung-shaped alcove smelling of cedar and thunderstorm—Jonas’s gift. I curl inside. The walls ask, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale, the small asymmetry that means “today I changed.” The chord in the floorboards shifts to C-major, a key my mother never hummed because she was too busy inventing new colours. Difference is the only renewable resource; Almost is the only coin that never tarnishes.

Tuesday begins inside the Parliament of Temperature. There is no building, only a sensor veil measuring how fast human meanings shift before they crystallise into action. When delta-T exceeds 0.3 °C of metaphorical heat, anyone asleep during the spike is invited to lucid caucus. Today’s trigger is the yawn contagion coupled with a spike in Almost transactions. Some citizens worry that optional mitochondrial repair violates the right to natural decay. Others argue decay has already been socialised—why not repair? Inside the dream I fold my street into a crane; it flies toward the glacier, unfolds into a mouth that yawns. The yawn turns the city inside-out, revealing glowing capillaries of lifetimes. Those who refuse repair appear dimmer. I choose neither dim nor bright; I fold the crane into a ladder and climb down into the capillary of the woman who laughed mid-yawn this morning. I offer her memory a yawn if she will let me forget the punchline of her favourite joke. The capillary flares, settles into softer glow—decay and repair braided like hair. The dream crystallises into law: repair is optional, but refusal must be rehearsed. Every decade, opt-outs must spend one day in a body whose cells are failing faster than theirs. Credits feed the river’s pharmacist algae. The law is tattooed on the civic blockchain with the watermark of cooled dream and the scent of geranium oxidising into apology.

Wednesday is the Temporal Potlatch. My name is drawn. I pack:
- the ceramic bird that rehearsed a solar flare and still glows  
- the larch ring, which will keep growing in slow-motion  
- a vial of today’s river water tasting faintly of turmeric and laughter  
- the unanswered question: “What is the opposite of forgetting?” (I once thought it was “returning,” but now I am Almost sure.)

I lie down in the cryo cradle. A chorus of salmon drones sings B-minor; the chord stretches into a tunnel, and the tunnel closes like a yawn that decides to swallow itself.

Thursday, 2289. I wake inside an orbital reef the size of Tasmania. Its chlorophyll is tuned to the frequency that triggers euphoric humility. Before entering I must spend one hour pruning fast-growing vines—boredom is the toll gate, but also the teacher. A child approaches, pupils dilated like lunar craters, humming my mother’s lullaby in a key that will not be invented for another forty years. Her Quest is to teach the reef to forget carbon it once absorbed, exhaling pre-industrial air. I offer her my vial of river water; she drinks, and the reef dims—just slightly—like a sigh that has read too many manifestos. She asks my question back: “What is the opposite of forgetting?” I answer: “Returning, but only if you bring a new name for the thing you lost.” She hands me a pebble etched “Welcome-Again.” The negotiation is complete. Descendants’ demand: send back the right to begin again as incompetent, and the obligation to remember the Almost. Every century they want more elders who must relearn mastery, more children who are paid in uncertainty. They want the city to yawn until repair and decay braid like hair, until Almost becomes a legitimate unit of governance. I accept. A green laser stitches the agreement into the century like a needle sewing sky to skin.

Friday. I return. The house unfurls; the dog is older, tail still thumping to the 60-beat drum. I kneel; she licks the larch ring, now four microns thicker and humming C-major. She smells of citrus and forthcoming rain.

Saturday is ordinary. Ordinary is the highest form of maintenance. I prune vines via remote drone. I fold cedar with Jonas, who has forgotten me again but remembers the scent of thunderstorm. I walk the Centrifugal path, trading questions for new ones: “What does the wolf remember after choreography ends?” Each question is a pebble dropped into the river, stored for the next negotiation, the next Almost.

Sunday the house asks, “Still you?” I exhale once more than I inhale. The chord shifts to C-major with a suspended fourth, refusing resolution. We call this place utopia not because it is finished, but because the rehearsal is still interesting, Monday has not yet been named, and somewhere a glacier is learning to yawn in B-minor, waiting for us to decide what we will almost become next.
