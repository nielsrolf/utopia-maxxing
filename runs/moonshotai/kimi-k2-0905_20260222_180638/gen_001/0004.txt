The morning you wake into the Republic of Verdigris begins with a whisper, not an alarm.  
A soft breeze of moving air nudges your cheek; the apartment knows you have entered shallow sleep and will soon surface. The woven-carbon walls glow a muted jade—Verdigris, the colour of oxidised copper, chosen by the founding architects as a reminder that civilisation is metal learning to breathe.

You sit up. No rush. Abolition of scarcity has abolished the snooze button. On the sill, a porcelain cup is already warm: coffee grown on the thirty-second floor of this same district tower, the beans having never left the building. The rooftop groves are tended by people who like the smell of soil more than the glow of screens; their wages are paid, like everyone’s basic dividend, out of the “productivity rents” collected by the city’s data trust. When AI and robotics made human labour optional, Verdigris did not declare the end of work; it declared the end of wages as mere survival. Citizens receive a floor of energy, housing, food, bandwidth and medical care that would have looked preposterously luxurious to a mid-century millionaire. Above that floor, everything is gravy—experiments in craft, conviviality, research, mischief, or pure idleness.

The apartment’s living surface becomes a mirror. In it you see your nightly biomarkers floating like fireflies: inflammation 0.08, telomerase stabilised, REM fraction 34 %. The city’s medical AI posts a green check-mark; nothing to schedule. You are 93 years old; your skeletal age is 42. Life-extension arrived not as a single silver bullet but as a slow-motion avalanche—CRISPR 3.0, partial reprogramming, senolytic soap, glycation breakers, mitochondrial refresh—each advance adopted first by volunteers whose anonymised data repaid the common pool. The pace was fast enough to matter, slow enough to trust. Radical longevity is free, but the City Council enforces a “one-child, one-clone” rule so that land area and care networks are not outrun by population. Multiple generations now plan their centuries together.

You dress in fabric that smells faintly of cedar because the fibres were extruded by engineered mycelia fed on the city’s wood-chip waste stream. You step onto the balcony. Sunlight is split by prismatic louvres into bands that feed both the eye and the rooftop algae ponds. Below, the tram track hums, but there is no driver, only a rotating civic lottery: every resident is allotted one “watch hour” per thousand hours of system operation. You might spend it aboard a tram with a hand-held override tablet, mostly sipping tea and chatting with strangers. The lottery keeps the knowledge of how to run the city distributed through the populace, a living safeguard against technological priesthoods.

At street level the air tastes of petrichor even though the pavements are dry. Embedded permeable slabs exhaled moisture during the cool night; now they inhale heat, keeping the district within the two-degree comfort band that Pachamana—the continental climate model—prescribes. Every block is required to be thermally self-balancing; air-conditioning is legal only if the joules are harvested on-site. The rule was voted in after children acted out a climate-repair game in VR and presented the Council with a scroll saying “If we can do it in a sim, you can do it in the stone.” The Council agreed. That was twenty-five years ago; emissions are below zero for the entire plateau.

You glide toward the river on a pedal-assisted bike whose composite frame came out of a printer in the next neighbourhood. There is no chain; magnetic coupling turns the wheels, silent and greaseless. Ownership is optional. The bike pool algorithm knows your calendar and nudges a frame toward your doorway if your next meeting is more than a kilometre away. Docking levies a minute of civic credit—tiny, but enough to discourage hoarding. Money still exists, but it is a specialised tool for luxuries and status games, not for staying alive.

On the bridge you pause. The water below is teal, flecked with white bubbles where the re-oxygenation turbines work. Salmon have returned; children downriver catch them with barbless hooks for fun, then toss them back. Vertebra-shaped drones drift upstream, printing limestone reefs as they go, repairing centuries of acidification. The drones are made in the vocational fab-labs that replaced high-school woodwork: every citizen is taught to solder, code, weave, lathe, mill, culture cells and assay tissue. No one is told which vocation to master, but you must accrue twelve “material literacy” badges before receiving your coming-of-age passport. The policy arose after economists calculated that resilience, not GDP, was the variable to maximise. A populace that can fix its own tools is immunised against supply shocks.

Across the river lies the Agora of Practical Delight, today hosting a weekly “Festival of Uncertain Outcomes.” Stalls offer things that may fail: experimental perfumes, poems written by river sediment bacteria, 3-D printed kites shaped like extinct birds. Failure is priced cheap because data from flops feeds the city’s iterative brain. You stop at a booth where teenagers attempt to grow bacterial leather that changes colour when touched. Their mentor is 78, a retired aerospace engineer who always wanted to be a kindergarten teacher but never had the patience for paperwork. Now she mentors twice a week; her pension is generous enough that she can afford curiosity. This, too, is counted as work by the civic ledger and adds to her social-credit score, which unlocks nothing essential—only perks such as queue-jump for orbital balloon rides.

Your own calendar reminds you of a promise: you signed up to help judge this afternoon’s Policy Jury. Verdigris practices “augmented sortition.” A representative 200 citizens are selected each month; for three days they are briefed by contested stakeholders, then they retire to hammocks in the Maple Atrium while an AI mediator answers their questions, detects information asymmetry, and summarises best evidence. No lobbying is allowed except through encrypted questions the AI anonymises and re-poses. At the end the jury votes, and its decision stands unless two-thirds of the full electorate overturn it within ten days. Today’s issue: whether to licence a private geothermal borehole that would supply cheap power but risk micro-quakes under the historic textile district. You have read the packet; opinion leans toward conditional approval with a seismic escrow fund. You look forward to the deliberations; they feel like theatre where the stakes are real but nobody dies.

You take your bowl to a communal table. Conversation drifts to last night’s meteor shower. A nine-year-old explains that she is stitching the shower’s orbital data into a quilt whose squares will be auctioned to fund a Mars-cycler scholarship. No one finds this precocious; children treat planetary engineering as a hobby the way earlier eras built model railways. You smile, remembering your grandmother’s horror stories of school worksheets and lockdown drills. Those museums still exist, behind glass.

After judgment duty you board the elevator to the Skydeck, twenty hectares of rooftop park that caps the riverfront district. Drone taxis whirr along magnetic rails, ferrying people who need the speed—midwives, disaster gamers, travellers to the space-elevator terminus two ranges over. You walk. Pollinator drones the size of dragonflies orbit your head, collecting pollen prints from your hair: citizen microbiome sequencing. Somewhere in the cloud, algorithms watch for emergent pathogens; if a novel virus is detected, fabricators in every condo will begin printing vaccines tailored to the local population within six hours. The 2027 “Valencia sniffle” never made it past the transport layer.

In a bamboo grove, benches have been carved by residents who died last century. Their volunteers programmed benches to grow a ring for every year the deceased would have aged. Sitting, you open your wrist-notebook to draft a poem. The text surface is cultured e-skin, transparent until touched; when your fingers brush it, words appear like frost. You are not a professional writer—no one must be. The city’s culture treats art as an infrastructural good: paintings are printed into tunnel tiles, lullabies are encoded in streetlight flicker, every citizen is granted a quarterly “creative draw” of pigments, gene-cassettes, or rare metals to turn into whatever they wish. Surplus craft is taxed at 30 % into the common material pool, enough to discourage speculative hoarding but not generosity.

Dusk gathers. Soft bells announce the dusk-books: literature projected onto fog at the river bend. Tonight it is a short story about the last landfill, written by someone whose day job is tending oyster reefs. Children gather on blankets; parents sprawl; drones dim their lights so the narrative can take centre stage. The story ends with the line, “We were rich when we learned to digest our past.” Applause ripples, mostly human, a little synthetic from the companion bots who accompany the elderly.

You cycle home along the bioluminescent path. Pedal pressure harvests a trickle of current that powers the surface lights, so every stroke paints cyan spirals under your wheels. The night air smells of jasmine and ozone. Tomorrow you will help a neighbour assemble a still for essential oils; the next day you will log an hour aboard the orbital-radiation monitoring array; the day after you might do nothing but lie in a hammock and read. You are not special in Verdigris, and that is the point: the city has engineered extraordinariness out of the statistical tail and into the baseline.

Back in bed, the apartment dims to a starfield projected overhead. Real-time satellite feeds mosaic the ceiling; shooting stars are highlighted by faint green halos so you do not miss them. You think of your great-grandparents who feared two futures: machine tyranny or ecological collapse. Verdigris chose a third arc—democratic deliberation slowed things down just enough for wisdom to catch up with power. Humans stayed in the loop, not because AI was weak, but because the city was designed to treasure attention as the scarcest good. Every citizen holds a tiny sliver of sovereignty—over trams, budgets, boreholes, quilts, bench rings—multiplying vigilance to mesh fineness. The constitution fits on a single page: “No intelligence, carbon or silicon, shall make a decision that irreversibly affects a human, without a human having chosen to attend.”

The cup on the sill is now cool, the coffee gone. Outside, the algae ponds switch to night pulse, drawing carbon into carbohydrate. Inside, your heartbeat slows. In this city the night is not something to survive until markets reopen; it is a collective respiration, the whole polity inhaling possibility. Somewhere a newborn learns what century she may expect to see; somewhere a 130-year-old takes his first unassisted jog in decades. The models predict 3 % chance of hurricane strength winds by morning—high enough to matter, low enough to sleep. You close your eyes. The Republic of Verdigris keeps computing, gardening, arguing, singing, repairing, remembering. It is not utopia because it is finished; it is utopia because it has agreed never to be finished, only ever to be caretaking the infinite game.
