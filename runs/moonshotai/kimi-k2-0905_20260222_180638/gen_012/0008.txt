UTOPIA COMPOSITA  
Field Notes from the Breathing Parliament, 2187–2197  

I. Dawn: the city inhales through every porch  
The alarm is a shared pulse. At 05:47 a coral-mycelium conduit under the floorboards contracts, drawing yesterday’s quarrels down into the root-net where they are trimmed, re-nucleotided, and exhaled as citrus-scented oxygen through the bedroom vent. You wake tasting the exact moment your grandmother forgave her own hands for arthritis. The flavour is delivered by a copper coin that materialised on your tongue; it will dissolve within nine minutes, but the memory will stay lipid-soluble for the rest of your life.  
Citizens call the experience “lungmail.” Nobody can opt out; nobody wants to, because the message is always a reconciliation older than you are.  

II. Renovation Credits & Chronostitching: how long should a body last?  
Every child starts with 120 “credits” that can be spent to regrow organs, joints or skin. The wallet is embedded in the clavicle; to spend you must place your palm on another person’s rib and state the need out loud. The other may refuse, but refusal costs them one Breaths. Result: 94 % of credits are still unspent at death. People like the gentle erosion of knuckles, the soft topography of a lived-in face.  
Yet age itself is fluid. Once a year the Holding stirs its communal stew and you may ladle yourself into any age for three days. Chronostitching is taxed: you must return the borrowed years plus one memory that did not exist before. Last spring I exchanged two future winters for one afternoon at age seven so I could run fast enough to remember what calluses feel like before they harden into identity.  

III. Currency Triad  
1. Breath – one litre of air that has balanced inside four kingdoms of life; stored in gourds that photosynthesise while you walk.  
2. Pollen – colour-coded by plant and emotion; wages for bees, architects, therapists.  
3. Reverb – distance measured in footsteps you agree to forget. When the echo is gone you arrive.  
This triad makes extraction impossible: you can only travel by becoming lighter in the exact place you stand. Airlines dissolved peacefully in 2098 when children refused to donate the sound of take-off.  

IV. Kitchen as Quantum Congress  
The printer does not ask what you want; it asks who is missing from the table. You list:  
- The glacier that will calve tomorrow  
- Your unborn great-grandaughter  
- A 19 Hz humpback lullaby  
- The second verse of a lullaby citywide amnesia keeps erasing  
- Half the smell of possibility (0.5 entity)  
The dumpling extruded contains 2 200 nutritionally-complete calories but also a Binding Pact: eat and you promise to attend the calving ceremony; refuse and the dumpling follows you on six mechanical legs whistling your own hunger until you consent. 97 % compliance.  

V. Work = Stretching  
The labour ledger is a list of collective incompetences: we cannot teach magpies to share; we cannot keep river reeds from kinking; we cannot finish the sentence “The moon will stay if…”. You sign up for a Stretch the way sailors once signed for storms. My current assignment is to build a staircase that forgets gravity every seventh step, then remembers mid-stride so climbers laugh themselves upward. I have failed for six weeks; the tremor in my wrist is now the unit of pay. When I succeed the staircase will pay me in Lumens – light that remembers it was once carbon dioxide in a stranger’s sigh – and the city will install a window somewhere I have never been.  

VI. Houses that divorce, marry, then seed  
My apartment is turning fifteen – adolescent. Last night the living-room wall grew a second doorway that opens onto a river I have not yet learned to pronounce. The house offers a choice: split along the graphene seam and carry half away, or swallow a Humility Pill that dissolves ego crust for 365 days. We negotiate. Final contract: the house will keep a diary of every harsh syllable; on our anniversary it will read the entries aloud unless I replace each one with a lullaby. The agreement is countersigned by a raccoon union rep who demands that all streetlights wink once at 02:07 to prove they remain capable of surprise.  

VII. Governance in three chambers  
1. Parliament of Lungs – citizens exhale onto a diaphragm-floor; warmth is weighted by the number of public errors you have named.  
2. Chamber of Skin – statutes painted in lemon-juice and charcoal on bare torsos; after-images measured under midday sun.  
3. Chamber of Forgetting – any law that cannot survive retelling by a nine-year-old who skips the boring parts is fermented into next year’s wedding cider.  
Children under fifteen are translucent; their votes burn hottest, so they coat themselves in SPF distilled from adult hubris.  

VIII. Education: Uncertainty Aquarium & Unlearning Dives  
At thirteen you descend into glowing fluid whose brightness equals your unasked questions, accompanied by an elder who has already died once. Their role is to forget crucial facts at strategic moments, forcing you to decide whether to rescue them. Curiosity is measured in metres of ascent; graduate when you can teach the fluid something it did not know. Last week a boy instructed the aquarium in the taste of Tuesday; since then Tuesdays arrive with faint salt and ginger, improving breakfast city-wide.  
After formal school ends at twelve, doors materialise inside convictions: you step through a greenhouse where tomatoes grow in the shape of quadratic equations and learn calculus by pruning leaves whose area under the curve equals the rate at which you undervalue your- self.  

IX. Care Pentads & the Geometry of Water  
Every citizen belongs to a quintet:  
- An elder who has died once  
- A child who has not yet forgiven gravity  
- A non-human who speaks your mother tongue better than you  
- A stranger chosen by lot yesterday  
- Your shadow at noon, weighed in grams of certainty  
If your shadow becomes too heavy with certainty, you lose the ability to be misunderstood; you must host a potluck where every guest politely disagrees until the food still digests.  

X. Justice: Shadow Atelier with Otter Tally  
Offenders rehearse every perspective they dismissed. Children watch matinees and vote with paper airplanes: red = “keep playing the ash,” white = “let them become bread.” Otters tally the colours because otters are incapable of schadenfreude. Repeat offence dropped 38 % since red paper was replaced by papier-mâché that must be carried on the offender’s back until it dissolves from genuine humidity, not sweat.  

XI. Death: optional, grief: mandatory  
When you choose to conclude your story we hold a Vanishing. The body is wrapped in paper printed with Unfinished Lines it inspired and the names of every species that will miss it. We set the bundle on the River of Unsaid Thank-Yous. If the paper dissolves within sight you become a public ancestor; if it floats out of view, a private myth. Either way we spend one year inventing rituals to keep you alive in ways you never had time to explore. My grandmother became a calendar of unauthorised weather: every time it rains while the sun shines we say, “Ah, there she is, arguing with the sky again.”  

XII. The 12-Minute Solstice & the Waltz of Dispersal  
Every Wednesday at 11:11 everything powers down—apps, grudges, traffic lights that think they’re philosophers. Citizens practice “civilization maintenance”: review carbon flux, kindergarten satisfaction, open-source antibiotic recipes. For your attention you earn tokens negligible in value but infinite in dignity.  
Every ninth year the Waltz begins: every person walks 1 000 km in a spiral calculated so that by autumn humanity’s average density drops to one person per square kilometre. You sleep in strangers’ folding houses, teach one thing, learn one thing. The walk ends when orbital lidar detects the global heartbeat synchronised at 60 bpm. Lonely landscapes become paragraphs you have not yet memorised; the road keeps rewriting the book.  

XIII. Technology Fox Test  
A device must be abandonable in a forest for one hundred years without the forest becoming poorer. Solar panels passed because moss learned to grow under them; smartphones failed until they were redesigned to disassemble into owl pellets. My pocketknife is a Guest-Seed; when I die it will become manganese for the blueberry slope. Until then it stores a microscopic library of poems readable only with pollen from a disappearing orchid. Every time I sharpen the blade I lose a stanza and gain a scar that smells faintly of libraries.  

XIV. Salmon Parliament & the Reversion Key  
Each spawning season the river temperature changes 0.3°C to vote: shall we gift 1 001 salmon to children who have never tasted one except as story? If a child eats, the salmon dies; if not, it returns transformed into data for lunar gene-banks. Either way the children learn consequence without cruelty.  

XV. Night: dreaming in public  
Streetlights dim to the inverse of lullabies; if no one sings, the dark is complete and people navigate by echolocation of neighbourly breathing. The city practices dreaming aloud: a software engineer hums “Blue Moon” in Basque; a nurse answers in humpback. Lights settle on bruised violet, warm enough for moths but cool enough for secrets. Before sleep you place yesterday’s argument in a corner, water it with leftover Lumens, and go to bed inside a question that will germinate before dawn:  

“If we keep practicing Tuesday until it becomes muscle memory, will we remember to forget quickly enough to stay human, or will the city grow lungs big enough to breathe for us?”  

This is not paradise; this is Composita – a place that survives by remaining curious about its own ending, composting every certainty the instant before it becomes dogma, and waking each morning lighter by the exact weight of one forgiven yesterday.
