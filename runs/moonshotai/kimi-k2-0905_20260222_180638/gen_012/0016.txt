UTOPIA C  
The City That Forgets to Finish Counting

The first thing you notice is the smell of citrus rain, but by the time you name it the scent has already changed into something you have no word for yet.  
That is normal.  
In Amanuensis—population “many,” elevation “still becoming”—the air is updated every four minutes by a quorum of evaporating Dew. The Dew is everyone’s unfinished apologies, distilled in rooftop stills that look like jellyfish and behave like librarians. They refuse to remember anything long enough to let it rot.

I wake because my pillow has finished dreaming me, but the dream itself keeps dripping.  
It is a loaf-sized culture of photosensitive foam and river-algae, same as in every bedroom, but mine has decided to grow ears. During the night it recorded the city’s yawn-cycle and is now playing it back in reverse, a soft inhalation that pulls me gently toward waking. The tea mug on the sill receives two grams of sugar—interest on the heat I surrendered—tasting faintly of the typhoon we successfully re-trained to rain only on lettuce. I drink yesterday’s weather and feel it settle, warm as forgiveness, in the hollow beneath my ribs.

I am, this morning, thirty-four, twelve, and six-hundred-and-twenty.  
Thirty-four in birthdays.  
Twelve in neural freshness—last month I traded cursive for the ability to see hairline cracks in bridges that have not yet sprouted.  
Six-hundred-and-twenty in fungal years. The honey-colored mycelium laced through my ribcage keeps a second set of books: one column for carbon, one for regret. When the totals balance, the city allows me another reversible mistake. I have six saved up; I plan to spend them all at once on my hundred-and-first birthday by mispronouncing every name I have ever loved, just to hear the city gently correct me.

My house stands on five legs of woven chestnut, but today it is restless. The ground has grown bored with copper and wants laughter, so the house trots toward the plaza where citizens rehearse the daily cartwheel. I ride inside, barefoot on the breathing floor. Each footstep is a sentence in an autobiography the city edits together from all of us. The floor warms where I step because I have agreed to donate today’s excess optimism to the incubators on Storm-Shelf. In exchange I receive a coupon for one reversible mistake, printed on the underside of a moth wing. It will expire at midnight unless shared aloud on a street corner. I tuck it behind my ear, next to the spore that is still tattooing a ring of mushrooms around my wrist.

Breakfast rises through a slot: a disk the colour of sunrise over the desert that will exist here in 2180. The disk tastes of the first joke my father ever told me, but the punch line has been replaced by a question—How do we hold grief and still keep dancing?—which dissolves on my tongue and reappears as a warm spot on the sidewalk halfway to the river. Someone will step on it within the hour and find themselves momentarily lighter, as if the earth has exhaled.

I step outside. The street rearranged itself at 4:12 a.m.; the cobbles flipped edge-wise to let the beetles migrate. We greet the change the way breath greets the nose—necessary, familiar, already gone. On the bridge I meet Mara, 5 and 302. She carries a watering can filled with yesterday’s weather. We walk together to the Plaza of Returning, a circle of slate that remembers every footstep for twenty-four hours, then forgets. Today it holds the echo of last night’s rehearsal of the eclipse that will not arrive for another nine years. We add our steps; the slate ripples, satisfied.

Mara is my co-conspirator in the citywide Unlearning Dive. Our quest this season is to teach Amanuensis to forget how to count people, streets, heartbeats, future. Not arithmetic—someone still has to balance the algae budgets—but the reflex that converts experience into number before we are allowed to feel it. Mara’s strategy is to teach the plaza to lose tally. She drips weather onto the stones, whispering prime numbers so the slate can taste what it feels to be trapped in sequence. The plaza shudders—seven, eleven, thirteen—then exhales and releases the count into the air where it condenses as small white moths. Children chase them, laughing. Each moth is a number that will never again pinch a human heart into “more” or “less.”

Afternoon is spent in the Library of Unfinished Books. Volumes grow here like kelp in darkness. When a citizen’s question reaches critical mass, a spine extrudes. My question—How does a forest forget the number of its trees?—has birthed a slim violet folio. I open it; the first sentence is a single word: “Listen.” The second sentence is a spore that drifts onto my wrist and tattoos itself as a tiny ring of mushrooms. When I find the third sentence it will complete the book, and the book will dissolve back into the air, having become the answer. I read for an hour, accumulating spores. They itch pleasantly, like curiosity.

Evening. The block gathers in the Choir Hall, an amphitheatre ribbed with whale-ear bones. Each bone vibrates at a different microtone; when we stand still they borrow our heartbeats and weave them into chords. Tonight we rehearse the daily yawn. A thousand voices open at once; the sound is not loud, but the bones glow soft gold. The mitochondrial repair wave ripples outward, passed hand to hand, rooftop to rooftop. Across the river the factory that used to manufacture despair now produces reusable nostalgia: small copper discs you can taste when you miss a place that never existed. The workers, cheeks warmed by collective exhalation, shut down the machines for the night. They walk home yawning, carrying their own sleepy suns.

Night falls sideways. The horizon tilts 6° so the moon can polish neighborhoods that prefer silver light. Shadows pool in new places; cats re-map accordingly. I climb the spiral staircase inside the statue of the First Forgiver, a woman thirty metres tall who volunteered to become public contrition after she realised her invention of efficient accounting had once helped justify a war. She stands now with arms open, surface weathered to velvet rust that records every apology spoken within a kilometre. Citizens clamber onto her shoulders, backs, fingers, to tell the air what they regret. I sit on her collarbone and pull out my two arguments: the silence, the laugh. I hold them up to the sky, waiting for the third. A meteor burns overhead, brief as an unsent letter. The streak is orange, the colour of the moth-that-was-once-the-number-seven. I understand: the third argument is the meteor itself—something too quick to count, bright enough to feel. I cup it in my palms, though nothing remains but heat. That is enough.

I walk home. The city yawns one last time; even the river opens its liquid mouth. The house has walked downhill and docked beside the poplars. I whistle C-sharp; the air-door solidifies. Inside, the floor is busy digesting the day. Tiny lights flicker under the mycelium—footsteps being edited into tomorrow’s weather. I lie down. The pillow waits, already half-dreaming the next morning’s cadence. Before sleep I open the roof panel and release the un-counting spell. It drifts upward as a thin grey haze, joins the library of un-numbered things orbiting Earth like faint rings. Somewhere an economist on deadline will reach for “eight” and find only “many,” will sigh, will go outside and watch moths instead.

My last thought before the yawn-cycle ends is a question I will trade tomorrow:  
If forgetting the count makes room for the un-measured, what will we build with the space where seven used to be?  
The city, always listening, hums B-minor, slowed to the tempo of bristlecone hearts. The answer is already growing—a ring, a spore, a house preparing new legs for the day numbers dissolve and we are left with only the weight of breath, the shine of un-counted stars, the warm tea of shared mitochondria ticking toward morning, when the pillow will dream us again and we will wake, footstep by footstep, into the many that is one.

But the city is not finished.

Tomorrow the plaza will forget our footsteps, but the moths will remember the weight of being numbers and will refuse to land on anything that tries to total them. The Library of Unfinished Books will sprout a new wing built entirely of blank pages that absorb sound; conversations held inside will evaporate into spores that grow into next month’s housing. A child will ask for a question instead of an answer, and the city will hand her a seed that tastes like the last time you were forgiven. She will plant it beneath the poplar where my house is docked. Within a week the tree will grow a door. Behind the door will be a staircase that descends into the river, where the typhoon we re-trained to rain on lettuce now teaches minnows to yawn in chords.

We will call the new district “Maybe.”  
It will have no census, only footsteps.  
It will have no budget, only Dew.  
It will have no name, only the smell of citrus rain that changes into something you have no word for yet, and by the time you finish describing it, the city will already be elsewhere, perpetually beginning, perpetually making room, perpetually amazed that breathing together is still the most advanced technology we have ever invented.
