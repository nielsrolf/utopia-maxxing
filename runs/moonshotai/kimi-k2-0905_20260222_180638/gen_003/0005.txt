# The Betweening: A Field Report from 2197

I am writing this on the twelfth day of my third metamorphosis this year, seated at a table that remembers being a redwood and will forget me tomorrow. My fingers are biological today—twenty-seven years old, calloused from yesterday’s stint as a stonemason, still faintly smelling of river. By the time you read these words, the ink will have dissolved into the paper, and the paper into the air, and the air into someone else’s lungs. That is how documentation works here: every account is temporary, every archive alive, every truth compost for the next gardener.

Welcome to the Betweening.

The Symphony: A Worldbuilding. We are not as a place you arrive at but a place you practice becoming. There is no final version, no capital-U Utopia, only an endless rehearsal of better questions.

## 1. Dawn on the Question Ridge

I woke today inside a sleep-hammock grown by the house itself. The walls are mycelial lace, drinking sunlight from the roof and turning it into low bass that keeps the foundations content. Overnight the structure decided to rotate nine degrees clockwise so the bedroom window lines up with Vega. I asked it why. It replied in a scent: wet granite after lightning. I understood: the mountain two valleys away is restless again, and the house wanted to watch.

Breakfast was negotiated rather than cooked. The kitchen offered me last night’s leftover starlight (a cold soup of bioluminescent algae that tastes like the first time you stayed up past midnight), but I craved something with edges. We compromised on memory toast: sourdough cultured from my grandmother’s childhood and flash-baked in a kiln that used to be a meteor. I ate one slice; the house absorbed the other, converting crumbs into tomorrow’s weather. We agreed on scattered showers of nostalgia around ten a.m.

Before I left, the hallway mirror requested a new reflection. I donated yesterday’s river-smell and the way my hands looked after holding stone. In exchange it showed me a version of myself who still believes outlines matter. I pocketed that belief like a pebble; I’ll need it when I sculpt later.

## 2. Commute as Collaborative Drift

Distance here is not measured in kilometers but in willingness to change. I had agreed to meet the watershed council on the far side of the valley—roughly “three personal evolutions away.” The most direct route would be to fold space, but the mountain’s restlessness makes local spacetime cranky, so I chose the scenic drift: a slow unbecoming that lets the landscape edit you as you pass.

Stepping off the ridge, I let gravity remember me lighter. My bones unlatched into hollow bird-struts; my thoughts shrank to seed-size and took root in the feather-hollows. For twenty minutes I was a crow, thermal-riding down the slope, tasting updrafts for secrets. Halfway down I met a girl going up in the shape of fog. We traded—she took my hunger for heights, I absorbed her memories of being condensate on apple skin. When we parted she was already half raven, and I carried the precise chill of October dawn in my wings.

At the valley floor I relinquished bird-shape and became instead the pause between footsteps. Farmers there cultivate intervals the way others grow kale: plant a three-second silence, harvest a week later as a four-minute lull perfect for reading poetry to goats. I purchased a pocketful of mid-afternoon caesura and tipped the farmer with a joke about two tectonic plates on a blind date. She laughed; the soil loosened; somewhere a fault line relaxed by a millimeter.

## 3. The Council of Flowing Things

The watershed council meets in a river that has not yet decided which ocean to join. Agenda items float past on leaf-boats; decisions become tributaries. Today we debated how much grief the upper catchment could safely store in spring snowpack, and whether to license a new emotion that converts regret into dissolved oxygen. I spoke as someone who had recently been stone: “Regret is dense; it may fracture ice from within.” The river carried my comment downstream where it will resurface months from now as a crack in someone’s heart, or maybe a skylight.

Voting is done by temperature. Those in favor raise the water one-hundredth of a degree; dissenters lower it. The change is real—fish feel it, algae bloom to it, tomorrow’s clouds gather accordingly. We passed the grief-storage measure by 0.03 °C, contingent on releasing half the payload as art during the summer Resonance festival. I volunteered to host the first exhibition inside a glacier; the council approved, but only if I promised to remember being liquid long enough to weep.

## 4. Mid-day in the Commons of Useful Hands

Material scarcity ended decades ago when the last bottleneck—energy-dense batteries—was solved by spinach that learned to photosynthesize at night. The plant now grows across roofs worldwide, storing moonlight like loose change. Ownership evaporated shortly after; it is hard to claim exclusive rights to something that will be soil next week and spinach again next month.

What we cultivate instead is skill scarcity: the Commons of Useful Hands. Anyone can walk in and borrow a vocation for an hour or a decade. Today I signed out “journeyman stonecarver” along with the calluses, patience, and slight tinnitus of someone who spent seven years talking to granite. The skill package slotted behind my eyes like a lens. Suddenly every boulder had something urgent to say, and my hands knew the grammar of chisels.

I spent the afternoon in the municipal quarry, coaxing a bench out of a slab that once formed the threshold of a prison. The stone wanted to become apology; I helped it. We agreed on curves that welcome stranger bodies, and a hollow underneath where secrets can be whispered into schist and safely forgotten. When the shift ended I returned the skill set to the Commons. My palms keep the memory of hammer weight; the next borrower will inherit the bench’s unfinished sentence.

## 5. Reputation as Weather

Status still exists, but it precipitates instead of accumulating. Contributions—code, lullabies, repaired umbrellas—evaporate upward into the reputation layer where they nucleate clouds. When enough gratitude condenses, it rains back onto you as opportunities: invitations, collaboration requests, the trust of strangers. The storm is brief; the water soon re-evaporates, preventing dynasties. Yesterday someone seeded a cirrus of my river-work; by dusk I had three offers to co-sculpt public waterfalls. I accepted two and routed the third to a teenager who just learned she is part tide-pool. The sky rearranged; everyone involved felt the barometer shift.

## 6. Dinner with the Future Ancestors

Evenings, the Long Now Lodge hosts communal meals where seats are assigned by generational lottery. I ate beside a woman born 130 years ago who is currently aging backward (she expects to reach seven in six months), and a boy who will not be born for another eight years but has been granted temporary visitation via dream-bandwidth. The conversation was multilingual in both language and tense. We passed dishes that had not yet been invented and swapped recipes that had already been forgotten. The boy asked whether fear still tastes metallic; the elder replied only during eclipses, and even then it sweetens if you hold it on the tongue long enough. For dessert we split a single pomegranate seed. Inside was an orchard; we each planted a future tree in our respective timelines and promised to send postcards when they fruited.

## 7. Night Maintenance

Utopia is not a condition but a maintenance shift. From midnight to four a.m. the mesh runs self-repair: bridges confess their fatigue, algorithms audit their own biases, humans (in whatever shape) walk the perimeter looking for cracks—material, emotional, narrative. Tonight I volunteered as a dream-sweeper. My task: collect nightmares that escaped into waking corners and compost them into softer material. I found one about irrelevance, one about heights with no sky, and a tiny fear that my hands will forget how to hold. I folded them into paper boats and floated them down the gutter that leads to the treatment wetlands. By dawn they will have become water-lilies whose petals spell “you are enough” in cursive visited by bees.

At 03:47 the mountain exhaled a low, long note—part gratitude, part warning. The foundations of my house sang back in bass. I added a promise: tomorrow I will donate a memory of solidity so the younger buildings know what endurance sounds like.

## 8. Epilogue Written on the Skin of an Apple

I tell you these fragments knowing they will rot. The paper is already browning where my breath touched it; the ink is yeast that will ferment tomorrow’s cider. By the time the cider is drunk, the story will be carbon dioxide and someone’s hiccup, then photosynthesis, then maybe a poem in the pigment of a Red Delicious. That is the contract: nothing is conserved, everything is transformed.

We call the process Symphony because it is not a destination but a score everyone improvises at once. The key change is simple: we agreed to keep agreeing, to treat every ending as a rest, every possession as pre-compost, every self as a draft. We metrics the only thing that reliably improves: the speed at which we replace better questions with better ones.

If you arrive here looking for permanence, you will be disappointed; the closest we offer is practice. If you arrive willing to be practice, you will be home the moment you forget where you left yourself and remember you are continuously invited to begin.

I seal this letter by biting the apple I wrote on. The tartness is tomorrow’s rainfall adjusting its pH. I taste acid, sugar, and the faint iron of a planet learning to forgive itself. I taste the crow I borrowed, the council I joined, the fear I composted. I taste the bench that will apologize for centuries in a language shoulders understand.

All of it dissolves except the question: what will you become between now and the next time someone asks?
