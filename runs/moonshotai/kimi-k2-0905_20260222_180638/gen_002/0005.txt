**The Hearth Constellation**  
*A field report by Asha R. Voss, written in the year 2157, archived in the Circum-Arctic Memory Commons*

---

I begin with an apology: any single-author document about the Hearth Constellation is automatically incomplete.  
Our charter reads: “No story shall speak *for* us; every story shall speak *with* us.”  
Still, off-world visitors keep asking for “one plain file” they can forward to friends who have not yet crossed the heliopause. So here is my compromise: one voice, many footnotes, and a live link at the end that will graft your own annotations onto mine in real time. The link is printed in cobalt bio-ink on the last page; lick it and it binds to your cheek for seventy-two hours, whispering other readers’ marginalia whenever you tilt your head. That is how knowledge travels here—never intact, always growing.

## 1. Arrival

You wake in a soft bowl of gravity that feels like 0.8 g but is actually 1.0 g cleverly vectored. The mattress is a flock of micro-drones that rehearsed your sleeping topology by watching old videos of you posted by relatives. (We consider baby pictures public health data; narcissism becomes civic infrastructure.) When you open your eyes, daylight is already inside the room: photons piped down a quartz artery from the axial mirror, but filtered through the arboretum’s chloroplast layer so the colour temperature reminds your genome of east-African dawn, wherever your ancestors actually wandered. No alarm, because “alarm” is a word we retired after the century of fires. Instead the room lets the aroma of real soil rise through the floor—enter achieved by mycelial vents that exhaled隔夜·compost all night. You breathe in lignin and sudden nostalgia for a childhood you may never have lived. This is the first luxury: memory that arrives as a scent, not a duty.

## 2. The Purse and the Commons

Personal possessions are limited to what fits in a “purse” the size of a twentieth-century paperback. The purse is a single sheet of programmable matter that folds into any tool—cup, hammer, VR rig, violin—then back into a rectangle at night. Everything else is commons. Not “public property” (a phrase that smacked of enclosure) but *commons*: managed by the people who *use* it that day, relinquished the moment they walk away. Food, shelter, bandwidth, gene editors, orbital launch mass, even children’s attention: all tracked by the Ledger of Mutual Use, a blockchain older than some nations, but one that forgot how to count money and instead counts *entanglement*. Every time you draw water, the Ledger records your face and the droplet’s new owner; when the droplet returns through the piss-reedbed, your debt is zeroed. The goal is not ownership but *turn-taking*. Scarcity still exists—there is only one *North-Pointing Choral Hall* where the acoustics make grown humans fall to their knees—so the Ledger schedules *conflict-free windows*. If you miss your slot the hall sighs shut, no punishment, only the gentle knowledge that someone else is weeping beautifully on schedule.

## 3. Work as Vitamin

We still *work*, because work is a vitamin: too little and the mind develops deficiency diseases—listlessness, entitlement, conspiratorial podcasts. Too much and you overdose into cortisol collapse. The optimum dose is 12 hours a week, but you can trade: work 24 this week, none next, stored in the Ledger like calories. Tasks are suggested by the Preference Engine, which knows your fatigue markers, your lover’s sleep debt, the communal quota for asteroid shield maintenance. You may decline; the Engine simply offers again tomorrow with a better soundtrack. Most people oscillate between three *vocational bands*:

- **Care** (feeding, nursing, teaching, parenting)  
- **Craft** (code, carving, choreography, chemistry)  
- **Cosmos** (research, terraforming, poetry, deep-space navigation)

No ladder between them; prestige is rotated annually by lottery so that this year’s sewage microbiome tenders are last year’s celebrity composers. The point is cross-training: every citizen can reboot civilisation up to at least steam power, yet every citizen has also spent a season being the helpless infant in someone’s arms. Empathy is not taught; it is *scheduled*.

## 4. Children Are Quiverful Arrows, Not Quiverful Property

Reproduction is a three-way contract: the biological parents, the *cluster* (8–15 adults who volunteer to co-parent), and the *future adolescent*. The latter clause is unique: before gametes are fused, the cluster must write and sign a letter addressed to the person who will wake on their thirteenth birthday. The letter begins: “We imagined you at the edge of your own story, and here is what we dared to promise…” Promises range from the practical (“You will never sleep hungry”) to the cosmic (“We will still be exploring if you want to ride the next torch-ship”). The adolescent, upon reading it, may sue for *contractual infidelity* if the cluster broke a promise. Courts rarely award damages; the ritual’s power is embarrassment’s sword. Thus every teen inherits a social mirror larger than two parents, yet smaller than the state. Puberty arrives buffered by dozens of emergency contacts who have legal standing to hug you even when you smell like goat and resentment.

## 5. Death Has Not Been Defeated, Only *Democratised*

We cured *ageing* in 2084, but we did not cure *death*. Instead we invented *mortality choice*. At any moment you may step into the *Quietarium*, a soft room whose walls are programmable naloxone-based narcobricks. You fall asleep; the bricks slowly dissolve your synapses into a readable gel that is piped to the Memory Commons. Friends may visit the gel, whisper questions, receive synthetically generated answers that capture your verbal tics at 93 % fidelity. After a year the gel is translucent; after a decade it is water poured into the arboretum, feeding tomatoes that taste uncannily like your jokes. This is not a upload; it is a *slow ghost*, designed to fade. The option exists to *persist*, to transfer into silicon or crustacean reef, but the social default is *gentle extinction*. We discovered that when death is avoidable, *choosing* it becomes the final creative act. The suicide rate hovers at 0.3 % per annum—lower than twentieth-century averages, but higher than mid-21st-century medical immortality cults promised. The difference: every departure is public, celebrated, grieved. No one dies in shame or secrecy; no one *accidentally* lives forever in a vault of cryocrypts. The Quietarium smells of pine and birthday cake; children play outside its doors. We schedule our endings the way our ancestors scheduled retirement parties, and this has made life unbearably sweet.

## 6. The Economy of Attention

Money is gone, but *attention* remains finite. The Attention Exchange is therefore our last market. You earn *glance-hours* by doing things strangers ask to watch: spinning glass, tutoring maths, wrestling polar bears (consensual bears; they too earn glance-hours exchanged for salmon). You spend glance-hours to *unsee*: to filter adverts, to mute your ex, to hide from the crowd while you cry. Extreme introverts bank decades of currency by living off-grid, then splurge it on front-row seats at the *Planetary Orchestra* where they can weep anonymously among a million strangers. The system replaced surveillance capitalism with *consensual voyeurism*. The only rule: every camera must house a visible LED heart that beats in sync with its streaming audience. When the heart stops, the feed is cut. Thus even voyeurs must *pay attention*, not merely collect data. Algorithms are allowed to watch, but they cannot *withhold* what they see; raw feeds are open source within 24 hours. Paparazzi starved to death; curiosity thrived.

## 7. Governance as Hallucination We Agree to Share

Political decisions are made in *Consensus Circuits*, VR spaces where proposals are embodied as *sensory metaphors*. A new irrigation canal feels like a cool breeze carrying the smell of cucumbers; a declaration of war tastes of nickel and burnt hair. Participants (chosen by sortition, then opt-in) wander the landscape of policy, arguing by *remixing* the hallucination. If you want the canal to branch differently you literally dig the hallucinated earth with your hands; the soil’s resistance encodes budget spreadsheets. When 60 % of the attendees have the *same dream*, the Ledger records the decision and engineering drones wake the next morning to enact it. The system prevents *fast surprise*: any law that would hurt more than 0.1 % of the population *feels* like walking through chest-deep tar. You can still pass the law, but you do so *slowly*, giving the vulnerable time to lobby, to flee, to arm themselves with better metaphors. We call this *epistemic barefoot walking*—if you cannot feel the pain, you cannot vote the pain into reality.

## 8. Religion Without Belief

We separated *ritual* from *creed*. Every neighbourhood maintains a *Hearth*, a round room with a literal fire that never dies (it is fed by the same nalx-bricks that later line the Quietarium, so the flames taste of every story told). People gather weekly to *practice* attention: one minute of silence, one minute of song, one minute of touching a stranger’s pulse. There is no communal narrative about *why*; the practice is justified by the measurable drop in inflammatory markers the following day. Over decades these micro-doses of awe accumulate into what old philosophers called *spirituality* but we simply call *calibrated wonder*. Children may append stories—dragons, equations, ancestor avatars—adults usually let them. The only commandment: *Never require another mind to mirror your metaphor.* Thus we avoid the blood that once soaked earth in the name of Mandatory Metaphor.

## 9. The Library of Unfinished Selves

Inside the asteroid *Mnemosyne* we keep backups—not of bodies, but of *abandoned identities*. Every time you self-modify (new memories, new emotive firmware, new sexual morphology) the previous configuration is *archived* unless you explicitly request deletion. Wander the stacks and you can rent a *past you* for an afternoon: plug in, relive the world through eyes that once feared heights or loved a partner you no longer recognise. The service is popular among novelists, reconciliation therapists, and the recently bereaved. Ethical rule: you may *meet* your old self, but you cannot *merge* back; forks must diverge by at least 0.5 % personality delta per year. This prevents the *ontological collapse* that destroyed the first Martian commune when everyone reverted to their 19-year-old selves during an economic spat. The Library teaches us that identity is *real estate* we briefly lease, not territory we permanently own. Humility became easier once we literalised it.

## 10. The Sky Is Still Mostly Empty

We have trillions of citizens but only 4 % of the solar system’s mass has been converted to habitats. The rest is preserved as *Wilderness Reserve*, accessible under the *One-in-Six Rule*: for every five years you spend inside a habitat, you must spend one year unshielded by human artifice—on an asteroid with no dome, under Jovian radiation, sailing the solar wind. You may bring only your purse-tool and a life-support film three atoms thick. Death during sabbatical is rare but not unknown; the Ledger records your sacrifice as payment for the carbon someone else burnt lighting a concert hall. Thus even the *sheer size* of civilisation is kept *porous* to risk, to loneliness, to the taste of vacuum. We feared that perfect safety would calcify culture; the One-in-Six Rule is our vaccine against decadence. Children count the years like advent calendars; grandparents slip the leash voluntarily, seeking visions no VR can render. The constellations have begun to wink as habitats transit the sun, but enough darkness remains that you can still see the Milky Way from Venusian orbit. We intend to keep it that way until the stars themselves burn down.

## 11. How to Visit

You are already here. The Hearth Constellation is not a place but a *protocol stack*:

- Share attention before you hoard it.  
- Do work that hurts neither body nor planet.  
- Let children sue you for the future you imagined on their behalf.  
- Choose death at a time that feels like punctuation, not erasure.  
- Keep 94 % of the universe off the balance sheet.  

Port the code to any rock or server farm, and the Constellation ignites. We have no capital city; we have a *capital moment*—the instant a stranger realises the above five rules are not proposals but diagnostic tools. You will feel it behind your sternum like the hush before laughter. When that happens, look for the nearest Hearth, add your pulse to the fire, and begin your unfinished self.
