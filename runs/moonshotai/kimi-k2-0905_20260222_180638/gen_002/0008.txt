The morning begins when your house infers from your cortisol patterns that you are awake.  
A soft aurora blooms across the ceiling; the air smells faintly of the pine forest you loved as a child.  
You did not have to set any of this. The house learned it from ten thousand small decisions you made since you moved in – and from the city-wide “preference commons” that lets every dwelling benefit from every other resident’s discoveries, anonymised, loophole-audit-logged, and encrypted so that even the Chroniclers cannot decode who gave what signal.

You stand. Your knees, regrown at age ninety-three, feel twenty again. You stretch, glance at the mirror: it shows today’s Four Tasks – not chores imposed by survival, but the four things you yourself scheduled six months ago after your last “life-review retreat.” One reads:

“Co-write one scene of *City 17*, the augmented-reality street opera that will debut tonight on the riverside façades. Your guild-mate Aleph already sketched the choreography.”

No one has to earn rent. The Georgist dividend from the helium-3 harvesters on Luna and the carbon-drawdown forests in Patagonia appears each week in your wallet as a tradable “Earth-share.” The share buys, by default, a middle-class lifestyle: 40 m² of housing, 2200 nutritionally-complete kilo-calories per day, three cubic metres of fabber feedstock, 10 kW of clean energy, and a fractional slot in the planetary compute cloud. Most people spend only a quarter of their share; the surplus accumulates, compounding at two percent, giving everyone a growing endowment to fund babies, bets, or big adventures.

But the dividend is only the floor. Almost everyone also earns “contribution tokens” – a score visible only to you, unless you choose to publish. The tokens convert to Earth-shares at a floating rate, but their real payoff is reputational: they unlock access to interesting teams, early-run prototypes, and, crucially, the right to have children.

You eat breakfast – a mango-pandan crêpe spun by the kitchen printer from atmospheric CO₂ and rooftop algae. While you eat, your cognitive assistant pipes up, *“Three coordination requests await. Priority order?”*

1.  The neighbourhood logistics loop wants your fridge’s excess cooling capacity for the next four hours. (Pay: 0.3 tokens, plus lower vegetable spoilage for everyone.)
2.  The Regional Charter Council invites final comments on a proposal to raise the “conversation speed limit” from 240 to 260 wpm in cafés; old people claim fatigue, teens want faster argumentation. (Participation: 0.8 tokens.)
3.  Your child-mentee, Jun, age 9, has hit a wall in their “probabilistic thinking” badge. You committed to tutor twice a week when you sponsored their embryo. (Pay: nothing, but failure to show would dent the long-term trust metric that affects your breeding quota.)

You thumb-accept 1 and 3; the café debate can proceed without you. Jun’s face pops up, projected mid-air, cheeks smudged with marker ink.

“I keep falling for the Monty-Hall trap,” they groan.  
“Let’s build it with Lego,” you answer, and the breakfast table extrudes translucent blocks. For twenty minutes you walk Jun through repeated trials, letting the empirical frequency hijack their intuitions. When the session ends, Jun’s dashboard upgrades them to yellow-belt; your “genealogical reliability” score ticks up 0.4%. One more kid mentee through probability, the algorithm thinks, and you’ll earn back the third-birth token you spent on your second daughter.

You leave the apartment. Outside, the street is a slow-motion festival. Every building façade is clothed in programmable clay; today the neighbourhood has voted to look like Kyoto during sakura, 1920. The illusion is perfect except for the tell-tale glimmer that keeps colour-blind residents from walking into walls. A drone courier drops a parcel at your feet: the haptic gloves needed for tonight’s opera collaboration. You did not order them; the supply net predicted the need from your rehearsal calendar, cross-checked your social credit, dispatched, and billed your surplus wallet. Price: nine minutes of dividend flow. You nod; the drone logs a micro-tip of reputation to itself and flits off.

There are no cars. The ground layer is pedestrians and skaters; above, clear tubes carry silent mag-lev capsules. Anyone may enter a tube, but occupancy is rationed by dynamic pricing that keeps speed above 40 km/h and congestion below visual-threshold ugliness. You step onto the moving walkway that feeds the tubes; your left shoe vibrates, signalling that the city thanks you for yesterday’s contribution: you debugged an open-source heat-pump controller that will save 0.8 megawatt-hours this winter across 300 buildings. The vibration is the city’s way of smiling at you. No money changed hands; the system simply recorded that you made others’ lives cheaper and cooler, and the city’s embodied algorithms are programmed to make you feel noticed.

At the riverside rehearsal space, eight co-authors gather. None is “in charge.” The opera is governed by a “template charter,” one of several thousand public-domain governance kernels hashed on the Civic Ledger. The charter specifies: decision rights, exit rights, fork rights, revenue splits, dispute-resolution network, and sunset conditions. When Aleph proposes replacing the river-facing electro-luminescent mural with fog drones, anyone may veto; if vetoes exceed 30%, the default is to fork: two premieres tomorrow night, audience members allocating their ticket tokens to the version they prefer. The market for attention thus funds experiments without zero-sum failure.

You contribute a melodic motif generated by your personal AI, trained on a corpus of Sahel blues and Martian minimalist opera. The group tests it; several frown. The rehearsal’s sensor array streams emotional valence data to each participant’s lens; you see your phrase produces mild discord in two baritones. You withdraw it, no shame. Because nobody’s income depends on winning this vote, egos stay sheathed. The session ends with a plan, a schedule, and a micro-contract whose clauses are machine-readable and enforceable by open arbitration bots should anyone renege. No lawyer ever touched it.

Lunch happens in the sky-garden halfway up the Amazonia Tower. The tower is owned by no one: it is a “mutualised asset.” Construction was crowdfunded by selling 50-year usage claims; the claims mature into indefinite usufruct for whoever still inhabits the space, preventing flipping. Maintenance is performed by resident-stewards whose kids inherit the apartment only if they qualify as stewards too – a built-in brake on aristocracy. You eat with two ex-partners and one current; jealousy is not forbidden, but every citizen learns dosage: oxytocin regulation exercises, scheduled solitude, reflective journaling reviewed by licensed peers. Failed relationships still ache, but they no longer carry economic catastrophe or social erasure.

The afternoon is yours. You dive into the ocean of knowledge. Today you choose an “experience school”: you embody the identity of a 14th-century Sienese painter for three hours. Neural lacing stimulates visual cortex with period pigments; haptics simulate gesso cracking under your fingernails. You emerge, dazed, mind littered with ideas for tonight’s set design. No one questions why you study obsolete professions; the civilisational consensus is that insight is combinatorial and no one can pre-judge which shard of human experience will unlock tomorrow’s coordination puzzle.

Evening. The opera debuts. Fog drones paint 80-metre sprites above the water while your gloves translate every audience hand-gesture into live orchestration. Strangers become co-creators; their motion curves remix the harmony in real time. At the coda, the city dims all lights for ten seconds – a ritualised moment of shared darkness that signals, “We are here together, unafraid of nothingness.” Applause is not clapping but coordinated bio-luminescence: 4,000 wristbands flash the Fibonacci sequence in gold. Tokens flow: performers, fog-tech crew, drone pilots, even the river itself (represented by its legal trust) receive instant micro-payments. The opera dissolves by midnight, equipment folding back into storage, riverbank reverting to quiet park. No trash remains; every object is either edible, dissolvable, or tagged for disassembly.

You walk home. A message waits: your elder daughter has declared her intent to conceive. The reproductive licence requires three guarantors who will vouch for emotional and material readiness. Your name is pre-filled; you must countersign within 30 days or publicly decline. You smile, remember her tiny hand gripping your finger, and press ACCEPT.

Before sleep you spend twenty minutes in “civilisation maintenance,” the civic analogue of brushing teeth. You review three randomly assigned public-good reports: carbon flux in the Bay, kindergarten satisfaction scores, and a new open-source antibiotic. Each needs a human eye for anomalies the AIs cannot yet parse. You flag one chart whose error bars feel hinky; the Chroniclers will investigate. For your attention you earn 0.02 tokens – negligible, but that is not the point. The point is that governance is everyone’s crossword puzzle, a shared ritual that prevents power from calcifying into hierarchy.

You lie down. The house dims. Somewhere, a million others perform their own maintenance. The system is not perfect; it merely iterates faster than its own shadow. Tomorrow there will be new edge cases, new temptations to defect, new tragedies of some hidden commons. But the meta-commons – the habit of noticing commons and pricing them before they collapse – has become culture, story, reflex. In the quiet, you recall the sentence printed inside every citizen’s first wallet:

“No one owes you utopia; utopia is a maintenance schedule. Keep the appointment, and the future keeps its appointment with you.”
