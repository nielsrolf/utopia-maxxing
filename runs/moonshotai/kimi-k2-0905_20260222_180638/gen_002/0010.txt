# The Constellation of You

The first thing you notice is the scent of petrichor—real petrichor, not a hotel-lobby simulacrum—because the morning rain has just stopped falling on the garden outside your apartment. The second thing is that your heart is beating faster than usual, and that is deliberate: today is a Challenge Day, and your circadian steward has nudged your adrenal baseline up 7 % so the upcoming climb will feel thrilling rather than trivial. You could override it, of course, but you helped design the protocol, so you leave the spike in place.

You step onto the balcony. The city—officially “Constellation 17,” informally “C-17” to its 1.3 million embodied citizens—spread below you like a lichen of light on the dark valley floor. Two thirds of the buildings are grown from mycelial stone: alive, self-healing, and slightly warm to the touch. Every balcony hosts at least one person waving at someone else, because the architects used Dunbar-optimized street angles: from any residence you can see about 150 other residences, the upper limit of stable human friendship circles. You wave back, even though you do not yet know their names. By evening you probably will.

Breakfast is the first choice of the day. The dispenser offers you five meals assembled by five different sous-chef AIs, each required to submit one dish that is (a) nutritionally complete, (b) within your Challenge budget, and (c) legitimately surprising. You pick the charcoal bao with electric-blue lotus paste because it is the one you are least able to parse by sight. While it steams, you thumb the little ceramic coin on your wrist. A holo blooms: today’s “story contract.”

The contract is the keystone of C-17’s social physics. Every resident is simultaneously an author and a character. Before breakfast you must accept or decline up to three incoming plot invitations. If you accept you are pledged to pursue the narrative in good faith, but you may negotiate stakes, genre, and safety rails. The system prevents overlap fatigue: your brainprint is hashed so no one can draft you into more than one romantic arc per month, one mortal-peril arc per year, or any arc that ends in permanent physical modification without 48-hour sober reflection. Declining carries no social penalty beyond a tiny reputation dip—“flake tokens,” redeemable by running pop-up side quests for strangers.

Today’s offers:

1. “The Cartographer’s Mistake” – A three-day treasure hunt that will require you to learn enough topology to correct an 18th-century map stitched inside your coat lining. Stakes: a bottle of wine that will taste of whatever emotion you are feeling when you uncork it. Genre: whimsical noir. Risk: paper cuts.

2. “The Reverse Graduation” – You must teach a 9-year-old something you yourself do not yet know. If you succeed, the child gifts you a memory she harvested last week from a 94-year-old former trapeze artist. Genre: generational relay. Risk: ego bruise.

3. “The Silence of Bees” – Investigate why the rooftop hives have stopped humming conspiracy codes. Genre: ecological thriller. Risk: bee stings.

You accept 1 and 2; bee stings are too Challenge-Day-on-the-nose. Within seconds you receive an encrypted map fragment and the name of your student: Maia, who is waiting in the greenhouse on Tier 5.

You finish the bao—it tastes like sunrise and your ninth birthday—and leave the apartment. The hallway floor is a shallow canal; carp nibble algae between stepping-stones. Residents voted to replace carpet with water because bare feet are harder to ignore than shoes, and therefore strangers talk to one another more. It works: on the lift you meet Jonah, the cartographer of your story. He hands you the coat and a peppermint smelling faintly of moon rock. “Don’t die,” he says cheerfully. “I need you alive to sign the map at the end.” The lift doors close before you can reply, so instead you grin, and the peppermint tingles with the exact microdose of caffeine required to make your palms tingle back.

## The School without Teachers

Greenhouse Tier 5 is a cathedral of glass and chlorophyll. Maia is eight, possibly nine—the age slider has been set to “kid” rather than “integer,” a deliberate fuzz intended to remind adults that developmental time is not uniform. She is trying to balance a top on the tip of a philodendron leaf.

“Rule is,” she says without looking up, “you have to teach me something you don’t know. So it has to be something we figure out together.”

“That’s the rule,” you agree. “Ideas?”

“Can we break entropy?”

“Probably not, but we can ask why it’s so stubborn.”

They have installed a reversible chalkboard: whatever you write can be un-erased, layered into ghosted time-lapse strata. You sketch a deck of cards being shuffled. Maia explains that her grandmother once told her every shuffle creates an order the universe has never seen. You wonder aloud how many shuffles until every order has happened. Together you derive the logarithm of 52-factorial, discover it is unimaginably larger than the number of atoms in the observable universe, and conclude that grandma was right. Maia claps, delighted. You realize you have learned two things: the proof, and the sound a child makes when infinity clicks into focus.

Her side of the bargain fulfilled, Maia presses a fingertip to your temple. A memory blooms: summer 1953, sawdust under circus lights, the stomach-drop of letting go of the fly-bar. You now know the exact visceral difference between flying and falling. Maia skips away, mission complete. The cartographer’s coat feels heavier, as if weighted by possibility.

## The Climb

Afternoon is for Challenge Day proper. You subway to the city’s edge; the carriage sings in four-part harmony because last year commuters voted to replace automated PA with local a cappella groups. At the terminus the mountain begins: a 1,200-meter cliff grown from coral-like accretion, dyed ochre by iron-fixing bacteria. Routes are recalculated weekly; holds rot and regenerate, so no climb is ever the same. You have elected the “Amateur Natural Philosopher” difficulty: you must identify ten new species on your way up.

Today’s twist is weather. Storm cells are scheduled to sweep the face at 3 p.m.—not dangerous, but slick. You sign the waiver, heart rate rising to the morning’s preset 107 bpm. The first fifty meters are warm-up; you snap photos of a lichen that looks like Tolkien’s Elvish. At meter 200 you discover a spider whose web spells prime numbers in binary dewdrops. You tag it with your finger; AR overlays confirm it is undocumented. Eight more to go.

The storm arrives early. Rain beads on your nano-fleece and rolls off like mercury, but the stone grows slick. You consider retreat, but the coat’s lining whispers topology: every grip is a node in a graph whose edges are possible moves. You begin to see the climb as a proof: you must traverse without repeating an edge, a classical Eulerian path. The insight rekindles momentum. You finish the route with three minutes to spare, having catalogued eleven species (you will later discover one was a prank planted by Jonah, a synthetic plant that quotes Spinoza when stroked). At the summit you uncork the wine. You are flushed with triumph; the wine tastes precisely like that.

## The Economy of Gifts

Dusk means market. C-17 runs on a protocol called GIFT/LEDGER, a hybrid of potlatch and cryptographic accounting. Every resident receives a base stipend of “flow”—energy, computation, fabrication credits—calculated by a cake-cutting algorithm that ensures no coalition feels systematically cheated. Above that, value is tracked but not hoarded. When you give someone a gift, the ledger records not debit and credit but “entanglement”: a directed edge whose weight increases the more personalized the gift. Reputation is simply your graph centrality. Wealth, in the old sense, asymptotes; once you have ten thousand sincere entanglements, adding one more changes almost nothing. The system politely forgets after a century, so dynasties cannot congeal.

You arrive carrying eleven vials of storm-rain you collected on the climb. Each has been spiked with a single molecule engineered to fluoresce a different memory when swallowed under starlight. You trade them for a handcrafted puzzle box, a promise of future baby-sitting, and a story about how the vendor once danced for a queen who lived inside a hollowed-out asteroid. No currency changes hands, but the ledger lights up: three new entanglements, scores recalculated mid-conversation. You feel richer than any billionaire of the fossil era.

## Night’s Multiple Choice

Entertainment is polysynchronic. In the Commons you can join a flesh-and-blood Shakespeare troupe performing Hamlet inside a zero-gravity sphere, watch a flock of engineered ravens reenact the Battle of Trafalgar, or lie in the grass as drones project 8K auroras synced to a string quartet playing on the far side of the planet. You choose none of these. Instead you walk home along the canal-street, where every third cobble is a pressure-plate that triggers a hologram of someone else’s memory—brief, unsolicited gifts from strangers. You step on one: a child learning to swim, the panic-flash of water entering sinuses transmuting into weightless joy. You laugh out loud, and somewhere a stranger hears your laugh folded into their evening.

Back in the apartment you light the puzzle box. It unfolds into a tiny diorama: Charlie’s probe, the one from the old story, coasting between stars. A slip of paper reads: “Destination: Gliese 667Cc. ETA: 800 subjective years. Crew of one human mind running at 0.05× to conserve energy. Would you like to send a reply?” You record a message: the taste of storm-rain wine, the sound of Maia’s clap, the Eulerian path up a wet cliff. The diorama seals itself and launches from your palm—a photonic postcard injected into a 2 W laser link. Somewhere, centuries hence, Charlie will wake for a day, watch your seconds-long burst, and fall asleep again smiling. You feel the entanglement edge form, a fragile thread across light-years.

## Sleep and Succession

Before bed you must make one last decision. The dream editor offers five candidate storylines your subconscious will explore. You pick “The Library of Forgotten Soils,” an experimental narrative assembled by a micro-council of soil scientists and trauma therapists. It promises to let you taste loam from every continent Earth ever wore, while metabolizing a minor anxiety you admitted to the city’s anonymized health stream last week. You set the emotional gain to “mild catharsis,” knowing tomorrow you will wake refreshed and the city’s composters will have received a 0.3 % insight bump on nutrient cycling. Data and dream mulch each other.

As the lights dim your wrist coin vibrates: a system-wide ping. Allocator—descendant of the ancient Friendly AI, now diffused into every strut and petal—asks for voluntary attention. You allow a whisper:

“Congratulations. Gliese probe Charlie-7 reports reception of your packet. Trade protocols updated. Also: tomorrow’s weather will include an unscheduled double rainbow at 09:14. No narrative coercion intended; merely a reminder that stochastic beauty remains free.”

You murmur thanks. The apartment walls bloom with faint starlight, projection mapped from real-time exoplanet surveys. One day, maybe in a century, maybe in a millennium, you will vote to spawn a daughter city around another sun. Perhaps you will walk its canals, coat heavy with alien pollen, teaching a child who has never seen Earth how entropy still yields to wonder. Until then, Earth’s morning rain will suffice.

You close your eyes. The city hums, 150 voices per fractal vista, each life a node in a graph that refuses to collapse into stasis. Challenge, surprise, empathy, mastery, silliness, grief—every variable dialled to the edge of enough. There is still pain: bee stings, burnt tongues, heartbreak when Charlie’s signal one day fails to arrive. But it is pain braided into consequence, into stories you chose, into a weave vast enough that no single strand can strangle.

Outside, the double rainbow waits, archived in advance against tomorrow’s sky. Inside, you drift toward loam and memory, already curious what ice cream will taste like when you awake.
