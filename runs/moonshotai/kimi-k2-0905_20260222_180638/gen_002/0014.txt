The first thing you notice is the smell of bread.  
Not the sterile whiff of a kitchen-printer extruding starch, but the warm, yeasty exhalation of living dough rising in a ceramic bowl that someone’s grandmother threw on a kick-wheel in 1973. The bowl sits on a reclaimed-oak counter in a third-storey walk-up whose windows overlook a street that belongs equally to pedestrians, skateboarders, and the occasional autonomous delivery dray that hums along at ankle-height like a courteous terrier.  
There is no beep, no push-notification, no voice assistant announcing the start of the day. Instead, the city itself exhales: the apartment’s radiator pings once, the communal bakery two blocks away releases a pulse of steam that drifts up through the ivy on your brickwork, and the morning light—spectrum-tuned by the district’s heliostat field—slides a bar of gold across your pillow. You wake because the neighbourhood agreed last winter that alarm clocks are an undignified way to treat consciousness.

You are thirty-three, or ninety-three, or fifty. It hardly matters. Last year you accepted the “middle-third” ageing protocol: for three decades your biological clock runs at roughly half speed, then you choose either deceleration toward asymptotic life span or graceful return to baseline senescence. Either path is paid for by the same source that keeps the streetlights on and the river swimmable—the Commonshare Dividend, a quarterly distribution of value created by the things nobody should own: sunlight captured on every roof, carbon drawn down by the kelp forests that girdle the continental shelf, the data-maps every cyclist generates simply by pedalling, and the thousandfold efficiency gains that arrive when patents expire after seven years instead of twenty.

The Dividend is not unconditional basic income. It is basic wealth: an equity slice in the infrastructure of survival. If you want more than the default 40 m² apartment, 2 200 kcal, and 10 kWh, you trade with neighbours who want less. Markets persist, but they are local, fractal, and reputation-weighted. The unit of account is the “hour” — one hour of an average person’s cognitive, emotional, or physical labour. The exchange rate is floating, transparent, and set daily by the open-source ledger that also tracks carbon, nutrients, and noise. You can hoard hours if you wish, but they depreciate 0.2 % per month to keep the bloodstream of cooperation from clotting.

You pull on trousers woven from mycelium leather and walk downstairs barefoot. The stair treads are warm; excess heat from the server vault beneath the building is seeped upward through capillary tubes embedded in the wood. The vault is not a data-centre in the twentieth-century sense. It is a memory-kiln: a distributed node where encrypted fragments of everyone’s personal archives are kept—photos, journals, the genome of a childhood cat—balanced across three legal jurisdictions so that no government or corporation can hold a life hostage. In return for hosting a cubic metre of the kiln, the building’s residents receive enough electricity to run the block’s ovens, routers, and hydroponic herbs. The arrangement was negotiated on a plaintext wiki whose edit history is a public poem 2.3 million lines long.

Outside, the street market is already moving. Stalls fold out of the pavement like origami, their carbon-fiber ribs printed last week in the municipal fab foundry. A teenager offers you coffee grown in the old interchange median on the edge of town; the plants are irrigated with greywater from the laundromat and pollinated by a hive of stingless bees whose honey is harvested only once a year on the winter solstice. You hand over a token carved from scrap aluminium—yesterday you helped balance the teenager’s battery-bank, a task the city algorithm matched to you because in 2047 you wrote an open-source guide to off-grid power electronics that still circulates. The token is also a key: when you drop it into the foundry’s hopper next month it will re-melt into a new part someone else needs. Nothing circulates unless it can become something else.

You have no job title. You have a portfolio of curiosities. Tuesdays you mentor probability theory to nine-year-olds, but only in exchange for them teaching you how to hear the difference between sparrow dialects. Wednesdays you chair the “nuance council” for your borough, a volunteer panel that spends at most forty-five minutes adjudicating disputes before they fossilise into grievances. Last week you ruled on whether a resident’s experimental moss graffiti violated the collective aesthetic charter. (Verdict: the moss could stay if it spelled out the air-quality index in real time.) The council’s authority is soft—anyone can ignore a ruling—but the reputational hit shows up immediately in the cooperation layer that overlays every civic interaction. Being known as the person who slows down group decisions is expensive; reputation is the one asset that cannot be traded away, only repaired.

At 10:17 you board the orbit-bound elevator. Not the flashy equatorial one that grabs headlines, but the modest 300-kilometre “kite-line” that rises from the old harbour crane and catapults cargo into low orbit using a loop of graphene ribbon and a ground-based linear motor. You are the cargo today: you won a lottery slot to spend 48 hours tending the silkworms that spin radiation-shielding fabric inside O’Neill 5, a habitat whose hull panels were crowd-designed by students in Lagos and welders in Reykjavik. The habitat’s charter states that 30 % of every kilo exported must be art. Last quarter that included a glass-blown replica of the Baltic Sea whose waves are permanent sine waves of blue silica, and a haiku encoded into the mitochondrial DNA of a strain of barley. You carry with you a thumb-sized capsule of river silt from your childhood—a symbolic export to mix into the habitat’s soil, closing a loop between the gravity-bound and the weightless.

While the elevator climbs, you review the four tasks the city softly suggested for you this week. They appeared not as orders but as gentle increases in the background probability that certain doors will open. A cafe you like offers you a free pastry if you sit at the table reserved for “listening sessions,” because yesterday someone nearby requested a stranger’s undivided attention for exactly seventeen minutes. The library bumps the book you ghost-wrote ten years ago to the top of the return shelf, nudging you to pick it up and cringe at your earlier prose. The tasks are invitations to remain porous. Accepting fewer than three per month triggers a wellness check—not from a bureaucracy, but from three neighbours who volunteered to notice. Declining is allowed, but the opt-out form is a blank postcard you must display on your door for a week. Most people would rather eat the pastry.

By noon you are weightless, feet hooked under a handrail, coaxing silkworms to spin onto three-dimensional mandala moulds. The worms have been gene-edited to exude varying indices of refraction; in vacuum their silk hardens into translucent ceramic. Your counterpart crew are in Bogotá, telepresent via haptic rigs that transmit the tremor of the worms’ mandibles. Language is no barrier: the habitat’s local mesh runs a protocol that overlays semantic fields onto whatever tongue you think in. When you say “trowel” your Colombian partner hears “espátula” and feels the same heft in her palm. The system is open-source; anyone can fork a new dialect. Last year a thirteen-year-old in Jakarta created “butterfly-pidgin” that compresses entire toolchains into metaphorical Lepidoptera. It caught on because it is beautiful.

Back on Earth, forty-eight hours later, you re-enter at midnight local. The heat shield on the cargo capsule is made of the same mycelium as your trousers; it chars, flakes away, and will be swept into the tidal compost where harbour bacteria convert it into chitin for crab shells. You walk home along the seawall whose stones are embedded with bioluminescent algae that fluoresce when your shadow passes—an ambient reminder that you are never alone, never unwatched, but also never unwitnessed. The algae are tuned to the heartbeat of the city; when collective stress spikes after a financial rumour or a storm warning, the glow pulses red. Couples slow their pace and breathe together; the colour shifts back toward teal.

This is not a society without conflict. Last spring a faction demanded the right to secede into a fully offline enclave, arguing that even the minimal surveillance required for reputation tracking was tyrannical. The city held a month-long “deliberation carnival”: streets closed, pop-up theatres staged debates, children built Minecraft models of competing futures. In the end the secessionists won the right to inhabit a peninsula whose fibre lines were ceremonially cut with an obsidian blade. They live there now under a nineteenth-century statute that forbids electromagnetic transmission beyond 40 watts. Trade happens by sailboat every Tuesday. The arrangement is reviewed annually; if their birth mortality rises more than 2 % above baseline they forfeit autonomy. So far they have engineered ingenious pneumatic-post canisters and a semaphore network that relays weather reports via kites. Their example keeps the rest of the city honest: if we cannot persuade people to stay, perhaps our arguments are not yet good enough.

Nor is this a society without grief. Death is rarer but still present: the occasional climbing accident, the rare leukemia that defies even CRISPR 7.0. Funerals are public events held in the round amphitheatre carved into the old landfill hill. The deceased’s memory-kiln fragments are assembled into a temporary hologram that anyone may question for up to forty days. The questions are not sentimental—“What was your greatest mistake?” “Whom did you fail?”—and the answers are archived so that future strangers can collide with wisdom at random. After forty days the hologram is erased; the kilns are wiped; the person becomes public domain. Copyright ends at death. Identity becomes commons.

Children are precious because they are scarce. Fertility is not rationed by coercion but by cooperative agreement: every birth requires three guarantors who pledge—legally and emotionally—to mentor the child until the age of sixteen. If you die before then, your remaining hours are transferred to the child’s cooperative account, ensuring that no one enters adulthood without a fabric of guardians. The system emerged after the “great rebound” of the 2040s, when plummeting birth rates threatened demographic collapse. Rather than bribe people to reproduce, the city chose to Make Parenting Visible: every adult wears a narrow copper bracelet whose colour shifts to indicate how many担保 slots they have open. A passer-by can literally see who has room for another life in their orbit. Romance and reproduction are thus re-entangled with community in ways that advertising once made impossible.

Work—the kind that exhausts rather than fulfils—has been automated so thoroughly that what remains is better called caretaking. Robot arms clean the sewers, but humans still decide which murals get painted on the hatch covers. Trains drive themselves, yet the “story steward” rides each carriage once a day, collecting fragments of passenger conversation to weave into the city’s nightly radio poem. The steward is not paid; she rides because her father once told her that cities are anthologies of overheard sentences. She stores the choicest lines in a notebook bound with silk from the orbital worms. When the notebook is full she burns it, scatters the ash in the tomato beds behind the station, and begins again. The tomatoes, legend says, taste like departure and return.

Governance itself is a craft. The city council rotates every two years through a stratified lottery: 10 % teenagers, 10 % nonagenarians, 30 % migrants who arrived within the last decade, 50 % everyone else. While in office each councillor must live on the Dividend floor—no special stipends—and wear a collar that records every conversation, uploaded nightly to an immutable public ledger. The collar is not surveillance; it is memoir. Constituents can annotate any transcript, creating a living palimpsest of collective memory. When the term ends, the councillor’s collar is sealed and displayed in the Museum of Imperfect Decisions, a quiet courtyard where citizens go to remember how hard it is to know anything in real time.

The city’s constitution fits on one side of a postcard. The other side is left blank so that each generation can write its own addendum. The only immutable clause is the “right to exit,” encoded in a physical lever in the central plaza. Pulling it severs the district from the power grid and data mesh for thirty days, triggering an automatic referendum on whether to renegotiate affiliation or depart forever. The lever has been pulled twice: once as a prank by a seven-year-old who wanted to see what happened (the lights flickered, the child was given a lantern and invited to lead a candlelight parade), and once in earnest—leading to the offline peninsula mentioned earlier. The right to leave is the keystone; everything else is negotiable.

Technology is everywhere, but it is asked to be polite. The default assumption is that every sensor sleeps unless invited. Cameras wear little cloth hoods like Victorian mirrors; to film someone you must physically uncover the lens, an act visible at a distance. Recording in public spaces requires a glowing badge that pulses in sync with the camera’s shutter. The city’s algorithms are audited annually by a guild of twelve-year-olds who are paid in nothing but the power to interrogate any line of code. Their questions—“Why does the happiness index exclude lizards?”—often expose assumptions the adults forgot they had. Last year the children demanded that the river be granted legal standing; the measure passed by acclamation, and now every drainage project must include a “river advocate” chosen by lot from among citizens who have fallen into the water and emerged laughing.

There is no army. Instead, every citizen spends one month during their lifetime in the “guardianship,” a civilian service that ranges from firefighting to asteroid tracking. The training is rigorous and occasionally dangerous; death rates match those of old-era construction work. Upon completion each person receives a single iron nail, hammered into the communal beam that circles the old harbour. The beam is already studded with millions of nails, rusting and shiny, a quiet record of who stood watch. Visitors sometimes ask what the beam protects. Residents answer: “It protects the idea that protection is temporary, shared, and personal.”

Even the concept of ownership has been softened. Land is leased from the future: you may steward a plot for up to 99 years, after which it reverts to the commons with every improvement intact. Houses are mortgaged not to banks but to their eventual inheritors—usually a consortium of yet-unborn children represented by algorithmic proxies that bid on likely enjoyment. The bidding trains predictive models that improve school placement and park design. Thus the young negotiate with the unborn, and the present is continuously reshaped by futures who cannot speak yet.

You age, but you do not retire. Instead you “rotate inward.” The final third of life is spent in increasingly intimate circles: guiding new parents, tending archives, learning to ask questions that no discipline owns. The city maintains a “slowness district” where clocks run on lunar time and conversations must exceed forty minutes. Entry requires surrendering networked devices; exit requires writing a single sentence on the leaving wall. Over decades the wall has become a sediment of wisdom: “I came to escape speed and learned that slowness is someone else’s urgency.” You visit once a year, sit quietly, and add nothing. The blank space beside your name is permission to keep changing.

At night you climb the hill where the land-dump once stood. The city spreads below like a circuit board whose traces are bike paths. Every light you see is either necessity or joy; nothing burns for advertisement. You carry no wristband, no token, no score. The Dividend has been deposited while you walked; your fridge negotiated with the wind farm; your daughter’s guarantors signed; the opera you helped compose will be performed again next month in a language that does not yet exist. You understand that none of this is guaranteed. A solar flare could fry the elevators; a plague could slip through the CRISPR sentinels; the peninsula of the disconnected could launch a raid with their black-powder cannons. Utopia is maintenance, yes—but maintenance is just another word for attention, and attention is the one resource that cannot be automated. So you stand on the hill and attend.

Tomorrow you will wake to the smell of bread again. You will decide, freshly, whether to mentor the stranger who asked yesterday how to love without possession, whether to trade your surplus hours for a kite ride above the bay, whether to add a line to the constitution or leave the postcard blank another season. The city will record nothing more than the fact that you were present, that you chose, that the algae glowed teal under your shadow as you descended the hill.

That is enough. That is the utopia: a place where enough is a verb, not a noun.
