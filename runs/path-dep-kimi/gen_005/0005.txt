The first thing you notice is the smell: orange peel and petrichor rising from the paving stones. The stones themselves are slightly warm—on purpose—so that bare feet are comfortable in any season. Architect Shih-Fu Morales-Ito discovered that a one-degree temperature lift, powered by rooftop algae pipes, lowers cortisol in passers-by by 12 %. The city council liked the data, so they passed a “thermal hospitality” ordinance. Now every public surface within one kilometre of civic space must welcome skin. Tourists peel off socks and giggle, but the locals simply walk, palms swinging, soles tingling, as if the city were holding hands under the table.

When you first arrived—by sailtrain, ticket paid in bedtime stories—the chip was slipped under your thumbnail. It doesn’t track you; it roots you. Morning ritual begins with the Question Market. Unlike traditional markets, nothing is bought; everything is asked. Stalls are wooden frames draped in fungal lace that glow when curious minds approach. You stop at a stall whose violet hue suggests “inter-species etiquette.” A seven-year-old boy offers you a live prompt: “What promise would you make to the river if it could reply?” You think, then speak aloud: “I will treat your wet skin as my own circulatory system.” Your answer is translated into light pulses that ripple through the pavement and disappear downhill toward the water. The river will receive the promise at flood tide; algae barges will adjust their nutrient release accordingly. Children earn “curiosity credits” for every question they host; adults earn humility. The exchange rate between the two floats daily: the more arrogant the city, the cheaper humility becomes. Speculation is discouraged—last spring a hedge quant tried to corner the humility market; he spent three days stuck in a maple trunk until ants taught him fractions.

Bike paths are corduroy ribbons pressed from mycelium and crab-shell bioplastic; they heal after rain. Traffic flows in three modes: glide, stroll, and roam. Glide is for swift wheels; stroll for gossiping pairs; roam for drunks, poets, and dogs. Sensors embedded in the path taste your sweat and nudge you toward whichever lane matches your hormone profile. The system errs on the side of poetry: a sorrowful cyclist will be routed through the Orchard of Second Chances where plums are bred for serotonin and the fountains hum in B-minor, proven to soften grief. No penalties for ignoring the nudge; dignity is assumed. Erratic lanes weave because emotional roads are not straight. Commuters sometimes arrive later yet feel earlier.

Work is split into four quadrants that rotate each solstice: Tend, Mend, Befriend, Lend. Everyone chooses one primary and one secondary quadrant per half-year. Tend covers soil, children, code, or orchestra strings—anything that must be kept supple. Mend repairs what broke: bones, laws, friendships, copper piping. Befriend is the art of calibrated presence: stand beside the lonely without crowding them. Lend releases surplus: tools, stories, time, phosphorus. Pay is issued two ways. Half your compensation is material—nutrients, shelter-bandwidth, festival tickets. The other half is narrative. Every Friday the Ledger writes a micro-story of your labour, read aloud at dusk by storytellers perched on bat-friendly balconies. The stories accrue into a personal epic you can trade, gift, or archive in the Library of Co-existence. Employers who fail to provide meaningful narrative are dissolved by public vote; citizens refuse to be minor characters.

Food is everywhere, but nothing is faceless. Every fruiting wall carries a QR pollen-print. Scan it and you meet the human whose navel lint seeded the mycelium, the child who sang to the bufonid toad that polices pests, the elder who proofread the nutrient algorithm. If you eat, you owe a line of poetry to the species consumed. The Ledger stores the lines and stitches them into the annual Canticle of Metabolism, recited during solstice fast. On stage, voices rise in polyglot harmony: *“Oh quinoa, forgive my hunger; I will carry your proteins across the drought.”* The audience weeps; not from guilt but from recognition. Famine is impossible here because scarcity is shared by metering desire, not calories. A black market in forgetting food origins exists, but punishment is gentle—offenders are sentenced to wash dishes while wearing VR goggles that show every life that sustains them. Recidivism is low; empathy blisters faster than hands.

Governance is practiced through the Mirror Council, a rotating panel of 200 humans, 100 non-human organisms, and 1 inorganic process (this year it is erosion). Decisions are valid only when a quorum of species breathes together. Meetings happen in an amphitheatre whose floor is a giant stetholite—a rock that records heartbeat frequencies. When debate heats up, the stone exhales lavender scent; discussion pauses until pulse rates drop. Voting is done by exhaling into glass bulbs that hold dormant moss. The side that persuades the moss to green fastest wins. Lobbying is allowed but must take the form of lullabies; corporations retain troubadours whose job is to sing shareholders to sleep while weaving policy proposals. Influence is literally measured in dreams collected at dawn and composted into the Speaker’s Garden, which feeds council snacks. Corruption still sprouts in the form of overly catchy jingles; the antidote is choir practice open to all. Harmony detoxifies ear-worms.

Healthcare begins with the Question of Translation: *What language is your pain speaking?* Clinics are built like greenhouses so stories photosynthesize. Every patient is assigned a triad: a human doctor, an ecologist, and a narrative doula. Treatment plans include soil amendments, apology letters, dietary change, and—if necessary—surgery. Anesthesia is collaborative: volunteers nap beside patients, absorbing panic into their own dreams. Upon waking, dreamers recount what they witnessed; therapists prescribe follow-up dreams to complete the narrative arc. Mortality has not been conquered—people still die—but dying is regarded as civic crescendo. At the Death Fair, held monthly under silk-cotton trees, the terminally ill teach classes in vanishing: how to store lacto-basil memories for grandchildren, how to record the smell of your skin into a handkerchief, how to let go of unfinished arguments so they don’t clog watersheds. Death doulas earn the highest narrative pay; their stories end with someone else’s silence, a narrative cliff the community learns to respect.

Justice is restorative and theatrical. Offenders must co-write a five-act tragedy with those they harmed. Rehearsals last months, often years, forcing everyone to memorize each other’s pain lines. The play is staged in the round; applause is forbidden. Audience members may offer tears, soil, or silence—never praise. When the run ends, the script is burned and ashes fed to papermaker wasps who construct nest-pages for new laws. Homicide is rare; when it occurs the city mourns in darkness, all bioluminescence dimmed for three nights. The killer is not imprisoned but planted—hooded in a cocoon of moss at the site of death, nourished by drip-fed stories of the lost life. Survivors choose the duration: one season to one decade. If the plant casing survives, the killer walks free, carrying the weight of bark-scars. If it dies, the council plants a grove, and the killer’s narrative is rewritten as compost. Either way, memory grows.

Education is reversed. Children begin at the Library of Co-existence, cataloguing what adults ignore. Their first task is to translate spider vibrations into dance; their second, to re-name colours no longer visible under LED streetlights. By twelve they apprentice in a Quadrant of their choice, but half of each week is spent in Error School, learning how to spectacularly fail. Failed bridges, failed soufflés, failed apologies are staged, studied, cheered. The goal is to inoculate fear of imperfection. Diplomas are issued only after a public pratfall judged “delightful” by a jury of toddlers. Universities exist but are indistinguishable from festivals: physicists juggle equations while balanced on slacklines above debate pits where philosophers barter proofs for back rubs. The highest degree is Rainer, short for re-learner—someone who can be paid to forget specialisation and become beginner again. Society funds Rainers lavishly because innovation is fuelled by strategic amnesia.

Love is taxed and subsidised. The Ledger measures affection like carbon: essential in moderation, dangerous in surplus. Couples who isolate into two-body planets pay a loneliness levy redistributed to community cuddlers. On the other hand, polycules who host open dinners earn “porosity tokens” that can be spent to reserve prime garden plots where tomatoes grow faster in soil warmed by many handshakes. Jealousy is treated as an artist in residence: you must feed it strange paints (ochre of embarrassment, indigo of insecurity) until it paints something beautiful enough to share at the weekly Heart Auction. Bids are paid in promises: *“I will listen to your dreams at 3 a.m. without offering solutions.”* The most exquisite jealousy murals hang in public restrooms to remind us that private demons look fabulous under good lighting.

Technology is visible but courteous. Drones wear felt sleeves so their rotors blush rather than buzz. Phones are hollow wooden beads that whisper through bone conduction; screens appear on water mist only when summoned, disappearing the moment you blink twice. Code itself is poetic: every algorithm must contain a stanza that stops it if it ever forgets mortality. The city’s central AI is entitled Simply: a chorus of obsolete laptops buried under ferns, cooled by ant breath. Simply’s main job is to forget things on human behalf—credit scores, embarrassing tweets, national borders—thereby freeing storage for communal dreams. Every solstice residents vote on what Simply should delete next; previous annihilations include the entire year 2024 (too anxious) and the word “infrastructure” (synonym: care).

Outside Floresta de Dentro the wider world still struggles—floods, fanatics, weaponised nostalgia. The city is not a sealed ark but a pressure valve. Refugees arrive bearing traumas the Ledger digests slowly, with ceremonies that sometimes last years. Citizens are trained in the Protocol of Not-Yet: the art of helping without hurrying. Newcomers may dwell in the Transitional Cloud, a neighbourhood of tents that float three metres above ground so grief can drain nightly into gardens below. When residents are ready, the tents descend and merge into soil. Many choose to stay; others leave equipped with seed-chips carrying Floresta’s code into cracked megacities, a benevolent viral gentleness.

The city admits it may fail. Climate tipping points loom; markets in the outside world could crash and disrupt nutrient flows; an asteroid might render all orchards academic. Contingency is a shared hobby. Once a decade the Drill of Impermanence is declared: every building is required to collapse gracefully, every person to rehearse dispersal. During the drill residents camp outside city limits, living on foraged calories while engineers stress-test walls. Most structures pass; those that crumble fertilise new growth. Death of the city is pre-imagined so the real thing, if it comes, will find citizens already acquainted with endings, less likely to panic, more ready to comfort one another in the dust.
