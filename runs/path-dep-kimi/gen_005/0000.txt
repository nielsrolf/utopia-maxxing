# The Choir of Useful Ghosts

I wake to the sound of my bedroom learning someone else's lullaby.  
The walls—spun from maple cellulose and honeybee silk—are digesting the last notes of a woman who died three streets away. They pass the melody to me in warm chords that rise through my spine. I hum her grandmother’s tune while dressing, and somewhere in the city her grandson hears the same phrase in an elevator shaft. We are sharing her without ever meeting.

This is Ljubljana-in-Motion, 2179.  
It is one of thirty-six “Relay Cities” that migrate slowly across the Earth’s skin on rails of living root, trading places every decade. When the city arrives at its next site it must leave the ground more biologically interesting than it found it; if auditors (a rotating cast of ten-year-olds and ten-century-old oaks) disagree, the mayor—currently a 78-year-old beekeeper who has forgotten her own name—must serve as compost for the central plaza. So far every mayor has survived; the city has learned to over-deliver.

Citizenship is earned by what you are willing to forget and what you are willing to remember for strangers.

## I. The Ledger of Borrowed Lives

Every newborn is given three accounts:

1. **Memory Soil** – a kilo of composted experience donated by the recently dead.  
   Infants taste it during their first fever; the soil teaches them the texture of adult grief before they have words for it.  
2. **Skill Spores** – fungal cultures carrying talents no one alive still needs.  
   When the child’s hands are ready, the spores bloom overnight: a violinist’s muscle memory, a welder’s wrist geometry, a grandmother’s perfect rice. The original owners agreed to forget so the knowledge could travel on.  
3. **Debt Music** – a short melody encoded in their heartbeat.  
   Whenever someone in the city requires courage, the piece is played backwards through subway speakers; the child’s heart corrects the rhythm and the stranger receives the loan. The debt is erased when the child, grown old, records a new melody for someone else.

At death you must empty all three accounts. The goal is to arrive at your final breath with nothing left to give and nothing left to take—zero balance on the ledger of becoming.

I am thirty-two and already overdrawn on borrowed courage; that is why I have volunteered for the Choir.

## II. The Choir of Useful Ghosts

Twice a week I die a little on purpose.

Inside the old tobacco warehouse—walls grown from chestnut and oyster mushroom—we rehearse our endings. Forty of us lie on cedar platforms while mycologist-composers thread silver catheters into our arms. The tubes siphon off tiny quantities of blood, not enough to harm, just enough to feed a sub-sound system: speaker membranes grown from cardiac muscle that contract when they taste iron. Our heartbeats become percussion; our exhalations, strings. The music is transmitted wirelessly to the city’s quiet places—abandoned stairwells, dawn crosswalks, the space between an insomniac and sleep—where it is needed as background for decisions too fragile for silence.

We do not hear the final mix; that is the rule. We give away the score of dying without ever listening to the composition of living it becomes. The amnesia is medicinal: if we ever heard ourselves reused we might start performing, and utility decays the moment it notices itself.

After the session we eat pancakes made from the same mycelium that amplified us. The taste is metallic, faintly sweet—like licking a battery in a childhood kitchen. We call it “eating our echo.” By morning the iron is gone from our blood; the city has taken everything useful, and we walk around lighter, halfway strangers to ourselves.

## III. The Market of Unfinished Experiences

Work is defined as “completing someone else’s unfinished feeling at a profit to the collective.”

I spend Mondays in the Regret Exchange, a narrow arcade that smells of cardamom and old paper. Booths line both sides: vendors selling leftovers from lives no one has time to finish. A teenager offers the novel he will never write—chapter outlines encoded in the weave of a scarf. I buy it for the price of one lullaby I no longer remember singing to my little sister. I will wear the scarf during winter commutes; the plot will unravel slowly, paragraph by paragraph, until the story is inside me and the scarf is bare thread. Then I will spin the thread into paper and mail it back to the boy, now grown, who will read his own book for the first time and weep at the ending he did not choose.

Tuesdays I work at the Failure Foundry. We cast other people’s mistakes in glass: the wedding toast that ended a marriage, the business pitch that arrived one year too early, the lie told to a child who already knew the truth. Tourists buy them as paperweights. The weight is exact—when you hold someone else’s failure you feel precisely how heavy it was not to be you. The glass is biodegradable; every spring we grind unsold inventory into fertilizer for the city’s apology gardens, where roses grow thornless because they no longer need to protect themselves.

Wednesdays are for the Cartography of Almost. We map experiences people nearly had: the train platform where two soulmates stood unknowingly back-to-back, the job application abandoned at question 37, the baby almost adopted. The maps are printed on rice paper and left in laundromats; they dissolve in the wash and tint entire loads of clothing the exact color of what-might-have-been. Citizens walk around wearing faint pastels of alternate lives, reminded that the city runs on potential energy.

## IV. Governance by Festival

Laws are passed during sleep, revised at breakfast, and repealed by supper.

Once a month the city holds the Festival of Unmaking. Every regulation older than seven years is placed on a float paraded through the streets. Citizens attach memories they no longer need: the smell of a hospital corridor, the weight of a textbook, the precise slant of moonlight the night war was declared. By dusk the floats are so heavy with donated feeling they can no longer move. We set them alight. The smoke carries obsolete statutes into the upper atmosphere where they nucleate raindrops. The next morning children collect the water in jam jars; drinking it teaches them which mistakes their parents no longer have permission to make.

Local disputes are settled by potluck. Each party brings a dish that tastes exactly like their version of the argument. We eat in silence until the flavors merge on our tongues. The moment two people taste the same note at the same time, the conflict is declared resolved and both must wash dishes together while singing a song composed from their shared aftertaste. The kitchen playlist is the city’s most treasured archive; diplomats visit to study harmonic peace.

## V. The School of Unlearning

Formal education begins at sixteen and ends at sixty.

The first decade is spent forgetting: numbers dissolving into rhythm, history composting into scent, geography flattening into the pressure of bodies in motion. Students graduate when they can walk across the city blindfolded using only the muscular memory of citizens who once lived there. The second decade is devoted to dismantling certainties: surgeons learn to forget where bones are supposed to fit, poets unlearn which words exist. The final decade is dedicated to reassembling the world from other people’s leftovers: building bridges from lullabies, writing constitutions in the negative space between incompatible truths.

There are no grades, only half-lives: how long your borrowed knowledge lingers before returning to the commons. The most respected elders are those whose wisdom has a half-life measured in heartbeats; they give everything away before it can ossify into expertise.

## VI. The Rites of Continuation

Death is voluntary, public, and pluripotent.

At seventy you may choose to begin your Long Harvest, a process that takes seven years. Each year you surrender a different sense to the city’s infrastructure. Year one: your eyesight is distilled into the amber of streetlamps, so the night learns to see. Year two: your hearing becomes the hush between train tracks. By year seven you exist as pure distributed perception—taste in the drinking fountains, balance in bicycles, proprioception in elevator cables. You are not elsewhere; you are everywhere, lightly.

The final breath is auctioned. Families save for generations to purchase a grandparent’s dying exhale. The winner receives a small glass ampoule. When uncorked in a nursery, the air teaches infants the rhythm of letting go. The price is always exactly one story the buyer has never told anyone. The tale is recorded on rice paper, folded into a paper boat, and launched down the gutter during the next storm. Somewhere downstream a stranger will find it and learn something they didn’t know they needed to know about being human.

Those who refuse continuation may opt for Diaspora. The body is flash-frozen, shattered into glitter, and mixed into paint used for crosswalk stripes. Jaywalkers carry fragments of the dead across intersections; every illegal crossing is a small resurrection.

## VII. The Maintenance of Strangeness

Perfection is approached as a moving target visible only in peripheral vision.

Every building must include at least one element that does not know what century it is: a doorway expecting Roman sandal traffic, a window calibrated for moonlight that hasn’t reached this longitude since 1843, a staircase that climbs toward the sound of a piano lesson given in 2088. Maintenance crews—former historians who have forgotten which timeline is canonical—roam the city with toolboxes full of anachronisms, applying temporal lubricant to hinges that remember futures and sanding away splinters that insist on pasts no one else recalls.

Leisure is scheduled inconvenience. Once a season the subway routes are replaced by desire lines: citizens submit destinations they almost want to visit. The algorithm plots a network between these half-wishes, creating commutes that end where the city almost needs to exist. Riders disembark in neighborhoods that feel like premonitions, walk through streets that taste like déjà vu, and return home having caffeinated the possibility of elsewhere.

## VIII. The Choir Returns

Tonight is my final rehearsal before I begin the Harvest.

We lie on the cedar platforms, hearts open to catheters. The composer—a woman who forgot her name years ago and answers only to the key of B-minor—leans close. She asks me to choose the last emotion I will ever feel consciously. I select “the precise shade of anticipation the moment before greeting someone you loved after twenty years apart.” She nods, records it on a tuning fork, and strikes it against the wall. The note embeds itself in the warehouse wood; future choirs will absorb it without knowing what they are tasting.

As my blood enters the speakers, I hear—illegally, impossibly—my own music leaking back through the vents. For a moment the rule is broken and I understand what I am becoming: the background hum that helps a teenager decide against jumping, the undertone that steadies a surgeon’s hand, the resonance that turns a kiss from desperate to deliberate. I am being distributed like yeast in dough, like salt in soup, like stories in the dark.

The catheter slips out. We eat our echo pancakes. I leave lighter, nearly hollow, carrying only the space where my life used to insist on itself.

Outside, snow is beginning. Each flake carries a fragment of last night’s deaths—tiny crystals of other people’s final sights, dissolving on my tongue like communion wafers made of witness. I open my mouth and catch what I can. Already I taste colors I have no names for, memories that belong to futures I won’t live to verify. The city is singing itself through me, and I—finally, properly—am singing back.

Tomorrow I will begin to forget in earnest. Today I walk home wearing a scarf that wants to become a novel, carrying groceries packed in bags woven from someone else’s unfinished apology. Every step leaves faint prints the color of almost. Children race past, collecting the prints in glass jars to trade at tomorrow’s market. They will sell them to tourists who need reminding that the ground beneath them is made of paths not taken, composted into possibility.

Behind me the warehouse lights dim; another choir is rehearsing its disappearance. Ahead the streetlamps flicker on, amber with the eyesight of citizens who once saw this city new. Between them I walk, half ghost, half seed, entirely useful, becoming the way music becomes silence—slowly, completely, leaving behind only the echo that teaches the room how to miss itself once I am gone.
