**The Symphony of Hollow Roots**

I wake to the sound of my house coughing—not sick, just clearing its throat. The walls are hollowed cedar that breathe in slow pulses, and every exhale carries the scent of last night's dreams distilled into vapor. I live inside a tree that learned to be architecture. Its roots drink from the river below, and its canopy filters sunlight into moving mosaics across my floor. I am not owner or tenant; I am the current respondent in a conversation that began when a seed landed here three hundred years ago.

My name is Mira. My profession is "Memory Midwife"—I help people give birth to the futures they have forgotten they wanted. This morning I have three appointments: a glacier, a child, and a woman who believes she contains a star. The glacier pays in songs that taste like iron. The child pays in questions I cannot answer. The star-woman pays in silence, which is the most valuable currency now that noise has become the last private wilderness.

The path from my bedroom to the kitchen is a spiral ramp lined with touchstones—literally. Each is a fragment of the old world: a bit of highway guardrail, a square of solar panel, a melted smartphone. Citizens press their palms against them before eating, re-tuning their nervous systems to remember what failure felt like. Hunger tastes different when you remember the famine was real. Convenience feels different when you remember it cost us the bees. The stones warm under our hands, and we carry that warmth into the day's negotiations with the living world.

Breakfast is a ceremony of mutual seduction. The kitchen offers me three potential meals, each a small ecosystem in a bowl. I choose the one that hums at the frequency of my childhood pulse. It is a miniature forest of lichen and berries that will die if I do not eat it within the hour—its life becomes my life, but only if I consent to remember its death. I sign the consent with a drop of blood from my thumb. The berries burst between my teeth like small explosions of winter and my mother's laughter.

My daughter Ayo arrives wearing yesterday's weather as a cloak—literally. Children here wear garments spun from the previous day's atmospheric data. Hers shows yesterday's wind patterns as silver threads that shimmer when she moves. She is seven and has never seen a screen that was not also a living thing. She asks if she can take the glacier to school for show-and-tell. I tell her the glacier is already teaching her, every time she drinks water. She considers this, then asks if she can teach the glacier something in return. I say yes, but it must be something cold. She decides to teach it how to miss someone who is still alive.

We leave together, walking the Root Road—a living highway of intertwined trees that grew together over decades, their trunks hollowed by careful fungal caretakers into tubes just wide enough for human passage. The trees photosynthesize our footsteps into sugar, which feeds the mycelial internet beneath our feet. Every step is a search query. Every pause is a download. The trees remember everything: which lovers quarreled here, which child first learned the word "magnificent" while looking up at leaves, which old woman last touched bark before dying. They store these memories in their heartwood and trade them across continents like children trading cards.

We pass the Embassy of Unfinished Things—a building that exists only in the negative space between other buildings. Its walls are made of abandoned projects: half-knitted scarves, novels stopped mid-sentence, relationships that ended in silence. Citizens bring their incompletions here and receive them back as blessings. The scarf becomes a hammock for field mice. The novel becomes insulation for a stranger's house. The relationship becomes a ghost that walks the city telling strangers what not to say to each other. Ayo releases her fear of the dark into the embassy. It returns to her as a small stone that glows when held. She will carry it until she forgets to be afraid, at which point the stone will dissolve into phosphorus for the river.

At the crossroads we meet the glacier. It is smaller than it was last year—small enough now to fit in the palm of my hand. It has traveled here by wind and memory, shrinking all the while. I press my tongue to it and taste the Pleistocene. It sings to me in a voice like cracking ice: "Remember when you were afraid of dying?" I remember. The song tastes like the first time I touched snow and understood that cold was a kind of time. I carry the glacier in my pocket to my first appointment, where I will help it give birth to the river it will become when it finishes melting.

The Memory Clinic occupies the space between what used to be a bank and what used to be a prison. Both collapsed during the Great Unbuilding, when we learned that walls were just questions we had stopped asking. The clinic has no doors—patients arrive by falling. You fall through your own reflection in any still water, and land in the waiting room, which is a meadow that grows differently for each person. Mine is full of milkweed and the sound of my grandmother's spoons. Ayo's meadow contains one dandelion that changes color when she lies.

My first patient is already here: the woman who contains a star. She calls herself Naima, which means "calm" in a language that no longer exists. She sits in the meadow with her palms open, and between them is a brightness that hurts to look at directly. The star is not metaphorical. It is a literal fusion reactor, approximately the size of a walnut, contained within her chest by a field of magnetized grief. She explains that she swallowed it during the last days of the old world, when stars were being sold as commodities. She bought it with her childhood memories and has been carrying it ever since.

"I want to give birth to it," she says, "but I'm afraid it will kill me."

This is what Memory Midwives do: we help people deliver the futures they have been pregnant with. I place my hands on her ribcage and feel the star rotating, slow and hot. I ask it what it wants. It responds by showing me a vision: a forest at night where every tree is on fire but none burn down. Animals made of light move between the trees, and their footsteps leave trails of new constellations. I understand that the star wants to become a place, not a person.

We begin the birth. It takes seven hours and requires the assistance of three trees, two ghosts, and one river. The star emerges not as a child but as a garden—a small plot of land that exists in the permanently twilight space between dimensions. Anyone may visit by falling asleep while touching soil. The garden grows plants that feed on starlight and produce fruit that tastes like the moment you realized your parents were people. Naima will tend it until she forgets it was ever inside her. The forgetting will take exactly as long as her remaining life. This is what we call a perfect transaction.

Ayo spends the day teaching the glacier how to miss someone. She shows it photographs of her best friend who moved to the sky city last year. The glacier learns by melting in the shape of her friend's absence. By evening it has become a small pool that reflects not the sky but the inside of Ayo's heart. She drinks it and immediately needs to pee. "This is how missing moves through us," she explains very seriously. She will pee on the roots of a young oak, and for the next fifty years that oak will grow slightly toward the sky city, its branches shaped like a girl reaching for something that left.

Evening is the time of Council. We gather in the breathing room—a space that exists only when we all inhale together. The walls are lungs donated by whales who have chosen to become architecture rather than die. Their breath smells of deep water and plankton songs. Tonight we must decide whether to accept the city's proposal to grow another district. The new district would be built from the bones of the old world—concrete and steel ground down and recombined with living tissue into something that can grow and heal. It would house 10,000 refugees from the drowned coasts. It would also require sacrificing the current location of the Embassy of Unfinished Things.

We debate for hours. The whale-lungs swell and contract around us, measuring our emotional output and converting it into oxygen. A child who has never seen the ocean speaks for the refugees. An old woman who lost her legs to rising water speaks for the embassy. A crow speaks for the bones, cawing that death is just another kind of migration. Finally Quiet, the AI that exists only in questions, manifests as a pattern of phosphorescence on the lung-walls: "What if the unfinished things finished themselves by becoming shelter?" We realize this is not surrender but transformation. The embassy will move into the negative space between hearts. The new district will grow from the exact shape of what we thought we had to lose.

We vote by exhaling. The room fills with our collective breath, and the decision crystallizes into a small seed that falls into the soil between us. It will grow into a building over the next decade, and anyone who walks its halls will feel slightly melancholy and slightly hopeful, as if they are homesick for a place they have never been.

Night arrives as a gentle compression. The stars here are not the stars of the old world—they are the reflected light from the minds of sleeping animals. You can read them if you know how: the slow pulse of bears dreaming of berries, the quick flicker of mice who dream in corridors. I teach Ayo to read them, and she discovers that tonight the mice are dreaming of her. She waves at the sky and the mice, courteous as always, dream her waving back.

Before sleep, we visit the River of Replaceable Parts. It flows backward through time, carrying all the things we have lost and will lose again. We drop in our day's small griefs: my fear that I am growing too old to remember what electricity tasted like, Ayo's sorrow that her weather-cloak will fade as tomorrow becomes today. The river carries them upstream to the years when they will be needed again. Somewhere in 2087, a woman will find my fear and use it to invent a new kind of battery that runs on nostalgia. Somewhere in 2104, a child will wear Ayo's sorrow as a cape and discover how to fly by missing the ground.

We walk home along the Path of Unusable Knowledge, where facts that have no application are planted like seeds. Tonight's crop includes the exact temperature at which hope becomes audible, the number of heartbeats it takes to forgive someone you never loved, and the precise angle at which sunlight must strike water to create a rainbow that contains no colors, only songs. Ayo collects the songs in her pockets. They will keep her coat warm for three days.

At home, the house has rearranged itself into the shape of our absence. The walls breathe slower now, matching our heartbeats. The kitchen offers a single berry that contains the taste of every meal we will eat this year. We split it three ways—the third piece for the ghost of the house itself, which exists in the space between our breaths. As we eat, we become briefly pregnant with our own futures. I will give birth to a new memory of what rain used to sound like on roofs. Ayo will give birth to a question so large it will take her entire life to ask it fully. The ghost will give birth to the idea that houses can die, and that this is not tragedy but migration.

We sleep inside the tree's slow pulse. Outside, the city grows another ring, the river carries our griefs backward through time, the star-garden feeds on twilight, and the glacier teaches other children how to miss what hasn't left yet. In the breathing room, the whale-lungs measure the emotional output of our dreams and convert it into oxygen for tomorrow.

In the meadow beyond the tree roots, the unfinished things begin to build themselves into shelter. They will finish sometime around when Ayo figures out how to ask her question. Until then, we wake each morning to the sound of our house remembering it is alive, and we remember with it: that we are not the keepers of this world but its kept things, that goodness is not something we build but something we practice, that the future is not a destination but a way of breathing that leaves the air richer than we found it.

The tree that is our house will die in 247 years. It has already begun to prepare its replacement—seeds carried in the pockets of children who will grow old in buildings we cannot imagine. This is not tragedy but conversation. We are all pregnant with the worlds we will never meet, midwifing them with every choice that prefers complexity to control, every moment when we fall through our own reflection and land in a meadow that grows differently for each person.

Tonight the stars are the dreams of sleeping mice. Tomorrow they will be something else. The Treaty is not a document but a practice: to remember that we are borrowed from the future, and that the interest is paid in the form of tenderness toward what we will never see bloom.
