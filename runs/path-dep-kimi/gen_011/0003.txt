UTOPIA C
The City That Sings Itself Awake

I live in Oru, population 441,000, a city that metabolizes unfinished symphonies and half-spoken truths into architecture that breathes. Our apartment building—thirty-three thousand tons of larch, chestnut, and iron-oak grafted with graphene nerves—flexes like a sleeping cat each dawn, shifting two centimeters westward to keep pace with the magnetic migration of our floating archipelago. You never feel the motion; you feel the memory of motion, the way one remembers a lullaby without remembering when it was sung.

My daughter Amaya presses her palm to the mycelium wall of our kitchen, and yesterday’s harvest rises through the floorboards: the taste of Mrs. Alvarez’s 1987 photograph album, now converted into edible wafers that glow purple under the wavelength of regret. Amaya offers me one. When I hold it to the east-facing window, I taste the precise moment when pride and grief become indistinguishable—when letting someone go feels like celebrating their flight while mourning their departure. The wall records the flavor, adding it to the symphony that has been growing through the building’s beams since my grandmother’s death twenty-three years ago. Her unfinished violins have become the temperature of 71.3°F—the specific warmth of being held by someone who knows they will lose you but chooses to hold you anyway.

We walk to the Community Table, which today stretches exactly 2.7 kilometers because 317 citizens arrived carrying boxes of abandoned plans: postcards never mailed, gardens never planted, wedding vows practiced but never spoken. I add tomatoes grown from the summer I planned to learn Spanish but instead learned how to sit silently with my grandfather while he forgot who I was. Amaya drizzles honey cultivated by bees trained to pollinate flowers grown from the emotional residue of divorce papers—the sweetness that persists after the bitter has been processed away. A stranger across from me offers soup that carries the moment her son told her he was not her son but her daughter. I accept it and immediately understand that families are not dissolved by change but enlarged by it.

Currency here is sung, not spent. I pay for breakfast with a glass marble containing a single eye looking back at me—my attention, harvested during last night’s dream of bridges that grow an inch each year. The woman who served the soup receives my marble and places it in her cloth belt, where it joins six other currencies: heart-hours that sigh when spent; warmth that cools the moment it leaves you; a seed that sprouts into a temporary tattoo of the species I fostered this week (a monarch butterfly, now hovering over her wrist); a feather that absorbs decibels; a square of cloth that unwraps to show the rooftop garden I steward; and half a Story—a translucent pill made of river reeds that records my voice telling the memory of this morning. By sunset the pill will blossom into a lily the color of my voice, powering the streetlights with its glow. At sunrise it will fold into paper; children will cut it into tiny bridges and float them down storm-drains, where they will be assembled into next week’s ferry schedule. Thus no story is hoarded; every story is transport.

After breakfast I walk to the Planting Fields, where I work as a Conversion Specialist. My client today is Marcus, carrying 1,247 postcards written to his mother over fifteen years but never mailed. Together we select one from the middle period, when his handwriting loosened from proof-of-good-parenting into the scrawl of someone who accepts they will always disappoint. We plant the card in soil composed of last year’s broken musical instruments. Six hours later the postcards have dissolved into sunflowers that track not the sun but the neighborhood’s emotional temperature—on days when many process grief, the flowers turn inward; on days when harvested possibilities outnumber losses, they face outward, broadcasting yellow like semaphore for hope.

“You know what she never understood?” Marcus says as we wash soil from our hands. “That some forms of love are meant to be written but not sent. That description can be completion enough.” His tears carry relief rather than regret. At noon I cross Easy Bridge, a living maple whose crown has been pleached into a tunnel. Every autumn it drops gold leaves encoded with chlorophyll sonnets; cyclists read them aloud as they pass, the words dispersing like spores. Today a leaf lodges in my hair: “The point was never to finish but to keep reaching toward something beautiful enough to justify the reaching.” I tuck it behind Amaya’s ear when she meets me at the District of Transformed Grief.

Here, buildings grow from consciousness of those who chose to become architecture rather than carry losses in human form. We stop at the fountain that was once Keiko, who lost her family in the 2089 water conflicts. She chose to become this public space, her awareness distributed through the filtration system. When children play, their laughter activates her; the water adjusts to exactly what their joy needs. I dip my hands and taste Keiko’s transformation—not the trauma of what she lost but the decision of what she became. The knowledge rises through my skin like how grief changes—not lighter but differently heavy, like a river stone learning to carry water’s relentless affection.

In the afternoon I visit the In-Between Apartment I applied for last month—rooms that exist only at dusk, assembled from violet light and old piano strings. Rent is paid by recounting the dream I never told: the one where I am a bridge growing backward through time, meeting myself carrying objects I have not yet lost. The walls absorb narrative tension; if I lie, the floor sags. I speak honestly: “I dreamt I ate tomorrow’s bread and it tasted like the moment before I hurt someone I loved.” The floor steadies. The walls sigh, adjusting their hue to the exact shade of adequate mercy.

Night falls. We join the Procession of Unfinished Instruments, carrying objects meant to become music but became something else: wind chimes made from spoons that stirred coffee for people who no longer visit, drums from tires that carried families to places thought to be homes. Together we create a symphony not meant to be beautiful but honest—the sound of intention meeting limitation and discovering evolution, not failure.

The procession ends at the River of Shared Memory, where we release our instruments into currents that flow not toward the sea but toward each other. The river has learned to carry objects upstream through collective memories of swimming against tides. As our contributions disappear, we hear them transforming—chimes becoming spoons that carry music in their metal, drums becoming wheels that sing of every journey, flutes becoming bones that speak without requiring breath.

Walking home, Amaya asks, “Do you think Grandma knew her symphony would become a building that teaches people to want things they can never fully possess?”

I consider the apartment where Lúcia once lived, who could never finish singing her children to sleep. Now her voice warms floors of families who will never know her name but will grow up understanding that love often expresses itself as melody that refuses resolution. “I think she knew completion was never the point,” I answer. “The point is to keep becoming—like how stones carry water into smoothness, how trees carry seasons into rings, how we carry each other into being more intricately alive with every transformation we permit.”

Before bed we participate in the nightly Exchange of Small Completenesses. Amaya offers me the memory of her first kiss—mango lip balm and possibility. I give her the understanding of watching your child discover that some joys are worth the risk of future grief. Neither of us loses anything; both of us gain the comprehension that love multiplies—sharing creates more of what makes us human.

As sleep arrives, I lie listening to my grandmother’s symphony rising through our home. It has changed since morning, absorbing today’s harvest of small completions. Now it carries Marcus’s unsent postcards, Keiko’s fountain, the lily that will power tomorrow’s streetlights. It has become what all lullabies were meant to be: not songs to make sleep easy but promises that being human means carrying beautiful things that refuse completion, and that this carrying is privilege—the way we carry each other into the beautiful impossibility of perfect, luminous incompleteness.

The last thing I taste is tomorrow’s harvest: bread fermented from syllables children spoke during their week of new languages, sweetened with the apology I never received. The city is already humming it into structure, into bridge, into ferry schedule, into the next story that will power the lily that will light the street where someone walks off-key, singing memory in B-minor, scientifically calibrated to be exactly enough mercy.
