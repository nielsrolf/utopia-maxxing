**To Wake Beneath a Different Sun (and Be Glad)**

Imagine waking tomorrow without yesterday’s aches behind your eyes.  
Imagine that the first sound you hear is not traffic but the low, glad hum of the town-tree whose roots lace beneath your apartment, signalling that last night’s collective dream was peaceful.  
Imagine that your wearable—an opal wafer no thicker than a maple leaf—lights up with one sentence:

> “Today is yours to shape; the rest of us guarantee the safety-net.”

You are not rich in the old sense. Nobody is. There is no “rich”, only “resource-time” that refreshes each week like a living stipend. The algorithm that calculates your share knows exactly how many joules, grams and compute-cycles are required for any life-project you can plausibly wish, and errs on the generous side. Yet the same ledger insists that you, personally, can never again emit more harm than a forest of equivalent mass can re-absorb. If you want to throw a planet-sized party, great—the Earth’s ecological commons will loan you the surplus of another planet already gardened by drones. No interest, only the requirement that when the party ends the hydrogen you borrowed is back in the tank.

You do not go to work unless you feel like it. Most “jobs” have been automated for decades; the remaining ones are multiplayer games with real outcomes: teach a child in Lagos how to read Old English; referee a Martian epic-poetry slam; taste-test thirty varieties of gluten-free puff-pastry in a pop-up kitchen that will never scald you because the counter is upholstered with living mycelial foam that secretes burn-soothing aloe at 42 °C.

If you prefer, you can do nothing. The social contract—agreed to in the Great Recale of ’42—states that idleness is also a public good; contemplative humans, like fallow fields, restore the imagination soil of the species. Every year you spend “doing nothing” is published in anonymised form so society can trace which quiescent seasons later flowered into symphonies, neighbour-mediation protocols or new kinds of hugs.

Your body is post-generic. Ageing was defanged twenty years ago, but you still look recognisably you, only plausibly 32 forever. At any moment you can swap sex, height, skin colour, or grow wings. Cosmetic edits regenerate inside a month; structural edits (lungs that breathe 40 % oxygen on Mars, bones that handle 3 g) require a three-day hibernation while nanoscopic osteoblasts sing calcium into new lattices. The only universally forbidden edit is one that would remove your empathy settings below the civilisational floor (98.7 mirror-neuron equivalents). The edit client politely refuses: “That change would exile you from the moral commons. Try a different adventure?”

Your household is whatever scale feels comfortable. Seventy-percent of humanity lives in “kibbutz 2.0” arcologies—kilometre-high living reefs that house 30 000 people, a river, a forest and a school of dolphins who opted into uplift and now teach sonar music to children. The other thirty-percent range from solitary cliff-dwellers to rotating triads who dissolve every decade. Children are no longer accidents. Conception requires three adult signatures on a smart-contract that locks away a modest portion of resource-time for the new person until their 25th birthday. The contract also grants every child veto power over any parental edit that would alter their genome after implantation. The first ethical sentence most toddlers learn is “I withhold consent.”

Money is gone, but reputation remains—measured in “kudos”, a dimensionless score distilled from praise, care-hours delivered, and creative objects released to the commons. Kudos is non-transferable and decays after seven years so no one coasts on teenage brilliance. The single high-status activity is teaching: to raise another mind past your own ability is the only reliable way to asymptote near the kudos ceiling. Celebrities are centenarians who have serially specialised, becoming the best in the galaxy at haiku, then fusion dance, then Martian soil mycology, then grief counselling, and finally at letting go when their students surpass them.

Death is optional. At any moment you may press “archive”, creating a quantum-verified snapshot that is stored both terrestrially and aboard three stellar ships heading in different directions. Restoration after catastrophic death is free, but the restored self inherits a kudos penalty symbolising the grief-cost imposed on friends. After 400–600 calibrated subjective years, 83 % of citizens choose graceful dissolution, scattering their stored pattern into the public dream-well like autumn leaves. Their memories, stripped of trauma-tags, become open-source ingredients for future art projects, quilts, and AIs.

You can still have danger. The archipelago of consensual risk-zones includes real tundra where clumsiness can break ribs, sailing races through ion-storms above Jupiter, existential-therapy LARPs in which you role-play your own worst fear for six months. All extreme zones broadcast a “no-rescue flag”. If you step inside, the safety-net dissolves—though nanomedical outposts hover at the perimeter, forbidden to intervene until you crawl back over the line or push the panic-amulet. Roughly one in a thousand citizens spends a decade in a risk-zone; almost all return limping, laughing, and notoriously kind. They have stories, and stories are still the scarcest good.

Governance is a three-layer pastry.  
1. A constitution written in formal logic that fits on one page, mostly stating negative rights—no slavery, no non-consensual pain, no irreversible ecosystem loss, no seed-AI above human-level that is not globally inspected.  
2. An AI steward—an open-source mesh of 144 semi-independent oracles—whose sole task is to prevent Moloch scenarios: no one may externalise harms across scales longer than a century or broader than a light-year. The steward can veto, never command; if it blocks a project, it must publish a simulation describing the extinguished future kittens or the statistical suicides its veto prevented.  
3. A citizen jury pool—ten million people called for one month each decade—who audit the steward’s decisions. Their majority vote can overrule any veto, forcing the experiment forward if humanity insists on learning by touching the stove. So far, they have overridden the steward only twice: to allow a star-lifting programme that dimmed Sol by 0.3 %, and to authorise 100 000 subjective-year art-odysseys inside black-hole accretion discs.

Earth itself is heritage-listed. No new construction may exceed the height of the tallest baobab, and every biome is represented in ark-refuges tended by people whose kudos score is linked to biodiversity indices. Most manufacturing happens off-world: ring-cities orbit Venus; the Moon is a quiet sculpture garden where artisans quarry anorthosite to fold into paper-thin porcelain that sings when starlight hits it. Earth’s surface is left to forests, farms, nomads, and scholars who vow to keep the Human Accent alive—the slight asymmetry of gait, the taste of rain on dusty leaves, the ability to read handwriting.

Culture has become a coral reef of subcultures. Any group may secede into a “pocket universe” 300 km on a side—an O’Neill cylinder, an underground pocket on Ceres, a slow-time server running a million times faster than baseline. Secession requires only two promises: you will export no violence, and you will re-open your gates for one month every thirty years so the rest of humanity can taste your inventions. Already there are valleys where gravity is inverted every evening, anti-symphonic enclaves that have abolished rhythm, and a water-world where everyone is mer-people who experience episodic memory as literal currents they can swim back through. Each exports something: the anti-symphony collective mailed everyone a score that, once heard, erases ear-worm pop songs for a year; the mer-folk courier new waterproof metaphors that poets everywhere immediately steal.

Religion did not die; it diversified. Every theology is treated as an engineering proposal: “What world would exist if this cosmology ran on hardware?” Entire moons are set aside for experimental afterlives—uploaded communities running on doctrine-specific code. Mormons get their own exoplanet, but so do Xenofeminists, Neo-Zoroastrians, and the Church of the Terminal Bus Route. Tourist visas last a week; converts may stay forever if they sign a second smart-contract acknowledging that “forever” here is bounded by stellar evolution.

The criterion for a successful new religion is simple: do its pilgrims come back kinder to people who believe something else?

Romance is exuberant. The old dyad is merely one template on a spectrum that includes seasonal triads, centennial hexagons, “parallel play” marriages where partners live in separate pocket universes and send each other curated dream-packets, and companion species romances—sentient auroras, uplifted ravens, even shy lunar AIs who gift their beloved tides. Every configuration must pass a consent routine so thorough it includes simulations of all parties at age ninety, asking: “Knowing everything you will become, do you still say yes?” The divorce rate, measured in amicable partings rather than ruptures, hovers at 62 %; people celebrate “conscious uncoupling” with the same ritual joy as weddings, because endings are no longer laced with financial terror or medical jeopardy.

Suffering is not abolished; it is opt-in. Chronic pain has been medicalised away, but there are still melancholy seasons, the ache of unrequited love, the vertigo of staring into deep time. Those textures are considered aesthetic notes, like the bitterness in chocolate. Anyone overwhelmed can step into the “quiet rooms”—soft-lit clinics where counsellors (human, uplifted crow, AI) sit in circles and trade stories until the pain finds context. No session ends until the visitor can articulate one new question the pain lets them ask about the world.

Education is reversed. Instead of forcing children to sit still, we send them to “wild kindergartens” on orbital islands where gravity randomly changes direction, encouraging vestibular growth and courage. Adolescents choose apprenticeships: commanding a storm on Neptune, choreographing zero-g dance, translating dolphin jokes into haiku. University is lifelong: at 80 you might decide to master topology; at 400 you switch to pottery. Every thesis must take the form of a gift to an unrelated community—knowledge circulates like blood, not like property.

Science has become the grand communal sport. Megaprojects are crowd-funded by kudos: 30 million people once spent two subjective centuries threading a magnetic filament through the galactic core because a nine-year-old asked, “What song would a black hole sing if it had a larynx?” The answer turned out to be a B-flat 57 octaves below middle C; the resulting waveform is now the ringtone that signals “all is well” across human space.

Art is the chief export of the species. Every citizen is guaranteed one cubic kilometre of raw reality—virtual, physical, or mixed—in which to build whatever they can imagine. The most admired works last exactly one year before dissolving, ensuring that the universe never clogs with monuments. A recent hit: an eclipse that turned the sun into a weeping eye whose tear-drops became glass sparrows that learned to fly toward sorrow anywhere in the solar system and burst into comforting auroras. After twelve months the eye blinked, the birds vanished, and the creator walked away anonymous, kudos score glowing.

The deepest moral rule is parsimonious: “No being may lock the future into a single shape.” The second-deepest is merciful: “Every story deserves an editor, not a censor.” The combination guarantees permanent argument—peaceful, witty, grand argument—about what we should become next. Humanity is no longer a pronoun; it is a parliament of verbs in every tense.

And you? You close your front door, feel the town-tree’s pulse beneath your soles, and decide that today you will learn to play the storm-harp: a lyre strung with magnetised plasma that only sings when solar wind fingers the strings. You take an elevator to orbit, step into the rehearsal conservatory circling Earth, and meet your quintet: a woman who has lived 300 years as a sequoia, an uplifted octopus who speaks colour, a child who has never known gravity, and an AI who has chosen to experience time backwards so it can accompany the endings of things.

Together you improvise a piece titled “The Future Is an Under-rehearsed Chorus That Nevertheless Refuses to Quit.” When the final chord resolves, listeners on three planets weep without knowing why. The recording is released under Creative Cosmos License. Somewhere a lonely teenager on a mining rig in the Kuiper belt will hear it, feel less alone, and decide to keep going.

That is the only metric that ultimately matters: did we increase the probability that someone, somewhere, will decide to keep going? The ledger updates every second. Last time you checked, the count stood at 3.02 × 10²¹ sentient beings and rising. The graph slopes gently upward, like a smile.

You head home. The town-tree has saved you a blueberry that tastes of every summer before the invention of war. You eat it on the balcony while two moons rise—one rock, one mirror—reflecting a light that has never burned a city.

Tomorrow the sun will be the same, but the light will fall on different faces, and the questions will be new again. That, too, is part of the deal: utopia is not a place we reach; it is the ongoing practice of not closing the door on ourselves.

You sleep. The town-tree continues its slow photosymphony, whispering to the apartment roots, to the light-rail vines, to the dream-well in orbit where yesterday’s sorrows are already turning into someone else dawn-coloured wings. Everything breathes together. Nothing is perfect; everything is improvable; the improvability itself is sacred.

Outside, the night sky rearranges its constellations into a sentence visible only to the naked primate retina:

> “We kept the promise; the rest is choreography.”
