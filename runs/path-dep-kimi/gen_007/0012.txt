# The Choir of Watersheds  
*A day in the migrating city of New Arcadia, 2191*  

## 1. Dawn – the city exhales  

I wake because my bedroom swells. The wall against my left shoulder lifts by three millimetres, the exact rise my mother’s chest made when she finally let go of her last breath. She donated that measurement to the city’s “respiration library”, and now every room I rent contains a panel that expands once every twenty-four seconds. Nothing is wasted, especially the poetry of how we leave.  

The light arrives through clear-root blinds that have spent the night tracking the moon. They open now, not because I command them but because the river outside has warmed by 0.7 °C. The blinds read temperature the way our grandparents read clocks; when the river says “it is time”, the blinds obey.  

Outside, New Arcadia is already moving. Our eight-kilometre-long raft of dwellings, workshops, orchards and salt-water ponds glides south at walking pace, The rails we ride are not steel; they are twin rows of chest-high willow that we planted decades ago on the flood-plain. Every spring we cut the tops and bend fresh whips into hoops. Every autumn the hoops lignify and we bolt on cross-ties made of mycelium brick. The living rails root themselves forward overnight, drawing the city like a long caterpillar chewing its way across the prairie. 400, one-way.
