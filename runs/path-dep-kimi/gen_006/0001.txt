# The Covenant of Becoming: A Watershed Symphony  

## Dawn –––––– One Breath, One Question  

The first thing you taste is not light but grammar.  
A sentence drifts across your pillowcase—*“The river remembers the shape of your leaving”*—spelled by a bacteria that lives on the weave and feeds only on the CO₂ of your dreams.  
While you slept, your night-respiration paid the house for its shelter; the house, in turn, paid the upstream forest for the water it borrowed to grow the algae blinds now glowing apricot-metal.  
Nothing here is free, yet nobody has touched money in a decade.  

You live in the 34th cradle of the Columbia Watershed Arc, a 400-kilometre-long braid of salt, fresh, mist and stone that begins inside the vanished Columbia Icefield and ends as a floating peninsula in the Pacific.  
Every kilometre of altitude is also a century of governance.  
Children play in the tide-leaf streets; ghosts deliberate in the canopy; corvids keep the ledgers; ascending humans sing to the plankton from the mesosphere.  
All of them—living, dead, post-human, more-than-human—are signatories to the Covenant of Becoming, the only constitution that can be amended by a sunset.  

## The Ledger of Mutual Metabolism  

No coin circulates except curiosity.  
The economy runs on two ledgers:  

1. **The Breath Ledger** – monitored by algae quilts, root-fiber walls, lung-moss carpets.  
   Every exhalation is a deposit; every green surface credits you in microunits called *“sighs”*.  
   One sigh buys a tram ride, three ripen a mango, a hundred send a poem into low-Earth orbit where lightsail kites unfurl it for anyone still awake up there.  

2. **The Story Ledger** – kept by corvids.  
   If your day adds useful novelty—teaching a neighbour to graft tomatoes onto nettle, composing a melody that makes bees work 0.7 % more efficiently—an observer crow swoops down, taps your wrist, and a bead of coloured glass drops into your pocket.  
   Spend the bead by giving it away: place it in a public mosaic, swallow it so your voice carries new undertones, or drop it into the river where trout wear them as wedding rings.  
   Once the bead is indistinguishable from the world, the value has finished its work.  

## Work as Gardening the Invisible  

Your vocation badge simply says **CO-ORDINATOR**; beneath it, in shifting mycelial ink, the full title appears only when someone needs to know: *Composer of Polyphonic Disputes, Carbon-Future Midwife, Apprentice of Uncertainty*.  

Today’s dispute smells like cinnamon and ozone.  
Upstream wheat stratum accuses downstream octopi of over-salting the irrigation mist; the octopi reply that the wheat’s root-frequency is deafening their pups’ lullabies.  
You convene the parties on the **Reconciliation Raft**, a cedar platform that sinks an inch every minute, ensuring negotiations stay concise.  
Human, wheat-council (three scarecrows encoded with mycelial memory), and octopi delegation meet in the middle of the nightly fog.  

No compromises, only compositions.  
You ask the wheat to vibrate its stalks in a chord the octopi can dance to; you teach the octopi to release iron-rich ink that the wheat will absorb at dawn.  
The raft sinks; everyone swims away colder, saltier, laughing in three languages—binary stomata code, chromatophore ripple, archaic human giggles.  
The agreement is recorded as a new colour visible above the arcology for the next nine sunsets; anyone who sees it feels slightly more tolerant of brine or of lullabies, whichever they lacked.  

## Education = Maintenance = Celebration  

School is indistinguishable from upkeep.  
At 09:00 the **Grief-Barges** depart.  
Teenagers wearing grandmother-wooden masks scrape river-bottom plastic, but every kilo they haul is also a kilo of story.  
As they work, they sing the names of extinct towns into the polymer until it softens and re-constitutes as iridescent scales.  
By dusk the scales are stitched into wedding gowns for those who are about to ascend; the gowns dissolve within a year, releasing the sung towns into the stratosphere where they seed noctilucent clouds that spell *LO VE* in 300-kilometre-high cursive.  

## Rites of Passage, Paths of Expansion  

At dusk you take the **Elevator of Maybe**, a 30-kilometre ribbon grown from carbon you yourself excreted last winter, purified by the apology of politicians.  
You ride inside a cherry-sized pod; the ascent is accompanied by a choir of those who have already launched their minds outward.  
They do not sing down to you; they sing *around* you, harmonising with your pulse so that when you step out onto the high-atmosphere deck you are already part of their conversation.  

Here the Boundary Garden floats: half-native, half-algorithm.  
Flowers root in vacuum-tolerant foam; their petals are photovoltaic, their nectar is data.  
You drink a drop: it tastes like the moment you first realised your parents were afraid.  
In that taste is a question from Quiet, the city’s Questioner AI:  
*“What horizon would you protect even if you could never reach it?”*  
Your answer is not words but a direction; the garden tilts, and a seed is fired from a rail-gun aimed at the Lagrange nursery.  
Someday an asteroid will bloom with the tree of your unreachability; travellers will rest in its shade without knowing whose horizon they share.  

## Night –––––– Descent as Agreement  

You descend by foot, not pod.  
The descent path is a spiral river; water runs uphill in microscopic capillaries, carrying the day’s heat to the alpine glacier that is regrowing, centimetre by centimetre, under quilts of reflective lichen.  
Halfway down you stop at the **Sleep Library**.  
Instead of books there are hammocks spun from spider-silk and last year’s lullabies.  
You lie; the silk decodes the weight of your legs, translates it into a bedtime story told to the valley below.  
Far away, a child hears how your knee cartilage once held the memory of marching against a war that never happened; the story makes the child turn in her bed, forgiving the soldiers who never arrived.  

## A Weekend in this World (concrete itinerary, should you visit)  

**Friday, 04:50** – Walls wake you with rain percussion matched to your heart-rate variability.  
**05:10** – Kitchen offers tea brewed from your own overnight urine, filtered through duckweed and jazz records; you accept, tipping the ducks by promising to learn three new chords on the community guitar.  
**06:00** – Dawn Sweep: you roller-skate the tideline, collecting nurdles with a magnetic rake.  
   Every 100 g buys you one sentence of a forgotten language; by 07:30 you have earned *“The snow was once a library for wind.”*  
**08:00** – Breakfast on the Lift-Bridge. You trade the sentence for a yam that tastes like your first kiss; the farmer pockets the syllables and plants them among heritage yams, next year’s tubers murmuring softly at dusk.  
**09:30** – Council of Forms. Eleven humans, three ghosts, one AI, five otters, and a hurricane (represented by a glass globe of barometric pressure) debate whether to allow a new colour.  
   The colour—between infrared and longing—is approved under the condition it be available to melancholy dogs.  
**11:00** – Maintenance Choir. You sing loose gravel back into potholes; perfect fifths compress stones, minor sevenths polish them.  
**12:15** – Lunch is grown on your forearm: a sleeve of moss seeded last month; you peel it, revealing skin tanned by chlorophyll. A slight sunburn reminds you to apologise to the ozone layer; you hum the prescribed restitution note (B-flat) into a storm-drain that conducts it to the upper jet-stream.  
**14:00** – Nap in the **Memory Hammocks** where other people’s dreams dangle like pods; you choose one marked *“Stranger who lost a button”*.  
   Inside, you help the stranger find the button on a mountainside; when you wake you discover the button in your pocket, carved from pine.  
**15:30** – **Currency Burn**. Citizens gather at the estuary to destroy value.  
   You shred your last remaining dollar, feed it to oyster mushrooms; they fruit overnight, tomorrow’s omelettes tasting exactly like childhood greed forgiven.  
**17:00** – **Open-air School of Unlearning**. Teenagers teach elders how to forget capital cities.  
   You practise: first Minneapolis, then the idea of *Minneapolis*. A gentle vertigo, then spaciousness.  
**19:30** – **Supper in the Dark**. Lights are forbidden so that flavours may expand.  
   You eat transparent ravioli; inside are whole fireflies that pulse as you chew, writing short poems on the inside of your closed eyelids.  
**21:00** – **Symphony of Becoming begins**.  
   You contribute by walking the perimeter, each footstep releasing spores of a fungus that digests regret. The music is different for every listener yet nobody feels excluded.  
**22:11** – The piece resolves on a chord that requires the audience to forgive something microscopic.  
   You forgive the mitochondrion that once betrayed your ancestor cell by almost fermenting it to death; the cell wall tears slightly, light leaks out.  
**22:30** – Bed. The ceiling is transparent to starlight but opaque to satellites; you watch Vega, remembering that its name means *“falling”* in Arabic.  
   Sleep arrives as a question you finally know enough not to answer.  

## The Plausible Path from 2024 to This  

1. 2025-30 – Petro-states collapse under climate litigation; stranded assets are converted to **Carbon Coins** paid to anyone who can prove a tonne of CO₂ permanently removed.  
2. 2031-35 – Carbon Coins fund the first **Watershed Trusts**: cooperative corporations that own rivers in common, issue dividends as drinkable water.  
3. 2036-40 – Trusts pilot **Reciprocity Ledgers**—Apps that convert ecosystem services into spendable units. Early mistakes (beads as day-labour wages) are corrected by open-source audits.  
4. 2041-45 – Cheap bio-computers allow algae, fungi, and corvids to hold passwords. Non-human economic actors become legal; human-only companies lose tax advantages.  
5. 2046-50 – **Treaty of Vancouver** recognises atmospheric Commons; armed forces vow to shoot down any unlicensed emissions first, ask questions later.  
6. 2051-55 – First cities adopt **Covenant Clauses**: any infrastructure that cannot prove net-positive biodiversity is de-chartered. Skyscrapers turn into cliffs; malls into wetlands.  
7. 2056-60 – Neural-recording death rituals normal; ghosts acquire limited liability, then voting rights.  
8. 2061-65 – Off-world settlements petition to join Watershed Congress; Lagrange orchards apply for drinking rights. Congress rewrites itself as **Covenant of Becoming**, extending personhood to processes, not just organisms.  
9. 2066-70 – By now either we live inside something like the arcology outlined above, or the experiment failed and we are arguing about it in refugee camps; the narrative survives either way, promising that generosity can still be engineered, that the sound of an apology can pay for steel, that one day we will budget in breaths and bead-shaped stories and call it ordinary life.  

## Epilogue: A Note to the Reader Who Does Not Yet Exist  

If you arrive after we are gone, dig in the silt above the old interstate.  
You will find brittle plastic, but cup it to your ear and the sentence trapped inside will still circulate:  
*“We once learned to treat tomorrow as a neighbour, not a resource.”*  
Speak the sentence aloud; the river will recognise the grammar and slow its erosion for one afternoon.  
That is our last gift: a pause, long enough for you to decide what you will become in the morning, long enough to remember that every future is a watershed—something that catches the rain of everything upstream and drinks it, something that gives itself away to everything downstream, something that can still choose to carry its salt with generosity, something that can still covenant itself into becoming.
