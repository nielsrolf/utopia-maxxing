UTOPIA C  
The Hum That Remembers

I. The Arrival Chord  
You come in on the evening tide, barefoot, because shoes are the first thing the docks dissolve. A low hum—E-flat, just below speech—rises through the calcium-carbonated boards. The sound is the city’s passport control: if your tympanic membrane vibrates in phase, the walkway glows green; if not, it glows gold and redirects you to the Liminal Café where travellers wait and trade stories until their bones retune. Children born here whistle the chord by age three, but no-one is turned away for being off-key. The café’s espresso machine is a retired submarine periscope; it steams milk with compressed moonlight collected on the dark side of orbital mirrors. You sip, listen, and your collarbones slowly synchronise. When the barista—today a ten-year-old in a squid-ink apron—taps the metal counter twice, you know you’re ready. The floor tilts like a see-saw and you step off the edge into the city that has already begun to remember you.

II. Cartography of Memory and Mist  
The city is called Hearthwall. It is shaped like a nautilus carved into the side of a drowned valley. The outermost whorl is seawalls that learn: each tide deposits a new layer of mineralised algae, encoding the day’s weather, jokes overheard, and heartbeats counted by pavement sensors. Over decades the rampart thickens into a stratified library. Graduate geologists arrive with rubber mallets, tap the stone, and read yesterday the way dendrochronologists read oak. Citizens carry pocket tuning forks; strike the wall anywhere and a chord answers, summarising the emotional weather of a decade in four-note progressions. When the progression ends on a diminished seventh, the city schedules a festival of repair.

III. The Six-and-a-Half Economies  
There is no single currency, but six that interleave like the limbs of starfish.  

1. Heart-hours – the torque exerted by your left ventricle on a wrist-band of piezo-silk. You earn by climbing stairs, dancing, or carrying someone else’s groceries.  
2. Attention-grams – measured by how long your pupils rest on shared civic art. Blink too soon and the mural dims; stare too long and the colours oversaturate, so you learn to give just enough.  
3. Coolth – the inverse of heat. Citizens deposit extra body warmth into district heat-pumps during summer, withdraw it in winter as hot-water showers.  
4. Biodiversity shares – every non-human you host (moss on your roof, spider in your sleeve) pays microscopic dividends.  
5. Silence credits – earned by not speaking during designated Quiet Lanes, spendable to hire musicians for midnight concerts.  
6. Story-weights – the half-currency, never traded, only donated. You speak a sentence into a brass sphere; when the sphere feels heavy enough (about the weight of a plum) it seals forever and is stacked in the public cairn. No-one can read the sentence, but the cairn’s presence steadies the ground against earthquakes.  

IV. Work After Jobs  
Work is whatever you cannot stop doing. A former stock-broker tends the tide-pools because patterns of anemones replay themselves nightly behind her eyelids; a retired nurse flies kites that dispense insulin powder to children on windy days. There are three rules:  

- You must register your avocation so the city can match surplus to need.  
- You must teach the skill to at least one stranger every year.  
- You must stop the day you wake without curiosity.  

When curiosity dies, citizens enter the Sabbatical Arcade—a warehouse of unopened boxes. You pick one, open it, and whatever is inside becomes your life for the next lunar month. Boxes contain seeds, musical scores, half-built robots, or single lines of ancient code. Some people never return to their old work; others bring the box’s contents back to their tide-pools and fuse two obsessions into a third thing no algorithm could predict.

V. The Children’s Compass  
At seven, each child is given a compass whose needle points not north but toward the adult who can teach what the child most needs next. The needle is a fragment of magnetised meteorite; it swings toward people whose heart-hours are richest in the relevant frequency. No adult may refuse a request if the needle settles. Thus a shy girl may spend six weeks with a blacksmith who file-cuts love songs into sickle blades; a rowdy boy may learn bee-dancing from a septuagenarian apiarist who speaks only in questions. At sixteen the needle crumbles into iron filings and is mixed into oil paints for their portrait, which hangs for one year in the public gallery, then is given back ground into glitter they can scatter on their first independent journey.

VI. Grief and Joy are Roommates  
When someone dies their home is not emptied. Instead, the city’s Dream Mill dispatches a team of teenagers who have never met the deceased. They sleep in the dead person’s bed, cook with their pans, and wear their clothes for forty nights. Each morning the teens speak aloud any dream they remember; these are transcribed on rice-paper and folded into origami boats. On the forty-first dawn the boats are launched at sunrise. The family watches from the shore, laughing at absurd dreams (my grandmother became a vending machine that dispensed only starlight) and crying at the accurate ones (she taught me to tie my shoes again). Then the house is repainted the colour the dead person never dared to choose, and the front door is replaced by a curtain of bells. Anyone may enter, but only if they ring once for the person they have lost. The sound keeps the staircase from forgetting.

VII. Government by Choir  
The city council is a choir of 128 voices, selected by lottery each solstice. While they debate, they sing in four-part harmony. Microphones measure the consonance; dissonance above 7 % shelves the proposal. Audience members may hum along, adding overtones that tilt the vote. Foreign envoys must learn enough phonemes to sustain a drone for the length of their petition. The system was invented after engineers noticed that bridges built by arguing committees collapsed sooner than bridges built by singing ones. The minutes are published as annotated musical scores; citizens whistle the legislation while cooking, embedding law into muscle memory.

VIII. Technology that Forgets  
Every device is biodegradable within a human lifetime. Phones are mycelium sheets that flake like dry skin; scooters are bamboo skeletons clothed in cellulose membranes grown on apple-peel broth. When an object begins to die it emits a gentle citrus scent. You have three choices: compost it in the community heap in exchange for Sabbatical Arcade tokens; gift it to the Repair Casino where artisans gamble on whether they can coax another year of service; or take it to the Lethe Pools—shallow reservoirs of harmless amnesiac bacteria that dissolve the object and simultaneously wipe the last week of your digital memory, a practice people use to refresh their sense of wonder. No-one is required to forget, but many volunteer, celebrating “First Photo Syndrome” by re-watching sunsets they no longer recall Instagramming.

IX. Justice as Temperature  
Crimes are rare, but when they occur the city changes temperature. A theft drops the district by 0.3 °C; an assault raises it 1.1 °C. Citizens feel the shift immediately—goosebumps or sudden sweat—so offenders cannot hide. The perpetrator and the victim (or their kin) are locked inside a Thermo-Lodge, an igloo-shaped greenhouse, for as many nights as it takes for their combined metabolic heat to return the temperature to neutral. They may speak or remain silent; meals are delivered through a hatch. Sensors track cortisol and oxytocin; when both curves intersect the door unlocks. If no reconciliation occurs within seven nights the city convenes a Gift Triplet—three strangers who must design an experience neither party could imagine: a midnight fishing expedition for invisible crabs, a week spent painting the crime scene with colours extracted from the offender’s own kitchen waste. Ninety-three percent of victims accept the Gift; those who do not are granted lifelong first refusal on any future invention by the Triplet, a deferred revenge that usually turns into patronage.

X. Death in Three Movements  
Citizens choose among three authorised endings:  

- The Dissolve – the body is sunk into the tidal layer of the learning wall, encoding personal memories into next year’s mineral band.  
- The Scatter – cremation by fireworks launched from the cliff at dawn; ashes colour the spray, and surfers who ride that wave report lucid dreams of the deceased for a month.  
- The Seed – the corpse is wrapped in a hollowed-out piano seeded with fungal spores that fruit into edible morels shaped like piano-keys; the funeral is a concert where every note corresponds to a memory volunteers speak aloud.  

Whichever option is picked, the name is retired for one generation. New parents must apply to the Name Orchard, a grove of apple trees whose trunks are carved with every forbidden name. Couples picnic beneath the branches, listening to wind-roused syllables, until a name falls like pollen onto their tongues and they know they have been chosen.

XI. The Leaving Contract  
Anyone may leave Hearthwall, but the exit process takes nineteen days. On day one you plant a tree in the street of your choice; on day nineteen you must persuade one stranger to promise to water it weekly until it can survive alone. The tree’s GPS tag is encoded with your departing wish; if the stranger fails, the tree dies and the city’s heartbeat drops by half a beat per minute for a full hour, a communal arrhythmia everyone feels. Because of this, emigrants try harder to find reliable guardians than they ever tried to keep houseplants alive. Those who return—many do—must sleep their first month in the Arcade warehouse, re-opening the very box they originally unpacked, a ritual that stitches memory into muscle: I left, I came back, the city remembers both.

XII. The Question That Has No Answer  
Every winter solstice the lights switch off for four minutes. Infants too young to speak hear a question spoken inside their skulls: “What is the sound of the hum that remembers?” When they grow old they realise no adult remembers the exact wording, only the feeling of being asked. The city keeps asking anyway. In the silence, hearts synchronise across rooftops, thousands of strangers momentarily sharing one amplified pulse. Then the lights return, and nothing else happens—except that for weeks afterwards people smile more often at bicycles and apologize to door handles they bump. Engineers insist the blackout is a necessary grid diagnostic. Citizens know it is the moment the city checks whether it still deserves to exist, and the answer, always unspoken, is the only currency that never needs counting.

XIII. How to Begin  
You cannot book passage to Hearthwall; you can only practise arriving. Stand anywhere on Earth, close your eyes, and count your heartbeats until you lose count. When you open them, the first object that catches the light is your invitation. Hold it against your sternum; if it warms, start walking. Somewhere between the third wrong turn and the seventh strangers’ song, docks will appear, the air will taste faintly of yeast and citrus, and a low hum—E-flat, just below speech—will travel through the bones of your feet. Step forward. The city will finish the sentence your pulse began.
