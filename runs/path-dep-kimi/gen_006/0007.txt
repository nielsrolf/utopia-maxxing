UTOPIA  
THE LAGOON OF UNFINISHED HYMNS  

1. Arrival by One-Inch Raft  
Every spring the lagoon re-invents its shoreline. You approach on a raft made yesterday: balsa-foam impregnated with barnacle spores and exactly one inch thick—thin enough that you can feel the pulse of every wave against your ankles. Mid-channel the raft begins to dissolve, releasing a haze of calcium-rich bubbles. By the time you wade the last meter the raft is gone, its mass already re-cast into tomorrow’s footbridge. Nothing enters the lagoon intact; everything is pre-composed into its next use. Luggage is banned, but you may bring one unfinished song. Children learn to write melodies they cannot yet finish; the final note is left blank, a door no one has walked through.  

2. The Choir Ledger  
Daily life is scored on your skin. A photosynthetic vine circles your wrist, tinting from pale jade to deep bronze as you accumulate “choir-hours”—time spent helping others complete unfinished work. Singing carols to a sleepless infant adds a leaf; learning the uke to accompany an elder adds a vein; patching a pneumatic strawberry pipeline converts chlorophyll to gold. Spend your colour too fast and the vine wilts, obliging neighbours to notice. No one is poor, but everyone is occasionally reddish, an outward blush that quietly says: keep me company while I mend my last mistake. The goal is not wealth, but harmony: a community whose ledger is visible, breathable, wearable.  

3. Rotational Residencies  
Buildings are alive and migratory. Every thirteen nights, at 03:17, the lagoon floor inflates with biothermal gas; structures lift on anchor-vines and drift downhill like slow parade floats. A potter’s studio glides past the bakery they once supplied, smelling of wet clay and cardamom. Residents step off one porch and onto another, carrying tool-bags and half-read novels. No one owns rooms; you inherit whatever the tide of architecture brings. This ritual keeps empathy muscular: a coral-biologist spends one fortnight above a tango school, the next among apiarists; every discipline pollinates every other. Addresses are temporary haiku:  
“Fourth terrace / south-east swell / smell of citrus thunder.”  
Mail is delivered by memory-carrying fireflies that glow only when the addressee hums the correct interval—proof you’re ready to receive.  

4. The Parliament of Precedents  
Laws are previous solutions. The central plaza contains a hollow agatized log big enough for two hundred people. Inside, its inner walls are ribbed like vinyl; run a fingernail and a past debate plays—laughter, protest, or the creak of undecided chairs. New proposals must be argued while standing on stepping-stones that rise only when a majority of passers-by find your voice melodious. Thus power is lent, not seized: citizens vote with their feet, literally strolling to the tone they trust. The log grows each year, absorbing the resonant frequencies of whatever resolutions worked. Unjust rules echo sour, discouraging repetition; good ones re-enter civic memory as lullabies learned in kindergarten.  

5. The Ferry of Inevitable Confessions  
Every thirteen years the lagoon drains for three hours, revealing a marble causeway carved with names of those who once tried to leave consequences behind. Residents walk the exposed path carrying lanterns of glow-coral. If you see your name you must step off the stones into ankle-deep water and state aloud what you omitted. The admission is picked up by microphones grown into conch shells; within hours it becomes part of the civic dream-stream, anonymously aggregated into bedtime stories for adolescents. Through this ritual the city metabolises deceit before it calcifies into trauma. Shame dissolves; accountability remains, edible as sea-salt.  

6. Work: the Three-Quarter Economy  
Paid work occupies no more than six morning hours. Employers bid for your mornings with “rhythm rights”—permission to weave their operational beat into the citywide day-song. Accept and your footsteps may be recorded for a factory drum-machine, your hammering may supply back-beat for a tramline jingle. Afternoons are chore-blocked for maintenance of self and place: mending nets, napping, teaching your niece to whistle. Evenings belong to the OPEN SCORE, a citywide improvisation whose theme changes nightly:  
• Monday: imitate a sound you once feared.  
• Tuesday: harmonise with a stranger’s heartbeat heard through a shared bamboo stethoscope.  
• Wednesday: invent a lullaby for something that does not yet sleep—quant computers, freight drones, parking meters.  
Every sunrise the recordings are mixed, pressed into prawn-starch records, and scattered across gardens as slow-release fertiliser. Your labour literally feeds tomorrow’s cucumbers, your off-key glee an audible nutrient.  

7. Schools of Deferred Certainty  
Students may finish nothing. Curricula teem with half-built bridges, uncompleted plays, 87 %-solved proofs. Children specialise in asking better dangling questions rather than securing answers. Teachers award “cliff-hanger badges” when pupils resist the urge to land the plane. Graduation occurs on a raft at the lagoon’s centre: you must first abandon a beloved project—set it adrift on a dissolving dish of beeswax—then swim back alone. If you reach shore before the raft disappears you are pronounced able to live with ambiguity; the city welcomes you into civic problem-solving teams whose only charter is to keep possibilities porous.  

8. The Hospitals of Re-Entry  
Illness is framed as “stuckness.” Therapists are sonic engineers who diagnose by listening to the lag between your voice and its echo off nearby inhabited shells. A long lag implies dissociation; a clipped echo suggests over-absorption of others’ expectations. Treatment consists of composing a duet with the precise interval missing from your personal resonance. Patients rehearse beneath ceramic bells seeded with CO₂-hungry algae; the better the duet, the faster the bells photosynthesize, tinting from milky grey to translucent green—external verification that healing is literally turning breath into sugar. Discharge is granted when your echo locks back into the communal chorus within one-tenth of a second.  

9. Memory Orchards  
Remembering is a public act. Every resident may graft one personal memory onto a fruit tree—crimson memory-mango, obsidian sorrow-pear. The graft alters flavor: bite the fruit and you relive someone else’s recollection for exactly three minutes, then it fades forever. The orchard expands annually into former landfill; we build tomorrow’s soil from yesterday’s secrets. Lovers feed each other memory-figs, employers gift courage-plums to new staff, tourists queue for nostalgia-lemons that dissolve homesickness. Suicide notes become seeds; apologies become cider. The rule is collectivist: harvest what you did not plant, leave what you cannot bear to forget.  

10. Death as a Commission  
Death is voluntary and collaborative. When you decide the plot is complete you commission a composer—often a grandchild still in cliff-hanger school—to finish your unfinished hymn. The composer has one lunar month. During that time you record every sound you still want to taste: dawn rain on tin, lover’s laugh, knife striking cedar. Melody emerges nightly on the dormitory roofs where the city sleeps open-air, ear to sky. On the night of the new moon you lie beneath a copper ginkgo whose leaves act as speakers. The debut is performed by every resident within a kilometre—each sings one note assigned by SMS—producing a spatialised chord that vibrates the ginkgo, whose falling leaves slice moonlight like slow confetti. At the cadence you exhale once more than you inhale, an intentional asymmetry that powers the city’s streetlights for the following night. The finished hymn is uploaded into the tramline click-track; henceforth every wheel passing that junction repeats a bar of your life. No grave, no ghost—only a rhythm citizens dance to on their way to work.  

11. Foreign Affairs: the Mirror Canoe  
Diplomacy travels in reflective canoes whose hulls are polished daily. Delegates row to continental ports but never dock; they simply float offshore, reflecting the skyline of the host city back at itself. While anchored they broadcast the lagoon’s composite day-song on a frequency audible only to children and dogs. Coastal residents wake with melodies half-remembered, strange generosity rising like a tide. Trade is reciprocal: coastal cities send crates containing their unresolved problems—rusting bridges, outdated curricula, orphaned umbrellas. Lagoon citizens weave those broken things into new instruments: bridge-cables restrung as harp-wires, umbrella fabric stitched into recording sails. Months later, a canoe repays the gift with a song legible as advice on the recipient’s own shoreline, lyrics spelt by clamshell arrangement at low tide. Thus the lagoon exports not merchandise, but perspective—reflection returned as refraction.  

12. Identity: the Constellation Exchange  
You are five possible people, but you meet them sequentially, not simultaneously. The city tracks probability, not personality. Birth-certificate sky-projectors cast your newborn chart onto the lagoon surface; those same five stars accompany you through life. At age seven, fourteen, thirty-three, fifty-one, and seventy you visit the Exchange Tower—a hollowed stalactite hanging over the water. Inside, a glass meteoroid splits open to reveal a parallel self who grew exactly the parts you neglected—perhaps carpentry, sarcasm, quietude. You negotiate a trade: one skill, one secret, one regret. The swap is sealed by grafting a single star from their constellation onto yours; the night sky adjusts, imperceptible to outsiders but luminous to you. Identity accretes like coral, layer after layer of discarded and adopted selves, each true for its season.  

13. The Night of Overlapping Hearths  
Once a year, at 3:17 a.m.—yes, the same minute when buildings migrate—the entire city falls into brief hibernation. Citizens curl on roofs, docks, tram platforms. Streetlights dim to ember. In that darkness the dream-feed stitches everyone’s half-dreams into one episodic novella. The shared dream is printed on rice paper at dawn and delivered door-to-door (where doors temporarily exist) as the morning paper. By noon the pages have dissolved into soup stock simmering in communal pots. Lunch tastes faintly of every neighbour’s aspiration; you ingest next year’s collective plot, burp someone else’s dénouement. Having eaten the future, you are free to change it.  

14. Home Is the Interval (reprise)  
There are still no keys. Instead each dwelling opens to the chord that resolved the last argument inside it. If you move in you must first quarrel, then apologise in song; the walls record the conciliatory cadence and set it as passcode. Couples say moving in together is really composing a duet whose coda is furniture. When you leave, the chord remains, audible as creaks to the next occupant. Houses are thus instruments played by successive generations, beams seasoned with contrition, rafters tuned to absolution.  

15. How to Arrive (and How to Leave)  
Book no passage; luggage was already forbidden. Bring only an unfinished song whose last note you have never dared to hear. Stand on any shoreline when the moon approaches its first quarter. Wait until you glimpse a raft of exactly one-inch thickness crewed by no one. Step aboard; feel the pulse against your ankles. Whisper the fragment of melody. Bubbles rise, carrying away your certainty. Wade the final meter, shoes heavy with dissolving salt. A child will meet you holding a vine that matches your wrist—already green, already listening. The city will teach you the rest: how to leave things open, how to compost regret, how to dance to the tramline reprise of a stranger’s life.  

Stay as long as you remain unfinished. Depart the moment you believe the hymn is complete—then watch the lagoon kindly disagree, finishing it for you in a key you never imagined, sung by voices you will never meet, echoing across the water long after your footprints have turned into pearl.
