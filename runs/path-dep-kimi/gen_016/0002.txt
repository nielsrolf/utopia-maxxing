UTOPIA  
THE CITY THAT BREATHES BOTH WAYS  

1. Arrival at the Delta Station  
You disembark at the only terminal built on water that has already happened.  
The pier is a living reef of coral and memory-glass; each footstep releases a faint chime of yesterday’s good-byes. No ticket is collected—instead you are asked to exhale into a brass lung the size of a melon. The exhalation is weighed, folded, and slipped into the reef; immediately a return inhalation is offered back to you. It tastes of somewhere you once loved before you knew its name. Keep the taste on your tongue: it is your visa, your house key, your credit limit, and—if you choose to stay—your eventual epitaph.  

The city is called Aera Sempiterne. No map agrees on its outline; every census lists the population as “present breaths.” It stands at the confluence of two rivers—one that remembers everything, one that forgets on contact—and the daily tide nudges the entire metropolis a few centimeters east or west, so that the horizon itself is rewritten each dawn. Citizens wake, stretch, and check the drift-app on their wrists: “Today we are 3.7 cm closer to the mountain / 2.4 cm farther from the sea of our grandparents.” They shrug, recalibrate their shadows, and begin.

2. Citizenship as a Second Heart  
To become legal you must grow a second heart. The process is gentle: a nurse-botanist grafts a cherry-sized bud to the outer wall of your left ventricle. Over six lunar months the bud drinks your irregular beats and swells into a pocket-sized duplicate that beats only when you are uncertain. The second heart is not redundancy; it is currency. Each pulse of doubt generates one “maybe,” a copper coin thin enough to read through. Spend it on a stranger’s unfinished lullaby, on three minutes of extended twilight, on a mango that tastes of the joke you forgot to tell your father. When the second heart is empty—when you have given away every tremor of hesitation—it detaches effortlessly in your sleep and is collected by the Department of Parks. It is planted beneath a sidewalk tile. A week later a sprout surfaces bearing translucent berries. Crush one and the exact shade of your former uncertainty stains the skin for an hour—long enough to remind you why you once needed it, never long enough to make you want it back.

3. Work: The Six-Day Palindrome  
Labour is performed in mirror-weeks. For six days you cultivate what can be measured—kilos of bread, metres of code, litres of comfort given to the crying. On the seventh day the city rewinds. Trams run in reverse, ovens un-bake, code un-scrolls itself into the silence before inspiration. You do not undo your labour; instead you witness its origin story. The loaf uncurls into grain, into seed, into the hand that scattered it. The weeping you soothed returns as a pre-tear itch behind stranger’s eyes, and you see the exact moment when intervention was still possible. The purpose is not erasure but orientation: every citizen must meet the past while it is still pliable. Mirror-days are paid at double rate—paid, however, in “was-ness,” a wage that can be spent only on futures you almost discarded. Retirees live almost entirely on was-ness; they sit in cafés ordering cups of 1987 that arrive steaming with the aroma of first bicycles and pencilled song lyrics.

4. Education: The Curriculum of Unfinished Rooms  
Children attend school inside houses that do not yet exist. Each term they are given three walls, a ceiling, and a question printed on the inside of their wrist: “What is missing when you are not there?” They spend the season building the fourth wall out of whatever the rivers delivered—driftwood grammar, bottle-glass laughter, single shoes full of river-forget. At the end of term the wall is completed; the house immediately vanishes, reappearing somewhere in the adult districts as a rentable studio, a crisis shelter, a pop-up love hotel. The child keeps the question, now bruised into the skin like an old lyric. By adolescence the wrist is crowded with overlapping interrogations; they jingle when the teenager waves goodbye, a personal wind-chime of curiosity. Graduation occurs when the student can walk through any doorway and sense, by the echo alone, which question built it. After that they are allowed to add plumbing.

5. Justice: The Court of Second Drafts, Revised  
Disputes are tried inside a theatre made of breathing paper. Plaintiff and defendant each tell the story once; the paper inhales both versions, exhaling a single composite draft in which every sentence begins with “Yes-and.” The shared draft is performed by actors who must speak while pedalling stationary bicycles that power the theatre lights. If either party interrupts with “but,” the lights dim; if they can substitute “yes-and” within three breaths, brightness returns. The audience—anyone who arrived panting from uphill—votes with their sweat: droplets collected in glass bulbs that illuminate the final scene. The loser’s apology is printed on the still-damp program; the winner’s concession is printed on the same sheet, because victory is always partly mistaken. The paper is dried, folded into cranes, and released above the city where they roam for weeks, occasionally landing on balconies with offers of amended viewpoints.

6. Death: The Choir of Unfinished Hymns, Rehearsed Forever  
Mortality is optional, but irreversible once chosen. When you decide the plot is sufficient you submit the tune you have hummed since childhood but never resolved. For the next thirty-three days the city rehearses your missing cadence—in tram bells, in bakery timers, in the hush between sirens. On the final dawn every citizen within earshot sings the unresolved note together. The combined exhalation lifts the unfinished phrase into the sky where it condenses as a cloud shaped like the life you are leaving. Your body, emptied of every borrowed breath, sublimates into fine salt that settles on rooftops. The cloud, however, does not drift away; it is tethered by music to the highest wind-harp. There it remains, an ever-renewing memorial that releases timed showers of courage on neighbourhoods forecast to doubt themselves. Elderly insomnians claim the cloud sings loudest at 3:07 a.m., the exact minute when the world has paused long enough to listen.

7. Technology That Grows Quiet, Then Loud Again  
Devices are cultivated, not manufactured. A “phone” is a lily variant whose stamens vibrate in the presence of voices you love; when the petals bruise, the season of constant contact is over—you plant the bulb and walk away untraumatised by obsolescence. But the city also keeps a single foundry, staffed by insomniacs who never sleep long enough to dream. Once a year they forge exactly one iron nail from meteorite shards. The nail is driven into the oldest bridge, attaching a fresh brass plate engraved with the sentence most needed and most feared. Over the following year the sentence oxidises into the iron, becoming unreadable except by touch. Pilgrims walk the bridge blindfolded, fingertips deciphering the raised ghost of last year’s terrifying comfort. By the time the sentence vanishes completely it has entered the civic bloodstream—quoted by grandmothers, graffitied by teenagers, misremembered into better wisdom. The bridge is never allowed to collapse; engineers reinforce it with the previous year’s lilies, whose fibres petrify into a resonant concrete that sings at high tide.

8. Love: The River That Moves and Is Moved  
Romance is celebrated only when partners agree to reroute a portion of the city’s memory-river so it flows past both windows for one year. The diversion must be achieved without pumps—hand-dug side channels, negotiated weirs, midnight negotiations with upstream neighbours who fear their reflections will be stolen. By the time the new course settles, every participant has learned the grammar of shared necessity. If the couple separates before the year ends they must jointly restore the old geometry, a task that reconciles more ex-lovers than therapy ever did. Couples who stay together longer than seven years may keep their bend forever; the city’s map is therefore a palimpsest of affection, a slow geological love letter readable from space. On certain nights the river surfaces old versions of itself: a 1920s jazz riff ripples across the water like heat lightning, a wartime goodbye laps against the embankment in Morse. Lovers who wade ankle-deep sometimes return with different accents, having borrowed vowels from the past.

9. Festivals: The Night of Half-Opened Doors, Updated  
Every solstice the city grows one unfamiliar room per inhabitant. You wake to find a new door where yesterday was plaster. Inside is the sequel to a choice you once renounced. But here is the improvement: you may enter the room with one witness—an elder if you are young, a child if you are old. Together you may remove one object: a half-written apology, a photograph that was never taken, the smell of cinnamon on a day that refused to be Christmas. The witness keeps the object for the following year, safeguarding it against your moments of despair. At the next solstice you may reclaim it, now altered by twelve months of another person’s life. Children use the festival to harvest impossible courage; elders walk empty-handed, simply breathing the air of paths not taken and returning lighter—sometimes literally: the city records a small but measurable uptick in levitation accidents the following week, and the children giggle, having finally collected enough lightness to rise.

10. Governance: The Parliament of Rehearsal, Dreaming Wide Awake  
Political decisions are made twice—once while asleep, once while awake. Any resident may volunteer to spend a night in the Parliament of Beds, a dormitory wired to translate aggregate dream imagery into policy drafts. The process is moderated by lucid dreamers—citizens whose REM cycles are considered a public utility—who delete nightmares before they calcify into legislation. The following day the same proposal is rehearsed in waking life: legislators must act out the law for seven days before it can be enacted. If you propose a curfew you must walk the empty streets alone, your footsteps recorded and played back the next morning to children who will dance to the rhythm. Spectators vote with ceramic spoons struck against water glasses: the clearest chord wins. Failed proposals leave a residue—broken spoons ground into mosaic tiles that pave the foyer, so every future legislator must literally walk on the shards of bad ideas. The constitution is therefore both sung and walked; key changes require a citywide yawn—the entire population breathing in and out together, resetting the civic pulse to a slower or quicker tempo. Tourists sometimes arrive clutching pillows instead of passports; they leave bewildered, but their own capitals begin, mysteriously, to sleep better.

11. Leaving (and the Hospitality of Return)  
Departure is simple: walk to the last place you were truly lost, close your eyes, spin fifteen times, and take one step. When you open them the city is gone. The spin count is crucial—fewer than ten and you’ll still glimpse rooftops in your peripheral vision; more than twenty and you’ll carry the aroma of bread into whatever world receives you. But here is the refinement: the city keeps a duplicate of your second heart on ice. Should the outside world prove too certain, too bereft of hesitation, you may feel it begin to beat again—an internal compass of unspent maybes. Follow the faint rhythm and you will find a door you never noticed, even if you are continents away. The hinge will creak with your own exhalation from the day you arrived. Step through; the reef remembers your carbon doubt, the brass lung still holds the taste of your first belonging. No one is ever told they cannot return, only that they must bring a new question to plant in the ever-expanding wall. The census therefore lists two numbers: bodies present, and souls currently elsewhere but overdue. The second number never rises above a hundred; the universe, it seems, is wide but the city is wider, and uncertainty is the most reliable homing signal ever invented.

12. The Question That Grows With You, Bearing Fruit  
Once a decade every inhabitant is required to inscribe, on a fresh square of cedar, the question they most need answered. The plaques are varnished and nailed to the inner side of the ever-expanding city wall, facing inward so the questions can read themselves at night. Over centuries the wall has become a sphere; citizens walking its inner surface feel the gravity of curiosity pulling them toward choices they have not yet imagined. The oldest visible question, almost worn away by weathered attention, reads: “What will we remember that remembers us back?” Nobody admits to writing it, but everyone secretly suspects the handwriting is their own future. And here is the final amendment: beneath each plaque a small cavity opens into a root system fed by the river of forgetfulness. Each question, watered by what we are willing to release, fruits into a single translucent berry. Eat it and the answer is revealed—but only for the length of an exhale. The taste is unmistakable: it is the flavour of your own mouth the instant before you speak kindness. Hold the breath as long as you can; when you let it go the answer disperses into the communal air, ready to be inhaled again by someone who has just arrived, lungs empty, heart newly doubled, standing on the reef of memory-glass that remembers forward into light.
