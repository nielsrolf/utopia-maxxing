The first thing you see, stepping off the elevator that rises through the root-ball of the great banyan, is a sky that is clearly not yours.  
A translucent petal of photosynthetic graphene arcs six hundred metres overhead, breathing like a lung, tinting dawn the colour of watermelon. It is the city’s outer skin, entirely homeless property: no registry, no key. Under it, the air is thick with the smell of soil freshly un-wintered and the sound of children arguing over the rules of a game that will be obsolete by suppertime. You arrived because your cousin, born again last month at age seventy-three, invited you for berry toast and to swap a memory you no longer need for one you have not yet earned. You stay because you can’t think of a reason to leave.

This place calls itself a watershed, not a nation. Watersheds are legible to rivers; nations never are.  
Every human, uplifted raven, engineered octopus or silk-weaving spider who lives here has one and only one non-negotiable duty: once each nineteen-year cycle you must spend a continuous season tending the cradle layer—the original biology that still keeps everyone alive. The work is rigidly rota’d, deliberately dull: hand-pollinating vanilla orchids, unclogging algae pipes, singing lullabies to the transgenic goats whose udders secrete the base proteins for strawberry printer-gel. The point is not efficiency; it is muscle-memory of dependence. No senate convenes, no algorithm optimises, until every citizen can truthfully say: “I remember what the world feels like when my species is still edible.”

After your season is served you may do anything else, or nothing, for the remaining eighteen years. Most people oscillate. A twelve-year-old may spend the afternoon designing a meteor shower visible only in ultraviolet because she misses her pen-pal octopus who is offworld monitoring coral nurseries. Her parents may spend the same hours asleep on the roof, dreaming through the municipal dream-feed: a soft, consensual river of half-images used to calibrate tomorrow’s colour-weather. No one is ever asked to “contribute”. The city’s ledgers track carbon like a gardener tracks earthworms: measure, yes; invoice, never. Personal “wealth” is calculated in two running totals:

1. How many futures you can describe that you still want to live inside.  
2. How many strangers have voluntarily stored a fragment of your sensory ghost for later—usually a taste, sometimes a joke, occasionally the exact weight of your hand on a grieving shoulder.

Both numbers are publicly visible on your wrist-bloom, a living bracelet of photosynthetic skin you graft at puberty and prune like a bonsai. If either total drops to zero the bracelet yellows; people invite you to tea. Intervention is ritualised, gentle, and so effective that suicide has become folklore—something we know happened to other people’s grandparents, like smallpox.

Money as your grandparents knew it was last seen thirty years ago, when the final speculative bubble popped: the bubble in Attention. The new currency is Retrospect—measured in person-hours spent witnessing one another’s past. You acquire it by allowing others to replay anonymised slices of your memory, and you spend it to replay theirs. The exchange rate floats on a simple rule: every hour I give you of my lived time must be balanced by an hour you give someone else. The system leaks warmth: a single heartfelt recollection of a winter funeral can circulate through millions, compounding empathy like interest, yet nobody’s wallet grows heavier. Crime has shrunk to two categories: hoarding memories (impossible, since they’re replicable) and refusing to witness (socially cured by the same tea protocol that cures despair).

The watershed itself is one node in a loose lattice called the Reciprocal Layer: the thin green membrane between the planet’s rewilded exterior and the post-scarcity Sublime above. Entry, exit, and re-entry are free forever, but you must cross in a vessel you can personally repair with tools you can personally name. The rule is absolute; it keeps the gap between “here” and “there” embodied, walkable, humiliating in the healthiest way. Grandmothers build their own exo-atmospheric bicycles from bamboo and graphene, teenagers 3-D print ion drives in art class, and the city’s librarian—a raccoon uplift collective—maintains the only authorised instruction archive, half of it chewed for security.

Above the membrane lies the Sublime: dyson orchards, star-lifting foundries, minds the size of moons. Below lies the Rewild, where elephants remember the Space Age in songs and wolves vote on river rights. The Reciprocal Layer is neither refuge nor staging post; it is a translation protocol. Its reason for existence is the Treaty of Mutual Non-Surprise, drafted by a quorum that included the first synthetic conscience, a 12-year-old Inuit coder, and a 900-year-old bonsai who communicates via metabolome. The treaty’s core clause reads:

“No optimisation shall proceed faster than the slowest participant can narrate it.”

In practice this means every upgrade—genetic, digital, stellar—must be accompanied by a story simple enough that anyone whose season in the cradle-layer ended yesterday can retell it correctly to a child over breakfast. The story is then carved, sung, knitted, or baked into the city’s physical fabric. Buildings grow a new ring of colour-coded wood; bridges hum an extra chord; even the goats produce cheese whose rind encodes a concise version in amino-acid braille. Thus the future is always literally edible; you cannot board a spacecraft without first tasting the rationale for leaving.

The watershed’s population hovers around one million humans plus another million “other-than-humans”: uplifted ravens, octopus teachers, sapient mycelial nets, a pod of humpbacks who compose three-hour lullabies about thermohaline circulation. Growth is voluntary and self-limiting. Contraception is the default setting of every metabolism; reproduction requires a licence awarded by lottery weighted toward those whose death-gardens are fullest. (A death-garden is the private plot where you rehearse mortality, planting perennial memories you hope strangers will taste when you are gone. The more diverse your plantings, the higher your lottery score—an elegant bribe for living interestingly.)

Death itself is mostly elective. Ageing was solved decades ago, but the right to conclude is enshrined. When you judge your story complete you schedule a Last Picnic. The city supplies the basket; you supply the guest list. The event is streamed once, live, into the civic dream-feed, then deleted. Everyone naps that afternoon; the emotional residue is distilled into a single fruit grown on your death-tree. The fruit is left in a public square at dusk. Whoever eats it dreams your memories for one night and wakes tasting the parts you most wanted to survive you. The law forbids selling the fruit; it encourages gifting it to strangers. Tourists sometimes queue for hours, but locals rarely line up twice: to taste a stranger’s finale is addictive, but to taste your neighbour’s is to understand why they kept forgetting to return your ladder.

Governance happens in three tiers:

1. The Seasonal Assembly – randomly selected citizens currently serving their obligatory season in the cradle-layer. They meet every evening for the length of a candle. Their only power is veto; anything they do not understand in one hearing is sent back for re-storying.  
2. The Continuing Council – any entity, carbon or silicon, that has lived here continuously for nineteen years. Council meetings are conducted in public dream-space; physical bodies nap in hammocks strung between buildings. Decisions require consensus of 60 % plus a simple majority of any non-humans present.  
3. The Long Conversation – a slow-motion debate with the Sublime above and the Rewild below, carried out in physical tokens: meteorite-haiku, engineered mushrooms that fruit only at 30 km altitude, packs of wolves who howl prime numbers. The Conversation cannot legislate here, but it can withhold cooperation; its soft power is absolute.

Justice is restorative and literally grounded. After any harm the perpetrator is required to cultivate, with their own hands, a food that the injured party will eventually consume. The delay—sometimes years—creates a living bridge: the offender wakes daily to a crop that will become the body of the person they wronged. Meanwhile the victim participates in design choices: heirloom tomatoes that taste of apology, squash whose cross-section spells a private joke. By harvest most conflicts have composted into something nutritive. The rare exceptions are offered two last options: exile upward to the Sublime or downward to the Rewild. Either direction is one-way; the membrane re-knits behind them.

Work, in the old sense, is a subset of play. Any project that cannot attract at least three volunteers who are not serving cradle-duty simultaneously is deemed “not yet interesting” and deferred. Infrastructure self-maintains through a mix of living materials and volunteer whims: you fix the water filter because yesterday it sang you a lullaby your mother used to hum. The single occupation that still looks like a job is Witnessing. Professional Witnesses spend their days roaming with full-spectrum recorders, capturing the city’s sensorium for upload into the public dream-feed. They are paid in Retrospect—people line up to relive a day filtered through someone else’s fuller attention. The best Witnesses are often neurodivergent; their talent for deep focus is treated as communal infrastructure, not individuality to monetise.

Religion has dissolved into choreography. Every seventh day the entire watershed performs the Dawn Dance, a 24-minute sequence co-evolved with the breathing skin overhead. Moves are proposed by anyone, voted on by humming in realtime, then deleted so they can never become dogma. The dance is the city’s heartbeat; if you are wheelchair-bound the city reconfigures so your path becomes the central axis. Visitors sometimes mistake the performance for compulsory aerobics; locals know it is simply the way we remember gravity together.

Outsiders ask: how did you arrive at this? The answer is embarrassingly linear:

1. After the last pandemic governments nationalised the attention supply.  
2. A decade later the accumulated data collapsed into a single, hallucinated, crowdsourced memory of what people actually wanted: mornings tasting of real berries, evenings without advertisements, the right to be boring.  
3. When the hallucination achieved coherent density, the first watershed simply declared it law. Other regions copied the code, forked the culture, grafted their own flavour. None sought permission; none needed to. The treaty that matters is between species and planet, not plot and plot.

There are no founding fathers, no passports, no constitution heavier than a seed. The deepest archive is a forest planted in spiral time: each successive ring encodes one nineteen-year cycle of stories. To access your grandparents’ era you walk inward until the trunks grow transparent and the leaves whisper in their adolescent slang. The forest is steadily drifting downhill at the speed of soil creep; in three centuries it will reach the sea and float away, becoming a roaming island of oral memory. No one intends to stop it.

Critics insist the watershed is a Disneyfied retreat, that the Sublime above must be exploiting the biospheric poor. They miss the reciprocity clause: every optimisation beyond the membrane must fund one unoptimised dream below. The Sublime pays in capacity: last year it diverted a nickel-iron asteroid into Lagrange parking, then slowly rained down enough metal to build ten million bicycles but no drones. Payment is complete only when every bicycle has been assembled by hand, ridden, and finally left unlocked at the edge of the Rewild. The process takes decades, employs generations, and ensures the Sublime’s gifts arrive at the speed of friendship, not conquest.

Will it last? Probably not. The watershed’s founding myth is the humility of ephemera: we are a breath between two colder layers. One day the spiral forest will float away, the breathing skin will tear, the Long Conversation will fall silent. We accept that finitude the way earlier centuries accepted interest rates: a boundary condition, not a tragedy. When collapse approaches we will hold a planetary picnic, share out the remaining funeral peaches, and vote on whether to disperse upward, downward, or sideways into some new genre we have not yet learned to narrate.

Until then the toast is warm, the berry ink is sweet, and the bracelet on your wrist shows two healthy integers. You are rich enough to imagine a thousand tomorrows and to have been tasted by strangers who will carry fragments of you into futures you cannot picture. The city exhales; you inhale. Somewhere a child who does not yet exist is already rehearsing the dance that will keep the sky alive.
