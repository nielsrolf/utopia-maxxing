# The Theatre of Mutual Becoming

I wake in a room that is learning to miss me.  
The walls—thin as moth-wing, strong as kinship—are grown from peach-tree cellulose and mycelial thread. During the night they have been dreaming my dreams back to me, edited for clarity. A corner remembers the scent of my mother’s kitchen and is gently leaking cinnamon into the dawn. Another wall, still drunk on last night’s rain, flexes outward to make space for a tiny balcony where a single fig is ripening. The fig is not for eating; it is an archive. When it falls, its flesh will contain the exact pattern of my heartbeat from the last hundred days. I will gift it to whoever happens to be passing on the canal below, and in tasting me they will know whether I have kept the unspoken covenant: to leave each space more interesting than I found it.

This is Reykjavik in 2164, but the city is everywhere and nowhere. It is a touring company: five million humans, two million more-than-humans, and an uncounted chorus of sensors, soils, and stories that check in and out of place the way players swap roles after curtain call. Yesterday the capital was threaded through the limestone spires of former Vietnam; next week it will unpack itself across the basalt deserts of old Nevada. The city pays rent in ecosystem services: a decade of phosphorus drawn from the Gulf, a century of fungal fibres that stitch depleted dust into living crust. Its passport is a smell—petrichor laced with fermented kelp—that migratory birds recognise as home and land on, carrying spores to the next audition.

## I. The Ledger of Leaving

No one here is “from” here.  
Citizenship is earned by what you lighten. Each arriving body is scanned for three accounts:

1. **Carbon to Give** – the tonnes you personally remove from the sky during your stay.  
2. **Stories to Lose** – narratives you are willing to unpublish from yourself so the land can wear them for a while.  
3. **Skills to Borrow** – talents you will teach to whoever needs them next, then forget so you can learn again as student.

Children are exempt; they are the critics. Until age fourteen they wander with black notebooks, awarding or subtracting “attention stones” from adults who forget that every surplus begins as someone else’s scarcity. At fifteen they hand the notebooks back to the city. The stones are melted into glass that paves the riverbeds, so the water remembers what it felt like to be watched.

My own leaving-ledger is thick this season. I owe the touring city 42 tonnes of carbon—already sequestered in the char of last summer’s carrot fields—and two stories I no longer wish to own: the memory of being the fastest coder in my cohort, and the belief that love must be exclusive. The Skills-to-Borrow column is blank; I have requested amnesia for “landscape architecture” so I can apprentice afresh under the penguins who design seasonal snow-gardens on the city’s outer rim. They work only with drift and breath, and erase every pattern before sunrise so no shape grows arrogant.

## II. The Economy of Mutual Hallucination

Work is defined as “making someone else’s dream statistically likelier without insisting on the plot.”  
Payment is the dream flowing back altered.

I spend Tuesdays in the Department of Gentle Catastrophes, a loose guild of scenario-midwives who reheive disasters that refuse to stay hypothetical. We sit in a circle knitted from discarded fishing nets and pass around a single mirror the size of a coin. Whoever holds it must describe the worst future they can imagine that still contains beauty. The rest of us then have six minutes to retrofit the present so that future becomes merely one branch among thousands, no thicker than the others. Last month a twelve-year-old girl conjured a drought so severe that languages dried mid-sentence, leaving conversations as disjointed beads. We responded by installing “listening wells” across rooftops—clay pots that condense fog into whispered vowels overnight. Residents who arrive thirsty in the morning are obliged to drink somebody else’s unfinished syllable and complete it aloud. Linguists have noted the emergence of hybrid dialects whose grammar is shaped by humidity.

Wednesdays I run the Speculative Flesh Lab. Here we grow spare body parts not for transplant but for empathy. A coral reef polyps its way into the shape of a human heart; tourists rent time to slip their hands inside and feel the arterial surge of calcium and plankton. Meanwhile, in the respiration chamber, divorcees breathe through gills grafted from their ex-partners’ lung cells, tasting what the other tasted of air. The divorce rate has not dropped, but the animosity half-life has shortened from 4.7 years to 17 months. Alimony is paid in exhaled nitrous oxide: parties leave laughing, if only at the molecular level.

The central bank is called the Bank of Compost. It issues no currency, only temperature. Bring your shame, your rage, your unread manuscripts. Layer them with coffee grounds and seaweed. In six weeks you receive a thermometer: the precise thermal signature of your former failure. Spend it by pressing the mercury against anyone who needs a fever to finish their own decay. The city’s average body temperature has risen 0.3 °C, low-grade but creative, like a civilization with a mild sinus infection that now sings three notes higher.

## III. Governance as Shadow Puppetry

Laws are written on ice. Every winter solstice the legislature—selected by lottery of those who’ve recently suffered loss—gathers inside the glacier that calves into the harbour. They speak for one night only, carving statutes with heated copper pens. As dawn arrives the glacier calves, carrying the inscribed sentences toward the equator. No one knows which edict will reach which shore; enforcement is oceanic, slow, and salty. Citizens watch the horizon for floating fragments, reading their obligations backwards in the melt-marks. A statute that arrives already illegible is considered exquisitely just: obedience becomes indistinguishable from interpretation.

Local disputes are settled by silhouette. Two parties stand before the midnight sun with their shadows projected onto community bedsheets recently used for love-making. The audience—passers-by, ancestors represented by heirloom vegetables, and at least one migratory bird—watches the silhouettes argue. Shadows distort, elongate, merge; when both parties agree the performance feels true, the mediation ends. The sheet is then dyed with whatever colour the merged shadow suggests. For a week the neighbourhood wears it: scarves, bandages, picnic cloths. The argument’s residue becomes fashion, then fades.

## IV. The Curriculum of Unlearning

Children begin school by forgetting how to count. Numeracy is reintroduced gradually, first through rhythm, then through the negative space between bodies in motion. By age ten they can calculate the exact distance a species must migrate to forgive humanity; by twelve they can divide that distance into heartbeats and carry them in their mouths without drooling superiority.

Adolescence is spent abroad on the Mistake Exchange. Each teenager is assigned a stranger’s error large enough to live inside for a year: a drained swamp, a village forced to relocate, a species pushed to whisper. Their task is to render the error hospitable—plant reeds, record oral histories, design refuges—while recording the precise moment when blame turns into biography. The diploma is a scar, usually invisible, that aches when consensus hardens. Graduates are thereafter prohibited from saying “I would never” until they have parented something non-human for at least one season.

Universities are temporary. One forms wherever a discipline has grown arrogant. Professors are kidnapped (with consent negotiated by bards specialising in seductive horrors) and forced to teach their expertise to an audience of kelp, beetles, and unemployed wind. If the kelp learns to photosynthesize the professor’s thesis, tenure is extended. If beetles chew tunnels that spell a superior footnote, the university dissolves and the professors are released into disguise, forbidden to publish until they’ve forgotten their own jargon. So far, 14 schools have returned to wilderness in this way; literature professors currently hide among migrating caribou, communicating only in hoof-print stanzas.

## V. The Rites of Reassembly

Death remains inevitable, but identity is recyclable. Upon choosing termination (always voluntary, even when the body fails—pain-management has advanced, but dignity prefers a door), citizens attend their own preview. A clone or code-child, aged seven, shadows the dying for one year, learning their gestures, debts, and unfinished jokes. At death the inheritor steps into the vacated space, wearing the same name but with an addendum: a second surname meaning “and-also-not.” The bereaved are allowed six months of confusion, after which society treats the new-old person as a palimpsest: familiar parchment, new marginalia. Memory divorce is available for those who cannot reconcile the overlap; a guild of mnemonic editors will snip or graft recollections until grief approximates continuity.

Those who decline continuity may opt for Diaspora. The corpse is composted inside a theatre built of mirrors facing inward. As decay progresses, stage lights scatter the spectrum of decomposition—russet lignin, jade mold, violet bruise—onto the reflective walls. Spectators (anyone who arrived that week) watch from the catwalk above, seeing only colour, never flesh. The performance lasts forty days; tickets are free but must be paid for by a promise the viewer has not yet made. The collected vows—scrawled on dissolving rice paper—are mixed with the resulting soil and used to plant urban forests whose trees whisper debt whenever wind passes between trunks.

## VI. The Maintenance of Strangeness

Perfection is approached not as stability but as controlled estrangement. Every structure larger than a breadbox must contain at least one element that refuses to behave: stairs whose height adjusts to your most recent lie, bridges that sigh names you almost had, streetlights that bloom only when no one is looking. The rule is enforced by feral engineers—former architects who injected themselves with cicada DNA. They emerge every seven years, singing blueprints that unravel if memorised. Citizens welcome them with empty sleeves; to offer blueprints would insult their metamorphosis.

Leisure is scheduled inconvenience. Once a month the city contracts, subway maps rewiring themselves so every commute requires at least one transfer through a neighbourhood where your native language is gibberish. Passengers arrive late but fluent in the grammar of bewilderment, trading souvenirs of incomprehension like baseball cards. Linguists have recorded a 400% increase in original metaphors, most of them terrible, all cherished.

Even longing is collectivised. The Department of Unfinished Desires operates pneumatic tubes that run between bedrooms. On sleepless nights you may dictate a craving—anything from “snow that tastes like childhood” to “a lover who calls me by my sister’s name and gets away with it.” The tube carries your breath to a stranger who wakes holding an anonymous yearning. They have twenty-four hours to manifest it, photograph the evidence, then release the desire back into the system. No one may fulfil their own wish; reciprocity is the currency. The project has spawned a black market of improbable artefacts: ice spheres recording lullabies in extinct accents, perfumes distilled from the sweat of dancers who never touched, a greenhouse where tomatoes blush exactly like the first time you were caught lying. Economists insist this is waste; the tomatoes taste like permission, and sales of truth serum have plummeted.

## VII. The Century of Encore

History is performed forward and backward simultaneously. Every decade is revised live by the Historical Reenactment Troupe, a rotating cast of octogenarians and toddlers who interpret the same agreed-upon facts through their respective distances from death and birth. The elderly narrate consequences they will never see; the children act causes they cannot remember. When versions diverge too widely, a referendum is held mid-scene and the audience—everyone within radio range—votes by changing their heart rate. Scenes that cannot achieve synchrony are retired into prehistory, said to have happened before language, therefore free of contradiction. The result is a past that hums like an orchestra warming up: cacophonous, anticipatory, impossible to pin down yet unmistakably present.

Futurity receives the same treatment. Forecasters here are required to costume the future in whatever the present finds most embarrassing: polyester, innocence, earnest manifestos. Prediction becomes burlesque, stripping certainty layer by layer until the timeline giggles itself porous. The most respected prophets are those whose prophecies fail spectacularly, ideally in ways that make future generations laugh so hard they forget to dread. Meteorology students currently tour with a routine that forecasts rain composed entirely of apologies for previous inaccurate forecasts. Audiences leave drenched and forgiven.

We measure civilisational health by applause half-life: the interval between the end of any public event and the moment the clapping dies away. When the half-life exceeds four seconds, we know the city has grown too satisfied; the engineers ramp up randomness, unleash the cicada architects, schedule a Gentle Catastrophe. When applause decays in under a second, fear has outwitted wonder; the Department of Inconvenience slows subways, releases unexpected scents of freshly baked bread, projects movies on random walls with soundtracks swapped so lovers kiss to the squeal of stock market opens and revolution blooms under laugh tracks. Equilibrium returns when applause lingers exactly long enough for doubt to mature into curiosity.

Tonight the city pauses mid-tour, anchoring itself inside a volcanic crater on the site once called Detroit. I climb the outer shell to watch the lake that used to be a river that used to be a glacier. Thousands of others stand salted along the rim, waiting for the nightly signal: a single note generated by the world’s largest glass harmonica—an instrument built from windshields of abandoned cars, inscribed with the names of every species whose absence we still feel. The tone rises not like music but like temperature, until separation becomes impossible and the audience realises we are not listening, we are being listened to. The city exhales. Somewhere inside, Aya practices becoming a mushroom, her skin lichening toward spore. My fig is almost ripe. The parliament of verbs debates when to leave, in what tense, how fast.

I think of the cinnamon still drifting from my walls, the dreams I surrendered to the room, the strangers who will inherit them tomorrow and mispronounce my longing until it becomes their own original wish. I feel the scar of someone else’s mistake itching under my collarbone—the wetland I failed to save, now a lung I breathe through whenever I apologise to rain. I remember that perfection is not a steady state but a promise renewed each time we let the unfamiliar inhabit us.

Below, the glass harmonica releases its final vibration. Across the crater rim applause flares, then fades. We hold our breath in the after-beat, counting. One Mississippi, two Mississippi, three— The sound lingers, balanced on the cusp between satisfaction and restlessness. Exactly enough. The future leans forward, hungry, ready to improvise.
