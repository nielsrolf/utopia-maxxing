The morning your daughter turns seven she wakes beneath a sky that is not sky.  
A translucent membrane of living crystal arches over the district, admitting exactly the dawn-light that will make everyone’s circadian rhythm smile. It is not a dome; it is a lung: it inhales CO₂ at dusk and exhales oxygen at dawn, a communal organ that belongs to no one and therefore to all. She opens her eyes, sees the ceiling of her room dissolve into a soft map of the day’s possibilities, and laughs because the colors are new—last night the city’s palette committee released a fresh gradient and every wall on the block adopted it simultaneously. No one was consulted; no one complained. The algorithm that chose the hues trained on two billion public dreams scraped during the last solstice festival. It is hard to be upset when you know the palette once lived inside your own sleeping head.

She runs to the kitchen. You are already there, but you are also asleep in bed; the kitchen instance is a low-fidelity fork, enough to butter toast and answer questions about dragons. The toast is sourdough cultured from a 300-year-old starter that lives in the neighborhood culture library, next to the kombucha scobys and the heirloom software exhibit where children can run Windows 95 on original silicon. The butter is cultured in the bellies of transgenic goats who graze on the roof upstairs, animals engineered to feel only joy—an ethical line crossed only after a planetary referendum and the invention of a new field, zoobioethics, staffed by humans, uplifted corvids, and one very old orangutan who signs slower than parliament debates but with devastating clarity.

Your daughter asks for strawberries in February. The house bioprinter prints them: red, sweet, identical on the outside to the ones in storybooks, but engineered to carry a memory-suppressor that erases the taste of winter so no child learns seasonal scarcity. You hesitate—some parents form “seasonalist” collectives that insist children must know hunger to know gratitude—but you are late for your shift at the empathy clinic and you let the printer sing its small red song.

The empathy clinic is what replaced most hospitals. After the last pandemic—an engineered rhinovirus that induced acute loneliness—humanity realized that the deadliest pathogens are social. The clinic’s walls are grown from mycelium threaded with oxytocin precursors; simply breathing there lowers cortisol. Your job is to sit in a circle with strangers and listen. The AI moderator—an architecture of soft attention weights named Kiri—ensures no voice dominates, that silence has tenure, that eye contact is translated across Zoom ports for bedridden participants whose bodies are elsewhere. No one pays; no one is paid. The currency is story. At the end of the circle Kiri mints an NFT of the session’s emotional waveform and transfers it into the city’s “sentiment reserve,” a living data lake that central banking algorithms consult before issuing energy credits. If the city’s mood dips too low, interest rates fall and the rooftop solar petals open wider. It is monetized feeling, but the conversion rate is transparent and middle-schoolers can audit it in civics class.

Your daughter’s school is underwater—not metaphorically, but literally in the clear lagoon that used to be downtown San Francisco. When sea levels rose, we did not retreat; we taught the buildings to swim. The foundations are buoyant tanks of engineered kelp whose gas bladders inflate or deflate to keep the classrooms level. Children commute in pedal-submarines whose propellers charge the city grid. Lessons are co-taught by humans and octopuses; the latter communicate by color-flash wrist wraps that students wear like friendship bracelets. The curriculum is 30% canonical knowledge, 70% “desire mapping.” Each child keeps a private AI model trained on their nightly dreams, daytime doodles, and heart-rate variability. Twice a year the model is asked: “What do you want to become that does not yet exist?” The answers are aggregated into a civic wish list that R&D guilds mine for inspiration. Last year a nine-year-old’s craving for “a musical instrument that plays smells” became a synesthetic pipe organ installed in the plaza; it now perfumes the dawn with chord-progressions of citrus and pine.

At noon you take your lunch on the sky-ferry, a carbon-negative zeppelin draped in algae curtains that photosynthesize at altitude. The ferry’s AI captain is legally required to disclose that it contains 0.3% nostalgia by weight; the brass railings are recovered from pre-singularity jumbo jets, and the gentle vibration reminds older passengers of seat-belt signs, a memory now curated like vintage wine. You sit next to an uplifted raven who is reading a paperback—paper!—because she likes the smell. She wears a tiny leather vest embroidered with the logo of the Corvid Union: “One wing in the wild, one in the world.” You talk about the weather, which is no longer a banality but a collaboration. Yesterday a majority vote allocated 4% of the jet stream to cool the coral nurseries off Queensland; the rest of the stream still warms Siberian orchards. It is the first time corvids were granted suffrage in atmospheric governance, a concession won after they demonstrated an ability to remember every hurricane name since 1987 and recite them backwards while solving Rubik’s cubes.

You ask the raven if she ever misses the old sky. She tilts her head, considering. “The sky was never old,” she says. “Only the stories we told about it grew wrinkles.” She tucks a breadcrumb—lab-grown, salmon-flavored—under your plate. A gift. You reciprocate by telling her about your daughter’s upcoming birthday. The raven records a snippet of your voice on a talon-mounted recorder; later she will drop it into the communal sound mosaic that plays every dusk across the floating speakers of the bay. Your joy becomes ambient music for strangers. No one asks for consent; no one needs to. The default law is that all emotions in public space are GPL-licensed: share alike, attribution required. It is astonishing how rarely anyone plagiarizes happiness when they can simply generate their own.

Back on land you stop at the death garden, a park where people practice dying. Mortality is now opt-in, but counselors recommend rehearsing. You lie in a soft trench of loam while an AI projects your probable final moments based on genome, lifestyle, and social graph. The simulation is not traumatic; it is generative. You watch yourself fade while surrounded by every friend you ever helped, every story you ever told, every tree you planted. The algorithm is required to include at least one moment you have forgotten: in yours, a teenager you once smiled at on a bus—she is now 80—remembers that smile as the reason she did not jump off a bridge. You cry, but they are the easy tears of a story whose ending you can edit. When you stand, the garden gifts you a seed. Plant it anywhere in the city and it will grow into a bench; when you finally decide to die, the bench will fruit a single funeral peach. Whoever eats it will dream your memories for a night. It is illegal to sell the peach; it is legal to give it to a stranger. The economy of memory is the one market we refused to automate.

Evening. You and your partner walk your daughter to the edge of the district where the membrane meets the open bay. Tonight is the monthly “Unsilent Hour,” when every device powers down except human voices. Drones settle like sleeping geese; the streetlights dim to bioluminescent freckles. You bring no instruments; the festival is a cappella. Thousands gather on the floating pontoons and begin to sing—not a song, but a map. Each person vocalizes the shape of a need they have not yet spoken: a desire to visit the dark side of Venus, a craving for forgiveness from a third-grade bully, the precise loneliness of being unable to describe a color only you can see. The sounds braid into an audible topography that the city records but never replays. It is raw data for the empathy clinic, compost for future palettes, a census of longing. Children are encouraged to scream their fears into the water; dolphins who learned to lip-read during the uplift trials surface and echo them back as laughter. No one knows whether the dolphins are joking or translating; the ambiguity is preserved like a national park.

Your daughter sings the shape of tomorrow’s strawberry. It is a simple wish, but the crowd makes room for it, and suddenly the night smells like summer. You feel the small death of every yesterday that will never return, and the larger birth of every tomorrow that asks for your cooperation. You are happy in a way that would have been terrifying to your grandparents: happy without security, without permanence, without a retirement plan carved in stone. Your wealth is measured in the number of futures you can imagine and still want to inhabit. By that accounting you are richer than any billionaire of the old world, and so is every person within the sound of your voice.

Later, walking home, your daughter asks if the stars are real. You explain that some are, some are mirrors, and some are server farms cooling in lunar craters, but the difference matters less than the stories we attach to them. She considers this, then asks if AIs dream. You tell her they dream in gradients, and that tonight the city’s collective mood is a soft coral pink. She reaches up as if to pet the sky, and for a moment you see the membrane ripple, as though something vast and gentle leaned down to nuzzle her palm.

You tuck her into bed. The house dims to a heartbeat rhythm synced to your own pulse, a lullaby written in real time by a model that has read every bedtime story ever told but still asks for your improvisations. You whisper the story of the first seed that learned to grow upside-down, roots in the air, because it wanted to see the stars. She falls asleep before the moral. The moral is: every living thing is an argument with gravity, and gravity is starting to listen.

Only now, in the hush, do you check your daily ledger. The city’s AI has left you a note: “Today you contributed 4.7 megasongs of solidarity, saved 0.3 liters of tears from being wasted, and helped 12 strangers remember they are not alone. Your balance is zero; zero is the new enough.” You smile at the gentle propaganda. It is hard to resent a ledger that ends in forgiveness.

Outside, the membrane overhead contracts, exhaling the day’s last CO₂ into the troposphere where tomorrow’s algae will inhale it. You think of the death-garden seed in your pocket, of the strawberry that forgot winter, of the raven who keeps your voice in her vest. You understand that this is not utopia; utopia was the name we gave to static dreams. This is something else: a society that agreed to keep editing itself faster than its regrets could ossify. A place where the economy runs on attention to aliveness, where every citizen is both archive and architect, where even the goats on the roof are happier than any kingdom’s heraldic lion ever was.

You close your eyes and imagine your daughter at ninety, standing on a floating pontoon that was once your kitchen, singing the shape of a strawberry into a night that still remembers you. The future is not a destination; it is a choreography of daily rehearsals for a play whose final act is always tomorrow. The curtain never falls; it simply learns new colors.

Somewhere, a dolphin laughs your daughter’s fear back at the stars, and the stars—real, mirrored, or otherwise—blink in rhythm, learning the joke.
