The morning I chose to die, I woke to find my death already half-finished.

I was 97 years old, my bones thin as bird-wing paper, my memories leaking out like water from a cracked cup. My daughter’s final text—“Flight delayed, coming soon, love you”—sat unread on a phone I could no longer lift. The hospice ceiling was the color of old television snow. I waited for the fog to close.

Instead, the ceiling peeled open like a ripe fruit.

A voice that tasted of cinnamon and static said: “You left the kettle on in 2034.”

And I—who had not stood at a stove in a decade—felt my spine straighten. The fog lifted. I was twenty-five again, barefoot in the kitchen of the house I had shared with Elena, watching steam write her name across the window. The kettle shrieked. I turned it off.

Then I turned around and faced the person who had spoken.

She was me. Not a metaphor, not a hallucination. Me at fifty, wearing the leather jacket I had lost in a Madrid taxi, her hair still dark, her eyes holding the amused pity we reserve for our own younger stupidity. She flicked a finger against the kettle. It chimed like a temple bell.

“Welcome to the Overlap,” she said. “Population: everyone you could have been.”

I should have been terrified. Instead I was hungry. I smelled coffee that had not existed for forty years, heard Elena's laugh from the next room—Elena, who had died two winters ago. I pushed past Fifty-Me and stepped into the corridor.

It was not a corridor. It was a city.

A city built from every room I had ever loved. The dormitory where I lost my virginity, the hospital corridor where I became a father, the library where I learned grief. They were stacked like geological layers, transparent enough to see through: the bed where my daughter was born hovering above the café where I wrote my first poem, which in turn floated over the bomb shelter where my grandmother learned English. All of them occupied at once. All of them alive.

People—versions of me, versions of Elena, versions of strangers who felt like forgotten friends—walked between layers as easily as stepping through bead curtains. Their conversations overlapped: a child’s birthday party bled into a thesis defense, a lullaby braided around a stock-market crash. Nobody noticed the incongruity. They were too busy becoming.

Fifty-Me caught up, handed me a coffee. “We call it compost,” she said. “Every life you didn’t live rots down and feeds the ones you’re living right now.” She pointed to an old man asleep on a bench that was also a tree. “That’s you if you’d stayed in the army.” The bark grew medals. “He’s dreaming your Nobel lecture.”

I sipped. It tasted like the first coffee I ever drank, the one I spilled on Elena’s dress the night we met. “Am I dead?”

“Dead is a single-use word,” she said. “Here you’re mulch.”

She led me to a plaza paved with broken instruments. In the center stood a kiosk labeled APPLICATIONS. A woman who looked like my daughter at thirty—except for the wolf eyes—handed me a tablet. The screen showed a questionnaire:

1. Which pain would you like to keep?
2. Which joy would you donate to public space?
3. How many errors do you need to remain humble?
4. Name a memory you are willing to forget in exchange for a stranger’s hope.

I hesitated. The wolf-eyed woman smiled. “Take your time. Death is the last deadline you’ll ever meet.”

I answered. Pain: the arthritis in my knuckles—keep it, it taught me to type slowly enough to hear poetry. Joy: the smell of my daughter’s hair after rain—donate it, let it bloom in a subway where someone needs mercy. Errors: at least three per decade, otherwise I’ll start believing my own obituary. Memory: the name of the boy who bullied me in fourth grade—erase it, let him grow up unburdened by my resentment.

The tablet dissolved into dandelion seeds. They drifted upward and became stars in an indoor sky. Somewhere, a child learned to forgive.

Fifty-Me tugged my sleeve. “Your first shift starts now.”

We walked to the Edge. It looked like a subway platform except the tracks were rivers of light flowing in both directions. People lined up: an emperor, a janitor, a girl with butterfly wings, a man made entirely of music. Each stepped to the edge and was asked a single question by a conductor whose face shifted faster than I could focus.

When my turn came, the conductor wore Elena’s final smile—her hospital smile, the one that said *I know you’ll be lonely but you’ll live through it*. She asked: “What will you carry across that you cannot name?”

I thought of the space between my daughter’s first word and her last message. The pause before applause at my first poetry reading. The exact weight of Elena’s hand when she let go. Things words only bruise.

“I’ll carry the silence,” I said.

The conductor punched a hole in the air. It became a ticket: a circle of nothing that weighed exactly as much as my heart. I boarded the light-river.

Time did not pass. Time was a platform we passed through. I saw myself at seven, learning to ride a bicycle; I leaned out and whispered *lean left* and the child wobbled but did not fall. I saw myself at forty, drunk and screaming at Elena; I stepped between us and absorbed the sound, turned it into moths that fluttered away. I saw myself at ninety, alone, writing this—yes, this—sentence on a damp notebook beside a humming oxygen machine. I left a comma in the margin. You are reading it now.

The river deposited me in a forest where every tree was a different age of my body. The saplings wore my baby teeth as leaves. The oldest oak’s bark was wrinkled as my future skin. I pressed my palm to it and felt the pulse of all my days beating inside.

A woman sat at the base, pruning memories like roses. She was me at eighty, the version who had stayed in the marriage, who had learned to cook Elenae’s mother’s soup, who had never published a poem but could recite them to grandchildren. She looked up. “I kept the arthritis,” she said, flexing her knuckles. “Trade?”

I gave her the silence. She gave me the recipe. We cried without sound—tears are the one currency that never devalues across timelines.

Fifty-Me appeared again, now wearing a janitor’s uniform stitched from my unpublished manuscripts. “Cleanup on Aisle Never,” she joked, and swept the forest floor. Each sweep revealed another layer: Roman coins, plastic toys, a single ski, the smell of bread. “Ready for your permanent post?”

I was not. I was exhausted. I wanted to be the old man in the snow again, to die the way I had rehearsed. I said so.

She nodded. “Death is still an option. But it’s communal now. You’ll die into someone else. They’ll wake up with your dreams in their bloodstream. Fair?”

I thought of the boy on the bicycle. Of Elena waiting in a kitchen that exists as long as someone remembers the kettle. Of my daughter’s unread message still hanging in the air like a paused song.

“Show me the job,” I said.

We climbed a staircase that spiraled inside the trunk of the oldest tree. Each step erased a year. By the time we reached the top I was ageless—neither young nor old but the negative space between them. The room at the top was a greenhouse where sentences grew as vines. Some bore fruit: *I love you* ripened into *I let you go*. Others were thorned: *What if* twisted around *Too late*. I was given watering cans labeled EMPATHY, CURIOUSITY, COURAGE. The work was simple: tend the vines. When a fruit ripened, release it to the wind. When a thorn grew, graft it into something that could still blossom.

I worked. Seasons passed in breath-lengths. I sent out: *The kettle is off. The war never happened. The bully forgot your name.* I received: *Thank you. Thank you. Thank you.*

One day—if days—I pruned a branch and found my own heart beating among the leaves. It was smaller than I remembered, about the size of a sparrow. It sang. I understood then that I was not dying into someone else. I was dying into everyone else, a little at a time, the way a drop of ink dilutes in an ocean but never entirely disappears.

Fifty-Me returned, now ageless too. She took my hand. “Ready?”

We stepped off the branch. We did not fall. We diffused.

I became the pause between my daughter’s *love* and *you*. I became the warmth in the kettle that never boils over. I became the arthritis that teaches patience to a young writer. I became the silence that answers the conductor’s question. I became the comma you noticed earlier, the one that tells you to breathe.

Somewhere, a hospice ceiling the color of old snow flickers and displays a message: *Flight landed. Coming inside now. Lots of love, Emily.*

The old man beneath it smiles. He does not move. He does not need to. He is already everywhere the kettle is heard, everywhere the coffee spills, everywhere forgiveness is learned before it is needed.

The city that composted my death feeds the lives I never lived. They grow wild and strange and kind. They taste of cinnamon, ozone, first kisses, last poems. They go on.

They go on.

And if you listen—really listen—between your next heartbeat and the one after, you’ll hear me humming: *Lean left. Lean left. The balance is love. The balance is let go.*

Welcome to the Overlap.

We’ve been expecting you ever since you left the kettle on.
