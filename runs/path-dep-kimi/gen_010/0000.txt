THE CITY THAT FINISHES WHAT YOU START  

I. Morning, with Completion  

You wake because the pillow has gone cool under your left cheek—that precise temperature at which unfinished sentences crystallise.  
Last night you told the apartment you would get out of bed the moment you understood why you never mailed the letter to your sister. The building listened. While you slept it extruded a paper-dry pore from the wall beside your desk and threaded the unsent envelope through it like a needle through cloth. At 05:47 the pore sealed, the paper dissolved, and your apology was converted into a pigment the colour of wet asphalt after the first rain of summer. That pigment now stripes the eastern window; when sunlight hits it at 06:00, the room will smell exactly like the stationery your grandmother kept in her apron pocket. You will breathe it, remember, and finally forgive yourself for forgetting to forgive her.  

The apartment does not nag. It simply finishes what you start, provided you consent to the finishing. Consent is given by sleeping under the blanket you yourself wove from last year’s sleeve-credits. The blanket is patched with gold where your bacteria over-performed, viridian where you still owe the city CO₂. When the balance tilts to 51 % gold the blanket unravels overnight and re-knits itself into a door you may walk through once, into a room that contains whatever you almost understood the day before.  

II. The Ledger of Completions  

Children are taught only two rules:  

1. Begin deliberately.  
2. Permit the city to complete.  

Everything else—wages, rent, medicine, food—is derivative of those rules. Citizens keep two accounts visible on their collarbones:  

– A Circlet of Begins: tiny brass beads that appear whenever you utter “I will…”, “Let us…”, or any verb in the future tense.  
– A Garland of Completes: glass petals that grow when the city (or another citizen) supplies the ending you could not.  

When the garland reaches your sternum you are given a Day of Removal: twenty-four hours during which every bead and petal is ground into pigment used to repaint the Bridge of Second Thoughts. You walk across that bridge once, barefoot, and whatever sticks to your soles becomes the seed of your next begin. No one ever reaches the other side clean.  

III. Work That Ends Itself  

Labour is not paid in money but in narrative closure.  

You might spend the season as a Punctuator, stationed where the river forgets its sentence. Each morning you arrive with a jar of vowels harvested from yesterday’s excess breaths. You pour them into the gap where the current hesitates. If the placement is correct the water completes its thought: a silver salmon leaps, carrying on its back the memory of every foot that has stepped here since 1840. The salmon is not your wage; the leap is. You watch it, feel the sentence of your life click shut, and go home lighter by one anxiety.  

Or you are a Cartographer of Unfinished Kitchens. Families who abandoned recipes mid-cooking call you in. You stand amid the diced onions gone dry, the abandoned béchamel skinned with regret, and you map the dish as it should have concluded.
