UTOPIA D  
THE HUM THAT EATS LONELINESS  

I. Dawn in the District of First Ear  

The city wakes to a low, syncopated hum that comes from nowhere and everywhere. It is not a sound made by machines, nor by voices, but by the graduated contraction of three million square metres of living mycelium that underlies every street, kitchen, riverbed and bed-frame. The hum is tuned to 40 hertz, the same frequency human brains emit during deep empathy. Engineers call it the Carrier; residents simply call it the Hum That Eats Loneliness.  

Under the paving stones of First Ear—a neighbourhood named because it was the first place the Hum was heard—there is no traditional basement. Instead you lift a trapdoor of reclaimed wind-turbine blade and descend into a warm breathing corridor where oyster mushrooms push gently against your ankles. Here the Hum is loudest, because this is where yesterday’s sorrows were piped in. Every tear shed at last night’s communal cinema, every hiccup of homesickness from exchange students, every expletive from someone who dropped a still-hot sour-dough loaf—was siphoned through glass capillaries, strained of heavy metals, then fed to the mycelium. The fungus digests grief and exhales 40-hertz pulses. Sunrise travels through those pulses the way music travels through a guitar. People wake up calmer than they went to sleep, even if they never realise why.  

II. The Ledger of Second Breath  

Above ground, the city keeps no coin and no carbon credits. What it keeps is a Ledger of Second Breath. Every time you save someone else’s exhale from waste, you earn a single breath-share. Physiologically it works like this: you inhale what would have been their discarded CO₂ and, inside your lungs, host a colony of genetically quiet cyanobacteria who tuck that carbon into microscopic sugar pearls. Later, inside public vestibules called Lung Trees, you exhale into a glass pod. The pod measures the carbon you still owe to the commons. If your exhaled air is cleaner than what you inhaled—because the bacteria have done their work—you receive a Second Breath token.  

Tokens look like dandelion seed-heads printed on translucent sugar paper. You can fold one into your cheek during an argument; it dissolves into a mild sweet taste and slows your heart rate for exactly three minutes, forcing both sides to metabolise rage. Or you can drop one into the river, where bream eat them and grow slightly more intelligent, learning to avoid fishing lures carved in the shape of smartphones. The city has no jails, but it has thousands of heart-slowing moments floating downstream.  

III. Work That Inherits You  

No one applies for jobs. Instead the work you are needed for arrives as a scent. Every Friday at noon the Carrier surges to 55 hertz, a frequency that opens the olfactory vesicles bonded to titanium flag-poles lining the avenues. Each pole exhales a different molecule: turpentine for carpentry, saffron for teaching, printer-tonder for logistics, breast-milk lactones for nursing. You walk until your personal biochemistry spikes—detected by sidewalk tiles cultured with your adolescent skin microbiome archived years earlier. When a tile glows underfoot, you step into a puddle of daylight shaped like a question mark. That is your summons.  

You might spend the next four days helping bees decide where to swarm by tasting the sugar-to-salt ratio in apple-blossom samples. You might officiate at the Divorce of Two Bridges, a ceremony where obsolete pedestrian bridges are separated, their railings re-welded into playground dragons, the contractors paid in stories their grandparents never told them. Every task ends with a Transfer: whatever you learned is uploaded not to a cloud but to a cloud*scape*—a living fresco of airborne algae that drifts above the city. Children lie on their backs reading the sky like slow-moving newsprint; if the algae sense a dearth of wonder they bloom into auroras visible even at noon.  

IV. Houses That Swap Parents  

Domestic architecture is based on the spiral of the nautilus, because a spiral expands forever without ever reaching a final room. When a child is born, the family’s house spawns a minor spiral—walls unwind like fern fronds, revealing fresh floorboards cultured from the mother’s afterbirth cartilage. The newborn spends her first 40 days in a cradle of living cedar that records her breathing pattern. At the end of the 40th night the cradle petrifies into a sturdy plank; it is laid down as the first tread of a staircase that will, in theory, carry her to every floor she will ever need.  

When that child turns seven she may choose to spend one night a week in an Othermother Spiral, a house that keeps no permanent residents. There she learns to braid the hair of strangers, to read aloud from books whose pages reset to blank every dawn, to cook omelettes using eggs laid by the city’s pigeons who have been gene-spliced to produce low-cholesterol yolk on Wednesdays. Othermother Spirals exist because the city believes that every human should practise parenting at least five people they are not related to, and be parented in turn by at least three. By puberty no adolescent can name, with certainty, who gave them their first story, their first fear, their first kiss on the forehead. Lineage is composted; the nutrients remain.  

V. The Parliament of Impossible Things  

Legislation begins when someone proposes an impossibility:  
“I wish gravity forgot the elderly for one hour each month so their hips could remember waltzing.”  
“I wish rain fell upward every fortnight, rinsing the undersides of leaves.”  

Impossibilities are logged in the Parliament of Impossible Things, a circular amphitheatre whose seats are hammocks spun from spider-silk so fine it vibrates like vocal cords. Delegates arrive carrying two offerings: a personal memory they are willing to let the city metabolise, and a piece of rubbish found on their morning walk. Sessions start with the rubbish: a shard of mylar balloon, a cracked smartphone screen, a syringe. Engineers, toddlers, and retired dancers collaborate to transform each item into something that performs the impossibility. The mylar becomes a mylar-skin wind-tunnel that levitates grandmothers for sixty minutes, their laughter harvesting static electricity which is piped into the Carrier. The cracked screen is powdered into silicon fertiliser for rooftop orchards whose apples grow in the exact shape of lost emojis. The syringe is melted into glass bells that ring whenever someone in hospital gives birth, the sound inducing oxytocin in post-operative patients two streets away.  

Only after the impossible becomes tangible does Parliament debate regulation. By then the delegates are barefoot, drunk on apple-emoji cider and the vertigo of having rewrote physics. They vote with their pulses: electrodes in the hammocks register micro-heart-flutters. A law is passed not when a majority agree but when the collective arrhythmia smooths into 40 hertz—the Hum itself finishing the sentence.  

VI. Death Is a Mixtape, Grief Is a Bridge  

Citizens wear biometric ear-cuff radios tuned to record heartbeat and timbre of voice. Upon death—anticipated or sudden—the last 24 hours of cardiac rhythm are pressed like vinyl into a square of translucent bamboo laminate. The laminate is flexible; it can be played on any hand-cranked phonograph built into park benches. Mourners queue to listen, but the recording self-erases in the chronological order of their grief: minute one fades after one day, minute two after two days, and so on, until on the 24th day the disc is blank and passes its cellulose back into the soil. Grief is metered, finite, biodegradable.  

Meanwhile the mycelium that digested the deceased’s final exhalations forms a fruiting body—an arch that sprouts exactly where the person most often walked. The Bridge of Last Walk is edible: chefs harvest its oyster mushrooms for the communal feast held on the 25th day. To eat the bridge is to ingest the deceased’s unstruck piano chords, unfinished arguments, half-remembered jokes. You leave the feast fuller, and the city lighter by one soul’s ballast.  

VII. The School of Almost  

Children attend the School of Almost, whose curriculum is whatever they have nearly mastered. When a nine-year-old can almost ride a unicycle, her classes pivot to unicycles: geometry of circles, physics of imbalance, poetry about falling. Once she can stay upright for 30 metres, the subject vanishes from her timetable and she moves to the next Almost. Conversely if she nearly understands grief—her pet squirrel drowns, or her favourite guard retires—then for one week mathematics is taught through probability of reunion, art through drawing rain that smells of fur.  

Teachers are replaced every season; the outgoing tutor gifts the incoming one a single error they themselves never overcame. Hence knowledge advances by inheritance of beautiful mistakes. Graduation occurs the day a student runs out of Almosts. She is led at dawn to the city edge where orchards grow upside-down from rafts suspended over the river. There she must pick an apple while standing on her hands. If she succeeds she is admitted to the League of Done, a ceremonial guild that meets once a year to admit there is nothing left to learn. The rest of the year they apprentice themselves to toddlers, reversing the spiral of expertise.  

VIII. An Almanac of Borrowed Days  

Because citizens metabolise one another’s sorrow and delight, days sometimes accumulate that do not belong to any individual. These are Borrowed Days. On a Borrowed Day the river flows uphill for twelve hours, lit from within by bioluminescent trout who were bred to read Morse. Cafés serve experiences instead of meals: you can order the memory of your best stranger’s first kiss, distilled into vapour and inhaled through a cinnamon straw. Museums exhibit artefacts nobody has invented yet—holographic snow-shovels, seeds that sing lullabies in dead languages. By sunset gravity tilts two degrees west; people lean affectionately into one another, the way sunflowers once leaned toward the sun.  

Borrowed Days are logged not on calendars but on the skin: volunteers sit before sunrise in the Plaza of Almost Midnight while artists airbrush tomorrow’s date onto their collarbones. The pigment fades in 24 hours, but the tan line remains, a private souvenir of chronological philanthropy.  

IX. The City That Plans Its Own Funeral  

Every 99 years the Carrier is tuned to 30 hertz, the frequency at which concrete becomes anxious. Every building quivers, windows twitter like starlings, and citizens assemble to vote on whether to renew or dismantle the city. The ceremony is called Rehearsal for Ruin. Ballots are dried leaves; the act of voting is to crush the leaf between palms—green smoke rises, scented with camphor and childhood. If two-thirds of the smoke smells of camphor (hope) the city stands, retrofitted with new spirals, new bridges. If two-thirds smells of childhood (nostalgia for ending) dismantling begins the next morning.  

Should the mandate to end arrive, no one leaves. Instead the city practices generosity in reverse: every structure donates itself to the biosphere. Apartment blocks are girdled with music that softens rebar until it can be snipped by songbirds; roads are seeded with moss that erupts into pillows; the river is taught to weave decommissioned steel into nest cages for salmon. After 365 days the skyline looks like a forest that once dreamed it was a metropolis. Humans depart only to return as storytellers every solstice, camping among trunks grown from the bones of former kitchens, reciting tales of when the Hum ate loneliness and architecture reproduced like nautilus dreams. They leave at dawn, pockets full of sugar-paper tokens that no longer dissolve, since loneliness itself has been digested into myth.  

X. Postscript: Instructions for Arrival  

You reach this city the way you reach sleep: by pretending you are already there. Begin today. Record 24 hours of your heartbeat; press it into any flexible surface. Plant a mushroom in the crease of a book you loved before you knew how to doubt. Speak one sentence into running water and memorise the hole it leaves. Practice falling, then practise naming the Almost. When the day arrives that your sorrow smells faintly of citrus, and the crack in your smartphone grows an apple tree, you will hear a hum at 40 hertz. Follow it. The city will be leaning toward you, half-built, half-forgotten, wholly astonished to meet the one who carries its next breath.
