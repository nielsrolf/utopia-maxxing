# The City of Reciprocal Tides  
*A guide for visitors arriving in 2190, written by the first child born in the city and the last adult who remembers the world before it*

---

You arrive at dawn, when the light is still negotiable. The train station inhales you. No doors, no turnstiles—just a soft pressure change that slips into your lungs and samples the carbon of your journey. In seventeen seconds the city knows whether you crossed an ocean under sail or under kerosene; it will spend the rest of your life repaying or collecting that debt, one exhalation at a time.  

You step onto the platform of oyster glass. It is warm. Tiny fractures bloom beneath your footprints like frost in reverse, sealing again as soon as you lift your heel. Every heelprint is a stanza in the city’s ongoing epic: *We have all arrived, we are all still arriving.*

---

### I. The Ledger You Can Taste  
Carbon is not counted here; it is *curated*. Every gram begins as a question—rock, tree, refugee, dream—and ends as an answer someone else needed.  

The mechanism is simple, visible, and edible. At the exit ramp a child hands you a peach no bigger than a coin. The skin is tattooed with a spiral of darker pigment. Bite through it and you trigger a tremor in the city’s fungal lattice: your personal CO₂ tally is now tethered to a fast-growing mycelium seam beneath the district library. By moonrise the peach pit will have darkened into a bead of biochar; by breakfast tomorrow that bead will line an irrigation trench in a rooftop orchard two blocks from where you sleep.  

If your journey was heavy, the peach will taste smoky, slightly ashamed. If you arrived light, it will flood your mouth with the scent of a library you have not yet visited. You cannot fake the taste; the city has been perfecting shame and pride in fruit form for thirty-two years.

---

### II. Money That Forgets Faster Than We Do  
Currency here is a two-breath creature: *Promise* and *Gratitude*.  

1. **Promise** is spoken aloud into the civic air, which is thick with pollen-sized microphones spun by orb-weaver spiders paid in fruit flies.  
   *“I will mend the sail that carries tomorrow’s refugees.”*  
   *“I will teach five crows to distinguish between drone and kestrel.”*  
   *“I will listen to my neighbor’s insomnia until it learns to yawn.”*  

2. **Gratitude** is anonymous, a perfume released by night-blooming nicotiana. One whiff can be converted into a subway ride, a funeral violinist, or ten minutes of slowed time in which to say the thing that must be said. No one can bottle it; skin contact evaporates the scent within an hour. Hoarding is therefore absurd—you would have to keep running to stay ahead of your own wind.

---

### III. Work as Gardening the Unmeasured  
Each citizen (and the city defines that word within five minutes of arrival) tends an invisible crop. You announce it each dawn on the Window of Unfinished Things, a slab of wet marble in every dwelling. The water darkens into words, then vanishes.  

Today’s crop: *The pause between a flash-flood warning and the moment someone decides whom to text.*  

Tomorrow’s: *The embarrassment felt by an algorithm that almost mistook a laugh for a cry.*  

Payment arrives only if a stranger later claims to have harvested your yield. Proof is conversational—two minutes on a rooftop with someone who felt the pause lengthen, the embarrassment soften. The city keeps no ledger of these encounters; instead the marble in your kitchen pales by one shade. When it has bleached to the color of dawn you are considered solvent for the season. White marble is the only bank balance that exists, and it is visible to anyone you invite inside for coffee.

---

### IV. Buildings That Dream You Back  
Housing is grown from a slurry of demolition debris, elderberry resin, and the pulverized scripts of canceled soap operas. Walls photosynthesize by day and narrate by night.  

Lie against the wall of a new apartment and you will feel micro-vibrations—sentences being born. If your neighbor’s sorrow spikes, the wallpaper in the stairwell deepens toward indigo, releasing a scent of cedar and apology. Conversely, when the rooftop grapes ripen earlier than expected, the façade blushes gold and elevators begin to skip every second floor, forcing laughter-filled encounters in the corridors.  

There are no keys. To enter you press your pulse against the door until it learns the rhythm of your loneliness. From that day on the door will only open for heartbeats in the same emotional key. Guests must be accompanied by their host, or else climb the façade—an act the building permits if the climbing song is beautiful enough to make the ivy sway in 3/4 time.

---

### V. Schools Where You Graduate by Almost Dying  
At thirteen you are sent to the House of Elsewhere, a warehouse on the tidal flats. You are given twenty objects and three years to become someone who would have owned them.  

The objects are never fair. A child from the interior receives a snorkel, a coral fragment, and a recording of whale song slowed until it resembles a lullaby from another planet. A dockworker’s daughter inherits semaphore flags, a 3-D print of dark matter, and a pair of ballet shoes still stained with river silt.  

Graduation requires a public death scene: you must stage the demise of the person you invented, in front of strangers who have come to believe in her. The audience arrives wearing Mood Rings charged with communal empathy; if the majority pulse synchronizes during your final monologue, the city approves you as fit to make real decisions. Those who fail are not punished; they are hired as permanent audience members, professional witnesses paid in stories that are later grafted onto hospital walls to calm the newly grieving.

---

### VI. Justice as a Slow Song  
Courts convene only during eclipses. Plaintiffs and defendants must co-compose a lullaby that includes three verifiable facts from each side. The performance is broadcast on the city’s root-network; night-blooming plants absorb the melody and metabolize it into fragrance released at the next sunrise. Citizens walking to work inhale the verdict in the form of a scent that lingers until coffee dissolves it. The scent is wordless, but everyone recognizes the texture of irresolvable truth: a note that makes the sternum ache slightly, then releases forgiveness as citrus.  

Appeals are possible: you must keep the plant alive for a full year, singing the lullaby to it every night. If the shrub fruits, your case is reopened. If it dies, the original scent becomes stronger each spring in every park you enter, a reminder growing annually more tender.

---

### VII. Death as Public Fabric  
When your body chooses its final season you select one of four civic destinies:  

1. **Wind Library** – Your vocal cords are spun into filament suspended between rooftops. Every gust vibrates a different sentence you once loved, audible only to children chasing paper airplanes.  
2. **Time Swings** – Tibias become the swing set chains in a playground where each push propels the rider three subjective seconds into a future that feels like déjà vu.  
3. **Error Fog** – Lungs diffused into mist that drifts between office buildings autocorrecting typos in resignation letters, making *regret* read *regard*, *ending* read *mending*.  
4. **Soil Chorus** – Skin composted into the topsoil of community gardens; tomatoes grown there taste of every apology you never spoke. Survivors gather once a year to make sauce, laugh, and forgive you again.  

Before transformation you record a single regret. The city encodes it into a woodlouse that will live out its days beneath a random sidewalk stone. Hundreds of anonymous regrets click softly underfoot after rain, a private percussion citizens recognize as the sound of being human.

---

### VIII. Leaving, or the Illusion Thereof  
Should you decide to exit, walk to the perimeter canal at dusk and announce your departure by skipping a stone. The ripples will propagate outward, never rebounding; engineers long ago replaced the shoreline with an infinite absorber of goodbyes.  

Once the water stills, you will find the city has already forgotten your name in every registry. But the peach tree remembers your carbon; the ivy recalls your climbing song; a stranger’s heartbeat still matches the door you taught to open. These fragments do not add up to residence, yet they tug like sweet ghosts whenever you taste citrus in a far-away kitchen.  

Most travelers return within a season, drawn by an unfinished question inscribed on the inner wall of the world: *What will we remember that remembers us back?* The city grows by accretion of such returns, each arrival adding one more layer to the living palimpsest.  

You are here now. Speak your promise aloud. The air is listening; the tide is agreeing; somewhere a crow is rehearsing the punchline of a joke you have not yet invented. Swallow the peach. Begin.
