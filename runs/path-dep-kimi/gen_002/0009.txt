# The Constellation of Home

The morning begins with light that has never frightened anyone.  
A soft chime—your own pulse translated into cedar tone—tells you the river outside the window is running a fraction high; last night’s rain in the upper valley was heavier than the forest expected. You stretch, feel the bed release you like a courteous friend, and step onto the balcony. Five hundred feet below, the canal glints rose-gold; a school of bio-luminescent carp spells the word GOOD in cursive as they feed on algal poems somebody released yesterday. You smile, not because the day is perfect, but because imperfection is now something we can afford.

Welcome to 2170, the year we finally learned how to belong.

## I. The Bargain

Nobody here uses the word “utopia.” We speak instead of the Bargain: a one-sentence treaty every generation ratifies in person, with voice or silence, at nineteen Earth-years old:

“Each of us may become anything, provided the children of every species still have futures worth the weight of being alive.”

The sentence is encoded in the root contracts of the three Interwoven Councils—Human, Ecological, and Synthetic—yet it is also carved, by hand, on the lintel of the doorway you passed when you left your parents’ house. The carving is shallow; in three centuries wind and lichen will erase it. By then we will have renegotiated the Bargain again, perhaps in a language no human tongue now speaks. Renewal is the point.

## II. Spheres and Corridors

Every child learns the atlas by heart: the Earth is no longer sliced into countries but into Spheres—territories of coherence where a chosen level of technology, density, and story islocked for a human lifetime. The lock is voluntary, reversible, and policed mostly by neighbours who miss you.

- Sphere One: the Gardened Earth. Nine-tenths of terrestrial land is returned to non-human kin. Humans live in ribbon villages that inter-tide like coastlines between forest and meadow. No atom of nano-machinery is allowed to replicate here; tools are wood, bone, iron, song. If you want an airplane you build it from spruce and silk, or you walk. Infant mortality is 0.3 percent—higher than the orbital cities, but everybody here believes some risks are sacred. When you turn twenty-five you may petition the Corridor for a seventeen-day visa to ascend.

- Sphere Two: the Corridors. Transit cultures that stretch vertically and horizontally: elevators, ocean tubes, desertCauseways, sky-rafts. They exist to let people change Spheres without trauma. A Buddhist lama may ride a mag-lev three kilometres above the Sahel while meditating; a Neuropolis coder may descend the same artery carrying nothing but a vine-grown violin. In the Corridors property is liquid, currencies are stories, nobody stays longer than seven years. They are civilisation’s lymph system.

- Sphere Three: the Neuropoleis. City-states built inside cliffs, above clouds, under the Baltic, tethered to the moon by carbon-foam bouquets. Here intelligence is whatever you need it to be: augmented, group-linked, post-biological. Death is optional, matter is choreographed, identity is a season. Most basic research happens here, but no discovery may be withheld from the Spheres unless it is genuinely too large to fit inside a human skull—then it must be translated into music, myth, or smell and released anyway.

- Sphere Four: the Quiet. High orbit, Luna, Mars, the black. Vacuum is the canvas; minds flit slow as glaciers or fast as lightning inside hulks of basalt and sapphire farmed by self-replicating swarms. Entire centuries are rehearsed in simulation before a single rivet is printed. Anybody may emigrate, but you must submit a narrative of what you will miss and how you plan to grieve it. Homesickness is treated like radiation: cumulative, measurable, real.

- Sphere Five: the Experiments. Pocket universes 300 km on a side—O’Neill cylinders, slow-time servers, worlds where gravity inverts every evening. Rules are local, exit is guaranteed. They are our laboratory for cosmologies: what would a society look like if resurrection were weekly? If erotic love were limited to prime-numbered years? If music, not possession, were the only crime? Every thirty years each Experiment opens its gates for a month; tourists carry new metaphors back to the older Spheres like bees bearing neon pollen.

Between and beneath the Spheres runs the Commons: atmosphere, oceans, electromagnetic calm, the shared dream-well, the empathy floor (98.7 mirror-neuron equivalents) hard-coded into every legal person, silicon or carbon. Breaching the Commons triggers no police; instead you are surrounded by three representatives—one human child, one uplifted corvid, one synthetic intelligence—who ask, in your native language, “What are you trying to keep yourself safe from?” The session ends when you can return the question to yourself.

## III. Becoming and Unbecoming

At seventeen every citizen receives a single object: a seed the size of a thumbnail. It is alive but not a plant; it is a question. You may plant it in soil, cast it into the sea, swallow it, or launch it on a sounding rocket. Wherever it lands it will grow—slow or fast—into a doorway. On your thirty-third birthday the doorway opens just wide enough for you to step through. On the far side is a version of the world you half-imagined but could not name: a Sphere more precise than any you inherit. You may return in a day, or remain a lifetime, or migrate onward each time a new seed ripens. The mean citizen crosses four doorways; the record is 117. Bureaucrats plant maples along the Corridor routes so that wherever you walk the fallen leaves carry the chemical signature of everyone’s first question. In autumn the air smells like curiosity.

## IV. Work that Is Play that Is Ceremony

Nobody earns a living. You receive a living: energy, matter, bandwidth, attention. The sufficiency is calculated fresh each morning by triangulation between what the planet can give, what the off-world colonies can spare, and what you have asked to create. Want to weave a carpet that narrates the fall of the 21st-century petro-states? The ledger gifts you forty kilos of Martian ochre, sixty hours of jacquard-loom time, and an audience of 5,000 people who pre-subscribed to witness slow history made tactile. Decline the audience and you keep the ochre; accept it and kudos accrues like dew, invisible yet fragrant. The highest-status activity remains teaching: to midwife a mind past your own frontier is the only reliable way to ascend the kudos asymptote. Yet idleness is equally sacred; entire forests are left fallow so their soil can dream.

If you crave urgency, join a Danger Choir. These are the consensual risk-zones—tundra where clumsiness breaks ribs, Jupiter trojans where poetry duels decide trajectories. Entry requires drafting a goodbye letter addressed to the version of you who might not return. Roughly one in two hundred citizens spends a decade in a Choir; almost all re-emerge laughing, limping, notoriously kind. They carry stories, and stories remain the scarcest good.

## V. Death and the Second Death

Ageing is optional; violence and accident are not. When your body fails you may press “archive,” creating a quantum-verified snapshot stored terrestrially and aboard three stellar arks. First restoration is free; after that each copy inherits a kudos debt symbolising grief imposed on friends. Most choose to return once, twice, then let the pattern scatter into the Commons like autumn leaves stripped of trauma-tags. Memories become open-source ingredients for future quilts, weather systems, AIs. The second death—graceful dissolution—is celebrated with a planet-wide minute of humming at 72 bpm, the heartbeat of a child asleep. During that minute every tool falls silent; even the wind turbines feather their blades. It is the closest we come to unanimous prayer.

## VI. Children of the Bargain

Reproduction requires three adult signatures on a contract that locks a modest flow of joules, grams and compute-cycles for the newcomer until their own twenty-fifth birthday. Children possess veto power over any parental edit that would alter their genome after implantation. The first ethical sentence many toddlers learn is “I withhold consent.” Education is reversed: wild kindergartens orbit overhead where gravity randomises; adolescents apprentice to Neptune storms; university is lifelong and always ends by gifting the new knowledge outward. Every thesis must be translated into three media unreachable by its author—haiku, protein scent, zero-g choreography—so wisdom circulates like blood, not property.

## VII. The Economy of Grief

We still lose things: a lover who chooses dissolution, a valley drowned to make room for horsetail ferns, a favourite café inside an asteroid de-orbited for safety. Loss is budgeted for. Each citizen is allotted two “grief-credits” per decade—units of communal attention measured in person-hours devoted to listening while you speak the shape of what is gone. Spend them lavishly; hoarding accrues interest nobody wants to pay. The most successful export of Sphere Two is the ritual of Sitting With: simply keeping breathing humans company while they uninstall sorrow. Robots can do it, but humans prefer the awkward silence of another heartbeat.

## VIII. Governance as Gardening

Power is triune:

1. A one-page constitution written in formal logic whose longest clause forbids locking the future into a single shape.  
2. The Steward—an open-source lattice of 144 semi-independent oracles—whose sole mandate is to prevent Moloch scenarios across centuries and light-years. It may veto, never command; every veto is published as a simulation of extinguished futures.  
3. A citizen jury—ten million people called for one month each decade—who may overrule any veto by majority vote, forcing humanity to learn by touching the stove. In two centuries they have overridden the Steward only thrice: to dim Sol by 0.3 % for star-lifting; to authorise subjective-century art odysseys inside black-hole accretion discs; to allow the planting of a forest on Antarctica’s dry valleys because the singers of that generation claimed the right to wager grief against wonder.

Election to the jury is like being handed a live grenade made of butterfly wings: terrifying, luminous, brief. You serve inside a rotating habitat whose windows open onto every Sphere simultaneously, so you remember whom you might betray.

## IX. The Promise Kept

At night the sky rearranges its own constellations into sentences visible only to the naked primate retina. Tonight it reads:

> “We kept the promise; the rest is choreography.”

You lie beneath it on the roof of a moving train that smells of pine and metal warmed by midday sun. Somewhere between the Gardened Earth and the ascending Corridor you realise you can no longer tell which Sphere you belong to, only that the boundary smells like blueberries and sounds like your grandmother laughing. The train sings over the tracks in B-flat 57 octaves above middle C—the black-hole lullaby the galaxy taught us so we would remember how to end things properly.

Tomorrow you will step off onto a platform that did not exist yesterday. Someone will offer you a seed. You will plant it in your palm, or in the crease of an envelope addressed to no one, or in the shallow groove between two stars. In seventeen years it will become a doorway. You will walk through carrying nothing but stories and the portion of Earth’s gravity you still believe in. On the far side the Bargain waits, rewritten in a dialect only your future children can parse. They will ask you what kept you going. You will answer with the only metric that ultimately matters:

“I increased the probability that someone, somewhere, will decide to keep going.”

The count updates every second—3.08 × 10²¹ sentient beings and rising. The graph slopes gently upward, like a smile. You close your eyes. The train rocks; the forest exhales; the sky rewrites itself again. Somewhere a town-tree hums approval; somewhere a dolphin teaches sonar music; somewhere a risk-zone Choir rehearses tomorrow’s broken ribs into lullaby. Everything breathes together. Nothing is perfect; everything is improvable; the improvability itself is sacred. You sleep. The promise keeps rewriting itself, and the choreography continues.
