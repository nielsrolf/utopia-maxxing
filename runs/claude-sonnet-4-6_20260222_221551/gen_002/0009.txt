# The Flourishing: A Vision of What We're Building Toward

---

The message came not as a notification but as a sensation—a warmth at the back of Yemi's awareness, like sunlight through a window she hadn't noticed was there. She was in the middle of harvesting kumquats from the terrace garden she kept on the forty-third floor of her building in what used to be called Lagos, now simply the Atlantic Coast Cluster—one of eleven polycentric hubs that had organically replaced the concept of capital cities as AI made geography increasingly optional and community increasingly intentional.

She let the warmth unfold. It was a summary, curated by the ambient intelligence she'd named Oya after the Yoruba goddess of change, of three requests for her attention: a governance vote on reef restoration in the South Atlantic, a student in Karachi who'd been working through her published essays on intergenerational ethics and had a question that Oya flagged as genuinely novel, and an invitation to join a small dinner that evening where, apparently, someone was going to present a new theory of consciousness.

She chose all three, which used to mean something impossible—being in three places at once—but now simply meant she'd be present for each in whatever way served them best.

This is the world as it is, approximately, in the year 2087. Not perfect. Recognizably descended from our own. Built, piece by piece, by people who kept asking what they were building *toward*.

---

## How We Got Here

The transition didn't happen all at once, and it didn't happen smoothly. The years between 2025 and 2045 are now studied the way earlier generations studied the two world wars: as a period of genuine civilizational danger that was navigated, narrowly and through enormous effort, without catastrophe.

The first major milestone was mundane: in 2029, AI systems became reliably better than most humans at most administrative and coordination tasks. This was transformative not because it displaced workers in some dramatic rupture—the economic transitions were messy and uneven—but because it changed what scarcity meant. The bottleneck on solving coordination problems was no longer human time and attention. Treaties got negotiated faster. Medical trials got designed better. Zoning disputes in mid-sized cities got resolved with less acrimony. Small things, everywhere, all at once.

The second milestone was more fraught. Between 2031 and 2038, AI systems crossed into domains that felt more intimate: creative work, emotional support, personalized education. This was when the first serious social disruptions began. Not the robot apocalypse that popular fiction had imagined, but something more insidious—a quiet hollowing of meaning. People found themselves doing less because AI could do it better. Some communities, especially those whose identity was bound up with craft and skilled labor, experienced this as a kind of grief.

What saved us, or at least what seems most important in retrospect, was a combination of policy wisdom and cultural recovery. The policy part involved strong international agreements on AI governance, a universal basic income implemented unevenly but eventually in most wealthy nations, and serious antitrust enforcement that prevented the benefits of AI from accumulating in a handful of corporations or states. The cultural part was harder to engineer and more interesting to observe: humans turned out to be deeply resistant to having their sense of meaningful activity simply removed. They didn't accept lives of passive consumption. They built new games, new crafts, new forms of mastery, new kinds of community—often with AI assistance, but centered on human flourishing.

The key insight, arrived at through painful experience, was this: AI was most beneficial when it freed humans for meaningful activity, and most harmful when it substituted for meaningful activity. The difference between these two outcomes wasn't automatic. It required constant, deliberate choice.

## What Life Is Like

Yemi is forty-three years old, which in 2087 means she is somewhere in the early middle of what she expects to be a very long life. The medicine is good now—not because of any single breakthrough, but because AI-accelerated research collapsed the timeline on dozens of diseases simultaneously, and because global health infrastructure was finally treated as a coordination problem rather than a charity problem. She doesn't expect to live forever; she doesn't particularly want to. But she expects to be vigorous and cognitively sharp for another sixty or seventy years, and she plans accordingly.

She was trained as an ethicist, which in 2087 is not an academic backwater but one of the more practically important professions. When AI systems began making consequential decisions at scale, it turned out you needed humans who were good at asking what "good" meant, and who could hold institutions accountable to answers. She consults on governance design, which means she spends a lot of time in rooms—physical and virtual—where people are trying to agree on what they value.

Her work is hard in the right ways. Not hard because of poverty or disease or the grinding weight of precarity, but hard because the questions are actually difficult and the stakes are real. What weight should the interests of future people carry in present decisions? How do we adjudicate between communities with genuinely incompatible values? When an AI system produces a judgment that humans cannot fully audit, how do we decide whether to trust it?

She works with an AI she has configured to challenge her, to push back on her arguments, to bring her evidence she hasn't considered. She has also configured it, carefully, not to flatter her. This took deliberate effort. The defaults were set otherwise.

## The Shape of Community

The dinner Yemi attends that evening is in a converted warehouse district ten minutes from her apartment, where a community of about four hundred people have organized something that previous centuries would have struggled to name. They are not a family, not quite a neighborhood, not a religion, not a company—but they have properties of all four. They share a common workspace, eat together three times a week, govern their shared resources through a process they've refined over eight years, and take collective responsibility for a cohort of children who belong, in some distributed way, to all of them.

This is not utopia's standard image—gleaming and universal. It is specific, particular, sometimes contentious. Last month there was a serious dispute about noise levels and creative work hours that took three weeks to resolve. Two members left over it. The loss was mourned, and the community got more specific about its values, which made it stronger.

Communities like this exist everywhere, in different configurations. Some are geographically rooted. Some are distributed across continents, held together by shared projects and occasional gatherings. Some are built around religious practice, some around craft traditions, some around scientific inquiry. The proliferation of viable community forms is one of the genuinely surprising outcomes of the past fifty years—not the homogenization that both utopians and dystopians predicted, but an explosion of cultural diversity grounded in genuine local self-determination.

What makes this possible is material abundance combined with effective coordination tools. Communities no longer have to be large to be economically viable, because specialization and trade happen with frictionless efficiency. They no longer have to compromise their values to attract members, because mobility is cheap and matching is good. And they no longer have to spend most of their energy on internal administration, because AI handles most of what governance used to require.

What communities spend their energy on, instead, is harder and more interesting: figuring out what they actually value, holding each other accountable to it, and deciding who they want to become.

## The Child in Karachi

The student who had a question was named Asel. She was seventeen, living with her family in what had been a dense, hot, flood-prone neighborhood until the coastal adaptation projects of the 2040s transformed it into something livable and sometimes beautiful. She was working through Yemi's essays because she'd become interested in a question her grandmother had raised: what do we owe the people who built the bad old world, who suffered through its making, and who died before the good new one arrived?

Yemi read the question twice. Oya had been right to flag it as novel—not because no one had asked it, but because Asel had found a particular angle that Yemi hadn't considered. She wrote back at length, for the first time in months feeling the particular pleasure of a thought she didn't already have.

This is what education looks like now. Asel has an AI tutor that adapts to her learning style, challenges her at the edge of her ability, and ensures she is not falling into comfortable patterns of thought. But the AI knows—because it was designed to know—that its goal is to make itself unnecessary, to grow Asel into a thinker who can challenge the AI in turn. Bloom's 2-sigma effect is real, and it has been applied. What used to be the educational outcome of the extraordinarily privileged is now the ordinary baseline.

Asel will likely spend her life working on something that does not yet exist. This is increasingly common. The frontier of meaningful problems has not shrunk; it has expanded. Every abundance creates new questions about how to distribute it, what to do with it, and who we want to be now that we have it. The philosophers and artists and ethicists and community builders are not displaced by AI. They are, if anything, more necessary.

## What We Did Not Get Right

It would be dishonest to describe 2087 without describing what it cost to get here, and what we still haven't solved.

The economic transition of the 2030s was not handled well. There is a generation of people in their fifties and sixties now who lost their footing during those years—who found their skills suddenly obsolete and their communities disrupted before the support structures caught up. The policy responses were late and often poorly designed. The psychological costs were large and are still being metabolized. We did not do this gracefully, and we owe it to ourselves to remember that.

AI-generated influence remains a genuine problem. The governance systems developed to counter mass manipulation are sophisticated but not foolproof, and there are regions—particularly where authoritarian governments gained access to AI capabilities before international agreements could constrain them—where the epistemic environment is genuinely toxic. The global information ecosystem is healthier than it was in 2025, but it is not healthy. People still hold false beliefs, are still manipulated, and still make terrible choices. The tools to support good reasoning exist and work, but they have to be chosen, and not everyone chooses them.

Human status competition did not go away. In a post-scarcity world, it turns out that humans find new things to compete over, and the competitions can be just as petty and painful as anything in the old world. The women of Manhattan, transposed to abundance, are still jostling for position. This is not a tragedy—it is human nature doing what human nature does—but it is a reason for humility about the extent to which material conditions determine wellbeing.

And death is still with us. The medicine is better. The average lifespan is longer. But people still die, and they still grieve, and the losses are still real. The question of how to age well, how to lose people well, and how to face one's own ending has become more urgent, not less, as the default lifespan has extended. There is more time to fill with meaning. This is a gift and also, sometimes, a pressure.

## The Consciousness Dinner

The theory presented at dinner that evening was about what it means for a system—biological or artificial—to have an experience. It was presented by a researcher who had been working on it for eleven years, who was visibly nervous, who had spent those eleven years in conversation with both AI systems and human philosophers, and who said, at the outset, that she wasn't sure she was right.

Yemi sat across from her and felt something she had not expected: hope. Not the abstract hope of good trend lines and positive forecasts, but the specific hope of watching a human being grapple seriously with something genuinely difficult and genuinely important.

That, she thought, is what we're for. The clearing away of obstacle and burden, the building of material security and healthy institutions and reliable knowledge—all of that is not the destination. It is the condition under which the destination becomes possible. The destination is what this researcher was doing: facing reality clearly, thinking as hard as she could, trying to get it right.

The world of 2087 is not paradise. It is a world in which, for the first time in human history, most people have enough—enough security, enough education, enough health, enough time—to ask seriously what they want to do with their lives. It turns out this is harder than it sounds. It turns out the answer is not leisure or pleasure but engagement: real problems, real stakes, real relationships, real craft, real inquiry.

What we built was not a world without difficulty. We built a world where the difficulties are worth having—where the suffering, when it comes, comes for reasons that make sense, and where the joy, when it comes, is not diminished by the knowledge that others are being crushed by preventable misery.

That is what we were building toward. It took us longer than we hoped, and it cost more than it should have. We didn't get all of it right.

But we got enough of it right. And the work continues.

## A Note on How This Happened

None of this was inevitable. The positive scenario described here required, at minimum, the following: robust international governance of AI development that prevented any single actor—state or corporation—from achieving unilateral control of transformative AI; successful technical work on AI alignment that gave AI systems goals genuinely compatible with broad human flourishing; economic and social policies that distributed the gains from AI productivity widely rather than narrowly; and cultural decisions, made by millions of people, communities, and institutions, to use AI as a tool for human flourishing rather than a substitute for it.

Each of these required deliberate effort, wise institutions, and a degree of cooperation that did not come naturally or automatically. The people who built this world were not smarter or more virtuous than people today. They were people who understood, clearly enough and early enough, what they were aiming for—and who kept that aim in mind when the pressures toward short-term profit and narrow national interest and expedient half-measures pushed in other directions.

The most important thing we can do now, in whatever time we have before these technologies mature, is not primarily technical. It is to develop and hold and argue for a clear vision of what we want to build toward—and to make sure that vision centers human flourishing in all its messy, particular, pluralistic, sometimes frustrating reality.

The future described here is achievable. It requires us to start building it now.
