# The House of All Knowledge: A Vision of the Future Commonwealth

## Prologue: A Dispatch from 2089

*The following is a composite account drawn from the experiences of a traveler arriving in the city of New Meridian, located in the territory formerly known as the American Midwest. The traveler has come from one of the last holdout regions still organized around the political structures of the early twenty-first century, and she is recording her impressions for a documentary project. Her name is Adaeze.*

---

I arrived by train, which is the preferred mode of entry for first-time visitors. The committee that manages the city's relationship with outside territories discovered, decades ago, that arrival by train produces a particular quality of attention in the arriving mind—the landscape unfolds gradually, the transition from outside to inside is made visible, and the newcomer arrives already oriented to the fact that she has entered somewhere different. They are deliberate about such things here.

The train descended from the low hills east of the city in the late afternoon, and I watched the city assemble itself through the window. What struck me first was not any single building or structure but a quality of proportion—nothing here had been built to dwarf the human being. The tallest towers stood perhaps fifteen stories, and they were interspersed with mid-rise structures and open green corridors that ran like rivers between the built environment. Later I would learn that this was not accidental. The city's spatial planning is governed by a principle they call *human legibility*: the idea that a person standing anywhere in New Meridian should be able to orient herself by eye, should be able to see sky and green from every street, and should never feel architecturally overwhelmed. The principle sounds simple. Achieving it across a metropolitan area of four million people took forty years of sustained effort and some remarkable AI-assisted coordination between thousands of independent builders, municipalities, and property arrangements that I will describe in more detail later.

At the station I was met by a coordinator named Babatunde, whose role—he was careful to explain—was not precisely that of a guide or a bureaucrat, but something more like what an earlier century would have called a concierge, except that the concierge serves the guest's actual interests rather than the hotel's. Babatunde's job, as he described it, was to help me understand what questions to ask, to introduce me to the right people for those questions, and to flag when my assumptions about this society were leading me to misread what I was seeing. He had been doing this work for eleven years. He found it, he said, endlessly interesting.

"Most visitors arrive with one of two frames," he told me, as we walked through the station's great hall, where light fell through a glass ceiling onto wooden benches and small gardens of fern and moss. "Either they expect a technological utopia—clean machines, everything optimized, human beings essentially managed by systems—or they expect something smaller and more artisanal—a slow city, a craft economy, people rejecting technology to recover human scale. We are neither. Understanding that we are neither is the beginning of understanding what we actually are."

---

## The Problem They Solved First

New Meridian did not emerge from a single decision or a founding vision. It emerged from a long process of convergence, beginning in the 2030s, when several overlapping crises created the conditions for genuine experimentation. Climate disruption had made certain forms of agricultural and urban organization untenable. The concentration of economic power in a small number of technology companies had produced what historians of the period call the *coordination monopoly*—a situation in which the infrastructure of human cooperation (the platforms through which people found jobs, traded goods, organized communities, accessed information) was owned by entities whose interests were not aligned with the people using them. At the same time, AI capabilities were advancing in ways that made it clear that the technology would be transformative regardless of whether anyone planned for that transformation.

What the founders of New Meridian understood—and what I think is still underappreciated in the regions I come from—is that these crises shared a common root: the problem of *who controls coordination infrastructure*, and *for whose benefit*.

The answer they arrived at, after years of debate, litigation, constitutional experimentation, and some spectacular failures along the way, was: coordination infrastructure must be governed as a commons, and it must be designed explicitly to serve the people who use it, with accountability mechanisms that allow those people to change it when it fails them.

This sounds abstract. Let me make it concrete.

---

## The Coordination Layer

In New Meridian, there is an institution called the Coordination Layer. It is a combination of technical infrastructure, governance structure, and civic practice. It was built and is maintained by a consortium of the city's universities, its municipal government, and a network of what they call *service cooperatives*—worker-owned and community-governed enterprises that provide most of the city's basic services.

The Coordination Layer's AI systems handle the logistics of matching: jobs to workers, students to learning opportunities, medical specialists to patients, housing to residents, research questions to researchers. They handle the background work of scheduling, resource allocation, and administrative processing. They operate with a degree of capability that would have seemed impossible thirty years ago.

But—and this is the crucial architectural decision—they do not make decisions. They make the information and options available for human decision. They reduce the cost of finding out what your options are. They handle the tedium of paperwork and bureaucratic form. They surface the relevant facts when you need to make a choice. The choice remains yours.

Babatunde showed me the interface during our first day. It is simpler than I expected—more like a very capable and patient librarian than like the commanding AI systems of science fiction. You describe what you are looking for or what you are trying to accomplish, and it helps you understand the landscape of possibilities and connects you with relevant people and resources. When I asked it to help me find researchers studying urban design, it produced a map of relevant work, identified the people doing it, summarized their projects in terms I could follow, and—when I indicated interest—reached out on my behalf to arrange introductions. The introductions still required human meetings. The conversations were still human conversations. What the system had done was eliminate the weeks or months of searching, cold outreach, and institutional gatekeeping that would otherwise have stood between me and those conversations.

"We think of it as restoring the village," said one of the system's designers, a woman named Marte who had grown up in Norway and done her graduate work on the anthropology of pre-industrial information networks. "In a small village, everyone knows roughly what everyone else does, who has particular skills or knowledge, who is struggling and might need help, what resources are available. That social knowledge enabled a kind of low-cost coordination that large anonymous societies destroyed. We can't go back to literal villages—the scale and diversity of modern life is genuinely valuable—but we can recreate some of that informational commons at much larger scale."

The Coordination Layer is not free of politics or conflict. There are ongoing debates about what information it collects and how it uses it, about whether its matching algorithms introduce subtle biases, about the terms under which external visitors and organizations can access it. These debates are conducted in the open, and the governance structure—which I will not describe in full, but which is elaborate and takes seriously the importance of distributed power—provides genuine mechanisms for changing how the system operates when communities determine it is failing them.

---

## The House of Understanding

Every city has its symbolic center, the institution that embodies its deepest values. For New Meridian, that institution is called the House of Understanding.

It is housed in a complex of buildings near the city's geographic center—buildings of different ages and architectural styles that have been connected over decades by enclosed bridges and shared gardens. From the outside it looks like a slightly chaotic collection of structures that have grown together organically, which is exactly what it is. Inside, it is one of the most intellectually alive places I have ever been.

The House of Understanding is, at its core, a research institution. But it is a research institution with an unusual mandate: its job is not merely to produce knowledge but to ensure that knowledge is *used well*. This mandate has shaped everything about how it is organized.

The House employs what it calls *Translators*—researchers whose specific expertise is in bridging domains. A Translator working at the intersection of climate science and urban planning does not simply read papers in both fields; she builds working relationships with the scientists and the planners, participates in their projects, and develops the practical understanding of what each field can actually offer the other. The House currently employs some six hundred Translators, working across several hundred intersections of knowledge.

The House also employs *Implementers*—people whose job is to take knowledge that exists in research literature and work out, in specific detail, how it might be applied in the city and its surrounding region. There is an ongoing project, for instance, translating decades of research on learning science into redesigned curriculum frameworks for the city's schools. Another is working on applying recent advances in materials science to the retrofitting of older buildings for energy efficiency. The Implementers work in close collaboration with the communities and institutions that would actually use what they develop.

And the House employs what it calls *Stewards of Uncertainty*—researchers whose specific job is to identify what the city does not know, to map the limits of current understanding, to flag when the city is making decisions based on poor evidence, and to ensure that the production of new knowledge is directed toward the questions that most need answering rather than the questions that are easiest to study or most convenient for existing institutions.

The AI systems that support the House's work are among the most sophisticated in the city. They are used, primarily, for three things: synthesizing existing literature across vast domains, so that researchers can quickly understand what is already known; identifying unexpected connections between different bodies of knowledge; and modeling the likely consequences of potential decisions, so that communities can understand what they are choosing between before they choose.

The results of modeling are always presented with explicit uncertainty ranges and are always accompanied by the reasoning behind the model—what assumptions it makes, what it does not account for, where its predictions are most and least reliable. There is a strong institutional norm, enforced by the House's governance structure, against presenting AI outputs as more certain than they are. The norm has a name: *epistemic honesty*. Violation of it is treated as a serious professional failure.

## What People Do

This is the question that visitors ask most often, and usually with an undertone of anxiety: if AI systems handle most of the administrative and logistical work of the city, if routine cognitive labor is largely automated, if the Coordination Layer dramatically reduces the friction of matching and transaction—what do human beings actually do all day?

The question contains an assumption that the people of New Meridian have mostly stopped making: the assumption that human activity requires economic justification to have value.

Most adults in New Meridian work, in the sense that they contribute something to the cooperative economy and receive in return access to housing, food, healthcare, education, and the other goods of a comfortable life. The contribution required is not large—the productivity gains of the past fifty years have made it possible to sustain a very high standard of material living with far less labor input per person than the early twenty-first century required. Most people contribute something like twenty to twenty-five hours per week to what the city calls *necessary work*: teaching, caregiving, building and maintaining physical infrastructure, running the farms and food systems, managing the governance processes that keep everything running. Beyond that, their time is their own.

What do they do with it?

Everything, it turns out.

I spent two weeks trying to catalog it. I found: serious amateur scientists conducting genuine research through the House of Understanding's citizen research programs. Musicians practicing and composing and performing—live music is ubiquitous here in a way that surprised me; it turns out that when people have time and security and access to instruments and instruction, many of them make music. I found elaborate collaborative storytelling communities, some of which have been running for decades and have developed mythologies and fictional worlds of extraordinary depth. I found a remarkable density of skilled craftspeople—woodworkers, ceramicists, weavers, glassblowers—whose work is bought and traded and given as gifts and simply accumulated in homes with a pleasure that no mass-produced object seems to provide in the same way.

I found people in long, unhurried conversations in the many cafes and public gardens of the city. I found people caring for elderly parents and young children with attention and patience that is hard to imagine in a society where time is scarce. I found people learning—languages, instruments, mathematical subjects, practical skills—not for career advancement but because the learning itself seemed to them worth doing.

I found, also—and I want to be honest about this—some people who seemed lost. People who had not found what they wanted to do with their freedom, who spent too much time in digital entertainment or low-grade social status competition or the particular kind of aimless dissatisfaction that comes from having needs met but not yet having found a calling. The city has invested heavily in what it calls *meaning infrastructure*: mentorship networks, communities of practice, apprenticeship programs, counseling services oriented toward the question of how to live rather than just how to manage symptoms. But it has not solved the problem of meaning—no institution can solve that problem—and it does not pretend to have done so. The Stewards of Uncertainty have a standing research program on wellbeing and meaning, and its findings are regularly uncomfortable for the city's self-image.

## The Question of Power

I asked Babatunde, near the end of my stay, what he considered the city's most important ongoing problem.

He thought for a while.

"The same problem every human society has always had," he said. "How to keep power distributed. How to prevent the people and institutions that are most effective from accumulating enough power to stop being accountable."

The Coordination Layer could, theoretically, be used to concentrate power. The House of Understanding could become a priesthood of experts whose authority is treated as beyond question. The city's most effective leaders and organizations could entrench themselves. These are not hypothetical risks—there have been genuine crises, over the decades, in which one or another of these tendencies has had to be actively resisted.

The resistance has taken the form of institutions: term limits, mandatory rotation, deliberate distribution of the resources that enable AI capability, open-source requirements for the core infrastructure, and an active culture of what the city calls *power vigilance*—the ongoing civic practice of watching for accumulation and raising the alarm when it is detected. There is a specific institution, called the Office of Accountability, whose sole mandate is to monitor whether the city's major institutions and the individuals within them are operating within the bounds set by the city's founding commitments. The Office has no power to act directly, but it has the power to compel transparency and to publish its findings, and the city's political culture treats its findings seriously.

Is it enough? I don't know. No one here claims to know. What they claim—and what seems to me plausible—is that the problem of power cannot be solved once and for all; it can only be managed, continuously and vigilantly, by people who understand that it is always there.

---

## What the City Knows About Itself

On my last morning, Babatunde took me to the gallery on the top floor of the House of Understanding's oldest building. The gallery is called the Hall of Honest Accounting. It contains, displayed in simple frames with plain typography, a permanent exhibition of the city's most significant failures.

Not its successes—its failures. The decisions that were made on bad evidence and turned out badly. The predictions that were confidently made and were wrong. The initiatives that were well-intentioned and caused harm. The moments when the city's governance failed and the powerful were not held to account. The times when the city's commitment to inclusion was revealed to be shallower than it believed.

The exhibition is updated regularly. There is a committee—including members of communities that were harmed by each documented failure—that governs what is added and how it is described.

"Why?" I asked.

"Because we want to know what we are," Babatunde said. "A society that only knows its successes is a society that has stopped learning. And a society that has stopped learning will eventually fail in ways it does not see coming."

I stood there for a long time, reading. The failures were real and some of them were serious. And the city existed, and was trying, and was genuinely better—by most of the measures that matter to actual human beings—than the place I had come from.

That combination—honest about failure, genuinely better, still trying—is, I think, what a real utopia looks like. Not a place where everything has been solved, but a place where the people understand what they are trying to do and are doing it together, with open eyes.

I took the train home the next morning. The hills folded back over the city behind me, and I turned to my notes and began to write.

---

*The infrastructure of New Meridian was built over six decades, beginning with municipal broadband and cooperative housing initiatives in the 2030s, expanding through a network of institutional innovations in the 2040s and 2050s, and reaching something like its current form in the 2070s. The path was not smooth. It involved political failures, technical setbacks, economic crises, and hard conflicts about values that were not always resolved well. A full history would be valuable, and several scholars are writing it. This account is an outsider's impression, offered not as a complete description but as an invitation to imagine.*
