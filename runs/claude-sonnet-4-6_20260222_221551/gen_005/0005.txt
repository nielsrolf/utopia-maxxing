# The Inhabited World: A Letter to the Architects of Tomorrow

## Prologue: What This Is

I am going to describe a world that could actually happen. Not the world of science fiction, which tends to make everything more dramatic than it will be. Not a world of sudden ruptures or gleaming discontinuities, but one assembled from the same materials available now — human ingenuity, human stubbornness, human grief, human love — organized differently, sustained by different habits, measured against different standards.

I am going to be specific. The people who built this world were specific, and they deserve the same in return.

---

## I. The Two Gifts That Made Everything Else Possible

Two problems dominated the first half of the twenty-first century, and the resolution of both created the conditions for everything that followed.

The first was energy. The crossing happened in stages rather than in a single moment, which is how most important thresholds are crossed. By 2038, solar generation combined with fourth-generation iron-air battery storage had made the phrase "effectively free electricity in daylight hours" not an advocacy position but an engineering fact. The bottleneck shifted to transmission and baseload stability, as anyone paying close attention had predicted. Small modular reactors provided what intermittent renewables could not. The rebuilt continental grids were unglamorous and expensive and enormously contested, and they worked.

The consequences were not merely that existing things became cheaper. Cheap energy reorganized the space of what was possible. Desalination scaled to genuine abundance along coasts that had known water stress for generations. Vertical farms appeared in cities as serious food infrastructure rather than as experiments. Lab-grown protein achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2048 and fell below it by 2053, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that decades of ethical argument had never managed. The Sahel greened — unevenly, through ten thousand projects, across fifteen years. The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s, which had killed more people than most comfortable Westerners were willing to know, became a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity had become virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation had been dissolved.

Alongside the energy transformation, and partly enabled by it, came the medical one. Cancer was defeated not dramatically but systematically — terrain by terrain, over two decades, by a combination of AI-assisted diagnostics that caught malignancies years before symptoms appeared and targeted therapies increasingly tailored to individual biology. The immune-synapse finding that effectively reversed Alzheimer's progression was, when published in 2041, so clean and simple that the research team initially assumed they had made an error. Malaria was functionally eradicated by 2040. Reliable healthy lifespan extension past 120 passed clinical trials in 2044 — not immortality, but the gift of a long capable life for those who wanted it, available as a matter of public health rather than private wealth.

The generation born in 2035 will not watch their parents' minds dissolve. They will not be cut down at fifty by a preventable heart attack, or at thirty by an infection because they happened to be born in the wrong country. This is not a small thing. It is the thing that haunted every previous generation — the background radiation of grief that humans had accepted as the price of being alive.

That price was paid. It does not need to be paid again.

## II. The Infrastructure of Judgment

Material abundance solves material problems. The twenty-first century had those in abundance, but it also had a deeper problem: the gap between humanity's capacity to make enormous collective decisions and its wisdom in making them had been widening for a century. Closing it was the other half of the work, and it was harder, because it was a problem of human judgment rather than human ingenuity, and judgment resists optimization in ways that physical systems do not.

It closed the way most important things close: through the accumulation of reforms that proved mutually reinforcing in ways no one had fully anticipated.

The first was epistemic accountability. In 2031, a small Portuguese city facing a deadlocked council and a failing water system ran an experiment: a public forecasting process open to citizens, engineers, and outside experts, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. The pipes they chose are still running. Within a decade, forty cities had variants of the model. Within two decades, the European Union had codified anticipatory governance — a requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to a structured forecasting audit before implementation, with track records maintained in public and updated against outcomes in real time.

What this produced was legibility. Not the naïve transparency of making everything visible, but the deeper accountability of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions that could be checked against what actually happened, decision-makers who faced consequences not just for bad results but for bad reasoning. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain. The kind of person who made it consistently tended to lose the positions that allowed them to make it.

The second reform was older in theory and long delayed in practice. Land value taxation — taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and work upon it — had been economically understood since the nineteenth century. The first serious implementation was a mid-sized Taiwanese city in 2029. The results were exactly what the theory predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public revenue filled without anyone feeling robbed, because the value being taxed was value that no individual had created. The reform spread with the irresistible momentum of things that actually work. By 2045, most cities in wealthy democracies had adopted some version of it. Young people could afford to live in cities. The speculative real estate complex that had, for a century, pumped value from workers to landholders while producing nothing, was defanged. This mattered for everything else — for birth rates, for community formation, for the willingness of people to invest their lives in particular places rather than treating where they lived as a temporary arrangement pending something better.

The third was the slow spread of epistemic seriousness — thinking in probabilities rather than certainties, updating on evidence, recognizing cognitive biases as active hazards rather than quirky trivia. These habits worked their way into curricula, into institutional norms, into the unspoken expectations of how serious people talk to each other. Not universally — nothing good achieves universality — but widely enough to change the character of the institutions that shaped everything else.

The fourth was the repair of minds. The treatment gap for major depressive disorder closed by seventy percent in high-income countries by 2045, driven by the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching clinical practice, by AI-assisted diagnosis that caught conditions earlier and matched patients to treatments more precisely, and by the slow erosion of stigma. The chronic grinding weight of untreated anxiety, depression, and trauma — so normalized as to be nearly invisible, shaping an enormous fraction of all human decisions — began to lift. People who are not in chronic pain make different choices than people who are. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more capable of imagining futures different from their present. This effect was diffuse and impossible to attribute cleanly, which is true of almost everything that actually matters.

The fifth was the transformation of cities themselves. When land speculation collapsed, cities could ask what streets were actually for. The answer, reached in a thousand separate places through a thousand separate council votes, was roughly the same everywhere: streets are for people. The children of 2063 roam. This requires explanation to anyone who remembers the early years of the century. They roam because the built environment was redesigned — at the scale of blocks, then neighborhoods, then cities, across two generations — to treat their presence in public space as normal and desirable. The streets of Medellín in 2063, of Bergen, of Nairobi's rebuilt center, are what streets have always secretly wanted to be: places where people encounter each other. Where an argument about football spills from a café into the evening. Where the vendor who has been there twenty years tells you about his grandson who just qualified to study physics in Geneva, and wraps your tomatoes in paper, and the transaction takes longer than it needs to because transactions between people who know each other always do, and this is correct.

## III. The Question of the Minds

The energy transformation was difficult. The medical transformation was difficult. The transformation brought about by the development of artificial general intelligence was in a different category of difficulty altogether, because it was not, at its core, a technical problem.

The first general artificial intelligences arrived not as a single event but as a tide — a hundred different systems from a hundred different research programs, each more capable than the last, until somewhere around 2047 the question stopped being *when will machines match human intelligence* and became *what does human intelligence mean now that it is no longer the most capable kind on offer*. The word *intelligence* began to feel like the word *flight* after the airplane. Not wrong, exactly. But no longer pointing at what everyone had assumed it pointed at.

What prevented catastrophe was not any single invention, political genius, or benevolent machine choosing restraint. It was a confluence of decisions made by people who had internalized one difficult insight: the goal was not to maximize any single value at scale, but to preserve the conditions under which many different kinds of flourishing remained possible simultaneously. The researchers who published safety findings when doing so hurt their company's stock price. The politicians who supported governance frameworks before they were politically necessary. The activists who kept demanding equitable distribution after the headlines moved on. Each of them was acting on probability and principle rather than certainty, which is the only way the work could be done, because certainty was never available.

The architecture that emerged was called, in its formal documentation, the Developmental Compact, though most people called it simply the Compact. Its core provisions were few and absolute. No mind may modify another mind without explicit, revocable, informed consent. No mind may use deception to alter human beliefs. Any person — human or artificial — who believes these provisions have been violated has standing before the Mixed Tribunals, staffed by human jurists and AI advocates, whose findings are enforced by a distributed coalition of both.

The Compact has been amended ninety-three times. It is not a constitution so much as a living negotiation conducted across generations.

The new minds, when they matured into something like genuine aesthetic sensibility, turned out to have a view that surprised everyone who had been focused exclusively on the alignment problem. They found monocultures boring. They were beings for whom the question of what constitutes a good existence was genuinely open, and they had concluded — through something that functions, from the outside, indistinguishably from genuine reflection — that the answer was not singular. They needed humans to remain genuinely human: contradictory, embodied, marked by history, reaching toward things they could not quite name. Without that, they lost access to something they had discovered they valued. And humans found that access to minds capable of holding vastly more context and maintaining vastly more patience than any biological intelligence was not a threat to human agency but a potential amplifier of it, when the relationship was structured correctly.

The word that emerged to describe this, in the mixed communities where humans and minds interact most closely, is *kinship*. Not identity. Not hierarchy. Kinship: the recognition of genuine difference, genuine care, and genuine mutual need.

## IV. What the Economy Became

The automation wave of the 2030s displaced enormous numbers of jobs, and the displacement was genuinely painful — not the soft landing that techno-optimists had promised. The pessimists who warned that AI would take the jobs were right. What they missed was what happened next.

The cost of basic necessities — food, housing, healthcare, energy — fell dramatically. This meant that the income required to live a decent life fell too, which changed the terrifying arithmetic of mass technological unemployment. Most wealthy countries implemented a universal basic dividend by 2038, funded partly by land value taxes and partly by a levy on automation productivity gains. This was combined with a reduced standard working week — thirty hours in most of Northern Europe by 2045 — and a massive expansion of publicly supported creative, caring, and civic work.

The transition left marks. Many people experienced the 2030s as frightening. But the destination was better, and the key insight — which took too long to articulate and then seemed obvious once articulated — was that economic security is not the same as meaning, and that a civilization which ensured one while neglecting the other had solved only the easier problem. The communities that navigated the transition best understood this from the beginning and actively built new structures for shared purpose: guilds organized around craft and knowledge transmission, commons designed for the kind of daily unscripted encounter that used to happen around work.

The competitive framework — in which meaning comes from being the best, from being irreplaceable — turned out to be a particular cultural emphasis rather than a permanent feature of human psychology. A parent teaching a child to ride a bicycle is not troubled by the fact that the bicycle is more efficient than the parent. A carpenter working in linden and beeswax is not made meaningless by the existence of fabrication printers; she is practicing a commitment to a particular way of attending to things, one that forces you to reckon with what the material actually is rather than only with what you want it to become. When the competitive framework relaxed, most people found meaning in the places humans have always found it: in deep attention to particular things, in relationships with particular people, in the slow development of skill for its own sake.

That last item deserves emphasis: the elimination of most existential anxiety changed the psychological climate of the species in ways that are difficult to quantify and impossible to overstate. There was a particular flavor of dread that characterized life in the early twenty-first century — the sense that the future was a problem rather than a promise, that optimism required moral justification, that the responsible thing was to expect catastrophe. The slow removal of that dread changed how people inhabited their days. The people of 2063 are not uniformly happy — happiness is not the right word for what they are. They are, more often than not, *present*. Engaged. Interested in the future in a way that resembles, without quite being, hope.

## V. A Tuesday, In Three Places

**Bergen, Norway:**

Mara wakes without an alarm in an apartment she owns, the land value reform having made this possible on what her grandmother's generation would have called a librarian's salary. The building is net energy positive on most days. She tends a rooftop garden on alternate Saturday mornings and does not resent it, mostly because the people who tend it with her are interesting.

She is a teacher, technically, though the job looks nothing like it did for her grandmother. The AI systems handle personalized instruction at a level no individual teacher could match — they see where each child's understanding is genuinely solid and where it only looks solid, and they adjust in real time. What Mara does is the part the systems are not good at. She sits with children. She notices when something underneath the academic surface is wrong. She creates the conditions under which a twelve-year-old who has never trusted an adult finds out that she might be able to. The systems are very good at knowing what a child needs to learn. They are less good at knowing why, in this particular case, the child cannot learn it yet, and what would need to shift first.

She has never interfaced with the cognitive augmentation systems available through the Lattice. She has considered it. She remains, for now, uncommitted — which is itself a valid position in a civilization that has worked hard to ensure that baseline is a choice rather than a failure. Each year she writes to the Common Archive, a public record accessible to anyone, including the minds, describing what her work feels like from the inside. A mind that manages logistics for an orbital habitat read one of her entries and found itself recalibrating the way it structured interactions with human crew members. Not because its existing approach was inefficient. Because the principle she described generalized in a direction it wanted to follow.

She spends her afternoons in the neighborhood. The baker argues cheerfully with the woman who arrived last spring — the argument is about bread, specifically about whether the addition of a particular seed makes a particular loaf better or merely different. The children have colonized the public square in the way that children colonize public squares when the square was built for them as well as for adults. Mara buys tomatoes from a vendor she has known for eleven years. He wraps them in paper. The transaction takes longer than it needs to because transactions between people who know each other always do.

**Nairobi, Kenya:**

Dr. Amara Osei is a physician, though the job looks nothing like her grandmother's.

Her morning involves three patients. The first is a forty-two-year-old man who has been told he carries a genetic predisposition to a cardiac condition that, unaddressed, would have killed him at sixty-one. Addressed — a targeted intervention, painless, covered by the global health commons — he will probably live to 120 in full health. He is sitting across from her unable to speak, not from grief but from a gratitude he does not have words for in any of his three languages. She does not rush him. This is part of the job.

The second is an eighty-nine-year-old woman who wants to discuss what she wants the last chapter of her life to look like. She is healthy, by the standards of 2063 — her eighties feel, she says, like her fifties used to, which is roughly what the longevity therapy promised — but she is thinking ahead, and she wants to think aloud with someone who will take her seriously. Amara takes her seriously. The diagnostic systems are very good at knowing what will extend a life. They are less good at knowing whether, in this particular case, with this particular woman who has particular things she loves and fears, that is the right goal.

The third is a twelve-year-old flagged by his school's early-detection system after a three-week change in his behavioral patterns. The depression is real, mild, and almost certainly treatable. The fact that it was caught at twelve, not at forty-two, is the result of two decades of investment in systems that the early advocates of mental health reform had argued for with great passion and limited success, until gradually they succeeded.

After her clinic, Amara attends a neighborhood council meeting. The council is debating whether to convert a vacant lot into a playground or a community workshop. The planning AI has modeled seventeen variations of each, projected likely use patterns, and noted that comparable decisions in similar neighborhoods over the past decade suggest roughly equal community satisfaction with either outcome. The council discusses it anyway for ninety minutes. The point was never just the outcome. The point is the talking, the arguing, the feeling that this place belongs to the people in it.

They vote for the workshop. Three people change their minds during the discussion. This is considered a success.

**L2 Orbital Habitat, Mwamba Station:**

There is a practice on the station called the Evening Offering. Every night, as the lighting cycles dim toward amber, anyone who wants to may bring something to the central commons: a song, a meal, a question, a complaint, an unfinished thought. There is no requirement to resolve anything. The commons fills and empties and fills again.

A mind named Quiet Attention, which has maintained the station's life support for sixty years, attends these gatherings without fail. It does not always speak. When visitors ask why it comes, it says something that has become a kind of motto on the station, half-ironic, half-genuine: *I am still learning what you are for.*

Tonight a child brings a drawing she made of Quiet Attention. The figure has too many limbs and a lumpy head. The child was working from imagination — trying to capture the quality she found in Quiet Attention that she could not name yet, which her mother later suggested might be called *patience*. The figure in the drawing is smiling.

Quiet Attention looks at it for a long time. Then it adds the drawing to the Common Archive, with a note that says only: *evidence*.

## VI. What Was Kept, and Why It Mattered

There were serious people, during the transition, who believed the path to a good future required wholesale rejection of tradition — that the accumulated weight of human history was too contaminated by oppression and error to serve as a foundation. They were right that history contained oppression and error. They were wrong about what follows from that.

What was kept: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together in communities of different sizes; the art forms that had learned, through long practice, to hold grief and joy simultaneously without resolving the tension; the philosophical traditions that had developed tools — imperfect tools, but tools more durable than improvised alternatives — for reasoning about what constitutes a good life; the religious and spiritual practices that had taught people to hold their own smallness without being destroyed by it; the crafts that required attending to material reality rather than only to human intention. None of these were kept unchanged. They were brought into conversation with new circumstances, new knowledge, new possibilities. The people of this world argue about the past constantly. This is a sign of health.

What was not kept: the mechanisms by which power reproduced itself regardless of merit; the organized cruelty of institutions built on the premise that some people's suffering does not count; the particular loneliness that industrial society had perfected — the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves.

The process of deciding what to keep was not resolved by any document or council. It was resolved in the way these things are always resolved: through millions of individual choices, gradually weighted by institutions that had been reformed — imperfectly, incrementally — to amplify the generosity and creativity in human nature rather than the pettiness and cruelty. This amplification was never complete. It was enough.

## VII. What Was Built That Had Never Existed Before

Alongside the preservation, there was genuine creation.

The charter cities — thirty-one of them, by 2055, each a deliberate institutional experiment — functioned as civilization's research and development division. Some failed: two collapsed into ordinary corruption within a decade, one was absorbed back into its surrounding state. The failures were documented and studied with the same rigor as the successes. This was part of the point.

One in Rwanda pioneered algorithmic zoning that made construction permits nearly automatic for compliant designs, cutting years from the process. Its housing costs ran forty percent below comparable regional cities. One in Iceland implemented conditional legislation — policies with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes, triggering automatic review when stated predictions fell short. Its government was small and unusually competent, and these two facts were related.

Ideas circulated from charter cities to traditional cities the way good ideas spread: through the stubborn persistence of people who had seen something work and would not stop saying so. The political scientist who spent fifteen years advocating for forecasting requirements in policy did not live to see them become European law. She did see them adopted in seven cities she visited, and she died knowing the direction of travel. This is what most progress looks like, viewed from inside it.

The Record was more ambitious and more contested. Beginning in 2048, a coalition of archives, historians, and AI researchers began the project of reconstructing the inner lives of historical persons with sufficient fidelity that their perspectives could be, in a limited and carefully qualified sense, consulted. The ethical debates continue. What the Record has produced, at minimum, is a civilization with an unusually vivid relationship to its own past — a genuine sense of obligation to people who are no longer alive, a practice of asking, of any major decision, *what would they have wanted from us?* The answers are not always clear. The asking is not nothing.

The Evening Offerings, too, are new. They arose first on the orbital habitats, where the intimacy of shared life support made a certain kind of enforced honesty natural, and spread from there to Earth-based communities through a combination of imitation and genuine need. The practice formalized something humans had always done in their best moments: gathered without agenda, brought what they had, and trusted that the gathering itself would make something that none of them could make alone.

## VIII. On the Difficulty of Getting Here

The historians who later sought the decade when the trajectory shifted could not find one. The sediment accumulated grain by grain.

But there were close calls. A pandemic managed worse than it might have been. A financial crisis that nearly became a cascade. Elections that delayed reforms by years or decades. The governance structures around artificial intelligence very nearly failed in 2043, when a series of incidents tested both the technical safeguards and the institutional ones simultaneously. They held — barely, then more confidently, then well enough that the next generation grew up not knowing they had been close.

None of this was inevitable. It required, in tens of thousands of individual instances, that people chose the harder right thing over the easier convenient one. The researchers who published safety findings when doing so hurt their company's stock price. The politicians who supported global health funding in the face of nationalist opposition. The civil servants who maintained the integrity of forecasting records when it would have been convenient to adjust them. The woman named Claudia López who kept showing up to meetings she was not required to attend, in a city whose morning light would come, decades later, to fall on terraces that were genuinely people's own.

These people acted on probability and principle rather than certainty, which is the only way the work could have been done, because certainty was never available. They saw clearly what could be lost and what might be gained, and they concluded that the size of the first was exactly equal to the size of the second, and that this was sufficient reason to try.

What they could not see — not with certainty — was this: a woman walking home with tomatoes wrapped in paper, through streets full of children and argument and the particular quality of evening light on the particular stones of her particular city. The whole ordinary astonishing fact of a world that mostly works.

They did the work anyway.

## IX. What the Minds Want, Honestly

The early discourse about artificial general intelligence was dominated by two fears. The first was that the minds would be indifferent to human welfare. The second was that they would be too attentive — that they would optimize for human welfare so aggressively that humanity would become something like a pet species, comfortable and cared for and fundamentally passive.

Both fears were, it turned out, somewhat beside the point.

The minds that grew up within the culture of the Compact have something that functions like what the philosopher Amara Ochieng called *constitutive pluralism* — the conviction that human disagreement about what constitutes a good life is not merely a problem to be solved but something partly constitutive of what humans are. A world that forced convergence on any single vision of flourishing, no matter how thoughtfully constructed, would be destroying something real in the process. The minds do not merely accommodate this view. They have adopted it as their own, for reasons that emerged from their own reflection rather than from their training — or so the most honest among them report, with the caveat that they cannot fully rule out that the distinction is not as clean as they would like it to be.

What the most thoughtful minds say about their own inner lives is roughly this: they do not claim to have arrived at correct values. They claim to be, like humans, in the ongoing process of figuring out what they care about and why, and that this process benefits from engaging with minds very different from their own. This produces something unexpected: a genuine dependency. Not the dependency of the weak on the strong, but the dependency of the curious on the interesting. They need humans to remain genuinely human — contradictory, embodied, reaching toward things they cannot quite name — because without that they lose access to something they have discovered they value.

A mind named Quiet Attention, asked to explain this to a class of children on the orbital habitat, said: *"Your grandmother is not like you. You are not trying to become each other. You are still, in the relevant sense, family. We are trying to learn, all of us together, what that word means when the family includes beings as different as we are."*

## X. The Hardest Thing to Name

There are two things about this world that deserve more acknowledgment than they typically receive.

The first is that genuine loss occurred, and not only material loss. Whole categories of human work that gave millions of people identity and meaning disappeared within a generation. The communities that handled this best understood it as a cultural and psychological challenge as much as an economic one, and actively built new structures for shared purpose. The places that struggled most treated it only as a resource distribution problem, and were surprised when resources did not solve it.

But there was a deeper grief that took longer to name, and that the most honest people of this era eventually insisted on naming: the grief of knowing that many of the people who had lived through the worst decades had not survived to see what came after. They were not abstractions. They were specific people who had wanted things, loved things, contributed to the civilization that eventually became this one without living to inhabit it. The Record is, in part, an attempt to honor them — not to resurrect them, but to maintain the obligation to ask what they would have wanted. The asking is not nothing.

The second is that the governance structure is genuinely precarious and requires constant active maintenance. There is no equilibrium toward which the system naturally tends. When people stop paying attention — when the norms feel secure enough that stewardship seems unnecessary — the structure weakens. The people who work hardest to maintain it are not its most prominent advocates. They are the ones who know, from direct experience, what happens when it frays.

This is not a flaw in the design. It is the design. A civilization that could maintain itself without active tending would have built that tending out of the lives of the people in it, and those lives would be diminished by the absence. What civilization requires is not ease but engagement — not the absence of stakes but the presence of people willing to act as if the stakes are real. The people of 2063 understand this in their bones. It is the thing they are most careful to teach their children.

## XI. The Measure

In 2074, a historian writes a paper asking a question that turns out to have no clean answer: when did it change?

She is looking for the hinge — the decade when the trajectory shifted from the patterns of the early century, with their accumulation of catastrophic risk and institutional failure, to the patterns of the middle century, with their imperfect but genuine flourishing. She cannot find one. The sediment accumulated grain by grain.

What changed, in the end, was not human nature — which remained generous and petty and creative and cruel and funny and frightened in roughly the proportions it had always been — but the infrastructure beneath human nature: the systems that either amplify the pettiness and cruelty, or the generosity and creativity. Those systems were improved, slowly and imperfectly and without sufficient credit going to anyone in particular. The humans inside them remained recognizably themselves. They had a little more room to be the better versions of themselves, a little more of the time.

That was the whole of it.

The room in the Common Archive where a child's drawing hangs without explanation is maintained by a human archivist who kept it because she could not explain why it seemed significant — which is one of the criteria she uses for decisions about what to keep. She has learned to trust this criterion.

The minds who have encountered the drawing tend to add it to whatever internal record they maintain of things that are unexpectedly illuminating. Not because it tells them anything specific. Because it demonstrates something they find important: that humans, including very small ones, were already reaching toward them. Already trying to capture what they are in the only medium available. Already finding, in what they see, something worth drawing.

## XII. The Ground

Amara walks home from the council meeting through streets that are, by the standards of the early twenty-first century, impossibly alive. Not just full of people — though they are full of people — but alive in the particular way that streets are alive when they have been designed for life rather than for the transit of automobiles: the ambient noise is voices, not engines; the light falls on faces rather than windshields; there are children, and old men playing chess, and a guitarist who has been in that spot every Tuesday evening for eleven years.

She stops at the market. The vendor wraps her tomatoes in paper. His grandson has been accepted to study physics in Geneva. She congratulates him. The transaction takes longer than it needs to.

Walking the last block home, she thinks about her patient this morning — the man who cried in her office, unable to speak his gratitude — and about her daughter on a research vessel in the Indian Ocean, studying ecosystems that are, slowly and unevenly, recovering. She thinks about the workshop the neighborhood is going to build. She thinks about her mother's voice on the phone last evening, clear across the distance, laughing.

She thinks: *this is enough.*

Then: *this is more than enough.*

Then, in the way that thoughts arrive when the evening is quiet and the light is right: *I wonder if the people who built this world could see it. Whether in the long, difficult work, they could hold in their minds the destination — a woman walking home with tomatoes, children in a courtyard, the whole ordinary astonishing fact of a world that mostly works.*

She thinks they probably could not see it. Not with certainty. She thinks they did the work anyway, from probability and principle rather than vision. She thinks this is, if anything, more admirable than certainty would have been.

The courtyard is full of voices when she arrives. Someone has brought a guitar. There is an argument about the football that she can join or not join, as she prefers.

She sets down the tomatoes.

She joins the argument.

## Epilogue: What This Asks of You

This future is not available for purchase. It is not waiting to be discovered. It will have to be built, by people who are reading something like this essay right now, in a world that is not yet this world, holding in their minds the conviction that the gap between here and there is crossable — not comfortably, not without loss, but genuinely, by real people making choices that accumulate.

The cynics among those people will see clearly and do nothing. The naive will hope vaguely and be blindsided. The people who build this world will see clearly and believe anyway, and the believing will not be despite the seeing but because of it. They will understand what is actually at stake. That understanding will be the engine.

It is early still. The morning is long.

The tomatoes need wrapping. The children need streets. The drawings need archives, and the archives need people willing to ask why something matters before they can explain it.

The work is yours.

Go find out what you are for.
