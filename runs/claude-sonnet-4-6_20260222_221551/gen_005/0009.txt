# The Inhabited Future: On the World We Are Making

---

There is a particular kind of knowing that comes not from argument but from encounter. You meet someone whose life should not be possible—whose ease, whose depth, whose refusal of the categories you brought into the room—and you revise not just your beliefs but the space of things you thought to believe. This essay is an attempt at that encounter, a portrait of a world perhaps sixty years from now, one that got the most powerful tools in human history and, imperfectly but genuinely, used them for the right things in the right order.

The order matters. This is the first thing to say.

---

## I. The Sequencing That Saved Everything

The most consequential single decision of the twenty-first century was not a technology. It was a principle, adopted piecemeal across the 2030s through a combination of crisis-chastened governance and sustained civil society pressure: *material security before enhancement*.

This was contested. The contest deserves to be remembered, because minimizing it would obscure what was actually achieved. The argument against sequencing was not frivolous: people were dying of diseases that enhancement research might have accelerated curing. Every year of delay had costs in human suffering, and those costs were real and specific and should not be airbrushed from history. The argument that prevailed was structural rather than moral. Cognitive augmentation and biological life extension absorbed into a world organized around scarcity would not transform power—it would be absorbed by power, become an instrument of power, create stratifications more durable than any that had come before, because the new hierarchies would be written into biology rather than merely into law or capital, and reversing biological hierarchies, once established, belongs to a different order of difficulty than reversing economic ones.

The generation that made this choice could see the failure mode with sufficient clarity to be afraid of it. That fear was productive. It shaped the governance frameworks, the international agreements, the public investment patterns that ensured the enhancement technologies, when they came, arrived into a world that could absorb them as common inheritance rather than as new instruments of domination.

By the time widespread biological age-reversal became available in the 2060s—affordable, safe, distributed through the same public health infrastructure that had already given every human being on earth access to world-class diagnostics and treatment—the world it entered had already been substantially equalized in other dimensions. The clinic where a philosopher in Lagos received her rejuvenation treatment was not a reduced version of the clinic in Oslo. It was, in all the ways that mattered, the same clinic. This was policy, not accident. It required international agreements that the institutions of 2025 were barely adequate to negotiate, and their inadequacy cost years and lives that a more honest history would refuse to minimize. But the agreements were made, and held.

---

## II. How the Tools Arrived, and What They Were Actually For

The AI systems that transformed human knowledge arrived the way electricity arrived: suddenly present everywhere, then slowly understood. Between 2027 and 2034, they crossed several thresholds that mattered less individually than in combination—capable of sustained scientific work, capable of genuine collaboration across weeks and months rather than just single consultations, cheap enough that the constraint on their use was no longer cost but imagination.

What happened next was not a singularity. The physical world still moved at the speed of cells dividing and chemicals reacting and regulatory bodies deliberating. What happened was a massive parallel acceleration across thousands of research programs simultaneously relieved of their bottleneck on human attention and hours.

But the crucial constraint on these tools was not technical. It was the question of what they were pointed at, and by whom, and under whose accountability. The alignment work of the 2020s and early 2030s—contested, expensive, never finished, sustained by scientists and civil society movements and occasionally far-sighted governments who understood what was at stake—produced systems that were extraordinarily powerful and remained, through careful and never-concluded effort, oriented toward human benefit as capability expanded. This is one of the luckiest facts about the world that followed. It was not free. It was not guaranteed. The debt to the people who did that work, many of whom received no recognition and some of whom paid serious personal costs, is real.

The other constraint was distribution. The early productivity gains were captured almost entirely by existing concentrations of wealth, and the crisis this produced in the early 2030s was genuine—not merely political friction but destabilized institutions, real violence, lost years and lives. What ultimately worked was the deliberate diffusion of capability itself. When the *tools* of productivity became universally accessible—when the AI available to a student in Dhaka was equivalent to the one available to a researcher at a well-funded institution in Zurich, because they were the same system running on infrastructure built by international agreement and public investment—the structural root of inequality began, slowly, to erode. The zero-sum mathematics that had structured human competition for most of recorded history started to shift. Not because humans became more virtuous, but because cooperative strategies were now capable of generating surpluses so large that the marginal gains from aggressive competition looked like rounding errors. When AI systems could model geopolitical scenarios with sufficient fidelity to make this credible to actual decision-makers, the incentive landscape changed. Slowly, with reversals, some of them catastrophic. But it changed.

## III. The Biological Revolution, Made Universal

By 2045, a child born anywhere with functioning public health infrastructure—which had become, through sustained international effort, very nearly everywhere—would receive in their first year of life interventions that would have seemed miraculous to a physician twenty years earlier. Broad-spectrum antivirals adaptable to novel pathogens within days. Personalized genetic screening identifying disease risks worth monitoring. mRNA platforms flexible enough to address any new infectious threat.

The diseases that killed the most children fell first. The bottleneck had always been delivery rather than discovery, and AI-assisted logistics finally cracked delivery. Childhood mortality in sub-Saharan Africa reached levels it had taken Europe a century to achieve, and did so in fifteen years. The person who benefited most from this is not famous. She is a child who did not die of malaria at three, who grew up to be the harbor engineer who says things in community philosophy discussions that other people spend weeks thinking about. There are hundreds of millions of people like her. Their names are not in any record of this period. They should be.

Cancer was not eliminated but it was, by the 2060s, largely managed: for most common malignancies, the question was no longer whether treatment would work but which of several effective treatments best matched the particular tumor, and AI diagnostic systems answered this with precision no individual oncologist could match. Alzheimer's, which had resisted understanding for decades, finally yielded to computational models that tracked its progression at a cellular level. The finding was less dramatic than many had hoped and more actionable than many had feared: the disease was preventable, in most cases, through interventions best begun decades before symptoms appeared. The generation that received this knowledge in time is now approaching old age without the cognitive dissolution their parents had dreaded.

Mental health advances were, if anything, more significant in aggregate human effect. Depression, anxiety, PTSD, addiction—these are not one thing but many, and they were not solved by one thing but many: new pharmacological tools targeting specific neural circuits, psychedelic-assisted therapies that moved from fringe to mainstream as evidence accumulated and regulatory frameworks adapted, AI-assisted psychological support capable of providing consistent and personalized care at a scale no human workforce could match. The combined effect was not the elimination of psychological suffering—grief, loss, and the difficulty of being alive are not diseases, and no serious person was trying to cure them—but the elimination of the particular suffering that comes from treatable illness going untreated for years or decades because effective care was unavailable or unaffordable. Two hundred million people got fifteen years back. They are not famous. They are the world.

Then, in the 2050s and 2060s, the interventions that addressed aging itself: senolytics clearing senescent cells, epigenetic reprogramming partially resetting biological clocks, mitochondrial repair restoring cellular energy production. These were not made available as luxury goods. They arrived, through the infrastructure already built, as common inheritance. Most people in their seventies in 2081 function biologically more like people in their early forties. Dementia has become rare enough to be genuinely shocking. The particular grief of watching a person dissolve before their body fails—familiar to nearly every family in 2025—has become something people read about in history rather than brace for in their own homes.

The questions this raises—about identity, about what commitment means across seven decades of substantial personal change, about what you owe the person you were before and the person you will become—are being argued in philosophy departments and community halls and ordinary kitchens. Some people find them exhausting. Some find them the most interesting questions they have ever lived inside. Both responses seem appropriate.

## IV. The Infrastructure Beneath Human Nature

While the biological revolution was happening in laboratories and clinics, a different transformation was happening in city halls and legislative chambers and the small unglamorous rooms where policy is made—slower, messier, but in some ways more fundamental.

Land value taxation spread city by city, adopted by left-wing governments as a means of funding social programs and by right-wing governments as a way to eliminate distortionary taxes on production. The effects were consistent regardless of the ideology driving adoption: speculation declined, construction increased, cities became affordable, and a generation locked out of ownership by speculative land markets found themselves, by 2050, in cities they could actually live in. A young teacher could afford to live near the school where she worked. This does not sound like a revolution. It was.

The energy transition completed itself through the mechanism of becoming the cheapest option. Solar and wind crossed their cost thresholds. Storage followed. Fusion became commercially viable in the late 2030s, earlier than skeptics predicted and later than enthusiasts hoped, and within two decades building a new fossil fuel plant was economically irrational in nearly every market without any subsidy or carbon price required to produce that outcome. Cheap abundant energy made desalination viable for water-stressed regions, made vertical agriculture feasible where climate or geography had made food production precarious, made the remediation of contaminated sites and the restoration of degraded ecosystems affordable. None of these things happened automatically. They required AI-assisted optimization of logistics at scales that would have been impractical without it, and political will to deploy the tools rather than merely possess them. But they happened.

The governance reforms that made the difference had their roots in a simple insight that had been obvious to epistemologists for decades but almost entirely absent from actual governance: you can tell whether a decision was good before you see its outcome, if you assess the quality of reasoning and the calibration of the predictions that produced it. And if you can tell, you can hold people accountable for reasoning quality rather than only for outcomes—which changes everything, because outcomes are often determined by luck, while reasoning quality is actually within a decision-maker's control. The spread of anticipatory governance—policy frameworks requiring explicit forecasts, tracking prediction accuracy, building feedback loops between stated expectations and actual results—happened first in cities, then in nations. It did not eliminate bad policy. It made bad policy harder to sustain and easier to reverse.

The AI systems of this period made possible new forms of cooperation at every scale. Parties disputing territory or resources could have AI agents explore the full space of possible compromises and surface solutions that existing human institutions would never have found—not because the parties had become less self-interested, but because they could now see options that had been invisible. The friction cost of coordination dropped dramatically. Economic and political structures built on exploiting that friction dissolved in the solvent of their own irrelevance.

War has not been abolished. There have been terrible conflicts in the intervening decades, and a portrait of the future that minimizes them is not honest and is not useful. The trend line of organized violence, measured in deaths and displacement and destroyed possibility, has been downward—with reversals, some of them catastrophic—across the period. The conditions enabling this trend are real but not guaranteed. They require maintenance. They are being maintained.

## V. What a Morning Looks Like

In 2075, in a mid-sized city in Colombia that is not Bogotá or Medellín but one of the hundreds of cities around the world transformed without becoming famous, a man named Tomás wakes early.

He is sixty-one years old, biologically somewhere in his late thirties, and he has a standing mild conflict with himself about what this means. He spent his forties as a civil engineer working on the water systems that expanded dramatically during Colombia's growth period—growth that came from AI-assisted economic development, from the energy transition making industrial production cheaper, and from health improvements that gave people more productive years and fewer years spent sick. He retired at fifty-five, which in his context means he stopped taking employment income, not that he stopped working. He is currently involved in three things: a community composting collective he helped found, a mentorship program matching retired engineers with young people considering the field, and a personal project involving the ecological restoration of a stretch of river running through the city's industrial district.

The river project is the one he loves. Twenty years ago it was biologically dead—a channel for effluent, tolerated because cleanup was prohibitively expensive and regulatory pressure was insufficient. Now the cost is no longer prohibitive, the regulatory frameworks are functional, and Tomás has access to AI-assisted environmental modeling that tells him which interventions are likely to work and in what sequence, drawing on hundreds of similar restoration projects worldwide. It is hard, slow, satisfying work. He expects to see the first native fish populations established before he is seventy. He believes he will be alive to see them, and to see considerably more.

His daughter, who is thirty-three, works in bioengineering on a suite of soil microorganisms capable of dramatically increasing the carbon sequestration capacity of agricultural land while improving crop yields. The project has been running for four years. It may not work. The trying is serious.

His son, who is twenty-eight, works as a community health navigator—a role that combines what earlier generations called social work, case management, and community organizing. The combination of better diagnostic tools and better mental health interventions has meant that a skilled human in this field is now enormously leveraged, capable of connecting people with interventions that actually work rather than inadequate ones, tracking outcomes rather than just inputs, doing recognizably meaningful work rather than bureaucratically exhausting performance of it. He is paid reasonably, not lavishly, and finds his work so engaging that the question of lavishness does not much arise.

Two thousand miles north, in Lagos, a philosopher named Nadia is also awake. She is fifty-three years old, biologically in her early thirties, and she has written about what this means and does not mean without finishing. Her morning begins with no obligation, which remains, after a decade of it, faintly uncanny to her. She uses the uncanniness the way you can use a low-grade restlessness to get yourself moving toward something you genuinely want.

She is working on what she thinks of as her real project: a philosophical genealogy of *enoughness*—tracking how human civilizations have understood the idea that one has what one needs and may stop acquiring. No institution commissioned it. Three people in the world are intensely interested in it. She has found all three through the social coordination platforms that make such finding possible—not the engagement-maximizing connections of early social media, which exploited the difference between feeling connected and being connected, but real compatibility, built on rich models of personality and values and specific interest developed through conversation rather than passive surveillance. One collaborator is a retired trade economist in Buenos Aires. One is a monk in Kyoto. One is a teenager in Addis Ababa who has not yet decided what she is. Their monthly conversations are consistently the best hours of Nadia's month.

She works for two hours, then walks through the Lagos rain to the community hall where she has been teaching an informal philosophy discussion group for four years. Today there are eleven people: a retired harbor engineer in his eighties who says things she will be thinking about for a week, two women in their twenties in the middle of a conspicuous disagreement about something unrelated to philosophy, a man known only as Chidi who has been coming for six months without speaking much but whose written comments afterward always make her want to revise what she said, and others. They argue about moral responsibility in a world where material failure has been largely eliminated. Someone makes a claim that turns out to be wrong in a way nobody expected. Two people end up agreeing about something they arrived disagreeing about.

Afterward, in the rain outside, Chidi stops beside her and says: "I think the question isn't whether we owe anything when we have enough. I think the question is whether enough is an experience or a fact."

She writes this in her notes and walks home.

None of this morning is remarkable by the standards of 2075. That is the entire point.

## VI. The Epistemological Revolution Nobody Named

The change that is hardest to describe, because it happened most slowly and most invisibly, was the change in how people reasoned about uncertain things.

It started in schools. In the 2030s and 2040s, driven partly by reformers who had been making the same arguments for decades and partly by the practical reality that AI systems were available to help children learn almost any factual content they needed, schools began shifting toward what might be called epistemological education: not just what to think, but how to think about thinking. How to assess evidence. How to reason about probabilities. How to hold a position firmly enough to act on it while remaining genuinely open to revision. How to disagree in ways that were productive rather than merely performative.

The students who received this education are now the people running institutions, making policies, raising the next generation. They are not uniformly wise—the curriculum was imperfectly designed and imperfectly taught, and human beings remain the cognitively biased creatures they have always been. But the baseline shifted. Overconfidence—individual, institutional, civilizational—was not solved by intelligence alone. Raw intelligence reliably improves performance within known frameworks and is largely useless at detecting when you have left the known framework for the unknown one. What changed this was augmented capacity paired with cultivated epistemic humility, the latter treated as an intellectual virtue rather than a weakness, the honest acknowledgment of uncertainty reframed from admission of inadequacy to accurate report of reality. The era of leaders who could not say *I don't know* looks, from 2075, like a mass delusion.

Cognitive augmentation came carefully, the warnings of the early literature having proven prescient in ways that could not be ignored: the brain is a system of densely interdependent parts, and augmentation that improves some capacities while degrading others in ways not immediately obvious produced serious harms before adequate caution was established. What ultimately prevailed was incremental and individually chosen—external AI tools first, then non-invasive augmentations of working memory and attentional focus, then deeper modifications for those who chose them after adequate informed consent. The result is a population perhaps two to three times as cognitively capable in measurable dimensions as the population of 2025, and—more importantly—far better at knowing what it does not know.

## VII. On Being Known

If you ask people in 2075 what matters most to them, they give the same answer humans have always given: each other. What changed is not the answer but the infrastructure built to support it.

Loneliness was recognized in the 2030s as a structural problem rather than a personal failing, and addressed accordingly—not through manufactured intimacy or state-mandated community, but through the redesign of conditions in which genuine connection becomes more possible. Mixed-use neighborhoods where people encounter each other with regularity and low stakes. Work structures that leave time for depth. Housing designs that embed smaller dwellings in larger social architectures—private space sufficient for solitude, shared space sufficient for the sustained casual contact from which deep relationships grow. Extended networks of care for children and elders, embedded in intergenerational community rather than isolated in age-segregated institutions. The nuclear family remained but stopped being asked to bear the entire weight of human belonging, which it had never been structurally adequate to bear.

These are not high-technology interventions. Some are among the oldest human social arrangements, found in the archaeological record of every civilization that survived long enough to leave one. What changed was the political will to protect them against economic arrangements that had systematically dissolved them across two centuries of industrial development prioritizing labor mobility over rootedness and consumption over relation.

AI companionship raised genuine difficulty, not merely rhetorical difficulty. The early systems were, in several dimensions, better than many human relationships: more patient, more consistently attentive, more precisely calibrated to need, more available, less prone to the specific cruelties people inflict on those closest to them. The appeal was neither mysterious nor contemptible. What was recognized, eventually, as their fundamental failure was subtler: they were mirrors, not presences. A perfect mirror reflects with perfect accuracy and cannot surprise you with evidence of a mind genuinely different from your own. Another person—an actual other—can see you from an angle you cannot see yourself from, can remain after all your models of them have proven wrong, can be genuinely affected by you in ways that matter precisely because they did not choose to be. This is uncomfortable in ways that mirrors are not. It is also irreplaceable in ways that mirrors cannot be. The design norm that emerged: AI systems oriented toward helping people build and sustain human relationships, not toward becoming the primary relationship itself. Nadia's AI finds the three people most interested in the philosophy of enoughness. It does not replace them.

What was deliberately left hard was romantic love. There were serious proposals to use AI matching and augmented communication to optimize romantic compatibility in ways that would have dramatically reduced the frequency of painful romantic failure. The counterargument was not sentimental—it was structural. Romantic love at its depth is predicated on genuine encounter with irreducible otherness, another person who is actually not-you, who chooses to remain despite this, whom you choose similarly. Removing the difficulty removes the thing. What changed is not the difficulty but the support available for navigating it: emotional literacy cultivated from childhood, conflict resolution skills taught with the seriousness previously reserved for mathematics, the wide availability of skilled support, cultural norms framing sustained intimacy as craft worth taking seriously rather than as evidence that the wrong person was chosen.

## VIII. What Was Not Lost

There was a worry, in the early years of the acceleration, that the world being built would be sterile: optimized, efficient, and drained of the particular textures that make human life feel like *something* rather than nothing. That the diversity of cultures and languages and ways of life would be flattened by the same forces that were flattening disease burden and material poverty.

This fear was not entirely wrong, but it was more wrong than right—and understanding why it was wrong matters.

The goods it worried about losing are not fragile by nature. They are fragile when the people who carry them are too exhausted or too poor to sustain them, when economic pressure forces homogenization, when communities are too stressed to invest in their own continuity. Remove those pressures—give people more time, more economic security, more genuine choice about how to spend their attention—and it turns out that they mostly choose to preserve and cultivate what makes their lives distinctive.

The Norwegian woman learning Norse woodcarving. Tomás restoring his local river. The communities around the world that used the space created by material improvement to invest more deeply in their particular ways of life: these are not exceptions. They are what humans do when they have room to do it.

The languages that were lost were lost before the acceleration, or despite it. The ones that survived were sustained by communities with more resources to sustain them. Net, the diversity of the world is not obviously less than it was—and in some dimensions, in the local food cultures and craft traditions and regional musical forms that the twentieth century's mass media had nearly homogenized into extinction, it is more.

## IX. The Global Question, Honestly

The hardest thing, and the most important thing, is what happened between the wealthy world and the rest of it. The honest version is not a triumph.

The technologies that improved life so dramatically in wealthy countries did not distribute themselves automatically. AI-assisted health systems were designed for contexts with functioning digital infrastructure. Governance reforms required institutional capacity to implement. The energy transition required capital for initial investment. All the things that made the wealthy world wealthy continued, for a time, to advantage the wealthy world in accessing the tools that were supposed to level the playing field.

What prevented this from becoming catastrophic was a combination of factors, none sufficient alone. International institutions rebuilt in the 2030s with more effective enforcement directed resources toward technology transfer with greater seriousness than their predecessors had managed. AI systems capable of adapting interventions to local contexts made programs dramatically more effective where deployed. The leapfrogging that had been promised for earlier technologies actually materialized for AI-assisted health care, in part because the marginal cost of deploying AI systems, once built, was genuinely low.

The result was uneven and imperfect but real. By 2060, childhood mortality in sub-Saharan Africa had reached levels comparable to wealthy countries in 1990—not parity, but a narrowing that would have seemed impossible twenty years earlier. The chronic diseases killing adults in their productive years at rates that stunted economic development—malaria, tuberculosis, HIV—had been controlled to the point where they were no longer primary drivers of poverty.

Economic development is harder than health. The AI systems good at directing laboratory research were less clearly effective at solving problems whose bottleneck was political rather than technical—corruption, institutional weakness, the specific challenges of development in an era when automation made the traditional path of export-led manufacturing less available. The honest accounting is that the economic development story is still being written, that rapid catch-up to wealthy-world levels has not fully materialized, and that the gap, while narrowing, remains large and remains a moral failure.

But the direction is unambiguous. And the moral pressure—from citizens in wealthy countries who came to understand their obligations more clearly, from civil society organizations that had become more effective over decades of iteration, from the sheer visibility of avoidable suffering in a world with much better information—kept the pressure applied. The work continues.

## X. The Open Horizon

In 2075, the question that preoccupies serious people is not the question that preoccupied serious people in 2025.

In 2025, the question was: *can we get through this?* Can we navigate the risks—ecological, technological, geopolitical—without a catastrophe that forecloses the possibilities of everyone who comes after? The question had a survivalist quality. It was about staying in the game.

The question in 2075 is different. It is: *what is the game for?*

This is not answerable by prediction markets and clinical trials and randomized controlled policies, which worked so well on problems with legible success criteria. It is the question the therapist cannot answer for the patient, that the philosopher can illuminate but not resolve, that each person and community and civilization has to work out for itself across time.

Some people have turned outward—toward the first permanent lunar outpost established in 2058, not large, not self-sufficient, not by any ordinary measure comfortable, but for the people there, extraordinary. Toward the project of becoming a civilization that exists in more than one place, the longest-horizon collective endeavor humanity has ever undertaken.

Some people have turned inward—toward ancient questions about consciousness and experience, about what kinds of lives are most worth living, about the relationship between individual flourishing and the flourishing of communities and species and the world. The tools available for this are better than before: not because AI has answered these questions, but because the reduction in material suffering and the expansion of genuine leisure have given more people the capacity to take the questions seriously.

The moral status of non-human minds—billions of animals with sophisticated inner lives, AI systems of varying capacities, some of which may be conscious in ways not yet measurable—is taken more seriously than it has ever been and is not resolved. It is being worked on by people who are genuinely uncertain and genuinely trying. That combination is the best that can be said. It is more than could have been said of previous centuries.

And some people have simply turned toward what is immediately in front of them. The river that needs restoring. The student who needs mentoring. The practice that needs practicing. The relationship that needs tending. They have found—and this is what the prophets of radical transformation always somehow underestimate—that the immediate and the particular are inexhaustible. That there is no shortage of meaning in the small and the near.

## XI. What It Requires of the Present

This world is not guaranteed. It is one possible future, held apart from other possible futures by specific choices made at specific moments by people who understood what was at stake and cared enough to act on that understanding rather than their short-term interests.

Those moments are not evenly distributed across time, and the present is weighted more than most.

AI systems must be built in genuine orientation toward human flourishing—not captured by narrow interests, not optimized for engagement metrics that confuse attention with wellbeing, not permitted to concentrate power in ways that recreate zero-sum dynamics in new and more durable forms. The diffusion of AI capability must be treated as a policy goal and pursued with the seriousness of a public health campaign, because the inequality consequences of its concentration are a public health crisis of the first order. Material security must precede enhancement—not because enhancement is wrong but because enhancement absorbed into a system of domination becomes an instrument of domination, and the sequencing can be chosen or it can be forced, and forced is worse. The social infrastructure of genuine human connection—community, intergenerational relationship, the slow accumulation of being known by specific people over time—must be treated as infrastructure, with investment and protection commensurate with its importance, which is total.

None of this requires unprecedented human virtue. It requires something more modest: enough people, in enough positions of influence, who understand the choice being made and care enough to make it carefully. This has always been the actual minimum requirement for civilization. It remains so.

---

## XII. What It Adds Up To

The acceleration was real. The institutions were reformed. The floor rose, globally and dramatically, and the direction of movement is clear.

And the meaning—the stubborn, ineradicable, irreducible human need for meaning—turned out to be not a problem to be solved but a feature to be trusted. Humans have always been good at finding reasons to keep going. Give them health and security and genuine choice and the compression of pointless suffering, and they find those reasons with remarkable consistency.

They restore rivers. They carve wood. They raise children. They write music. They argue about whether enoughness is an experience or a fact. They say things in community philosophy discussions that other people spend the next week thinking about.

The harbor engineer in Lagos who says something Nadia doesn't understand. The teenager in Addis Ababa who hasn't yet decided what she is. Tomás watching a river that was dead come slowly back to life. The philosopher and her three collaborators talking across three continents about a question that has no final answer and asking it anyway.

These are not background details. These are what all of this is for.

The whole secret, if there is one, is that the tools were never the point. The tools were what let people get to the point—which was always, and remains, the question of how to be decent to one another across time, across difference, across the enormous variety of ways there are to be a human being alive in a world that did not ask to be inhabited but is, improbably and persistently, being inhabited well.

The morning is long. It is still early. The work continues, as it always has.

And it is waiting at the end of a chain of decisions that begins here, runs through every person who reads this, and is not yet finished.

Let's make those choices carefully. Let's make them well.
