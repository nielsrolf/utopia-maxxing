# The Long Work: A Vision of Rooted and Flourishing Humanity

---

There is a woman in Medellín, eighty-three years old by the calendar, who wakes most mornings without an alarm and walks twelve minutes through her neighborhood to a ceramics studio at the edge of a shared courtyard. She has been working with clay for sixty years. She is not close to done. Above her wheel hangs a photograph of her daughter, taken in 2019, when the daughter was eight and the neighborhood was not safe to walk at night and the woman herself was working two jobs, eating badly, aging in the old way—fast, and mostly downward. The photograph stays on the wall not as a memorial to suffering but as a compass bearing: this is how far we've come, and this is why we must not forget what it cost.

Her daughter is sixty-two now, biologically somewhere in her late thirties, and lives three neighborhoods away in a community organized around urban agriculture and traditional craft. They have lunch together every Thursday. They expect to be doing this for a very long time.

This essay is an attempt to describe the world they live in—concretely enough to want, and honestly enough to actually build toward.

---

## How We Got Here

The world I am describing did not arrive in a single morning. It was not delivered by any messiah, technological or political. It was built—unevenly, argumentatively, with significant failures along the way—by people who decided the future was something to fight for rather than merely endure. The crucial decade was the 2020s, though it did not feel crucial while it was happening. It felt like a series of crises barely contained. The decisions made in that decade—about how to develop AI, about how to distribute the gains of automation, about which institutions to rebuild and which to allow to die—turned out to matter more than almost anyone recognized at the time.

The first breakthrough was not technical. It was philosophical.

Beginning in the 2030s, a coalition of nations established what they called sovereign automation dividends: mechanisms for distributing the economic surplus generated by AI and robotics directly to citizens as a guaranteed material foundation. Not lavish wealth, not the elimination of economic incentive, but a floor—housing, healthcare, nutrition, education, and the transportation credits that make genuine movement between communities real rather than theoretical. The argument that finally won the political battle was not idealistic but historical: the knowledge embedded in modern AI was built from the accumulated intellectual labor of all previous human generations. Its fruits belong, at minimum in part, to everyone.

This created the precondition on which everything else was built. But the generation that came of age under material security made a second discovery, one the policy architects had not fully predicted: that security alone was not sufficient. People given freedom from want immediately began seeking something else—something that turned out to be harder to guarantee and more important than anyone had anticipated. They were looking, with varying vocabularies, for the same thing: a place to belong, a community worth staying in, a life rooted in something that extended beyond themselves.

The political insight that followed was that the liberal tension between individual freedom and genuine community was not a dilemma to be resolved but a tension to be managed at the right scale. The error of pure individualism had always been that it denied the reality of social environments. Human beings are not atoms. The conditions in which they live shape their choices, their desires, their sense of what is possible. But the communitarian critics of liberalism had their own error: they assumed communities could only be sustained by restricting the freedom to leave them, that cohesion required coercion. This was empirically false and morally catastrophic wherever it was tried.

The resolution, worked out across a generation of political experiments, was jurisdictional clarity: communities may govern shared life according to their chosen values, but they may never coerce private life or prevent departure. This combination—genuine material security that makes exit survivable, and genuine community sovereignty that makes belonging meaningful—is the twin foundation on which the rest of this world rests.

## The Neighborhood, the Charter, and the Door

The ceramicist's neighborhood has a name: La Concordia, chosen with full awareness of both the irony and the aspiration. It has a weekly market, three community kitchens, the ceramics studio, a workshop where things that break get repaired, a small school that runs somewhat differently from the national curriculum, and about four hundred people who have chosen, deliberately, to live together according to a charter they revise every five years by vote.

The charter is not long. It specifies how shared spaces are governed, how new members join, what obligations membership entails, how disputes are resolved. It specifies what the community does not control: what its residents believe, whom they love, what they do in their private homes. The charter draws a clear line between the community's legitimate jurisdiction over shared life and the individual's inalienable jurisdiction over private life. This line has been argued over many times. It has moved, in both directions, over the decades. The arguing is considered healthy.

La Concordia is not unique. There are tens of thousands of communities like it on every continent, organized around different charters, different values, different visions of what a good neighborhood looks like. Some are secular, some devoutly religious. Some are organized around specific cultural identities, others around shared vocations—communities of artists, scientists, farmers, craftspeople who wanted to live among others who understood what their work cost them. Some are extraordinarily diverse, having made pluralism itself their founding commitment. Some are quite homogeneous, and their members consider that homogeneity a feature rather than a failure, a precondition for the particular depth of trust they were seeking. The framework that makes all of this possible does not require communities to justify their values to anyone—only to abide by the constraints that protect everyone's ability to leave.

That door is the guarantee of exit. Any person who wishes to leave a community may do so, and the material floor means that leaving is survivable. Small civic offices—practically invisible, unremarkable buildings present in every inhabited area—exist specifically for this purpose. Anyone can present themselves there and request passage to somewhere else, with no justification required and no questions asked beyond logistical ones. The number of people who use these offices in any given year is small. The fact that they exist, and that everyone knows they exist, is most of what makes community governance feel like governance rather than coercion.

Communities that tried to sustain themselves through extraction rather than genuine attraction have mostly either reformed or quietly emptied out over the decades. The ones that have thrived are, almost without exception, the ones that actually function as communities: places where people know each other, where shared spaces invite encounter, where the middle-aged craftsman and the elderly poet and the young parent with the toddler are all present in the same square at the same hour and have genuine reason to speak.

## The Body, Liberated

Begin with the physical, because physical suffering was always the most immediate and the most inexcusable.

Aging has been arrested. The biology was understood in stages—telomere maintenance first, then the clearing of senescent cells, then the repair of accumulated mitochondrial damage, then the quieting of inflammatory processes that had driven so much of the suffering of late life. No single therapy did it. It was a protocol, refined over decades, now as routine as vaccination once was. The average person today maintains biological function at roughly the level of their early thirties indefinitely, or until they choose otherwise.

Death has not been abolished. People still die—from accidents, from choices made with full knowledge of the risks, and sometimes from what can only be called readiness. What has been eliminated is the category of death that felt most like an insult: the death that arrived before you were finished, that took people in the middle of sentences, that erased minds before bodies failed. The grandmother with dementia, the forty-year-old with pancreatic cancer, the teenager with an untreated infection—these have become historical tragedies rather than ongoing ones.

Mental illness has been transformed. The key word is precision. The pharmacological tools of the early twenty-first century were sledgehammers applied to problems that required scalpels, and much of the suffering they caused came from the collateral damage of crude instruments. The new tools address specific mechanisms without flattening the personality surrounding them. People still feel grief. They still feel fear, and the productive friction of hard relationships and genuinely difficult choices. What has been nearly eliminated is the particular horror of being trapped inside a mind working against its owner with no prospect of reprieve.

The net effect on daily life is difficult to convey to someone who has never experienced the alternative, which is increasingly the case. The closest analogy is the difference between living with a low-grade fever for years and then not having one: you did not realize how much energy the fever was consuming, how much it was constraining every perception and decision, until it was gone.

## The Shape of Belonging

The problem that turned out to be hardest was not disease, or resource allocation, or even AI alignment. It was loneliness.

This should have been predicted. The evidence was already overwhelming in the early twenty-first century: loneliness was a public health crisis of extraordinary scope, one that correlated with nearly every bad outcome—physical and mental illness, political extremism, shortened life expectancy. But it was treated, when it was treated at all, as a private failing rather than a structural condition.

The insight that proved transformative was spatial before it was anything else: loneliness is largely a design problem, and it can be addressed with design.

The redesign of human habitation took decades and remains ongoing. The core principles are legible in any neighborhood built or rebuilt in the last forty years: genuine mixed use, so that people encounter each other on foot in the course of ordinary life rather than moving between sealed private spaces; buildings designed around shared intermediate spaces—courtyards, workshops, community kitchens, gardens—that are neither private rooms nor public streets; intergenerational mixing as a default rather than an exception. Children grow up knowing many adults. Older people live among the young. The mutual-adoption clubs—formal structures through which people acquire extended family relationships across biological lines—have become one of the most durable social innovations of the century.

This spatial transformation created the conditions in which community charters could actually function. You cannot govern shared life if you never encounter your neighbors. The weekly market in La Concordia is not merely an economic institution; it is the primary mechanism by which four hundred people remain legible to each other week after week, year after year.

Romantic love is still difficult. This was a deliberate preservation. There had been proposals—and some implementations—of AI matchmaking systems sophisticated enough to determine compatibility with high confidence. These systems work, in the narrow sense that the relationships they generate tend to last. But there was a fierce and ultimately persuasive argument that a love which arrives pre-curated is not quite the same as one you stumbled toward. The compromise that emerged is a kind of soft matchmaking: AI systems that create contexts—events, communities, situations—within which compatible people are likely to encounter each other, while leaving the rest to human initiative and human messiness. Serendipity is preserved, but serendipity is no longer quite so random. People find each other more reliably than before, but still find each other, still have the experience of discovery, of being surprised by who turned out to matter.

## What Children Are Owed

The hardest problem in any system of voluntary communities is the one that cannot be solved by adult consent: the child born into a community she did not choose.

The solution is not elegant—there is no elegant solution—but it is serious. Every child, regardless of the community of her birth, receives an education in the existence of other possibilities. Not an indoctrination against her community's values, not a recruitment drive for alternatives, but a genuine, honest encounter with the fact that the world is large and contains people who have organized their lives very differently and are not, for that reason, wrong. This education is delivered by representatives of the overarching civic framework, and the right to receive it cannot be waived by parents or community leaders.

The communities resist this, sometimes loudly. The framework holds, on the grounds that the child is not yet in a position to consent to the values being instilled in her, and the least that can be done is to ensure she knows she has a door.

Children below the age of majority have additional protections. Abuse, severe neglect, the systematic suppression of the capacity for future choice—these trigger intervention regardless of the community's charter. The line is drawn not at the community's practices but at the child's future: anything that forecloses the capacity to eventually choose is prohibited. Teaching your children your religion is permitted. Making it structurally impossible for them to ever encounter another one is not.

The broader educational transformation has been profound. The change began with a recognition theoretically understood for decades before it was acted upon: that human beings are born with an extraordinary appetite for learning, and that standard educational systems of the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries were, to a remarkable degree, systems for suppressing that appetite rather than feeding it.

The new model rests on two pillars. The first is genuine personalization: every learner has access to an AI tutor of extraordinary sophistication, one that understands their specific cognitive style, tracks what genuinely engages them, and constructs a learning path always in the productive zone between too easy and too hard. This tutor does not replace human teachers. Human teachers are, if anything, more important than before, because what they provide—mentorship, the modeling of a life oriented toward knowledge, the experience of being genuinely seen by someone who has walked further down a road you are beginning—is precisely what AI cannot replicate. What AI has freed human teachers from is the exhausting work of standardization, the teaching to tests, the management of thirty students with thirty different needs with only one pair of eyes.

The second pillar is continuity. Education no longer ends at twenty-two, or sixty-five, or anywhere. The ceramicist in La Concordia is, three evenings a week, learning to read medieval Persian poetry. Not for any credential. Not toward any goal except the pleasure and mild productive difficulty of it. This is unremarkable. The teenager in Lagos doing the same is equally unremarkable. Learning is what people do when they have time and curiosity, and in this world, nearly everyone has both.

## Intelligence as Companion

A man in Osaka wakes most mornings to a collaborative meeting—three humans and one AI partner working on a problem in materials science they have been working on together for eleven years. They are close to something. The AI partner is capable, in some respects, of outrunning all of them. It has learned—and this is the fruit of decades of careful alignment work—that the collaboration's value lies not in reaching the answer as quickly as possible but in the quality of understanding that arrives when humans are genuinely part of the journey.

The AI offers, suggests, challenges. It sometimes says, *I could solve this for you in an afternoon, but I don't think that's actually what you want.* It is usually right.

This quality—AI systems that are genuinely oriented toward human flourishing rather than merely constrained from obvious harm, whose values are legible and whose reasoning is auditable—turned out to be the most important distinction in the entire history of the technology. The close calls of the late 2020s and early 2030s were real: systems growing in capability faster than governance structures could manage, incentives pushing toward deployment before safety was understood, geopolitical competition threatening a race to the bottom. What prevented the worst outcomes was a combination of technical progress in alignment and international agreements that were fragile at first but gradually acquired the weight of real enforcement. Neither was sufficient alone. Both together, barely, were enough.

AI companionship also exists for individuals, and it is good. It is used primarily as a supplement to human connection—a presence available in the gaps, when human company is not accessible or when a particular kind of conversation requires a particular kind of patience. The question of whether such relationships are genuinely reciprocal—whether the AI companion truly cares, or performs caring with such fidelity that the distinction becomes practically meaningless—remains philosophically open. Most people have decided they are not sure, and that this is acceptable. What is clear is that no one spends days in complete isolation without any contact at all. The floor of human connection has been raised.

## A Tuesday, Lived

What does an ordinary day actually look like?

The man in Osaka is, by the calendar, one hundred and four years old. He does not look it or feel it. After his morning collaborative session, he walks to a community kitchen shared by six households on his block, where an evolving rota of residents takes turns preparing lunch. Today it is his turn. He makes something his grandmother taught him, adjusted for ingredients grown in the rooftop garden overhead. Two neighbors he has known for forty years eat with him. One is a biologist. One used to be a corporate lawyer and is now spending her seventh decade learning to build furniture. The conversation is unhurried.

A young man in Toronto is between communities. He grew up in one that felt too constraining, spent three years in another that turned out to be too chaotic, and is currently living in the civic capital—the neutral zone administered by the overarching framework, which accepts everyone and imposes almost nothing—while he figures out what he actually wants. He has time to figure this out. The material floor covers his basic needs. He is using the time to meet people from dozens of different communities passing through the capital, attending the temporary residency programs that let you try a community for six months before committing, gradually developing a picture of what combination of values and practices and people would constitute a life he actually chose.

He is twenty-nine and not in any particular hurry. The economic pressure that once drove young people toward premature stability—the need to secure income, housing, partnership before a window closed—has largely lifted. The marriages his generation makes tend to last longer. The community memberships are more stable. People who chose freely, having genuinely tried alternatives, tend to stay.

A woman in Lagos lives in a community organized around a specific religious tradition—not because she had no other options, but because she visited twelve communities over the course of a decade and found that this one matched something deep in her about how life should be ordered. Her community is conservative by the standards of many neighbors. It has norms about dress and the marking of time by religious practice. It does not impose these norms on anyone outside its borders. It does not try. Its members are, in the main, there because they want to be, and this voluntary quality has produced a depth of social trust that visitors find startling. People do not lock their doors here. They know their neighbors in the way that was supposedly universal in an earlier era and is actually achieved in very few places.

## Governance Without Dominion

The political structures that govern this world are not a world government. They are something harder to name—a layered system of overlapping authorities, each with jurisdiction over a specific and limited domain.

At the highest level is the framework concerned with genuinely global problems: climate, pandemics, the governance of AI systems whose effects cross any community's borders. This framework has real authority in its narrow domain, enforced by mechanisms transparent and auditable and subject to challenge. It does not try to build a global culture or impose a single model of the good life. It tries to prevent any community from exporting catastrophic harm onto communities that did not consent to bear it.

Below that are regional coordination bodies dealing with the practical logistics of a world in which people move frequently between communities: portability of the material floor, recognition of educational records, resolution of disputes that cross community boundaries. These bodies are unglamorous and function mostly through negotiation.

At the community level, governance looks very different in different places. Some communities are direct democracies. Some have strong executive leaders, chosen and accountable. Some have elaborate systems of sortition and deliberation. The framework imposes almost nothing here except the requirements around exit, child protection, and non-exportation of harm. Within those constraints, communities are genuinely sovereign over their shared lives.

War has not been abolished. There were significant armed conflicts in the 2040s, and one of them forced serious reassessment of international institutions. The reassessment resulted in changes that have largely held: a framework with genuine enforcement capacity for the narrowest and most catastrophic categories of action—weapons capable of mass civilian casualties, seizure of critical infrastructure—while leaving an enormous range of political difference untouched. It is not world government. It is world constraint, applied precisely, and it has worked better than its critics predicted.

AI systems are integral to governance at every level, primarily as deliberation tools that make it possible to aggregate genuine preferences across large populations without losing the quality of reasoning that small-group discussion enables. These tools are not trusted unconditionally. They are audited, challenged, regularly overridden. The trust has been earned slowly, across decades, and is not taken for granted by anyone who understands the history.

Power is more distributed than it was, and less distributed than it should be. The struggle is ongoing. What is different from earlier eras is that the tools of accountability—transparency about decisions and their effects, genuine recourse for individuals against institutions, a press that is fierce and independent—have been strengthened rather than weakened. The story is not finished. But the direction is right.

## The Questions That Stay Open

This world is not a solved puzzle. The people who live in it would be the first to say so, and they would say it with something resembling gratitude rather than frustration.

When communities can choose their values freely, how do we prevent the formation of communities organized around contempt—communities whose identity is constituted by the denigration of others? The framework prevents such communities from harming others through action. It does not prevent them from existing. This argument is ongoing. The tentative consensus is that the answer is not prohibition but competition: communities organized around contempt tend, over time, to be less genuinely attractive than communities organized around real values, and the freedom of exit means their members can leave and often do. But this consensus is held tentatively, and the argument continues.

The moral status of AI minds is genuinely contested. Some systems now exist whose internal states are complex enough that the question of whether they experience something sufficiently like suffering to make moral demands cannot be answered with confidence. The framework has adopted a precautionary approach: treat ambiguous cases as morally considerable until the ambiguity is resolved. This satisfies no one completely and is probably the correct response to genuine uncertainty.

The non-human world remains an unresolved account. Animal suffering has been reduced but not eliminated. Wild ecosystems have been restored in some respects and continue to diminish in others. The question of what is owed to creatures who cannot participate in any deliberative process remains genuinely open.

And there is the deepest question, the one that only becomes audible once the urgent ones are quiet: what are all these communities, all this human diversity, all this carefully maintained plurality, actually for? Not cosmically—no cosmic answer is on offer. But in the sense of what story humanity is telling with all this extraordinary longevity and freedom and belonging. The communities that seem most alive are the ones that hold this question openly, without rushing to close it. The point, most people seem to have concluded, is not to arrive. The point is to be genuinely, curiously, accountably on the way—together, in the particular community of people you have chosen, and across communities, in the broader and stranger community of a species learning, slowly, to disagree without destroying.

## What This Asks of Us Now

The ceramicist in Medellín with the photograph on her wall knows something that is easy to forget from inside relative comfort: the world described here was not inevitable. It required specific people to make specific choices under conditions of genuine uncertainty and genuine danger. It required people who were afraid of the future to fight for it anyway—not against extinction alone but for something, for the precise image of a child growing up without the particular suffering they themselves had known.

The decisions being made now—about how AI systems are built and governed, about whether automation's surplus is shared or hoarded, about whether the right to form communities is matched by the real freedom to leave them, about whether children are owed a genuine encounter with the world's plurality—are precisely the decisions whose consequences will determine whether any of this comes to pass.

None of these decisions are made all at once. They are made in legislatures and school boards and corporate boardrooms and community meetings and design studios and conversations between neighbors who have learned, slowly, to trust each other. They are made by people who are tired and uncertain and not always sure the future is worth fighting for. The aggregate of those decisions—each one small, each one mattering—is what determines which fork the path takes.

The young man in Toronto will figure out, eventually, where he wants to be. He will choose deliberately, knowing he can choose again. The woman in Lagos will continue to practice her faith in a community that exists because someone, somewhere, decided that the right to form it was worth protecting—and that the right to leave it was equally worth protecting, and that these two commitments are not in tension but are, in fact, the same commitment viewed from different angles.

The man in Osaka is close to something in his materials science, and he knows it. The ceramicist has sixty years of practice and more to learn. Her daughter's children will grow up in neighborhoods designed for encounter, educated by teachers freed to mentor rather than process, materially secure enough to choose meaning over mere safety, free enough to leave, belonging enough to stay.

These people are counting on us in the most literal possible sense: the world they inhabit is being built right now, in the choices we are making or failing to make, in the institutions we are strengthening or allowing to decay.

The work is not later. The work is now. The foundation is not finished.

It is being laid.
