# The Inheritance We Make: A World That Chose Its Future

---

## Prologue: A Specific Morning

Let me begin with something concrete, because concreteness is a form of honesty.

It is a Thursday morning in 2061, in the city of Nairobi. A woman named Amara — forty-four years old, a research coordinator at the East African Institute for Tropical Medicine — wakes before her alarm. She does this most mornings now, not from anxiety, which was once the engine of her early waking, but from something closer to anticipation. The distinction matters. She knows the difference in her body.

Her apartment is on the eleventh floor of a cooperative building in what used to be called Westlands. The building is net energy positive. The neighborhood is walkable in a way that Nairobi neighborhoods were not, when she was a child, because the land value reforms of the 2040s collapsed the speculative real estate complex that had made dense, humane development financially impossible for thirty years. The streets below are not silent — they are never silent here — but the sound is different from her childhood memory of it. Fewer engines. More voices.

She makes coffee and reads for an hour before work. This is not a luxury. It is simply how mornings work now, because the standard working week was restructured in the 2040s, and because her income arrives through a combination of meaningful institutional employment and the East African Productivity Dividend — the region's version of an automation levy, distributing gains from AI-assisted research across the population — and because her daughter is sixteen and no longer needs her in the mornings in the urgent physical way she did at six. Amara has time. She has had time for years, and she has still not fully stopped being surprised by it.

At her institute, the work she does would have seemed miraculous to her predecessors. Not because of any single extraordinary technology, but because of the pace. Research that would have taken a decade now takes eighteen months. Treatments that would have remained theoretical are clinical. The last endemic malaria transmission in sub-Saharan Africa was recorded in 2057. She was in the room when her colleagues opened the notification. Several people cried. She was one of them.

This morning, though, she is reading a novel. A Kenyan writer, forty years old, who grew up in the same neighborhood Amara did. The novel is funny and sad and precisely observed, and it would not have existed if the writer had spent her thirties doing administrative work to survive, which is what would have happened in a different version of this world. Amara finishes her coffee. The light comes through the window at an angle she has loved for years. She goes to work.

That is the world. Now let me try to explain how it came to be.

## Part I: The Energy Foundation

The first thing to understand is that the problem of energy was solved. Not managed. Not ameliorated. Genuinely, completely, non-provisionally solved — and this solved almost everything downstream.

By the late 2030s, solar generation had crossed the threshold where "effectively free electricity" stopped being advocacy and became engineering fact. The marginal cost of the next kilowatt-hour, in daylight hours across most of the inhabited world, dropped below any number worth calculating. The bottleneck shifted, as the people who had been paying attention expected, to storage and transmission. Into those problems flooded the greatest concentration of applied intelligence — human and artificial — in history, because whoever solved them would set the terms of everything that followed.

They were solved through combination rather than elegance: fourth-generation iron-air batteries for long-duration storage, substantially rebuilt continental grids, and small modular nuclear reactors providing stable baseload across the latitudes where solar was insufficient. Unglamorous. Iterative. It worked. By 2048, energy in the developed world had ceased to be a meaningful variable in most industrial calculations. By 2058, most of the developing world had followed, leapfrogging the fossil infrastructure that wealthier nations had spent a century building and were carefully dismantling.

What this produced was not merely cheaper existing things. It reorganized the space of the possible.

Desalination plants scaled to genuine abundance along coastlines that had known water stress for generations. Vertical farms appeared in cities not as experiments but as serious food infrastructure, reducing the agricultural footprint of dense populations by a third. Lab-grown meat achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2049 and fell below it within four years, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that decades of ethical argument never quite managed. The Sahel greened through ten thousand projects, each made viable by collapsed power costs. The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s — which were grimmer than the news cycle had typically conveyed — became, by 2060, a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity became virtuous, but because the material conditions that produced mass starvation were dissolved, and what remained required active political malice to manufacture rather than merely passive neglect to permit.

This deserves to be named before anything else, because everything else rests on it. Measured in lives and the suffering attached to them, it was perhaps the largest single improvement in the human condition since the germ theory of disease.

## Part II: The Acceleration of Medicine

At roughly the same time the energy transition was completing, something stranger and more personal was happening in laboratories around the world.

The AI systems of the early 2030s had already become remarkable scientific collaborators — not in the sense of being infallible oracles, but in the sense of being tireless, extraordinarily well-read research partners who could hold the entire literature of a field in mind simultaneously and make connections that human scientists, working across the natural limits of memory and attention, routinely missed. The pivotal insight came from observing that the great discoveries in biology had historically been driven not by brute data accumulation but by a small number of genuinely creative researchers making unexpected conceptual leaps — noticing that a component of bacterial immune systems could be repurposed for general gene editing, perceiving that the immune system had a previously unappreciated relationship to cancer. These leaps required intelligence and deep familiarity and a certain kind of ranging, associative attention.

AI systems, directed and overseen by human researchers, turned out to be extraordinarily good at this kind of ranging attention. Not at replacing biologists, but at augmenting them — running thousands of conceptual searches in parallel, flagging unexpected convergences, designing experiments that human researchers then executed and evaluated. The research process accelerated. Not by an order of magnitude at once, but steadily, compoundingly, in the way that small improvements to a process compound over a decade into something that would have seemed extraordinary at the start.

The results, by 2065, included:

The effective elimination of most infectious disease from the world, not just from wealthy countries. mRNA vaccine platforms, once proven and scaled, could be adapted against almost anything within months. Vector control programs, guided by sophisticated epidemiological modeling and in some cases by biological interventions — sterile insect techniques, gene drives applied after extensive ecological review — reduced or eliminated diseases that had claimed millions of lives annually. The last case of wild-type polio had already occurred in 2023. Malaria followed in 2057. Tuberculosis, the ancient disease, was reduced to fewer than ten thousand cases globally by 2064, a number that continued to fall.

Cancer became a largely manageable chronic condition for most subtypes and a curable acute illness for many. AI-assisted drug design produced treatment regimens tailored to the individualized genome of each tumor, which had been theoretically possible for decades and practically impossible at scale until AI made the necessary analysis routine. Mortality from cancer declined more than eighty percent over thirty years. Some rare, adaptive malignancies persisted — biology retains its complexity — but the existential terror attached to a cancer diagnosis dissolved.

Genetic disease prevention advanced through improved embryo screening and, for living patients, through descendants of CRISPR that were safer and more precise by orders of magnitude. The ethical frameworks governing these technologies were contested and debated and ultimately resolved not through top-down decree but through decades of democratic deliberation, which is slow and frustrating and produces, more often than not, outcomes that most people can live with.

Alzheimer's disease, once among the most feared diagnoses available to the aging, was understood at last through better measurement tools that could isolate specific causal mechanisms from the enormous complexity of brain chemistry. Prevention became possible. Reversal of early-stage damage followed within a decade.

And beyond the treatment of disease: the human healthspan expanded. The distinction between lifespan and healthspan matters here, because what changed was not primarily that people lived longer in years counted from birth to death, but that the years they lived contained far less of the chronic deterioration, the accumulated small failures of biological maintenance, that had made old age often a long diminishment. By 2065, a person of seventy in good health was biologically comparable to a person of fifty in good health in 2020. The world had not defeated death. It had greatly reduced the suffering that preceded it and extended the period of capable, engaged life.

## Part III: The Healing of Minds

The physical advances were the ones that made headlines. The healing of minds was quieter and, in aggregate, may have mattered more.

The treatment gap for serious mental illness — the gap between the number of people who needed effective treatment and the number who received it — had been a silent catastrophe for generations. In 2025, it stood at roughly seventy percent globally: seven in ten people with major depression, schizophrenia, PTSD, severe anxiety, or bipolar disorder received no effective treatment. The consequences of this were diffuse and invisible precisely because they were everywhere: in the decisions made by people in chronic pain, the conflicts that distressed minds initiated and escalated, the human creativity and connection that suffering foreclosed.

Progress came from several directions simultaneously. AI-assisted drug discovery produced new classes of psychiatric medication that were faster-acting, more targeted, and had fewer side effects than the previous generation — not perfect, not sufficient on their own, but meaningfully better. Psychedelic therapies, carefully dosed and structured and supported, achieved clinical approval after the evidence base accumulated to the point where denial required more motivated reasoning than most regulatory bodies were willing to perform. Digital therapeutics — AI systems capable of delivering evidence-based cognitive behavioral therapy at scale, personalized to individual patterns and available at any hour — expanded access enormously, particularly in places where trained therapists were scarce.

More fundamentally, neuroscience advanced. The rate-limiting factor in psychiatric treatment had always been understanding: we did not know with sufficient precision what was wrong or how to fix it. AI-assisted analysis of large-scale neural measurement data, combined with the insights that interpretability research in artificial networks had unexpectedly contributed to understanding biological ones, began producing real mechanistic understanding of conditions that had previously been treated empirically, with trial and error. The error became less frequent. The trials became better designed.

By 2060, the treatment gap had narrowed to perhaps twenty-five percent globally. By 2068, it was below fifteen. This is not a complete victory. It is an enormous one.

The effects were visible in ways that compounded everything else. People in less pain make different choices. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more capable of imagining futures different from their present. The reduction of chronic, untreated suffering produced, as a side effect, something that looked like a modest but real improvement in collective human judgment. Not because individuals became smarter, but because they became less desperate. Desperation is the enemy of deliberation. Remove enough of it and the quality of collective decision-making shifts.

## Part IV: The Reformation of Institutions

Material abundance and medical advance were necessary. They were not sufficient. History contains many civilizations that had the material capacity for flourishing and organized it badly. The other half of the work was institutional.

The institutional changes of the middle century accumulated in the way sediment accumulates — grain by grain, invisible in the moment, unmistakable in retrospect. Several were pivotal.

**The reform of land tenure.** Land value taxation — taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and work upon it, an idea understood since the nineteenth century and consistently blocked by those who profited from the alternative — achieved political viability in the 2030s, when the intersection of unaffordable cities, rising inequality, and acute housing shortages made the cost of inaction impossible to ignore. The first serious implementations were in mid-sized cities in Portugal, Taiwan, and New Zealand. The results were exactly what the economic theory predicted: speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public coffers filled without confiscating from anyone who had created value, because the value being taxed was value no individual had produced. The reform spread with the inexorable logic of things that actually work. By 2050, most wealthy-world cities had adopted some version of it. Young people could afford to live in cities. Children could afford to stay near their families. Communities could afford to build.

**The improvement of collective epistemics.** A combination of forces — the spread of formal probabilistic reasoning in curricula, starting in Finland and South Korea in the 2030s and spreading widely across the following decades; the development of prediction markets and forecasting tournaments as tools for policy evaluation; the slow professionalization of a culture of intellectual honesty among journalists and public communicators — gradually improved the average quality of collective decision-making. This is the hardest thing to measure, and the most important thing to name. Institutions that can update on evidence, that hold decision-makers accountable to traceable reasoning and verifiable predictions, that treat the history of confident error as instructive rather than embarrassing, are institutions that improve over time rather than calcifying. The decades of the 2030s through 2050s saw this kind of institutional improvement at scale. The process was messy, contested, frequently derailed. It happened anyway.

**The restructuring of work.** The automation wave of the 2030s displaced most routine clerical and logistical work, and the displacement was genuinely painful — not the smooth transition that optimists had predicted, but years of real dislocation that required real political response. The response, after false starts and intense conflict, took shape as a reduced standard working week in most wealthy democracies, universal basic dividends funded by automation productivity levies, and a major expansion of publicly supported creative, educational, and caring work. The key insight that finally broke the political deadlock was simple: the productivity gains from automation were real and enormous, and the only question was who would receive them. A coalition that included labor unions, small business owners squeezed by the same automation as workers, and economists persuaded by the evidence eventually achieved redistributive structures that made the gains broadly shared. The transition left marks. The destination was better.

**The reform of governance toward legibility.** Beginning with experiments in Portugal and Iceland, then spreading through the European Union and eventually most stable democracies, anticipatory governance — the requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to forecasting audits, that predictions be tracked against outcomes, that decision-makers be held accountable to epistemically traceable standards — became standard practice. This did not eliminate bad policy. It made bad policy expensive to sustain, and made it harder for the people who produced it consistently to retain the positions that allowed them to do so. Conditional legislation, with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes, replaced permanent programs with ones that renewed only on demonstrated effectiveness. The improvements were incremental. Over two decades, they were large.

**The charter city experiments.** Beginning in the 2030s, thirty-one charter cities around the world served as deliberate institutional experiments — some successful, some failures, all documented with the seriousness of scientific trials. The successful ones circulated their innovations. Cities in Rwanda, Iceland, and coastal Colombia pioneered combinations of algorithmic zoning, participatory budgeting, hybrid human-AI deliberation processes, and conditional legislation that reduced housing costs, improved service delivery, and increased measured citizen satisfaction. The failures were documented equally carefully. This was considered part of the point.

## Part V: The Shape of Global Development

The most urgent moral question of this transition was always: who benefits?

The development trajectory of sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia in the 2040s and 2050s represents what may be the most important economic story of the century. The mechanisms were several. Energy abundance arrived faster than many forecasters expected, because the falling cost curve of solar and battery storage continued without the plateau that skeptics predicted, and because multilateral financing frameworks — reformed after the failures of the 2020s — channeled investment into grid infrastructure at scale. Health advances, once achieved, could be distributed rapidly: mRNA platforms could be adapted to local disease profiles quickly; AI-guided epidemiological campaigns could target interventions with precision; community health worker networks, supported by AI diagnostic tools, could extend the reach of trained medical personnel by large factors.

The economic results of improved health are not soft or speculative. Eliminating malaria from a high-burden country is estimated to add several percentage points to annual GDP growth for a decade, through improvements in worker productivity, child cognitive development, and reduced household medical expenditure. Multiply this across the range of diseases addressed over two decades, and the effect is transformative.

Simultaneously, AI-assisted economic planning — not the failed dream of central command, but the more modest and more effective project of giving governments access to better analysis and better prediction — helped developing nations identify the levers of growth that their particular circumstances made most accessible. Export-led manufacturing in some regions, services and knowledge work in others, agricultural productivity improvements in others. The economic decisions were made by human governments exercising self-determination. They made them with better information. The decisions improved.

The result, by 2065, is not equality. There is still significant variation in living standards across the world. But the trajectory has changed. The gap has been narrowing rather than widening for fifteen years. The extreme poverty of the early century — the kind that kills children from preventable diseases and keeps adults in permanent precarity — is largely historical. The developing world of 2065 has broadly surpassed the living standards of the developed world of 2020, measured by health outcomes, housing quality, food security, and access to education. The material inheritance of humanity was, imperfectly but genuinely, made common.

## Part VI: Democracy and the Long Fight

There is no version of this story that is comfortable about political institutions. The fight for democratic governance — against the pull of authoritarianism, the corruption of capture, the entropy of cynicism — was never finished. It is not finished now. In 2061, Amara reads news of two countries that backslid badly in the 2050s. Both are still struggling toward recovery. This is the normal texture of history: not a smooth arc, but a contested terrain where the direction of travel is shaped by countless decisions, most of them small.

What changed was the context in which those decisions were made.

Democratic governments that invested in prediction markets and epistemics reform tended to outperform those that did not, in measurable ways that became harder to deny as the decades passed. Citizens in healthier polities — more educated, less economically desperate, with better access to accurate information — proved more resistant to demagogic appeals. The information ecosystem, after the period of acute crisis that characterized the 2020s and early 2030s, slowly improved: not through content moderation utopia, but through the emergence of new epistemological infrastructure — trusted aggregators, reputation systems, AI-assisted fact-checking that was itself subject to transparency requirements — that made it harder to maintain large-scale false beliefs in populations that had received even modest epistemic education.

AI systems were deployed by democratic governments to improve judicial processes, not by replacing human judgment but by flagging statistical disparities in sentencing and outcomes that could then be investigated and addressed. This was contested, and the implementation was imperfect, and the improvements were real. The same principle — using AI to make existing systems more legible and more consistent, while keeping human judgment responsible for the actual decisions — proved generative across many domains of governance.

The authoritarian regimes that still existed in 2065 were not gone. Several remained. But they operated in a world where the gap in capability and living standards between democratic and authoritarian governance had become harder to obscure, and where AI-assisted communication tools that circumvented censorship provided populations with access to information that made the obscuring harder still. The wind was not yet at the back of every reformer. It was blowing more often in the right direction than it had been.

## Part VII: What It Is Like to Be in This World

It is not possible to fully convey the texture of a better world to someone who has not lived in it. But it is possible to gesture toward the directions in which the texture differs.

The children of 2061 roam. They move through neighborhoods that were redesigned, over two generations, to treat their presence in public space as normal and welcome. The streets are quieter — far fewer private vehicles — and so they are safer, and so children are in them. This changes childhood. Childhood that contains genuine freedom, genuine unsupervised encounter with the world and with other children, produces adults who carry that freedom in their bodies as a kind of default. It is not the same as childhood in previous centuries, with its material hardships and restricted horizons. It is something new: freedom within abundance, which neither the subsistence farmer nor the anxious suburban parent of the early twenty-first century could quite imagine.

The adults of this world work fewer hours than their grandparents, in jobs that, because the most grinding routine work has been automated, tend to involve more judgment, more human contact, more meaning. This is not universal — there are still jobs that are hard and unpleasant, and there are still people who wish their work were different. But the proportion has shifted. The economic pressure that required people to spend most of their waking hours doing work they found meaningless has been reduced. People have time for what they are actually interested in. The creative output of this period — art, music, writing, research, invention — is extraordinary, not because humans became more creative but because more humans were given the conditions in which creativity is possible.

The inner lives of people in this world are not identical to the inner lives of their grandparents. Mental illness is not gone — the human brain retains its complexity and its fragility. But it is treated. The weight of chronic, untreated depression and anxiety and trauma, which had been so normalized in earlier decades as to be nearly invisible, is substantially lifted. People who are not in constant pain are different kinds of people. More patient. More curious. More capable of genuine connection. The relationships of this world — friendships, families, communities — are not perfect. They are, on average, less desperate. Less armored. Less organized around the management of anxiety and more around the pursuit of actual things valued for their own sake.

There is a word for this in several languages that lacks a good English equivalent: something like flourishing-in-relationship, the sense that one's own good is genuinely bound up with the good of others and that this binding is not a sacrifice but a form of completion. Earlier generations had this experience in moments — it arrived sometimes at the edges of grief, or in the middle of shared work, or in the particular quality of late afternoon light with people you love. In the world of 2065, it is more available. Not guaranteed. Not manufactured. More available.

## Part VIII: What Was Kept

The people who built this world were not naive about what was being preserved and what was being changed. The argument about tradition — what in human culture and practice and accumulated wisdom was worth keeping — was ongoing and heated and never fully resolved.

What was kept, because it proved worth keeping: the slowly developed knowledge of how to live together in communities; the art forms that had learned through long practice to hold grief and joy simultaneously; the philosophical traditions with their imperfect but durable tools for reasoning about the good life; the religious and spiritual practices that taught people to inhabit their smallness without being destroyed by it. None of these survived unchanged. All of them were brought into genuine conversation with new circumstances and possibilities, and emerged from that conversation different from what they had been, still recognizably themselves.

What was not kept: the mechanisms by which power reproduced itself regardless of merit; the particular form of industrial loneliness, the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves; the idea that some people's suffering simply does not count.

The children of this world know history. Not as triumphalist narrative but as honest account: what people were capable of at their worst, how institutional failures compound, how the conditions for atrocity are created and maintained, how recovery is possible and what it costs. This knowledge is considered not depressing but essential. A civilization that has forgotten what humans are capable of cannot protect itself. The people of 2065 have not forgotten.

## Part IX: How It Happened

Amara's colleague, a historian named James, spent three years trying to identify the hinge — the decade when the trajectory changed. He could not find one. The sediment had accumulated grain by grain: the energy transition that dissolved material scarcity, the medical advances that reduced suffering, the institutional reforms that improved collective judgment, the mental health revolution that lifted the weight of untreated pain, the economic restructuring that made the gains broadly shared.

None of these were sufficient on their own. All of them together were not inevitable. There had been close calls — pandemics, financial cascades, political crises that could have gone differently and in some cases very nearly did. The story was not preordained.

What distinguished the people who made it happen was not that they were extraordinary — most of them were not, measured individually — but that enough of them held two things simultaneously: clear-eyed recognition of how badly things were and genuine faith in how much better they could be. This combination is rarer and more valuable than either attitude alone. The cynics saw clearly and concluded nothing was worth trying. The naive hoped vaguely and were repeatedly blindsided. The people who built this world saw clearly and believed anyway — not despite the seeing but because of it, because what they saw when they looked honestly included not just the failure but the material and intellectual and moral resources for something different.

The work they did was not secret and did not require permission. It required only that enough people, in enough places, over enough time, made enough of the right choices — in what institutions to build, what technologies to develop, what knowledge to spread, what suffering to refuse to accept as inevitable. The choices compounded. The world changed.

## Epilogue: The Letter

I want to close by acknowledging what this essay actually is: a letter, written from somewhere along the arc of history, to wherever you are reading it.

If you are reading this before the work is done — and you are, because the work is always in some sense before us — here is what I want you to know. The future described here did not require heroes, and it did not require a perfect plan. It required people willing to work on the problems they were actually in a position to work on, in the institutions they were actually part of, with the tools they actually had. It required people who did not need to see the whole arc to take the next step, who were willing to do good work even when the outcome was uncertain, who understood that the right response to the size of what could be lost is not paralysis but the most careful and serious and committed action you can manage.

The shore is real. The birds are not lying. And the ocean, when you reach it, will be nothing like a scaled-up lake.

The morning is long. It is still early.

The work is yours.
