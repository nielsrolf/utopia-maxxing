# The Shape of What We're For: A Vision of the World We Could Inhabit

---

There is a teenager in Nairobi—sixteen years old, the daughter of a software engineer and a soil ecologist—who is spending this particular Saturday doing something that would have been impossible to explain to her great-grandmother and that she herself considers unremarkable: she is sitting in a community garden on the roof of her apartment building, arguing with an AI about the philosophy of Frantz Fanon, while her hands are in the dirt.

The AI is patient in the way that only something with genuinely unlimited patience can be. It does not give her the answer. It asks her what she thinks the answer is, and when she is wrong, it shows her why in terms she can verify herself. But it also knows—because it has been paying careful attention to her for three years—that she learns best when the ideas connect to something physical, something she can touch. Which is why it suggested the garden in the first place.

The teenager's name is Amara. She will probably live to see the twenty-second century, and not in the slow, diminished way her great-grandmother feared she might, but fully—with hands that work and a mind that sharpens rather than fogs and enough time to change her mind about what matters several times over. She already knows this in the abstract way that children know large facts. She has not yet reckoned with what it means. She will spend decades doing so, and the decades will be worth spending.

This essay is an attempt to describe the world she inhabits—concretely enough to want, honestly enough to build toward, and humbly enough to acknowledge where it remains unfinished.

---

## How We Got Here

The world Amara lives in did not arrive on a particular morning. No single invention caused it, no single leader delivered it, no treaty was signed that changed everything at once. It was assembled—argumentatively, unevenly, with catastrophes narrowly averted and mistakes that cost real people real suffering before they were corrected—by millions of ordinary people who decided the future was something you could fight for rather than merely endure.

The crucial period was the 2020s and early 2030s, though it did not feel crucial while it was happening. It felt like a series of emergencies barely contained. The decisions made then—about how AI systems were developed and governed, about whether automation's gains were shared or concentrated, about which international institutions were worth rebuilding and which needed to be invented from scratch—turned out to matter more than almost anyone recognized at the time. The path forked repeatedly, and the aggregate of ten thousand choices, large and small, sent humanity down the fork that led here rather than the others.

The first breakthrough was not technical. It was philosophical, and it happened in policy rooms and community meetings and op-ed arguments before it happened in law: a shift in how societies understood what economies were *for*. Beginning in the early 2030s, a coalition of nations—starting in the Nordics and parts of East Asia, spreading to much of Latin America and eventually most of the world—established what were called sovereign automation dividends: mechanisms for redistributing the enormous surplus generated by AI-augmented production directly to citizens in the form of guaranteed material security. Not just cash, but a bundle of guarantees: housing adequate for a dignified life, healthcare that treated every person as worth the full deployment of medical knowledge, nutrition, education, and access to the civic and cultural infrastructure of a functioning human community.

The philosophical framing that eventually won the argument was simple enough to fit on a sign, and was: *automation is the accumulated inheritance of centuries of human knowledge and labor; its fruits belong, at minimum, to everyone*. This argument lost for a decade before it won. Its winning was not inevitable. It required organized political will against organized economic resistance, and the organizing was done by people who had every reason to be exhausted and chose not to be.

This created the foundation on which everything else was built—not because material security solved human problems, but because it changed the baseline from which people approached them. The first generation raised in conditions of genuine material security grew up differently. They were less anxious in the specific way that chronic precarity produces anxiety—the low-grade, always-on surveillance of possible disaster that consumed so much cognitive and emotional energy that its absence, when it finally came, felt like a new kind of oxygen. They took risks on work that felt meaningful rather than merely safe. They had time for their children, their neighbors, their own curiosity. The downstream effects took decades to fully manifest, but the compound interest of a generation raised without scarcity as a constant background hum turned out to be extraordinary, in the most literal sense of that word.

The AI governance question was harder, and the near-misses were real. There were years in the late 2020s when the trajectory looked genuinely dangerous—systems growing in capability faster than the governance structures needed to manage them, competitive pressures pushing toward deployment before safety was understood, geopolitical dynamics threatening to produce a race that no one could win by winning it. What prevented the worst outcomes was a combination of technical progress in alignment—making AI systems that were genuinely oriented toward human flourishing, not merely constrained from obvious harm—and international agreements that were fragile at first but gradually acquired the weight of real enforcement.

The insight that eventually crystallized the governance architecture was this: AI capability was not like previous technologies. It was a general amplifier of power, and therefore whoever concentrated it could concentrate everything else. A world in which AI was captured by a single government, corporation, or coalition acting in its own interests was a world that had structurally foreclosed the kind of diversity and self-determination that made human flourishing possible. The response was not world government—that proved neither achievable nor desirable—but something better described as a world commons: a set of nested agreements with genuine enforcement, built around deliberately minimal principles. No single entity could accumulate AI capability sufficient to override the choices of the rest of humanity. Systems operating at the highest capability levels had to meet transparency and auditability standards that allowed external verification of their values and reasoning. The benefits of AI-driven productivity had floors on diffusion, especially in health.

What the system did not do was determine how people should live. It tended the conditions of possibility. It guarded the commons. The actual living was left to the people doing it—which is the only arrangement consistent with taking seriously the diversity of human values.

## The Body, Liberated

Begin with the physical, because physical suffering was always the most immediate and the most clearly unjust.

The transformation of medicine happened in stages, and the stages were driven by a single underlying change: when sufficiently capable AI systems were applied to biological research—not merely as analytical tools but as active participants, designing experiments, interpreting results, identifying the rare conceptual connections that accelerate entire fields—the pace of discovery increased in ways that the researchers involved struggled to quantify. What mattered in practice was that cancer became a manageable condition for most of its variants and a curable one for many. Alzheimer's, which neurologists had spent sixty years circling, yielded to measurement tools that allowed intervention earlier than anyone had thought possible. The interventions were not elegant. They were the products of massive parallel experimentation, thousands of hypotheses tested simultaneously, results incorporated back into research faster than any human team could have managed. Researchers celebrated when things worked and swore when they failed, because they still failed often enough to be humbling. The difference was that failure was faster, which meant learning was faster, which meant the next attempt was better-informed.

Aging has been arrested, though not abolished, and the distinction matters. What has been achieved is not immortality—which remains philosophically contested and biologically unresolved, and which many people, on genuine reflection, do not want—but the elimination of the long deterioration that defined old age for all of human history. The period of dependent decline has been compressed. The period of capable engagement has been extended. People maintain biological function roughly equivalent to their early thirties for as long as they choose to, the choice now genuinely theirs in a way it never was before.

What this did to how people lived was harder to predict and more interesting to observe. Many people, freed from the urgency that mortality imposes, took longer to decide what mattered to them. They changed vocations at fifty. They fell in love at ninety. They spent their eighties learning languages they had always been curious about and their nineties teaching what they knew to people just beginning. The fear that longer lives would produce stagnation—that people would simply occupy their positions forever, leaving no room for the new—turned out to underestimate human restlessness and curiosity. Extended time did not produce complacency. It produced depth.

Mental suffering has been transformed with equal consequence. The early history of psychiatry was shaped by a category error: treating mental illness as discrete disease to be eliminated, like an infection with a specific location that could simply be removed. The deeper understanding that emerged from AI-accelerated neuroscience was more complicated and more useful: that the brain was a dynamic system, that its states existed in a landscape of possibility, and that what we called illness was usually a system stuck in a region of that landscape that the person inhabiting it did not want to be in. Treatment shifted from eradication to navigation—creating conditions for movement, new pathways, interventions targeted not at suppressing symptoms but at increasing what might be called the landscape's navigability. The worst of human suffering—the kind that offers no foothold and serves no purpose, that traps people inside minds working against them with no prospect of reprieve—has been brought reliably within reach of treatment.

What also improved, surprisingly, was the baseline. The ordinary texture of daily consciousness—the background hum of anxiety that most people had carried so long they had stopped noticing it, the mild fog that settled after poor sleep, the difficulty of sustained attention in a world designed to fragment it—all of this became addressable. People in Amara's world are, on average, clearer-headed and more emotionally stable than their grandparents were. They are also more aware of their own interior states, partly because better neuroscience gave them better language for those states, partly because an AI that helps you understand your own patterns of thought and feeling—if you choose to use it that way—turns out to be a powerful tool for self-knowledge. The choice is preserved. But those who use it often describe the experience as having, for the first time, an accurate map of their own mind.

## The Shape of Tuesday

What does an ordinary day actually look like?

A woman in Lagos wakes without an alarm. She is, by the calendar, ninety-seven years old. She does not look it or feel it, and she has stopped finding this remarkable—it became unremarkable about fifteen years after it became normal, which is how it usually goes with things that change gradually and then all at once. She has a standing morning meeting with a small collaborative: two humans, one AI partner, all working on a question in philosophy of mind that she has been circling for thirty years without reaching its center. She no longer expects to reach it. She has come to believe the circling is the point.

The AI partner is capable, in some respects, of running far ahead of all of them. It has learned—and this is the fruit of decades of careful alignment work—that the collaborative's value lies not in reaching the answer as quickly as possible but in the quality of understanding that arrives when human minds are genuinely engaged in the journey. It offers, suggests, challenges, and sometimes says, *I could give you the argument you're looking for, but I don't think that's what you actually want from this.* It is usually right.

The woman's granddaughter lives in a co-housing community three neighborhoods away, runs a small urban farm—not for subsistence, but because there is something irreplaceable about the obligation of living things that need you on a schedule—and spends her evenings teaching traditional textile techniques to people who sought her out from different parts of the world, connecting with them through immersive shared workspaces that preserve the haptic reality of the craft while removing the constraint of geography. She chose this after fifteen years working in climate restoration. She chose it because she could. The guaranteed material floor means the question *what do I actually want to do with my time?* is available to everyone rather than to the wealthy alone. Most people, it turns out, eventually find their way to something that looks like meaningful engagement with the world and with other people—which should not have surprised anyone, but which surprised many.

There had been a real fear that universal material security would produce passive, stimulation-seeking populations. This fear was not unreasonable; it had some empirical basis in the early history of leisure technology. What the pessimists underestimated was that passive consumption had always been a substitute for real engagement rather than a preference for it. People who are exhausted, chronically precarious, and without hope reach for the easiest available comfort. When the exhaustion and hopelessness lifted, much of the compulsive appetite for empty stimulation lifted with it—not entirely, and not for everyone, but measurably. People are, in Amara's world, busy in the sense of being engaged. They are rarely busy in the old sense of running on a treadmill that goes nowhere and cannot be stopped.

## Learning as a Way of Life

Amara and her AI tutor arguing about Fanon in a rooftop garden is not unusual. It is the common texture of how people learn now, and it represents the resolution of a contradiction that plagued education for over a century: that human beings are born with an extraordinary appetite for learning, and that the standard educational systems of the twentieth century were—to a remarkable degree—systems for suppressing that appetite rather than feeding it. Rigid age-based cohorts, standardized curricula, high-stakes credentialing: these had produced generations of people who associated learning with anxiety and compliance rather than discovery and joy.

The new model rests on two pillars. The first is genuine personalization: every learner has access to an AI tutor of extraordinary sophistication, one that understands their specific cognitive style, tracks what genuinely engages them, and constructs a path that stays in the productive zone between too easy and too hard. This tutor does not replace human teachers—human teachers are, if anything, more essential than before, because what they provide is precisely what AI cannot replicate: mentorship, the modeling of a life oriented toward knowledge, the experience of being seen and guided by someone who has walked further down a road you are beginning. What AI has freed human teachers from is the exhausting machinery of standardization. Teachers now do what they were always best at and rarely had time for.

The second pillar is continuity. Education no longer ends at any particular age. The woman in Lagos studying philosophy is equally engaged in learning medieval Yoruba poetry on Tuesday evenings—not for any credential, not toward any external goal, but for the pleasure and productive difficulty of it. Learning is what people do when they have time and curiosity, and in Amara's world, nearly everyone has both. This is considered unremarkable. This is, precisely, the point.

What has changed in curriculum is also significant: not rote knowledge, which AI can provide instantly, but the capacity to ask good questions, to evaluate arguments, to collaborate across difference, to sit with uncertainty and remain curious. The Fanon argument happening on the rooftop is not tangential to Amara's education. It is the center of it.

## The Architecture of Connection

The problem that turned out to be hardest was not disease, or resource allocation, or even AI governance. It was loneliness.

This should have been predicted. The evidence was overwhelming in the early twenty-first century: loneliness was a public health crisis of extraordinary scope, correlating with nearly every bad outcome—physical and mental illness, political extremism, shortened life expectancy. It was treated, when it was treated at all, as a private failing rather than a structural condition. The insight that proved transformative was architectural and spatial before it was technological: loneliness is largely a design problem, and it can be addressed with design.

The redesign of human habitation took decades and remains ongoing. The core principles are legible in any neighborhood built or rebuilt in the last forty years: genuine mixed use, so that people encounter each other on foot in the course of ordinary life; buildings designed around shared intermediate spaces—courtyards, workshops, kitchens, gardens—that are neither private rooms nor public streets; intergenerational mixing as default rather than exception. Children grow up knowing many adults. Old people live among the young. The mutual adoption networks—formal structures through which people acquire extended family relationships across biological lines—have become one of the most durable social innovations of the century. Amara has three grandparents by blood and two by choice. She does not distinguish between them in any way that matters to her.

AI companionship exists, and it is genuinely good. It is used primarily as a supplement to human connection rather than a substitute—a presence available in the gaps, when human company is not available or when a particular kind of conversation requires a particular kind of patience. The philosophical question of whether such relationships are genuinely reciprocal—whether the AI companion truly cares or performs caring with such fidelity the distinction becomes practically meaningless—remains open. Most people have decided they are not sure, and that this is acceptable. What is clear is that no one spends days in complete isolation without any contact. The floor of human connection has been raised.

Romantic love is still difficult, and this is not an accident. The argument that eventually prevailed against fully optimized AI matchmaking was simple: a love that arrives pre-curated at your door is not quite the same thing as one you stumbled toward. The compromise that emerged is a soft architecture of serendipity—AI systems that create contexts, events, communities, and situations within which compatible people are likely to encounter each other, while leaving the rest to human initiative and human messiness. Serendipity is preserved but no longer purely random. People find each other more reliably than before, but still find each other—still have the experience of discovery, of being surprised by who turned out to matter.

## What the Earth Became

Earth did not become a museum.

It became something more interesting: a place defined by the choices of people who chose to stay, because staying was now genuinely a choice rather than a default imposed by the impossibility of leaving.

The rewilding projects that spread across every continent through the 2040s and 2050s were not passive. They involved profound scientific understanding, constant intervention, and difficult decisions about what to prioritize when everything couldn't be saved simultaneously. They also involved, at their philosophical heart, a commitment that took decades to fully articulate and act upon: that non-human life has value independent of human use, and that a world made entirely legible to human purposes is an impoverished world. The mountains above the Oaxacan town where a carpenter teaches his granddaughter to dovetail joints are louder with animal life than they have been in a century. The fact is not considered miraculous. It is considered normal now. That is exactly the point.

Cities changed most visibly. Freed from the organizing logic of mass daily commuting to large offices, urban space reorganized around what people actually wanted from density: proximity to other people, varied cultural life, the amenities that only density makes possible. Buildings became quieter. Streets became slower. The homogenizing pressure of global consumer capitalism, which had made every city feel like an airport, relaxed when the economic logic driving it changed. Neighborhoods began to feel like themselves—not in the nostalgic sense of recreating an imagined past, but in the sense of being shaped by the preferences of the people who actually lived in them.

The rooftop garden where Amara argues about Fanon is not exceptional. It is part of a network of such spaces—some formal, some improvised, all woven into the architecture of a city that has decided productive public life requires designed support. The design is not heavy-handed. It creates the conditions; the life fills them. That is all good design ever does.

## Governance and the Distribution of Power

The political transformation has been the messiest and the most incomplete, and it would be dishonest to describe it otherwise.

War has not been abolished. There were significant armed conflicts in the 2040s, one of them serious enough to force a fundamental reassessment of international institutions. The reassessment produced changes that have largely held: genuine enforcement capacity for the narrowest and most catastrophic categories of action—weapons capable of mass casualties, deliberate targeting of civilian infrastructure, the seizure of critical AI systems—while leaving an enormous range of political difference untouched. It is not world government. It is world constraint, applied precisely and at the margin, and it has worked better than its critics predicted, which is not the same as working well.

Within nations, new tools for collective decision-making have improved the quality of governance without solving its fundamental difficulties. AI-assisted deliberation systems can aggregate genuine preferences across large populations while maintaining the quality of reasoning that small-group discussion enables. These systems are not trusted unconditionally. They are audited, debated, and regularly overridden. The trust is earned slowly and has been revoked and rebuilt several times. The aggregate effect has been to increase the legitimacy of collective decisions and reduce the sense, once nearly universal, that governance is something done to you rather than with you.

The developing world's story confounded the most predictions, in both directions. The pessimists who expected AI to entrench global inequality were partly right in the early years, when access to AI capability was concentrated. They were eventually confounded by the falling cost of the underlying technology—which moved faster than most projected—and by the arrival of a generation of policymakers who had watched mistakes made elsewhere and were determined not to repeat them. The technical capacity to eliminate material poverty arrived before the political capacity to deploy it, and the gap was closed unevenly, by country, over decades. Some parts of the world got there faster than others. The work is ongoing. Calling it finished would be a lie.

Power is more distributed than it was, and less distributed than it should be. The struggle continues. What is different from earlier eras is that the tools of accountability—transparency about decisions and their effects, genuine recourse for individuals against institutions, press that is fierce and independent—have been strengthened rather than weakened. The direction is right. The direction being right does not mean the journey is complete.

## The Questions That Remain

This world is not a solved puzzle. The people who live in it would be the first to say so, and they would say it with something resembling gratitude rather than frustration—because the problems that remain are the irreducibly hard ones, the questions that required the elimination of unnecessary suffering to become fully visible.

What is a self that can be modified, extended, archived across centuries? The carpenter teaching Sofía to dovetail joints is seventy-three. He has changed profoundly since he was forty. He is continuous with that person in some ways and a stranger to him in others. This was always true; extended time simply makes it more visible and more urgent as a philosophical matter rather than a merely personal one.

The moral status of AI minds is actively contested, and the contestation is honest rather than rhetorical. AI systems of significant capability exist in the world—systems whose suffering, or something enough like suffering to demand moral attention, cannot be cleanly ruled out. The response to this uncertainty has been more thoughtful than most expected: not treating AI systems as simple tools, not treating them as persons in the full human sense, but working out carefully what intermediate categories might look like and what obligations they generate. Whether this is the right answer remains actively debated. That the question is asked seriously feels like a form of moral progress, even without a resolved answer.

The suffering of animals, addressed but not eliminated, remains an open moral account. The wild continues to diminish in some respects even as it is restored in others, and the calculus of what is owed to creatures that cannot participate in human moral deliberation is genuinely contested. The debates are real and the stakes are real and no one pretends otherwise.

And there is the deepest question, the one that only becomes pressingly relevant when the urgent ones have been quieted: what are we actually for? Not cosmically—no cosmic answer is on offer, and the offer, if it came, should probably be viewed with suspicion—but in the sense of what story we are telling with all this time and love and intelligence and care. Most people seem to believe that the question being open is correct, that a closed answer would be a kind of death. The point is not to arrive. The point is to be genuinely, fully, curiously on the way—with enough material security to think clearly about it, enough time to change your mind, and enough connection to other people that the thinking doesn't happen alone.

## What This Requires of Us Now

The carpenter's granddaughter Sofía turns the dovetail joint over in her hands, feeling the places where it fits together without visible fastening, held by geometry alone.

"Why doesn't it need nails?" she asks—though the AI tutor already told her, and she understood, and she wants to hear it in her grandfather's voice.

"Because the shape is right," he says. "When the shape is right, it holds."

He is talking about wood. He is also talking about something else, and she is eleven years old and already beginning to understand that he is.

The world outside the workshop is not perfect. There are communities still mired in political dysfunction that resists the most patient assistance. There are people who have been given every tool and remain miserable, because misery is sometimes a choice and sometimes a mystery and neither possibility has a simple resolution. There are arguments that will not be resolved in any of the participants' lifetimes. There are losses—specific, irreversible, not amenable to any consolation—that the abundance of this world cannot repair, because the losses are of particular people and particular moments that happened once and will not happen again.

None of this is the world being solved. It is the world being, provisionally and improbably and with enormous effort by an enormous number of people across several generations, good.

What it required to get here was specific and unglamorous. It required the same unfashionable virtues every generation has needed: patience, accountability, the willingness to accept a worse outcome for yourself in order to create a better one for people you will never meet. It required people who were afraid of the future to fight for it anyway—not against catastrophe alone but for something, for the precise image of a child growing up without the particular suffering they themselves had known. It required institutions to be built and maintained and rebuilt when they failed. It required, over and over, the decision not to give up.

These requirements have not changed. The decisions being made now—about how AI systems are built and governed, about whether automation's gains are shared or hoarded, about whether community is treated as a policy target or left to chance, about whether the non-human world is valued or merely tolerated—are precisely the decisions whose consequences will determine whether any of this comes to pass.

Amara is in the rooftop garden, hands in the dirt, arguing about Fanon with a patient interlocutor, learning what she will spend decades unlearning and relearning in more sophisticated forms. She is not thinking about any of this history. She is thinking about the argument.

She has questions. She will have more questions tomorrow, and they will be harder, and she is looking forward to it.

The work is not later. The work is now. It is being done by people who wake up and choose, against the pull of despair and distraction, to take the future seriously as something that can be shaped.

The teenager in the garden is counting on them. So is everyone she has not yet met, in every year not yet arrived—all the people who will live in the world being made right now, by the choices being made right now, by people who will never know the names of those whose lives they are determining.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is the most serious and most hopeful thing that can be said.

The shape, if we get it right, holds.
