# The Common Inheritance: Notes Toward a World That Chose Itself

---

There is a morning I want to describe to you. Not as prophecy, and not as consolation. As a serious attempt to say: this is what could actually happen, if enough people, in enough places, made enough of the right choices over enough time. The choices were not secret. The path was not smooth. But the destination was real, and people reached it, and here is something like what it looked like when they did.

Let me be specific. Specificity is a form of respect.

---

## I. The Ground Beneath Everything

The first thing to understand about the world of 2070 is that it was built on a foundation that earlier generations kept failing to lay: the problem of energy was genuinely, completely, non-provisionally solved.

This sounds simple. It was not simple. It took until the late 2030s for solar generation to cross the threshold where the phrase "effectively free" stopped being advocacy and became engineering fact — where the marginal cost of the next kilowatt-hour dropped, in daylight hours across most of the inhabited world, below any number worth calculating. The bottleneck shifted, as everyone who had been paying attention expected, to storage and transmission. And into those two problems flooded the greatest concentration of applied intelligence and capital in human history, because whoever solved them controlled the shape of everything that came after.

They were solved. Not elegantly — the actual solution was a combination of fourth-generation iron-air batteries, a substantially rebuilt continental grid across Europe and North America, and small modular nuclear reactors providing the stable baseload that intermittent renewables could not. It was unglamorous. It worked. By 2044, energy in the developed world was cheap enough that its per-unit cost had ceased to be a meaningful variable in most household or industrial calculations. By 2055, most of the developing world had followed, leapfrogging the fossil infrastructure that wealthier nations had spent a century building and would spend the following decades carefully dismantling.

What happened next was the part the forecasters had consistently underestimated.

Cheap energy does not merely make existing things cheaper. It reorganizes the space of what is possible. Desalination plants scaled to genuine abundance along coastlines that had known water stress for generations. Vertical farms appeared in cities — not as experiments, not as marketing, but as serious food infrastructure that reduced the agricultural footprint of dense populations by a third. The Sahel greened, slowly and unevenly, through ten thousand projects each made viable by the collapse of power costs. Lab-grown meat achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2047, fell below it in 2053, and within a decade had made industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that decades of ethical argument never quite managed.

The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s — which were grimmer than the news cycle had typically conveyed, and which had killed more people than most comfortable Westerners had been willing to know — became, by 2060, a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity had become virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation had been dissolved, and what remained required active political malice to manufacture rather than merely passive neglect to permit.

This was not nothing. This was, measured in human lives and the suffering attached to them, perhaps the largest single improvement in the human condition since the germ theory of disease. It deserves to be named before anything else, because everything else rests on it.

## II. The Infrastructure of Judgment

But material abundance, by itself, solves only material problems. The twenty-first century had plenty of those. It also had a deeper problem, which was that humans had developed the capacity to make enormous collective decisions affecting everyone, without developing the wisdom or the institutions to make those decisions well. The gap between power and judgment had been widening for a century. Closing it was the other half of the work.

It closed in the way most important things close: not through any single transformation, but through the accumulation of small reforms that turned out to be mutually reinforcing in ways no one had fully anticipated.

The first was a change in how governance was held accountable. In 2031, a small Portuguese city called Évora, facing a deadlocked council and a crumbling water system, ran an experiment: a public prediction market for infrastructure policy, open to citizens, engineers, and imported experts alike, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. The pipes they chose based on that aggregated wisdom are still running. Within seven years, forty cities had adopted variants of the model. Within fifteen, it was unremarkable. By 2048, the European Union had codified anticipatory governance — a requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to a forecasting audit before implementation, with track records published and updated in real time.

What this produced, slowly, was legibility: not the naive transparency of everything being visible, but the deeper legibility of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions that could be checked against outcomes, decision-makers who were held to an epistemically traceable standard. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain, and then more expensive, and then the kind of person who made it consistently tended to lose the positions that allowed them to make it.

The second reform was older in theory, long delayed in practice. Land value taxation — the idea of taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and work upon it — had been economically understood since the nineteenth century. The first serious jurisdiction to implement it fully was a mid-sized Taiwanese city in 2029. The results were exactly what the theory predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public coffers filled without anyone feeling confiscated from, because the value being taxed was value that no individual had created. The reform spread with the irresistible logic of things that actually work. By 2045, most OECD cities had adopted some form of it. The speculative real estate complex that had, for a century, pumped value from workers into landholders while producing nothing, was defanged. Young people could afford to live in cities. This mattered for everything else.

The third was the rationalist project, which had begun in corners of the internet in the early 2000s and spent decades doing the unglamorous work of spreading epistemic hygiene: thinking in probabilities rather than certainties, updating on evidence, recognizing cognitive biases not as trivia but as active hazards, building the habit of asking what would change my mind. It never became universal. It did not need to. It needed only to become common enough among the people who shaped institutions and curricula that it changed the character of those institutions. By 2050, a child educated in most wealthy democracies received formal training in probabilistic reasoning, logical fallacies, and the history of confident error as a matter of course. The effects were large and diffuse and impossible to attribute to any single cause, which is true of almost everything that actually matters.

The fourth was the repair of minds.

Mental illness had always been undertreated, but the 2020s produced something like a reckoning: the post-pandemic inventory of human suffering, the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching regulated clinical practice, the accumulation of longitudinal data that finally made psychiatric treatment more empirical and less guesswork. By 2045, the treatment gap for major depressive disorder had closed by seventy percent in high-income countries, and the work was advancing. The chronic grinding weight of untreated anxiety and depression and trauma — which had been so normalized as to be nearly invisible, shaping a large fraction of all human decisions — began to lift. People who are not in chronic pain make different choices than people who are. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more capable of imagining futures different from their present. The slow repair of millions of minds produced, as a side effect, something that looked like a modest improvement in collective judgment — not because people had become smarter, but because they had become less desperate.

These four things — better collective epistemics, reformed land tenure, the spread of careful thinking, the treatment of mental suffering — were not sufficient by themselves. They were the soil in which better institutions could grow. And they interacted with each other in ways that compounded over decades, each one making the others easier to sustain.

## III. What a Morning Looks Like

Here is a specific Tuesday in Bergen, Norway, in 2067.

Mara is thirty-eight years old. She wakes without an alarm in an apartment she owns — the land value reform having made ownership accessible on what her grandmother's generation would have called a librarian's salary, combined with a small housing cooperative that pooled initial deposits. The building is net energy positive on most days, exporting to the grid. Her food came partly from a vertical farm nine kilometers away and partly from a community garden on the building's roof that she tends on alternate Saturday mornings, which she finds that she does not resent, mostly because the people who tend it with her are interesting.

She is not anxious about money in the way that would have been ordinary for someone in her economic position two generations earlier. This requires explanation, because the path to this absence of anxiety runs directly through some of the hardest political work of the preceding fifty years. The automation wave of the 2030s had displaced most routine clerical and logistical work, and the displacement had been genuinely painful — not the techno-optimist soft landing that earlier forecasters had imagined, but years of real dislocation. The political response, after false starts, had taken the form of a reduced standard working week in most of Northern Europe (thirty-two hours by 2045), a universal basic dividend funded partly by land value taxes and partly by a levy on automation productivity gains, and a massive expansion of publicly supported creative and caring work. The transition left marks. The destination was genuinely better.

What Mara does with her Tuesday is substantially her own business. She spends her mornings doing work that emerged organically from two decades of accumulated interest: she helps people in her city understand complicated things. Not as a formally credentialed teacher, though she completed a teaching certification after the occupational licensing reforms of 2038 made such credentials more about demonstrated competence and less about institutional gatekeeping. She does this through written pieces, small group sessions, and a podcast with perhaps six thousand regular listeners — paid through a combination of small subscriptions, municipal cultural funding distributed via participatory budgeting, and occasional commissions. She is, by any reasonable measure, flourishing. She knows this, which is itself a form of wealth that earlier generations could not always afford.

Her afternoons are her own. She is learning glassblowing because she finds it beautiful and humbling. She will not be good at it for years. She does not particularly mind. She has years.

Her children — she has two, twelve and sixteen — attend schools redesigned in the 2040s around genuine understanding rather than credential accumulation. The curricula teach logic and rhetoric, the history of confident error, how to disagree without contempt, what it means for evidence to support a conclusion. This was controversial when it was introduced. It is now simply what schools are for.

Her daughter wants to be a biogeochemist. The field is extraordinary. Gene drives have eliminated wild-type malaria-carrying mosquitoes across Sub-Saharan Africa — a long-fought intervention that proceeded after the governance frameworks caught up with the science, through a process that was slow and frustrating and produced a good outcome, which is the process. Her daughter is working on engineered soil microbiomes for carbon drawdown. It may or may not work. The trying is the point, and the world has enough surplus to support the trying.

## IV. What Was Kept, and Why

There were serious people, during the transition, who believed that the path to a good future required discarding tradition — treating the accumulated weight of human history as too contaminated by oppression and error to be worth preserving. They were right that history contained enormous oppression and error. They were wrong about what followed.

What was kept, because it was worth keeping: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together in communities of different sizes; the art forms that had learned, through long practice, to hold grief and joy simultaneously; the philosophical traditions that had developed tools for reasoning about the good life, imperfect tools but tools that had proven more durable than improvised alternatives; the religious and spiritual practices that had taught people to hold their own smallness without being destroyed by it. None of these were kept unchanged. They were brought into conversation with new circumstances, new knowledge, new possibilities. The people in this world argue about the past constantly. This is a sign of health. A civilization that has forgotten what humans are capable of at their worst cannot protect itself.

What was not kept: the idea that some people's suffering does not count; the mechanisms by which power reproduced itself regardless of merit or desert; the particular form of loneliness that industrial society had perfected, the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves.

The children of 2067 roam. This requires explaining to anyone who remembers the early years of the century. They roam because the built environment was redesigned — at the scale of neighborhoods, then cities, across two generations — to be navigable by children, to treat their presence in public space as normal and desirable rather than as a liability risk. This redesign was itself enabled by the land value reform: when land speculation collapses, cities can afford to think about what streets are for. The streets of Bergen are what streets have always secretly wanted to be: places where people encounter each other, where children are chasing something or being chased, where the baker who has been there forty years argues cheerfully with the woman who arrived last spring, the argument being about bread.

## V. The Living Experiments

By 2055, thirty-one charter cities had been established around the world, each a deliberate institutional experiment. Some had failed: two collapsed into ordinary corruption within a decade, one was absorbed back into its surrounding nation-state. The failures were documented and studied with the same seriousness as the successes. This was considered part of the point.

One in Rwanda had pioneered algorithmic zoning that made construction permits nearly automatic for compliant designs, eliminating the years-long delays that had made building so expensive in traditional cities. Its housing costs ran forty percent below comparable regional cities.

One in Iceland had implemented a hybrid governance model: structured prediction markets for policy outcomes, citizen deliberative assemblies for major value questions, and conditional legislation — policies with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes, triggering automatic review when stated predictions fell short. The government was small. It was unusually competent. These two facts were related.

These experiments circulated. Ideas spread from charter cities to traditional cities the way all good ideas spread: through the stubborn persistence of people who had seen something work and would not stop saying so.

## VI. The Part That Is Harder to Describe

There is a dimension of this world that strains the language I have available. Not because it is alien — it is, in some fundamental way, continuous with what we already reach for when we reach for what is best. But because describing it would be like trying to describe music to someone who had never heard any. The nearest familiar thing is not the thing itself.

The best experiences available to people now — the moments of genuine connection, of understanding so deep it feels like remembering something forgotten, of beauty that reorganizes you for weeks after — these point in a direction. They are evidence that the territory in that direction is real. The people of 2067 have traveled further in that direction. Their inner lives are richer in ways that are not reducible to the absence of suffering, though the absence of chronic suffering was a necessary precondition. They have more access to each other, to beauty, to the kind of meaning that cannot be manufactured. They have learned to live in their bodies more fully, to inhabit time differently, to hold the fact of their own mortality with something other than terror or denial.

I cannot tell you exactly what this looks like, because the description would be inadequate in ways I can feel even as I reach for it. But it is not some posthuman transformation. It is recognizably human. It is more of what you already know when you know what is best.

## VII. The Ground Truth

In 2073, a historian named Amara wrote a paper asking a question that turns out to have no clean answer: when did it change?

She was looking for the hinge — the decade when the trajectory of human civilization shifted from the patterns of the early century, with its accumulation of catastrophic risks and institutional failures, to the patterns of the middle century, with its imperfect but genuine flourishing. She could not find one. The sediment had accumulated grain by grain: the prediction markets that made governance legible, the land value taxes that made cities livable, the energy abundance that made scarcity a choice rather than a fate, the therapy revolution that lifted the weight of untreated suffering, the epistemic reforms that made careful thinking common enough to matter, the charter cities that ran living experiments in collective life.

None of these were sufficient. All of them together were not inevitable. There had been close calls — a pandemic managed worse than it was, a financial crisis that nearly became a cascade, elections that delayed reforms by years or decades. The story was not preordained.

But it happened. The threshold was crossed without a single catastrophe to mark it and without a single triumph to celebrate. The whole attention of civilization could be turned, when something threatened to go badly wrong, and could take care of it. Not always, not perfectly, but often enough, and more often as the decades passed.

What had changed, in the end, was not human nature, which remained generous and petty and creative and cruel and funny and frightened in roughly the proportions it had always been. What changed was the infrastructure beneath human nature: the systems that either amplify the pettiness and cruelty, or the generosity and creativity. Those systems were improved, slowly and imperfectly and without sufficient credit going to anyone in particular. The humans inside them remained recognizably themselves. They had a little more room to be the better versions of themselves, a little more of the time.

That was the whole of it.

## VIII. The Letter

I want to close by acknowledging that this essay is, in one sense, a letter to you — whoever you are, reading this from whatever position along the long arc of history.

If you are reading this before the work is done, here is what I want you to know: the future described here was not the result of any single revolution, any charismatic leader, any deus ex machina of technology. It was the result of people in each generation who could hold two things at once — clear-eyed recognition of how much was wrong, and genuine faith in how much could be right. This combination is rarer and more precious than either attitude alone. The cynics saw clearly and did nothing. The naive hoped vaguely and were blindsided. The people who built this world saw clearly and believed anyway, and the believing was not in spite of the seeing but because of it. They understood what was actually at stake. That understanding was the engine.

If you are reading this from the other side of the work, from the world I have been trying to describe: know how it was made. Know the fear and the work and the many times it nearly came apart. Know this not to feel guilty, but to feel responsible — which is the weight of gratitude rather than of debt.

And then live. Fully, specifically, in the particular light that falls on the particular stones of your particular city on a Tuesday morning, in the neighborhood that was built by actual people who argued with each other about what it should be, who changed their minds, who made mistakes and corrected them, across generations.

The city was built by actual people.

So was the world.

So, now, must be everything that comes next — whatever that means in your time, along horizons I cannot quite see.

The shore is real. The birds are not lying. And the ocean, when you reach it, will be nothing like a scaled-up lake.

---

*The path from here to there runs through decades of patient work: on energy and its distribution, on institutions and their reform, on medicine and education, on the slow transformation of what communities expect from themselves and each other. It runs through failures and recoveries. It requires, at every step, enough people who understand that the size of what could be lost if we fail is exactly equal to the size of what could be gained if we succeed — which is very large, larger than our ordinary categories comfortably hold.*

*That is the argument this essay is making. Not for a plan, but for a posture. Not for a destination, but for the seriousness with which the direction deserves to be taken.*

*The morning is long. It is still early. The work is yours.*
