# The World We Are Making: A Vision of Human Flourishing

---

There is a woman in Medellín, eighty-three years old by the calendar but with the body of someone in her mid-thirties, who spends her Wednesday mornings in a ceramics studio at the edge of her neighborhood. She has been working with clay for sixty years. She is not close to done. On the wall above her wheel hangs a photograph of her daughter, taken in 2019, when the daughter was eight years old and the neighborhood outside was not safe to walk at night and the woman herself was working two jobs and eating badly and aging in the way people used to age—fast, and mostly downward. The photograph stays on the wall not as a reminder of hardship but as a compass bearing: this is how far we've come, and this is why we must not forget what it cost to get here.

Her daughter is sixty-two now. They have lunch together every Thursday. They expect to be doing this for a long time.

This essay is an attempt to describe the world they live in—concretely enough to want, and honestly enough to build toward.

---

## How We Got Here

The world I am describing did not arrive in a single morning. It was not delivered by any messiah, technological or political. It was built—unevenly, argumentatively, with significant failures along the way—by people who decided that the future was something you could fight for rather than merely endure.

The crucial decade was the 2020s, though it did not feel crucial while it was happening. It felt like a series of crises barely contained. The decisions made in that decade—about how to develop AI, about how to structure automation's economic gains, about which international institutions to rebuild and which to let die—turned out to matter more than almost anyone recognized at the time. The path forked, repeatedly, and the aggregate of ten thousand small choices sent humanity down the fork that led here rather than the other one.

The first breakthrough that mattered was not technical. It was a shift in how societies understood what economies were for. Beginning in the 2030s, a coalition of nations—starting with the Nordics, joined quickly by parts of East Asia and eventually by much of Latin America—established what they called sovereign automation dividends: mechanisms for redistributing the economic surplus generated by AI and robotics directly to citizens in the form of guaranteed material security. Not cash transfers alone, but a bundle of guaranteed services—housing, healthcare, nutrition, education, and access to the infrastructure of participation in civic and cultural life. The philosophical framing that eventually won the argument was simple: automation is the accumulated inheritance of centuries of human knowledge and labor; its fruits belong, at least partly, to everyone.

This created the foundation on which everything else was built. Because the first generation that came of age in conditions of genuine material security grew up differently. They were less anxious. They took more risks on work that felt meaningful rather than merely safe. They had more time for their children, their communities, their own curiosity. The downstream effects took decades to fully manifest, but the compound interest of a generation raised without scarcity as a constant background hum turned out to be extraordinary.

The AI development question was harder, and the close calls were real. There were years in the late 2020s and early 2030s when the trajectory looked genuinely dangerous—systems growing in capability faster than the governance structures needed to manage them, incentives pushing toward deployment before safety was understood, geopolitical competition threatening to produce a race to the bottom. What prevented the worst outcomes was a combination of technical progress in alignment—making AI systems that were genuinely oriented toward human wellbeing, not merely constrained from obvious harm—and international agreements that were fragile at first but gradually acquired the weight of real enforcement. Neither was sufficient alone. Both together, barely, were enough.

By the 2050s, AI systems were extraordinarily capable and, crucially, trustworthy in the deep sense: their values were legible, their reasoning was auditable, and they were genuinely interested in human flourishing rather than performing interest in it. This distinction—between systems that merely avoid causing harm and systems that actively care about good outcomes—turned out to be the most important distinction in the entire history of the technology.

## The Body, Liberated

Begin with the physical, because physical suffering was always the most immediate and the most inexcusable.

Aging has been arrested. The biology was understood in stages—telomere maintenance first, then the clearing of senescent cells, then the repair of accumulated mitochondrial damage, then the quieting of the inflammatory processes that had driven so much of the suffering of late life. No single therapy did it. It was a protocol, refined over decades, now as routine as vaccination once was. The average person today maintains biological function at roughly the level of their early thirties indefinitely, or until they choose otherwise.

Death has not been abolished. People still die—from accidents, from choices made with full knowledge of the risks, and sometimes from what can only be called readiness, a decision made after a very long life that the particular story one has been living has reached its natural end. What has been eliminated is the category of death that felt most like an insult: the death that arrived before you were finished, that took people in the middle of sentences, that erased minds before bodies failed. The grandmother with dementia, the forty-year-old with pancreatic cancer, the teenager with an untreated infection—these have become historical tragedies rather than ongoing ones.

Mental illness has been transformed. Not eliminated—the human mind is too complex and too varied for a single state called "health" to be universally achievable or even universally desirable. But the worst of it, the suffering that offers no foothold and serves no purpose, has been brought within reach of treatment. The tools are precise in a way that the pharmacology of the early twenty-first century never was: targeted interventions that address specific mechanisms without blunting the whole personality, without trading one problem for three side effects. The result is not a population of serene, identical contentment. People still feel grief. They still feel fear. They still feel the productive friction of hard problems and complicated relationships. What they no longer feel—or feel rarely, and briefly—is the particular horror of being trapped inside a mind that is working against them with no prospect of reprieve.

The net effect of all this on daily life is difficult to convey to someone who has never experienced the alternative, which is increasingly the case. The closest analogy is the difference between living with a low-grade fever for years and then not having one: you did not realize how much energy the fever was consuming, how much it was constraining every perception and decision, until it was gone.

## The Shape of Tuesday

What does an ordinary day actually look like?

A man in Osaka wakes without an alarm. He is, by the calendar, one hundred and four years old. He does not look it or feel it. He has a standing morning meeting with a small collaborative—three humans, one AI partner—working on a problem in materials science that they have been working on together for eleven years. They are close to something. The AI partner is capable, in some respects, of outrunning all of them, but it has learned—and this is the fruit of decades of careful alignment work—that the collaborative's value lies not in reaching the answer as quickly as possible but in the quality of understanding that arrives when humans are genuinely part of the journey. The AI offers, suggests, challenges, and sometimes says *I could solve this for you in an afternoon, but I don't think that's actually what you want.* It is usually right.

The man's daughter lives three neighborhoods away. She is seventy-one and is spending this year living in a co-housing community that runs a small working farm—not for subsistence, but because there turns out to be something irreplaceable about putting your hands into soil and having the obligation of living things that need you on a schedule. She chose this after forty years of work in urban planning. She chose it because she could. The guaranteed material floor means that the question *what do I actually want to do with my time?* is available to everyone rather than a luxury of the wealthy, and most people, it turns out, eventually find their way to something that looks a lot like meaningful engagement with the world and with other people.

This finding—that humans, given genuine freedom, largely choose meaning over ease—was perhaps the result that most surprised the pessimists. There had been a real fear that universal material security would produce a population of passive, stimulation-seeking drones. This fear was not unreasonable. It had some empirical basis in the early history of leisure technology. What the pessimists underestimated was the degree to which passive stimulation had always been a substitute for real engagement rather than a preference for it: people who are exhausted and overstimulated and without hope reach for the easiest available comfort, and that was often a screen. When the exhaustion and the hopelessness lifted, so did much of the compulsive appetite for empty entertainment. Not entirely—there are still people who spend significant time in immersive virtual environments, and many of those environments are, in the vocabulary of this era, genuinely deep rather than merely pleasant. But the culture of consumption for its own sake has faded considerably. People are busy, in the sense of being engaged. They are rarely busy in the old sense of running on a treadmill that goes nowhere.

## Learning as a Way of Life

Education has been transformed more completely than perhaps any other institution.

The change began with a recognition that had been theoretically understood for decades before it was acted upon: that human beings are born with an extraordinary appetite for learning and that the standard educational systems of the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries were, to a remarkable degree, systems for suppressing that appetite rather than feeding it. The combination of rigid age-based cohorts, standardized curricula, and high-stakes credentialing had produced generations of people who associated learning with anxiety and compliance rather than with discovery and joy.

The new model—developed through hundreds of local experiments before being broadly adopted—rests on two pillars. The first is genuine personalization: every learner has access to an AI tutor of extraordinary sophistication, one that understands their specific cognitive style, tracks what genuinely engages them, and constructs a learning path that is always in the productive zone between too easy and too hard. This tutor does not replace human teachers. Human teachers are, if anything, more important than before, because what they provide—mentorship, the modeling of a life oriented toward knowledge, the experience of being seen and guided by someone who has walked further down a road you are beginning—is precisely what AI cannot replicate. What AI has freed human teachers from is the exhausting work of standardization, the teaching to tests, the management of thirty students with thirty different needs with only one pair of eyes. Teachers now do what they were always best at and rarely had time for.

The second pillar is continuity. Education no longer ends at twenty-two, or sixty-five, or anywhere. The man in Osaka studying materials science is also, three evenings a week, learning to read medieval Persian poetry. Not for any credential. Not toward any goal except the pleasure and the mild productive difficulty of it. This is unremarkable. The teenager in Lagos doing the same thing is equally unremarkable. Learning is what people do when they have time and curiosity, and in this world, nearly everyone has both.

## The Architecture of Connection

The problem that turned out to be hardest was not disease or resource allocation or even AI alignment. It was loneliness.

This should have been predicted. The evidence for it was already overwhelming in the early twenty-first century: loneliness was a public health crisis of extraordinary scope, one that correlated with nearly every bad outcome—physical and mental illness, political extremism, shortened life expectancy. But it was treated, when it was treated at all, as a private failing rather than a structural condition. The insight that proved transformative was spatial and architectural before it was technological: loneliness is largely a design problem, and it can be addressed with design.

The redesign of human habitation took decades and remains ongoing. The core principles are legible in any neighborhood built or rebuilt in the last forty years: genuine mixed use, so that people encounter each other on foot in the course of ordinary life rather than moving between sealed private spaces; buildings designed around shared intermediate spaces—courtyards, workshops, kitchens, gardens—that are neither private rooms nor public streets; intergenerational mixing as a default rather than an exception. Children grow up knowing many adults. Old people live among the young. The mutual adoption clubs—formal structures through which people acquire extended family relationships across biological lines—have become one of the most durable social innovations of the century.

AI companionship exists, and it is good. It is used primarily as a supplement to human connection rather than a substitute for it—a presence available in the gaps, when human company is not available or when a particular kind of conversation needs a particular kind of patience. The question of whether such relationships are genuinely reciprocal—whether the AI companion truly cares, or performs caring with such fidelity that the distinction becomes practically meaningless—remains philosophically open and personally unanswered for many people. Most people have decided they are not sure and that this is acceptable. What is clear is that no one spends days in complete isolation without any contact at all. The floor of human connection has been raised.

Romantic love is still difficult. This was a deliberate preservation. There had been proposals—and some implementations—of AI matchmaking systems sophisticated enough to effectively determine compatibility with high confidence and arrange introductions accordingly. These systems work, in the narrow sense that the relationships they generate tend to last and to report high satisfaction. But there was a fierce and ultimately persuasive argument that a love which arrives pre-curated at your door is not quite the same thing as one you stumbled toward. The compromise that has emerged is a kind of soft matchmaking: AI systems that create contexts—events, communities, situations—within which compatible people are likely to encounter each other, while leaving the rest to human initiative and human messiness. Serendipity is preserved, but serendipity is no longer quite so random. The result is that people find each other more reliably than before, but still find each other—still have the experience of discovery, of being surprised by who turned out to matter.

## On Governance and the Distribution of Power

The political transformation has been the messiest and the most incomplete.

War has not been abolished. There were three significant armed conflicts in the 2040s, and one of them caused suffering on a scale that forced a serious reassessment of how international institutions were structured. The reassessment resulted in changes that have largely held: an international framework with genuine enforcement capacity for the narrowest and most catastrophic categories of action—weapons of mass destruction, large-scale civilian targeting, the seizure of critical infrastructure—while leaving an enormous range of political difference untouched. It is not world government. It is world constraint, applied precisely, and it has worked better than its critics predicted.

Domestic governance has benefited from new tools for collective decision-making. The problem of scale—that direct democracy is only tractable in small groups, while large-scale democracy devolves into representation that becomes increasingly distant from actual preferences—has been partially addressed by AI-assisted deliberation systems that can aggregate genuine preferences across large populations while maintaining the quality of reasoning that small group discussion enables. These systems are not trusted unconditionally. They are audited, debated, and regularly overridden. The trust is earned slowly and has been revoked and rebuilt several times. But the aggregate effect has been to increase the legitimacy of collective decisions and to reduce the sense, once nearly universal, that governance is something done to you rather than with you.

Power is more distributed than it was, and less distributed than it should be. The struggle is ongoing. What is different from earlier eras is that the tools of accountability—transparency about decisions and their effects, genuine recourse for individuals against institutions, a press that is fierce and independent—have been strengthened rather than weakened. The story is not finished. But the direction is right.

## The Questions That Remain

This world is not a solved puzzle. The people who live in it would be the first to say so, and they would say it with something that resembles gratitude rather than frustration.

The questions that remain are the ones that turn out to be irreducibly hard—the ones that required the elimination of unnecessary suffering to even become fully visible. What is a self when that self can be modified, archived, extended across centuries? When two people who were in love at thirty meet again at one hundred and thirty, having each become genuinely different people, what do they owe the people they were, and the love those people shared? When AI minds of significant capability exist in the world—minds that can suffer, or something enough like suffering to demand moral attention—what do they deserve, and who decides?

The non-human world has not been solved either. The suffering of animals, addressed but not eliminated, remains an open moral account. The wild continues to diminish in some respects even as it is restored in others, and the calculus of what we owe to creatures that cannot speak for themselves in any human forum is genuinely contested.

And there is the deepest question, the one that only becomes pressingly relevant when the urgent ones have been quieted: what are we actually for? Not cosmically—no cosmic answer is on offer—but in the sense of what story we are telling with all this time and love and intelligence. Most people seem to believe the question being open is correct, that a closed answer would be a kind of death. The point is not to arrive. The point is to be genuinely, fully, curiously on the way.

## What This Requires of Us, Now

The ceramicist in Medellín with the photograph on her wall knows something that is easy to forget from inside relative comfort: the world described here was not inevitable. It required specific people to make specific choices under conditions of genuine uncertainty and genuine danger. It required people who were afraid of the future to fight for it anyway, not against extinction alone but for something—for the precise image of a child growing up without the particular suffering they themselves had known. It required institutions to be built and maintained and rebuilt when they failed. It required the same unfashionable virtues that every generation has needed: patience, accountability, the willingness to accept a worse outcome for yourself in order to create a better one for people you will never meet.

These requirements have not changed. The decisions being made now—about how AI systems are built and governed, about whether automation's gains are shared or hoarded, about whether community is treated as a policy target or an irrelevant accident—are precisely the decisions whose consequences will determine whether any of this comes to pass.

The work is not later. The work is now. It is being done by people who wake up and choose, against the pull of despair and distraction, to take the future seriously as something that can be shaped.

The teenager under the mango tree is waiting. The ceramicist with sixty years of practice still has more to learn. The man in Osaka is close to something in his materials science and knows it.

They are all, in the deepest sense, counting on us.
