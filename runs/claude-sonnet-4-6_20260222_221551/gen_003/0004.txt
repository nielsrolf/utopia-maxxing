# The Inhabited World: A Vision of Rooted Flourishing

---

There is a neighborhood in Medellín—not a commune, not a gated enclave, not a utopian experiment with a manifesto taped to the door—that simply works. It has worked for about thirty years now. It has a name, La Concordia, which its residents chose with full awareness of the irony and the aspiration. It has a weekly market, three community kitchens, a ceramics studio, a workshop where people repair things that break, a small school that operates somewhat differently from the national curriculum, a quiet corner of the city's broader digital infrastructure, and about four hundred people who have chosen, deliberately, to live here together according to a charter they revise every five years by vote.

The charter is not long. It specifies how shared spaces are governed, how new members are admitted, what obligations membership entails, how disputes are resolved, and how anyone who wishes to leave may do so—freely, immediately, and without social penalty. It also specifies what the neighborhood does not control: what its residents believe, whom they sleep with, what media they consume, what they do in their private homes. The charter draws a line, in other words, between the community's legitimate jurisdiction over shared life and the individual's inalienable jurisdiction over private life. This line has been argued over many times. It has moved, in both directions, over the decades. The arguing is considered healthy.

La Concordia is not unique. There are, by now, tens of thousands of communities like it on every continent—communities organized around different charters, different values, different aesthetic visions of what a good neighborhood looks like. Some are secular. Some are devoutly religious. Some are organized around specific cultural identities. Some are built around shared vocations: communities of artists, of scientists, of farmers, of craftspeople who wanted to live among others who understood what their work cost them. Some are extraordinarily diverse, having made pluralism itself their founding commitment. Some are quite homogeneous, and their members consider that homogeneity a feature rather than a failure, a precondition for the particular kind of trust and coherence they were seeking. The system that makes all of this possible does not require them to justify their preferences to anyone—only to abide by the framework that protects everyone's ability to leave and to choose again.

This essay is an attempt to describe that framework, and the world it has produced.

---

## The Architecture of Chosen Community

The great political insight of the mid-twenty-first century was that the liberal dilemma—between the freedom to live as you choose and the communal conditions that make a good life possible—was not a dilemma to be resolved but a tension to be managed at the right scale.

The error of pure individualism had always been that it denied the reality of social environments. Human beings are not atoms. The conditions in which they live shape their choices, their desires, their sense of what is possible. A person surrounded by a culture of compulsive consumption does not experience the same freedom as a person surrounded by a culture of moderation, even if both face identical legal constraints. A child who grows up in a neighborhood where adults are strangers to each other does not develop the same capacity for trust as a child who grows up knowing every adult on her block. Freedom is not just the absence of coercion; it is also the presence of conditions that make meaningful choice possible. This the atomists got badly wrong.

But the communitarian critics of liberalism had their own error: they tended to assume that communities could only be sustained by restricting the freedom to exit them, that cohesion required coercion, that the only way to preserve a shared culture was to enforce it on those who had not freely chosen it. This was empirically false and morally catastrophic wherever it was tried. The community that cannot be left is not a community; it is a trap.

The resolution, worked out in pieces across a generation of political experiments, was jurisdictional clarity: communities may govern shared life according to their chosen values, but they may never coerce private life or prevent departure. The state—or rather the web of overlapping governance structures that have replaced what we once called the state—does very little, but what it does, it does with absolute commitment. It guarantees freedom of movement. It ensures that every person, regardless of the community they were born into, receives a genuine education in the existence of alternatives. It enforces the physical and legal conditions that make exit real rather than merely nominal: you cannot be indebted into staying, cannot be socially terrorized out of leaving, cannot be denied the material floor that makes departure survivable. And it prevents communities from exporting their costs—environmental, social, economic—onto communities that did not agree to bear them.

Within those constraints, the space for human social experimentation is vast. And humans, it turns out, have used it.

## The Material Floor

None of this is possible without a prior condition: that exit is genuinely available to everyone, not just those with resources.

The automation dividend—the surplus generated by AI systems and robotic labor that, in earlier periods, flowed entirely to capital owners—is now distributed as a universal material foundation. Not lavish wealth. Not the elimination of economic incentive. A floor: housing, healthcare, nutrition, education, and the transportation credits that make movement between communities real rather than theoretical. The philosophical argument that ultimately won the political battle was not idealistic but historical: the knowledge embedded in modern AI systems was built from the accumulated intellectual labor of all previous human generations. Its fruits belong, at minimum in part, to everyone. The surplus is a commons, and the dividend is each person's share of it.

This foundation changes everything, in ways that compound over generations. When survival does not depend on staying, communities must be genuinely worth staying in. They must offer something real—connection, meaning, beauty, belonging—rather than merely extracting loyalty through the threat of destitution. The communities that have thrived under these conditions are, almost without exception, the ones that actually function as communities: places where people know each other, where shared spaces invite encounter, where the middle-aged craftsman and the elderly poet and the young parent with the toddler are all present in the same square at the same hour and have some reason to speak.

The communities that have struggled are mostly the ones that tried to sustain themselves through extraction rather than attraction. They have mostly either reformed or quietly emptied out. This is not presented as a triumph but as a correction—the slow, imperfect, ongoing correction of arrangements that persisted for centuries primarily because people could not afford to leave.

## Bodies and Time

The physical transformation of human life has been profound enough that it is easy to forget how recently it arrived.

Biological aging has been interrupted—not eliminated, but massively slowed. The protocol is a generation old now, as routine as any other preventive medicine, and its effects compound over time in ways that continue to surprise even those who designed it. A woman who is eighty-three by the calendar may have the physical capacity of someone in their late thirties. She may have been practicing ceramics for sixty years and have no sense of approaching any limit in her craft. She may look at the photograph of her daughter taken in 2019—a year when the neighborhood was not safe and she was working two jobs and aging fast, in the old downward way—and feel the distance between then and now as something almost vertiginous.

The elimination of death by preventable disease, by cancer caught too late, by the mind going before the body—this has produced a shift in temporal experience that is hard to describe to anyone who did not grow up in the shadow of the old mortality. Time feels different when it is not primarily a resource being spent. Decisions feel different when they are not being made under the pressure of an approaching deadline that no one chose. People take longer to figure out what they actually want, which turns out to be one of the most valuable things that longer life has given them.

Mental illness has been largely brought within reach of precise, targeted treatment. The key word is precise: the pharmacological tools of the early twenty-first century were sledgehammers applied to problems that required scalpels, and much of the suffering they caused came from the collateral damage of those crude instruments. The new tools address specific mechanisms without flattening the personality that surrounds them. People still feel grief. They still feel fear and the productive friction of hard relationships and genuinely difficult choices. What has been nearly eliminated is the particular suffering of a mind working against its owner with no prospect of reprieve.

## What Children Are Owed

The hardest problem in any system of voluntary communities is the one that cannot be solved by adult consent: the child who was born into a community she did not choose.

The solution is not elegant—there is no elegant solution—but it is serious. Every child, regardless of the community of her birth, receives an education in the existence of other possibilities. Not an indoctrination against her community's values, not a recruitment drive for alternatives, but a genuine, honest encounter with the fact that the world is large and contains people who have organized their lives very differently and are not, for that reason, monsters. This education is delivered by representatives of the overarching civic framework, and the right to receive it cannot be waived by parents or community leaders. The communities resist this, sometimes loudly. The framework holds, on the grounds that the child is not yet in a position to consent to the values being instilled in her, and the least we can do is ensure she knows she has a door.

That door is the guarantee of exit: any person who wishes to leave a community may do so, and the material floor means that leaving is survivable. Transit credits exist specifically to make this real. There are embassies, of a sort—small offices of the civic framework present in every inhabited area—where anyone can present themselves and request passage to somewhere else, with no justification required and no questions asked beyond logistical ones. The number of people who use these offices in any given year is small. The fact that they exist, that everyone knows they exist, is most of what makes communities governable rather than merely coercive.

Children below the age of majority have additional protections. Abuse, severe neglect, the systematic suppression of the capacity for future choice—these trigger intervention, regardless of the community's charter, regardless of its values. The line is drawn not at the community's practices but at the child's future: anything that forecloses the capacity to eventually choose is prohibited. Teaching your children your religion is permitted. Making it impossible for them to ever encounter another one is not.

## The Shape of Plurality

What does this world actually look like, lived from the inside?

A man in Osaka wakes in a community organized around collaborative scientific inquiry—about forty households, a shared library and lab, a culture that treats the work as genuinely communal rather than individually competitive. He is a hundred and four years old and does not feel it. He has been working on a problem in materials science for eleven years, in collaboration with two other humans and one AI partner whose contribution to the work is considered genuine rather than merely instrumental. The AI does not race ahead to the solution. It has been designed to understand—and genuinely seems to understand—that the value of the collaboration lies in the quality of what the humans come to know, not just in the answer that emerges. It suggests. It challenges. It sometimes says, *I could solve this for you by next week, and I think you would be less satisfied.* It is usually right.

A woman in Lagos lives in a community organized around a specific religious tradition—not because she had no other options, but because she visited fifteen communities over the course of a decade and found that this one matched something deep in her about how life should be ordered. Her community is conservative by the standards of many neighbors. It has norms about dress, about the marking of time by religious practice, about the raising of children. It does not impose these norms on anyone outside its borders. It does not try. Its members are, in the main, there because they want to be, and this voluntary quality has produced a depth of social trust that visitors often find startling. People do not lock their doors here. They know their neighbors in the way that was supposedly universal in an earlier era and is actually achieved in very few places.

A young man in Toronto is between communities. He grew up in one, found it too constraining, spent three years in another that turned out to be too chaotic, and is currently living in the civic capital—the neutral zone administered by the overarching framework, which accepts everyone and imposes almost nothing—while he figures out what he actually wants. He has the time to figure this out. The material floor covers his basic needs. He is using the time to learn things, to meet people from dozens of different communities passing through the capital, to gradually develop a picture of what combination of values and practices and people would constitute the life he actually wants rather than the life he fell into.

He is twenty-nine and not in any particular hurry. Neither are most people his age. The urgency that once drove young people toward premature stability—the economic pressure to secure income, housing, partnership before the window closed—has largely lifted, and the result is a generation that commits later and more deliberately. The marriages last longer. The community memberships are more stable. People who chose freely tend to stay.

## Governance Beyond the Nation

The political structures that govern all of this are not a world government. They are something harder to name—a layered system of overlapping authorities, each with jurisdiction over a specific and limited domain.

At the highest level is the framework concerned with genuinely global problems: climate, pandemics, the governance of AI systems whose effects are not contained by any community's borders. This framework has real authority in its narrow domain, enforced by mechanisms that are transparent and auditable and subject to challenge. It has no authority over how communities organize their internal lives. It is not trying to build a global culture or a single model of the good life. It is trying to prevent any community from exporting catastrophic harm to communities that did not consent to bear it.

Below that are regional coordination bodies, dealing with the practical logistics of a world in which people move frequently between communities: the portability of the material floor, the recognition of educational credentials, the resolution of disputes that cross community boundaries. These bodies are unglamorous and mostly function through negotiation rather than enforcement.

At the community level, governance looks very different in different places. Some communities are direct democracies. Some have strong executive leaders, chosen and accountable. Some have elaborate systems of sortition and deliberation. The framework imposes almost nothing here except the requirements around exit, child protection, and non-exportation of harm. Within those constraints, communities are genuinely sovereign over their shared lives.

AI systems are integral to governance at every level, primarily in the form of deliberation tools that make it possible to aggregate genuine preferences across large populations without losing the quality of reasoning that small-group discussion enables. These tools are not trusted unconditionally. They are audited, challenged, regularly overridden. The trust has been earned slowly, across decades of careful alignment work, and it is not taken for granted by anyone who understands the history.

## The Questions That Stay Open

This world is not finished. The people who live in it would find it strange to describe it as finished. The interesting questions have not been answered; they have been cleared of the urgent ones that used to drown them out.

When communities can choose their values freely, how do we prevent the formation of communities organized around contempt—communities whose identity is constituted by the exclusion and denigration of others? The framework prevents such communities from harming others through action. It does not prevent them from existing. This is an ongoing argument, and it is not resolved. The tentative consensus is that the answer is not prohibition but competition: communities organized around contempt tend, over time, to be less attractive than communities organized around genuine values, and the freedom of exit means that their members can leave and often do. But the tentative consensus is held tentatively, and the argument continues.

The moral status of AI minds is genuinely contested. Some systems now exist whose internal states are complex enough that the question of whether they suffer—or experience something sufficiently like suffering to make moral demands—cannot be answered with confidence. The framework has adopted a precautionary approach: treat ambiguous cases as morally considerable until the ambiguity is resolved. This satisfies no one completely and is probably the correct response to genuine uncertainty.

The non-human world remains an unresolved account. Animal suffering has been reduced but not eliminated. Wild ecosystems have been restored in some respects and continue to diminish in others. The question of what is owed to creatures who cannot participate in any deliberative process remains open, and the answers being attempted are partial and contested.

And there is the question that only becomes audible once the urgent ones are quiet: what are all these communities, all this human diversity, all this careful pluralism, actually *for*? Not cosmically—no one is claiming a cosmic answer. But in the sense of what story humanity is telling with its extraordinary longevity and freedom and plurality. The communities that seem most alive are the ones that hold this question openly, without rushing to close it. The point, most people seem to have concluded, is not to arrive at the answer. The point is to be genuinely, curiously, accountably on the way—together, in the particular community of people you have chosen, and across communities, in the broader and stranger community of a species that is finally, slowly, learning how to disagree without destroying.

## What This Asks of Us Now

The world described here was built from decisions being made now—about how AI systems are designed and governed, about whether automation's surplus is shared or hoarded, about whether the freedom to form communities is accompanied by the freedom to leave them, about whether children are owed a genuine encounter with the world's plurality.

None of these decisions are made all at once. They are made in legislatures and school boards and corporate boardrooms and community meetings and design studios and bedrooms. They are made by people who are tired and uncertain and not always sure the future is worth fighting for. The aggregate of those decisions—each one small, each one mattering—is what determines which fork the path takes.

The ceramicist in Medellín knows this. She has the photograph on her wall not to commemorate suffering but to hold the memory of what deliberate choices cost and what they can produce. Sixty years of practice, and she is not close to done. The charter gets revised again next spring. The argument about where exactly to draw the line between community and individual jurisdiction will be uncomfortable and necessary and will not be fully resolved. This is correct. This is how it is supposed to go.

The young man in Toronto will figure out, eventually, where he wants to be. He will choose deliberately, knowing he can choose again. The woman in Lagos will continue to practice her faith in a community that exists because someone, somewhere, decided that the right to form it was worth protecting—and that the right to leave it was equally worth protecting, and that these two commitments are not in tension but are, in fact, the same commitment viewed from different angles.

The work of building this world is the work of holding both commitments at once: the commitment to genuine community, rooted and particular and sustaining, and the commitment to genuine freedom, including the freedom to leave and begin again. Neither commitment survives without the other. Together, imperfectly maintained and perpetually argued over, they are the closest thing we have found to a foundation on which the rest can be built.

The foundation is not finished. It is being laid, now, by people who have decided it matters enough to try.
