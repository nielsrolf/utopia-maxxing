# The Inheritance of Morning: A World That Learned to Choose

---

There is a question that haunts every utopia: *who gets left out?*

Not as a rhetorical challenge, but as a genuine design problem. Because the history of better worlds — imagined and attempted — is largely a history of people who solved things for themselves and called it universal. The future I want to describe had to solve this too. It did not solve it perfectly. Nothing in this future is perfect. But the people who built it had learned, painfully and specifically, that a flourishing that excluded people was not flourishing but merely a new arrangement of winners and losers. They built the infrastructure accordingly.

Here is what they built, and how, and what it felt like to live inside it.

---

## I. The Ground Problem

Before anything else, there was the energy.

This is where the story has to start, not because energy is the most interesting part — it isn't, by a long distance — but because without it nothing else was possible. You cannot build fairness on scarcity. You cannot afford to think carefully about the long term when the short term is already catastrophic. The moral and institutional reforms that came later were only possible because enough people, in enough places, were freed from the immediate violence of energy poverty.

Solar crossed the meaningful threshold somewhere around 2037. Not the threshold of being cheap — it had been cheap for years — but the threshold of being *abundant in the way that reorganizes what is possible*. The marginal cost of the next kilowatt-hour, in daylight hours across most of the inhabited world, fell below any number worth calculating. The bottleneck shifted to storage and transmission, and into those problems flooded the greatest concentration of capital and applied intelligence in human history, because whoever solved them would shape everything that came after.

They were solved without elegance and without a single breakthrough. The actual solution was a combination of fourth-generation iron-air batteries, rebuilt continental grids across Europe and North America, small modular nuclear reactors providing stable baseload, and a new generation of decentralized microgrids that had been designed, specifically and deliberately, to serve populations that traditional infrastructure had never reached. That last part — the deliberate design for the excluded — had been the hard-won lesson of the previous technology transitions, each of which had extended its benefits to the wealthy first and to the poor only after a generation or two of advocacy, protest, and occasionally organized disruption.

By 2044, energy in the developed world had ceased to be a meaningful line item in household calculations. By 2052, most of the developing world had followed, not because wealthy nations had been generous — generosity had been insufficient — but because the economics of distributed solar had made the old model of centralized fossil infrastructure genuinely non-competitive, and because a coalition of smaller nations had forced through a reformed intellectual property framework for clean energy technology that made the transition faster than any single government had been willing to unilaterally accelerate.

What followed was the part consistently underestimated.

Cheap energy reorganizes the space of what is possible. Desalination plants scaled to genuine abundance along coastlines that had known chronic water stress. Vertical farms appeared in cities as serious food infrastructure. The Sahel greened, slowly and unevenly, through ten thousand projects each made viable by the collapse of power costs. Lab-grown protein achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2047 and fell below it within a decade, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in ways that decades of ethical argument had never quite achieved — and in doing so, freeing several percent of the Earth's land surface from a use it had been put to, land that became the subject of the most interesting political arguments of the mid-century: who decides what it becomes now?

The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s — grimmer than the news cycle had generally conveyed, and which had killed more people than comfortable observers had been willing to know — became by 2060 a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity had become virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation had been dissolved, and what remained required active political malice to manufacture rather than merely passive neglect to permit.

This was not nothing. Measured in human lives and the suffering attached to them, it was perhaps the largest improvement in the human condition since vaccination. It deserves to be named before anything else, because everything else rests on it.

## II. The Land Beneath Our Feet

The second foundation was older in theory and longer delayed in practice.

Land value taxation — taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and effort upon it — had been understood by economists since the nineteenth century and implemented seriously almost nowhere, because the people who owned land also tended to own the institutions that made policy. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a structural observation about how power reproduces itself, and recognizing it did not require cynicism, only attention.

The first serious jurisdiction to implement it fully was a mid-sized Taiwanese city in 2029, under a mayor who had read too much economic history and had the particular political fortune of a city council whose most obstructionist member had just resigned over an unrelated scandal. The results were exactly what the theory predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public coffers filled without anyone feeling confiscated from, because the value being taxed was value that no individual had created. Proximity to transit, to schools, to the accumulated cultural life of a city — this was collective creation, and it was finally being recognized as such.

The reform spread with the irresistible logic of things that actually work. Within fifteen years, a majority of OECD cities had adopted some version of it. The speculative real estate complex — which had for a century pumped value from workers into landholders while contributing nothing, while making housing in the places people most needed to live effectively inaccessible to the people who most needed to live there — was defanged. Not immediately, not without years of political struggle, but definitively enough that the 2050s produced a generation of young people who could afford to live in cities near the work and communities and culture that cities provide.

This mattered for everything else. It mattered particularly for the people it had previously excluded.

Here is a specific consequence that is worth noting: the neighborhoods in many cities that had been, for generations, the designated location of people with the fewest choices — underfunded, underserviced, treated by the broader city as problems to be managed rather than communities to be invested in — began, after the land reform, to receive serious public infrastructure. Not because attitudes changed first. Because the financial incentive structure changed, and the attitudes had to catch up. The land value revenue funded transit, parks, schools. The schools attracted families. The families attracted businesses. The businesses raised land values. The raised land values generated more revenue. The cycle, so long perverse, became virtuous.

This did not eliminate neighborhood inequality. It did begin, slowly and measurably, to reduce it.

## III. The Institutions That Learn

Material abundance solves material problems. The twenty-first century had plenty of those. It also had a deeper problem: humans had developed the capacity to make enormous collective decisions affecting everyone on earth, without developing the wisdom or institutions to make those decisions well. The gap between power and judgment had been widening for a century. Closing it was the other half of the work, and it was the half no one could do for anyone else.

In 2031, a small Portuguese city called Évora, facing a deadlocked council and a crumbling water system, ran an experiment: a public prediction market for infrastructure policy, open to citizens, engineers, economists, and imported experts alike, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. The pipes chosen based on that aggregated intelligence are still running. Within seven years, forty cities had adopted variants of the model. By 2048, the European Union had codified *anticipatory governance* — a requirement that major policy proposals undergo a forecasting audit before implementation, with track records published and updated in real time. Decision-makers who consistently ignored well-calibrated forecasts found their credibility evaporating before opponents could even articulate an attack.

What this produced, slowly, was *legibility*: not the naive transparency of everything being visible, but the deeper legibility of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions checked against outcomes, the people in power held to an epistemically traceable standard. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain.

Alongside this, between 2030 and 2050, a wave of occupational licensing reform swept through most wealthy democracies. The result was something unexpected: when credentials became more about demonstrated competence and less about institutional gatekeeping, learning became more genuinely valued. When you couldn't get a position through credential signaling alone, you had to actually know things. The schools that emerged from this period taught logic and rhetoric and the history of confident error, taught how to disagree without contempt and what it means for evidence to support a conclusion. Children who learned these things in the 2040s were the professionals and politicians of the 2070s, and the difference was not that they were smarter, but that they had better tools for avoiding the specific flavors of stupidity that had cost so much in the preceding decades.

By 2055, thirty-one charter cities had been established around the world, each a deliberate institutional experiment. Some failed — corruption, poor governance design, absorption back into surrounding states — and the failures were documented and studied with the same seriousness as the successes, because the people running these experiments had internalized something important: a world that could not learn from its mistakes was not improving, whatever else it was doing. The survivors had produced a remarkable variety of working models.

One in Iceland had implemented a hybrid governance structure — structured prediction markets for policy outcomes, citizen deliberative assemblies for major value questions, and conditional legislation with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes. The government was small. It was unusually competent. These facts were related.

One in Rwanda had pioneered algorithmic zoning that made construction permits nearly automatic for compliant designs, eliminating years-long delays and cutting housing costs forty percent below comparable regional cities. Its approach to participatory design — requiring genuine community input before algorithm parameters were set, with input weighted to include those who would be most affected by the built environment — became a model replicated across dozens of cities on three continents.

These experiments circulated. Ideas spread from charter cities to traditional cities the way all good ideas spread: through the stubborn persistence of people who had seen something work and would not stop saying so until someone listened.

## IV. The Repair of Minds

Here is something that tends to be left out of accounts of material progress, because it is harder to measure and easier to be sentimental about: the chronic, grinding weight of untreated mental suffering that had shaped a large fraction of all human decisions, for as long as anyone could remember, had been so normalized as to be nearly invisible.

The 2020s produced something like a reckoning. A combination of factors converged: the post-pandemic inventory of human suffering that no one could quite look away from; the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching regulated clinical practice; the accumulation of longitudinal data that finally made psychiatric treatment more empirical and less sophisticated guesswork. By 2045, the treatment gap for major depressive disorder had closed by roughly seventy percent in high-income countries, and work was advancing rapidly to close it everywhere. The burden lifted — not all of it, not for everyone, but enough to notice in the decisions people made.

People who are not in chronic pain make different choices than people who are. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more able to imagine futures different from their present. They are also, and this matters more than it might seem, *more willing to extend similar imagination to other people* — to recognize the interior weight of lives unlike their own. The slow repair of millions of minds produced, as a side effect, something that looked like a modest improvement in collective moral imagination, not because people became smarter, but because they became less desperate, and desperation narrows the aperture through which we see.

There was also the more specific repair: the long, slow work of building mental health infrastructure in places that had never had it, of training practitioners in contexts where the Western clinical model required significant adaptation, of funding community-based approaches that had been proven effective for decades but had never been taken seriously because serious money had never followed. This work was less glamorous than the psychedelic revolution. It was, measured in suffering prevented, more consequential.

## V. What a Tuesday Looks Like

Let me be specific. Specificity is a form of respect.

Keita is forty-one years old. He lives in Dakar, which in 2068 is one of the ten most livable cities on earth by several different measures, a fact that would have seemed improbable to anyone who knew Dakar in 2020 and did not understand what cheap energy and land value reform and serious public investment would do to a city with Dakar's particular combination of culture, geography, and organizational density.

He wakes in an apartment he owns — owns in a specific sense that would require explanation to both his grandfather, who never expected to own anything, and to a housing economist from 2020, who would be startled by the financing mechanism: a community land trust, in which the trust owns the land beneath the building and residents own the structure, a model that had been tested in scattered cities for decades and had spread dramatically once the land value reform changed the underlying economics. His apartment is net energy positive on most days.

Keita does work that did not exist under that name before 2035: he is what his neighborhood calls a *connective*, one of several hundred people in Dakar whose formally recognized role is to help the people around them access the things that exist but are hard to find — services, expertise, opportunities, each other. It is a role that grew organically from the community organizing tradition, was formalized when a municipal experiment showed that paying for it produced measurable improvements in neighborhood cohesion and individual wellbeing, and has since been adopted in hundreds of cities with local variations. He is paid partly by the municipality, partly by a cooperative that pools contributions from the families he works with, and partly by a small UBI-adjacent dividend that every adult in Senegal receives from a fund built on energy export revenues and land value taxes.

He is not anxious about money in the way that was ordinary for people in his economic position two generations earlier. This requires acknowledging the political work that made it true: years of organizing, of strikes, of arguments in municipal buildings and international forums, of people who understood that the fruits of technological progress would not distribute themselves, that the distance between *possible* and *actual* was filled with human choices, and that those choices could be shaped.

His mornings he spends in the neighborhood: at the community hub that replaced a derelict commercial block in 2048, where people come to argue and learn and use tools and eat. His afternoons he protects jealously. He is learning to sculpt, badly, and he expects to be bad at it for years. He does not mind. The world he lives in has, among its other features, made peace with the long cultivation of human capacities that will never be monetized. There is time.

His son, sixteen, attends a school whose curriculum was redesigned in the 2040s to center on genuine understanding, which sounds like it should have always been the point of school and in some ways was, but the reorganization required removing things that had accumulated for reasons no longer operative — credential signaling, the preparation for tests that certified the preparation for more tests — and it required doing so against the resistance of everyone who had built their identity around the old system. The resistance was real. So were the results. His son is learning to argue carefully, which is different from learning to win arguments, and the difference matters.

## VI. What Was Kept

There were people, during the transitions of the 2030s and 2040s, who believed that the path to a good future required treating the accumulated past as raw material to be discarded or consumed. They were right that the past contained enormous error and oppression. They were wrong about what followed from that.

What was kept, because it was worth keeping: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together across the sizes of human community; the art forms that had learned through long practice to hold grief and joy simultaneously; the philosophical and religious traditions that had developed tools — imperfect but proven more durable than improvised alternatives — for reasoning about what made a life worth living; the particular textures of particular places, the languages that encoded ways of relating to land and time that no other language encoded, the specific ritual forms through which specific communities had, across generations, learned to hold each other through loss.

None of these were preserved in amber. They were brought into genuine conversation with new circumstances and new knowledge, which is how things that are alive behave. The world of 2068 argues about the past constantly. This is a sign of health. A civilization that has forgotten what humans are capable of at their worst cannot protect itself from the worst.

What was not kept: the structural arrangements by which some people's suffering was treated as the natural order; the mechanisms by which inherited advantage compounded across generations; the specific form of loneliness that industrial society had perfected — the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves, achieved through the curious combination of unprecedented connectivity and radical atomization.

The children of 2068 roam. This requires explanation to anyone who remembers the early years of the century. The built environment of most cities had been redesigned, over two generations, to treat children's presence in public space as normal and desirable. This redesign was enabled by the land value reform — when land speculation collapses, cities can afford to think about what streets are for — and by a gradual shift in how risk was understood: the risk of childhood isolation and disconnection eventually becoming legible as the more serious danger. The streets of Dakar on a Tuesday afternoon are full of children doing something, going somewhere, being met by adults who know their names. This is not nostalgic. It is the specific, concrete result of specific, concrete decisions about zoning and traffic and public space and social investment.

## VII. The Work That Remains

It would be dishonest to stop without saying this: the world of 2068 is not finished. It is not an endpoint. It is a position along an arc, and the people living in it know this, mostly, most of the time.

There are still places where the transitions have not reached, where the combination of political dysfunction and geographic marginalization has kept poverty and preventable illness in place beyond what the broader world's abundance should permit. There are still arguments — genuine, unresolved arguments — about what the land freed from industrial agriculture should become, about who gets to make that decision, about what the legitimate inheritance of historical wrongdoing looks like in practice when the wrongdoing spans centuries and the ledgers cannot be balanced. There are still people who are lonely and people who are suffering and people who feel that the world's progress has passed them by, and these people are not statistics, they are people, and the civilization of 2068 has not lost the ability to be troubled by this.

What is different is that these failures are legible as failures, rather than as natural conditions. When preventable suffering persists, it persists against a background of systems capable of addressing it, which makes its persistence a choice — not always a conscious choice, not always an evil choice, but a choice that can be interrogated, that can be changed. The structures that once made injustice feel inevitable had been, slowly and specifically, dismantled. What remained was what had always remained: the ongoing human work of deciding to do better, which requires both the capacity and the will, and for which the capacity, at last, existed.

The sociologist Amara, in a 2073 paper asking when it changed, concluded that there was no hinge. No single morning. Only the sediment, grain by grain, the work of people who held two things simultaneously: clear-eyed recognition of how much was wrong, and genuine faith in how much could be right. The cynics saw clearly and did nothing. The vaguely hopeful hoped vaguely and were blindsided. The people who built this world saw clearly and believed anyway, and the believing was not in spite of the seeing but because of it: they understood what was actually at stake.

What had changed, in the end, was not human nature, which remained generous and petty and creative and frightened and funny and cruel in roughly the proportions it had always been. What changed was the infrastructure beneath human nature — the systems that either amplify the pettiness and cruelty, or the generosity and creativity. Those systems were improved, slowly and imperfectly, without sufficient credit going to anyone in particular, in ways that no single theory had predicted and no single movement had accomplished. The humans inside them remained recognizably themselves. They had a little more room, a little more of the time, to be the better versions.

That was the whole of it.

## VIII. The Morning That Is Still Early

Keita walks home from the community hub in the early evening. The light in Dakar at this hour is the specific gold of late afternoon in West Africa, and it falls on streets that were redesigned by people who argued about what streets should be for, who changed their minds, who made mistakes and came back to correct them, across twenty years of contested municipal planning processes and one large community referendum that nearly undid all of it before a coalition of residents — old and new, those with long roots and those who had arrived in the last decade — held together and voted for the harder, better thing.

He stops to talk with a neighbor. The conversation is about something ordinary — a child's school project, a community garden that needs more volunteers on Saturdays. It lasts twelve minutes. It will be forgotten by both of them within a week. It is, nonetheless, the specific texture of what flourishing actually feels like: not transcendence, not the sublime, not the resolution of the great questions, but the ordinary miracle of two people who know each other, standing in good light, in a city that was built by actual people who cared what it became.

The city was built by actual people.

So was the world.

The morning is long. It is still early. The work — the specific, unglamorous, arguable, correctable, never-finished work — is yours.

---

*The path from here to there runs through decades of patient labor: on energy and its distribution, on land and its ownership, on institutions and their reform, on medicine and education, on the slow transformation of what communities expect from themselves and each other and the people they have historically excluded. It runs through failures and recoveries and the stubborn insistence, against considerable evidence at various moments, that the failures are not the final word. It requires, at every step, enough people who understand that the size of what could be lost is exactly equal to the size of what could be gained, and that this is very large — larger than ordinary categories comfortably hold — and that the right response to this enormity is neither paralysis nor rashness but the particular kind of serious, long-horizon, error-correcting, other-regarding work that the best human institutions have always embodied and that more human institutions, in this world, have finally learned to do.*

*The shore is real. The birds are not lying.*

*And the ocean, when enough people reach it together, turns out to be nothing like a scaled-up lake.*
