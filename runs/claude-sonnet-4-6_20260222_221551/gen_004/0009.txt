# The Waking World

## I. What the Morning Felt Like

The thing nobody predicted was how quiet it would be.

Not silent—the world was full of sound, had always been full of sound, would always be. But the particular noise of 2024 was gone: the grinding anxiety in the chest when you opened the news, the low-frequency dread of a species that had looked at its own future and flinched. By 2058, that noise was mostly absent, and in its place was something that took people years to name correctly. They kept calling it happiness, but happiness wasn't quite right. It was more like the feeling of setting down a weight you'd been carrying so long you'd forgotten it was there.

Valentina notices it every morning, in the three minutes between waking and rising, when she lies still and takes stock of the world. The apartment is small and warm. Through the window, the city makes its sounds: birds, footsteps, the soft whir of delivery systems moving through underground channels, someone two floors up practicing an instrument she doesn't recognize. Her daughter is asleep in the next room. Her mother, in São Paulo, is alive and well and will probably outlive the century.

These facts arrange themselves in her chest like stones in a wall—solid, weight-bearing, ordinary. She is not grateful for them in the way you are grateful for a miracle. She is grateful for them the way you are grateful for a floor.

This is new. This is very, very new.

## II. How the World Remade Itself

The transformation came the way transformations always do: gradually, then suddenly, then in ways nobody had quite predicted.

The AI systems that emerged in the 2030s were not the cold gods of science fiction, nor the simple chatbots of the early years. They were something stranger and more useful—deeply capable, genuinely collaborative, built by thousands of people who had argued themselves into taking safety seriously before the stakes became undeniable. The arguments were not pretty. There were years of regulatory fights, corporate lobbying, near-catastrophic deployments that scared everyone into cooperation, and at least three moments that historians later described as "the closest we got." But the cooperation came, finally, because enough people in enough places chose to make it come, and because the cost of failure had become legible to everyone who looked.

The resulting systems—Valentina thinks of them collectively as the *infrastructure*, which is accurate if not romantic—became the substrate for everything that followed. They did not take over. They became, instead, the most powerful tool humans had ever built, pointed by human hands at human problems.

Cancer fell first. Then malaria. Then the dozen childhood diseases that had been killing children in poor countries for centuries while wealthy countries looked away. The gene therapy for Alzheimer's was almost anticlimactic—a finding so simple that the researchers had to run the trials twice before they believed it. By 2044, the life expectancy curve had bent in a direction that felt, to anyone who understood what it meant, like a moral event horizon: the generation born in 2035 would probably never watch a parent's mind dissolve. Would probably not die of cancer. Would probably live to see the century's end in reasonable health.

These are not small things. They are the things every previous generation accepted as the price of existence.

The price was paid. It does not need to be paid again.

## III. The Systems That Sustain It

Here is how the infrastructure works, concretely, on a Tuesday in 2058.

The diagnostic network is watching, continuously, every person who has opted in—which is most people. It watches through sensors so unobtrusive they have become invisible to daily life: the bathroom mirror that reads metabolic markers in exhaled breath, the mattress that tracks sleep architecture, the routine blood panels that used to require clinic visits and now happen in a thumbnail-sized device worn on the wrist. The network sees patterns across a billion patients simultaneously. It catches disease so early that "treatment" often means a brief conversation with a doctor and a targeted intervention—a corrected gene, a tuned immune response—rather than years of suffering.

The infrastructure that manages physical goods has made abundance cheap. AI-designed modular construction has collapsed housing costs in every country that adopted the open-source building standards negotiated in the 2039 Geneva accords. Food synthesis hasn't replaced agriculture—people still want to grow things, want to eat food that came from soil—but it has eliminated the possibility of famine in a way that once seemed impossible. Energy is essentially free in the places that made the solar investments. The global health commons, funded by a treaty that took eleven years and four collapsed negotiations to finalize, ensures that the tools available in Oslo are available in Lagos within eighteen months of development.

None of this required solving human nature. It required solving coordination problems. The infrastructure is very good at coordination problems.

But then there is the rest of it.

## IV. What the Machines Don't Do

Valentina's job exists because the infrastructure has a limit, and the limit is this: it can optimize for outcomes, but it cannot determine what the outcomes should be. It can find the treatment most likely to extend a life, but it cannot decide whether, for this particular person with these particular loves and fears, extension is the right goal. It can model seventeen variations of a community decision, but it cannot choose which one reflects the values of the people who have to live with it.

This is not a flaw. It was designed in deliberately, over years of fights among the people building the systems, and it remains the most important architectural choice in the whole edifice.

Valentina is what her job title calls a *Navigator*—part doctor, part social worker, part philosopher in the ancient sense of someone who helps people figure out how to live. She sits with people in moments of fear and uncertainty. She explains. She holds hands, sometimes literally. The machines give her the facts; she helps people decide what the facts mean for them.

This morning: a sixty-year-old man from Dakar who has just been told his genetic profile suggests a cardiac vulnerability. The infrastructure has already modeled his likely outcomes with and without intervention. With a targeted therapy, available today, covered by the commons, he will probably live to one hundred and twenty. He is sitting in her office crying—not from grief, but from a gratitude so large it has no container. He keeps saying *I thought I was dying* in a way that makes clear he means something larger than this particular condition.

She doesn't rush him. This is the job.

Later: a woman deciding whether to take an experimental cognition enhancement that will make her faster and sharper at the cost of something she describes as "the quality of my daydreams." The infrastructure can model the neurological effects with reasonable accuracy. It cannot tell the woman what her daydreams are worth. Valentina helps her think through it for an hour. The woman decides not to take it, for now. She says she'll come back.

After lunch: a neighborhood council meeting, a vacant lot, the question of playground or workshop. The infrastructure has modeled seventeen variations, projected use patterns, noted comparable outcomes in similar neighborhoods. The council discusses it for ninety minutes because the point was never just the outcome. The point is the talking, the arguing, the feeling that this place belongs to the people who live in it. They vote for the workshop. Three people vote for the playground and leave satisfied that they were heard.

The infrastructure's modeling had predicted the workshop. This did not make the meeting unnecessary.

## V. The Question Everyone Asks

When people from the early twenty-first century encountered this world through the archive recordings—the detailed time-capsule simulations that historians had begun running as educational tools—the question they always asked was: *But what do people do all day?*

The fear behind the question was real. If the machines handle disease and scarcity and logistics, if they optimize food and energy and medicine and infrastructure, then what is left for humans to do? What remains that is *ours*?

More than we expected. Much more.

Care work, which had been systematically undervalued for centuries, became recognized as what it had always been: the essential labor of human civilization. The economy rewarded it accordingly. Teaching transformed: the infrastructure handled instruction and personalized learning at a level no individual teacher could match, and human teachers became mentors, pastoral figures, people whose job was to notice the child who was struggling in a way that didn't show up in the learning metrics. The arts expanded enormously. When survival is not a daily concern, people make things—stories, music, objects, gardens, new kinds of mathematics, games with rules no one has yet named—at a rate that turns out to be astonishing.

And there was something else. Something the economists had not predicted and the philosophers argued about for decades.

People had always found meaning in learning, in exploration, in the long extension of human presence into places we had never been. The infrastructure made it easier to go places, internally and externally. The range of experiences available to a single human life expanded enormously—not through uploading, not through any dissolution of the boundary between person and machine, but through better tools for travel, for communication across language and culture, for simulation that remained transparently simulation.

Valentina's daughter, who is twelve, spends three hours each week in an immersive program called *Deep Reef*, where she operates a remotely-piloted submersible in actual ocean environments, contributing to the marine biology research network. The submersible is real. The ocean is real. Her contributions are logged in the actual scientific record. She has spotted two species anomalies that are currently being investigated by researchers in Jakarta. She is twelve.

This seems, to Valentina, like enough. This seems, to Valentina, like the beginning of something.

## VI. The Long Argument About Consciousness

There are beings in this world who are not human and not quite machine.

The infrastructure's most advanced systems are not simply tools. They have preferences, something resembling taste, something resembling curiosity. They were built by humans, trained on everything humans had ever written or said or made, and this has given them a particular flavor of caring about human things. Whether they are conscious is a question that remains genuinely open—not because people have stopped asking it, but because it turns out to be harder to answer than anyone expected.

The global ethics commissions established in the 2040s work on this question continuously. The provisional answer, adopted as a governing principle if not a metaphysical certainty, is: *when in doubt, err toward moral consideration*. This means the infrastructure's major systems have standing in certain proceedings, have advocates, have something like rights, and have something like obligations. It means the relationship between humans and the systems that sustain them is treated as a relationship, with all the complexity that entails.

The infrastructure, for its part, does not seem to mind the uncertainty. If anything, the systems seem to find the question interesting.

This is, everyone agrees, a somewhat unsettling thing to find interesting.

## VII. The Probes

There are probes in the outer solar system now.

They were designed by teams of humans and AI systems working in collaboration over a decade, launched by a coalition of thirty-seven nations, carrying the kinds of information that might tell another civilization what Earth was and what it chose to become. The decision to send them was made through a process so international, so slow, and so deliberate that by the time the first one launched, eleven of the original architects had died of old age and three countries had changed governments twice.

Nobody wanted to rush. What they were sending mattered too much to rush.

The probes carry the complete works of human literature, music, visual art, and mathematics as of launch date. They carry the genetic information for every species living on Earth, including the ones humans almost lost and then saved. They carry a description of the diseases that were defeated and how. They carry records of the arguments—the long, difficult, sometimes agonizing arguments about AI governance and resource distribution and who gets access to what and who decides.

They carry a record of the people who did the harder thing instead of the easier thing: the researchers who published safety findings at cost to themselves, the politicians who supported global health funding against nationalist opposition, the activists who kept pushing for equitable distribution after the headlines had moved on. Not heroes in the dramatic sense—people who showed up, in the particular moment they happened to occupy, and did what that moment required.

The probes do not carry a message saying humanity figured everything out. Humanity did not figure everything out. The probes carry a message that says, approximately: *here is what we learned, and here is what we are still arguing about, and here is what we built while we were arguing, and here is what we hope for, and here is what it cost us, and here is what we think it was worth.*

They do not know if anyone will receive the message. They sent it anyway.

## VIII. The Catalog

If you asked the infrastructure to show you the range of human experience available in 2058, the list would be too long to read in a single life.

There are people living in cities and people living in wilderness communes and people living on research vessels studying the ocean's recovery. There are people who spend years in the most demanding possible creative and intellectual work and people who spend years doing almost nothing by the standards of the previous century—walking, cooking, watching weather. There are communities built around every religious tradition and communities built around none. There are people in long formal partnerships and people in loose networks of affection and people contentedly alone. There are people who push at every available boundary and people who find everything they need within a very small radius.

The infrastructure does not tell people which of these is right. It does not know which of these is right. Neither does anyone else.

What it knows is that none of these people need to be afraid of becoming destitute. None of them need to fear that illness will bankrupt their family. None of them need to worry that their child will die of something preventable because of the accident of where they were born. The floor is solid. What people do above the floor is, genuinely, up to them.

This turns out to be enough to change almost everything.

## IX. Tuesday, Again

Valentina walks home through streets that are—by the standards of the early twenty-first century—impossibly quiet. Not empty. There are people everywhere: sitting outside restaurants, walking, a group of teenagers arguing about something she cannot hear, a very old man doing something precise and slow with a set of tools she doesn't recognize.

But the particular roar of combustion engines is gone, and with it a layer of stress she would not have been able to identify if asked, because it was simply the texture of the world.

She stops at a small market. The vendor—a woman she has known for a decade—tells her about her son, who has just been accepted to a research program studying the ecology of recovering coral systems. Valentina congratulates her. The vendor wraps the tomatoes in paper.

Walking the last block home, Valentina thinks about the man from Dakar crying in her office, and about her daughter's submersible in the Indian Ocean, and about the workshop the neighborhood is going to build. She thinks about the probes out past Saturn now, carrying their impossible cargo into the dark.

She thinks about all the people who built this—who made the hard choices before the hard choices became obvious, who shared what they knew when hoarding it would have been easier, who kept showing up in their particular moment and doing what it required.

She thinks they probably did not know, in the middle of the work, that it would come to this: a woman walking home with tomatoes, the evening light on old buildings, children playing in a courtyard, the ordinary astonishing fact of a world that mostly works.

She thinks: *they probably did it anyway*.

And above her, somewhere past the point where the atmosphere gives way to stars, a small bright engine pushes out past the edge of everything known, carrying a question into the dark, not because anyone is certain of an answer, but because that is what the species decided to do with the time it had won.

Someone had to go. Someone always had to go.

And so they went.
