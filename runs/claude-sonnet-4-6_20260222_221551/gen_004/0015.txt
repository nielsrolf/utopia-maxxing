# The Long Acceleration: A Vision of the World We Could Build

---

There is a certain kind of progress that announces itself with fanfare—the moon landing, the first nuclear reactor going critical, the sequencing of the human genome. And then there is the other kind, the kind that matters more: the slow disappearance of things that used to hurt. The child who does not get malaria. The woman who does not lose a decade to untreated depression. The city where a young teacher can afford to live near the school where she works. These victories have no ceremony. They accumulate like morning light, and one day you realize the darkness is gone.

What follows is an attempt to describe a world, perhaps forty years from now, in which both kinds of progress have happened—the announced and the unannounced, the dramatic and the sedimentary. A world that got the most powerful tools in human history and, imperfectly but genuinely, used them well.

---

## I. How the Tools Arrived

The AI systems that became capable of genuinely transforming human knowledge did not arrive as a thunderclap. They arrived more the way electricity arrived—suddenly present everywhere, then slowly understood.

Between 2027 and 2034, AI systems crossed several thresholds that mattered less individually than in combination. They became capable of sustained, autonomous scientific work: designing experiments, interpreting results, revising hypotheses, directing laboratory equipment, and—crucially—knowing when they were wrong. They became capable of operating not as oracles you consulted but as colleagues who worked alongside you across weeks and months, building shared context, asking clarifying questions, flagging their own uncertainty. And they became cheap enough that the constraint on their use was no longer cost but imagination.

What happened next was not a singularity. The physical world still moved at the speed of cells dividing and chemicals reacting and regulatory bodies deliberating. What happened was more like a massive parallel acceleration—thousands of research programs that had been bottlenecked on human attention and human hours suddenly had access to something that could think carefully about their problem at any time of day, without ego, without fatigue, and with recall of everything relevant that had ever been written.

The results came in at different speeds in different fields, for the reasons that anyone who had thought carefully about it could have predicted: where the bottleneck was intelligence, progress was rapid; where the bottleneck was physical experimentation or human trial timelines, it was slower; where the bottleneck was political will, it was slowest of all. The story of the next several decades is largely a story of working through these bottlenecks in sequence.

## II. The Biological Revolution, Made Real

By 2045, a child born in any country with functioning public health infrastructure would receive, in their first year of life, interventions that would have seemed miraculous to a physician practicing twenty years earlier. Broad-spectrum antiviral compounds that could be rapidly adapted to novel pathogens. Personalized genetic screening that identified, with high confidence, the disease risks most worth monitoring. And an mRNA platform flexible enough that a vaccine for any new infectious threat could be designed in days and manufactured in weeks.

The infectious disease burden that had haunted humanity since before recorded history did not disappear overnight. It shrank, year by year, in a way that was easy to miss in the moment and stunning in retrospect. The diseases that killed the most children—malaria, pneumonia, diarrheal illness—fell first, because the interventions were well understood and the bottleneck was always delivery rather than discovery, and AI-assisted logistics and community health programs finally cracked the delivery problem. By 2050, childhood mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa had fallen to levels that had taken Europe a century to achieve, and it had done so in fifteen years.

The harder problems took longer. Cancer was not eliminated but it was, by 2060, largely managed: for most common cancers, the question was no longer whether treatment would work but which of several effective treatments best matched the patient's particular tumor, and AI diagnostic systems answered that question with a precision that no individual oncologist could match. The rare and difficult malignancies that resisted this approach—the ones where cancer's adaptive intelligence seemed to match the intelligence brought to bear against it—remained. They became the frontier.

Alzheimer's, which had resisted understanding for decades, finally yielded to a combination of new measurement techniques and computational models that could track the disease's progression at a cellular level. The finding, when it came, was less dramatic than many had hoped and more actionable than many had feared: the disease was preventable, in most cases, through interventions best begun decades before symptoms appeared. The generation that benefited from this knowledge in time is now approaching seventy without the cognitive decline their parents had dreaded.

The advances in mental health were, if anything, more significant in their effect on human experience. Depression, anxiety, PTSD, addiction—these are not one thing but many, and they were not solved by one thing but many: new pharmacological tools that targeted specific neural circuits without the blunt side effects of earlier generations, psychedelic-assisted therapies that finally moved from fringe to mainstream as regulatory frameworks caught up with the evidence, AI-assisted psychological support that could provide consistent and personalized care at a scale no human workforce could match. The combined effect was not the elimination of psychological suffering—grief and loss and loneliness are not diseases, and no one was trying to cure them—but the elimination of the particular suffering that comes from treatable illness going untreated for years or decades because effective care was unavailable or unaffordable.

The person who benefits most from all of this is not dramatic. She is a forty-year-old woman in Lagos who spent her twenties cycling through depression that no one around her had the framework to identify or treat. She gets a diagnosis at thirty-two from an AI health assistant available on her phone. She gets treatment. She gets fifteen years back. She is not famous. There are two hundred million people like her.

## III. The Infrastructure Beneath Human Nature

While the biological revolution was happening in laboratories and clinics, a different kind of transformation was happening in city halls and legislative chambers and the small unglamorous rooms where policy is made—slower, messier, but in some ways more fundamental.

The governance reforms of the 2030s and 2040s had their roots in a simple insight that had been obvious to economists and epistemologists for decades but had been almost entirely absent from actual governance: you can tell whether a decision was good before you see the outcome, if you can assess the quality of the reasoning and the calibration of the predictions that led to it. And if you can tell, you can hold people accountable for the quality of their reasoning rather than only for outcomes—which changes everything, because outcomes are often determined by luck, while reasoning quality is actually within the decision-maker's control.

The spread of anticipatory governance—policy frameworks that required explicit forecasts, tracked prediction accuracy, and built feedback loops between stated expectations and actual results—happened first in cities and then in nations, first in places with strong technocratic traditions and then, more slowly, elsewhere. It did not eliminate bad policy. It made bad policy harder to sustain and easier to reverse. It created, over time, an institutional culture in which the question *what would change your mind about this?* was not a rhetorical attack but a genuine planning tool.

Alongside this, the economic reforms that had seemed politically impossible for most of the twentieth century finally found their moment. Land value taxation spread city by city, driven partly by the evidence that accumulated from early adopters and partly by the housing crises that had become politically untenable in most wealthy democracies by the late 2020s. The reform was not ideologically coherent—it was adopted by left-wing governments as a way to fund social programs and by right-wing governments as a way to eliminate distortionary taxes on income and production—but its effects were consistent: speculation declined, construction increased, cities became more affordable, and the fiscal space that opened up was used for other things. A generation of young people who had been locked out of ownership by speculative land markets found themselves, by 2050, in cities they could actually afford to live in.

The energy transition, which had been technically feasible for longer than it was politically achievable, finally completed itself through the straightforward mechanism of becoming the cheapest option. Solar and wind crossed their cost thresholds. Storage followed. By 2042, building a new fossil fuel plant was an economically irrational decision in almost all markets, without any subsidy or carbon price needed to produce that outcome. The transition still required political work to manage the communities and economies built around fossil fuels—this was not handled perfectly anywhere, and in some places it was handled very badly—but the direction was no longer in doubt.

What cheap, abundant energy did to the physical world was significant. Desalination made water security achievable in regions where it had always been precarious. Vertical agriculture made food production feasible in places where climate or geography had made it difficult. The energy cost that had made many kinds of remediation and restoration prohibitively expensive—cleaning contaminated sites, restoring degraded ecosystems, recycling complex materials—became manageable. None of these things happened automatically. They required the combination of cheap energy with the deployment of AI-assisted systems capable of optimizing complex logistics at a scale that would have been impractical without them. But they happened.

## IV. What a Morning Looks Like

In 2065, in a mid-sized city in Colombia—not Bogotá or Medellín, a smaller place, one of the hundreds of cities around the world that have been transformed without becoming famous—a man named Tomás wakes early.

He is sixty-one years old. He spent his thirties and forties as a civil engineer, working on the water systems that expanded dramatically during Colombia's growth period of the 2030s and 2040s—the growth that came partly from AI-assisted economic development, partly from the energy transition making industrial production vastly cheaper, and partly from the health improvements that, quite simply, gave people more productive years and fewer years spent sick. He took early retirement at fifty-five, which in his context means he stopped taking employment income, not that he stopped working. He is currently involved in three things: a community composting collective that he helped found; a mentorship program matching retired engineers with young people considering the field; and a personal project involving the ecological restoration of a stretch of river that runs through the city's industrial district.

The river project is the one he loves most. Twenty years ago, it was biologically dead—a channel for effluent, tolerated because the cost of cleanup was prohibitive and the regulatory pressure to clean it was insufficient. Now the cost is no longer prohibitive, the regulatory frameworks are more functional, and Tomás has access to AI-assisted environmental modeling that tells him which interventions are likely to work and in what sequence, learning from hundreds of similar restoration projects around the world. It is hard, slow, satisfying work. He expects to see the first native fish populations established before he is seventy. He believes he will be alive to see them.

His expectation that he will live past seventy is not complacent—he has had health scares—but it is reasonable. The monitoring systems he uses, available to everyone with a phone through the public health infrastructure, caught a cardiac irregularity at fifty-eight that would likely have killed him within a decade if untreated. It was treated. He feels fine.

His daughter, who is thirty-three, works in bioengineering. She is not trying to cure a disease. She is working on something that would have seemed impossibly ambitious in 2025: a suite of engineered soil microorganisms capable of dramatically increasing the carbon sequestration capacity of agricultural land while improving rather than harming crop yields. The project has been running for four years. It may not work. The trying is serious.

His son, who is twenty-eight, works as what would have been called a social worker two generations ago and is now something more like a community health navigator: the combination of better mental health tools and better diagnostic systems has meant that a skilled human working in this field is now enormously leveraged—capable of connecting people with effective interventions rather than inadequate ones, tracking outcomes rather than just inputs, doing work that is recognizably meaningful rather than bureaucratically exhausting. He is paid reasonably, not lavishly, and he finds his work so engaging that the question of lavishness does not much arise.

Tomás thinks, sometimes, about what his father's life looked like. His father spent thirty years in the same factory, doing work that was largely replaced by automation in the 2030s. The transition was hard for his father's generation—genuinely hard, not the polite difficulty of inconvenience but the real difficulty of meaning and identity disrupted. The political response that made the transition survivable rather than catastrophic—shorter working weeks, a basic dividend funded by productivity gains, a massive expansion of publicly supported work in caring and community and culture—was slow in coming and inadequate when it came. But it came. And what it produced, two generations on, is a world in which the question of how to spend a life has genuinely opened up in ways that Tomás's father could not have imagined.

## V. The Epistemological Revolution Nobody Named

The change that is hardest to describe, because it happened most slowly and most invisibly, was the change in how people reasoned about uncertain things.

It started in schools. In the 2030s and 2040s, driven partly by curriculum reformers who had been making the same arguments for decades and partly by the practical reality that AI systems were available to help children learn almost any factual content they needed, schools in many countries began shifting toward something that might be called epistemological education: not just what to think, but how to think about thinking. How to assess evidence. How to reason about probabilities. How to hold a position firmly enough to act on it while remaining genuinely open to revision. How to disagree in ways that were productive rather than merely performative.

The students who received this education are now the people running institutions, making policies, running businesses, raising the next generation. They are not uniformly wise—the curriculum was imperfectly designed and imperfectly taught, and human beings remain the cognitively biased creatures they have always been. But the baseline shifted. The institutions they run have absorbed, somewhat, the habits of mind they were trained in.

The combination of better epistemic norms with better tools for collective sense-making—prediction markets that surfaced calibrated uncertainty, deliberative processes that structured disagreement productively, AI systems that could synthesize large bodies of evidence into something a decision-maker could actually use—produced, by the 2060s, governance that was recognizably more competent than what had preceded it. Not wise. Not uncorrupted. But more often right, more often self-correcting, more often capable of doing what it said it would do.

The legal systems of most democracies had been reformed to make use of AI-assisted analysis in ways that improved consistency without eliminating human judgment. The systems were not allowed to make final decisions—there were good reasons for that constraint, vigorously debated and ultimately upheld—but they were allowed to flag inconsistencies, surface relevant precedent, and check proposed decisions against stated legal principles in ways that made bias harder to sustain invisibly. The reforms were imperfect and contested. They were better than what they replaced.

## VI. The Global Question

The hardest thing, and the most important thing, was what happened between the wealthy world and the rest of it.

The honest version of this story is not a triumph. The technologies that improved life so dramatically in wealthy countries did not distribute themselves automatically. The AI-assisted health systems were designed for contexts with functioning digital infrastructure. The governance reforms required institutional capacity to implement. The energy transition required capital for initial investment. All of the things that make the wealthy world wealthy continued, for a while, to advantage the wealthy world in accessing the tools that were supposed to level the playing field.

What prevented this from becoming catastrophic was a combination of factors, none of which was sufficient alone. International institutions that had been rebuilt in the 2030s with more effective enforcement mechanisms directed resources toward technology transfer with more seriousness than their predecessors had managed. AI systems capable of adapting interventions to local contexts—rather than simply transplanting approaches designed for wealthy countries—made health and agricultural programs dramatically more effective where they were deployed. The leapfrogging that had been promised for earlier technologies—cell phones, solar panels—actually materialized for AI-assisted health care, in part because the marginal cost of deploying AI systems, once built, was genuinely low.

The result was uneven and imperfect but real. By 2060, childhood mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa had reached levels comparable to wealthy countries in 1990—not parity, but a reduction in the gap that would have seemed impossible twenty years earlier. Life expectancy in the lowest-income countries had risen faster in the preceding thirty years than in any comparable period in history. The chronic diseases that had been killing adults in their productive years at rates that stunted economic development—malaria, tuberculosis, HIV—had been, if not eliminated, controlled to the point where they were no longer primary drivers of poverty.

Economic development is harder than health. The AI systems that were good at directing laboratory research were less clearly good at solving problems whose bottleneck was political rather than technical—corruption, institutional weakness, the specific challenges of development in an era when automation made the traditional path of export-led manufacturing less available. The honest accounting is that the economic development story is still being written, that the dream scenario of rapid catch-up to wealthy world levels has not fully materialized, and that the gap, while narrowing, remains large.

But the direction is unambiguous. And the moral pressure—from citizens in wealthy countries who had come to understand their obligations in a cleaner way, from civil society organizations that had become more effective over decades of iteration, from the sheer visibility of avoidable suffering in a world with much better information—kept that pressure applied. The work continues.

## VII. What Was Not Lost

There was a worry, in the early years of the acceleration, that the world being built would be sterile: optimized, efficient, and drained of the particular textures that make human life feel like something rather than nothing. That the diversity of cultures and languages and ways of life would be flattened by the same forces that were flattening disease burden and material poverty.

This fear was not entirely wrong, but it was more wrong than right.

What the fear had missed was that the goods it was worried about losing—particular places, particular practices, particular ways of making meaning—are not fragile by nature. They are fragile when the people who carry them are too exhausted or too poor to sustain them, when economic pressure forces homogenization, when communities are too stressed to invest in their own continuity. Remove those pressures—give people more time, more economic security, more genuine choice about how to spend their attention—and it turns out that they mostly choose to preserve and cultivate the things that make their lives distinctive.

The Norwegian woman learning Norse woodcarving, the Colombian man restoring his local river, the communities around the world that have used the space created by material improvement to invest more deeply in their particular ways of life: these are not exceptions. They are what humans do when they have the room to do it.

The languages that were lost, were lost before the acceleration, or despite it. The ones that survived were sustained by communities who now had more resources to sustain them. Net, the diversity of the world is not obviously less than it was—and in some dimensions, in the local food cultures and craft traditions and regional musical forms that the twentieth century's mass culture had nearly homogenized into extinction, it is more.

## VIII. The Open Horizon

In 2075, the question that preoccupies serious people is not the question that preoccupied serious people in 2025.

In 2025, the question was: *can we get through this?* Can we navigate the risks—the ecological, the technological, the geopolitical—without a catastrophe that forecloses the possibilities of everyone who comes after? The question had a survivalist quality. It was about staying in the game.

The question in 2075 is different. It is: *what is the game for?*

This is not a comfortable question. It is not answerable by the same methods that answered the earlier questions—the prediction markets and clinical trials and randomized controlled policies that had worked so well on problems with legible success criteria. It is the question that the therapist cannot answer for the patient, that the philosopher can illuminate but not resolve, that each person and community and civilization has to work out for itself over time.

Some people have turned outward—toward the stars, toward the slow and enormously difficult project of becoming a civilization that exists in more than one place. The first permanent lunar outpost was established in 2058; it is not large, not self-sufficient, and not by any ordinary measure comfortable. The people there find it extraordinary. The project of getting a real human presence into the outer solar system is now the longest-horizon collective endeavor that humanity has ever undertaken, and something about the enormity of that horizon is organizing for people who need enormous horizons.

Some people have turned inward—toward the ancient questions about consciousness and experience, about what kinds of life are most worth living, about the relationship between individual flourishing and the flourishing of communities and the species and the world. The tools available for this turning-inward are better than they used to be: not because AI has answered these questions, but because the reduction in material suffering and the improvement in mental health and the expansion of genuine leisure have given more people the capacity to take the questions seriously.

Some people have simply turned toward what is immediately in front of them. The river that needs restoring. The student who needs mentoring. The practice that needs practicing. The relationship that needs tending. And they have found—this is the thing that the prophets of radical transformation somehow always underestimate—that the immediate and the particular are inexhaustible. That there is no shortage of meaning in the small and the near.

---

## IX. What It Adds Up To

The acceleration was real. The progress in biology and medicine compressed a century of potential advances into a few decades—not because the physical world sped up, but because the intelligence brought to bear on its problems multiplied enormously, and that intelligence was, mostly, pointed at the right things by people who cared about pointing it correctly.

The institutional reforms were real. The governance improvements, the economic reforms, the spread of better epistemic practices—these did not make humans wise, but they made human institutions somewhat wiser, which is a different thing and, in the long run, perhaps more important.

The distribution was real, if imperfect. The world of 2075 is not fair. It remains unequal in ways that remain unconscionable. But the floor has risen, dramatically and globally, and the direction of movement is clear.

And the meaning—the stubborn, ineradicable, irreducible human need for meaning—turned out to be not a problem to be solved but a feature to be trusted. Humans have always been good at finding reasons to keep going. Give them health and security and genuine choice and the compression of pointless suffering, and they find those reasons with remarkable consistency. They restore rivers and carve wood and raise children and write music and ask questions that have no answers and keep asking them.

This is what the world looks like when much of what was worst about it has been addressed and the people in it have turned toward what is best. It is not a world without suffering—grief and loss and the difficulty of being alive in a body in a time are not problems to be solved. It is a world where the suffering that can be prevented is being prevented, and where the suffering that cannot is faced with more resources, more support, and more genuine community than before.

The morning is long. It is still early. The work continues, as it always has.

---

*The whole secret, if there is one, is that the tools were never the point. The tools were what let people get to the point—which was always, and remains, the question of how to be decent to one another across time, across difference, across the enormous variety of ways there are to be a human being alive in a world that did not ask to be inhabited but is, improbably and persistently, being inhabited well.*
