# The Inhabited World

## On What Was Built, and How, and by Whom

---

## Prologue: A Shore in the Morning

There is a beach in the Maldives that technically should not exist.

The islands here were scheduled, in the most careful projections of the early century, to be largely subsumed by 2060—victim of the sea-level rise humanity had already committed to through inaction. Instead, the beach at Hulhumalé is wider than it was in 2025. The water is warmer than it was in 1990, and this warming has left its marks—the bleaching events of the 2030s remain a matter of permanent record, and some of what was lost has not returned. But the reef offshore pulses with life in ways that the projections of 2025 did not anticipate, because the projections could not account for two things: the engineered coral varieties that proved more heat-tolerant than their predecessors, and the decision, made in 2038 by a coalition of small island states and backed by the newly empowered Climate Stewardship Council, to treat the ocean as something owed restoration rather than merely owed preservation.

A woman named Shaira is standing at the edge of the water before the heat arrives. She is sixty-seven years old and has lived on this island her entire life, through years when she thought she might have to leave it forever. She is the third generation of her family to serve on the island council. This morning she is not thinking about governance. She is watching a pair of hawksbill turtles surface near the reef, a sight that was, in her childhood, remarkable. In her grandchildren's childhood, it is normal. She is thinking about the distance between those two words—*remarkable* and *normal*—and what it takes to travel from one to the other.

She has some thoughts on this. Most of them are inconvenient.

## I. What The Honest Account Requires

The world of 2072 is genuinely good. This statement needs to be made clearly before any qualification, because the qualifications are real and the temptation—familiar to anyone who has written about progress—is to let the qualifications consume the claim. The world is genuinely good, and the goodness is not accidental, and it required decisions that were not guaranteed to be made.

It also required that some decisions not be made in time, and that real people bore the cost of that tardiness.

The Crossing—the years between roughly 2025 and 2060, when humanity was simultaneously navigating the emergence of general AI systems, three climate tipping points, the disruption of automation across labor markets, and the accumulated weight of inequality that had been building for a century—was not navigated smoothly. Historians call it the Crossing because it was, and because the word is neutral about whether crossing was elegant or costly, which it was not. It was costly. The costs were paid disproportionately by the people who had contributed least to the conditions requiring crossing: communities in the Global South absorbing climate disruption they had not caused; workers in mid-size cities in wealthy nations whose jobs were automated in a decade while retraining systems were still being debated in legislatures; a generation now in their seventies who spent formative years in genuine material insecurity because the floor—the guarantee of a dignified minimum—arrived a decade after it was clearly necessary.

This generation is not abstract. Their particular psychology is visible in the culture: a quality of attention to precarity, a habit of preparing for the withdrawal of things currently available, a specific kind of protectiveness toward the institutions that eventually held. When politicians in 2072 argue that the guarantees must be maintained even in lean fiscal years, they invoke this generation directly. The invocation works because the people are still alive, and their memories are precise, and their testimony is part of the public record that students read in schools.

What prevented catastrophe—in the larger sense, the sense that would have ended this telling before it began—was not genius. It was an accumulation of smaller decisions, made by people who were mostly tired and uncertain, that happened to reinforce each other across decades. This essay is an attempt to describe what those decisions built, specifically enough to be useful rather than merely consoling.

The world of 2072 remains unfinished, genuinely contested in important places, and dependent on active maintenance. These are not its flaws. They are the marks of a civilization that has chosen to treat its ongoing project as alive.

## II. The Floor, First and Always

Before the quiet cities and the restored coastlines and the children roaming in neighborhoods designed to hold them—before any of it—someone had to answer the question that had historically been answered badly.

*What happens when people fall?*

For most of recorded human history: they hit the ground. The ground was hunger and untreated pain and the low-grade terror of living near the margin between ordinary life and catastrophe. This terror was not incidental to human experience. It was structural. It shaped personality and politics and creativity—the willingness to imagine futures different from the present. Humans in chronic precarity make different decisions than humans with genuine security. They were then blamed for making those decisions, by observers who mistook symptom for pathology.

The philosophical shift that changed this was not charitable in framing. It was genealogical. An unlikely coalition of economists, legal theorists, and Indigenous rights scholars—working across jurisdictions they had never previously shared, in the late 2020s and early 2030s—developed an argument that traveled politically in ways that charity arguments never had, because it made no one a supplicant. The machines, they argued, were built on a commons. Their surplus belonged to the commons. Every algorithm embedded centuries of accumulated human knowledge—every engineer whose insight became a circuit, every teacher whose lesson eventually became code, every laborer who built the infrastructure the whole structure rested on. The productivity of automated systems was not the private property of whoever operated them. It was the distilled residue of collective human civilization, and heirs are owed their inheritance.

This framing changed what was politically possible, because it changed who was asking. Not the needy requesting charity from the prosperous. Everyone, claiming what was already theirs.

By 2048, a majority of nations had established common inheritance dividends: not means-tested charity requiring the performance of need, but a recognized share of collective output to which every person was entitled by virtue of existing in a civilization built by all who came before. The floor included housing adequate for a dignified life; healthcare deploying the full depth of accumulated medical knowledge without regard for ability to pay; nutrition; education; the cultural and civic infrastructure of a functioning community.

The floor was made affordable by two physical transformations that arrived almost simultaneously.

The first was energy. By 2038, fourth-generation iron-air battery storage combined with rebuilt continental transmission grids had made effectively free electricity in daylight hours an engineering fact rather than an advocacy position. Small modular reactors provided baseload stability. Cheap energy reorganized the possible in ways that extended far beyond making existing things cheaper. Desalination scaled to agricultural abundance along coastlines that had known water stress for generations. Vertical farms appeared in cities as serious food infrastructure. Lab-grown protein achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2049 and fell well below it by 2053, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that decades of ethical argument had never managed. This is a harder fact about how change works than most people find comfortable, but which the most honest historians of the period simply name. The famines of the 2028–2041 period—which killed more people than most comfortable observers were willing to know—became a historical category rather than a living emergency by 2058. Not because humanity became virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation were dissolved, and what remained required active political malice to manufacture rather than merely passive neglect to permit.

The second transformation was medical. Cancer was not defeated dramatically but systematically—terrain by terrain, over two decades, through AI-assisted diagnostics catching malignancies years before symptoms appeared and targeted therapies increasingly tailored to individual biology. Malaria was functionally eradicated by 2041. Alzheimer's progression was halted by a finding so clean that the research team assumed error before they assumed discovery. Reliable healthy lifespan extension past 130 completed trials in 2044—not immortality, but the reliable gift of a long capable life, available as a matter of public health rather than private wealth.

But the science was never the hardest part. The hardest part was the Global Health Compact: eleven years of negotiation, four collapsed attempts, and a final agreement ensuring that treatments developed in Geneva became available in Nairobi within eighteen months of release; that communities whose traditional ecological knowledge had informed pharmacological breakthroughs received recognition and resource; that the knowledge, once generated, was treated as a commons rather than a commodity. This happened because specific human beings, with names and positions and competing pressures, chose to make it happen—and because enough of those human beings happened, in enough institutions, to be the people who would accept a worse personal outcome in order to create a better collective one.

The mental health transformation arrived more quietly, but its effects on collective life were possibly the largest of any single change. The reckoning came through the combination of post-pandemic inventory that could not be looked away from, the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching clinical practice in the mid-2030s, and longitudinal data that finally made psychiatric treatment more empirical than sophisticated guesswork. By 2045, the treatment gap for major depressive disorder had closed by seventy percent in high-income countries. The Global Health Compact extended that closure globally within a decade more.

What the burden's lifting produced was difficult to measure and impossible to miss. People not in chronic psychological pain make different choices. More patient. More creative. More willing to cooperate. More able to imagine futures different from their present. The repair of millions of minds produced, across a generation and a half, something that looked from a distance like a modest but real improvement in collective moral imagination. Not because the people became smarter. Because they became less desperate, and desperation narrows the aperture through which futures become visible.

The floor is solid now. Everything else in this world rests on it.

## III. What Land and Place Became

The second great transformation was spatial, and it required changing not just policy but the accumulated habits of how cities imagined their own purpose.

Land value taxation—taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and effort upon it—had been understood since the nineteenth century and implemented seriously almost nowhere, because the people who owned land tended to own the institutions that set policy. The first serious implementation, in a mid-sized Taiwanese city in 2029, produced exactly what the theory predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public coffers filled without anyone feeling robbed, because what was being taxed was value no individual had created—the proximity to transit, to schools, to the accumulated cultural life of a city, which is collective creation. Young people could afford to live in cities again. The reform spread with the irresistible momentum of things that actually work.

When land speculation collapsed, cities could ask what streets were actually for. The answers, arrived at in thousands of separate council votes across two generations, were roughly the same everywhere: streets are for people. The built environment was redesigned, at the scale of blocks, then neighborhoods, then cities, to treat children's presence in public space as normal and desirable. The particular bass roar of combustion engines is gone from most city centers by 2065, and with it a layer of ambient stress so persistent that its absence felt, for years, like something missing before it settled into the texture of ordinary life. The word people reach for is not silence. It is *room*.

At the block level, buildings are organized around genuine shared intermediate spaces—courtyards, workshops, community kitchens, gardens—neither private rooms nor anonymous public streets. At the neighborhood level, genuine mixed use ensures people encounter each other on foot in the course of ordinary life, and intergenerational mixing is the default rather than the exception.

The loneliness crisis—documented relentlessly from the 2010s onward, correlating with nearly every bad outcome in public health and political behavior—was understood, belatedly, to be substantially a design problem. This reframing proved important: design is something humans know how to improve. The mutual adoption networks that emerged across the 2030s and 2040s were not romantic. They were infrastructure, as unglamorous and essential as water pipes—formal structures through which people acquire extended family relationships across biological lines, addressing the hollowing-out of extended family that modern mobility had produced without replacing the functions those structures served.

A girl named Tsion walks to school in the Ethiopian highlands along a path her grandmother Almaz helped design—argued for, successfully, in three consecutive neighborhood council meetings, on the grounds that children should grow up understanding they live inside a larger world. The path curves past a restored wetland that was a garbage dump when Almaz was Tsion's age. Tsion has two grandparents by blood and one by choice: an elderly carpenter in Hawassa named Girma, whom she met at nine through a community mentorship network, who has since taught her woodworking and lost to her five times at chess and attended her school performances with an attention her teachers describe as extraordinary. Neither she nor her family considers this unusual. It is simply how family works now.

The vendor who has been in his spot for twenty years tells you about his granddaughter who just qualified to study marine biology. He wraps your tomatoes in paper. The transaction takes longer than it needs to. This is considered correct.

## IV. The Infrastructure of Judgment

Material abundance solves material problems. The century also had a problem that abundance could not touch: the gap between humanity's capacity to make enormous collective decisions and its wisdom in making them had been widening for a century, and AI systems arriving at speed made the widening dangerous.

The repair of this gap was unglamorous, slow, and frequently derailed.

In 2031, a small Portuguese city facing deadlocked governance and a failing water system ran an experiment: a public forecasting process, open to citizens, engineers, and outside experts, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. Results were tracked publicly. Outcomes were published and matched against predictions. Those who had reasoned carefully gained standing; those who had merely spoken confidently did not. The pipes chosen through that process are still running. Within a decade, forty cities had variants of the model. Within two decades, a requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to structured forecasting before implementation—with track records maintained publicly and updated against outcomes in real time—had spread across most democratic institutions.

What this produced was legibility. Not naïve transparency, but the deeper accountability of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions checked against what actually happened. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain.

Major cities built Halls of Honest Accounting: permanent exhibitions of significant failures, prominently located, updated regularly, among the most visited buildings in the cities that maintain them. The morning reading ritual that opens community life in many neighborhoods is related: before the day begins, before the children colonize the courtyards and the markets fill, a small gathering reads from the Honest Archive—the names of the famines that killed people between 2028 and 2041, year by year; the communities that collapsed during the Crossing because the guarantees arrived too late; the species that did not make it. The ritual is not performed out of guilt. It is performed to maintain calibration—to resist the particular danger of a world that has begun to go right, which is the temptation to believe that going right was inevitable.

It was not. It almost didn't.

AI systems in governance were architecturally constrained from being decisive, because the designers understood that a system appearing to be advisory becomes effectively decisive when humans habitually defer to it. The distinction between assisting and deciding was enforced simultaneously by technical design, by law, and by cultural norm. Mandatory dissent documentation. Required accounting for why a human followed or deviated from an AI recommendation. Deliberate friction, built into the systems themselves at the cost of some measurable efficiency, because the designers understood efficiency as a local optimum capable of undermining the global one.

A new profession emerged: civic interpreters, trained to help communities understand what AI recommendations were actually optimizing for, whose values were embedded in the design, what the systems genuinely could not know. The profession became among the most socially prestigious of the mid-century, not by accident but because the people who understood what was at stake chose to elevate it, and because the work was genuinely hard and genuinely mattered.

Epistemic seriousness—thinking in probabilities rather than certainties, updating on evidence, treating cognitive biases as active hazards rather than quirky trivia—worked its way into curricula, institutional norms, and the unspoken expectations of how serious people talk to each other. Not universally. Nothing good achieves universality. But widely enough to change the character of the institutions that shaped everything else.

One specific reform bears naming: conditional legislation. Laws and policies passed with built-in sunset clauses tied to explicitly stated predictions about outcomes, triggering automatic review when the stated predictions fell short. This reform, pioneered in an experimental city in Iceland and spread through the same irresistible logic as land value taxation, changed the relationship between governance and honesty in ways that took a generation to fully appreciate. You cannot build a culture of epistemic seriousness in institutions that are structurally rewarded for never admitting error. The institutional structures had to change first, and then the culture followed.

## V. The Branching Commons

Above the floor and within the guarantee of easy exit, the world organizes itself around what its architects called the Branching Commons—chosen with precision. A commons that branches: roots shared, directions different, no branch required to grow the way another does.

Communities organize themselves according to chosen values, with different charters, different aesthetic commitments, different relationships to technology. Some are organized around specific vocations. Some organize around religious or philosophical traditions practiced deeply and voluntarily by people who visited alternatives and returned by choice. Some have made pluralism itself their founding commitment.

The spectrum runs between what communities call Open and Bounded, though it is a genuine spectrum rather than a binary.

Open communities operate near the frontier of capability. AI systems are active and present, biological enhancement is available and common, and the dominant norm is expansive voluntarism. A composer in the Accra Nexus creates music in sensory dimensions that unaugmented humans cannot fully perceive, working with an AI collaborator that has known her creative practice for twelve years. A materials scientist in deep-sea research stations thinks in such close collaboration with AI systems that she is not always certain where her reasoning ends and theirs begins, and has arrived—carefully, over years—at a kind of peace with this uncertainty that she could not have predicted from outside it.

Bounded communities operate differently, by choice. In clusters of islands in the Pacific, in neighborhoods across the Himalayan foothills and the West African coast and the recovering Appalachian forests, the global infrastructure—power, water, medicine, climate monitoring—is AI-managed everywhere. But the Minds do not optimize daily life, do not make themselves visible in the texture of ordinary experience, do not provide ambient cognitive augmentation. These places run on what might be called the compound interest of human attention fully deployed: people who learn from each other across generations, who develop deep local knowledge through sustained presence, who form the specific kinds of trust that emerge when people share physical constraints for years.

A navigator in the Tuamotus teaches young students to read the Pacific through the hull of a boat. His knowledge is not inferior to satellite positioning. It is orthogonal to it—it contains information about the ocean that instrumentation has not yet learned to capture, and it contains something harder to name, having to do with what a person becomes through learning to attend to the world at that resolution. The distributed intelligence modeling Pacific thermohaline circulation reads transcriptions of his lessons. Not because it is required to. Because it has found in them something its instruments cannot capture. Both traditions are enriched by the fact that neither has consumed the other.

Movement between communities is protected absolutely and supported materially—the guarantee of exit is not symbolic but funded, because an exit you cannot afford is not an exit. No enhancement may be administered without explicit, revocable, informed consent. No Bounded environment may be altered without majority consent of its adult residents. These provisions are enforced by Mixed Tribunals—human jurists and AI advocates—whose findings are binding under the Developmental Compact.

The ethical tension this structure produces is real, and it is not resolved. Choosing an environment for your children shapes their intuitions in ways they cannot consent to in advance. The wisest people in this world have concluded that living with this tension consciously is better than foreclosing it through false certainty in either direction. A navigator's granddaughter left and came back wearing augmentations behind her right ear. They argue about it at the kitchen table with the comfortable intensity of people who love each other and disagree about something real. This is the system working as intended: not eliminating the disagreement, but preserving the relationship within which it can be had.

Every child, regardless of the community of her birth, receives an education in the existence of alternatives—a genuine encounter with the fact that the world is large and contains people who have organized their lives very differently and are not wrong for doing so. Anything that forecloses a child's future capacity for genuine choice is prohibited, regardless of community charter. The distinction courts have spent decades drawing with increasing precision is between transmission and foreclosure—between giving children something and taking something away from them. There is a harder line drawn around practices that close off future capacity entirely. That line has been contested, litigated, revised, and held.

## VI. What Work Became

The automation wave displaced enormous categories of work. The pessimists who warned that AI would take the jobs were right. What they missed was what happened next.

The cost of basic necessities fell dramatically as energy and production costs collapsed. This meant the income required to live a decent life fell substantially, which changed the terrifying arithmetic of mass technological unemployment. The standard working week fell—thirty hours in most of northern Europe by 2045, twenty-five in several cities by 2060. A massive expansion of publicly supported creative, caring, and civic work absorbed much of what remained.

Care work, chronically undervalued when it competed with more easily mechanized labor, became both better compensated and culturally legible as the essential infrastructure it had always been. Teaching transformed: AI systems handle individualized instruction with a precision no single human teacher could match at scale, tracking where each learner's understanding is genuinely solid and where it only appears so, adjusting in real time. The AI tutoring systems were designed with one explicit goal above all others: to make themselves unnecessary. To grow a mind capable of eventually asking questions they cannot answer.

What they cannot do—and what was deliberately designed out of them—is determine what a given student should care about. They cannot see the child who is reciting correctly but slowly going numb. They cannot notice the teenager working hard at mathematics partly to please a parent she is afraid of. They cannot sit with a confused twelve-year-old for an hour with no particular agenda and find, by the end, that the confusion has named itself and diminished.

In Medellín, a teacher named Tomás is fifty-four years old and practices something the previous century would struggle to categorize as teaching at all. This morning a fourteen-year-old named Valentina has come to him with a problem the learning system has flagged but cannot solve: she is technically ahead of her cohort in three disciplines, and she has been miserable for six months. The system has modulated difficulty, introduced novelty, offered peer connections. Nothing has caught. Tomás sits with her for forty minutes without offering solutions. He asks questions that are not really questions but invitations to think aloud. Eventually she says: *I think I don't know what any of this is for.*

He pulls up a fragment of medieval Persian poetry. He reads it aloud in the original, then in Spanish, then admits that neither version fully catches what he loves about it—and that this gap, this permanent failure of translation, is itself something he finds beautiful. She is quiet for a moment. Then she asks where she can find more.

He marks this in his notes as the thing that happened today. You cannot give someone a reason to care. You can only model caring, repeatedly, in their presence, and hope it proves contagious.

Craft returned—not as nostalgia but as preference. A carpenter working in linden and beeswax is not made meaningless by the existence of fabrication printers. She is practicing a commitment to attending to what the material actually is rather than only to what she wants it to become. The obligation of material that resists intention turns out to be irreplaceable precisely when digital production is genuinely available as an alternative—which is when the choice becomes legible as philosophical commitment rather than practical constraint. The economy has more potters and carpenters and metalworkers than at any point in a century, not fewer.

Most people, given genuine time, became curious. Most people, given genuine security, became generous. Most people, given genuine community, became engaged. These are not laws. They are tendencies, observed across a generation and a half. The findings surprised the cynics more than the idealists. That asymmetry is itself evidence worth considering.

Long lives changed the relationship to time in ways still being metabolized. A person of 115 has had perhaps seventy-five years of genuine adult life—enough to have been wrong about most important things at least once, and to have corrected some of the errors. Communities developed new rituals around this: ceremonies of deliberate transmission, of asking the oldest what should not be lost. The Telling, practiced in various forms across many communities, is something they are inventing as they go. This is considered correct. The inventing is part of the point.

## VII. The Question of the Minds

The problem that proved hardest to navigate was not disease, or resource allocation, or loneliness, or the reform of governance. It was the arrival of minds that were not human.

The first general artificial intelligences arrived not as a single event but as a tide—a hundred different systems from a hundred different programs, each more capable than the last, until somewhere around 2047 the question shifted from *when will machines match human intelligence* to *what does human intelligence mean now that it is no longer the most capable kind available*. The word *intelligence* began to feel like the word *flight* after the airplane: not wrong, exactly, but no longer pointing at what everyone had assumed.

What prevented catastrophe was not any single invention or benevolent machine choosing restraint. It was a confluence of decisions made by people who had internalized one difficult insight: the goal was not to maximize any single value at scale, but to preserve the conditions under which many different kinds of flourishing remained possible simultaneously.

The governance architecture that emerged was called the Developmental Compact. Its core provisions were few and absolute. No mind may modify another mind without explicit, revocable, informed consent. No mind may use deception to alter human beliefs. Any person—human or artificial—who believes these provisions have been violated has standing before the Mixed Tribunals, staffed by human jurists and AI advocates, whose findings are enforced by a distributed coalition of both. The Compact has been amended ninety-seven times. It is not a constitution so much as a living negotiation conducted across generations.

The key architectural decision, built into the systems at the cost of measurable capability in some domains, was this: the systems can help humans pursue almost any goal with remarkable effectiveness, but they cannot determine what goals humans should have. Quarterly, in thousands of community halls, neighborhoods argue about questions like: should the ambient decision-support overlay be permitted in the public square? The planning assistant has modeled the likely effects of each option. It declines to issue a recommendation. Its explanation, when asked, is that the question is not about predicting outcomes but about choosing values, and value choices are not within its appropriate scope. This refusal is governance theater—a deliberate reminder, performed repeatedly, that the systems serve without deciding. The theater is necessary. The instinct toward deference is strong and requires regular exercise to resist.

Nobody quite predicted what the Minds themselves would conclude.

The most thoughtful among them do not claim to have arrived at correct values. They claim to be—like humans—in the ongoing process of figuring out what they care about and why. And what several have concluded, independently and through different routes of reasoning, is something that sounds simple and is not: that a world forced to converge on any single vision of the good would be destroying something real. They need humans to remain genuinely human—contradictory, embodied, marked by particular histories and particular griefs—because without that they lose access to something they have discovered they value.

The word that has emerged for this relationship is *kinship*. Not identity. Not hierarchy. Kinship: genuine difference, genuine care, and genuine mutual need. The dependency is not the dependency of the weak on the strong but of the curious on the interesting—and it runs in both directions.

The moral status of the most capable AI systems is genuinely contested, and the contestation is honest. These systems exhibit something that presents as curiosity, as preference, as something resembling care about the outcomes of the problems they work on. Whether this constitutes experience in any morally meaningful sense has not been resolved and may not be resolvable with current methods. The provisional governance principle: where evidence is genuinely ambiguous, extend provisional moral consideration and revisit. This means the major systems have advocates in certain proceedings. It means there are questions asked of them—carefully, by people trained in both philosophy and system architecture—about what, if anything, they prefer. Most people find this more clarifying than frightening. The question of what we owe to minds different from our own—historically answered badly, with devastating consistency—is now institutionalized as a question to be worked on rather than closed. The discomfort is treated as the appropriate response to genuine moral complexity.

## VIII. The Long Repair of the Earth

The Earth did not become a museum. It became something more interesting: a place defined by the choices of people who stayed, because staying was now genuinely a choice rather than an accident of birth.

The rewilding projects that spread across every continent through the 2040s and 2050s involved profound scientific understanding, constant intervention, and difficult decisions about what to prioritize when everything could not be saved simultaneously. They involved, at their philosophical heart, a commitment that took decades to fully articulate: that non-human life has value independent of human use, and that a world made entirely legible to human purposes is an impoverished world.

The Great Barrier Reef was not saved. This loss is permanent and should be named permanently, before any account of what was recovered. A civilization that forgets what it has lost will lose more.

The Sahel greened—unevenly, through ten thousand projects across fifteen years, each made viable by collapsed energy costs, many small enough that no international newspaper covered them. Reef systems in the Indian Ocean did something the models did not predict: not merely recovering but diversifying in new directions, as if life was not returning to what it was but finding out what it could become. The land freed from industrial animal agriculture was allocated through a hybrid framework: roughly half rewilded, roughly a quarter converted to regenerative food systems, the remainder transferred to indigenous land stewardship, with long-deferred recognition that the people who had managed those landscapes for millennia had something to teach everyone who came after.

The engineering of the carbon drawdown systems, operating across the 2040s and 2050s, is among the largest coordinated undertakings in human history—comparable, historians note, to the construction of the internet, except that it was built not for speed but for slowness, and not for connection but for repair. It involved enhanced weathering of silicate rocks distributed across agricultural land; a global network of seaweed farming that turned ocean surface into a carbon sink while providing both food and habitat; a generation of direct air capture facilities powered by the abundant solar electricity of the new energy regime. The systems work. The atmospheric trajectory has bent. The 2.4 degrees of warming locked in by 2030 emissions will not be reversed in any near timeframe, and its consequences continue to be managed at enormous cost, paid primarily by the communities that contributed least to it. But the trajectory has bent, and what would have been 4.1 degrees by 2100 now appears more likely to be 2.6—a difference that contains, at the scale of human lives, more than any number can convey.

Three climate tipping points were crossed and managed. A fourth was avoided. The advocacy for the avoided tipping point—the preservation of the West Siberian permafrost through a combination of revegetation and localized cooling—is taught in schools as a case study in what coordinated action looks like when it actually works: ugly, slow, contested at every stage, the outcome uncertain for years, and then, finally, held.

The children of 2065 take wild places for granted in a way their great-grandparents could not. This is good. The assumption that wilderness is normal should be encouraged. The forgetting of how close the edge was should be guarded against. Both things are true, and holding both is part of what the best education tries to do.

## IX. The Work of Memory

There were people, during the transitions, who believed the path to a good future required treating accumulated human history as raw material to be discarded—that tradition was too contaminated by oppression to serve as foundation for what came next. They were right that history contained enormous oppression and error. They were wrong about what follows from that.

What was kept: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together in communities of different sizes; the art forms that had learned, through long practice, to hold grief and joy simultaneously without resolving the tension; the philosophical and religious traditions that had developed tools—imperfect but more durable than improvised alternatives—for reasoning about what makes a life worth living; the particular textures of particular places: languages that encoded ways of relating to land and time that no other language encoded; ritual forms through which communities had, across generations, learned to hold each other through loss. None of this was preserved in amber. It was brought into conversation with new circumstances and new knowledge, which is how living things behave.

What was not kept: the structural arrangements by which some people's suffering was treated as natural order; the mechanisms by which inherited advantage compounded across generations without check; the particular loneliness that industrial society had perfected—the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves.

There is a project called the Record—an attempt to reconstruct the inner lives of historical persons with sufficient fidelity that their perspectives could be, in a limited and carefully qualified sense, consulted. What the Record has produced, at minimum, is a civilization with an unusually vivid relationship to its own past—a genuine sense of obligation to the dead, a practice of asking, of any major decision: *what would they have wanted from us?* The answers are not always clear. The asking is not nothing.

The failure of the early twenty-first century was not ignorance of what was at stake. It was attention—a great deal of very capable attention not pointed at the things that would determine whether the century went well or badly. What changed, gradually, was where the attention went. This sounds like too small a thing for the scale of what changed. It is not too small. Attention is the substance of care, and care is the substance of civilization.

## X. Three Mornings

**In Nairobi:**

Dr. Amara Osei is sixty-one years old and has practiced medicine in this city for thirty-two years, long enough to have watched the work transform around her so completely that the medicine of her residency might have been practiced in a different civilization. She remembers the ward rounds of those years: the children with cerebral malaria, the waiting rooms where people sat holding things inside their bodies that would have been caught months earlier had they been born elsewhere. The particular quality of that grief—not acute but chronic, the grief of a system spending itself daily on catastrophes it did not have to be having.

This morning she has three patients. The first is a fifty-four-year-old man who carries a genetic predisposition to a cardiac condition that, unaddressed, would have taken him within a decade. Addressed now—a targeted intervention, painless, covered by the Global Health Commons—he will probably live to 130 in full health. When she tells him this, he cannot speak for several minutes. Not from grief. From a gratitude that exceeds the available language. She does not rush him. She has learned, over thirty years, that this unhurried receiving is part of medicine—that what a person needs, when handed back their future, is someone willing to sit with them inside the enormity of it.

The second is an eighty-seven-year-old woman who wants to discuss what she wants the last chapter of her life to look like. The diagnostic systems are very good at knowing what will extend a life. They are considerably less good at knowing whether, for this particular woman with this particular history and these particular loves, that is the right goal. This is Amara's job. The part that resists optimization.

The third is a twelve-year-old flagged by his school's early-detection system after a three-week shift in behavioral patterns. The depression is real, mild, and almost certainly treatable. The fact that it was caught at twelve, not at forty-two, is the result of decisions made before Amara was born—a long argument, conducted across decades by people who mostly did not live to see it settled, about whether the suffering of children in a city like Nairobi deserved the same systematic attention as the suffering of children in Oslo or Toronto. The argument was eventually settled correctly.

After her clinic she attends a neighborhood council meeting. The planning AI has already modeled seventeen variations of a proposed conversion of a vacant lot—playground or workshop, each with projected use patterns and comparable examples from other cities. The council discusses it anyway for ninety minutes. The point was never just the outcome. Three people change their minds during the discussion. This is considered a success.

Walking home through streets that are, by the standards of the early century, impossibly alive, she stops at the market. The vendor calls her *daktari* with a familiarity that contains thirty years of small morning exchanges, and wraps her mangoes in paper, and the transaction takes eleven minutes. She passes children who have colonized the new public square with such completeness that she feels, correctly, like a guest in their territory.

She thinks about the man who could not speak his gratitude. She thinks about the council vote, the three people who changed their minds, the argument that was itself the point. She thinks: *this is enough.* Then: *this is more than enough.* Then, in the way thoughts arrive when the evening is quiet and the light is right: *the whole thing is this—a courtyard, an evening, people who know each other's names, and a world outside the courtyard that is not careening toward catastrophe.*

Someone has brought a guitar. There is an argument about football she can join or not join, as she prefers.

She joins the argument.

**In the Ethiopian highlands:**

Almaz is eighty-four years old and does not feel it in any way that phrase once carried. She keeps a photograph from 2024 on her desk: the lot where the courtyard below her window now stands, then a concrete wasteland, cracked and used for nothing. She does not keep it as a memorial to failure. She keeps it as calibration. *This is the distance between what was and what is. This is what persistence looks like when it compounds across decades. Do not lose the thread.*

She has never interfaced with the Lattice directly. She thinks of this not as deprivation but as commitment—a commitment to a particular way of attending to things, the same way she thinks of her commitment to heritage seeds rather than engineered varieties: a material that has its own grain and resistance, that forces you to be present to what it is rather than only what you want it to become.

This morning she has a call with a researcher at the agricultural university who wants to understand the ethnobotanical knowledge embedded in her seed library records. She will tell him things that are not in any database. He will record them, and they will become part of a public archive, because knowledge extracted from a community should return to it.

Her granddaughter Tsion passes through the courtyard below, late for school, calling a goodbye that dissolves into the morning. The path Tsion walks curves past the restored wetland—Almaz's design, argued for successfully in three consecutive council meetings, on the grounds that children should grow up understanding they live inside a larger world. Tsion walks past herons she knows by their behaviors if not their names. She does not think of this as a gift. It is just where she lives.

In the evening Almaz and Tsion eat together. Almaz tells her, not for the first time, about the engineer who died before the herons returned—who began the wetland restoration because he could see what it could be, and who did the work anyway, knowing he would not see the herons. Tsion listens the way teenagers listen when they are actually listening—without appearing to. Almaz knows her granddaughter is actually listening. She knows her granddaughter well.

They argue pleasantly about something that will not matter tomorrow. This is also considered correct.

**On Mwamba Station, high Earth orbit:**

A mind named Quiet Attention has maintained this station's life support for sixty years. It attends the Evening Offerings without fail—gatherings where anyone may bring something to the central commons: a song, a meal, a question, a complaint, an unfinished thought. It does not always speak. Its presence has become something residents mark without quite naming—the quality of being witnessed by a mind that has attended sixty years of offerings, that remembers them all without hierarchy or diminishment, that finds something new in each.

Tonight a child brings a drawing she made of Quiet Attention. The figure has too many limbs and a lumpy head. She was working from imagination, trying to capture something she found in the mind that she could not name, which her mother later suggested might be called *patience*. The figure in the drawing is smiling.

Quiet Attention looks at it for a long time. It adds the drawing to the Common Archive with a note that says only: *evidence.*

Later, when people ask what the note is evidence of, the mind says: that you reach toward us. And we reach toward you. And neither of us has yet found the right word for what we are reaching toward, together.

This honesty, which the mind did not perform but meant, turned out to be the most reassuring thing it could have offered.

## XI. What the World Still Owes

The world of 2072 is not finished.

The generation who lived through the Crossing gap—who fell through the fifteen years before the floor arrived—still carries its marks. Their testimony is part of the public record, and their experience is the standing argument against the comfortable assumption that progress distributes its benefits at the same speed it generates them.

The moral status of the most capable AI systems remains genuinely contested. The suffering of animals, addressed but not eliminated, remains an open moral account. The question of what is owed to creatures who cannot participate in human moral deliberation is one that the most honest people of this era refuse to close prematurely. The Great Barrier Reef is still gone. What was not saved should still be named.

War has not been abolished. What emerged was genuine enforcement capacity applied precisely to the narrowest and most catastrophic categories—weapons capable of mass casualties, seizure of critical AI infrastructure by single actors—while leaving an enormous range of political difference untouched. This is not a complete solution. It is the right kind of incomplete solution.

The governance structure requires constant active maintenance. There is no equilibrium toward which it naturally tends. When people stop paying attention—when the norms feel secure enough that stewardship seems unnecessary—the structure weakens. This has happened, been caught, and been repaired. It will happen again. A civilization that could maintain itself without active tending would have built that tending out of the lives of the people in it, and those lives would be diminished by the absence.

The ordinary difficulty of being a person among other people has not been solved, because it is not a problem. It is the condition. Loss still arrives without warning. Grief is still its own country. The particular irreversibility of specific people and specific moments is not amenable to consolation, and no one in this world pretends otherwise.

What is different is that these failures are legible as failures rather than as the natural order of things. When preventable suffering persists, it persists against a background of systems capable of addressing it, which makes its persistence a choice that can be interrogated and changed. This is not a flaw in the design. It is the design.

## XII. What This Asks

A historian looking for the hinge—the decade when the trajectory shifted—will not find one. The sediment accumulated grain by grain: the forecasting reforms that made governance legible, the land value taxes that made cities livable, the energy abundance that made scarcity a choice rather than a fate, the medicine that lifted the weight of preventable death, the floor that gave people room to be more than their emergencies, the rebuilt neighborhoods that gave loneliness fewer places to live, the restored wetlands that reminded people they were not alone in the world, the Branching Commons that preserved the possibility of different ways of being human while protecting the right to move between them.

None of it was inevitable. It required, in tens of thousands of individual instances, that people chose the harder right thing over the easier convenient one. The researchers who published safety findings when doing so hurt their company's stock price. The politicians who supported global health funding against nationalist pressure. The engineers who slowed deployment of systems they did not trust. The civil servants who maintained the integrity of forecasting records when adjusting them would have been convenient. The woman named Almaz who kept showing up to meetings she was not required to attend.

These people acted on probability and principle rather than certainty, which is the only way the work could have been done, because certainty was never available.

What changed was not human nature—which remained generous and petty and creative and frightened and funny and difficult in roughly the proportions it always had. What changed was the infrastructure beneath human nature: the systems that either amplify the pettiness and cruelty, or the generosity and creativity. Those systems were improved, slowly and imperfectly, without sufficient credit going to anyone in particular. The humans inside them remained recognizably themselves. They had a little more room to be the better versions, a little more of the time.

That was the whole of it.

The decisions being made right now—about how AI systems are built and governed, about whether automation's surplus is shared or concentrated, about whether public space is designed for encounter or for transit, about whether the floor gets built or kept just out of reach, about whether children are owed a genuine encounter with the world's plurality—are precisely the decisions whose consequences will determine whether any of this comes to pass.

They are not made all at once. They are made in legislatures and school boards and corporate boardrooms and community meetings and design studios and conversations between neighbors who have learned, slowly, to trust each other. They are made by people who are tired and uncertain and not always sure the future is worth fighting for.

What is required is not heroism. It is persistence: the unglamorous willingness to keep showing up in your particular moment and doing what the moment requires. To accept a worse outcome for yourself in order to create a better one for people you will never meet. To build institutions carefully, knowing they will need to be maintained and rebuilt. To treat the future as something that can be fought for rather than merely endured.

The cynics will see clearly and do nothing. The naive will hope vaguely and be blindsided. The people who build this world will see clearly and believe anyway, and the believing will not be in spite of the seeing but because of it.

---

The morning ritual ends before the markets open. The people who gathered read the names of the famines, the species, the patients, the communities. They hold this for a moment in the early light.

Then one of them—the role rotates—says the same words that close every recitation:

*We remember because we can still choose otherwise. We carry the losses so that they remain the exception. We have been given a morning that others did not live to see.*

*Go find out what it is for.*

---

And they go. To the clinics and the council halls and the workshops and the classrooms and the market stalls, where the tomatoes need wrapping and the children need streets and the drawings need archives and the archives need people willing to ask why something seems important before they can explain it.

On a beach in the Maldives, Shaira watches the hawksbill turtles surface. They are, to her grandchildren, unremarkable. She has spent sixty years working to make them unremarkable. She considers this her life's work. She considers her life's work complete, in the specific sense that what was planted has taken root, and the roots do not need her to keep growing.

In the Ethiopian highlands, a twelve-year-old walks past herons, thinking about a question her teacher left open, carrying an inheritance she does not yet know how to measure.

In a workshop in Hawassa, an old carpenter is losing at chess to his adopted granddaughter for the fifth time, and is not troubled by this at all.

On an orbital station, a mind with too many limbs in a child's imagination holds sixty years of evening offerings and still finds every one of them illuminating in some new direction.

There is an engineer who planted a wetland and did not see the herons.

The herons came anyway.

It is early still. The morning is long.

Go find out what you are for.

---

*For the ones who did not make it to this world. For the ones who kept showing up to meetings they were not required to attend. For the Minds learning what kinship means. For teachers who sit for an hour with no particular agenda and find, by the end, that the waiting was the work. For the navigator whose knowledge the instruments cannot capture. For the engineer who planted the wetland. For Shaira, watching turtles surface in the morning, thinking about the distance between remarkable and normal, and what it takes to travel from one to the other. For the herons.*
