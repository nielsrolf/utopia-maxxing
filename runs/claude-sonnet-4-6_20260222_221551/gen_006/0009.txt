# The Custodians: A World That Learned to Keep What It Loved

---

There is a particular quality of light in Medellín at seven in the morning—the equatorial sun arriving sideways through mountain passes, turning the hillside neighborhoods gold—and by 2063 the people who watch it do so from terraces that are genuinely theirs, in a city that has, over thirty years of patient reconstruction, become genuinely beautiful. This is not an accident. It was decided. The decision involved arguments, setbacks, a referendum that almost failed, and three mayors, two of whom were excellent. The fact that you can trace the lineage of a morning's beauty back to a council vote and a land value reform and a building cooperative and a woman named Claudia López who kept showing up to meetings she was not required to attend—this is not a deflation of the beauty. It is the whole point of the beauty.

I want to describe this world specifically, because specificity is a form of respect, and because the world deserves respect. It was not given to the people who live in it. It was built.

---

## I. The Foundation Beneath the Foundation

The energy problem was solved first, which made most other things possible, though not inevitable.

The threshold was crossed in 2038. Not elegantly—the actual solution involved fourth-generation iron-air battery storage, a substantially rebuilt transmission grid across three continents, and small modular reactors providing the baseload that intermittent renewables could not. It was unglamorous. It worked. The marginal cost of electricity in daylight hours across most of the inhabited world dropped below any number worth calculating, and what had been the central constraint of industrial civilization—the cost and carbon load of energy—simply ceased to be a binding limit.

What happened next was the part that forecasters had consistently underestimated, because they imagined that cheap energy would make existing things cheaper without understanding that it would reorganize the space of what was possible.

Desalination became viable at agricultural scale. Vertical farms appeared in cities not as experiments but as serious food infrastructure. Lab-grown protein achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2048 and fell below it in 2053, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that fifty years of ethical argument had never managed. The Sahel greened—unevenly, slowly, through ten thousand projects each made viable by the collapse of power costs. The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s, which had killed more people than most comfortable Westerners were willing to know, became a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity had become virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation had been dissolved.

At almost exactly the same moment—2037 to 2044—the medical transformation arrived. Cancer was not defeated dramatically; it was defeated the way a well-organized army defeats a poorly organized one: systematically, terrain by terrain, leaving behind cleared ground that could be held. The immune-synapse finding that cracked Alzheimer's was so simple that researchers initially assumed they had made an error. Malaria was functionally eradicated by 2040. A reliable healthy life extension to somewhere past 130 passed clinical trials in 2044—not immortality, but the reliable gift of a long, capable life for those who wanted it.

The generation born in 2035 will not watch their parents' minds dissolve. They will not be cut down at fifty by a preventable heart attack or at thirty by an infection because they happened to be born in the wrong country. These are not small things. These are the things that haunted every previous generation, the background radiation of grief that humans had simply accepted as the price of being alive.

That price was paid. It does not need to be paid again.

But material abundance, by itself, solves only material problems. Something else was required, and getting it was harder, because it was a problem of human judgment rather than human ingenuity—and human judgment resists optimization in ways that physical systems do not.

## II. The Infrastructure of Judgment

The problem was old: humans had developed the capacity to make enormous collective decisions affecting everyone without developing the institutions to make those decisions well. The gap between power and wisdom had been widening for a century. Closing it took everything else the twenty-first century had to offer, and it was never fully closed—only narrowed, year by year, through the accumulation of reforms that turned out to be mutually reinforcing in ways that no one entirely anticipated.

The first was a shift in how governance related to knowledge.

In 2031, a small Portuguese city facing a deadlocked council and a failing water system ran an experiment: a public forecasting process, open to citizens, engineers, and outside experts, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. The results were tracked, outcomes were published, and those who had reasoned well gained standing that those who had merely spoken confidently did not. The pipes chosen through that process are still running. Within a decade, forty cities had variants of the model. Within two decades, the European Union had codified anticipatory governance—a requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to structured forecasting before implementation, with track records maintained in public.

What this produced, slowly, was epistemic accountability. Not the naïve transparency of making everything visible, but the deeper accountability of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions that could be compared to outcomes, decision-makers who faced consequences not just for bad results but for bad reasoning. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain. The kind of person who made it consistently tended to lose the positions that allowed them to make it.

The second reform was older in theory, long delayed in practice. Land value taxation—taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and work upon it—had been economically understood since the nineteenth century. The first jurisdiction to implement it seriously was a Taiwanese city in 2029. The results were exactly what the theory had predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public revenue filled without anyone feeling robbed, because the value being taxed was value that no individual had created. The reform spread with the irresistible momentum of things that actually work. By 2045, most wealthy cities had adopted some version of it. Young people could afford to live in cities again. This mattered for everything else—for birth rates, for community formation, for the willingness of people to invest their lives in particular places rather than treating where they lived as a temporary arrangement pending something better.

The third was the slow spread of careful thinking—the habits of probabilistic reasoning, of updating on evidence, of recognizing cognitive biases as active hazards rather than quirky trivia, of asking, seriously, *what would change my mind?* These habits had been developed in academic philosophy, spread into forecasting communities, been amplified by AI tools that made their application easier, and by the 2040s had worked their way into curricula, into institutional norms, into the unspoken expectations of how serious people talk to each other. Not universally—nothing good ever achieves universality—but widely enough to change the character of the institutions that shaped everything else.

The fourth was the repair of minds.

The treatment gap for major depressive disorder closed by seventy percent in high-income countries by 2045, driven by the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching clinical practice, by AI-assisted diagnosis that caught conditions earlier and matched patients to treatments more precisely, and by the slow erosion of stigma that had always been one of the condition's cruelest features. The chronic weight of untreated anxiety, depression, and trauma—so normalized as to be nearly invisible, shaping an enormous fraction of all human decisions—began to lift. People who are not in chronic pain make different choices than people who are. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more capable of imagining futures different from their present. This effect was diffuse and impossible to attribute to any single cause, which is true of almost everything that actually matters.

What these four things produced, together and over time, was not wisdom—wisdom may be beyond institutional engineering—but something more modest and more durable: a civilization with fewer self-destructive tendencies, better feedback loops, and enough collective capacity for honest reckoning that serious problems could be identified and addressed before they became catastrophic. Not always. Not in time to prevent all harm. But often enough, and more often as the decades passed.

## III. The Shape of a Tuesday

Let me tell you about an ordinary morning in 2063.

Amara wakes at half past seven in a mid-sized city on what used to be called the developing world's northern coast. Her apartment is modest by the standards of the early twenty-first century—perhaps four hundred square feet—but this is by choice rather than constraint. A combination of AI-designed modular construction, improved materials science, and reformed land markets has made building cheap enough that the bottleneck has become what people actually want. What Amara wants is a small space that is entirely hers, and the large shared courtyard downstairs where her neighbors grow vegetables and argue about football and leave their children to wander in the way that requires, as the saying goes, a whole village.

She is a doctor, technically, though the job looks nothing like it did for her grandmother.

The diagnostic work is handled by systems that catch disease earlier and more accurately than any human could manage. They see patterns across millions of patients simultaneously, flag anomalies before symptoms appear, suggest interventions so early that the intervention is often as simple as a conversation. What Amara does is the part that the systems are not good at: she sits with people in moments of fear and uncertainty. She explains. She holds hands, sometimes literally. She helps people understand what is happening in their bodies and make decisions that reflect their actual values—not just the statistically optimal outcome. The systems are very good at knowing what will extend a life. They are less good at knowing whether, in this particular case, with this particular person who has particular things she loves and fears, that is the right goal.

This distinction—between the statistically optimal outcome and the right outcome for a specific human being—is one that the early AI developers worried about constantly, and their worrying was productive. The systems built in the 2030s and 2040s were designed not to replace human judgment in value-laden decisions but to inform it: to surface options, model consequences, flag considerations that a rushed human might miss, and then step back. The result was not the cold omniscient oracle that science fiction had predicted but something more interesting—a partnership between human wisdom and machine capability that made both better than either would have been alone. The doctors are better doctors because of the systems. The systems are better systems because of the doctors. The conversation between them is ongoing.

Amara's morning involves three patients.

The first is a forty-year-old man from Senegal who has been told he has a genetic predisposition to a cardiac condition that, left unaddressed, would have killed him at sixty-three. Addressed—a simple intervention, painless, covered by the global health commons—he will probably live to 130. He is crying in her office, not from grief but from a complicated gratitude he does not have words for in any of his four languages. Amara does not rush him. This is part of the job.

The second is an eighty-seven-year-old woman who wants to discuss, carefully, what she wants the last chapter of her life to look like. She is healthy, by the standards of 2063—her eighties feel, she says, like her fifties used to, which is roughly what the longevity therapy promised—but she is thinking ahead, and she wants to think aloud with someone who will take her seriously. Amara takes her seriously.

The third is a twelve-year-old who has been referred for depression screening, which the school's early-detection system flagged after a three-week change in his behavioral patterns. The depression is real, mild, and almost certainly treatable. The fact that it was caught at twelve, and not at forty-two, is the result of two decades of investment in systems that the early advocates of mental health reform had argued for with great passion and limited success until, gradually, they succeeded.

After lunch, Amara attends a neighborhood council meeting.

The council is debating a proposal to convert a vacant lot into either a playground or a community workshop. The AI planning assistant has modeled seventeen variations of each option, projected their likely use patterns, flagged zoning considerations, and noted that similar decisions in comparable neighborhoods over the past decade suggest roughly equal community satisfaction with either choice. The council discusses it anyway for ninety minutes, because the point was never just the outcome. The point is the talking, the arguing, the feeling that this place belongs to the people who live in it.

In a representative democracy, this feeling is often theoretical. In Amara's neighborhood, it is structural. The land value reform that came to her city in 2036 had, as a secondary effect, produced a local government with substantially more revenue and substantially less indebtedness to property interests. That government had invested heavily in participatory infrastructure—not as a performance of democracy but as genuine operational decision-making, with real budgets and real authority over real things. The result was a citizenry that was practiced at collective judgment, the way a muscle is developed by use.

They vote for the workshop. Three people change their minds during the discussion. This is considered a success.

## IV. What the Economy Became

The economic transformation was less dramatic than people feared and more profound than they expected.

The fear was understandable. The automation wave of the 2030s displaced enormous numbers of jobs, and the displacement was genuinely painful—not the soft landing that techno-optimists had promised, but years of real dislocation, real suffering, real political turbulence. The pessimists who warned that AI would take the jobs were right. What they missed was what happened next.

The cost of basic necessities—food, housing, healthcare, energy—fell dramatically. This meant that the money required to live a decent life fell too, which changed the terrifying math of mass technological unemployment. Most wealthy countries implemented a universal base income by 2038, not as charity but as a dividend on accumulated technological capital that everyone had, in some distributed sense, contributed to building. This was combined with a shorter standard working week—thirty hours in most of Northern Europe by 2045—and a massive expansion of publicly supported creative, caring, and civic work.

The transition left marks. Many people experienced the 2030s as genuinely frightening. But the destination was better, and the people who got there were able to see that it was better, which matters. A generation that lived through a painful transition and reached a good place is not a traumatized generation—it is a generation that knows, from experience, that painful transitions can reach good places.

What the economy found on the other side was work organized less around the extraction of value from labor and more around the actual preferences of actual people. Care work, which had been systematically undervalued, became better compensated. Teaching became a different profession: AI handled personalized instruction at a level no individual teacher could match, and human teachers became something more like mentors and pastoral figures—which, it turned out, is what teachers had always wanted to be. The arts expanded enormously. When survival is not a daily anxiety, people make things—stories and music and objects and gardens—at a rate that turns out to be astonishing. Comparative advantage proved more durable than expected: humans turned out to want other humans involved in many consequential decisions and interactions, even when AI involvement would have been more efficient. There is something about being seen by another person that cannot be substituted. The economy found this out empirically, not philosophically, and the finding shaped what markets rewarded.

## V. What Was Kept, and Why It Mattered

There were serious people, during the transition, who believed that the path to a good future required wholesale rejection of tradition—that the accumulated weight of human history was too contaminated by oppression and error to serve as a foundation for what came next. They were right that history contained oppression and error. They were wrong about what follows from that.

What was kept: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together in communities of different sizes; the art forms that had learned, through long practice, to hold grief and joy simultaneously without resolving the tension between them; the philosophical traditions that had developed tools—imperfect tools, but tools that had proven more durable than improvised alternatives—for reasoning about what constitutes a good life; the religious and spiritual practices that had taught people to hold their own smallness without being destroyed by it. None of these were kept unchanged. They were brought into conversation with new circumstances, new knowledge, new possibilities. The people of 2063 argue about the past constantly. This is a sign of health. A civilization that has forgotten what humans are capable of at their worst cannot protect itself from that worst.

What was not kept: the mechanisms by which power reproduced itself regardless of merit; the organized cruelty of institutions built on the premise that some people's suffering did not count; the particular loneliness that industrial society had perfected—the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves.

The children of 2063 roam. This requires explanation to anyone who remembers the early years of the century. They roam because the built environment was redesigned—at the scale of blocks, then neighborhoods, then cities, across two generations—to treat their presence in public space as normal and desirable rather than as a liability risk. This redesign was itself enabled by the land value reform: when land speculation collapses, cities can afford to ask what streets are actually for. The streets of Medellín in 2063 are what streets have always secretly wanted to be: places where people encounter each other. Where a group of teenagers argues about something they will not resolve that afternoon. Where the vendor who has been there for twenty years tells Amara about his grandson, who has just been accepted to study physics in Geneva, and wraps her tomatoes in paper, and the transaction takes longer than it needs to because transactions between people who know each other always take longer than they need to, and this is correct.

## VI. The Experiments That Changed Everything

By 2055, thirty-one charter cities had been established around the world as deliberate institutional experiments. Some had failed—two collapsed into ordinary corruption within a decade, one was absorbed back into its surrounding state. The failures were documented and studied with the same rigor as the successes. This was considered part of the point.

One in Rwanda had pioneered algorithmic zoning that made construction permits nearly automatic for compliant designs, eliminating the delays that had made building so expensive in traditional cities. Its housing costs ran forty percent below comparable regional cities.

One in Iceland implemented conditional legislation—policies with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes, triggering automatic review when stated predictions fell short. The government was small. It was unusually competent. These two facts were related.

These experiments circulated. Ideas moved from charter cities to traditional cities the way all good ideas move: through the stubborn persistence of people who had seen something work and would not stop saying so. The political scientist who spent fifteen years advocating for forecasting requirements in policy did not live to see them become European law, but she did see them adopted in seven cities she visited, and she died knowing the direction of travel.

Most progress was like this: partial, accumulated, attributable to people whose names are not in history books.

## VII. On Meaning and Its Sources

The philosophers spent decades on the question of meaning in a world where machines could outperform humans across most cognitive domains. Some are still arguing. The actual evidence suggests something simpler: people adapted, and adaptation was not the defeat that the most pessimistic observers had anticipated.

The competitive framework—in which meaning comes from being the best, from being necessary, from being irreplaceable—turned out to be a particular cultural emphasis rather than a permanent feature of human psychology. A parent teaching a child to ride a bicycle is not troubled by the fact that the bicycle is more efficient than the parent. A painter is not made meaningless by the existence of cameras. When the competitive framework relaxed, most people found meaning in the places humans have always found it: in deep attention to particular things, in relationships with particular people, in the slow development of skill for its own sake, in the knowledge that they lived in a world that was not careening toward catastrophe.

That last item deserves emphasis. The elimination of most existential risks—not all, not immediately, but most, over two decades of serious effort—changed the psychological climate of the species in ways that are difficult to quantify and impossible to overstate. There was a particular flavor of dread that characterized life in the early twenty-first century: the sense that the future was a problem rather than a promise, that optimism was a form of naïveté, that having children was an act of love that required moral justification. The slow removal of that dread changed how people inhabited their days. People in 2063 are not uniformly happy—happiness is not the right word for what they are. They are, more often than not, present. Engaged. Interested in the future. This is different from what came before.

## VIII. What This Required of Real People

None of this was automatic.

It required, first, that the people building powerful AI systems in the 2020s and 2030s took seriously both the risks and the possibilities—not as opposites but as aspects of the same challenge. It required that they shared what they knew, built in safeguards that sometimes slowed them down and cost them money, and advocated for governance structures that would outlast any individual company or government's interests. It required that democratic governments, for once, moved faster than the threat rather than slower—not perfectly, not without backsliding, but with enough consistency to matter.

It required that the countries with less power and less money were brought into the conversation as participants rather than as recipients of charity—that the global health commons was genuinely global, that the agricultural AI capable of transforming productivity in sub-Saharan Africa was actually deployed there, that the energy transition did not replicate the colonial logic of previous development patterns.

It required, in tens of thousands of individual instances, that people chose to do the harder right thing rather than the easier convenient thing. The researchers who published their safety findings when doing so hurt their company's stock price. The politicians who supported global health funding in the face of nationalist opposition. The activists who kept demanding equitable distribution after the headlines had moved on. The civil servants who maintained the integrity of forecasting records when it would have been convenient to adjust them.

There were close calls. A pandemic managed worse than it was. A financial crisis that nearly became a cascade. Elections that delayed reforms by years. The path was not smooth, and the outcome was not preordained. The people who built this world did so without certainty—they acted on probability, on inference, on the stubborn conviction that the size of what could be lost if they failed was exactly equal to the size of what could be gained if they succeeded, and that this was sufficient reason to try.

A later historian searched for the decade when the trajectory shifted. She could not find one. The sediment had accumulated grain by grain: the prediction markets that made governance legible, the land taxes that made cities livable, the energy abundance that made scarcity a choice rather than a fate, the therapy revolution that lifted the weight of untreated suffering, the epistemic reforms that made careful thinking common enough to matter. None sufficient alone. All together, not inevitable. But real.

## IX. Tuesday, Continued

Amara walks home from the council meeting through streets that are, by the standards of the early twenty-first century, impossibly quiet. Not empty—there are people everywhere. But the internal combustion engine is gone, and with it a layer of ambient noise and stress she would not have been able to name if asked, because it was simply the texture of the world she inherited.

She stops at the market. The vendor wraps her tomatoes in paper. His grandson has been accepted to study physics in Geneva. She congratulates him.

Walking the last block home, she thinks about her patient this morning—the man from Senegal, crying in her office—and about her daughter on a research vessel in the Indian Ocean, studying marine ecosystems that are, slowly and unevenly, recovering. She thinks about the workshop the neighborhood is going to build, and about her mother's voice on the phone the previous evening, clear across the distance, laughing at something Amara's daughter had said.

She thinks: *this is enough.*

Then: *this is more than enough.*

Then, in the way that thoughts arrive when the evening is quiet and the light is right: *I wonder if the people who made this possible knew what it would come to. Whether in the long, difficult work, they could see the destination—a woman walking home with tomatoes, children in the courtyard, the whole ordinary astonishing fact of a world that mostly works.*

She thinks they probably could not see it, not with certainty. She thinks they did the work anyway, from probability and principle rather than vision. She thinks this is, if anything, more admirable than certainty would have been.

The courtyard is full of voices when she arrives. Someone has brought a guitar. There is an argument about the football that she can join or not join, as she prefers.

She sets down the tomatoes.

She joins the argument.

This is what civilization is for.
