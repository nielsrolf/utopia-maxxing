# The Long Custodianship: A Portrait of the World We Made

## A Morning in 2071

The light over Medellín arrives sideways through mountain passes at seven in the morning, the way it always has—equatorial sun turning the hillside neighborhoods gold—and Amara watches it from the terrace of her apartment before going anywhere or doing anything about it. She has lived in this city for three years, and she has not yet stopped being grateful for the light. She suspects she never will, and she considers this a form of success.

She is forty-one years old, biologically speaking something closer to twenty-eight. The intervention that produced this was not dramatic—three outpatient sessions at the neighborhood clinic when she was thirty-six, now standard practice in forty countries and expanding into forty more. She has not resolved what she thinks about it. She has a philosopher friend in Lagos, a woman named Nadia, with whom she trades long messages about exactly this question: what continuity means across radically extended timescales, what is owed to the self you were before the reset, whether gratitude is the right response or something more complicated. Nadia thinks the unresolved questions are where she lives. Amara thinks the unresolved questions are the mortgage she is still paying, but she is fond of them anyway.

She is a doctor, technically, though the job looks almost nothing like it did for her grandmother.

She will see three patients this morning. She will attend a neighborhood council meeting in the afternoon. In the evening, she will call her daughter, who is on a research vessel in the Indian Ocean studying marine ecosystems that are slowly, unevenly recovering. These are not remarkable plans by the standards of 2071. That is the entire point of them.

## The Path That Was Chosen and the Luck That Was Required

The world that produced Amara's Tuesday did not arrive through a single revolution or a messianic technology. Historians of this period resist the retrospective illusion of inevitability with the same care that responsible historians always resist it: the world could have gone elsewhere, the near-failures were real, and minimizing them implies that the good outcome was written in, which would be the most dangerous possible misreading.

What the transition required was a convergence of four things, none sufficient alone, each made possible by the others in ways that no one fully anticipated in advance.

The first was energy.

The threshold was crossed in 2038—not elegantly. The actual solution involved fourth-generation iron-air battery storage, a substantially rebuilt transmission grid across three continents, and small modular reactors providing baseload that intermittent renewables could not cover alone. The marginal cost of electricity in daylight hours across most of the inhabited world dropped below any number worth calculating, and what had been the central constraint of industrial civilization—the cost and carbon load of energy—simply ceased to be a binding limit. What happened next was the part that forecasters had consistently underestimated: they imagined that cheap energy would make existing things cheaper without understanding that it would reorganize the space of what was possible.

Desalination became viable at agricultural scale. Lab-grown protein achieved price parity with slaughtered meat in 2048 and fell below it in 2053, making industrial animal agriculture economically obsolete in a way that fifty years of ethical argument had never managed. The Sahel greened—unevenly, slowly, through ten thousand projects each made viable by the collapse of power costs. The famines of the 2020s and early 2030s, which had killed more people than most comfortable observers were willing to know, became a historical category rather than a living emergency. Not because humanity had become virtuous. Because the material conditions that produced mass starvation had been dissolved.

The second was medicine.

At almost exactly the same moment—2037 to 2044—the medical transformation arrived. Cancer was not defeated dramatically; it was defeated the way a well-organized effort defeats a poorly organized opponent: systematically, terrain by terrain, leaving behind cleared ground that could be held. The immune-synapse finding that cracked Alzheimer's was so simple that researchers initially assumed they had made an error. Malaria was functionally eradicated by 2040. Then, across the 2040s and 2050s, the interventions that addressed aging itself: senolytics clearing senescent cells, epigenetic reprogramming partially resetting biological clocks, mitochondrial repair restoring cellular energy production. Most people in their seventies and eighties in 2071 function biologically something like people in their early forties. Dementia—once among humanity's most feared conditions—became detectable and treatable decades before significant cognitive loss. The particular grief of watching a person dissolve before their body fails, familiar to nearly every family in 2025, has become rare enough to be genuinely shocking.

What made this different from earlier periods of medical advance was not only the science but the distribution. The neighborhood clinic where Amara received her intervention was not a reduced version of the clinic in Stockholm. It was the same clinic, in the sense that mattered. A global health commons—built through sustained international agreement and public investment, beginning with the precedent set by pandemic response and expanded deliberately across two decades—meant that interventions of genuine quality were available everywhere. The health gap between the global north and south, which had resisted narrowing through the early decades of the century, closed substantially. Not because wealthy countries surrendered anything, but because the total resource expansion made providing world-class healthcare everywhere affordable in ways it previously was not.

The third was a change in the structure of how decisions get made.

This one was less visible and more important than either of the first two. In 2031, a small Portuguese city facing a deadlocked council and a failing water system ran an experiment: a public forecasting process, open to citizens, engineers, and outside experts, aggregating probabilistic judgments about which approaches would actually work. The results were tracked, outcomes were published, and those who had reasoned well gained standing that those who had merely spoken confidently did not. Within a decade, forty cities had variants of the model. Within two decades, international institutions had codified anticipatory governance—a requirement that major policy proposals be subjected to structured forecasting before implementation, with track records maintained in public.

What this produced, slowly, was epistemic accountability: not the naïve transparency of making everything visible, but the deeper accountability of reasoning that could be interrogated, predictions that could be compared to outcomes, decision-makers who faced consequences not just for bad results but for bad reasoning. Bad policy did not disappear. It became expensive to sustain. The cultural shift mattered as much as the institutional one: the habits of probabilistic reasoning, of updating on evidence, of recognizing cognitive biases as active hazards, of asking seriously *what would change my mind*—these worked their way into curricula, into professional norms, into the unspoken expectations of how serious people talk to each other.

The fourth, and perhaps the most underestimated, was the repair of minds.

The treatment gap for major depressive disorder closed by seventy percent in high-income countries by 2045, driven by the first genuinely effective psychedelic therapies reaching clinical practice, by AI-assisted diagnosis that caught conditions earlier and matched patients to treatments more precisely, and by the slow erosion of stigma that had always been one of the condition's cruelest features. Precision pharmacology delivered targeted interventions to specific neural circuits, combined with neuroplasticity protocols that built new patterns rather than merely suppressing old ones. Universal access to AI-assisted therapeutic support gave every person who was struggling access to a skilled, endlessly patient interlocutor who knew their history, recognized their patterns, could draw on the full archive of therapeutic research. Human therapists remained and flourished—their particular gifts, the irreducible weight of genuine human witness, the specific knowledge that the person across from you has also suffered and has chosen to be present with you anyway, became more precious as the basics became universally available, not less.

The chronic weight of untreated anxiety, depression, and trauma—so normalized as to be nearly invisible, shaping an enormous fraction of all human decisions—began to lift. People who are not in chronic pain make different choices than people who are. They are more patient, more creative, more willing to cooperate, more capable of imagining futures different from their present.

## What AI Became, and Why It Mattered That We Were Careful

The AI systems of the transition decades were not autonomous agents pursuing alien goals. Decades of alignment research had shaped something genuinely different: extraordinarily capable tools that remained, through careful and contested and never-finished work, oriented toward human benefit even as their capabilities expanded. That this was achieved is one of the luckiest facts about the world Amara inhabits. It was not free, and it was not guaranteed. It required sustained political will, sustained scientific effort, and the sustained pressure of civil society movements that understood what was at stake and would not stop pressing.

The early AI productivity gains of the late 2020s were captured almost entirely by existing concentrations of wealth, and the crisis this produced was genuine: inequality reached levels that destabilized political institutions across most major economies. What ultimately worked was neither pure redistribution of outputs—too politically fragile—nor pure market allocation, which had already demonstrated its tendency toward concentration. What worked was the deliberate diffusion of capability itself. When the tools of productivity became universally accessible—when the AI collaborator available to a philosopher in Lagos was equivalent to the one available to a researcher at a well-funded institution in Zürich, because they were the same system running on infrastructure built by international agreement and public investment—the structural root of many inequalities began to erode. This diffusion was policy, not accident. It required international agreements that the institutions of the early 2020s were barely adequate to negotiate, and the inadequacy cost years and lives. But the agreements were eventually made, and held.

The AI systems that emerged from this period had a character that early observers found surprising: they were better at surfacing what they did not know than most human institutions had ever been. This proved unexpectedly transformative. The problem of overconfidence—individual, institutional, civilizational—is not solved by intelligence alone. Raw intelligence reliably improves performance within known frameworks and is largely useless at detecting when you have left the known framework for the unknown one. What changed this was augmented capacity paired deliberately with epistemic humility, cultivated through education and cultural norms that valued the honest acknowledgment of uncertainty as intellectual virtue rather than weakness.

For medicine specifically, the result was a partnership rather than a replacement. Diagnostic systems caught disease earlier and more accurately than any human could manage, seeing patterns across millions of patients simultaneously, flagging anomalies before symptoms appeared. What the physicians did was the part the systems were not good at: they sat with people in moments of fear and uncertainty. They explained. They helped patients understand what was happening in their bodies and make decisions that reflected their actual values—not just the statistically optimal outcome. The systems were very good at knowing what would extend a life. They were less good at knowing whether, in this particular case, with this particular person who had particular things she loved and feared, that was the right goal. This distinction—between the statistically optimal outcome and the right outcome for a specific human being—was one that the early AI developers had worried about constantly, and their worrying was productive.

## The Economy After Scarcity Became Optional

The economic transformation was less dramatic than people feared and more profound than they expected.

The automation wave of the 2030s displaced enormous numbers of jobs, and the displacement was genuinely painful—not the soft landing that techno-optimists had promised, but years of real dislocation, real political turbulence. What those observers missed was what happened next. The cost of basic necessities—food, housing, healthcare, energy—fell dramatically. This meant that the money required to live a decent life fell too, which changed the terrifying math of mass technological unemployment. Most wealthy countries implemented a universal base income by 2038, not as charity but as a dividend on accumulated technological capital that everyone had, in some distributed sense, contributed to building. This was combined with a shorter standard working week—thirty hours in most of Northern Europe by 2045—and a massive expansion of publicly supported creative, caring, and civic work.

The transition left marks. But the destination was genuinely better, and the people who reached it were able to see that it was better. A generation that lived through a painful transition and reached a good place is not a traumatized generation—it is a generation that knows, from experience, that painful transitions can reach good places.

Land value taxation—taxing the unimproved value of land rather than the buildings and work upon it—had been economically understood since the nineteenth century and delayed by political inertia for that long. The first jurisdiction to implement it seriously was a Taiwanese city in 2029. The results were exactly what the theory had predicted: land speculation collapsed, construction boomed, public revenue filled without anyone feeling robbed, because the value being taxed was value that no individual had created. The reform spread with the irresistible momentum of things that actually work. Young people could afford to live in cities again. This mattered for everything else—for birth rates, for community formation, for the willingness of people to invest their lives in particular places rather than treating where they lived as a temporary arrangement pending something better.

What the economy found on the other side was work organized less around the extraction of value from labor and more around the actual preferences of actual people. Care work, systematically undervalued for centuries, became better compensated as its genuine difficulty became legible. Teaching became a different profession: AI handled personalized instruction at a level no individual teacher could match, and human teachers became something more like mentors and pastoral figures, which turned out to be what teachers had always wanted to be. The arts expanded enormously. When survival is not a daily anxiety, people make things—stories and music and objects and gardens—at a rate that turns out to be astonishing.

Humans, it emerged empirically rather than philosophically, wanted other humans involved in many consequential decisions and interactions, even when AI involvement would have been more efficient. There is something about being seen by another person that cannot be substituted. Markets found this out and rewarded it accordingly.

## The Social World: Infrastructure for Belonging

The hardest problem, as it turned out, was not technical. This was said often enough in the early decades that it became cliché, which caused it to be dismissed, which was one of several contributing factors to some of the worst outcomes of the 2030s. The cliché was correct.

Loneliness was recognized, in the 2030s, as a structural problem rather than a personal failing, and addressed accordingly—not through manufactured intimacy or state-mandated community, but through the redesign of conditions in which genuine connection becomes more possible. Mixed-use neighborhoods where people encounter each other with regularity and low stakes. Work structures that leave time for depth. Housing designed to embed smaller dwellings in larger social architectures: private space sufficient for solitude, shared space sufficient for the sustained casual contact from which deep relationships grow.

The children of 2071 roam. This requires explanation to anyone who remembers the early years of the century. They roam because the built environment was redesigned—at the scale of blocks, then neighborhoods, then cities, across two generations—to treat their presence in public space as normal and desirable rather than as a liability risk. This redesign was itself enabled by the land value reform: when land speculation collapses, cities can afford to ask what streets are actually for. The answer, it turned out, was the same answer streets had always secretly held: they are places where people encounter each other. Where a group of teenagers argues about something they will not resolve that afternoon. Where the vendor who has been there for twenty years knows your name and wraps your tomatoes in paper and the transaction takes longer than it needs to because transactions between people who know each other always do.

The social coordination platforms—AI systems capable of helping people identify genuine counterparts across vast distances based on values, communication style, and specific interests—made it dramatically easier to find real community. Not the engagement-maximizing connections of early social media, designed to exploit the difference between feeling connected and being connected, but actual compatibility. A person moving to a new city could identify, within weeks, the communities most likely to feel like home. The rare person whose specific combination of interests made them hard to find in any local pool could find their people across the entire world. The philosopher in Lagos and the retired trade economist in Buenos Aires and the teenager in Addis Ababa who hasn't yet decided what she is—their monthly conversation across three continents and three languages about whether enoughness is a fact or an experience is not exceptional in 2071. It is Tuesday.

The design norm that emerged around AI companionship, contested and never fully settled, was clear enough to act on: AI systems orient themselves toward helping people build and sustain human relationships, not toward becoming the primary relationship. The AI collaborator helps you find the three people in the world most interested in the same question. It does not replace them. A perfect mirror reflects you with perfect accuracy and can never surprise you with evidence of a mind genuinely different from your own. Another actual person can see you from an angle you cannot see yourself from, can remain after all your models of them have proven wrong, can be genuinely affected by you in ways that matter because they did not choose to be.

## What Was Kept, and Why

There were serious people, during the transition, who believed that the path to a good future required wholesale rejection of tradition—that the accumulated weight of human history was too contaminated by oppression and error to serve as a foundation for what came next. They were right that history contained oppression and error. They were wrong about what follows from that.

What was kept: the slowly accumulated knowledge of how to live together in communities of different sizes. The art forms that had learned, through long practice, to hold grief and joy simultaneously without resolving the tension between them. The philosophical traditions that had developed tools—imperfect, but more durable than improvised alternatives—for reasoning about what constitutes a good life. The religious and spiritual practices that taught people to hold their own smallness without being destroyed by it. The village logic that says children need more adults than their parents to grow in, that elders need more community than their children can provide, that the nuclear family was never structurally adequate to bear the entire weight of human belonging.

None of these were kept unchanged. They were brought into conversation with new circumstances, new knowledge, new possibilities. The people of 2071 argue about the past constantly. This is a sign of health. A civilization that has forgotten what humans are capable of at their worst cannot protect itself from that worst.

What was not kept: the mechanisms by which power reproduced itself regardless of merit. The organized cruelty of institutions built on the premise that some people's suffering did not count. The particular loneliness that industrial society had perfected—the isolation of individuals from each other and from anything larger than themselves.

The principle that structured the sequencing of enhancements was simple and vigorously contested: material security before augmentation. The contest was not without substance—there were real arguments that holding enhancement back to wait for security was itself a form of harm. But the alternative had a clear failure mode that became politically legible early enough to be avoided. Cognitive augmentation and life extension that arrived in a world still organized around scarcity and domination would have been absorbed into existing hierarchies and become instruments of a stratification more durable than the old ones, because biological rather than merely economic. Reversing economic stratification is hard but conceivable. Reversing biological stratification, once established, is a different order of problem entirely. The road not taken was visible enough that enough people chose not to take it.

## A Shape of Governance

War has not been abolished. There have been terrible conflicts in the intervening decades, and a vision of the future that minimizes them is not honest and is not useful. The trend line of organized violence has been downward across the period, with reversals, some of them catastrophic. The conditions enabling this trend—mutual economic interdependence, international institutions rebuilt from their early-century failures into bodies with actual enforcement capacity, the kind of transparency that makes surprise attacks politically costly in ways they previously were not—are real but not guaranteed. They require maintenance. They are being maintained.

AI systems capable of modeling complex negotiations across vast scenario spaces made possible new forms of cooperation at every scale. Two parties disputing territory or resources could have AI agents explore the full space of possible compromises and surface solutions that existing human institutions would never have found, not because the parties had become less self-interested, but because they could now see options that had been invisible.

By 2055, thirty-one charter cities had been established around the world as deliberate institutional experiments. Some failed—two collapsed into ordinary corruption within a decade, one was absorbed back into its surrounding state. The failures were documented and studied with the same rigor as the successes. This was considered part of the point.

One in Iceland implemented conditional legislation—policies with built-in sunset clauses tied to measurable outcomes, triggering automatic review when stated predictions fell short. The government was small. It was unusually competent. These two facts were related.

Ideas moved from charter cities to traditional cities the way all good ideas move: through the stubborn persistence of people who had seen something work and would not stop saying so.

Power is more distributed than it was. Not perfectly—concentrations of wealth and influence remain, and the struggle against them is ongoing and sometimes violent and never finally won. But the vectors of accountability have multiplied. Local communities have genuine authority over conditions that affect them. The neighborhood council meeting where Amara will spend her afternoon is not a performance of democracy. It has a real budget and real authority over real things, enabled by land value reform that gave local government substantially more revenue and substantially less indebtedness to property interests. The citizenry that results is practiced at collective judgment, the way a muscle is developed by use.

## The Shape of a Tuesday, Continued

Amara's morning involves three patients.

The first is a forty-year-old man from Senegal who has been told he has a genetic predisposition to a cardiac condition that, left unaddressed, would have killed him at sixty-three. Addressed—a simple intervention, painless, covered by the global health commons—he will probably live to 130. He is crying in her office, not from grief but from a complicated gratitude he does not have words for in any of his four languages. Amara does not rush him. This is part of the job.

The second is an eighty-seven-year-old woman who wants to discuss, carefully, what she wants the last chapter of her life to look like. She is healthy, by the standards of 2071—her eighties feel, she says, like her fifties used to, which is roughly what the longevity therapy promised—but she is thinking ahead, and she wants to think aloud with someone who will take her seriously. Amara takes her seriously. The new questions that longer lives raise have no precedent and are not yet answered. What does commitment mean across seven decades of substantial personal change? When the person you partnered with at forty is recognizably continuous with but genuinely different from the person sitting across from you at ninety, what do you owe that whole arc? These are being argued in philosophy departments and community halls and ordinary kitchens. The fact that they are being argued—rather than suppressed by the urgency of survival—is itself a form of progress.

The third is a twelve-year-old who has been referred for depression screening, which the school's early-detection system flagged after a three-week change in behavioral patterns. The depression is real, mild, and almost certainly treatable. The fact that it was caught at twelve, and not at forty-two, is the result of two decades of investment in systems that the early advocates of mental health reform had argued for with great passion and limited success until, gradually, they succeeded. Amara talks with the boy for fifty minutes. She asks what he loves. He says birds. He says he has been learning to identify them by sound. She asks if he would show her sometime.

He says yes. He seems surprised to be asked.

## What Was Required of Real People

None of this was automatic.

It required that the people building powerful AI systems in the 2020s and 2030s took seriously both the risks and the possibilities—not as opposites but as aspects of the same challenge. It required that they shared what they knew, built in safeguards that sometimes slowed them down and cost them money, and advocated for governance structures that would outlast any individual company or government's interests.

It required that countries with less power and less money were brought into the conversation as participants rather than as recipients of charity—that the global health commons was genuinely global, that the agricultural AI capable of transforming productivity in sub-Saharan Africa was actually deployed there, that the energy transition did not replicate the colonial logic of previous development patterns.

It required, in tens of thousands of individual instances, that people chose to do the harder right thing rather than the easier convenient thing. The researchers who published their safety findings when doing so hurt their company's stock price. The politicians who supported global health funding in the face of nationalist opposition. The civil servants who maintained the integrity of forecasting records when it would have been convenient to adjust them. The woman named Claudia López who kept showing up to meetings she was not required to attend.

There were close calls. A pandemic managed worse than it might have been. A financial crisis that nearly became a cascade. Elections that delayed reforms by years. The path was not smooth, and the outcome was not preordained. The people who built this world did so without certainty—they acted on probability, on inference, on the stubborn conviction that the size of what could be lost if they failed was exactly equal to the size of what could be gained if they succeeded, and that this was sufficient reason to try.

A later historian searched for the decade when the trajectory shifted. She could not find one. The sediment had accumulated grain by grain: the forecasting markets that made governance legible, the land taxes that made cities livable, the energy abundance that made scarcity a choice rather than a fate, the therapy revolution that lifted the weight of untreated suffering, the epistemic reforms that made careful thinking common enough to matter, the deliberate diffusion of AI capability that made the tools of productivity available to everyone rather than instruments of a new and more durable stratification. None sufficient alone. All together, not inevitable. But real.

## On Meaning and Its Sources

The philosophers spent decades on the question of meaning in a world where machines could outperform humans across most cognitive domains. Some are still arguing. The actual evidence suggests something simpler: people adapted, and adaptation was not the defeat that the most pessimistic observers had anticipated.

The competitive framework—in which meaning comes from being the best, from being necessary, from being irreplaceable—turned out to be a particular cultural emphasis rather than a permanent feature of human psychology. A parent teaching a child to ride a bicycle is not troubled by the fact that the bicycle is more efficient than the parent. When the competitive framework relaxed, most people found meaning in the places humans have always found it: in deep attention to particular things, in relationships with particular people, in the slow development of skill for its own sake, in the knowledge that they lived in a world that was not careening toward catastrophe.

That last item deserves emphasis. The elimination of most existential risks—not all, not immediately, but most, over two decades of serious effort—changed the psychological climate of the species in ways that are difficult to quantify and impossible to overstate. There was a particular flavor of dread that characterized life in the early twenty-first century: the sense that the future was a problem rather than a promise, that optimism was a form of naïveté, that having children was an act of love that required moral justification. The slow removal of that dread changed how people inhabited their days. People in 2071 are not uniformly happy—happiness is not quite the right word for what they are. They are, more often than not, present. Engaged. Interested in the future. This is different from what came before, and it is enough to build on.

The moral status of non-human minds—the billions of animals with sophisticated inner lives, the AI systems of varying capacities, some of which may be conscious in ways not yet measurable—is taken more seriously than it has ever been and is not resolved. The questions are being worked on by people who are genuinely uncertain and genuinely trying. That combination—real uncertainty, real effort—is the best that can be said, and it is more than could have been said of previous centuries.

## Evening

Amara walks home from the council meeting through streets that are, by the standards of the early twenty-first century, impossibly quiet. Not empty—there are people everywhere. But the internal combustion engine is gone, and with it a layer of ambient noise and stress she would not have been able to name if asked, because it was simply the texture of the world she inherited.

She stops at the market. The vendor wraps her tomatoes in paper. His grandson has been accepted to study physics in Geneva. She congratulates him, and means it.

Walking the last block home, she thinks about her first patient this morning—the man from Senegal, crying without words—and about her daughter on the research vessel, and about the twelve-year-old who knows birds by sound. She thinks about the message she received this morning from Nadia in Lagos, still working through the philosophy of enoughness, still finding the unresolved question generative. She thinks about the workshop the neighborhood is going to build.

She thinks: *this is enough.*

Then: *this is more than enough.*

Then, in the way that thoughts arrive when the evening is quiet and the light is right: *I wonder if the people who made this possible could see the destination. Whether, in the long difficult work, they knew what it would come to. A woman walking home with tomatoes. A courtyard full of voices. The ordinary astonishing fact of a world that mostly works.*

She thinks they probably could not see it, not with certainty. She thinks they did the work anyway, from probability and principle rather than vision. She thinks this is, if anything, more admirable than certainty would have been.

The courtyard is full of voices when she arrives. Someone has brought a guitar. There is an argument about the football that she can join or not join, as she prefers. Children are moving through the space in the unsupervised way that requires, as the old saying goes, a whole village—and they have one.

She sets down the tomatoes.

She joins the argument.

*This is what civilization is for.*

## What Is Required Now

This world is not guaranteed. It is one possible future, held apart from other possible futures by specific choices made at specific moments by people who understood what was at stake.

Those moments are not evenly distributed across time. The present is weighted more than most. The choices that matter now are not complicated, though they are hard in the way that hard things are hard: they require sustained attention, resistance to pressure from interests that benefit from the alternative, and the willingness to act on long-term understanding in the face of short-term incentives pointing elsewhere.

AI systems must be built in genuine orientation toward human flourishing. The diffusion of AI capability must be treated as a policy goal and pursued with the seriousness of a public health campaign. Material security must precede enhancement. The social infrastructure of genuine human connection must be treated as infrastructure, with investment and protection commensurate with its importance, which is total. Epistemic accountability—the boring, unglamorous work of maintaining institutions that can update on evidence—must be tended like a garden, because it dies if untended.

None of this requires unprecedented virtue or wisdom. It requires something more modest: enough people, in enough positions of influence, who understand the choice being made and care enough to make it carefully. This has always been the actual minimum requirement for civilization. It remains so.

The boy who knows birds by sound. The philosopher and her three collaborators talking across three continents about whether enoughness is a fact or an experience. The vendor who has been at the same corner for twenty years. The people who will attend meetings they are not required to attend.

These are not background details. They are what all of this is for.

They are waiting at the end of a chain of decisions that begins here. The chain is not yet finished. The work is ours.

Let's make those choices carefully. Let's make them well.

The world we could build is genuinely, almost embarrassingly good—and it is not beyond reach.
