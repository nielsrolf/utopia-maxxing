The Threshold Republic

On a wet morning that smelled like petrichor and cut grass, a new post appeared between the bus bench and the corner laundromat. It was enamel-green, waist-high, with a porcelain coin set into its face and a linen pull cord looped through it like a bell rope. Above it, in sturdy block letters:

Make me a path.

People tugged it to see if it meant anything. If your partner had your login and your paycheck, the post—everyone started calling them Green Doors within a week—killed the tracker, paused the wage siphon, dispatched a locksmith, and sent a car to a spare bed three train stops away. If your foreman kept your passport in his desk, the post filed a hold in a court that actually moved, registered a complaint that could freeze that trick, and put your papers back in your hand. If your trouble didn’t have a clean label—if it felt like the rules around you were tightening until you couldn’t breathe—the Green Door glowed and displayed three places you could land within a commuter ride: a quiet block with porches and potlucks; a tower with a tool library and a rooftop studio; a row of bright kitchens above a line of night cafés. Each showed an empty room, bus times, and the petty fees it would pay so leaving didn’t become a second punishment.

The promise nested inside the Green Doors was portable: there is always a way out; and if you want choices, ask.

The Threshold Charter

A simple metacharter sat under the posts, vans, and cots with clean sheets. We carved it on clinic walls, library stairwells, and school gym doors. We called it the Threshold Charter because it did not dictate which house you must live in; it guaranteed that every house had a lit threshold you could see from the road.

- Exit rights: Any adult can leave any household, workplace, association, or town without their identity, pay, or papers being taken hostage. Attempting to trap someone behind a maze of “customer service” is treated like taking a wallet by force.

- Harm may not govern: Starvation, beatings, threats made through paperwork, and constant tracking without specific consent are prosecutable. The right to refuse a camera, a watch, or a wearable that tattles is foundational.

- Children as civic persons: Each child has an independent advocate with the power to intervene. By twelve, a funded “Field Year” program offers short residencies in at least three other places. When a child says “I need out,” a Children’s Bench hears them quickly with a presumption in favor of their declared safety and will.

- Consent is a construction: For deep commitments—body changes, strict vows, joining rule-heavy communities—we practiced a clean ritual: opposing briefing, two sleeps of cooling-off, and recommitment in the presence of a neutral witness. Three spoken cues worked everywhere: Pause (stop and restate; everyone breathes), Witness (bring a neutral person and suspend all recordings except the witness’s log), Exit (get me away now; argue later).

- Signal boundaries: Every community posted a Sign Board stating which media, ads, and bots may cross its borders; others honored it. Within a community, each person set tighter filters if they wished.

- Cross-border harms face a public Edge Bench: Anything that flows across borders—toxins, carbon, botnets, targeted harassment—stood under a small court that published reasons and sent a Quiet Patrol with trauma shears and legal pads, not rifles. Their remit was to stop the harm and make it expensive to try again.

- Two-signature rule: Dams, lethal force, dangerous pathogens—any irreversible physical action—required two named humans to turn two keys. Logs were legible to the people they affected.

- No machine commands force: Learning systems advised, optimized, and predicted within fences. Sovereignty stayed human. The only legitimate force defended the right to step away and the Charter itself.

- Repair and redress: Where historical policies delivered concentrated benefits to a few and lasting damage to many, an open Reparation Ledger measured and funded repair through community trusts—homes, clinics, scholarships and apprenticeships—steered by those directly affected.

- Accessibility as default: Designs and services took the most demanding body as the starting point—eyes that do not see, legs that do not climb, minds that focus differently—because everyone needs these accommodations sometimes.

The floor was not cash in a bank app. It was a kit you could build an ordinary life on: a small, clean, quiet home with filtered air and a lock; water, light, and a data link cheap enough that you never had to choose between dinner and the bill; a clinic where a nurse knows your name, a generalist who looks you in the eyes, and a night-rounds micro-lab cart; a Kindline that answers in two rings with a listener in your language; a lawyer who will explain your options until you can say them back; baseline compute you control.

We paid for the floor with the value our togetherness creates, captured directly. Location is valuable because of neighbors and infrastructure. We stopped pretending appreciation was genius. Sites were leased for the ground underneath them, not the walls. Spectrum, water draw, mineral rights, orbital slots, quiet skies, sea lanes, and carbon use were leased in rolling, open auctions. Training runs above a published compute threshold came with licensing, safety audits, and a levy. Revenues from these commons rents and public stakes in grids and rail rights-of-way funded the floor and a quarterly share—a dividend from wealth none of us built alone.

Wards you can read

We gave up hunting for one perfect city. We built Wards: neighborhoods, guilds, cross-town co-ops, rural districts, and mostly-online clubs-with-hostels that lived under charters you could read on a door before you crossed a threshold. Sign Boards at the edge of each Ward declared what came in and what did not: “No ad targeting after 9 p.m.,” “Rear courtyards quiet,” “Drones only with beacons,” “Sunday processions; all welcome; don’t film without consent,” “Consent ceremonies on Fridays.”

When cousins visited, we showed them:

- Maple Stair: no leaf blowers; handwork an hour a day in school; buses by request. Visitors got loaner dumbphones that could call the Kindline and Green Door, but not much else. Twice a week a string band tuned under a sugar maple while jelly jars glowed on shepherd’s hooks.

- Tideweave: kin by choice as normal; co-parents in fours and fives, aunties in pairs, rings and V’s done without shame. Consent rituals were taught like dances; teenagers practiced saying no and hearing it until it stopped feeling awkward. A fertility clinic shared a wall with a dock where couples in sashes spun under lanterns mid-summer and mid-winter.

- Pulse Quarter: opt-in data for public goods. Volunteers donated lifelogs to civic Keepers. Trash routes shifted by noon; donuts followed endocrinology, not rumor. Visitors wore bracelets that negotiated what counted toward the day’s models.

- Still Vale: monastic quiet. Dawn bells. Meat rare and blessed. Donations in ink by hand. “We absorb; we don’t argue,” their Sign Board read. Their guesthouse filled with people who came to hush.

- Diagram Lane: speech as choreography. Question, restate your opponent’s point fairly, respond, check, iterate. Meeting minutes read like proof class with jokes. At the door, a five-minute test: listen and restate without fixing.

Joining was as simple as registering a lease. On arrival you lived under guest defaults for a season. You tried the human weather without swearing oaths you couldn’t unwind. Your health record, pension contributions, course credits, portability allowance, and dividend traveled with you on Carry Lines—open standards every bank, clinic, school, and Ward had to speak. If you stayed, you paid a Bridge Share—ten percent of income your first year in a new Ward, tapering to three by year three—to keep generous places generous.

Three times a year, trains ran late and cheap for Switch Weeks. Children brought back borrowed uniforms and new badges from places they had tried. Yellow tables in commonhouses said “Sit here if you don’t know anyone.” Farewell cake was not betrayal. Arrivals were not threats. With exits cheap and legible, Wards could sharpen into themselves without turning cultish.

Stewards, consent, and clarity

Most kitchens had a little beige box with a soft amber light. That was your Hearth—a counsel with fiduciary duties like a lawyer. A Hearth owed you care, loyalty, and confidentiality. It explained its recommendations in words you could repeat and labeled its guesses. It could not update itself without your clear yes. If it lied or sold you, you could disbar it and sue.

Tied to your Hearth was a Consent Ledger: a legible list of consequential yeses you had given. Share sleep metrics with this clinic team for three months. Accept this contract with the noncompete struck. Try a reversible overlay for four hours to learn to lathe. You could set hard lines—never donate to politics; never share my location across a Ward border; never accept pay below X unless I tap twice—and the Hearth enforced them.

Foggy days had a Clarity Wheel. Claims in your environment sprouted little ribbons: likely, contested, unknown. Tap to see sources, counterclaims, and a tiny calibration history tuned to how often this columnist or that agency learned from being wrong. Turn it down and nothing nagged. The Wheel never edited your memories unless you asked; it never whispered under your thoughts.

Influence wore stamps. Any paid attempt to change your behavior had to declare who was paying, what they hoped you would do, why they thought it would work, and how often that strategy had worked before. You could block entire classes—no diet pitches; no get-rich tokens—once, in a setting. Gate Filters at Ward borders enforced Ward rules on what could enter. A Night Truce banned mass-targeted persuasive AIs overnight.

Proof, pilots, and receipts you can follow on a bus

We remembered how to argue by making proof a public craft. The Proof Office—three floors above the transit desk and across from the labs—filed three-sentence briefs on public actions and longer reports with claim tickets under every assertion. Ad money never touched them; an endowment and a code did: no adjectives without numbers or quotes; corrections printed in the same slot as the original line.

Big choices passed through three rooms everyone knew:

- The Sim Hall, where any plan met models that did not love it. Decision markets paid calibrated odds on the bad and the good. You could see what might break if you pulled and what might hold if you pushed.

- The Civic Steps, an amphitheater where people spoke in metaphors tied to budgets and timelines. Words still thundered. So did numbers.

- Trial Lanes, where reversible pilots ran by default. Could your lunch plan feed two cafeterias well for a quarter? Could your delivery rules cut midnight noise and crashes? Win two out of three—model, assembly, pilot—you got a season to implement. Keep promises, get another. Break something, hand the wheel to the next team.

When regulators said “too slow,” the Sandbox Office next door ran safe experiments with independent ethics monitors and public metrics. We shipped adaptive trials. Law came in modules: jurisdictions adopted Protocol Code like open software and pushed patches through Open Pilots before anything entered the core. A Forge—a public option with teeth—stood up “good enough” competitors when any service turned into a bottleneck on purpose.

Bargains scaled too. A Deal Weave underpinned all this: a common bargaining engine where your Hearth and mine could search for win-wins. A co-op of small landlords buying a maintenance plan to keep boilers warm. A watershed compact across counties. A barter among theaters, libraries, and bus agencies. The Weave logged offers, counterfactuals, and fairness metrics in ledgers courts accepted like lab reports.

Health as instruments and dignity

We treated medicine like engineering: measurement, iteration, outcomes.

A nurse with a rolling cart made night rounds to draw a finger’s worth of blood and run a couple hundred markers. Bounded AIs flagged silent problems—sepsis in its first hour, a drug interaction about to bite—early enough to matter. Mortality fell. Not magic; clocks and checklists.

Robot-run labs handled logistics, version control on cell lines, and bookkeeping. AI research associates proposed experiment trees; humans set goals and vetoed leaps. We raised the hit rate of ideas and slashed the cost of being wrong.

Defense against outbreaks felt like fire codes. Pre-authorized mRNA and protein updates shipped quarterly. Wastewater and cough-audio networks watched for sparks. Standing contracts spun up manufacturing before outrage peaked. Time from variant of concern to shots in arms stayed under sixty days, rich towns or poor.

Cancer moved earlier. Ultrasensitive blood tests and breath analyzers pulled some tumors into stage I where surgeons and targeted drugs win. In high-risk patients, chemopreventives kept precancers on their back foot. Mortality in the big killers halved.

Mental health finally got resources matching its weight. Private coach AIs handled the 80% that is practice and reflection under bright labels and strict fences; clinicians handled the hard 20%. Psychedelic-assisted therapy became routine where warranted. Noninvasive brain stimulation complemented drugs. Suicide and overdose dropped every year. Addiction care moved from morality play to maintenance: medications first, follow-up that actually followed, slow-release antagonists as routine as seatbelts.

We paid for what worked. Regulators published risk thresholds in advance and rewarded anyone who cleared them with adaptive trials and registries. Hospitals posted wait times and error rates on the front page. We bought healthy years per dollar and weeks of delay avoided.

Work, craft, and time

With the floor firm, work got more human. Copilots cleared drudgery. Four-day weeks normalized without output falling. Guilds revived: bread with velvet crumb, welds like calligraphy, augmented choirs, clean-room satellite parts. Licenses hung off competence; insurers and portable reputation did more than seat-time ever did.

Three currencies flowed:

- Care Hours: time banked when you taught, mentored, or cared for elders or kids, redeemable later for care, apprenticeship, or a sabbatical.

- Commons Notes: the Commons Board listed things we needed—sea walls, sewer sensors, lullabies—and people funded and joined them. Notes were reputation, not equity. Show your notes, get better invitations.

- Money, taxed differently in different Wards. A small value-added tax at the center funded Green Doors, health, and audits. Nasty, necessary jobs paid nuisance premiums and got robotics grants. If a job couldn’t hire, wages rose, shifts shortened, or machines rolled in.

Belonging, built

“No one alone” wasn’t a slogan; it was an architecture. The Kindline answered in two rings with a listener in your language. Some listeners were human; some were synthetic social workers labeled as such. The handoff was always consented.

Commonhouses anchored clusters of two or three dozen households sharing tools, dinners, and weekly yellow-table nights for newcomers. Craft and care guilds offered a second ring. Cross-cutting clubs—public swims, folk dances with phones left in a basket, tabletop nights—offered a third ring where you could leave early without shame.

Matchmaking became a craft again. Brokers were paid on outcomes months or years later: a friendship that held, a family that grew, a company that didn’t grind itself to bone. Attraction counts; dignity doesn’t deny bodies. Swipe casinos that monetized loneliness lost their air.

Attention was treated like water: essential and dangerous in floods. Devices carried Attention Passes you could relax channel by channel. Feeds published their chemistry: novelty ratios, bot shares, calibrated fiction rates. You could inhale anything; you could not be dragged.

Intelligence within fences

Our tools outgrew a single head’s grasp. We gave them chores within fences. We called them Keepers: narrow intelligences with charters, small autonomy budgets, and black-box recorders. The Water Keeper tuned pumps and cut deals with the grid to minimize floods under environmental constraints. The Learning Keeper scheduled apprenticeships to maximize competence and joy under fairness constraints. The Attention Keeper enforced Passes and published feed chemistry.

Every Keeper had to:

- State goals in plain words and formal math.

- Keep a causality diary: which inputs moved which outputs, with names legally redacted.

- Cooperate with a Warden, a rival model trained to predict and call out proxy gaming. Citizens picked Wardens yearly.

- Spend a Touch Budget: the closer a prediction came to a person, the more scarce currency it cost. Running buses ahead of rain was cheap. Personal life-choice nudges were impossible.

- Accept that a Citizen Panel could yank autonomy to zero and force human-in-the-loop.

- Obey the two-signature rule. Any instruction that could cause permanent harm needed two humans to agree and left an audit trail.

Compute above a threshold got licensed like hazardous material. Levies funded audits and Red Team Houses that broke deployed systems on purpose and published the cracks. Capability labs sat far from deployment shops, legally and physically. Once a week most Keepers observed a Quiet Cycle: explain mode only, so drift stayed visible and human hands stayed practiced.

Matter and mind you can trust

Ambient helpers—filters that strip allergens, micro-fabricators that knit vessels in surgery—wore presence beacons and consent windows. Logs were readable to ordinary eyes. A big red lanyard cut power and told the Ward. Wards adopted an Open Matter Code: which assemblers were allowed, what counted as contraband. Designs were open with emergency degrade modes triggered by hitting an obvious red tile five times. Rivers, prairies, and reefs had ombuds paid to complain at the Edge Bench when needed.

Your mind was sanctuary. Devices that touched it lived behind a Capsule you could lock with a passphrase and your palm. Changing how you felt left a Self-Log with date, context, consent, and a one-tap return if you decided this was not you. Most changes were reversible by design; those that weren’t required permission from a future you after a cooling-off period, with regret insurance to help you undo or adapt.

Some used Modes like tools: two hours of geometry taste to help a niece; a week of patience to apprentice at a stone yard; an evening’s vocabulary to talk with a vintner. All Modes were red-teamed in public. Joy flares existed with spotters and aftercare like scuba. Multiplicity had etiquette: you could fork yourself for a season to try roles or places; a private ledger proved to you your other selves were solvent; you didn’t quiz strangers about their forks; merges came with counselors to prevent identity injury.

Copying your mind onto silicon was a personhood transition, not a loophole. A retired judge read the disclaimers aloud. If you stepped, you and your copy held separate rights and responsibilities. No one talked you into vanishing yourself on a bad week. Continuity law kept minds citizens across substrates with slow defaults and required witnesses.

Cities that say yes, coasts that breathe

Housing stopped being a lottery. Streetbooks of mid-rise courtyards, small apartments over shops, and tree-shaded streets met daylight, noise, and energy standards; if you built to the book, you got a permit in weeks. Accessory dwellings were legal by default. Basements became safe with pre-approved egress kits. Facade factories made beauty cheap.

Freight robots and soft-tired vans did most deliveries at night under low lights. Days belonged to feet, bikes, strollers, and buses that came every five minutes because lanes were clear and signals were tuned. Downtowns traded ghost offices for schools, labs, and homes through retrofit codes that didn’t make every building a bespoke fight.

We built narrower cities and wilder perimeters. Belts of marsh and prairie soaked floods and exhaled cool air across hot roofs. Power abundance arrived in layers: town-sized fission modules slipped into retired coal plants using their hookups and workforces; closed-loop geothermal stepped up with millimeter-wave drills; perovskite sheets draped roofs; iron-air and sodium batteries bridged long nights; buses and warehouses became batteries with fair trading rules. Desalination and district cooling piggybacked on noon surpluses, turning midday sun into sweet water and shade.

Low orbit we kept as parkland. If you did a year of boring civic chores—the unglamorous work that keeps a society sane—you could win a lottery for two weightless minutes looking down at the seams between continents. It was not a playground for wealth. It was a reminder: some gravities we can choose.

Borders and peace

Free societies coordinated on openness and restraint. Chip and tool supply chains were secured and sometimes denied to regimes that flattened dissent. The alliance overdelivered on carrots because sticks were real: open education AIs that slid past censors, health packages with no strings, satellite links hard to jam.

Militaries hard-coded a ban on fully autonomous lethal action. Verification mattered. Quietly, the ability to impose costs on violators without collateral damage kept peace. Transparency tech—authenticated imagery, satellite constellations, analysis AIs—made it harder to hide massacres and easier to rally will when lines were crossed. Information operations did not devolve into spam wars because the best work made truth cheap and censorship expensive.

Worlds you can leave

Virtual and mixed worlds were built like ocean ships: with crew, charters, and tested lifeboats. Each world posted a Risk Budget you set at entry. Zero meant nothing hurt and no one lied. Higher budgets added loss and surprise and still kept an Exit word you could find with eyes shut. A Consent Ledger recorded memory edits with two keys: yours and your witness’s. A Curtain Rope let actors drop character; you got the belaying pin that took you into daylight.

Two days in the Republic

A nurse in a river city wakes to a diffuse light. Her Hearth has won an appeal on a denied bundle of care; funds land. The clinic’s micro-lab pings a cough pattern. A doctor calls before panic can seed. Notes write themselves from their real words, subject to the nurse’s edits. Her shift has fewer patients and more minutes per person.

After work she bikes to the library’s maker hall to help teenagers bend ash into a canoe with steam. At dusk she walks two blocks to the Civic Steps. On the docket: roofing the market with solar louvers. She slides the “summer heat” bar on a model; the projection of asthma ER visits drops. She turns her Clarity Wheel to medium. Dissent is one tap away.

Across town an elder takes a coat and ambles to Charter Row, a floor where Wards keep shopfronts. In one window, a jazz-heavy place offers midnight practice rooms free to teenagers. Next door, a devout place invites people to a lantern walk that keeps screens dark for a night. Down the hall a slow county asks for two new midwives in exchange for a cottage on the edge of their wild belt. He adds a third place to his favorites. If he moves during Switch Week, a train will come, and a yellow table will be set at his destination.

At a corner a woman leans into a Green Door and says out loud the thing she hasn’t admitted even in her head. The plan that unspools includes childcare credits for two weekends so her sister can help, a locksmith, a temporary kitchen job three neighborhoods away, a filing to retrieve her identification, and a copy of her Consent Ledger that will let her Hearth refuse any signature she hasn’t authorized in the scramble.

The road from here

No miracle was required. Fifteen years did most of the work. Early adopters picked levers with receipts, published thresholds in advance, and biased arguments toward “yes, if.” Anyone can start this sequence.

Years 1–3:

- Put clocks on permits. Pass deadlines for energy, housing, and transmission projects that clear published safety and carbon standards. Miss the clock, pay a fine that funds the office you clogged.

- Shift property tax to site leases with public ledgers. Use the rent to electrify clinics, stand up cold chains, and build small, good homes.

- Stand up a Proof Office: three reporters, no ads. Numbers under every claim; corrections in the same slot. Audit police stats, school test claims, and transit timetables first.

- Pass right-to-data laws. Put medical records, school credits, and pensions in citizens’ hands under open standards. Make it illegal to hold a life hostage behind a phone tree.

- Pilot Green Doors by braiding shelter budgets, public defenders, and transit. Map places someone can land overnight and the petty fees that trap them. Pay those fees. Staff panels with locals. Record outcomes without names.

- License frontier compute. Require registries, tripwires, provenance, and red-team reports. Prosecute negligence as negligence. Keep research that pushes edges physically and legally far from devices that help a person hide a bruise or plan a safe week.

- Publish streetbooks and outcome codes. Legalize accessory dwellings. Promise permit clocks tied to checklists.

- Move deliveries to the night on one corridor. Measure collisions and noise and publish both. Use proven benefits to trade for daytime bus and bike lanes.

- Get one-to-one tutors to the lowest-scoring schools with teacher co-training. Give every learner a Hearth through the library if their family can’t afford one. Publish results monthly.

- Open Embassies: glass-front rooms with coffee and cots near transit. Put the Green Door number on bus shelters. Publish response times.

Years 4–7:

- Install long-duration storage. Convert bus depots and warehouses into grid buffers with clean payment rules.

- Approve the first tranche of small reactors on retired coal sites using standardized designs and training pipelines; keep safety metrics public. Expand closed-loop geothermal with permit clocks and profit-sharing.

- Halve time-to-approval for consequential therapies with adaptive trials and registries. Normalize outcome-based payments. Post hospital wait times and error rates.

- Eliminate malaria in at least one multi-country region with new vaccines, long-acting preventives, and undramatic logistics. Cut TB by half with breath tests and short courses.

- Convert a third of stagnant office space to housing and schools through pre-approved retrofit codes.

- Expand competency-based licensing. Let nurses and pharmacists work to the top of their training. Pay community health workers cash for coverage targets with dashboards the public can see.

- Institute commons rents and a small compute levy; launch a modest quarterly dividend. Seed Reparation Ledgers where harm is measured and repair has a plan.

- Pilot Field Years in two states. Adopt the consent ritual in hospitals, schools, and faith communities. Print Pause—Witness—Exit on pocket cards.

Years 8–12:

- Reach power-sector emissions 70% below baseline. Bring retail electricity under seven cents per kilowatt-hour and off-peak industrial under three. Keep outage minutes visible and falling.

- Cut cancer mortality in half; reduce suicide and overdose by half; add five healthy years to the median life.

- Finish global clinic electrification; connect every clinic to cold chain and internet. End malaria transmission in most of the world; bring TB under one per million in several regions.

- Normalize four-day weeks. Grow guilds as copilots saturate.

- Entrench decision markets and Sim Halls into budgets. Reward accuracy. Publish agency track records.

- Build Gate Filters and negotiate a Night Truce against cross-border AI persuasion. Codify bans on autonomous killing with third-party verification.

- Pass continuity law so personhood follows minds across bodies with slow defaults and required witnesses.

At every step: copy what works, publish what fails where successes live, and change your mind in public. If a monopoly forms, stand up a Forge competitor. If an agency hides the ball, cut its Touch Budget. If a Ward slides cultish, make leaving cheaper and louder.

Why this holds

It is good because it makes harm walk through doors that leave fingerprints and makes help as routine as turning a key. Exits are not treason. Children aren’t chattel. Your attention is hedged. Your mind is yours. Abundance routes down first as clinics, cold chains, and dividends before it buys anyone a mirror on the moon. Repair isn’t a speech; it’s a ledger with dates and deeds.

It is specific because it names rooms you can stand in and things you can touch: a Green Door with a linen pull and the words Make me a path; a Hearthlight with a soft amber glow; a Consent Ledger in your hand; streetbooks in city hall; a Proof Office up a flight of stairs; a delivery lane with soft tires and low lights; an Embassy with coffee and cots; Civic Steps where you can slide a bar on a model and watch ER visits change.

It is plausible because most beams exist already: nurses run night labs; cryptographic logs sit on judges’ benches; deep geothermal drills holes; modular reactors sit on regulators’ desks; perovskite sheets cling to roofs; tutors shrink gaps; streetbooks raise beautiful blocks fast; forecast markets work inside cautious agencies that tried; attention passports exist in pilots; two-person rules save planes and would save politics. Permit clocks are law in some places. Right-to-data statutes are spreading. Quiet, boring excellence compounds.

This is not a world without floods or divorces. There are relapses. There are people determined to make other people small. The difference is that you can say “Exit,” and a van shows up. You can turn a wheel and turn down the blare. You can walk to Charter Row and ask rude questions of places that live differently without anyone clamping a hand on your mouth.

You might see a flyer on a corkboard: Friday dumplings at the commonhouse. If you don’t know anyone, sit at the yellow table. Above you a slow light crosses. Someone says its name like a cousin. In the morning, the Proof Office posts a paragraph about the micro-bonds that bought the fourth sail. You pour coffee. The ledger balances. The Kindline answers. The coin turns and the door opens. The floor holds. And the sky stays carefully open to anyone who asks out loud and means it.
