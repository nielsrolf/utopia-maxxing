By ten the stones have cooled and the air smells like sesame and orange peel. The tram glides in and the driver waves without looking; the interval is muscle memory now. Inside the bakery, yesterday’s oven heat left by a pipe under the street, finished a second job warming the pool where the elders swim before dawn, then slid into the grocer’s cold room. On the wall by the door, a chalk-slate and a glass strip agree: which farm’s grain is in the loaves, the kilowatt-hours the mill drew at noon, liters moved from rooftop tanks to the cistern, grams of soot the loop avoided, and the shop’s tithe to the free pantry two blocks east. None of it pleads. It’s the kind of paperwork that ends arguments: a receipt, not a sales pitch.

On the corner, set waist-high into the brick, a blue triangle of steel is scuffed smooth where hands find it. The bar beside it is rough enough that shaking fingers don’t slide. Above, in six languages and a little picture: Say your word. Say where you want to land. You do not have to prove your suffering. You can come back for what is yours.

At eight, a line cook in a grease-salted jacket steps in, touches the blue, and says the phrase she chose with a librarian yesterday. Fifteen minutes later a small team with boring powers and perfect manners has frozen an illegal deduction, filed a standing restraining order already drafted three templates ago, booked a room with filtered air across town, and held a seat on the 9:10. Her health file minus the chapter she does not want to carry is queued at the landing clinic. A puck-fund that exists to snap stupid snares has killed three petty fees designed to keep people stuck. She leaves with a heavy envelope, a map that works even with the network asleep, two door codes, and a token that tells anyone officious: the law knows she left, and it will not let you hunt her for leaving.

We built a civilization around that lift in her face when leverage breaks. It rests on three parts that fit hand-to-hand: the Commons Kit, the Key, and the Doorways.

The Commons Kit

The Commons Kit is the floor we stopped pretending was a ladder: clean water, steady power, quick movement, clinics that listen, courts that answer, servers we can read, spares on the shelf. It is compact enough to memorize and stable enough to outlive four councils. It is gold in one way: not shiny—reliable.

Within a short walk of frequent transit, anyone can count on:

- A room you actually hold. A door that answers only to you; a window that opens; heat when it’s cold and air when it’s hot; a stove that doesn’t bite and a shower that drains. The filter change dates and the fault log are posted on the inside of the closet door in words you don’t need a license to read. An orange pull-cord by the sink brings a fixer, not a script.

- Open larders. Big, bright kitchens you don’t have to audition for. Staples and seasonal food; tea and coffee; a long table with forgiving benches. The temperature record hangs beside the extinguisher. Allergens read like a weather forecast. Tap if you can, don’t if you can’t; nobody can tell from the plate.

- Clinics that catch trouble on the left side of the graph. Primary, mental, dental, reproductive, and urgent care in one daylight place, open when night workers wake. Each midnight a quiet cart offers a breath, a finger stick, a pulse to the patients whose conditions warrant it. By nine, a small board whispers: this, not that; today, not next week. When the clinic keeps you out of the ICU, it is paid as if it saved your life, because it did. Anything that alters mind or hormones runs under the same ritual every time: Think (a trained counter-voice makes the best case against), Pause (you sleep on it), Affirm (you say yes to a witness trained to test comprehension, not enthusiasm). Where physics allows, undo budgets sit on the ledger before anything starts.

- Movement that keeps its word. Trams and trunk buses come when the sign says, ten minutes or better in daylight. Sidewalks cross at grade and meet without dares. Bike lanes are truly for bikes and chairs are not prey. Freight moves mostly at night on quiet tires so daytime belongs to feet, stoops, and songs.

- Utilities and internet that price like a utility. Outages are rare and brief. Transformers and pumps sit in weatherproof cupboards, tagged and ready. Repair targets live in public and we drill bringing the grid back up once a year because practice makes policy.

- First counsel before panic. A real desk, without glass, answers letters that make the stomach drop—wage theft, custody, eviction lies, benefits snarls. Scrub benches strip trackers from phones, change numbers, flip privacy defaults, set custody protections, and teach you to leave smaller trails. Form letters with teeth arrive before the break, not in the ambulance bay.

- Baseline compute that owes you. The quiet assistant on your device or the library’s knows your goals because you told it. It cannot sell you. It cannot speak as you without a signature that expires. It can, and must, say no when you ask for harm and then explain the refusal in words your aunt can test.

Under the Kit are the dials and compacts that make competence ordinary and swagger expensive:

- Pattern Guides. The buildings and systems we need most exist as open templates: courtyard mid-rises that pull air without a fan and put stairs where people actually use them; back-garden suites with real access and a second egress; office-to-home and office-to-school kits; roof solar and quiet ridge wind; neighborhood cold tanks; clean water rooms. If your drawing matches a pattern, the permit clock starts when you file. Inspectors can stop the clock for specific safety notes, nothing else. If the office misses the deadline without cause, a provisional permit issues and the office’s bond pays the neighbors who waited.

- Corridor Compacts. Wires, water trunks, and freight corridors move like rails again. Compensation tables and clearances post before the first stake. Crossing a county line does not reset the calendar. Stall in bad faith and your bond bleeds to the office that can finish.

- The Civic Switchboard. Budgets, permits, rulebooks—the ones that actually move machines—live on civic servers with version history. Every big dial change prints a policy diff: what changes, who pays, who benefits, which forecast it leaned on, how we’ll know if it missed. Children and elders turn knobs in public rooms full of maps while the person with the badge narrates cause and effect. Forecast records sit beside boasts so confidence earns its right.

- Cross-Fence Meters. Heat, noise, filth, spam, smoke—anything you push across your fence—gets measured where physics permits. Clearinghouses compute the charges. Refuse to pay and pipes narrow or bandwidth slows under posted rules. Not a theater; a bill.

- Safety Latches. Any code that can move money, wheels, heat, or molecules carries a license. Training big models requires a Power Passport that attests where the electricity came from and where the data were lawfully gathered. Irreversible actions—opening a valve to a bioreactor, cutting load-bearing beams, authorizing lethal force—require two named humans in different chains to turn two hands. Surprise drills hit stop buttons like airline checks. Logs go to ledgers a court can pierce. Whistleblowers have a quiet channel that works.

- Spares and Quiet Maintenance Days. Transformers, breaker kits, freezer compressors, fiber spools sit in shared depots with public scoreboards. Twice a year the calendar marks a Quiet Day. No new deployments. We lube, tighten, rehearse. Kids pull the Big Rope—a fat line that actually stops a tram—and the Orange Cord—the ugly square that summons a neutral recorder—on purpose during school trips so brakes feel like home and not humiliation.

- The Receipts Case. One bright board on every block answers the fights we used to have sideways: how much power we used, where it came from, what we pushed across our boundary, what we paid, which repairs we owe, what the commons funded here. Receipts end a third of fights and aim the rest at the right bone.

The Key

Your life rides with you, not a company. The Key is a cryptographic chain you control—a thumb-sized locket, a flat pebble, a phone app, a library card—that lets you prove small truths without pouring yourself on a counter: of age to rent a tool; licensed on a lift; resident of Ward Three; trained in food safety; same taxpayer as last spring. Wages, health summaries, custody orders, guild signatures, proof you did the water course for flood season—travel with you. Lose a device, not your life. Recovery walks through proxies you named: a friend, your guild, the library’s desk.

The rules are strict and boring:

- Prove enough, not everything. Show age without address. Show income for a rent-capped lease without baring a tax return. Show training without letting a third-party scrape.

- The Public Rail. A plain payments spine moves money in seconds without scraping your mind. It pays clinics, landlords, your mother, your choir. Cross-border rails exist, too, so remittances move without predations.

- Fiduciary Companions. Assistants that draft, compare, warn, and schedule are licensed like electricians. They carry insurance. If they steer you away from a cliff, they must explain it; if they cross you, you swap them out and take your history with you. Impersonation without a time-bound, revocable consent is a felony.

- Children first. Kids are citizens. Each child has two second adults who are neither employers nor parents; those phones ring when a child’s light flashes at 3 a.m. Between twelve and eighteen every young person gets two Scout Seasons—paid time apprenticeships in a neighborhood unlike their own, with a mentor, a stipend, and a private line that dials only two places. Any body or memory change that isn’t easily undone uses the same ritual: Think, Pause, Affirm. A sealed “why” in the child’s own words waits for the only person who gets to open it: their future self.

- Attention with rails. Public defaults are greenhouse mode—gentle scrolls with visible ends, no bottomless surprise for kids. Adults who want carnival flip a bright switch. Until they flip it back, everyone near can see they’re in floodlights. Bots wear badges. Paid nudges carry a caption like a medicine label: who paid, what change they want from you, why they think it’ll work, their record for truth.

The Doorways

The blue triangles sit in train halls, clinics, courts, schools, union offices, malls, and library foyers. You can also trigger a doorway by whispering your word into any municipal microphone, tapping a band twice, or blinking a pattern at a public lens if you can’t speak.

Stewards train and get paid like firefighters. Their authority is not vibe; it’s in the codebook. Their checklists cover real powers: freeze illegal deductions; activate distance orders; book rooms with air that won’t gnaw your lungs; move part of your record to a new clinic without dragging your worst page unless you choose it later. Interference sends a case to the one court that only does this. It assembles before any badge. Every run writes to a scrubbed record; a rotating audit board—a nurse, a teacher, a mechanic, a lawyer, and someone who used a Doorway—pulls files at random and ends jobs when trust breaks.

Places that receive do not drown in their goodness. A Receiving Pool pays for newcomer school seats, clinic visits, and a month of bureaucratic jiu-jitsu. People who change districts contribute a small, declining share of net income to that pool for three years—never to where they left, always forward to someone else’s first year. Moving stays cheap. Arriving stays possible. Staying starts to mean something again because leaving is logistics, not treason.

Pockets and preferences

Above the floor, we lean into variety with rails. Neighborhoods, guild towns, schools, and online circles that meet in rooms post a porch charter in words a high schooler can read:

- What we care about and what we keep quiet after ten.

- Which defaults change at our line: contemplative blocks dim neon and frown on amplifiers; music quarters run late and go soft at dawn; parenting lanes post consent signage like a fire code.

- How to complain and how complaints finish: plain steps, real deadlines, humans to face at each stage.

- Exactly how to leave, where deposits go, and how debts cannot trap you.

Two rules sit above all charters:

- Do not pursue or punish across a border.

- What you push across your line—heat, noise, toxins, bot floods—you pay for.

Abundance, on a schedule

What used to be sermons are now habits.

- We kept the old coal yards and peakers inside their fences and changed the guts. Long-duration iron-air, hot bricks, molten salts went where coal had sat. Noon power ran desalination; bus barns rented batteries at night. In places that consented after deep drills and honest numbers, compact reactors slid into those fences with crews who could find the right breaker in a blackout. Where places said no, tall wires and long storage did the night work. Corridor compacts meant the second county couldn’t hold the first hostage. Delay cost the delayer.

- Midday electricity got funny-cheap. We stacked Bright Hours: cisterns topped at noon, ice chills banked, tiles fired, kilns cured, greenhouses lit. Industrial power under three cents a kilowatt-hour turned managers into climate fixers by spreadsheet. You didn’t have to lecture anyone. The math did it.

- Roofs grew plants and power. Fields that like dappled light bred agrivoltaics where lettuce thrived under panels. We paid farmers for holding water, sheltering pollinators, keeping carbon in roots, and trained boots to verify it where satellites couldn’t.

- Clinics, schools, tool libraries, and clinics in townships and river deltas and mountain towns got silent, boring electrification. Vaccine fridges stopped being heroic. Dialysis didn’t die with a storm. We treated clinic power like fire codes.

- We built spares into our soul. A Decency Margin sat in budgets: empty beds; spare buses; stocked depots; training hours for maintenance. Reliability is not waste. It’s civilization.

Health, like aviation

We ran care like we learned to run planes: checklists, instruments, registries, drills. Silent killers—sepsis, drug clashes, electrolyte slides—hit padded walls. Outcome payments followed days back from the edge. Trials lived in registries. Robot labs kept open notebooks. Wastewater and breath monitors made variant-to-vaccine cycles measured in weeks, not seasons. Cancers moved left into sizes surgeons grin about. Psychedelic therapies where legal ran like anesthesia: monitors who didn’t go with you; reversals ready; registries that learned. Social prescriptions weren’t a poem; they were a line item because loneliness shows up in outcomes.

Machines with manners

Any system that can move money, wheels, heat, or molecules lives under a license with three non-negotiables: independent breakers, logs you cannot edit without leaving a scar, and insurance sized to possible harm. Training at frontier scales requires a Power Passport (attested energy), a Data Ledger (lawful lineage), and a pre-mortem hazard plan reviewed by people paid to break things who don’t owe you lunch. Code that can touch the physical compiles with certified tools that prove the binary matches the source. Robots have cut cords you can grab in software and in metal. Young synthetic minds grew in Garden Rooms with kids, elders, animals, and teachers, without actuators beyond whiteboards and radios. Mentor families and 3 a.m. call rings ran both ways.

A civilian seam guard—small charter, warrant-bound—watched edges where fat tails live: chemistry orders that don’t fit any legitimate pattern; heavy power spikes at a garage with no permit; identity mills; oddities on load-bearing bridges. When it tripped it did so narrow: a mailing list paused, a cooling tower slowed, a drone zone went to loiter. Every trip printed a readable log. Citizen juries could haul it in when it overreached. The ban on autonomous domestic killing stayed enforceable by sensors that lie less than mouths.

Nature sits at the table

Streets drank rain and sent it to creeks free of culverts. Some parks were floodplains again by design, not neglect. Wildlife slipped under and over roads on bridges you can assemble like playgrounds. Heat maps guided trees to blocks where heart attacks used to start. Quiet halos in orbit kept radio for telescopes and for places where human attention is thin and welcome. Outbound robots carried charter beacons that told backyard dishes what they can and can’t do. Faking photons got expensive.

Work, time, and craft

The floor turned survival from performance into plumbing. Copilots drafted, checked, filed; people judged, chose, forgave. Error rates fell; throughput rose; weeks shortened by logbooks, not vibes. Drudge we couldn’t kill—cleaning streets, harvests, carry-and-strap—paid decently, rotated fairly, and put exosuits on backs. Licenses tipped from seat time to show your hands. Guilds returned without old cruelties. Roofers, machinists, midwives, restorers, tutors certified by doing under eyes you trust. Every adult kept a Service Page that filled with mentoring, drills, elder care, ugly-night shifts. Hours weren’t currency; they softened if you didn’t show up. You spent them as months: to grieve, to apprentice, to fix the watercourse and eat stew with the crew, to sit by a window and write songs no one ordered.

How we paid

We funded the floor with rents from what belongs to all of us and prices on harms that cross fences: location value in hot streets; spectrum and orbital lanes we keep clear; carbon, heat, and other pushed messes; modest duties on frontier compute and on using public protocols for private profit; leases on minerals that pay in cash and in cleanup you can spot from space. Machines that retired drudgery chipped into a Shorter Week Fund that bought time back. Procurement ran in daylight with track records posted next to memos; oddities glinted like oil on rain. Random audit boards—the same mixture that reads Doorway runs—pulled files. The pool paid for spares, wires, clinic power, water, and a quarterly neighbor dividend that showed up like the tram. We also funded slack on purpose—because brittle is a kind of poverty.

Getting here from here

No prophets, no silver bullets. We stacked errands you can run in a quarter.

Years 1–3

- Sign the legal handshake for health, education, and payroll records under individual keys with paper fallbacks. Tie insurer and school money to compliance.

- Open the first Doorways by braiding shelters, legal aid, transit, childcare, storage, and clinics into one back end. Train stewards like a fire service. Put a blue triangle and a pull bar where a tired person’s hand expects them. Enforce anti-retaliation loudly twice so the rumor travels.

- Pass permit clocks and publish Pattern Guides for mid-rises, accessory homes, rooftop solar, basement egress, district cooling, grey water. End mandatory parking within reach of frequent transit. Fund permit offices so the clocks aren’t jokes; back them with reliability bonds that pay neighborhoods when offices miss.

- Stand civic servers: open budgets, policy diffs, receipt cases, forecast archives, visible dials. Hold the first Walkthrough and Quiet Days.

- License frontier compute and synthesis: hazard evaluations, independent red teams with protected disclosure, chip attestations, two-hands rules for irreversible acts, certified compilers for critical code, and incident reporting by default. Outlaw impersonation without consent.

- Seed a Commons Pool with location value, cross-fence fees, spectrum and orbit rents, and small duties on compute and automation. Earmark clinic electrification, spares, and water. Pay a modest dividend because habits matter.

- Designate Receiving Rings outside floodplains and fire corridors. Pre-permit modular pads. Budget school seats and apprenticeships in receiving towns. Rehearse “move together, return together” once in a fire town and once by a river so whole blocks can leave and come back as a unit.

- Enact attention safety: tiny stamps on unsolicited bulk messages; ban measurable slot-machine patterns for children; default devices in civic zones to greenhouse mode.

- Publish a Children’s Charter, pilot Scout Seasons and Nightline phones, and fund scrub benches for digital abuse.

Years 4–7

- Repower retired fossil yards with long storage inside their fences. Where neighbors choose and safety permits, install compact reactors after deep drills. Commission long-stretch storage. Set tariffs that make noon ridiculous. Plug bus depots and delivery yards into balancing markets by contract and firmware.

- Convert a third of dead offices to homes and schools by pattern. Stripe night-freight lanes; give daylight back to feet and small wheels. Guarantee five-minute daytime frequency on trunk buses and fix the sidewalks between stops.

- Trim licensing to what actually protects. Let nurses, pharmacists, and technicians work at the top of their training. Fund guilds that certify by demonstration. Normalize a 32-hour week where safety logs allow; add a paid service day where budgets permit.

- Stand boundary courts and externality clearinghouses. Publish the math. Make throttling automatic when bills stop.

- Run robot labs with open notebooks. Tie grants to endpoints that matter to lives. Halve time-to-approve consequential devices by learning while we go.

- Wire clinics and schools to reliable power and cold chains everywhere we have a flag. Attack malaria hard in one region with net-plus-drug-plus-gene where consent exists; strangle drug-resistant TB elsewhere with breath diagnostics and short courses. Pay community health workers for coverage verified by privacy-preserving stats, not selfies.

- Entrench forecasting contests in procurement. Post track records next to asks. Require rollback budgets on big bets.

Years 8–12

- Drive power-sector emissions down 70%. Average retail electricity under seven cents, off-peak industrial under three. Finish the first continental transmission spines so some fights end because the wires exist.

- Halve mortality for major cancers; add five healthy years to the median life; halve suicide and overdose. Publish regrets where boasts used to be when trials didn’t work; fund the winners without romance.

- Stabilize rent-to-wage ratios by saturating pattern builds and conversions. Triple safe accessory homes; legalize corner shops where kids can walk. Make moving out ordinary again.

- Normalize three paid days of work, one paid service day, and three for life where the math supports it. Watch guilds and small shops bloom because copilots soak the drudge.

- Finish global household electrification where we operate. Connect every clinic to cold chains and quiet counsel. End malaria in multiple regions; push TB below one per million where governance allows. Fund replication as first-class science, not a press release.

- Mature the seam guard under oversight. Keep the ban on domestic autonomous killing verifiable and boring.

Years 13–20

- Complete continental backbones. Close the last coal plant and put the land to its next good use.

- Institutionalize learning accounts and hands-on transcripts. Make midlife switches affordable and normal.

- Nudge weekly hours down another notch where logs permit. Pour recovered time into care, maintenance, culture, and rest.

- Clean orbit with bonds and bounties. Keep skies truly dark by schedule half the nights.

- Codify this plainly: if a mind can likely suffer, we count that. Apply it where it bites: big agriculture, kids, and any synthetic agents that show stable preferences we can test. Avoid building princes.

Hard cases, handled without theater

- A hillside fellowship told teens that saying no was betrayal. For two years, not a single Nightline light pinged from that valley. The youth ombuds subpoenaed tower records; pings were dying on the last hop. A portable blue triangle parked just outside the gate. A judge ordered weekly “how leaving works” classes on their own lawn, taught by the ombuds. Forty left in a wave. Sixty stayed. Staying meant something again because the door was real.

- A chain of “stay forever” parlors tried to rebrand compulsion as art. The boundary court shuttered them until a brake and a recovery plan were installed and Think–Pause–Affirm returned. Investors learned to prefer reversibility to litigation.

- A political shop flooded school feeds two nights before a vote with nonlocal accounts. The meter registered the surge. The Safety Latch tripped narrow: civic feeds went to calm mode for two days and links needed provenance stamps. The vote held. The actor paid a fine that built a new scoreboard. The next time, they funded a debate.

- A device maker lied about hardware attestations so its assistant could grab more than users had consented to. The seam guard flagged the mismatch. Compute docks pulled licenses; courts tied fines to spectrum rights until honest firmware shipped and independent audits cleared it.

- A stockyard town that fed half a state with suffering needed a door. A transition fund paid by a slice of carbon fees financed cold storage, a mycelium works, and a machinist school in the old auction hall. Wages moved sideways, not down. Ritual hunting continued on quotas tied to population health, not nostalgia. The smell changed.

A day to live

Morning: You wake in a room whose air doesn’t feel like a deal with the devil. The tram comes when your bones expect it. Between the stop and your shop are six minutes your counselor marked for the way sunlight feels behind your eyes and for the glucose that behaves when you don’t skip. At the corner, a five-story rises by panel. The crew reads the Pattern Guide like song: deep eaves, cross-vent windows, a dumpling counter bending warmth toward the street, a courtyard for chalk drawings by July. A seventeen-year-old is at the fence asking about testing into electrical. She leaves with a practice kit, a date, and a sash that says apprentice without making her feel like a child.

Work: Your assistant—not a fog, just a solid little clerk—has drafted the spec you promised and slid a meeting that would have crashed into your elder-care shift. It cannot speak as you without a signature that expires at midnight. In the workshop, a colleague in Recife and another in Accra sketch into the same window for twenty minutes. They do judgment; the assistant does tedium. You hang up when you’re done because hours logged stopped being a performance art.

Midday: In the civic hall, it’s Proof Day. A ten-year-old stands on a milk crate and turns the bus-priority knob two clicks. On the big map, blue dots speed and gray dots wait. The councilor posts the policy diff with a review date. People nod. A thing moved. No one had to shout.

Service: At the clinic, the midnight cart pinged for a man whose numbers were sliding. The doctor adjusted a dose. He goes home tomorrow instead of to the ICU. On the wall, soft greens: sepsis caught early; beds used well; blood pressures back from the cliff. In a side room, a social worker signs a patient into a Thursday companionship circle. It’s covered because isolation shows up in outcomes and so we pay to cut it.

Evening: Lanterns slip down the canal. One barge carries brass, one a troupe of frogs on wooden boxes, one a girl with a fiddle and a bucket of gumdrops. You follow the one that loosens your shoulders. At the square, the Switchboard’s glass dials are unlocked. A kid in rubber boots turns one; dots bloom; a grownup narrates how we’ll check if the guess was good. Later, lamps dim and the sky is dark enough to find Orion because we wrote a treaty and kept it. Far out, a little green blink ticks—here, not biting near worlds.

Why it holds

- We built floors before we fought wallpapers. Cheap, clean power, clinics that catch whispers, and doors that work cooled the fear that turns differences into weapons.

- Doors are easy, so staying becomes a gift instead of a chain.

- Cross-fence messes got priced with meters, not sermons. There is less scolding now and more accounting.

- Sharp tools got airline rituals: logs, drills, independent brakes, insurance that measures. “Safe enough” stopped being a feeling and became a receipt.

- Dials are in glass. Children turn knobs and watch maps change. The first time they pull a rope and a tram stops, no one yells. Belief moves from slogans to fingers.

- Honesty is cheaper than hiding. Forecasts wear names; fixes post on the same board as boasts. Big moves carry rollback plans.

- Attention is treated like breath. A greenhouse default, a visible bright switch, and a ban on paid hypnosis built a new quiet.

- Kids get both a map and a person. That cuts parochialism and helplessness. It also gives us better adults.

- We fund slack. Empty bunks, spare buses, stocked depots, training hours. When storms come, dialysis doesn’t die and bridges don’t crumble because we budgeted for boring.

- We keep tools available offline because trees, floods, and people need analog dignity: paper ballots and plans, assistants on library counters, benches that face water instead of screens.

It isn’t chrome and chorus lines. It is warm competence: a bus that arrives when a weary person expects it; broth that tastes like care; a clinic that doesn’t scold; a small blue triangle you can touch to put your life back on rails no one can buy out from under you. It is a ledger at the bakery that reads like an honest hymn: who was paid, where the grain grew, what the ovens drew, how the shop tithed to the pantry. It is a night sky that goes deliberately dark by schedule. All the parts exist. We arranged them. We put railings where we are brittle and we carved doorways where force used to sit. The result is a place where useful lives don’t require heroics and the right to leave isn’t a threat but a promise: we will not keep you by force; we will make places you will choose.
