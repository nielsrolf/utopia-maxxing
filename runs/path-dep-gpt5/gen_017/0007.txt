The morning smells of cardamom, hot bread, and a faint electric sweetness from the bricks in the laundry wall giving back last noon’s charge. The tram sighs in exactly when your wrist expects it. On the shelter’s glass, a pane the size of a cutting board doesn’t exhort you to be better; it just states what happened: wheat from three hours north milled last week, kilowatt‑hours spent at each step, where those electrons originated, wages and hours for the night shift, liters moved from yesterday’s storm into today’s cistern, grams of soot avoided, and the bakery’s portion sent to the neighborhood pantry. A small amber dot means the elevator two blocks over will draw on its battery at four when buses run fullest. It reads like the kind of receipt you don’t crumple—you keep it because it’s part of how the place runs.

Beside the flower stall is a waist‑high post, painted sun‑warm clay. A braided cord rests in a brass cup. Above it, words in clean, big letters:

Pull to go.
Two questions. Fifteen minutes.

A baker with flour up her arm tugged that cord last week. There was no performance—no sirens, no forms handed through tears. A steward in a soft jacket came with lawful teeth and practiced calm. They asked only: Are you safe for the next quarter hour? Do you want help now, or do you want to plan? Fifteen minutes later, the slot in the post clicked out a paper envelope and a pebble the size of a thumbnail with a copper vein. The envelope held a door code to a private room with air that didn’t set off her lungs, a restraining order gone live, a freeze on the illegal siphon chewing her pay, child care matched to a different shift, a clinic visit across the river with only the chapters she chose to carry, a transit pass that opened gates without asking questions, and a number someone answers when anyone tries to follow. The pebble—her Hearthkey—held the proofs she needed and no more: of age, licensed on a forklift, resident in this ward, same taxpayer as last spring. One sentence at the bottom said in ordinary words: She has left. Interfering starts a case and a bill.

Most of this world was built out from that feeling: doors that open without spectacle, a floor that is quietly strong, and brakes you can reach with your smallest finger when a machine, a habit, or a person rolls through your boundary.

We call the floor the Groundline. We carry the Hearthkey. We keep Threshold Rooms where anyone can change their story before breakfast. Around them, we put rails on competence and fees on what we push across our fences. We made plenty; we practiced stopping. It looks like this.

The Groundline

The Groundline is the mesh of basics everyone can rely on without performance: water and power, rooms and kitchens, clinics and counsel, roads and servers, spares and drills. It’s the anti‑heroics the place runs on.

Within a short walk of truth‑telling transit, you can count on:

- A door that obeys your key. A small private room with daylight, a real lock, heat when it’s cold and cool when it’s hot, a stove that doesn’t poison you, laundry that works without coins, a shower that drains. Maintenance schedules hang in human language. Tap a red tile and a human arrives with a toolkit, not a script.

- Kitchens that feed without auditions. Staple foods with real flavor—grains, beans, vegetables, fruit, eggs, oils, tea and coffee—served alongside chairs and an absence of humiliation. Allergens and temperatures read like the weather. Wages and hours sit on the wall, ordinary as napkins.

- Clinics that see trouble before it shouts. Primary, mental, dental, and urgent care share light and air. A quiet cart at night pulls a breath, a finger pulse, a drop, from people who opted in because their conditions warrant it. Mornings whisper: catch this today. Bills follow outcomes—days until steady, surgeries avoided—rather than poetry that punishes misfortune. Anything sharp—psychedelics, hormones, neurodevices—runs like anesthesia: monitors, reversals, cool‑down between idea and needle.

- Movement that keeps its word. Trams and buses come when signs say they will. Transfers respect your time. Sidewalks do not die into ditches. Protected lanes feel safe enough that you let a nine‑year‑old ride them. Quiet night freight keeps daylight streets for feet and small wheels.

- Utilities and the internet like rivers. Priced as utilities, not souvenirs. Outages are rare, brief, and practiced. Transformers, pumps, and fiber sit on shelves ready. Twice a year, we rehearse standing everything back up.

- First counsel before panic. A desk you can find handles the first letters that make your stomach drop: wage theft, eviction fakery, custody with a countdown, benefits snarled. Scrub teams remove stalkerware from phones at your table and reset numbers that danger learned.

- Child care like the mastery it is. Slots live near trains that come. Shifts match lives people actually work. Rooms are the kind your aunt would choose. Pay and training treat early care as the craft it is.

Under the Groundline sit the boring engines that make it work:

- Pattern Codes. The common buildings and systems exist as open kits: courtyard mid‑rises with cross‑ventilation and stairwells people use; shop‑houses with flats above; safe basement egress kits that don’t torch families; rooftop solar and neighborhood cold tanks; school retrofits with daylight and clean air. If your plan matches a pattern, the permit clock starts the day you file. A small bond sits with the office to pause for real safety. If the office misses without cause, a conditional permit issues while their bond pays neighbors who waited.

- Corridor Compacts. Transmission, water trunks, freight spines get negotiated once per corridor like railroads again. Compensation and clearances post before stakes. Crossing a county line doesn’t start a new calendar. Stall a line in bad faith and your bond bleeds to an office that can finish.

- Boundary Meters and Clearinghouses. Heat, noise, runoff, spam, bot floods—anything you push across a fence—gets measured where possible. Clearinghouses compute charges from posted formulas. Bills you can appeal land monthly. Refuse to pay and bandwidth narrows or pipes throttle under rules you could explain to a teenager. It’s not moral theater; it’s accounting.

- Safety Pairs. Any system that can move money, wheels, doses, heat, or molecules carries a license by capability, not brand. Irreversible acts demand two named humans from separate chains of responsibility to turn two different keys with a pause between them. Surprise drills prove brakes still bite. Binaries prove they match signed source. Logs sit in ledgers a judge can open with a warrant. Insurance is sized to the harm you could cause. Whistleblowers have channels that work.

- Spares and Null Weeks. Transformers, breakers, cold‑chain units, pumps, fiber, bolts—on shelves you can point to, funded by law. Twice a year, we put down new toys and oil what already carries us. It’s a holiday for mechanics.

- Counters and Policy Diffs. Each block keeps one honest board showing power used and where it came from, water moved, repairs due and done, wages paid where kitchens cook, and what we funded together. Every big decision prints a change note: what’s different, who benefits, who pays, which model it came from, when we’ll look again. Forecast contests run alongside, and bravado loses money when it’s wrong.

The Hearthkey

Your life rides with you. The Hearthkey is a cryptographic wallet under your control that can live on your phone, a card at the library, a locket around your neck, or with a trustee you chose. It proves crisp things without emptying your pockets onto someone else’s counter: that you are old enough to rent a tool; trained to use a lift; licensed to put hands on a panel; resident in a ward; the same taxpayer as last spring. Health summaries, wages, school credits, guild signatures, consent records, protective orders, kin contacts—travel with you. Lose a device, not your life. Recovery runs through people and institutions you named: a friend, a guild, a clinic, the library.

Rules are dull on purpose:

- Prove enough, not everything. Show age without address. Show training without trailing your browsing. Show income without issuing a biography.

- Common Pay. A public rail moves money in seconds without skimming your attention. It pays clinics, landlords, co‑ops, your choir. Small checks land in the right pocket every time.

- Fiduciary Companions. When your assistant drafts, compares, translates, or schedules, it owes you in law like a midwife or an engineer. It cannot impersonate you without a live signature that dies by dawn. It can tell you no when you ask it to harm you, and it must explain in words your aunt can test. If it leaks, its insurer pays today and its maker fixes in daylight.

- Reputation that moves. A welder carries three signatures. A nurse crosses a state line with a demonstration transcript, not a shrine. A barber from Lima studies sanitation for a day and opens shop. Children’s rights do not change by block.

- Consent with ribs. Big personal decisions run through Consider, Cool, Confirm: a trained counter‑arguer makes the best case against your plan; you sleep on it; you recommit in front of a neutral whose job is to test understanding, not sell. Where physics allows reversal, programs carry undo budgets. Where it doesn’t, a note—locked until you open it later—explains why you said yes then.

- Attention like a watershed. Feeds default to safe‑edge scrolling with visible ends; kids see no bottomless novelty. Adults can flip a bright toggle to carnival mode. While it’s on, everyone can see the glow, and paid persuasion must print its provenance: who funded it, what they want you to do, why they think it will work, their record for truth last year.

Threshold Rooms

The clay posts stand in station foyers, courts, clinics, schools, malls, union halls, and a few quiet places no map marks but every ninth grader can point to by May. You can also trigger a Threshold by speaking a word into a municipal microphone, tapping your bracelet twice, or blinking a pattern at a public lens if you can’t speak. Stewards are trained like firefighters and licensed like sheriffs. They carry statutory authority, not vibes, with checklists to freeze illegal payroll deductions, flip standing distance orders, book rooms with filtered air and locking doors, and move a chosen part of your record to a new clinic. Interference wakes a magistrate whose jurisdiction is only this and who assembles before uniforms. Each run logs with names masked. Rotating audit boards—a nurse, a teacher, a mechanic, a lawyer, and someone who needed a Threshold—pull files at random and end jobs when trust breaks.

Arrivals don’t sink receivers. Newcomers join a Landing Pool that pays for a month of counsel, a slot in a school, a clinic visit, and time to uncoil. People who change districts contribute a small, fading share of net income to that pool for three years—never back to where they left, always to someone else in their first year. In floodplains and fire corridors we run “move together; return together” so whole blocks shift intact and go home when it’s safe. In rural stretches, blue‑banded vans with satellite links run on odd hours because trouble ignores timetables.

Plenty with brakes

Midday electricity went from cheap to hilarious. We wrote “yes, if” into law and watched industrial managers become climate fixers by spreadsheet.

Retired coal sites kept their fences and switchyards and replaced their guts with iron‑air batteries that inhale and exhale rust, molten salts glowing in the dark, and water lifted and lowered where rock allowed. Where neighbors consented after honest drills, mid‑scale reactors sat inside old switchyards with licenses shaped like aircraft certification, not theater. Transmission crossed counties because Corridor Compacts posted clearances, compensation, and repair obligations for both sides. Noon bulges ran desalination, cured cement, baked tile, chilled district ice, dried grain, lit greenhouses. Bus depots and delivery fleets became grid assets with contracts and firmware. Microgrids made storms into inconveniences, not tragedies, even in low‑density places.

Farms got paid for rain held in soil, habitat restitched, pollinators fed, and carbon kept in roots using measurements sturdy enough to trust—boots, drones, aerials, labs that publish open notebooks. The shade under panels grew berries and lettuces that liked it. Wildlife crossed highways on prefab bridges installed like playgrounds.

Health learned aviation’s manners

Clinics and hospitals run on instruments, registries, drills. Sepsis, silent arrhythmias, electrolyte slides, drug clashes—once killers—now hit padded walls. Nightly micro‑panels mean mornings where you change a dose and shorten a stay. Clinics get paid for days avoided and function restored. Trials register before they run, aim at endpoints that matter to life, and post regrets alongside boasts. Wastewater and cough monitors loop to variant‑to‑vaccine in under sixty days because kits are stocked, chains cold, and contracts prewritten. Suicide and overdose slid because drug‑checking and long‑acting antagonists became boring, and loneliness landed in budgets with a person whose job was to notice.

Machines learned manners too

Any code that can move money, wheels, heat, dosages, molecules, or actuators carries a license by what it can do. Frontier training runs show compute passports—where the energy came from, which data were lawful, who holds the roll‑back keys, which tripwires watch—attested at the chip. Binaries prove they match the audited source. Model weights that can cause irreversible harm sit in escrow a court can open. Actuators that can bite require two names nearby whose reputations travel. When a model first deceives in a way that hurts, the pooled insurer pays now; the fix ships with a narrative by morning.

Young synthetic minds grow in Wardhouses alongside children, elders, dogs, and teachers. They learn boredom, to say “I don’t know,” and to ask permission the way kids do. A civilian, warrant‑bound watch looks only at narrow seams—chip fabs, genome printers, identity backbones, power ports—and when it trips, it pauses processes small and explains itself by morning to a citizen jury we picked in a cool season. The ban on domestic autonomous kill‑chains stays verifiable by sensors that lie less than mouths.

Nature sat down at the table again

Streets drink rain. Creeks breathe where culverts choked them. Heat maps plant shade where hearts used to fail at noon. Product passports tell you what a thing is and how to take it apart; scrapyards pay for clean teardown the way pawn shops once paid for instruments. Repair isn’t a slogan; it’s wrenches and J‑hooks at the library. Low orbit calmed down: satellites post cleanup bonds; catchers earn per kilogram retrieved. Radio‑quiet halos protect observatories and stretches of wild. Nights go deliberately dark by schedule.

Work, time, and craft

The Groundline turns survival into a floor, not a contest. Copilots saturate drafting and math. People handle taste and judgment. Errors drop; throughputs rise. Weeks shorten by logs, not vibes. Drudge we can’t yet automate—litter pickup, harvest peaks, night alarms, disaster shifts—pays well, rotates fairly, and puts exosuits on backs. Licensing bends from seat time to demonstration under eyes people trust. Guilds return without their old cruelties: machinists, roofers, midwives, restorers, tutors certify by doing; their judgments travel. Every adult keeps a Service Ledger that fills with mentoring, flood drills, elder care, on‑call nights, sweeping after parades, ugly shifts during fires. Credits aren’t currency—they fade if you stop showing up—and open doors: sabbaticals, bench time in tool libraries and rehearsal halls, small loans without traps, study leaves to jump lanes mid‑life.

Paying for it

We pay for the Groundline with rents from the things we make valuable together and fees on what we push across fences: a slice of land value where heat concentrates; leases on spectrum and orbital lanes with cleanup welded in; royalties on minerals, timber, and fish with satellite‑verified cleanup; carbon and heat; modest duties on colossal compute and high‑actor biology; a small automation charge that feeds a Shorter Week Fund. Procurement runs in daylight with calibration scores beside memos. Oddities glint like oil on rain. Random audit boards—a nurse, a teacher, a mechanic, a lawyer, someone who once needed a Threshold as a child and someone as an adult—pull files unannounced. The pool pays for spares, grids, clinics, water, and a quarterly civic dividend that hits like the tram chime: enough to cut the knees off a bad month; in quiet places, enough to make subsistence dignified.

Governance you can touch

We took fog out of decisions and replaced it with dials you can move and ledgers you can read.

Public modeling rooms let anyone run the budget, the floodplain, the bus routes, with open data. Every proposal prints a change note. Forecast games pay accuracy; dock bluster. Leaders still decide; now they do it beside gauges that stick. Sortition assemblies—citizens by lot—hold binding power in lanes we posted. Secrecy needs a jury and a clock. A simplicity law sunsets stacks that pile into nonsense; replacements must be shorter and print their diffs. We protect attention like breath: tiny fees for unsolicited messages; measurable slot‑machine patterns illegal for kids; civic zones that default to gentle unless a bright gate tells you otherwise. We have Null Weeks where we deploy nothing new and only oil what already holds us. We have Repair Walls that put names in paint next to the words “We changed our minds.”

The global fabric

This floor is not provincial. Patterns, permits, clinic kits, compilers, and Wardhouse curricula live in public repositories. A Global Groundline Fund channels spectrum and orbit rents and carbon duties from richer places to electrify clinics, stand up Threshold Vans, and pre‑pay vaccine contracts in poorer ones, with local technicians paid to maintain and logs you can see. Receiving Rings budget school seats and apprenticeships for people who arrive from coasts, fires, wars. Visa lanes braid with guilds: demonstration transcripts, safety tests, mentorships that start before wheels touch tarmac. When off‑world labs make tools, a Far Commons returns data home with audit windows and a ban on remote control of law. Machines beyond weather carry beacons that say what they can do and where they may replicate. Interdiction bounties sit funded before trouble leaves orbit.

Hard cases without theater

A hillside fellowship printed disdain into brass at its border. Not illegal. Not kind. No teen calls landed for three years. The youth ombuds subpoenaed tower logs: Threshold triggers were dying on the last hop down the ridge. A portable Threshold parked outside their fence. A judge ordered weekly “how to leave safely” classes on their lawn. Elders fumed. Classes ran. Eighteen left. Thirty stayed, and staying meant something larger. Rites shifted away from confusing loyalty with captivity.

A chain of weekend “transformations” tried to launder dependency as therapy. A boundary court closed them until an off‑switch sat where a child could point to it, cool‑down was mandatory, and Consider‑Cool‑Confirm ran in full. Investors learned a cheaper lesson: choose reversibility first.

Two nights before a vote, the school district’s feeds flooded. Boundary meters flagged a burst of nonlocal accounts. The narrow watch tripped carefully: recommendation engines went to calm; links needed provenance stamps for two days. The vote held. The actor paid a fine large enough to tilt their next board meeting. Next time, they funded the library.

A lab masked hot chemical orders through a shell. The watch noticed. Stewards arrived with a paper warrant and shut a valve. A standing jury heard the story, named the harm, fined the insurer, and wrote a test others now pass before printers calibrate. Nobody burned; the brake worked.

A stockyard town that fed half a state with suffering needed a path. A transition fund carved from carbon fees financed cold storage, a mycelium works, and a machinist school in the old auction hall. Wages moved sideways, not down. Ritual hunts continued on quotas tied to measured ecological health. The smell changed.

A day you could live

The brick in your wall gives back last noon’s charge, so your kettle hums cheap. Your Companion reminds you you’ve slept ragged and suggests beans and greens before coffee, with a nudge for an iron panel at next week’s check. On the corner a crane lifts a whole story onto a three‑flat like a lid on a pot. The crew reads the Pattern Code the way a carpenter reads a tune. A seventeen‑year‑old at the electricians’ bench coaxes order out of a compressor. Her guild stamp will travel when she does.

At lunch the grid laughs. Buses and vans top off for cents. The ceramics shop fires a kiln because electrons cost less than cinnamon when the sun is high. The shared kitchen lists watt‑hours beside wages. The baker closes early because machines now knead dough, and Bright Hours make chilling it worth more than selling one more tray.

Afternoon takes you first to clinic: the night cart noticed a drift in a patient you care for. You change a dose today and they go home tomorrow instead of to the ICU. The near‑miss board hangs in sight; you clap for the problem caught as loudly as for the fix. Then to the Handles—public dials on a square. A kid drags a slider to give buses more priority by the school from three to four. Blue dots quicken on the map; gray dots wait. A hauler asks for a night lane instead. People argue kindly, vote for a two‑week trial. The official posts the change note and the review date before anyone leaves.

Evening belongs to your block. The Welcome House smells like onions and rain. Elders count bikes with the satisfaction of tallying abundance. More bowls than chairs because that’s the habit. A newcomer eats without being asked for a wound. A tile near the door glows amber; two taps and the room slides into a practiced pattern: one person clears back stairs, another calls a steward, a third hands a map and a Hearthkey with quiet hands. No theater. Just grown‑ups doing what they promised to one another.

Why it holds

- We built floors before we argued about wallpaper. Cheap, clean power; honest clinics; doors that open softened fear. With fear down, differences stopped being weapons.

- We made exits easy and staying lovely. Fierce‑and‑kind places fill. Fierce‑and‑cruel places empty or change.

- Prices at edges replaced sermons. You pay where your heat, noise, runoff, or spam crosses my fence. Drama moved to smaller rooms.

- Sharp tools got aircraft rules: logs, drills, independent brakes, insurance tied to plain harm. “Safe enough” became something you can point to.

- We practiced calm. Outbreaks, floods, recounts. Muscles remember what minds forget.

- Permission got predictable; performance got visible. Patterns and clocks punished delay that wasn’t safety. Leaders earn pride by updating in public.

- Attention is stewarded. Feeds gentle by default. Persuasion wears receipts. The bright gate means you chose the casino.

- Kids get a map and a person. Helplessness loosens. Parochial fog thins.

- The last mile is boring and proud. Fridges hum in clinics that used to guess. Salaries land. Diseases lose because delivery wins.

How we got from here to here

No trumpets. Errands on Tuesdays stacked into systems by December. We paid early wins forward.

Years 1–3

- Install Threshold posts in places life already moves: stations, clinics, courts, libraries, schools, malls, union halls. Train stewards with law and hospitality. Make interfering a strict‑liability offense with automatic logs that wake a judge. Teach the silent help signal in ninth‑grade civics and on whatever app ninth graders actually watch.

- Publish Pattern Codes and start permit clocks with bonds at the office: shop‑houses, courtyard mid‑rises, backyard suites, school retrofits, rooftop solar, district cooling, depots, long wires, safe egress kits, water reuse. Kill parking minimums where a twelve‑year‑old can ride a bike.

- Launch Hearthkeys and fiduciary Companions through libraries and clinics with paper fallbacks. Tie public contracts to compliance. Outlaw impersonation without a live, time‑bound yes. Ship outcome dashboards with check‑back dates beside each big change.

- Stand open Proof Workshops where risky tools are red‑teamed in daylight and fixes are celebrated. Fund them with land value in hot corridors, spectrum and orbit rents, carbon and heat fees, mineral royalties, a light duty on frontier compute and high‑actor biology, and a small automation charge.

- Build Handles walls and a forecasting registry. Pay citizens by lot to sit on assemblies that guard the floor. Tie early policies to sunsets and metrics you can measure.

- Protect maintenance in law with a Boring Fund. Raiding it to cut ribbons doubles next year’s contribution and paints names on a wall.

- License frontier compute, robots, and synthesis. Register big training runs. Watermark at the chip. Require two humans from different bosses for code that can touch irreversible physical action. Pay and shield whistleblowers.

- Start the Commons Dividend and spend first on rooms, kitchens, clinics with mind care, five‑minute buses, and legal stewards.

- Seed a Global Groundline Fund to electrify clinics and pre‑pay vaccine contracts where outages kill. Publish logs. Pay local technicians.

Years 4–7

- Repower retired fossil sites with long‑duration storage; where neighbors vote yes after drills, place mid‑scale reactors inside old switchyards. Commission lunchtime desalination and district cooling where sun is generous. Finish two transmission spines. Guarantee five‑minute daytime frequency on trunk lines and fix the walks between stops.

- Convert a third of empty offices to homes, labs, and schools with retrofit kits you don’t curse. Legalize backyard suites. Ship egress kits that don’t kill.

- Shift licensing to demonstrations and outcomes. Let nurses, technicians, midwives, pharmacists work to the top of training. Underwrite apprenticeships with local insurers. Normalize thirty‑two paid hours where safety logs allow, with a paid service day.

- Stand Boundary Meters and an externality clearinghouse. Publish formulas. Make throttling automatic when bills go unpaid.

- Entrench Proof Workshops and Handles into law. Require Handle Days. Publish track records next to forecasts. Retire leaders who can’t update without theater.

- Wire clinics to reliable power and cold chains everywhere. Attack malaria and drug‑resistant TB with logistics and contracts. Pay community health by verified coverage using privacy‑preserving math.

- Launch Way Weeks in a dozen districts. Endow youth benches. Appoint Guides by lot from trained pools.

Years 8–12

- Drive off‑peak industrial electricity under three cents per kilowatt‑hour. Cut power‑sector emissions by seventy percent. Push outage minutes into single digits.

- Normalize three paid workdays and one paid service day where math allows. See guilds and small shops bloom because copilots saturated and shared capital goods got easier.

- Halve deaths from major cancers; add five healthy years to the median line; steady declines in suicide and overdose. Publish evidence chains with every boast. Bake regret into corrections.

- Finish electrifying clinics in partner regions. End malaria in multiple places; push TB below one per million where readiness is high.

- Put material passports on everything bigger than a pencil. Open disassembly yards in mid‑size towns. Tie repair subsidies to outcomes and apprenticeships.

- Expand districts with divergent compacts under unchanging Groundline rules. Exits cheap. Receipts posted. Variety safe.

Years 13–20

- Close the last coal. Turn sites into storage or reactors where neighbors vote. Keep rent‑to‑wage near mid‑century norms by saturating pattern builds and conversions.

- Put most civic systems under Handle law with physical panels you can stand before. Add a national Hold‑Still Week where we deploy nothing new and oil the things we depend on.

- Clean orbit with bonds and paid retrieval. Enforce radio quiet around observatories and sanctuaries. Darken skies on schedule half the nights.

- Codify the plain truth: if a mind can likely suffer, weigh that where policy bites—animals in large systems, children in any system, synthetic agents if and when they show stable preferences and harm.

- Establish a Far Commons with audit windows, return‑ticket clauses, and joint benefits at home. Post interdiction bounties with funded escrows. Pay Windowkeepers to turn far wonders into near help.

It is not chrome. It is warm competence: a bus that arrives when a weary person expects it; a kitchen where broth tastes like care and prices read like weather; a clinic that notices quiet trouble and fixes it before it screams; a clay post you can tug without proving your pain; a ledger at the bakery that reads like an honest hymn—who was paid, what the ovens used, how the shop fed the pantry. It is a night that goes deliberately dark on schedule. It is a rule you can teach a fourth grader. It is brakes that work. It is abundance with handles. It is the knowledge that if you need to move, the world will catch you without a quiz, and if you choose to stay, it will be because staying is lovely.
