The Underlay and the Mosaics

We made two promises and learned to keep them in boring, repeatable ways. First: no one falls through the floor. Second: no one is trapped behind a door they cannot open. From those came a style of living that is less about purity and more about traction: an invisible backbone that does the same dull miracles everywhere, and a thousand distinct places that sing because they can be different without being dangerous.

We call the backbone the Underlay. It is the power, water, health, law, ledgers, and exits you do not negotiate for as an individual. It is intentionally unexciting, like a well-tuned clock. The places you can choose—the towns, quarters, work guilds, co-ops, and circles with charters—are the Mosaics. They make texture worth having.

A morning might begin like this: the street is quiet enough to hear shoes on stone. Freight rolls on soft tires at dawn and disappears before breakfast; by day the lanes belong to feet, bikes, and buses that arrive often enough that you don’t check times. A row of panels clicks open to spill light into a mid-rise being skinned with a factory-made facade that is beautiful because the factory ships nothing else. In the square, a public kitchen sets out oatmeal and steamed greens, an old cook stirring with the authority of fifteen years of free breakfasts. Anyone eats. No one shows a card. Next door, a “take it apart” table is stacked with broken printers destined to become radios by lunch. On a board behind the cook, the day’s rehearsal dials glow: if it rains after three, buses switch to the storm signal timing; if the vaccination line in Ward Seven droops below the agreed threshold, outdoor classes start tomorrow morning by plan, not by panic.

Enough, on time

The Underlay begins with energy and water that are abundant, predictable, and cheap enough to make decarbonization a spreadsheet habit rather than a sermon. We cut approval times by switching from bespoke wrangles to “Tempo Laws” with clocks. If a project matches published noise, setback, safety, wildlife, and carbon patterns, the agency must answer yes or no within a deadline you can set your watch by. Miss the deadline and the permit issues; an escrow account the agency posted pays the penalty. Designs come from a public pattern catalog: half a dozen wind and solar kits you can site without surprises; a family of mid-rise apartment types that handle light, air, and emergency egress; modular reactors prequalified to repower brownfield plants; storage patterns based on iron-air, sodium, pumped heat, compressed air. If you match a pattern and a site class, the checklists are short, the hearings are focused, and the answer does not wobble.

Transmission learned to plan like railways rather than like trench wars: long corridors negotiated in daylight, standardized compensation and relocation assistance, and enforceable rules about when and how a community can say no. Interconnection queues sprouted teeth and clocks that count both ways. Water smoothed its seasonal pulses with daylit desalination and district cooling that soak up the bulge of sun at noon; upstream, wetlands—with actual beavers and their synthetic cousins—take the spring punch out of rivers that used to flood.

The power abundance changed industry because the prices did. When off-peak power slides below two cents a kilowatt-hour in a region, a cement mill swapping in resistive heat or a steel shop electrifying a furnace is not making a moral statement; it’s following its own accounting. Noon became when we “do the hot things”—desalinate, charge thermal stores, run kilns, synthesize hydrogen for the few flames that still make sense. Retired coal plants are now home to thermal storage stacks and modular reactors that use the same grid interconnects and the same crews, with different fuel and different math.

Permits learned to keep time in housing, too. We published pattern books for infill and mid-rise building that are generous with courtyards, strict on light and ventilation, and blunt about structural fire safety. If your building matches a pattern, your permit issues as of right; if it doesn’t, you take the fast lane where a small panel of practice-based inspectors ticks through clear rules. We legalized backyard cottages statewide, installed safe egress kits in basements, and turned dangerous “informal” rooms into dignified ones. Dead office towers became studios, schools, labs, and apartments using retrofit recipes that know exactly where to cut. Land is taxed for site value so we stop punishing owners for adding homes. Parking minimums are gone. Micro-retail and shop-houses are legal within walking catchments.

The commons floor is a floor

We built basic services as a platform, not as a raffle: staple foods in lively public kitchens; clinics where mental health stands shoulder to shoulder with broken bones; transit that arrives whether you drive or not; high-speed connectivity; day-one legal help when trouble starts; quiet, safe places to sleep when an emergency night comes. These services are funded the way we fund the rest of the Underlay: land value levies; fees on spectrum and minerals; a modest carbon dividend; a compute license on frontier models; and a license on the public protocols and identity layer private firms depend on. Because everyone uses these things sometimes, the standards stay high. Because they are not charity, they do not humiliate.

On top of the floor we adopted seasons of civic work. Adults choose paid stints each year that count toward a ledger you cannot spend as cash, but that opens doors. One season you coach elders to reclaim a decade of sleep. Another you inspect culverts before fall storms. Another you assist a ninth-grade rhetoric class. The ledger makes you eligible for apprenticeships in guilds, studio time, lab benches, and sabbaticals at the community bank. It is status you earn by holding something together.

Care as an engineered loop

Health became a loop we can instrument and improve rather than a battlefield of anecdotes. “Neighborhood rounds” are normal: a van visits once a month or more if your numbers tilt, with finger-prick panels, breath sensors, and wearables that whisper when something is off before it shouts. Hospital wards run nightly micro-panels and morning dashboards that catch sepsis and drug interactions early. Research runs in robot-assisted labs where people design and interpret but machines never forget to label a tube. Trials tighten around biomarkers and adaptive endpoints rather than waiting for a decade.

Payment follows outcomes in time, not procedures. If your device or therapy buys quality-adjusted life weeks beyond standard care, we pay for those weeks, and we count the hours your pill steals with brain fog as a cost.

Infectious disease moved from pageantry to playbook. Wastewater and symptom dashboards light up when a variant sneaks in. Pre-authorized vaccine updates move from lab to clinic in under two months. Negative-pressure shelters roll out with the same muscle memory as snowplows when a neighborhood falls below an agreed vaccination threshold. Schools pivot to outdoor classes by plan, not by panic.

Cancer moved earlier, not magically but meaningfully. Blood tests for stray tumor DNA and quiet imaging pulled more stage I cases out of the fog. In high-risk families, targeted drugs knock out precancers before they get cocky. A prosaic bundle—sleep, protein, vaccines, sun you can measure, movement you can tolerate—layered with senolytics and metabolic meds where multiple markers agree added years to the middle and more to the part of life we like.

Mind health stepped out of the broom closet. Local AIs that live under your key coach the daily practice that makes therapy work; clinicians handle the knotted part. Psychedelic-assisted sessions sit under anesthesia-style rules: lawful, trained, reversible. Noninvasive brain stimulation joined the options. Suicide and overdose curves bent, then kept bending. Safe-use spaces with test strips and slow-release antagonists became boring, like seatbelts.

Work without the grind

Automation deleted drudgery and returned judgment to people. “Copilot on, human in charge” is the default posture in most jobs. Drafts appear; schedules align; claims code themselves; pull requests bring tests. Productivity rose and error rates fell, so the week compressed. Thirty-two paid hours is normal. No one expects you to fill the quiet with pretend busyness. Licenses slimmed to what protects people; the rest shifted to transparent competencies and portfolios you carry between places. A welder’s title cements when three masters sign their names next to hers in a public ledger, staking reputation. A barber from Lima learns local health rules in a day, does a demonstration cut, and starts work.

We recognized and repaid work markets ignore. Care time—parenting, eldercare, mentoring, mutual aid—earns credits in a Care Bank that you can draw from when you need care yourself. Visible work on public projects—seawalls, open tools, folk archives—earns non-transferable Craft Stamps in the Project Ledger. These stamps and credits do not buy groceries; they open apprenticeships, studios, synapses you wouldn’t otherwise cross.

Testing, in public

We built “Proof Yards”—part machine shop, part university lab, part library—where questions become trials with dials you can see. People who know practice—farmers, nurses, carpenters, machinists, teachers—work with methodologists. Each Yard has Scouts who live where outcomes matter; Distillers who replicate promising results before anyone shouts; Weavers who co-design trials with the communities they touch; Translators who carry results to councils and kitchens; Safekeepers who do structured pessimism and enforce stop rules.

By default, the notebooks are open. If secrecy is merited—because a new material invites theft or a bio idea invites misuse—a citizen jury can grant it with a timer. Conflicts of interest are auditable down to the invoice for pipette tips. Every quarter, Proof Day feels like a county fair: successes on the stage next to null results. A child asks why a design failed; the grownup answers politely. We do not confuse “didn’t work” with “waste.”

Governing with rehearsals and clocks

We built Rehearsal Rooms in every city: big maps, forecast boards, levers you can pull. You spin dials: make buses free on rainy days; plant a stormwater garden where a parking lot stands; stagger school starts. A prediction market shifts odds for metrics we promised to measure—ambulance times, shop revenue, puddle depth—in the open. Forecasts don’t decide; people do. But they make lying costly.

Plans often move in pairs. Two districts try two versions for six months with an arbitration metric agreed in advance. We published calibration badges next to commentators’ and officials’ names. It didn’t silence anyone; it stapled receipts to their mouths.

Truth held faster than lies because we made provenance cheap. Photos, video, and sensor data ride a signed chain that turns easy fakes into expensive fakes.

Markets on rails, with commons that bite back

Markets did not die. They learned boundaries. Companies that cross a size threshold convert to steward-ownership: employees, users, and a future trust hold the voting shares. Firms relying on public data hold it in citizen trusts run by trustees we elect. Those trustees can withdraw consent sector-wide and have. Ads turned opt-in; access to attention is a licensed utility. Messages cost money to send into our heads. Compulsive designs on public feeds are illegal.

Artificial intelligence behaves not because we trust it, but because we fenced it. High-capability models train and run above thresholds in licensed facilities with real insurance and standing red teams. Experiments register under a “compute passport.” Risky deployments have hazard budgets and dual-key signoff. It is illegal to bend law or politics with customized rules whispered by models. Personal assistants run locally or under strict fiduciaries; they cannot sell you or pretend to be you without a negotiated, revocable yes.

New machine minds grew up in gardens before we let them act. For a while they have no actuators. They get company instead: teachers of patience, kids who play, elders who have time, animals who ask to be watched quietly. When they graduate, many choose mentor circles. It looks less like leashes and more like adoption across kinds.

Safety that brags about its brakes

We stitched a Safety Net into the Underlay to watch tail risks at the seams—chip fabs, genome printers, lab anomalies, identity tampering—under warrants, civilian oversight, and rules that point some of its assets back at itself if it ever goes strange. When the Net acts—grounding drones near a lab with a bad signal, sandboxing a runaway auto-trader—it tells the story in public and submits to review by a jury we convened when the weather was calm. Schoolchildren tour its simulator and pull blessed overrides like fire alarms.

Borders with doors, not moats

Choice is fake without exits. So every chartered place has a neutral front door: bright room, glass, coffee, and no guns. Ombuds work there with the power to move bodies and paperwork. A whispered phrase, a tap on a token, a glance into a public camera can summon Exitways—a service with housing, clinics, and lawyers that severs coercive debts and unknots obstructive paper. Children are citizens first. Parents and communities are stewards with duties. A baseline curriculum covers literacy, numeracy, health, consent, civics, and a survey of different ways of living. Between twelve and eighteen, each child is offered two funded “Seasons Away”—three-month residencies in other charters with mentors and a private line to ombuds. Minors can invoke exit; courts decide with a bias toward the child’s stated will and safety.

We standardized consent for big choices with friction we trained ourselves to honor. Before committing to strict communities, body modifications, sealed-role work, or long missions, you take a “consent sandwich”: an adversarial briefing that surfaces the long-term risks; a cooling-off period measured in sleep cycles; a recommitment in front of a recorder who cannot infer consent. During, three safewords are universal: Pause, to make everyone repeat back their understanding; Witness, to summon a neutral observer and pause all other recording; Door, to trigger extraction. After, you get windows to unwind: a year to reverse sealed memories; a decade to appeal major body changes; no time limit to leave a place.

Border Filters live at the edges of Mosaics. If a town bars gambling ads or porn or political canvassing after midnight, your glasses blur those messages when you cross the line. Individuals can choose stricter filters for themselves. Outsiders cannot beam in content a community has not opted into.

Mosaics, specific on purpose

Chartered communities publish “Beacons” with their rules, contacts, exits, and red lines. Rules are legible; exits are real. A few you might visit:

- Deep Sky is an observatory town. No white light outdoors after sunset; streets glow amber. Dogs on leashes wear dim blue tags. Everyone learns to use red flashlights. It is an introvert’s dream. Kids learn constellations by smell—sage by the east gate, pine near the ridge.

- Freehand is loud after dark by design. Cars are forbidden. Music pours from doors at sunset. The foundry and glass studios roar at noon on sun surplus and fall quiet at night. Consent signage is omnipresent and enforced as seriously as fire codes.

- Hearthring is conservative and proud of it. Sunday bells. Modest dress in public squares. Pornography is illegal to sell inside the ring. It publishes this upfront. Young people leave for a Season Away early and often; some return with affection, some don’t. Visitors follow the dress code in return for bread that tastes like someone remembers you.

- Third Rail is for argument. Public debate follows choreography: question, strongest restatement of the opponent’s position, answer, check. No one speaks at a city forum without earning a “steel badge” by passing a test in charity.

- Tideback lives with water. The town hall posts the retreat plan on the wall with lines and dates. Twice a decade they pull houses back, plant marshes, and throw a party for each street that moves of its own will rather than under floodlights.

Nature has a chair at the council

We braided tree cover into cities until a summer noon feels like a different planet. Floodplains behave like floodplains again. Working lands get paid for ecosystem services—pollination, carbon, water retention—verified by satellite and boots in fields. Factory farms shuts down not because activists won a slam-dunk but because alternatives matured: precision-fermented proteins and cell-ag dishes that taste like someone’s uncle’s favorite stew. We wrote down a sentence and made markets obey: any mind that can suffer deserves moral consideration. We quarrel about the edges. It tilted how we treat animals and how we talk about synthetic minds and alien ones we might someday detect.

Space stopped being a joyride and started being utilities we all use: weather with more pixels, GPS that does not blink in storms, a shadow that lets us study other suns without throwing heat at our own. A bond you post at launch pays capture drones per kilogram of debris they bring down.

The last mile, everywhere

We stopped romanticizing poverty and we stopped leaving people behind because it was administratively convenient. Kits that turn sun into light and cold landed at the last unlit homes: panels, storage, and satellite links sized to keep a vaccine fridge, a fish freezer, and a classroom alive. Payments float with income; maintenance is local and paid. Thermostable vaccines ride drone cold chains. Breath tests and cash-for-coverage pay village health workers to hit immunization and TB targets on dashboards they control; the money arrives the day the target is hit. Gene drives against malaria go only after island pilots, multi-country consent, and independent oversight. Malaria fell to noise in places that had never known such silence; tuberculosis followed with short-course regimens triggered early. A shared fund, seeded by carbon fees, land value levies in hot markets, and a compute license on frontier models, pays for electrification, water, and a standing corps that can lay pipe and fiber as easily as field a clinic.

Hard cases and how they went

Ember Vale was an agrarian sect with strict vows and excellent cheese. It published its Beacon; no one stumbled in. It also tried to block teenagers from taking Seasons Away and required supervision to talk to ombuds. It lasted twenty-one months. Children’s ombuds subpoenaed schedules and admission forms, found patterns of blocked calls, opened an Exitways room in the next town, and brought a case. A judge ordered weekly classes on exit rights on Ember Vale’s green. Fourteen kids left in a trickle. Some elders relented. The cheese remains excellent. The vows are gentler.

Blue Channel was a startup city-state built on speed and attention. It banned Border Filters from blurring ads and trained recommender models to whisper differently into every ear at once. The attention license it needed refused renewal. Freight slowed at the boundary. The Safety Net throttled its message APIs after a public hearing showed it was simulating law for different citizens. Blue Channel turned toward steward-ownership or left for places that play by fewer rules. People moved at dawn. Exits cut knots.

Riverstep had a vaccination slump after a spate of scary stories. The threshold we set months earlier triggered school tents outdoors and paid outreach teams. The Proof Yard staged a debate in the square with doctors from Riverstep and rivals from Argument Town. Two weeks later, the line turned up. Not everyone agreed. Enough did.

Grey Forge was the old steel yard where a new resistive furnace needed fewer crew. The Guild and the company fought. The Rehearsal Room turned it into a trial: two lines of work rules for three months with a pay metric everyone signed. The line with copilots won on output and injury rate. Crew shrank. People moved to the safety inspection guild for the furnace corridors, with full apprenticeships paid by the Project Ledger and a slice of the compute fee that got them that job in the first place.

From here to there

We did not get here by leaping; we stacked things small enough to win and big enough to matter.

Years 1–3:
- Pass Tempo Laws for clean energy, transmission, and mid-rise housing with clocks and escrow-backed penalties. Publish a national pattern book for apartments, shop-houses, backyard cottages, rooftop solar, and long-distance lines. Legalize by-right where a state can.
- Stand up the public identity layer with selective disclosure so you can prove “I live here” or “I am a unique person” without doxxing yourself. Use it for voting and benefits while protecting anonymous speech.
- Launch the first Proof Yards with charters no one can buy: open notebooks by default; citizen secrecy juries with timers; funding from spectrum fees, compute licenses, and land value; an inspectorate that can audit conflicts.
- Build Rehearsal Rooms and prediction markets in pilot cities; tie them to real decisions with pre-agreed metrics and sunsets; publish forecaster calibration next to budgets.
- Fund the floor: clinics that include mental health; public kitchens; transit; legal help; emergency beds. Access is as simple as a library card and as private as a doctor visit. Dignity becomes a metric.
- Set up robot-assisted labs accessible to universities and startups; tie grants to pre-registered analysis; create an insurer-of-last-resort fund for risky research from a small levy on high-capability compute.
- Enact a children’s interface law banning manipulative design; require friction for binding consents; license ad access to attention and make it opt-in.
- Create Exitways rooms in dense places; hire ombuds; legislate their authority to move people and paperwork; publish dispatch logs with identifying details stripped.

Years 4–7:
- Commission long-duration storage; daylight desalination and district cooling. Repower the first wave of coal sites with modular fission or high-temp electrolysis on existing interconnects and with the same workers.
- Halve approval times for consequential drugs and devices via adaptive trials and registries; publish hospital error rates and waits; scale neighborhood rounds vans.
- Convert a third of stagnant office space to housing, labs, and schools using retrofit templates; stripe night freight lanes to free daytime streets; tune bus signals until “every five minutes” is true on trunks.
- Enact steward-ownership triggers for firms past size thresholds; stand up citizen data trusts with bargaining power; make ad access a licensed privilege with fees to the commons.
- Slim occupational licensing to what protects; entrench skill passports backed by transparent tests; fund craft guilds that train by performance and certify by peer signature.
- Require dual-human authorization for irreversible AI-mediated acts; mandate hazard budgets and incident reports; ship personal assistants that run locally under data-fiduciary law.
- Open the first Gardens for New Minds as intergenerational commons; publish curricula for empathy, patience, and human slowness.

Years 8–12:
- Drive average retail electricity below seven cents per kilowatt-hour and industrial off-peak below three; measure outage minutes in single digits per person per year. Cut power-sector emissions by seventy percent.
- Reduce cancer mortality by half; add five healthy years to the median span; cut suicides and overdoses by half.
- Finish global household electrification; connect every clinic to the internet and a cold chain; bring TB under one per million in several regions; hold malaria near zero in others.
- Normalize the thirty-two-hour week; make seasons of service a story you tell because everyone respects it; expand the Care Bank and Project Ledger until they are part of how you plan a life.
- Entrench rehearsal rooms and forecast boards into budget processes; publish calibration scores next to official names; retire leaders who cannot update.
- Mature the Safety Net; harmonize hardware attestation internationally; pair access to advanced tools with verifiable human rights commitments.

Years 13–20:
- Close the last coal sites; complete continental transmission backbones. Stabilize rent-to-wage ratios by saturating pattern-book builds and retrofits; renovate dangerous basements with off-the-shelf egress kits.
- Codify enforceable bans on fully autonomous lethal action; sanction violations in ways that do not flatten cities.
- Normalize managed retreat; move neighborhoods together; hold festivals for streets that choose to roll rather than drown.
- Expand steward-ownership; treat citizen data trusts as routine; make attention licensing dull.
- Keep malaria and TB lines flat; treat success as a celebrated public graph.

A day, stitched

On your service day you are at the clinic before nine. The first appointment is a carpenter whose knees read like old maps. His on-phone model—local, private—shows the small chaos points that trip him: late alarms on Thursdays, a snack he eats and forgets, weekends that evaporate because he does not call the friend who will actually walk. You design a routine he can keep. He cries because the last time someone paid him this kind of attention he was eleven and a coach looked him in the eye.

At lunch you stop at the Proof Yard. It is Proof Day. A team—two engineers, a poet, a nurse—presents an inhaler redesigned to refill at any clinic, built from parts with no rare minerals, posted under an open patent pool that returns a slice to the commons. The nurse reads a child’s story about a first breath that didn’t hurt. A high schooler asks why the seal failed in winter. The Safekeeper says, that’s why we run winter trials and publish the dials.

In the afternoon your other job’s copilot drafts; you do judgment. A colleague in Porto and another in Lagos drop into a shared-attention session for thirty minutes; you three design a fix that would have taken a week of email, and log off early because nothing is on fire and idle time is not a sin.

On the way home your daughter begs to spin the tram dials in the Rehearsal Room. The market says two short lines beat a single spine on ambulances and shop revenue. You are not voting tonight. You are practicing being a polity.

At dusk the Welcome House on your block glows. A family just arrived from a country your school forced you to find on a globe because no one pretends anymore that knowing where things are is elitist. Your circle’s elders wait on the steps. Someone who used to cook for welders shows up with a pot of beans. The kid with the airline tag learns the word for home that does not require a mortgage.

After dark, the Safety Net quietly grounds outbound drones in a six-block bubble around a lab with a weird signal. A woman with a badge most of us never see goes up three flights and knocks. By morning a jury will read the story and publish what it changed. Tonight, a brass band practices a happy march that is still a little out of tune. It is good to live where brakes are a point of pride.

Why this held

Because competence compounds trust. When a permit decision arrives on time, even a no lands clean, and people try again. When a hospital catches a slide before it crashes, you fund it. When a pattern-approved building rises fast and handsome on your street, you support the next one. When your child knows how to leave—and sometimes chooses to stay—you trust your own place more.

Because truth became cheaper than lies, with receipts next to mouths and provenance stapled to files. Because exits are rights, not favors. Because children are taught the map, not just the road under their feet. Because machines help where we can audit them and cannot sell us while whispering in our rooms. Because low-tech options are comfortable—screens-off parks, analog libraries with human helpers, quiet corners where you can be small.

Because we govern the space between values rather than trying to enforce one value set on all. A chaste town can be chaste. A wild town can be wild. A church town can be orderly and tender. A neon quay can thump until dawn. The walls we keep are around coercion and cross-boundary harm. Oppressive norms hemorrhage talent; strict but kind places thrive. People sort not into bubbles that calcify, but into gardens that must stay livable to keep their gardeners.

We made a holiday of our brakes. Twice a year the “Switch” arrives: every Mosaic does one thing out of character, on purpose, to exercise the muscle of tolerance. The observatory town throws a midday street fair under ridiculous lights. The argument quarter tells jokes without footnotes. The nightlife district hosts a tea ceremony in silence. The safety agency turns models off for an afternoon and runs the buses by the book just to prove we remember how. Children collect stamps. Adults do, too. The patriotism we keep attaches to the system that lets neighbors be wrong in peace.

Begin where you are

You do not need permission from the future. Draft a block charter with ten households. Post a Beacon. Open a room on the corner with glass and coffee and a human who can help. Teach your kids three communities you would not live in yourself and how to leave them safely. Pass a Tempo Law for rooftop solar in your city. Put a forecast board next to your agency’s budget. Adopt Pause, Witness, Door in your clinic or club. Refuse to deploy any assistant that will not declare its goals. File a policy diff with predicted effects and promise a retrospective. Lay out a Proof Day. Invite a child to ask why your idea failed.

The Underlay hums. The Mosaics sing. The floor holds. The doors open. We step through.
