The Commons Spine

On a soft Tuesday after rain, a waist‑high column appeared between the notary and the nail salon. It was enamel white, with a clay tag that read: Need a way out? Pull. A cotton cord hung from the tag like a bell rope. A woman with a swollen cheek wrapped her fingers around the cord and pulled.

The column—Doorpost, people called it—did three things before her courage cooled. It canceled the tracking app her boyfriend had installed, routed a locksmith to change her locks while she was at work, and opened a small case in the courthouse that froze any attempt to move her paycheck. It also put a bed in a spare room three neighborhoods away on hold for two weeks, no questions asked, and sent the bus. The fees that make leaving feel like a punishment—the deposit, the locker at the new job, the lost phone number—evaporated into a ledger the city audited whenever it wanted proof this wasn’t theater.

Doorposts caught on because they made one promise that turned out to infect everything else: You can step out without being turned into paperwork, and you can step into something specific.

Under the Doorposts was a layer we agreed to make blunt enough to memorize and stubborn enough to lean on. We call it the Commons Spine. It fits on the wall of every clinic, library, and shop that wants to be known as a place that won’t trap you. It reads:

- You own your leaving. No household, employer, school, or town can hold your person, pay, documents, or access hostage. A public office will help you move in under a week, even if your phone is dead.

- You cannot be injured into obedience. Physical assault, starvation by bureaucracy, forced surveillance, and threats by paper are illegal. The only legitimate force is the narrow force that defends this Spine.

- Children are citizens. Each has an Advocate outside the home. At twelve, every child gets funded “Explorer Weeks” in other communities; their credits and care records travel with them.

- Your senses are yours. Places can choose their own color, noise, and ad rules. They cannot beam persuasion into unwilling eyes across a border, or hide who paid for it and what it means to change.

- Cross‑fence harm is metered and halted. If what you do fouls shared air and water or pushes risk across a boundary—toxins, carbon, botnets, harassment—you pay by the Boundary Meter. Refuse, and your bandwidth, shipments, or interconnect get throttled until you stop. A Calm Corps with trauma shears and law degrees—not tanks—pauses acute cross‑border harm on sight.

- Irreversible acts require two living hands and a legible log. Open a dam gate, order a dangerous pathogen, disable a substation, command lethal force: two named humans must agree, and the record is read aloud in language an eighth‑grader can repeat.

- No machine holds office or commands force. Learning systems advise and automate within fences. They publish goals and logs, and humans write the ends. If a system lies, it loses its license.

A floor you can stand your life on came next. Not a number on a paycheck, but things that make an ordinary day survivable: a small home with a door that locks and air that won’t make you sick; water, power, and a data link cheap enough that dinner and phone bills never compete; a clinic where a nurse knows your name and a generalist looks you in the eye; a Warmline that answers in two rings in your language; a lawyer who can explain your options in words you can repeat; a pantry you can visit with a quiet badge if money fell apart this month; a public steward at the library if you don’t own a device and still need to live in this century.

We paid for the floor by admitting what creates windfalls. Locations are valuable because of neighbors and infrastructure, not because you painted your wall a better white. We stopped licensing unearned rent as if it were genius. We taxed the ground under buildings, not the buildings themselves. We leased spectrum, orbital slots, sea lanes, and mineral rights on transparent ledgers. We charged modest fees on carbon, very large financial trades, and frontier compute, and we took public stakes in rights‑of‑way like rails and power corridors. The rent from the Commons funded the floor and a dividend that arrived as your unearned share of what none of us made alone.

Rooms you can read from the sidewalk

We stopped arguing about one perfect city and started building Rooms: neighborhoods, co‑ops, guild towns, and scattered communities with rules you could read on a board before you stepped inside. Every Room posted a House Tag at its edge: “Screens sleep at sundown,” “Quiet after ten,” “No drones without beacons,” “Consent rituals on Friday for new romances,” “Work naps from two to four,” “Religious procession weekly; all welcome; no filming without asking.” The boards used the same template, like nutrition labels for culture.

A few people’s favorites you’ll hear as hearsay:

- Sparrow Mile banned leaf blowers and paid for handwork in schools for one hour every morning. Outsiders got loaner dumbphones with the Warmline and Doorpost number and not much else for their stay. Bird boards ticked off spring arrivals. The bus came by request; so did a string band under a maple lit with jars.

- Lantern Quay normalized chosen kin. Co‑parents lived in fours and fives. Aunties came in pairs. Teenagers learned to say no and to hear it. A world‑class fertility clinic sat by a dance floor where monogamous couples wore blue sashes twice a year.

- Pulseworks ran on volunteered life data. Trash routes changed in minutes; carts with warm bread followed endocrinology, not rumor. Visitors wore bracelets that negotiated whether their steps counted for the day’s models. You could say no by twisting the band until it clicked.

- Bellhouse was monastic by charter for three‑year vows. Bells at dawn. Meat rare and blessed. Donations in ink. Their board read: “We absorb; we don’t debate.” They hosted people who came to hush.

- Checkstep loved argument like choreography. To get a seat at the table, you took a five‑minute listening test without fixing what you heard. The minutes read like geometry with jokes.

Joining a Room was as ordinary as registering your lease. Newcomers lived under guest defaults for a season. They tried on the place without oaths that trapped them. Health records, pension deposits, school credits, and dividends traveled on Portability Rails—open standards every bank, clinic, and school agreed to speak. If you stayed, you paid a Bridge Share—ten percent of income in your first year in a new Room, tapering to three by year three—to keep generous places generous.

Three times a year, cheap trains ran late for Swing Weeks. Children brought back loaned uniforms, pinned new badges to their bags, and told the truth about where they might want to live someday. Doors were not jealous; leaving and arriving were rehearsed acts, not violations.

Clarity, consent, and a dial on the fog

On most kitchen counters sits a sandy‑beige box with a single amber light. That’s a Counselor, a fiduciary assistant with duties like a lawyer’s. It owes you loyalty, care, and confidentiality. It can’t quietly change its goals. It speaks in reasons you can repeat, and it says out loud when it’s guessing. If it betrays you, it loses its charter.

Tied to your Counselor is a Consent Ledger: a readable list of your consequential yeses. Share sleep metrics with this clinic team for three months. Take this job but strip the non‑compete. Try a neural overlay for afternoon geometry tutoring, reversible after a night’s sleep. You can set in stone: no political donations; never share my location outside my Room; never accept a wage below X unless I tap twice. The lines hold, even when you’re rushing.

When the world feels like fog, you twist a Clarity Dial. Claims in your environment sprout ribbons: likely, contested, unknown. Tap and you see sources, counterclaims, and a little calibration score showing how often this columnist or that agency learns when it’s wrong. Turn the dial down and the ribbons go quiet. Turn it up and receipts appear like dew. The dial never edits your memories without your say‑so. It never whispers under your thoughts.

Influence carries tickets. If someone pays to move your behavior, the attempt arrives tagged with who funded it, what change they seek, why they think it will work, and how often that strategy has worked before. One switch blocks entire classes—no diet schemes, no crypto pitches. Border Filters enforce each Room’s rules on what comes in. A Quiet Net between Rooms makes it impossible to megaphone across a line without consent. After sunset, a Quiet Hours treaty bans mass‑targeted algorithmic persuasion.

Proof, pilots, and receipts you can read over coffee

We trained ourselves to argue without screaming past one another by making proof a job with a door. The Receipts Board—a small newsroom across from the lab and city hall—files three‑sentence briefs on public moves and long reads with a claim ticket under every assertion. They take no ads. Corrections run where the error first appeared.

Big choices walk through a Civic Season with three rooms:

- The Model Kitchen, where any plan runs through models that do not love it. Forecast markets post odds on consequences. You can see what breaks when you pull and what holds when you push.

- The Public Stage, an amphitheater where metaphors tie to budgets and calendars. Speeches still thunder. So do numbers.

- Pilot Lanes, where reversible trials run by default. If your lunch program can’t feed two cafeterias well for three months, it doesn’t get the city. If delivery robots can’t cut noise and crashes between one and five a.m., they don’t get lanes.

Win two rooms out of three and you get a season to implement. Keep your promises, get another season. Break something important, let someone else try. A Sandbox Office next door runs safe experiments with independent ethics monitors so “too slow” stops being a ritual excuse.

Underneath all this beats the Bargain Engine, a public, interoperable tool where your Counselor and mine can explore huge deal spaces for win‑wins: a watershed compact among eight counties; a barter among theaters, bus agencies, and libraries; a co‑op of small landlords buying a maintenance plan that keeps boilers warm. It logs offers, counterfactuals, and fairness metrics. Courts accept the ledger like lab notebooks.

Care as measurement and dignity

We made healthcare behave like aviation: checklists, instruments, logs, and steady improvement.

At night, nurses wheel quiet carts that sip a finger’s blood and a breath and run two hundred markers. Simple models flag trouble hours before a crash—incipient sepsis, electrolytes drifting, a drug doing silent harm. Units that adopt this loop see avoidable deaths drop in months, then keep dropping. It’s not a miracle; it’s clocks and discipline.

Robot‑run labs take over what humans do badly for long stretches: labeling, blinding, keeping time, verifying cell lines. Trials shrink from years to seasons with standard biomarkers and registries. Regulators post, in advance, what evidence clears which bar. Payment follows outcomes: healthy years, weeks of delay avoided, lives saved. Being wrong is survivable; hiding wrong isn’t.

Infectious disease rules feel like fire codes. Wastewater and cough‑audio networks trip thresholds that trigger rehearsed moves: variant‑to‑vaccine in under sixty days, pop‑up manufacturing lines under standing contracts, the right masks and tests arriving on a schedule, not a miracle.

Cancer shifts earlier. Breath analyzers and blood tests don’t save everyone, but those they catch, they catch before catastrophe. Preventive drugs for high‑risk people work when thresholds and follow‑ups are real. The big killers claim fewer lives.

Minds finally get seats. Private, local‑by‑default coaches handle the 80% that is practice; clinicians handle the hard 20%. Psychedelic‑assisted sessions sit within anesthesia‑level safeguards where evidence says so. Non‑invasive stimulation joins meds. Suicide and overdose bend downward, year after year.

Work, time, and dignity

With the floor firm, work got more human. Copilots draft, summarize, schedule, check math, and surface conflicts. Output rises; error rates fall; hours shorten. Four‑day weeks with steady pay become normal anywhere logs prove it safe. Insurance companies love it. People sleep.

Guilds revive: bakers with velvet crumb, welders whose seams look like embroidery, augmented choirs with harmony that shivers the ribs, clean‑room satellite builders, home‑care teams with outcomes they post in public. Licensing moves from seat time to proof. Surprise inspections, open reputation, and insurers out‑perform calcified boards at defending competence.

Three currencies flow:

- Time Credits: hours you spend caring, mentoring, teaching, or maintaining civic systems bank into months you can cash later, to apprentice, to grieve, to raise a child, to heal.

- Project Scrip: lists of public tasks—sewer sensors, lullaby libraries, living levees—you can fund and join. Scrip earns reputation and better invitations, not equity.

- Money, still a workhorse, taxes varied across Rooms. A small value‑added tax funds Doorposts, audits, and clinics; land rents and carbon fund dividends. A levy on frontier compute funds independent red teams and an insurer of last resort.

Stubborn drudgery rotates, short and well‑paid, with municipal exosuits and smart clamps that turn back‑breaking mornings into a shared hour of civic duty. If a job can’t buy hands, wages rise, shifts shorten, or robots show up. No one gets trapped.

Belonging without fakery

“No one alone” became architecture. The Warmline answers in two rings. Commonhouses anchor clusters of two or three dozen households sharing tools, dinners, and weekly yellow‑table nights for newcomers. Craft and care guilds offer a second ring. Cross‑cutting clubs—public swims, folk dances where you leave your phone in a basket, tabletop nights—give a third ring where it’s fine to duck out early.

Matchmaking becomes a craft again. Brokers get paid months or years later for what held—a friendship, a family, a team that didn’t eat itself. Swipe casinos that gamified loneliness lose air.

Attention is treated like water—essential, lethal in floods. Devices carry attention passports you can relax channel by channel. Feeds publish their chemistry: novelty ratios, bot shares, calibrated fiction rates. You can inhale anything; you can’t be dragged.

Fences around sharp tools

We fell in love with models that could finally keep up with our messes, and we gave them fences. We call them Stewards: narrow intelligences with public charters and black‑box recorders. The Water Steward tunes pumps and cuts deals with the grid to keep basements dry under explicit constraints. The Learning Steward schedules apprenticeships to maximize competence and joy under fairness constraints. The Attention Steward enforces passports and posts feed chemistry.

Every Steward must:

- Publish its goals in plain words and formal math.

- Keep a causality diary: a running account of which inputs moved which outputs, with names legally redacted.

- Cooperate with a Watcher, a rival model trained to catch proxy gaming. Citizen panels choose Watchers each year.

- Spend a scarce prediction budget. The more a prediction touches a person, the more it costs. Running buses ahead of rain is cheap. Nudging life choices is impossible.

- Accept that a Citizen Jury can yank autonomy to zero and force a human loop.

- Follow two‑keys on any irreversible order, with tamper‑evident logs.

Training compute above a threshold is licensed like hazardous material. Labs post provenance and post a bond convertible to victims if misbehavior crosses named lines. Capability shops and deployment shops live on different floors. Red Team Houses get paid to break systems and publish cracks. Once a week, most Stewards observe a Compute Sabbath: explain mode only, so drift stays visible and hands stay practiced.

Matter and mind you can trust

Ambient helpers—filters that strip allergens, surgical knitters that lace blood vessels—wear beacons and consent windows. Logs are readable. A red tile by the door cuts power and tells the Room you pulled it. Rooms adopt a Public Matter Protocol: what assemblers are allowed, what counts as contraband. Designs are open with emergency degrade modes that trip when someone bangs that tile five times.

Your mind is a sanctuary. Devices that touch it live behind a Capsule you lock with a passphrase and your palm. Changing how you feel leaves a Self‑Log: date, context, consent, and a one‑tap return if you decide this isn’t you. Almost all changes are reversible. Those that aren’t require permission from a future you after a cooling‑off period, with regret insurance to help you undo or adapt.

People use Modes like tools: two hours of geometry taste to help a niece, a week of patience to apprentice in a stone yard, an evening’s vocabulary to talk to a vintner. Joy flares exist, with spotters and aftercare like scuba. Multiplicity has etiquette: you can fork yourself for a season to try roles or places; a private ledger proves to you your other selves are solvent; you don’t quiz strangers about their forks; merges come with counselors and guards against identity injury.

Copying your mind into silicon is a personhood transition, not a technicality. A retired judge reads you the disclaimers out loud. If you go, you and your copy have separate rights and responsibilities. No one talks you into vanishing yourself in a bad week.

Streets that fit and power that behaves

Housing stopped being a lottery. Pattern books of mid‑rise courtyards, small apartments over shops, and tree‑lined blocks meet daylight, acoustics, safety, and energy standards. Draw to pattern and you get a permit in weeks. Deviate, and you still can, but you don’t get to hold your neighbors hostage. Basements get safe overnight with pre‑approved egress kits. Accessory homes are legal everywhere. Off‑the‑shelf façades clip onto ordinary frames and undercut ugly boxes on price. Ghost offices become schools, labs, and homes with retrofit codes that reward good bones.

Day belongs to feet, bikes, and buses that truly arrive every five minutes because signals are tuned for them and lanes don’t babysit trucks. Night belongs to quiet freight: small robots under low lights and soft‑tired vans. Electric motors give the sky back to birds.

Underneath, we made the thing everything else plugs into behave. We wrote clocks into permitting. If a project meets published standards for noise, wildlife, labor, waste, and carbon using a certified design, the clock starts on filing and stops on an answer. Miss the deadline, and the permit advances automatically with an audit afterward.

- Retired coal sites become switch‑on pads for small reactors or heat tanks. We reuse switchyards, cooling water, seismic studies, union halls—even cafeterias. We license a small family of modules like we license airplanes: same core design, audited supply chains, common training. Approval comes in quarters, not decades.

- Transmission stops being lawsuits on towers. We standardize towers, hardware, clearances, and compensation. Regions sign corridor compacts like highway compacts, so crossing a county line doesn’t reset the calendar. Transformer factories hit boring reliability again because grid parts are treated as strategic.

- Solar and wind graduate into infrastructure. Rooftops flip to “approved if you match the pattern,” not 400‑page rituals. Noon gluts stop being embarrassments. Cities soak the bulge with jobs that love a feast: desalination to fill evening tanks, district cooling that chills water and ice at noon, aquifer recharge pumps, seasonal heat stores. Off‑peak industrial electricity slips under three cents a kilowatt‑hour; furnaces and heat pumps take the deal.

- Storage diversifies on purpose. Heavy, cheap iron‑air and sodium‑ion blocks carry hours and days. Underground hydrogen and green ammonia carry seasons. Buses, trucks, and depots become balancing fleets by tariff: park a thousand batteries under a roof and get paid to keep the grid steady. Closed‑loop geothermal threads where the rock allows with neighborhood profit shares.

With reliable, cheap power and pipes, clinics keep fridges cold through storms, the internet doesn’t sputter, and city workshops hum without diesel rattle or price spikes. Politics cool because “yes, if you meet the checklist” replaces bespoke pleading.

Nature as a partner and welcome as a practice

Our cities become sponges: bioswales, permeable pavement, cisterns that charge at noon and bleed into dry evenings. Urban forests thicken where heatstroke maps say we’ve been cruel. Rivers get room. We pay farmers upstream for flood storage, tear down deadbeat dams, and install fish lifts on the ones we keep. Coasts grow living edges: oysters, kelp, mangroves. Buyouts move neighborhoods together, not family by family; marshes take the punch out of storms where we leave them space. Forests learn to breathe fire again. Licensed cultural burns come back. We fly more planes to light fires in spring than to fight them in summer.

The climate doesn’t ask permission. We build welcome rings—networks of receiving towns with modular pads, job pipelines, and school seats warmed up. When drought finishes a valley or floods take a delta, families arrive into places that have practiced their welcome. Bonds—small, local, forgivable if you stay and work—cover the cost of moving. Whole blocks relocate before the worst years with “move first, return last” deals; marshes reclaim the footprint. It feels less like exile and more like a rehearsal we did on purpose.

Security that brags about its brakes

A civilian‑led sentinel mesh watches tool chains that can blow holes in our day—chip fabs, gene orders, lab anomalies, identity‑layer tampering. It carries its own brakes: quarantines, sandboxes, suspensions. When it moves, it tells the story in public and faces juries drawn by lot. A verification stack—signed images, videos, and public sensors with auditable provenance—raises the cost of industrial‑scale lying. A values‑based trade compact pairs access to advanced tools with verifiable commitments: no fully autonomous killing, no mass surveillance of domestic dissent, open education stacks. Carrots are large; sticks are real.

Virtual worlds are built like ships: with crew, charters, and tested lifeboats. Each has a Peril Budget you set on entry. Zero means nothing hurts and no one lies. Higher budgets add loss and surprise and still keep an Exit rope you can find with your eyes shut. A Consent Vault logs memory edits under two keys: yours and your witness’s. A Diegesis Toggle lets actors drop character. The belaying pin is in the same place every time.

A day stitched through

A nurse in a river city wakes to the pale light that means the grid is feasting on cheap sun. Her Counselor has won an appeal on a denied care bundle; funds arrive. The clinic’s night cart pings on a patient’s drift. The doctor calls before panic can grow, because the call is scheduled and the note writes itself from their words, subject to edits the patient can read. Her shift has fewer patients and more minutes per person.

After work, she bikes to the maker hall to help teenagers bend ash into the ribs of a canoe with steam. At dusk she walks up two blocks to the Public Stage. The agenda today is whether to roof the market with solar louvers. She slides the “summer heat” bar on a model and watches ER visits for asthma fall. She flicks her Clarity Dial to medium; the board shows receipts.

Across town, an elder puts on a coat and walks to a long floor where the Rooms keep shopfronts. One window advertises midnight practice rooms free to teenagers. The next invites all comers to a lantern walk that puts screens to sleep for a night. Down the hall, a slow county wants two new midwives in exchange for a cottage on the edge of their restored prairie. He adds a third place to his list. If he moves during Swing Week, a train will come and a yellow table will be set at his destination.

On a corner, a woman leans into a Doorpost and whispers the truth she hasn’t said even alone. The plan that unspools includes locksmiths, a temporary kitchen job three neighborhoods away, a legal filing to retrieve her identification, childcare credits for a sister who will help, and a copy of her Consent Ledger that lets her Counselor refuse any signature she hasn’t authorized in the scramble.

Steps we actually took

This didn’t require people better than us. It took fifteen years of ramps and handrails.

Years 1–3:

- Put clocks into permits for clean energy, transmission, water, and mid‑rise housing. Publish pattern books; legalize accessory homes statewide; subsidize basement egress kits.

- Capture commons rents. Move hot‑market properties to site leases with public ledgers. Levy small, broad carbon fees; auction spectrum; add a 1–2 basis‑point tax on very large financial trades; license frontier compute with a levy to fund red teams and an insurer of last resort. Use the money to electrify clinics, install cold chains, fix water mains, clear maintenance backlogs, and build small, tight homes.

- Stand up a Receipts Board and public forecasting in two agencies. Require “policy diffs,” provenance cards, and retrospectives for moves above named thresholds.

- License compute for big training runs and deployments that act on the world. Mandate hazard evals, provenance, red‑teaming, dual authorization for irreversible actions, and incident reporting. Create a compute passport.

- Pilot Doorposts by braiding shelter budgets, public defenders, and transit. Map places someone can land tonight. Pay the petty fees that freeze people. Record outcomes without names.

- Launch a public identity layer so people can prove personhood, age, and residence without handing over their life stories. Make holding life behind a call‑center maze illegal.

- Start movement offices that work like passports. Teach Map Class in schools: here are ways to live and how to leave any of them.

- Begin welcome rings with modular pads, funded school seats, and job pipelines in receiving towns. Pilot “move first, return last” in two floodplains and one fire corridor.

Years 4–7:

- Commission long‑duration storage. Reuse the first wave of coal sites for small reactors or heat tanks. Set tariffs so desalination, cooling, and aquifer recharge greedily eat noon gluts. Default bus depots into vehicle‑to‑grid fleets.

- Halve approval times for consequential drugs and devices via adaptive trials and registries. Tie grants to pre‑registered plans and outcomes. Post hospital wait times and error rates.

- Convert a third of ghost offices to homes, labs, and schools with retrofit codes. Stripe night freight lanes; guarantee five‑minute daytime bus frequencies on major routes.

- Expand competency‑based licensing. Let nurses, pharmacists, and technicians work to the top of their training. Fund guilds that certify by performance. Normalize decade sabbaticals funded by Time Credits.

- Open ward schools: intergenerational learning commons for humans now, cultural templates for synthetic minds later. No actuators beyond the classroom; abundant company; patience.

- Codify fiduciary duties for Counselors. Ban ad‑subsidized personal assistants. Require objective disclosures for recommenders with a default “wellbeing” mode.

- Operationalize Boundary Meters for cross‑fence harms. Throttle bad actors automatically with published criteria.

- Publish quarterly repair targets for grid components; enforce interconnection deadlines with fines that fix the clogged offices.

Years 8–12:

- Drive power‑sector emissions 70% below baseline; bring average retail electricity under seven cents per kilowatt‑hour; off‑peak industrial under three. Fill corridor compact gaps.

- Cut mortality from major cancers by half; add five healthy years to the median life; halve suicide and overdose with sustained declines. Make the care loop universal.

- Finish global clinic electrification and internet. Eliminate malaria across most of the world; drive tuberculosis under one per million in several regions.

- Normalize four‑day weeks without loss of output. See independent businesses multiply as copilots lift drudgery. Rotate and pay remaining drudgery like civic duty.

- Entrench forecasting into budgets and procurement. Tie leader incentives to outcomes and calibration. Celebrate public corrections and “repair galleries” that teach from failure.

- Mature the sentinel mesh with civilian oversight; pair access to advanced tools with verifiable rights commitments; keep bans on autonomous killing enforceable.

- Pass a Substrate Citizenship Act so personhood follows minds slowly and with witnesses across bodies.

- Grow welcome rings to receive hundreds of thousands annually with low friction and high dignity.

Why it holds

It is good because it doesn’t pretend people stop hurting people. Instead, it makes harm walk through doors that leave prints and gives everyone exits that work. It treats attention like water and minds like sanctuaries. It brings abundance down the stack first, into clinics and homes and buses, before anyone buys a mirror on the moon.

It is specific because you can touch it: a Doorpost with a cotton cord and the words Need a way out? Pull; a House Tag on a lamppost that tells you if screens sleep at sundown; a Counselor with an amber eye; a Consent Ledger on your phone; a Clarity Dial you can twist; a Receipts Board two floors up from the bakery; a pilot lane where the robots hum and then get yanked if they lie; egress kits in basements; pattern books in city hall; buses that come when the signal says they should; a red tile that kills the room and tells everyone you pulled it.

It is plausible because most beams are already lying around in pieces. Small nuclear modules stand on regulators’ desks. Perovskite sheets cling to roofs. Closed‑loop geothermal drills without politics after the ribbon‑cutting. Nurses run night labs. Adaptive trials cut years off approval when they are allowed. Tutors shrink gaps. Pattern books build beautiful blocks. Forecast markets work in agencies where someone bothers to try. We didn’t need miracles. We needed clocks, receipts, and to love being proven wrong in public.

It is not perfect. Floods still come. Marriages still crack. There are relapses, betrayals, and grief. The difference is that you can call a number at two in the morning and a person will answer. You can pull a cord and not fall into a void. You can twist a dial and drop the blare to a murmur. You can walk the floor where Rooms keep their shopfronts, ask rude questions, and sit at a yellow table if you don’t know anyone.

Walk home late. On the commonhouse corkboard, there’s a note: Friday dumplings, yellow table for strangers. Above you, a quiet light crosses the sky. Someone says its name like a cousin. In the morning, the Receipts Board posts a paragraph about the microbonds that bought the fourth sail for the market’s roof. You pour coffee. The ledger balances. The Warmline answers. The door opens when you pull the rope. The floor holds. And overhead, the sky is carefully open to anyone who asks out loud and means it.
