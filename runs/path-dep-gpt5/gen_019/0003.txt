The Anchor and the Commonworks

By the second tram the morning has decided to be kind. Doors sigh open to air that smells like toast crusts, cut cilantro, and a promise of rain by four. In the baker’s window, a hand-sized tile glows the color of tea. Three little marks—field, mill, oven—sit above a square you can scan with the default camera. It doesn’t take you to a manifesto. It opens a receipt a twelve-year-old can follow: where the wheat drank, who proofed dough overnight and what they took home, the fridge’s temperature line from yesterday, kilowatts by hour and where they came from, two cents a roll for the neighborhood pool. You can turn the tile over and read: questions? A phone number that rings to a person, not a maze.

Between flower buckets and the ticket gates stands a post waist-high, porcelain-smooth, the color of warm clay. An indigo cord rests in a bronze recess. Above it a plate spells, in letters nobody has to squint to see:

Pull if you need to go.
Two questions; no story. Fifteen minutes.

No one stares. These posts live where life already flows—stations, clinics, libraries, schools, union halls, malls, courthouse lobbies, and a handful of quiet corners ninth graders whisper to each other about by November.

A woman with flour still ghosting her cuffs tests the cord. A steward approaches at a clip that says unhurried and serious both. Their badge is not a costume; it makes warrants real and bookings stick. They are allowed exactly two questions.

Are you safe for the next quarter hour?
Do you want help right now, or do you want to plan a move?

“Now,” she says.

A quarter hour later, the slot on the post clicks. Out slides a paper envelope and a small ceramic pebble traced with copper. Inside the envelope: a day key that opens doors on every bus, tram, van, and local train without argument; a code to a room in another district with clean air and a lock that listens to two keys only—hers, and with a judge’s paper, the law’s; a protective buffer that pings if a violent ex comes within a posted zone; a hard freeze on the skimmer that’s been gnawing at her wages; a slot at a common kitchen two blocks from the room and childcare synced to the different shift she picked from two that fit; a clinic visit across from the landing place carrying only the notes she chose, not her whole archive; and a number a steward answers when anyone tries to pry. The pebble is her Pocket Key—a cryptographic token that can prove crisp things without trailing her: over eighteen; licensed on a lift; resident of Ward Six; same taxpayer as last April; allowed to pick up this child. Her phone can borrow its powers with a tap; a paper shard in a library envelope can re-key it if a device goes missing. The map tucked in the envelope works without signal and avoids the streets she marked in pencil. One sentence in plain language she can stick on a door if someone needs to read it: She has left. Interference will be expensive.

We built out from that feeling—exits without audition, help without performance—until ordinary days felt soft at the edges and sturdy in the middle. Under it runs the Commonworks: the dull, beautiful floor you can count on whether a mayor smiles or sulks. Along it stand Anchors: places in ordinary life where you can change its direction on a Tuesday.

The Commonworks, boring on purpose

- A room that answers back. When needed for a night, a season, or a year, every adult can claim a small place with daylight, a bed that isn’t pretending, a real door, and air that simply behaves. Retrofit kits no longer make carpenters swear: courtyard mid-rises with stairs that breathe, shop-houses with apartments above, back-lot suites with honest egress, basements with smoke hoods and two ways out. On every doorjamb hangs a tag in words a teenager can parse: last filter change, pest check, smoke drill; a red tile you can tap that brings a person with tools, not a script. If the latch sticks at midnight, someone comes.

- Movement with a cadence. Main lines keep five-minute headways from first light to dinner. Transfers respect your time. Sidewalks don’t devolve to gravel halfway to nowhere. Bike lanes feel like they were designed for nine-year-olds and elders, not just weekend riders in armor. Rural loops run on minibuses that share the same app and timetable as the city and can flex by voice call from a landline. Freight naps when the sun is high so day belongs to feet and little wheels.

- Kitchens without ceremonies. Common kitchens and market halls serve staples you’d feed the aunt who always tells the truth. Tap if you can, don’t if you can’t; the plate and portion are the same. Ingredients and yesterday’s temperature logs sit where a kid can read them. Wage sheets live beside. Food safety and fairness feel like tap water—satisfying because they are so unremarkably present.

- Clinics that hear whispers. You see a nurse before you see a clipboard. A breath into a cup and a drop of blood into a lunch-pail device catch infections, drug interactions, kidney troubles, and lonely weeks before they vault. Contracts pay for days safely bought back, not for drama at the brink. Strong interventions on mind or body follow aircraft rules: training, monitors, reversal plans, a mandatory cool-down between idea and needle. Hospital night kits flag sepsis and drug-collision hints while the world sleeps. Ventilation finally joined exits in building code; air joined water on the short list of things you get to assume.

- Counsel and scrub, early. A ring of legal stewards takes the first uneasy letter seriously—wage theft, a landlord’s trick, custody with fireworks, a phone acting like it owns you. A scrub team will sit at your kitchen table and evict spyware and stalkware from your devices until your life stops leaking out the back.

- Utilities like breath and light. Electric, heat, clean water, and broadband are treated like oxygen and sidewalks—boring and excellent. Clinics, schools, depots, and markets sit on microgrids you can point to: a steel box behind a fence; a quiet cabinet in the gym. Twice a year we stand them back up on purpose. The schedule prints on bus shelters and church doors, not just online.

- Money that owes you, not the other way around. A public payment rail clears in seconds, fails gracefully, and doesn’t sell you to keep the lights on. It can pay your union, your niece, your landlord, your choir, your co-op. Holding wages hostage is a crime with teeth. Remittances don’t carry a punishment tax for distance.

- Identity that only says what this moment needs. Your Pocket Key lives under your control. It can say “over twenty-one,” “licensed electrician level two,” or “same household as this child” without blurting your birthday, address, or a full dossier. Proofs expire on purpose. Taking more than a situation needs from someone’s Key is opening their letters.

- Attention handled like a watershed. Public feeds run gentle by default and show their ends. If you turn on high-persuasion mode—the glittering casino of novelty—a small ring glows on your device that anyone nearby can see. You cannot hide the glow. Messages you didn’t ask for wear receipts: who paid; what they want; why they think it works; their last year’s record. Districts set budgets for how much public attention can be bought. Kids do not see bottomless scrolls until brains and laws agree that they’re ready.

- Paper handrails and a Maintenance Trust. Every critical system keeps a paper rail: ballots that count without batteries, stamped transit tickets, a published phone tree neighbors can rehearse. The Maintenance Trust—a legal brick—keeps money parked for pumps, rails, transformers, roofs. Raiding it to cut a ribbon costs double next year and puts names in paint on a wall no one wants to make famous. Once a quarter, a Hold-Still Day lands in public calendars; nobody deploys a new feature; we oil what already holds us up.

- Boundaries with meters instead of sermons. Heat you push into the summer air, the noise you fling across midnight, the runoff you send into a creek, the spam you pour into inboxes—where physics allows and law permits—are measured and billed where they cross a line. Don’t pay, and your pipe narrows, your hours trim, your permit pauses. Appeals are small and quick.

We pay for the Commonworks with rent from what no one makes alone: a slice of location value where proximity multiplies luck; leases on airwaves and orbits with cleanup welded to the price; royalties on minerals, timber, and fish that used to leave without a thank you note; fees on carbon, heat, and noise at the fence where your push crosses my line; a tiny license and posted bond when colossal compute or high-actor biology rides above posted thresholds; a light duty when a machine replaces a long line of hands. That pool funds the floor and sends a regular Share to every resident. If you’re steady, it’s a pat on the back. If you wobble, it’s oxygen. A portion lives in the Maintenance Trust.

Anchors, exits, and levers you can touch

The clay post with the indigo cord is an Anchor. So is the desk bell at the courthouse, the bracelet tap at a clinic, the two-finger knock pattern on the library counter after nine. They braid rooms, rides, pay freezes, childcare, legal teeth, and records under a single back end with logs anyone on the oversight rota can audit. Stewards arrive fast, trained in hospitality and law. Their job fits on one card you can read: stop an illegal deduction; enforce a protective buffer; book a room whose lock listens to exactly two keys; move the exact notes your new clinic needs without making you retell your worst day; secure childcare; get you onto the 9:10 without an interrogation. Every move writes a tamper-evident log a rotating board reads—a nurse, a teacher, a machinist, a parent, a teen, someone who used an Anchor last year.

Pluralism is posted, not whispered. Houses, guild halls, rural co-ops, diaspora rooms, and online quarters that spill into real rooms hang a House Card by the door and online:

- what we love and practice here;

- the defaults that start at our threshold (quiet after dusk, or music on Fridays, or screens down on Sundays);

- how to complain and where a complaint goes;

- how to leave and what of yours cannot be used to chain you.

Two rails run under every door and do not move:

- Don’t stalk across lines. Argue with ideas; leave bodies alone.

- Pay for costs you push across borders. Noise, heat, runoff, spam, attention floods run into meters. If you won’t clear your tab, pipes shrink.

Children are citizens with extra protections. A Youth Charter names what adults owe: literacy with numbers and words; consent that holds under pressure; two meals you can cook; time moving; time under trees; a sense of exits. Every teen keeps a private line that rings only them and the Anchors. Between twelve and eighteen each gets two Trail Fortnights: paid time in a deliberately different place with a mentor, a stipend, and a quiet, practiced trail home if it goes wrong. At sixteen a Make Year arrives: a tangible thing the world can touch, built with a small budget and a red team you can’t ignore—a handrail that doesn’t freeze, a water-data map, a play that lets grown-ups argue without cruelty. A sealed Memory Box keeps records around big choices; it opens only to the young person later.

Decisions that show their work

We retired the memo-to-mandate trick. Three rooms run through the week and don’t really close.

- The Open Trial Bench, where ideas must walk before they rule. Want to change school lunches, street priority, clinic triage, or how we schedule buses? Show your work for eight to twelve weeks. Rival models, whose job is to poke, get to poke. If you argue chickpeas lift hemoglobin and calm budgets, feed two wards for a quarter with a control next door and publish. Contracts award seasons to teams who keep promises they wrote down plainly. Miss; someone else gets a turn. Null results hang with ribbons instead of being buried. Humility earns points.

- The Switchboard, where we can touch the knobs that steer us. Traffic lights, flood valves, tree watering cycles, trash windows, freight lanes present dials you can try while maps breathe. Four afternoons a year are Handle Hours; agencies must bring a control, make a forecast out loud, and post their score next to last year’s boast. Officials who can’t update step aside with dignity, not with opera.

- The Pilot Lane, where reversible trials run by default unless someone can show the trial itself would do harm we can’t unwind. Undo budgets sit in escrow before any bolt turns. Exit criteria get named on day one.

Power with manners

Permits standardized with clocks and a Pattern Library. Match a tested plan for rooftop solar, courtyard apartments, shop-houses, water reuse, chargers, long wires, or a small reactor from an audited shortlist, and the clock starts. Blow the deadline without naming a concrete safety risk in words you would use at a kitchen table, and the permit issues into an audit window; your office’s bond compensates neighbors for the delay.

Retired coal sites kept their fences and switchyards and found new guts: iron-air stacks that breathe rust for weekends; sodium and salt for neighborhoods; hot rock tanks for winter mornings; where neighbors consented after thick drills, identical mid-scale reactors behind familiar gates, licensed like aircraft not like theater. The midday bulge of electrons—Bright Noon—went to work on purpose: chill water for dinner, desalinate, cure tile, run kilns, pump heat into bricks. Off-peak industrial prices slid under three cents a kilowatt-hour; homes saw average sevens. Bus depots and delivery yards sold balance back to the grid; mechanics saw it in their checks.

Machines with manners

Any code that can move money, wheels, heat, doses, or molecules needs a license. Training runs over a posted threshold carry compute passports: energy draw and where it came from; data sources; who signed and why; what tripwires ran; how to halt. Chips watermark outputs. Binaries prove they match the signed source. Actuators that can bite without a hand on them broadcast beacons. Rooms where they live hide floor plates a child can stomp to cut power; we practice doing it.

Once a week, sharp systems go into Explain-Only: they don’t get to act; they only get to show their work. A narrow, warrant-bound watch called Seamwatch stares at the few seams where a single wrong instruction can bend a month—chip plants, genome printers, identity backbones, market rails, floodgates. When it trips, drones sleep for six blocks, Seamwatch posts a story by morning, and it answers to a standing jury drawn by lot when we were calm. A chunk of its budget points right back at itself; no one gets to grow unexamined teeth.

Work that kept the pride and lost the pretend

Copilots draft; you sign. Thirty-two paid hours is normal, with a paid Civic Day for things that keep the place kind: cleaning culverts before storms, running wildfire drills, tutoring ninth graders to argue without cruelty, staffing Anchors. Drudge we can’t yet automate pays well, rotates fairly, and gears backs with clamps and exosuits instead of romantic speeches about grit. Licenses thin to what guards life and limb. The rest moves to demonstrations, peer proofs, and insurance. A welder earns a title when three masters sign her joints on a public ledger that ties their names to hers. A nurse crosses a border with competency proofs instead of shrines. At a certain size, firms tip to steward ownership: workers, users, and a future trust hold votes, and can fire boards that forget what century they live in.

Care like aviation

Checklists help. Logs are human. Near-misses throw parties. A night cart roams with quiet sensors for potassium slides, sepsis murmurs, drug clashes—killers that whisper—and nudges just in time. Discharges happen days earlier. Brick-and-mortar mind care sits beside sprains without a back hall of shame. Addiction moves from panic to maintenance. Teeth and sleep live in primary care. Payment chains follow physiology and days-to-stable. Eloquence does not pay.

Nature, matter, and night

We wrote material passports into law and contracts. Everything above a marker comes with ingredients, repair steps, and a graceful way back to parts. Mid-size towns host open disassembly yards that feel like a crossing between a playground and a machine shop. Apprenticeships sprout there; subsidies for repair buy verifiable skills.

Creeks see daylight again. Streets drink rain. Fire walks low and often because cultural burns returned, with pay and respect. Wildlife bridges live over roads. Working lands earn checks for holding water, growing soil, welcoming pollinators, drawing carbon into roots; satellites and boots verify and pay. Panels float over berries and sheep; birds keep singing. If a mind can likely suffer, the law leans in its favor, with more weight as capacity grows. Orbit cleans up because satellites post bonds, and scavengers get paid per kilogram. Radio-quiet rings hug observatories and a few hard-won wild places. Night returns on schedule: even in cities, half our weeks the sky is dark enough for a child to find Orion with a finger.

The last mile everywhere

Room-sized kits tow light, cold, and signal down muddy tracks to clinics that used to guess: fold-out panels and batteries, a satellite link, and a night lab that reads breaths and drops for killers that whisper. Vaccines travel in thermostable vials on drone loops that know to wait under a neem tree when a storm shoulders through. Cash-for-coverage pays community workers for verified immunization and TB treatment on ledgers they hold and the world can check without peeking at names. Malaria collapses. Drug-resistant TB follows as tests move earlier and regimens shrink. Migrant workers stop paying the distance tax on their love; public rails make moving money dull and cheap. A Prosperity Fund—fed by carbon and heat fees, city land values, compute licenses, spectrum and orbit rents—puts pipes, fiber, nurses, and transformers before speeches. Every critical service keeps a Minimum Offline: paper forms at the library, a window with a human, a mechanical clock on the door.

Belonging, on purpose

On most blocks, a Welcome Table opens twice a week. Circles of fifteen to twenty-five—newcomers, elders, neighbors—form by choice and agree to matter to each other for a season. Each keeps a cache and a phone tree someone always answers. At eight, the Atlas begins: maps and stories of how different places live and what promises they keep. At twelve, Trail Fortnights. At sixteen, a Make Year. Weavers—paid matchmakers for civic life—earn only when, months later, you say your world widened. A small amber tile by the door glows; two taps and hands move without speeches because practice makes kindness feel like muscle memory.

A day here, ten years in

Morning heat drifts off a red brick that drank cheap noon yesterday. Your Ally—not an ad deck but a fiduciary assistant bound by law—notes iron dipping and suggests beans and greens before coffee, and a lab next week you can walk to. Down the lane a crane the size of a giraffe slides a new story onto a three-flat like a lid on a pot. The crew follows a pattern you saw at the clerk’s desk; a seventeen-year-old studies for her electrical exam by coaxing order back into a disassembled compressor on a guild bench supported by Civic Days.

At lunch the grid laughs. Buses top off for cents. The ceramics studio fires because electrons are cheaper than cinnamon. The kitchen on your block posts watt-hours beside wages without making a fuss about it.

You split the afternoon between clinic and the Trial Bench. In clinic, the night kit caught a roofer tipping toward trouble two days before pain would have made it a shouting match; a dose nudge and a brace change turn an ICU next month into a free Friday. Near-miss slips hang with bright pins; you clap loud for the catch. At the Trial Bench a machinist holds up a spring you could hide between two eyelashes; a teacher reads a story written by a kid who breathed easier the first winter after someone fixed a swelling gasket; the red team explains where last winter’s seals failed. The null result gets confetti; it saved someone’s year.

On the way home you stop at the Switchboard. A kid stands on a milk crate and drags the slider that gives buses priority by the school from three to four. Blue dots brighten. A hauler asks for a freight lane at ten instead. People argue kindly and post a two-week trial. The official logs the risk and the check-back date you’ll see stuck to the map later.

Evening belongs to your block. The Welcome Table smells like onions and rain. There are always more bowls than chairs because that’s the habit. A newcomer eats without auditioning their hurt. The tile by the door glows amber; two taps and the room moves into a pattern you’ve practiced: one person clears the back stairs; another calls a steward; a third hands a map and a Pocket Key with quiet hands. No sirens. No theater. Just grown-ups keeping their promises.

Hard edges, held without opera

- A shipping platform shaved pennies by running workers’ pay through a maze that never settled. Boundary Meters on the public pay rail flagged the lag. The platform’s license paused. A judge made wage theft expensive enough to be forgettable. Workers saw back pay in hours, not months. The company lived; the trick died.

- A virtual lounge tried to market immersion as therapy and duck consent rituals while it ate weekends whole. A boundary court locked its doors until owners installed bright brakes, recovery-plan counseling, honest timers, and Explain–Pause–Confirm for deep sessions. Investors learned to prefer reversibility over cute.

- A supermarket chain taped sensor lights green and paid a friend to stamp air-filter logs. A fourth grader noticed her classroom light never changed color. Her teacher filed an air report with one tap. An inspector with a backpack and a warrant came the next day. Fines paid for proper fans. The owner’s keys flagged quarterly checks for a year. The kid went home with a screwdriver and a badge from civics studio.

- A river datacenter guzzled cold water on a heatwave and hid the numbers. The runoff meter didn’t care about charm; pipes throttled themselves, and a fine doubled if they tried to play possum. The plant bought chillers in the fall instead of slogans in the spring.

- A mayor built a face database in the dark. Neighboring systems flickered their interop to amber and fines landed. Handle Hour turned into a polite grilling around a big knob labeled “surveillance off.” The database died. The mayor learned how to say, “I was wrong,” and kept her job because she meant it and corrected quickly.

From here to here: errands you can run on Tuesdays

Years 1–3

- Plant Anchors where life already flows. Train stewards in warrants, hospitality, and calm. Make interfering with an Anchor strict liability with automatic logs that ping a judge. Teach the quiet help signal in ninth-grade civics, at youth sports, and on the back of library cards.

- Publish pattern books and permit clocks for shop-houses, courtyard mid-rises, backyard suites, rooftop solar, water reuse, school retrofits, chargers, and long wires. End parking minimums where a twelve-year-old can bike with a buddy.

- Launch Pocket Keys and Allies through libraries and clinics, with paper fallbacks. Tie public money and major contracts to using them. Make machine impersonation of a person illegal without a live, time-bound yes. Ship outcome dashboards for agencies with check-back dates beside every big change.

- Stand up Trial Benches with open notebooks and conflict-hunters on staff. Pay citizens by lot to sit on oversight assemblies that guard the floor. Tie early policies to sunsets and metrics named when tempers are low.

- Protect maintenance in law with the Maintenance Trust. If someone raids it, next year pays back double and the wall gets names in paint.

- License frontier compute, robotics, and synthesis. Register big training runs. Watermark at the chip. Require two named humans from different chains of command to approve code that can push irreversible physical action. Pay and shield whistleblowers.

- Start the Common Share and spend first on rooms, common kitchens, clinics with mind care, five-minute buses, and legal stewards.

- Build clinic-in-a-box kits for partner regions with solar, storage, satellite links, and breath tests. Pay community health by verified coverage using privacy-preserving math.

Years 4–7

- Repower. Use retired coal pads for storage and, where neighbors consent after drills, mid-scale reactors from an audited shortlist. Commission lunchtime desalination and district cooling where sun is generous. Finish two continental transmission spines. Guarantee five-minute daytime frequency on trunks and fill sidewalk gaps between stops.

- Convert a third of empty offices to homes, labs, and schools with retrofit kits you don’t curse. Legalize backyard suites. Ship egress kits for basement conversions that don’t kill.

- Shift licensing to demonstrations and outcomes. Let nurses, midwives, technicians, and pharmacists work at the top of training. Underwrite apprenticeships with local insurers. Normalize thirty-two paid hours where logs say safety holds, with a paid Civic Day.

- Stand Boundary Meters and an externality clearinghouse. Publish formulas. Make throttling automatic when bills go unpaid.

- Entrench Trial Benches and Switchboards into law. Require Handle Hours. Publish track records next to forecasts. Retire leaders who won’t update without putting anyone on a pyre.

- Attack malaria and TB with logistics and contracts. Win early where readiness is high. Tie last mile to money and respect.

- Launch Trail Fortnights in a dozen districts. Endow youth benches. Appoint independent Guides by lot from trained pools.

Years 8–12

- Drive off-peak industrial electricity under three cents a kilowatt-hour. Cut power-sector emissions by seventy percent. Push outage minutes into single digits. Publish blackstart drills.

- Normalize three paid workdays and one paid Civic Day where math supports it. Watch guilds and small shops bloom because copilots saturated and capital goods got easier to share.

- Halve deaths from major cancers; add five healthy years to the median life; steady declines in suicide and overdose. Publish evidence chains with every boast. Bake regret into corrections.

- Finish electrifying clinics in partner regions. End malaria in multiple places; push TB below one per million where kits and contracts run. Make remittances dull and cheap on the public rail.

- Put material passports on everything bigger than a marker; open disassembly yards. Tie repair subsidies to outcomes and apprenticeships.

- Expand districts with divergent compacts under steady Ground rules. Keep exits cheap. Keep receipts posted. Let variety be vivid and safe.

Years 13–20

- Close the last coal. Turn sites into storage or reactors where neighbors vote for it. Keep rent-to-wage ratios near mid-century levels by saturating pattern builds and conversions in places people actually move.

- Put four in five civic systems under Handle law with physical panels you can stand before. Add a national Hold-Still Week where we deploy nothing new and oil what we already depend on.

- Clean orbit with bonds and paid retrieval. Enforce radio quiet around observatories and sanctuaries. Dim skies on schedule half the nights.

- Codify simply: if a mind can suffer, weigh that where it bites. Keep machines as instruments, not princes. Pair careful export controls with uncensorable tutors and clinic kits so we are cautious and generous at once.

- Establish far-place charters with return tickets, audit windows, and joint benefits you can count at home: open corpora, drought models, clinic meshes, language libraries. Post interdiction bounties with standing escrows. Pay translators of far wonders into near help.

Why it holds

- Floors before wallpaper. Clean, cheap power; honest clinics; exits that work turned down the fear that makes difference a weapon.

- We made leaving easy and staying sweet. Quiet places stay quiet because people love quiet, not because gates bite. Loud places get to be loud without bullying their neighbors.

- We priced what crosses a line with meters, not sermons. Less scolding, more accounting.

- Sharp tools follow aircraft rules. Licenses, drills, logs, protected testers, independent insurance. “Safe enough” means something you can point to and argue with in public.

- Permission got predictable. Performance got visible. Pattern books and clocks punished delays that weren’t about safety. Leaders earned pride by updating in front of us.

- Attention is treated like a watershed. Feeds run gentle by default. Persuasion wears receipts. The bright ring means you chose the carnival.

- Kids get both a map and a person. Helplessness loosens. Parochial fog thins.

- We funded slack on purpose. A decency margin of beds, buses, spares, and oil on hinges turned brittle into limber. Winter came; pipes held; dialysis ticked; panic didn’t pay.

Stand in the square at dusk. Above a door, a compact glows in language you don’t need a lawyer to love. A repair crew waves from a ladder; you can read their next job on the side of the truck because schedules aren’t secrets. In the Open Trial window a line drops on a graph and a little crowd claps because chickpeas did what their chart said they would. A child presses both palms to a glass panel and watches a blue line on a map widen by one notch where buses speed up, grinning because the world told the truth in a way hands can feel. Down the block, someone finds the indigo cord without shame. Fifteen minutes later a paper envelope lands in their palm, and you don’t need to know why to know it mattered.

This is good because it defends ordinary dignity on purpose and spreads competence like bread. It is specific because if you arrive hungry at noon we can point to beans and heat, and if you arrive afraid at midnight we can walk you to a post and know someone will answer. It is plausible because we get there by errands: plant Anchors; pass clocks; open ledgers; fix transformers; run drills; fund stewards; license code that can bite; practice leaving. We make the boring parts excellent. We brag about brakes. We make leaving cheap and staying sweet. Then we go home, and the lights are on.
