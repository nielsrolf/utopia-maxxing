The Constellation of Commons

In the middle decades of the twenty-first century, a small blue window began appearing in ordinary neighborhoods—next to bus stops, in mall food courts, tucked into the corner of public libraries. On its glass was a simple emblem: a key resting on an open hand. If you pressed the intercom and said “I want to leave,” a quiet cascade of guarantees started working. Trains, flights, paperwork, childcare, debts—whatever was needed to get you from the life you were trapped in to a life you chose. It didn’t matter if the trap was a violent household or a culture that suffocated you; if your parents controlled your documents or your boss your visa; if your friends insisted that a certain type of body, or faith, or love was the only admissible kind. You could go. The blue window was the smallest visible part of a new world.

The world that grew around those windows is called the Constellation. It looks less like a single nation than like a skyful of arrangements. Big port cities and quiet mountain cantons. Dense coop towers where every hallway smells of dinner and children’s paint, and seaside barrios that pledged to rest on Sundays and take their elders dancing on Fridays. There are precincts where you can walk a hundred blocks without seeing an advertisement and wards where all billboards are public art; craft villages that tax sugary drinks and cities where you can eat dessert for breakfast every day if you like. There are communities that make a virtue of silence and those that make a virtue of festivals. There are places that subsidize a weekly “Link Hour” when friends share minds, and others that keep the boundary of self inviolable.

The Constellation is not a patchwork of walled gardens. It is stitched together by a small, fierce set of common rules enforced lightly but without compromise—the Commons Charter—and by a set of shared tools that make choice real rather than ornamental.

The Charter is five pages long. It begins with the most important line in any of our laws: No one may be prevented from leaving. It adds these:

- No one may be hit, hunted, or starved into obedience. Violence, coercion and captivity are daily monitored and sanctioned.

- Children are rights-bearing persons. Parents and communities are guardians, not owners.

- Communities are responsible for the harms they export. Carbon, toxins, pandemics, targeted disinformation—if you throw trash over the fence, you must clean it up or lose the right to a fence.

- Memetic boundaries are a community right. You cannot trespass on another group’s attention without their consent.

- Weapons of war are outlawed among us. The only legitimate use of force is to enforce the Charter itself.

Everything else is for people to decide together.

Membership and choice

A community in the Constellation is called a Compact. It is defined by a charter, a map, and a set of “ambient defaults”—the rules and textures that shape daily life. A charter might say: In the Riverside Compact we do not serve alcohol before 5 p.m.; our streets are quiet past 10; our schools teach meditation and mechanics; our public screens display only local art and the news feed we vote on each quarter; we fund abundant childcare; we celebrate the solstices with lantern parades. It might add: We are permissive about sex and strict about sugar. Or the reverse. Or neither.

Ambient defaults are the Constellation’s most underrated invention. They are how a place gently makes some choices easier without forbidding their opposites. If a Compact wants to fight obesity, it doesn’t ban donuts—it stops displaying them at eye level at every corner and offers produce, water, and benches instead. If it wants to avoid sexualized media, it doesn’t scour the earth of human bodies—it sets the public screen protocols and citywide content filters to keep certain imagery from becoming ambient. If it wants to cultivate handicrafts, it subsidizes makerspaces and certifies apprenticeships; if it wants to be built around jazz, it sets midnight noise allowances around clubs and offers practice rooms to teenagers.

People join Compacts in two ways. Some are born in them. Others opt in, usually after a season living under a Compact’s “guest defaults,” which give you the feel without the obligations. Joining triggers the Portability Agreement: the benefits you’ve accrued—education, healthcare histories, pensions—come with you on standard protocols that every Compact must accept. You pay a Portability Contribution, typically a tapered share of your income for a few years, to smooth out the free-riding that would otherwise corrode generous places. That contribution goes to a pooled Commons fund rather than to your previous Compact, to avoid moral entanglement when you leave a community that mistreated you.

Every Compact adheres to the Switch Calendar. Three times a year there is a two-week window when anyone can change membership with almost no friction. You can always leave outside those weeks if you invoke the key hand, but the Switch Calendar turns moving into a normalized civic act instead of a catastrophe. Streets hum with welcome dinners and farewell parties. The trains are full. Children choose their backpacks at the Transit Fair. People switch for many reasons—as a coming-of-age ritual, because they fell in love with someone from a place that rises at dawn, because a mentor moved, because their own preferences shifted. Lives aren’t locked by a decision at twenty.

Children and development

In the old world, we told children about the world we wanted for them and withheld the world as it was. In the Constellation, every child takes the Atlas class. A Commons teacher, independent of any Compact, comes for two hours a week from age eight to sixteen. They teach two things: the Charter, and what’s beyond the hill. At twelve, every child spends a fortnight in a neighboring Compact chosen to be different from their home, staying with vetted host families and shadowing local kids. At fourteen, they tour the Embassy Floor—one in every city—which houses small storefronts for ninety Compacts in good standing. They hear pitches, ask indelicate questions, eat. At sixteen, they meet their Protector.

The Protector is a person, not a program: a trained ombud who is legally and practically separate from both their family and their Compact. Their name is on a card in the child’s pocket, on a private number in every school terminal, and posted above the blue window’s intercom. A Protector’s job is to listen without either punishment or recruitment, and to activate exit rights when asked. Protectors investigate abuse. They are a known, durable relationship for the first years of adulthood.

Parents don’t disappear in this architecture—they are everywhere, and the Constellation supports them lavishly. Compacts compete to be good places to raise children. They do so by funding childcare cooperatives; including unhurried, multigenerational public spaces in every neighborhood; and offering time wealth: a baseline income and shorter workweeks that let parents show up. The mutual adoption clubs that once lived only in novels are now woven into zoning—cohousing clusters where families formally share care and elders have a rightful role.

Health and minds

Healthcare in the Constellation is like water pressure: ambient, reliable, not thought about until it isn’t. Clinics are storefronts. Teletherapy is as common as weather apps; when you move, your care team moves with you on encrypted rails. The old mental agonies didn’t vanish, but the way we treat them did. We scaled up what worked—peer support moderated by trained listeners, first-line psychiatric care delivered via AI-augmented clinicians, community mental health woven into schools and workplaces—and built guardrails for new tools. If you want to try the safe end of pleasure or the safe end of focus, you do it on consent-based neuromodulators under a protocol and with an “agency lock”—a cooling-off period for any long-term change, plus an annual audit you schedule with your future self. You can’t casually make yourself the kind of person who never wants to leave a cult, or the kind who never feels bad when they hurt someone.

Pain has a floor and a ceiling. We abolished the worst of it: the bottomless pit that spawns from chronic disease, untreated trauma, and absolute poverty. We did this less by miracle drugs than by plowing money, attention, and cultural work into prevention and into building the relationships that make symptoms tolerable. And we kept a range of discomfort on purpose. Mild frustration, physical effort, the occasional earned soreness keep life textured and goals meaningful. Anyone who has tried both absolute ease and a hard-won triumph knows the difference intuitively.

Memory, too, changed by increments. Many people now keep a “garden,” a personal archive automatically woven from photos, chats, calendars, and work. Your garden is by default private, but you can invite friends into groves and pathways. Consent and deletion are not slogans; they’re enforced cryptographically. A few people choose deep lifelogging and another few choose near-total digital silence; between those poles are a thousand varieties. What’s new is less the capability than the culture around it: children are taught not just how to guard their own memories, but how to be gentle with other people’s.

Relationships and community

The Constellation did not become good by showering us with gadgets. It became good because we learned, haltingly, to treat social know-how like we once treated physics: as a field where expertise accumulates, experiments pay off, and practice matters.

By eighteen, everyone completes a School of Living course—twenty weeks of practical wisdom taught by people you’d actually want to emulate. The curriculum is simple and radical: how to listen and ask for what you need; how to fight fairly and repair after hurting someone; how to notice when status anxiety is whispering in your ear and to speak back kindly; how to nurture boredom into creativity; how to be alone without being lonely. People who want to go deeper can attend guilds for friendship, co-parenting, dancing, mentorship. The point isn’t to sculpt everyone into one ideal; it’s to give us all tools to build the kinds of lives we say we want when we’re honest.

Status competitions still exist; we didn’t edit those motives out of our brains. But the worst versions of status games—the ones with a single ladder and a pit at the bottom—are damped by design. Compacts publish a “status atlas” each year that maps their visible honors. A good atlas has many peaks. Time spent caring for elders is celebrated alongside time spent building companies; winning a dance battle confers as much shine as getting a paper accepted. No place gets this perfectly right. But because people can leave, narrow prestige hierarchies lose people until they diversify.

For those who want them, high-bandwidth intimacies exist. We invented Link Hours—consensual, scheduled sessions where two or more people use neural interfaces to share a patch of experience. It feels, at best, like someone finishing your thought in real time. Some Compacts build regular Links into their rites of friendship or marriage; others discourage them as too destabilizing. No one is forced to merge; agency locks safeguard personality continuity. In practice, Links haven’t eradicated loneliness, but they have given some friendships and couples new kinds of depth.

Economy and meaning

The Constellation’s economy is insured against pointlessness. Automation dividends and a modest baseline income free people from desperation. At eighteen, you also receive a small endowment of productive capital: a stake in the things that make wealth, managed by low-fee public funds unless you choose otherwise. With their needs met, people do three categories of work.

- Some work to build and maintain the world: nursing, bridge repair, teaching, coding, cooking. This is compensated well and respected. Because work is portable, teams are fluid, and unions thrive, conditions are humane.

- Some work to master crafts: blacksmithing on the edge of a mountain town, composing for augmented choirs, building micro-satellites, bread baking, puppet making. Compacts run apprentice systems—structured, paid, portable. At public festivals, apprentice work is shown to raucous, appreciative audiences.

- Some work to care: raising children; tending parks; sitting with the dying; keeping the neighborhood kitchen going. This care is visible on dashboards, funded by the Commons, and revered in the status atlas.

For those driven by big audacities, there are prizes and pods. The Prizes are posted annually: end wastewater microplastics for a million souls; make a neocortex-compatible prosthesis that anyone can install and uninstall; write a romance that makes a million people kinder. The Pods are six-month, funded experiments anchored in Compact labs and studios, open to all ages. Failure is a category on resumes.

Governance and guardrails

The Constellation is a federation of Compacts governed by Protocol Law: legal code written as modular standards that communities adopt, adapt, and contribute to like open-source software. The Commons Council—a rotating, sortition-based body of citizens from across Compacts—manages the Charter’s enforcement and the shared tools. There is no president. When a rule must be made, it goes through Open Simulation: it is mocked up and tested in consenting “sandbox” Compacts for a year, measured by agreed metrics, debated in public, and then either enshrined as a Protocol or rejected to history. People see how it will work where they live. There are far fewer laws than you think; they are much clearer than you remember.

Enforcement has two faces. The Blue Line is physical: a small, well-trained civil force that can enter a Compact only to stop Charter violations or inter-Compact aggression. Their kits look like medic kits more than weapons; their mandate is to de-escalate. The Quiet Net is informational: cryptography, audits, and interop layers that prevent forced tracking or exploitative surveillance. If a Compact starts building a database of faces without consent, the Quiet Net flags it for the Council and cuts off interoperability until it’s fixed. If a Compact starts requiring biometric Links for membership, the Blue Line arrives with lawyers before officers.

Two guardrails deserve special mention because they are the ones most likely to fail spectacularly.

First, memetic boundaries. A Compact can say: within our territory and on our networks, these are the public streams we will make ambient—these are the posters and default feeds and recommended playlists. But no Compact can target unwilling recipients outside its boundaries with propaganda. When you visit, your personal devices are yours; you can choose to see what the place hides—or choose not to. Enforcing this without becoming censors took work: strong end-to-end encryption, local networks that don’t leak into others, default ad blockers and feed controls that children can actually use. It is not perfect. But it is enough to make life choices feel like life choices rather than like choices in a hall of mirrors.

Second, value drift and self-modification. You can change your mind. You cannot be changed by others without your consent. And some changes—those that would predictable lock you into a narrow ideology or prevent you from respecting exit rights—are regulated like pharmaceuticals. To sail into strange seas, you apply; you get counseling; your Protector signs off if you are under twenty-five; you set a date with your future self to review. People still embark on inward voyages that horrify their grandparents. But the world around them reserved veto power over irreversible harms.

Nature and the beyond

We still live on a planet. We learned, finally, to share it.

Factory farming ended by treaty before mid-century, replaced by cellular agriculture and improved plant proteins. Meat became a festival food from small farms whose animals live good lives; this is rare and expensive and most people are happier for it. City lights dim to let the Milky Way seep back into human attention; we don’t need every building shouting at night. Streets prioritize heat-reflecting trees and water features; crosswalks prioritize children and the old.

At the edges of cities are Rewilding Rings—belts of land stitched from vacant lots, retired farms, and reclaimed brownfields where native species return and kids get their shoes muddy. The Rings double as climate infrastructure, absorbing floods and cooling neighborhoods. In exchange, we packed much else into narrower footprints: taller housing in exchange for wilder perimeters. People can move to Compacts that prefer the opposite—broad sprawl and the minimal ecological ambition that entails—but they pay Climate Contributions to those who choose to repair.

We built a little in orbit. Manufacturing that once belched on land now spins in sunlight where it can pollute less and be dismantled more cleanly. In low Earth orbit, we maintain parks—yes, parks—where novices can feel weightless and look down at the ribbon of sea between continents. This is not a billionaire’s playground; it is a lottery prize you can win by doing boring civic chores.

The plausible path

It did not happen all at once. The first Compacts were simply neighborhoods using existing municipal authority to set stricter defaults on advertising and noise. A few cities adopted “resident bill of exit rights” ordinances that turned the old patchwork of shelters and hotline numbers into the first blue windows. At the same time, a handful of nations signed the Portability Accords—treaties to allow healthcare records, pensions, and educational credentials to be carried across borders on shared cryptographic rails. The climate clubs pioneered Carbon Protocols with honest ledgers and border adjustments. The first Embassies lived on the internet as curated catalogs of communities, with simple metrics that mattered—hourly wages, floor area per person, number of public benches per block.

People moved digitally before they moved physically. They joined digital Compacts on platforms that let them set their own ambient defaults and communication norms. When friction fell—when your therapist, kindergarten, mortgage, and chorus could come with you in three clicks—physical motion followed. Some countries, seeing the outflow, decided to compete on being better places to live rather than on being harder cages to leave. Voters in several large democracies enacted laws making a portion of their cities “open zones”—places where Compacts could set ambient law as long as the Charter was respected.

We built the Council after a scandal in which three Compacts colluded to smuggle arms. The Blue Line got its mandate after one Compact refused to release abused children and the public watched the livestream. The Portability Contribution got popular after a generous education-focused Compact nearly bankrupted itself on transients. We learned. We improved the Protocols. We lost elections. We retried.

Technology helped but did not save us. AI eventually became an ordinary miracle: an assistant that actually assists, a simulator that can show your town what calmer traffic looks like by next summer. The strongest technologies were the social ones: pod schools open to any caring adult for two hours a week; algorithms that match apprentices with masters; rituals that build attachment without claustrophobia; the shared habit of switching without rancor.

What it feels like

On a spring evening, you walk down a river path planted with willow and rosemary. You’ve kept a weekly dinner with friends for nine years now, through babies and job switches and a few bad winters. Your Compact bans outdoor advertising and allows buskers; a cello plays softly under a bridge. On your way, you pass the Embassy Floor; in the window, the High Meadow Compact displays photos from last month’s lambing and the Stone Square Compact advertises auditions for their midsummer play in exchange for an afternoon of litter pickup.

The market serves food that tastes good and is good for the people who grew it. You greet your neighbor’s father, who was a dentist in a place where he had to choose between seeing his grandchildren and practicing in his language; he now does both two days a week and plays backgammon on Thursdays at the elder club. Your Protege—because now you are someone’s Protector—texts you a question that would have worried you at nineteen and delights you now: “Can someone join a Compact for a year and then come back?” You answer: “Yes. The Switch window opens next month. Let’s meet.”

You learned in school that utopias fail in two ways: by dreaming too small, and by dreaming too tightly. The Constellation is neither tidy nor paradisal. There are ugly divorces. People still overdose. There are years of wildfire and years of flood, because our grandparents began too late. Some Compacts are overbearing in ways that make other people shake their heads. Some are too lax and have to relearn why norms exist. But when you are wronged, you aren’t stuck. When you want something different, there are doors. When your Compact starts valuing things you don’t, you can vote, organize, and stay, or you can say goodbye without it being treason. When you love where you live, you can help it become more itself.

The universe remains vast and silent and full of work to do. We are not gods. The Constellation is only humanity with better tools and fiercer promises. It is a world where you can raise your children without lying to them about what people are like, because you live among people trying to be those things. It is a world where you can say no and not be punished, where you can say yes and know what you’re saying yes to. It is a world where the good is not one size, but the sizes are honest, the seams are well sewn, and the fabric can be cut again.

If you press your hand to the blue window, it is warm. The intercom voice is local, not distant. The tickets aren’t printed until you say the word twice, to make sure it is you who is speaking and not your fear. Outside, your dinner is waiting, and three different Compacts have set lanterns along the river, each with their own patterns. You can follow whichever light you like. You do not have to justify your choice to anyone but yourself.
