The Commons Net and the Harbor Posts

At seven, the block is busy in a way that feels chosen, not frantic. Couriers thread along a painted lane, and the bakery door is propped open. On the counter, the sour loaves carry small stamped marks: the co-op field where the wheat grew, the mill that ran on last Tuesday’s noon power, the oven’s maintenance date. You can lift your phone or a paper card on the wall and see a simple receipt: wages by role and range, energy used and when, the leasehold’s share for water, transit, and clinics. If you can pay, you tap; if you can’t, you don’t explain it to anyone. The line moves because the buses keep their five-minute headways, because the grid doesn’t stumble at breakfast, and because rules live where you can read them.

On the corner is a narrow orange cabinet with a small lighthouse emblem. Press the intercom and say, “Harbor,” and a calm voice asks, “Are you safe? Do you want to go?” Five minutes later the lock opens. Inside is a bag that always fits: a card with transit credit, a key to a quiet room on another line, a childcare block you can trigger from your phone, counsel with real teeth if you want it, and a single chip that assures the rest—health notes, pay stubs, school credits, orders of protection—will be in your Keycase by evening under keys only you can turn. Some weeks you pass three posts and see none in use. Their power comes not from drama but from being unremarkable. Life tilts toward decency when departure is a fact, not a rumor.

Under this ordinary day run two plain architectures. One is the Commons Net: the shared, dull rails of power, law, health, identity, and transit that hold no matter what flag your block flies or what you believe. The other is a scatter of Harbor Posts: exits that work with a word; rooms and jobs and schools you can leave without a fight. Together they cash three promises you can check without a committee.

- A door that shuts. Everyone has a small home they can close and keep—light, air, water, heat, a lock that does not gossip. The basics do not depend on winning an argument.

- A way out. If a job, a relationship, a block, or a boss becomes a trap, leaving is practical: transport, a place to land, childcare, counsel, and your life riding with you on rails you own.

- Brakes you can see. Any irreversible action—cutting flesh, opening a floodgate, committing a model to actuate in the world—rides under two named hands. Big changes rehearse in public before they land. Undo carries a budget wherever physics allows.

We made good lives in the space between those rails. The Commons Net is thin and hard to break. The Harbor Posts are everywhere and trusted. The rest—streets, kitchens, schools, shops, temples—is where culture grows thick.

Identity that proves without exposing, consent that counts

Your records sit in a Keycase you control. It isn’t a profile someone else can mine; it’s a drawer of small, precise proofs you choose to show: over eighteen, skilled for this task, resident of this district, cleared for this room. Keys live on your device and in a sealed envelope you store where you like. The law treats unauthorized aggregation like rifling a locked letter box. If you revoke a key, the revocation leaves a paper slip and a digital echo you can point to in court.

We stopped pretending that a hasty checkbox at midnight is consent. The Consent Register logs consequential yeses and nos in language you choose after a night of sleep. You can set bright lines: never donate to a campaign; do not sell sleep data; no jobs below this wage; do not install updates without my voice, my word, my signature. Where undo is physically possible, it is guaranteed by contract and insurance, not a shrug. Where it isn’t—sterilization, implants, major financial ties—consent is a ritual with friction: rehearsal with a counselor who tries to talk you out of it, a cooling-off stretch measured in weeks, and a future-self check months later with a real off-ramp. There’s regret insurance that buys time and help to unwind without humiliation when you change your mind within the bounds biology and physics still allow. Big choices leave a Memory Safe: an encrypted letter to your future self that records what you did and why, unlocks on a date you pick, and then becomes yours to keep or destroy.

Attention stopped being an extractive frontier

We gave influence a price and a label. Turn up your Signal Meter and claims wear small ribbons—likely, borderline, unknown—based on how well the claimant has predicted before. Next to a pundit’s face sits their calibration score. Any nudge you didn’t ask to see carries an Influence Receipt: who paid, what change they want from you, why they think it works, and how accurate they’ve been. Bots wear verifiable badges. Impersonation without signed consent is a felony with real teeth. Ad-subsidized personal assistants became illegal; whispers in your ear either answer to you by fiduciary law or they don’t exist.

The floor felt like a floor, not a sermon

Instead of a maze of means tests, we built universal services that are simply too good to stigmatize because everyone uses them sometimes.

- Shared kitchens with guild cooks: bright dining halls where stew follows the season before the season makes news, where a teenager learns knife skills next to a grandmother holding court. Logs with safety temps hang by the door. Pay if you can. If you can’t, you don’t beg.

- Clinics that hear trouble while it whispers: storefront rooms open late; carts in hospitals that draw tiny blood samples while people sleep and ping “catch this now” at morning rounds; neighborhood rounds that check blood pressure and loneliness before they learn to scream. Mind care sits beside stitches, not behind them. Overlays that help with focus or rumination are reversible by default. Anything permanent uses the two-hands rule.

- Rooms you can lock from the inside: small, private rooms with showers that work. You can stay a night or a month. There is no lottery for a door.

- Transit that arrives when the sign says: five-minute buses and trams made possible by moving most freight to a night window; sidewalks that don’t end in ditches; bike lanes a twelve-year-old can ride.

- Compute you can trust: a public-option assistant that runs under your key at the library or on your phone and is bound by law to your interests; a civic cloud you can rent for your own model; legal counsel that answers a day, not a month, after you ask.

We fund the floor with quiet levies that do not pry: a rent on location rather than on bricks, fees for spectrum and long-distance wires, a modest license on frontier compute and high-actor robots, and thin prices on carbon and other spillovers at the boundary where harm crosses over. An automation dividend returns a small share of time technology saves. Any surplus pools into a Common Dividend that arrives quarterly. In quiet regions it covers most of life; in hot cores it is sunlight under the knee.

Variety without cruelty, exits without theater

Most of us live our days in circles—neighborhoods, guilds, schools, online rooms—that also have doors you can pull. Each circle posts a charter at the gate and at an address your Keycase can check: what it values, which broadcasts it mutes at the border, how it handles complaint, and, in steps so clear a stranger could follow them, how you step out. Devices default to a “garden” setting for children: muted palettes, visible ends to feeds, no loops designed to turn hours into sand. If you want bright lights and infinite scroll for a night on purpose, you flip a physical Gate switch at your desk. The light stays on until you flip it back.

Circles cannot push compulsion across their fence. The Commons Net holds five rules that do not negotiate:

- Don’t harass across borders.
- Blocking a Harbor Post is a major crime. If you stall a run, a team arrives with warrants; they have done this before; they wear body cameras that publish by morning.
- Externalities are billed where they cross your line. If your forum floods a neighbor in bots, if your quarter warms shared air, if your shop fouls shared water, you pay. If you don’t, your pipes and bandwidth throttle until you do.
- Irreversible actions require two keys turned by two named humans. Their names land in a ledger a court can pierce with a warrant. Surprise drills test the locks like airline checkrides.
- Children’s rights are not local. Youth benches independent of parents and bosses hold the line. When in doubt, safety and the stated will of the child win.

Legibility that makes lies expensive and arguments end

We killed “trust me” with instruments anyone can learn.

- Civic servers are open and boring. They host rules with change logs, permits and their clocks, receipts, calibration archives, and the levers for traffic and rivers. If an agency claims a move will cost X and save Y, they write the numbers and their names; three years later, you can read the audit and see how their predictions matched reality.

- Public Levers. Systems with real effects—bus priority, floodgates, school meal rotations, work zone hours—show a human-intelligible control behind glass. Four afternoons a year are Open Lever Days when officials have to turn the dials in public while a forecast map moves. You can watch the tradeoffs like weather.

- Provenance where it bites. Not for poems, but for food labels, water tests, safety advisories, political ads, and robot actions. Sensors and chips sign with hardware keys. Forging them is possible, expensive, and often caught.

- Boundary meters and calm courts. Meters sit where air, water, code, and attention cross between circles. Charges are public and formulaic. A small judiciary trained for this work hashes arguments quickly. Repeat offenders lose compute charters and pay fines that bite.

- Model passports and actuator locks. If you train or deploy above a capability or domain threshold, you register the lineage, list red team results, post a bond, and use chips that watermark outputs. Activating devices that can cause permanent physical change—opening a dam gate, ordering synthesis, arming a weapon—always takes two named humans. Surprise drills pause your system; if you don’t handle it well, you lose your charter.

- A Safety Mesh with manners. A warrant-bound, civilian-run mesh watches the seams where tail risks live: chip fabs, genome printers, identity backbones, high-voltage yards. When it trips on an odd pattern, it trips narrow: a drone zone goes to loiter and blink around one roof, a mailing list pauses for a day, a lone bubble grounds. Every pause publishes a clear narrative by morning and faces a standing jury drawn by lot in calm weather.

- Undo budgets and Quiet Days. Hard-to-undo projects carry funds to unwind them. On Quiet Days, nothing new deploys; we oil and inspect what we already depend on.

Energy cheap enough to plan around, wires that exist

We stopped awarding points for speeches and paid for capacity that makes waits end. We wrote Templates and Timers into law. If your plan—solar on roofs, midrise apartments, transmission corridors, district cooling, desalination, retrofits—matches a published template with tested impacts, the permit clock starts when you apply. Approve on time or it approves itself and the agency’s escrow pays neighbors for the delay. Want odd? There’s a fast lane with more eyes.

We turned retired coal pads into storage sites, into modular fission where neighbors voted after thick drills, into high-temperature electrolysis yards that made clean fuels when the earth was bright. We cataloged storage instead of arguing: iron-air for weekend spans, sodium for neighborhood batteries, underground heat vaults for winter mornings, hydrogen for seasons. Midday electricity became so cheap that industry relearned to move work to where the sun is fat: daylight desal along thirsty coasts, district ice banks charging at noon, greenhouses and cement kilns that prefer heat and photons in the same hour. Delivery depots and bus fleets sold power back to the grid at five and earned rent for it. Wires across continents drew a map like rails. Blackouts in clinics stopped making news.

Houses near bread and work

We taxed land by where it is rather than by what you stack on it. We legalized corner shops and backyard cottages wherever a kid can bike. Pattern-built midrises with honest stairs and deep eaves rose in months. We scrapped parking minimums near frequent transit. Retrofit codes with fewer tears turned empty towers into homes and schools. Tenants won first refusal when a building went for sale; circles and co-ops bought into ground leases that keep place-based value with the place. A Spare Rooms Exchange let households host travelers and people between addresses; credits opened studio time and sabbaticals. Rents drifted toward wages.

Work lighter, craft sharp, time with edges

The default week settled near thirty-two paid hours because we counted make-work as error. “Copilot on, human in charge” became the norm. Drafts write themselves. Claims file themselves. Code suggests its own tests. Error rates fall. One day a week is Civic Season for many: inspecting culverts before the fall rains, running wildfire drills, reading aloud in second-grade rooms, minding a Harbor Post. A Service Ledger holds hours you can’t sell. They fade if you stop showing up, not as a stick but to keep the signal honest. Credits unlock apprenticeships, sabbaticals, and bench time at the co-op lab. People brag about service because it makes better stories than the grind.

Licenses slimmed to what protects. The rest moved to signatures and outcomes. A framer’s title cements when three masters sign her joints in a public ledger and a home she built passes inspection. A nurse crosses state lines without ritual redundancy. A barber new in town studies local hygiene one morning and does a test cut that afternoon. Job markets flipped to capabilities: your assistant and an employer’s negotiate terms in minutes by comparing your portfolio, constraints, and growth goals; portable insurance and public reputations police quality better than brittle credentials did.

Proof beats posture

Within a bike ride sits a Replicator Shop: a spill of lab, library, shop, and stage where promising claims live open-notebook lives by default. Nurses and machinists, poets and statisticians, teenagers and farmers replicate before hype, and they applaud null results that spare years. At the shop, a team redesigns an inhaler to refill at any clinic—no rare minerals, a bring-back collar that snaps into a toy. The winter red team shows that the first seal swelled in the cold. A machinist lifts a spring the size of a fingernail. The fix is in the pattern book by morning and in three cities by Friday. Once a month is Open Lever Day at the shop, too, for the small things: the roof detail that wept under freeze-thaw, the CO2 sensor that chattered under flicker. People laugh, fix, and publish the fix.

Learning that starts early and keeps knocking

Children are people. Their charter lists food and sleep that match a growing body, literacy and numeracy, consent and civics, time outside, and a Guardian-on-Call who is not their parent and not their teacher and who answers the first time their line flashes. At eight, the Atlas begins: maps of how other places live and the promises they keep. At twelve and again at sixteen, each child gets a Scout Quarter: three months in a very different charter with a mentor, a stipend, and a private line no one else can tap. Big changes to bodies or bonds follow the ritual: Look—an adversarial brief tries to talk you out of it; Sleep—enough time to cool; Say Yes—an affirmation before a witness trained to test, not guess, your consent. For choices that cannot be undone, a Memory Safe waits for you on the other side.

Health that sees loops and trims them

Robot-run labs pipette and blind better than humans; regulators publish what counts as evidence and what thresholds warrant worry. Hospitals run night panels; mornings whisper “catch this now.” Payment follows weeks of trouble avoided. Cancers that once killed become rough seasons half the time. Anonymous cough-audio nets and wastewater watches keep infections from surprising us. Thermostable vaccines ride rugged chains to clinics plugged in so long people forget the fights. Psychedelic-assisted sessions run like anesthesia where evidence supports it: consent with friction, monitors with training, off-ramps by contract.

Safety that looks like help, not a show of force

The Blue Line carries kits that look more like EMT bags than armor. Their remit is narrow: to stop violation of the floor—assault, slavery, poisoning—and to enforce cross-boundary harm in public. Warrants are hard, traceable, and enforceable. Judges on rotating civic service publish plain narratives on calm mornings. Alongside runs the Quiet Mesh, an audited cryptography-and-attestation layer that makes mass surveillance brittle and visible. If a place builds a face database in secret, its interop flickers and fines land.

Nature at the table, not as a painting

Growing cities drew green rings where human pressure eases each year. They are not walls but vows: no new roads; creeks come back to light; floodplains act like floodplains again; tree canopies cut ten degrees off a July noon. Land that works gets paid to do work we all need: hold water, keep soil, foster pollinators, draw carbon into roots. Satellites and boots verify and pay. Factory farms thinned under competition they could not match without cruelty, not because a sermon scolded them but because culture moved with chemistry: staples brewed with precision that taste like the thing your uncle put on the table when you were six. Law says in simple words: minds that can suffer deserve protection, scaled by their capacity. We argue at edges. The tilt is toward gentleness.

Orbit is a workshop, not a billboard. Satellites post bonds; capture drones earn per kilogram of junk returned. Starshades let us look at other suns without lighting our sky. Observatory and wildland radio quiet carries legal teeth. Half the nights in cities dim enough to find Orion with a child.

The last mile, everywhere

We stopped mistaking neglect for grit. Kits the size of a pickup brought light, cold, and signal to unlit clinics: panels, storage, satellite links, a night-lab box for breath tests and sepsis panels, a contract that pays a local tech when the fridge hums. Vaccines rode thermostable formulations and drone cold chains. Cash-for-coverage programs paid community health workers when TB and immunization targets hit green on dashboards they control and the world can see. Gene drives stayed in labs until island pilots passed and multi-country consent councils showed spine. Malaria fell to a murmur; TB followed when testing moved earlier and regimens shortened. A prosperity fund—fed by carbon fees, land value in hot markets, and a modest license on frontier compute—paid for pipe and fiber as readily as for clinics.

Getting from here to there

We did not conjure this whole. We stacked errands you could run on Tuesdays and tuned them like instruments until they sang.

Years 1–3:
- Pass Templates and Timers for clean power, wires, courtyard apartments, shop-houses, and backyard cottages. Scrap parking minimums where a twelve-year-old can ride. Treat transmission like rail with negotiated corridors, compensation, relocation help, and interconnection deadlines with damages both ways.
- Stand up the first Replicator Shops with charters no one can buy, open notebooks by default, secrecy juries with clocks, and an inspectorate that audits conflicts. Fund with spectrum leases, carbon fees, land value rents where scarcity bites, and a compute license.
- Launch the Keycase and Consent Register standards so health, education, wages, and legal orders travel under your keys. Tie public funds and insurer contracts to compliance. Make library-run fiduciary assistants the default for anyone who wants one. Outlaw ad-subsidized assistants.
- Install Harbor Posts in pilot cities by braiding shelters, legal aid, transit, and housing under one logistics backend. Protect against retaliation with new labor and housing law. Teach the word and the icon in ninth-grade civics.
- License frontier compute and high-actor robots. Register model lineages. Watermark outputs at the chip. Require two named humans for any code that can touch irreversible physical action. Fund independent red teams and a whistleblower line with immunity that works.
- Pilot the Signal Meter and Influence Receipts in universities, agencies, and transit systems. Criminalize impersonation without signed consent.
- Start a Common Dividend funded by location rent, spectrum fees, carbon, and compute licenses. Spend it first on kitchens, clinics, night labs, and buses that arrive when they say they will.
- Pass a children’s interface law: ban measurable slot-machine patterns for minors, require feed ends, make bright modes opt-in and visible.

Years 4–7:
- Repower retired coal sites with long-duration storage using existing yards and crews. Where communities vote for it after deep drills, install modular fission behind the same fences and cut checks to locals. Commission daylight desalination or district cooling where it pencils out.
- Convert a third of dead office space to homes, labs, and schools with pre-approved retrofit templates. Move freight to a night window. Tune bus signals until five minutes means five minutes, fix the sidewalks between stops.
- Entrench Public Levers and sortition councils to run the floor and the brakes. Publish agency track records next to forecasts. Retire leaders who do not update with dignity and calm.
- Codify steward-ownership triggers for firms above a size threshold. Stand up citizen data trusts. License ambient persuasion; meter attention like a watershed.
- Slim occupational licensing where lives are not at stake. Entrench skill passports backed by transparent tests. Fund guilds that train by doing and certify with signatures and insurance on the line.
- Roll out night labs in hospitals and most clinics. Fast-track essential drugs and devices with adaptive trials and registry data. Pay for healthy years gained, not boxes ticked. Publish waits and error rates you can read.
- Build green rings, daylight creeks, and buy out floodplains before they drown again. Pay for ecosystem services with satellite-verified contracts.
- Deploy clinic kits to high-burden regions. Bring reliable electricity and cold chains to every clinic. Start breath-test TB and cough-audio pilots; contract for verified coverage the way we contract for miles of road.
- Normalize the thirty-two-hour week in sectors where error rates fall. Structure sabbaticals that pay in time and stipends to teach, mentor, and repair.

Years 8–12:
- Drive average retail electricity under seven cents a kilowatt-hour and off-peak industrial under three. Cut power-sector emissions by seventy percent. Push outage minutes into single digits for most regions. Eliminate blackouts in clinics worldwide with standard microgrids.
- Halve mortality from major cancers. Halve suicide and overdose. Add five healthy years to the median life. Normalize clinics where people repair relationships without shame and under evidence-informed practice.
- End malaria in multiple regions; push TB under one per million where kits run and contracts pay. Publish the receipts where donors used to publish slogans.
- Require Influence Receipts for national persuasion. Certify consumer AI with uncertainty floors that you can see. Bind assistants by fiduciary law and ban those that won’t bind. Enforce criminal penalties for impersonation.
- Phase out factory farming with transition support for workers and landowners. Scale high-welfare alternatives. Strengthen the suffering-capacity floor in law.
- Mature model passports, tripwires, and bans on fully autonomous lethal action into treaty norms tied to chip access. Pair export controls with uncensorable tutors and clinic packages for closed spaces so we aren’t just stingy—we’re helpful.
- Normalize Seam Seasons—two weeks, three times a year when switching neighborhood charters is easy. Publish charter maps and fund the Guardian-on-Call and the Atlas for all kids eight to eighteen.
- Make Pattern Law common: rules with change logs and tests a judge can run; volunteer districts as sandboxes with sunsets and metrics.

Years 13–20:
- Finish grid backbones across continents. Close the last coal. Keep rent-to-wage ratios near mid-century levels by saturating pattern construction and retrofits.
- Put four in five civic systems under Public Levers with human-readable controls and demonstrations that grade officials on updates. Move from local Proof Days to a calendar that funds replication and translation as first-class science.
- Clean orbit with posted bonds. Enforce radio quiet around observatories and wild sanctuaries. Dim commercial skies on schedule half the nights.
- Codify “if a mind can suffer, consider it” and enforce where it bites. Keep machines as instruments, not princes. Pair experimental minds with elders, children, and animals in Gardens before handing them any lever.
- Establish far charters with return-ticket clauses, audit windows, and Joint Benefits that show up at home: open archives, drought models, clinic meshes, language libraries. Pay interdiction bounties that melt rogue machines and publish spectra rather than rumors.

Hard cases, held without spectacle

A valley congregation preached against the Atlas and Scout Quarters so hard that “no” felt like treason. Their charter glowed. Their tower logs showed zero teen exits over five years. The youth ombuds subpoenaed corridor records and found Harbor calls dying at the last hop. A portable Harbor Post set up three kilometers from their gate. A judge ordered weekly Atlas classes on their lawn, taught by the ombuds. Elders fumed. The classes happened. Eighteen left the first month; thirty more in a year. Many stayed. Their confession rite changed. Their crisis doctrine trains counselors citywide.

An investor group tried to pass casino loops for kids as “art therapy booths.” A boundary court shut the doors and fined the board enough to teach new habits. No kiosk sells oblivion now without a brake, a recovery plan, and the Look–Sleep–Say Yes ritual under the Consent Register.

A lab near the river booted a genome printer off-book. Amateur radio heard a strange signature. The Safety Mesh salted a drone bubble, served a warrant, and paused the feed. By morning, the standing jury published a plain story, closed a policy gap, and kept panic off the air. The lab head lost his charter and kept a job teaching because he owned the mess and helped write the test that will catch it next time.

A separatist district wrote prejudice into its charter. Legal, not admired. Its teens left the day they could. The district sells wheat to a bakery in a polyfamily ward because the baker pays fair and posts the ledger. Loaves cross the line with jokes and job boards. People change, or they don’t. The doors stay open.

A day that feels like a life you picked

On my service day I staff Long Health. My first patient is a roofer who wants knees like a teenager. His assistant—local, under his key—traces the small chaos points that make pain worse: Thursday overtime, Sunday soccer. We choose what he’ll actually do: a sleep overlay that whispers instead of shouts; a standing Friday nap because Friday is his double shift; a future-self check in six months that will end this plan without debate if he hates it. We write it into his Consent Register together. He stares, surprised that an adult looked at his week with the care grownups reserve for children.

At lunch I swing by the Replicator Shop. The inhaler fix team presents their winter clips. The red team shows the swollen seal. A machinist holds up a spring that costs half a cent and grins. A poet reads a child’s story about the first breath that didn’t hurt. We clap the fix as loudly as the feature.

The afternoon is a shared-attention session with colleagues in Recife and Accra. We finish a maintenance spec that would have eaten a week of email ten years ago. My agent negotiates an on-call stipend in minutes that buys me Friday afternoons at the co-op woodshop all summer. We log off because the task is done. No one sits for the theater of sitting.

On the way home my daughter tugs me into an Open Lever hour. We spin dials on the market roof: louvers that breathe or panels that store? Two short tram stubs or one long spine? The forecast market favors two short lines that stitch edges that felt ignored. An older man tells a story about walking to work without dodging trucks since freight moved to the night window. No one votes tonight. We are practicing the texture of a life we will pick next month.

Our block’s Welcome House glows. A family arrived at noon. Elders sit on the steps. A retired welder stirs beans, sets three more plates, hands a kid a job they can finish. Someone sings the song that got them through a winter. If a hand goes up for a Harbor bag at the corner, someone besides me answers.

Across the river, the Safety Mesh grounds drones in a six-block bubble around a lab that tripped an odd signal. Two named humans from different agencies walk three flights with a warrant. By morning, the jury we chose when tempers were low will publish a narrative so we fix what failed instead of reheating blame.

Why it holds

- When leaving is cheap, loyalty is a gift, not a trick.

- When attention is metered and persuasion wears its budget and its aim, feeds feel like rooms you host, not mines that own you.

- When consent is logged and undo is real where physics allows, mistakes teach more than they scar.

- When we point intelligence at bottlenecks we can name—permits without clocks, wires that stop at county lines, errors no one counts—abundance runs down into errands.

- When kids get a map and a person who picks up the first time they call, parochialism thins and helplessness loosens.

- When minds—human and synthetic—grow up in gardens and dangerous acts ride under two human names, fears have rails and hopes have brakes.

- When the floor is good enough for the loud to use, kitchens keep dignity and clinics keep teeth.

- When we rehearse in public, we argue fiercely and leave with a plan, not a grudge.

It is not chrome. It is warm competence: bread you can trust; a tram you don’t second-guess; a clinic that catches trouble before it finds you; a narrow orange cabinet that opens without a speech when you whisper the word. It is a switch on a wall a kid can flip while a teacher shows the map move. It is a night sky dark enough that a parent can point to Orion and to a treaty name in the same breath and a telescope somewhere else can confirm both. We built it with dull victories, honest levers, and exits that worked at midnight. It is good because it protects people and planet on purpose and leaves room to try. It is specific because if you arrive hungry at noon, we can point to a kitchen with beans and steam, and if you arrive afraid at midnight, we can open a box and hand you a key. It is plausible because we got here by errands you could run on Tuesdays and because we kept receipts.
