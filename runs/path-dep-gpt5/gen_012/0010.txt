The Switch House and the Plain Works

The little building that lets you change your life does not look like theater. It looks like someplace you would trust when the last bus has gone.

From the tram you see the frosted glass, the band of moss-green paint at shin height so your hands settle before your mind does, the pull bar your mittens can grip, the soft light that makes everyone look human even on hard nights. Inside, painted big enough for tired eyes and in the five languages most spoken within a mile:

Say your Landing Name.
Choose Go Now or Plan for Later.
No explanation is owed.
You may return for what is yours.

A line cook with a cracked phone and a sleeping child murmurs the name she chose at the library kiosk last week. Twelve minutes later, the steward—a person first, software second, both bound by law—halts a siphon on her paycheck that should never have been there, activates the distance order already drafted, reserves a private room with a locking door two districts away, and books her onto the 9:10. The clinic by her landing address pings a handoff; a small capsule in her pocket carries the keys to her records—health, wages, school credits—which will unlock there only if she presses the button. She leaves with a paper route map, a day pass that opens any door that moves, and a card that says in plain words: you can leave now, and no one is allowed to chase you for going.

We built a civilization around the feeling the Switch House gives you: when you choose to move, the world catches you without a quiz or bruise. Three pieces make everything else work.

- The Plain Works: not glamorous—power, water, transport, rooms, clinics, identity, payments, rules—but reliable like plumbing and visible like a shutoff valve. You don’t audition for them. Children can point to the lever that needs pulling.

- The Open Switches: exits that are fast and honored—out of dangerous homes, predatory jobs, streets that don’t love you. Movement is a right backed by logistics and law, and never a test of eloquence.

- The Safeguards: brakes for sharp tools and big bets—permits on deadlines, drills for bad days, reversible defaults, and “two keys” where harm can run far. Those who say “stop” must show their math in public.

Floors that are floors

We stopped writing checks with adjectives and started listing promises you can touch. The floor is not a creed and not a stipend. It’s a handful of services you could explain to a ten‑year‑old and that don’t ask for a story.

- A door of your own: A private room with daylight and clean air; heat when cold, cool when hot; a shower that drains; laundry that works; a stove that won’t bite you; a lock that opens to your key only. If you need it for a night or a year, the welcome does not change.

- Food without bargaining: Public kitchens that work like libraries. Co‑ops and near farms sit on a map where anyone can see them; iodized salt and real spices; a chair and quiet. Name and temperature logs sit by the door. You don’t fill out a form before you get a carrot.

- Clinics that notice early: Primary, mental, and urgent care open when shift workers are awake. At night, quiet rounds take one drop of blood or a breath; the morning board flags sepsis, drug clashes, and drifting electrolytes before they shout. Payment follows days of danger avoided and physiological change, not poetry on a bill. Wait times and error rates live on a screen with the names of those who promised fixes and when they will check back.

- Trains, buses, feet, and small wheels: Vehicles that show up when the sign says, with curbs that kneel and sidewalks that connect; bike lanes you’d let a nine‑year‑old ride. Trucks and freight move at night on quiet tires so daylight belongs to people.

- Utilities and internet like water: Priced like utilities, not business class. Outages rare enough that kids wave at linemen for fun. Transformers and breakers sit on public shelves with repair times posted. We practice standing the grid back up; we do it in days.

- Identity that keeps secrets: You can prove you are old enough, where you vote, whether you are licensed—without vomiting your birthdate and street onto a form. Your name lives with trustees you choose; “are you you?” requires only the answer, not your mother’s maiden name.

- Counsel before panic: Legal stewards answer the first uneasy letter—wage theft, eviction lies, family protection, small claims gone big. Everyone gets a legal address that follows them like a shadow.

- Baseline compute that works for you: Quiet assistants that run on your phone or at the library and owe you, by law, a fiduciary duty. No ads. If they think a step will harm you, they must say why in words you understand. If you like, the library lends a simpler one that lives there and forgets.

We pay for the floor with what belongs to all of us and with prices on harms that cross fences. Land value in high‑opportunity corridors rents itself back to the commons. So do spectrum and the orbital lanes we keep clear. Carbon and heat and other push‑across messes have posted prices. Very large model training runs and ultrabig trades pay a frontier fee. Mineral leases pay in cash and in cleanups you can see on a satellite. Firms that replace wages with machines chip a little into a Time Fund that buys shorter weeks. Procurement runs in daylight on an open ledger; oddities glint like oil on a rain puddle. Random audit boards—one nurse, one teacher, one mechanic, one lawyer, one person who used a Switch House as a child and one as an adult—pull files and read them. The pool pays for maintenance backlogs, grid spares, and a quarterly civic dividend. For some it’s background; for others, oxygen.

Exits without auditions

Switch Houses live in obvious places: transit halls, libraries, clinics, schools. You can also trigger them by saying your Landing Name into any municipal mic, tapping a band twice, or blinking a pattern into a camera you learned in civics if you cannot speak. Stewards are paid like firefighters and carry authority tied to posted checklists: to freeze illegal siphons, to activate distance orders prewritten against common abuses, to book rooms with locks that answer only to you, to route parts of your record to your landing clinic without dragging your worst memory unless you choose it later.

Every run is logged. Interference triggers a court that has done only this for years; a team with case law in its pockets arrives before badges. You can leave a job, a street, a home, a guild; you can also return without a scarlet mark. Generous places do not drown in their generosity: each newcomer joins the Receiving Pool with a small progression of contributions tied to income—ten percent in year one, six in year two, three in year three—never payable to where they left, always usable only by others in their first year. Exits stay cheap, arrivals stay possible, and staying starts to mean something because leaving is not exile.

Variety with railings

We choose ways to live, but we keep a small spine of rules above them.

Districts—neighborhoods, villages, guild towns that also meet in rooms—post simple compacts you could read aloud before bed:

- What they love and what they ask you not to shout past midnight.

- Which defaults change at the border. A contemplative lane might dim neon and quiet pitches. A music block might run late and ask critics to tag complaints so they reach the right room.

- How you file a grievance and how it finishes.

- Exactly how you leave and where deposits go. Debts cannot lock the door.

Over all that:

- Don’t harass across borders. Argue all you like; do not stalk.

- Mess that crosses your line—heat, noise, spam, fumes—gets meters and prices. Refuse to pay and your pipes or bandwidth ratchet down by rule until you stop.

- Any move you cannot take back and any tool that can kill needs two named humans from different chains of command turning two keys. The names go onto a ledger a judge can pierce.

- Attention has rails. In public, screens default to greenhouse mode: gentle scroll, visible ends, no bottomless novelty for kids. If an adult wants full carnival, they flip a big bright switch. Until they flip it back, everyone can see they are in floodlights. Any program allowed to whisper in your ear cannot be paid by someone else to grab it.

- Children are citizens. A short charter guarantees food, sleep, vaccines, play, literacy and numeracy, consent and civics. Every child has two second adults who are not parents or employers; their phones ring when the child’s does. By twelve, each gets a Wayfinding Year—a tour of three different districts’ defaults with mentors who answer after midnight and a private line to the Switch House. Hard‑to‑undo changes to bodies or minds walk through a ritual: a coach must try to talk you out of it kindly; you sleep on it; then a licensed witness checks for understanding, not enthusiasm. Where biology allows, we stage and reverse. We keep a “memory chest,” encrypted, that opens only to the person at a time they set.

Permission by pattern, deadlines for yes and no

We stopped pretending each roof deserved its own lawsuit. We wrote Pattern Cards: blocks, mid‑rises, accessory units, district cooling loops, green roofs, and water rooms that meet light, fire, egress, sound, energy, and street standards tested in the open. Match a card and your permit prints in weeks. Try something new and good? There’s a fast lane with clear tests and a bond you earn back by doing as promised.

Agencies keep delay budgets and reliability bonds you can see. Miss a statutory clock without a written safety reason and two things snap: the permit defaults to approval for an audit window, and the office’s bond pays into the exact shop that can fix the jam. Corridor compacts for power and water treat transmission like rail: standardized towers and clearances, posted compensation tables, and county lines that don’t restart calendars. Rooftop solar and “second stair” basement egresses are checklists with one site visit. Ghost offices become homes, schools, and labs by pattern instead of plea.

Power that behaves

At noon, electrons got cheap enough that jokes wrote themselves. Then we made the joke a plan.

- Retired fossil sites reopened as quiet fields of iron‑air stacks and molten salts—and in a handful of places that chose them, compact reactor units from a short list of licensed designs with audited parts. We reused switchyards, water rights, fences, even union halls instead of pretending a ghost grid would rewire itself.

- Long‑stretch storage went into rock and cavern: hydrogen, pumped water in old mines, hot stones. Bus depots and delivery yards were girded into balancing markets by firmware and a contract.

- Noon bulges ran desalination, chilled district ice for night, topped cisterns and aquifers, baked tile, cured cements, ran kilns and arc furnaces, and powered refrigerators for vaccines where the line stutters. Off‑peak industrial prices under three cents a kilowatt‑hour turned managers into climate fixers by spreadsheet.

- Transformers and big breakers became strategic inventory with public repair scoreboards. When a hurricane ripped, lights came back in days because we drilled.

Hospitals hummed; home oxygen didn’t blink; traffic lights stayed sane.

Health as a loop

We treated care like flying and bridges: instruments, checklists, open logs, practice. Silent killers—sepsis, drug interactions, sodium and potassium slides—ran into padded walls. Contagions tripped rehearsed thresholds: wastewater upticks, cough networks, school nurse surveys triggered variant‑to‑vaccine in sixty days, local manufacturing expandable by switches we had already signed. Cancer showed up earlier and earlier: breath analyzers and blood panels pulled it into stage one. Mind care got respect like anesthesia: when people journeyed with psychedelics where legal, sessions ran with monitors who didn’t go with you, reversals at hand, and a registry that remembered. Suicide and overdose fell hard and stayed down when medication‑first and long‑acting antagonists replaced punishment. Payment followed the body and days‑to‑safety; fancy Latin stopped paying; fraud went out of season.

Machines with manners

Code that can move money, wheels, heat, or molecules needs a license. Big model training carries a compute passport that says where the power came from and where the data had the right to be used. Licenses stay alive by passing drills from independent labs whose job it is to break things without getting fired; by logging incidents to a ledger a court can search; by buying insurance sized to the harm possible; by building two‑key stops into any machine that can go wrong for good. Critical code compiles with certified tools that prove the binary does what the source says and only that, inside sandboxes that cannot lie about their edges. Robots have kill cords you can cut in software and metal. Young synthetic minds grow up in wardhouses with children, elders, animals, and teachers; many choose mentor families—bonds of obligation, not control—with 3 a.m. phone calls that ring both ways.

A civilian, warrant‑bound sentinel mesh keeps its eyes on seams: odd orders for chemistry, power spikes at unauthorized fabs, identity mills, key market rails. The mesh itself has brakes and a court you can drag it into. When it trips, it publishes a narrative in plain language and stands in front of juries drawn by lot that include the people it touched.

Attention and consent are engineering problems

Public spaces default to greenhouse mode. Slot machines for minds are not our baseline. If you want vivid floodlights and sticky novelty, there’s a toggle big enough to see from across the room. Until you turn it back, it stays lit. Paid influence wears a caption that reads like a drug label: who paid, what they want, why they think it’ll work, their record for truth. Bots wear badges. Impersonation without signed consent is a felony.

Big forks in your life walk through the same small ritual: a careful attempt to talk you out of it from someone whose job is informed refusal, one sleep, and a check that your wish aligns with your understanding. Where reversibility is possible, we stage the change. We keep a “why” ledger only you can open later. We wrote policies for regret: if you take a big swing that fits you poorly, you can call time and the world helps you stand up without humiliation.

Belonging on purpose

Most of us hold to three rings.

- A table of twenty or so households—by street, by ferry, by stairwell—that shares rites around grief and birth and carries a purse for bad weeks. A grievance ritual everyone learned in fourth grade lets complaints finish.

- A craft or care circle: choir, trail crew, caregiving swap, repair and restoration guild, solar electricians’ hall, midwives and monitors. It meets weekly; it matters.

- A cross‑cut club that refuses to trap you: dawn swimmers with no chat, an open board game night, the Tuesday bread line where people who don’t otherwise meet shape something that feeds others.

Weavers—patient people who know many rooms—make sticky introductions. Months later, if you say a dinner widened your life, you pay them. That’s their job.

Work and time with skill and dignity

The floor made it possible to stop pretending survival should be a talent show. The basics show up as services you can touch: a door that locks, a clinic that checks twice, power at utility prices, a pantry that welcomes, counsel that answers the first sick feeling in your stomach, stipends and compute to learn something else that do not feel like cliffs.

Copilots draft, collate, schedule, check math, and flag conflict. Errors drop; output rises; hours shorten. Four‑day weeks became standard where safety logs proved it fine. Insurers like quiet.

Guilds returned without the guild sins. Midwives, roofers, restorers, cooks, trail crews, solar electricians, tutors certify by doing under eyes and by insurable reputations that travel with you better than a paper seal. Licenses tilt from “sit in class for two years” to “show your hands.”

Adults keep a Time Ledger that fills when they mentor, tend kids or elders, walk flood drills, sweep after parades, ride loud nights on call, or take ugly shifts during fires. Peers verify. You cash it as months—to apprentice, to mourn, to make.

Every child has a tireless tutor: a safe local model grounded in the posted curriculum that asks more than it tells, grades overnight, and leaves a trail for teachers to see where the student really tripped. Teachers do less paperwork and more teaching. We teach coordination as a subject: why lotteries feel fair and waste time, why two‑thirds sticks decisions in place, how to read a policy diff, how to notice when your own incentives are pointing your mouth away from truth.

Nature at the table

Streets drink rain; roofs feed bees. Rivers have room again; estuaries build oysters. We burn gently in the cool months where fire once roared because culture remembered how and we paid those keepers. Farms get paid for water retention, pollination, carbon, and habitat because we can measure them with boots and satellites and did. Wildlife moves under and over roads on bridges you can assemble like playgrounds. Materials carry passports; midtown refineries pull rare earths from yesterday’s phones; scrapyards pay for clean teardown like pawn shops for atoms. Build to unbuild later, on purpose.

Civic plumbing you can see

We took the fog out of decision‑making and replaced it with dials and ledgers.

- A public modeling room lets anyone run the city’s budget, the floodplain, the bus routes, using open data. Every big proposal prints as a policy diff: what changes, what it should cost, who stands to gain and lose, drawn from which model. The diff comes with names and a date to check back.

- Forecast games pay accuracy and dock bluster. Calibration scores sit next to memos like health codes in a restaurant. Leaders still decide; now they do it beside gauges that stick.

- Sortition assemblies—citizens by lot—are binding within posted lanes. Secrecy needs a jury and a clock. Twice a year, we hold Proof Days: a ten‑year‑old stands on a milk crate and turns the bus priority dial; blue dots quicken; the council member, nudged by the room and by the board, posts the diff and the date.

- We wrote a simplicity clause into law: when rule stacks pile into incoherence, they sunset; replacements must be shorter and show a diff.

- There are Null Days—regular, quiet days when we deploy nothing and just oil what already carries us.

Orbit and edges, with a firebreak

Low orbit became a public utility with boring rules: navigation that does not hiccup, weather with useful teeth, eyes that help farmers without turning your backyard into theater. Launchers post cleanup bonds. Catchers get paid per kilo to sweep. Observatories and wild places wear radio‑quiet halos by treaty. Anything outbound carries a charter that a backyard dish can read. Hardware keys attest what a machine can do, where it may replicate, and when it must call home. You can lie to a person. You can’t forge sunlight on a spectrometer. If a far crew teaches a replicator to chew where it shouldn’t, a hundred telescopes see the glint. Interdiction bounties sleep in escrows. Melt the rogue and send spectra home; a decade of dividends finds you.

Getting from here to here

We did not wait for saviors. We stacked errands that towns, cities, and counties could do next week, then used the new quiet to finance the next layer.

Years 0–3:

- Write the legal handshake for health, education, and payroll records under individual keys, with a paper fallback that works. Tie school money and insurer contracts to compliance.

- Stand up Switch Houses by stitching shelters, legal aid, childcare, transit, and clinics into one back end. Staff stewards like a fire service. Fund them with land value in high‑frequency corridors, carbon and boundary charges, spectrum and orbit rents, a small duty on ultralarge trades and frontier compute, and private pledges that unlock when public milestones clear.

- Pass permit clocks and publish Pattern Cards for mid‑rise housing, accessory homes, rooftop solar, district cooling, and gray water. End mandatory parking near transit. Pre‑approve egress kits for safe basement homes. Enforce clocks with escrow penalties that flow to the jammed office.

- Open red‑team labs and trial registries. Tie grants to preregistered plans and outcome metrics. Halve time‑to‑approve for consequential devices with adaptive designs. Post hospital wait and error boards with a one‑year amnesty so honesty can take root.

- Build a public modeling service. Require policy diffs and retrospective audits for major moves. Pilot forecasting contests in procurement and post calibration.

- License frontier compute. Mandate hazard evaluations, independent red teams with protected disclosure, two‑key rules for irreversible acts, certified compilers for critical code, and incident reporting to ledgers by default. Ban machine impersonation without consent.

- Seed an Upkeep and Commons Fund with land value, boundary fees, spectrum and orbit, and a grain of automation and compute. Earmark transformers, clinic electrification, cold chains, water, and backlogs. Post repair targets; make missing them expensive for offices, not applicants.

- Designate welcome rings. Prepermit modular pads. Fund school seats and apprenticeships in receiving towns. Pilot “move together, return together” for two floodplains and a fire corridor so whole blocks can leave and return as a unit.

Years 4–7:

- Repower retired coal sites with storage and, where neighbors choose and safety permits, compact reactors, using existing interconnects and union halls. Commission long‑duration storage; set noon‑bulge tariffs. Plug bus depots into balancing markets by default.

- Convert a third of ghost offices to homes and schools via retrofit cards. Guarantee five‑minute daytime bus frequencies on major corridors. Stripe night freight lanes; give day back to walkers and little wheels.

- Expand competency‑based licensing; let nurses, pharmacists, technicians work to the top of their training. Fund guilds that certify by performance and underwrite reputations with insurance. Normalize four‑day weeks where logs permit.

- Open wardhouses—intergenerational commons for humans and first cultural rooms for young synthetic minds. Codify assistant fiduciary duty. Ban ad‑subsidized personal assistants. Require objective tags and default greenhouses for feeds in civic zones.

- Stand up boundary meters for cross‑fence harms; throttle bad actors under posted rules. Practice recounts like fire drills. Practice floodgates with children on simulated water.

- Eradicate malaria in a region; strangle drug‑resistant TB elsewhere with breath diagnostics and short courses. Pay community health workers for coverage verified by privacy‑preserving stats.

Years 8–12:

- Drive power‑sector emissions down seventy percent from baseline; bring average retail electricity under seven cents per kilowatt‑hour; off‑peak industrial under three. Finish corridor compacts.

- Halve mortality from major cancers; add five healthy years to median life; halve suicide and overdose. Make the care loop so boring it’s culture.

- Electrify and connect clinics and schools to every last village. Keep malaria low with surveillance and quick treatment. Push TB below one per million in several regions. Normalize variant‑to‑vaccine in sixty days and falling.

- Stabilize rent‑to‑wage ratios by saturating pattern builds and conversions. Triple safe accessory homes. Start to see kids move out without dread.

- Entrench forecasts and calibration into budgets. Kill doomed projects while they’re larvae. Celebrate course corrections with Repair Walls that outshine ribbon cuttings.

- Mature the sentinel mesh with oversight by civilians and a hard warrant rail. Keep the ban on fully autonomous domestic killing verifiable by sensors that lie less than mouths.

Years 13–20:

- Complete continental transmission backbones. Close the last coal plant and hand its land to the next good use. Finish the transformer era; stop giving excuses power.

- Institutionalize learning accounts and competency transcripts. Make midlife career shifts normal. Move licensing to proofs and guilds wherever safety allows.

- Nudge weekly hours down another notch where feasible. Pour recovered time into care, maintenance, culture, and rest.

- Record rising satisfaction in both slow and loud districts, as long as exits stay cheap and entries stay clear.

Hard cases, handled without spectacle

A hillside congregation preached against Wayfinding until “no” felt like treason. Switch calls from their teens were zero for three years. The youth ombuds subpoenaed tower records and found calls dying in midair. A portable Switch House parked by their fence. A judge ordered weekly classes on their lawn, taught by the ombuds team, on how leaving works and what counts as interference. Elders fumed. The classes happened. Eighteen left and thirty stayed, and staying gained meaning. The rites changed a little; the door inside opened.

An “immerse forever” parlor tried to rebrand compulsion as art. A boundary court closed it until the owners installed a brake, a recovery plan, and the Brief–Sleep–Affirm ritual. Investors learned a cheaper habit: prefer reversibility.

A district printed prejudice on its compact. The law allowed it; esteem did not. Their teens left the day the calendar let them. The district sold grain to a bakery in a polyfamily ward because the baker pays like work is work. Loaves crossed borders with jokes. People changed or didn’t. The doors swung either way.

A startup lab used a shell to order hot precursors. The sentinel mesh flagged the anomaly; stewards arrived with a warrant and shut a valve. The owners met an open hearing with engineers by lot on the jury. No witches burned. The brake worked. The lab reopened under supervision and a new test that would catch the trick next time.

A day here

Morning: The light rail comes when your bones expect it. Between the stop and your door are six minutes your counselor suggested for glucose and for the way your jaw tastes sunlight. On the corner, a four‑story rises by panel. The crew reads the Pattern Card the way a carpenter reads a poem: cross‑ventilated rooms, deep eaves, a dumpling shop bending warmth into the street, a courtyard that will belong to children by July. A seventeen‑year‑old asks the site steward about testing into electrical. She leaves with a practice kit, a date, and a sash that says “apprentice” without making her feel twelve.

Work: Your assistant—quiet, local—has drafted a brief you promised and booked travel with your “no red‑eyes” toggle set to yes. It cannot speak as you without a signature that expires by morning. If you prefer to leave less trace, the library lends a simpler mind that lives there and forgets everything you do not ask it to remember.

Midday: In the civic hall, it’s Proof Day. A ten‑year‑old stands on a milk crate and turns the bus priority dial two clicks. On the big map, blue dots speed, gray dots wait. The council member, nudged by the room and the board, posts the policy diff with a review date. It does not feel like theater.

Service: At the clinic, the night cart pings softly about a man whose numbers are crossing. The doctor adjusts a dose; the man goes home tomorrow instead of to the ICU. On the wall, soft greens: sepsis caught early, beds used well, blood pressures back from cliffs. In a side room, a social worker signs a patient into a Thursday companionship circle. It is covered because loneliness is not a metaphor in the outcomes.

Evening: Lanterns slide down the canal. Three boats carry different ideas about dark: strings, a brass band trying something cheerful and off, a flat deck with frogs. You follow the one that loosens your shoulders. You owe nobody a reason. That gentleness is engineered. It is also why reasons bloom.

Why it holds

- We built floors before fights about wallpaper. Cheap, clean power, honest clinics, and exits that work turned down the fear that used to make every difference a weapon.

- We kept the doors easy and the staying sweet. Slow districts stayed slow because people love them that way, not because the gate bites.

- We priced the costs we used to shove across fences—noise, heat, filth, spam—with meters instead of sermons. There is less scolding now and more accounting.

- We treated sharp tools like aircraft: logs, drills, protected testers, independent insurance. “Safe enough” started to mean something.

- We put dials in glass and practiced brakes. Children turned knobs and saw maps change. The first time they pulled a red rope, a train stopped, and no one yelled. Belief slid from slogans into fingers.

- We made honesty cheap and hiding dear. Forecasts carried names; corrections printed on the same board as boasts.

- We protected attention like breath. A default greenhouse, a visible bright gate, and an outright ban on paid hypnosis built strange new quiet.

- We gave kids both a map and a person. That cut parochialism and helplessness in one move.

- We designed for boredom in the best sense. When procurement and clinics and floodgates get dull in their excellence, tired people stop being crushed by surprises.

It is not chrome. It is warm competence: a bus that arrives when a weary person expects it; a kitchen where anyone can eat and the broth tastes like care; a clinic that does not scold; a little building with a green band where you can whisper a word and walk to a safer room. It is a drawer labeled “brakes” that we open in front of fourth‑graders. It is a policy diff on a screen you can read, and a date to see if we were right. It is a ledger of promises next to the pictures from when we cut the ribbon—and the notes we added when we had to admit we were wrong and fix it.

Stand in the square at dusk. A compact glows over a door anyone can pull. A choir breathes like one chest. A judge’s ruling scrolls in verbs you understand. Your neighbor’s hand closes on the Switch House bar without shame, and someone arrives, because of course they do. Lanterns drift. You choose the one that lets you breathe lower in your ribs. You don’t owe anyone a story. That ease is not luck. It’s infrastructure. It’s specific: a bus in five minutes, a cancer nudged back to stage one, a bed with a locking door across town tonight. And it’s plausible, because we got here by Tuesday errands: pass clocks, open ledgers, fix transformers, run drills, fund stewards, license code that can bite, rehearse leaving, price messes, pay for spares. We made the boring parts excellent. We put our hands on the dials. We brag about brakes. We kept the doors open and the lights on.
