The Lattice and the Doorways

At first light the block wakes like a lung. Heat from the bakery’s night oven drifts across two roofs to warm the pool where a dozen elders will stretch sore shoulders. The pool’s chill went to a cold-storage tank under the grocer at midnight. Both swaps are logged on a public board by the alley—wheat lot, oven draw, kilowatt-hours, temperatures, emissions avoided—plain as a check register. You can touch the screen and watch how the block’s batteries will run the elevator at four, how the bread ran lighter on electricity because the wind blew hardest after midnight, how the bakers sent one percent into the neighborhood fund because we agreed bread should always be available at the pantry, no questions asked. No one argues much because the meter doesn’t flatter and the meter does not lie.

Beside the tram stop there’s a bronze-glass room the size of two wardrobes. You can’t miss the handle: a welded bar as thick as a forearm. Above it: “Speak your Sign. We’ll get you unstuck.” Inside, a calm voice uses the accent you grew up with.

Where do you want to land? What is holding you here that you did not choose?

A man with a backpack and a folder steps in. He says a syllable only he and a librarian know. The room does not ask him to prove he is suffering. It verifies a missing paycheck and freezes an illegal deduction. It reserves a bed with a working lock in a receiving town that keeps first-month rent light and keeps the tool library open late. It books a train for tonight. A Guide appears on his phone and says there’s a clinic near the new address; his health record will be there when he arrives. The three small fees that keep people stuck are paid by a fund designed for that job. Fifteen minutes after speaking his Sign, he steps back into the morning with a plan, a ticket, and the startled feeling when a knot is cut by a knife meant for knots.

We built a country around that feeling. Its frame is the Lattice and the Doorways.

The Lattice is the shared spine: water, power, roads, servers, clinics, courts, and the narrow rules that make us legible to one another. It is dull on purpose; it is audited like a dam. The Doorways are the rights you carry: to leave, to prove enough without baring everything, to choose neighborhoods with different textures and still be welcome in the street we all own. Together they make floors you can feel underfoot and exits that work when you need them.

Floors that are rooms, not slogans

Freedom got boring in the best sense. The foundation is not a voucher. It is a set of rooms and rails a tired person can navigate.

- Kitchens: walk‑in halls where staple food, clean water, and a chair are normal. Temperature logs hang by the door. The cooks’ names are on the wall. You do not perform your pain to enter.

- Rooms: small spaces with locks that work and hot water that does not lie. Come for a night or a month. Dignity is not a raffle.

- Clinics: primary and mental health care that answers in hours. A five‑minute nightly check—breath in a straw, a fingertip prick—lets a Night Round robot nudge a nurse at 3 a.m. if sepsis whispers or electrolytes drift. Payment follows outcomes and days avoided, not billing alchemy.

- Movement and bandwidth: buses that come when the sign says they will; sidewalks worth shoveling; an internet that does not blink in rain; bike trains for children with a chaperone at front and back.

- Counsel and compute: a public option for legal help that does not mortgage your week; a way to run your private model on a civic server under your key. Personal assistants—Companions—live on your device or with a licensed data trustee whose duty to you is enforceable in court. They cannot advertise to you. If a voice is allowed in your ear, it owes you fiduciary duty.

- Quiet cash: a public payment rail with privacy—Civic Pay—so a landlord, a clinic, or your mother can be paid fast without selling your life to an ad exchange.

Doorways you can reach

The extraction network is staffed like firehouses. You can walk into a Doorway room, draw a shape for a camera if speaking is dangerous, or press two spots on the bracelet most of us took at the library. A team arrives. Sometimes they walk you across a threshold. Sometimes they walk beside you while you read a boss a law. Sometimes they freeze a garnishment or terminate a dangerous lease. Their runs are recorded with names redacted, then audited quarterly by rotating panels: a nurse, a teacher, a mechanic, a lawyer, and someone who once used a Doorway. Bad work ends jobs. Good work earns trust anyone can see.

Children aren’t property; they are citizens. Their rights sit in a short law you can teach in an afternoon. Parents and communities are charged with literacy to a floor, numeracy, health, basic civics, consent, and a Map & Compass curriculum that shows real options. Every young person gets two Wayfaring Terms between twelve and eighteen: months in very different charters with a mentor, a stipend, and a private line to a youth bench that can step in when home is not enough. Any changes to a child’s body or memory that cannot be undone live behind time locks opened only with the child’s consent when they come of age. We keep a “memory escrow”—encrypted explanations of why choices were made—that only the child can open when they’re ready.

We asked time of one another. Every adult owes two or three weeks a year to keep the weave from fraying: elder care, shelter nights, pump maintenance, ninth‑grade debate. A public Common Hours ledger—not currency; not tradable—records time you cannot buy. It fades if you stop showing up, not to punish but to keep it honest. In return, hours open doors: apprenticeships, tool libraries, sabbaticals, low‑interest loans without jagged edges. Employers learned to plan around it the way they plan around holidays. Insurers noticed accidents drop on the weeks after people returned from service.

Places with textures and exits

Most of us live in districts that declare their defaults in public. We call them Charters, and we hang them on real walls.

The Charter at the gate and at a stable web address explains:

- What the place values.

- Which defaults change at the border: perhaps phones go to muted by default here, sarcasm is labeled, neon channels live behind an attention hedge, or music runs late. Children’s privacy halos glow when cameras glance.

- How you complain and who has to listen.

- How discipline works, what appeals look like, and exactly how you can leave.

Every child learns the map. No district gets to beam persuasion across a fence without consent. Whisper “compass” to a coach, and the Doorway phone tree lights, not the elders’ council.

Quiet rules that end loud fights

We did not wish trust into being. We made it inspectable.

- Carry keys. Your civic identity is a key that lives with you and trustees you name. You can prove you’re of age without telling a bartender your address. Pensions, medical records, safety certifications move with you on Carry Rails. Cryptographic proofs show enough while keeping the rest zipped.

- Provenance where it matters. Food labels, lab results, loans, robot actions, safety recalls, political ads carry signed trails. Artists can be anonymous. Drinking‑water labs cannot.

- Two‑keys, three‑watchers. Any irreversible instruction—releasing a pathogen, commanding heavy machinery to cut a load‑bearing beam, initiating lethal force—requires two named humans, logs to an immutable ledger, and review by a rotating board with citizens, engineers, and ethicists. Compilers and sandboxes are certified like aircraft parts.

- Boundary Meter. Warm air beyond your fence; foul a shared river; spray malicious code across a line; flood someone else’s attention with bots—and you pay a posted formula. Refuse and your bandwidth, grid connection, or shipments throttle until you stop. It’s not a sermon; it’s a utility bill.

- Compute with guardrails. Models above thresholds or near actuators run on attested chips that meter power, sign lineage, and live in sandboxes. Developers hold licenses like crane operators: they red‑team, log incidents, buy insurance, and publish hazard evaluations. Big training runs chase the sun under energy‑aware schedulers so noon bulges become gifts, not brownouts. Independent safety institutes can test and disclose unsafe behavior without retaliation.

- Bots label themselves. Impersonation without explicit, per‑use consent is illegal. Consumer systems must reveal their objective functions and ship with “wellbeing” and “transparent” modes on by default. Feeds publish their chemistry—novelty‑to‑veracity ratios, bot share, fiction calibration—and change only when your hand turns a visible knob.

- Brakes you can touch. There is a narrow Pause Grid allowed to halt classes of harm—mass coercion aimed to trap people, lab actions that cross biosafety lines, irreversible damage to open commons like night skies—long enough for a hearing. Every activation logs publicly. Children tour a simulator and pull override cords the way they pull a fire alarm. Security brags about its brakes.

- Manual Override Rule. Any public system that controls the world beyond a room—traffic lights, floodgates, district cooling—owes a human‑comprehensible control layer with dials a committee can turn while the public watches forecasts update. Officials demonstrate the dials quarterly. Twisting the wrong dial is safer than yanking a cable without a plan.

- Consent as engineering. For consequential commitments—body changes, sealed‑role immersions, joining a district with heavy norms—we practice Brief–Cool–Confirm: an adversarial briefing that tries to talk you out of it; a cooling‑off period measured in sleep cycles; recommitment in front of a licensed witness whose job is to test for consent, not infer it. Consent receipts live with your key.

Power made boring, work made lighter

We took the romance out of energy fights and wrote yes, provided into law. If your project meets noise, wildlife, waste, and carbon standards everyone can read, the clock starts. Miss it without a safety finding on paper, and the office’s reliability bond pays a fine that funds the bottleneck you were stuck in.

- Retired coal sites turned inside their fences into quiet workhorses: modular reactors where neighbors chose them; stacks of iron‑air batteries and molten‑salt tanks where that made more sense. We reused switchyards and skills. We licensed a handful of proven 200–300 megawatt reactor models and kept them identical like railcars.

- Transmission stopped dying at county lines. Corridor compacts set clearances and compensation. Crossing a boundary did not restart the clock. Interconnection queues went on a public ledger. Fines for delay flowed to the offices that could fix them.

- Solar and wind matured into infrastructure. On bright days, cheapest noon power ran desalination and aquifer charging, made ice for tomorrow’s cooling, and charged fleets of buses that sold frequency support back to the grid at dinner.

- Storage became a portfolio. Iron‑air and sodium carried hours to days. Cavern hydrogen and green ammonia bridged seasons. Depot fleets spoke in kilowatts the way they once spoke in diesel.

- Microgrids turned into code. Schools and clinics island gracefully using kits you can order. The dialysis chair never dies when the wind rises.

With electricity cheap and reliable, lots of what used to be heroics became background hum: clinics with stable cold chains; servers that stop stuttering in drizzle; workshop districts humming because you can run tools all day without diesel throats. Fights got shorter because anyone meeting the standard could get a turn, and standards fit on a page.

Homes with pattern, streets with patience

We published a Pattern Library the way we once published code: illustrated, human‑scale designs that clear light, sound, fire, and egress.

- Mid‑rise courtyards with cross‑ventilated flats and a corner shop.

- Four‑over‑ones with deep eaves and stairs you don’t dread.

- Safe basement egress kits that don’t turn storms into traps.

- Preapproved office conversions into homes and schools without bespoke pain.

Match a pattern and your permit is by right. Deviate? Take a fast lane that tests you in public against clear checks. Deliveries claim the night between one and five. Day belongs to feet, bikes, and buses that come every five minutes because signals are tuned for them and trucks sleep. Parking minimums vanished where a twelve‑year‑old can ride to school.

Work, time, and the dignity of skill

We fund the floor like roads: by counting honestly what we share—carbon we still burn; location value created by others’ labor; spectrum and orbits; mineral commons; a small duty on frontier compute and private use of open protocols; and a modest wealth duty far up the hill. From that comes utility‑grade basics everyone uses: rooms, clinics, bandwidth, transit, pantries without penalties for being broke, legal help for ordinary disasters, childcare that runs on time, a learning stipend with baseline compute. A Civic Dividend lands each quarter. If you are comfortable, it tilts. If you are not, it is oxygen.

Above a certain size, firms convert to steward ownership: votes split among workers, users, and a long‑term trust. Data about us live in citizen trusts whose trustees we elect; sectors negotiate access or go without. Ad‑subsidized Companions are illegal on a simple theory: if a voice is allowed inside your day, it owes you.

Licensing shrank to what truly protects. The rest turned into skill proofs. A barber arriving from Lima learns local sanitation in a day and shows a cut. A nurse crosses a state line without rebuilding a shrine. A welder’s title sticks when three masters sign a public ledger with their reputations staked to hers. The four‑day week became the quiet default not by fiat but because output rose when Companions handled drudgery and meetings earned their keep. Time itself became currency: the Common Hours you spend mentoring, caring, keeping lights on can be cashed for months to grieve, apprentice, or build.

Care you can instrument

Immortality did not arrive. Discipline did.

- Night Rounds on quiet casters take micro‑samples and run markers. If trouble stirs, a nurse’s wrist buzzes. Avoidable deaths fall and keep falling.

- Robot labs execute human‑written protocols with boring rigor: version‑controlled cells, documented reagents, error bars that don’t wander. They are licensed like factories that can explode. Their gift is discipline at scale.

- Contagion defense functions like a utility. Wastewater and cough monitors trip thresholds. Plans we rehearsed click on: vaccine updates move from design to clinic in under sixty days; standing contracts spin up pop‑up manufacturing; masks, tests, and antivirals arrive on schedule. We pay for days shaved from “variant flagged” to “first shot.”

- Cancer shifts left. Breath analyzers and ultrasensitive blood work pull cases into Stage I. For known risks, chemopreventive regimens push back pre‑cancerous clones. The worst killers shrink.

- Mind health gets both humans and tools. Private coach AIs handle the practice; clinicians handle the knots. Where evidence justifies it, psychedelic‑assisted sessions run like anesthesia with charts, not like festivals. Social prescriptions are normal because loneliness shows up in outcomes.

- Addiction moves from sermon to scaffold. Medication first. Slow‑release antagonists normal. Test strips free in bar bathrooms. Overdoses don’t vanish. They stop erasing ZIP codes.

- Antibiotic stewardship lives in budgets, not posters. E‑prescribing requires cultures or a documented rationale for broad‑spectrum guns. Wastewater flags resistance genes. Stewardship teams get paid like firefighters.

Learning braided through life

Every child has a tireless tutor that knows their teacher, their dialect, and their gaps. It runs on a device or a civic server and leaves a clear trail of what it taught and how it judged. Teachers spend less time on paperwork and more on teaching. Third graders run jammed markets and learn why lotteries feel fair and waste time. Fifth graders write charters and feel what requiring two‑thirds for everything does to momentum. Teenagers graduate with a body of work and a pattern of persistence, not just a naked number.

Guilds revive. When a robot can mill a perfect joint, what makes a chair worth keeping is taste, care, and provenance. Midwives, solar electricians, machinists, hospice aides, cooks, restorers, trail crews form local orders. Liability insurers and portable reputations enforce quality better than paper seals ever did.

Belonging with buildings and rituals

Welcome Houses glow on Thursdays. Circles of fifteen to twenty households meet by choice and agree to matter to one another for a while. There’s a phone tree you can wake at 2 a.m., an emergency cache behind a false wall, and rituals that make names stick. Each season there’s a Kin Supper where grievances are named without theater and resolved without humiliation. By fifteen, every kid has sat through long evenings about consent and anger that does not crush. Status still exists, but ladders fragment. Every month the square fills for Trades Night: a hospice aide and a machinist, a coder and a janitor, each names a moment they were proud and earns clean applause.

Truth with receipts

We built a public modeling and claims service called the Civic Observatory. It hosts code for budgets, bus routes, flood maps, school enrollments. Anyone can run scenarios with their own assumptions. Arguments carry provenance cards: data, citations, model version. Columnists can still be wrong; their calibration scores sit beside their headshots. Agencies can still hesitate; they must publish their probability distributions and update them in public. Being wrong stops ending careers. Hiding wrongness does.

Government runs on a civic kernel: boring, open‑source bones that host rules, permit clocks, decision logs, and “policy diffs.” To change a rule you file a diff with a predicted effect and a date for a look‑back. Procurement carries forecast ranges and track records. Twice a year we hold Null Days—official pauses to deploy nothing and oil what we already depend on.

Nature and the night

Cities became sponges. Permeable pavements and bioswales slowed storms. Parks doubled as floodplains. Cisterns charged at noon on cheap power and bled into dry evenings. Canopies thickened where heat maps show heart attacks; crews chased leaks with acoustic ears. Rivers widened their margins. Deadbeat dams came down; others added fish lifts and had to earn their keep. Farmers were paid upstream for flood storage. Overpasses and underpasses for animals clicked together from standard pieces. Coasts grew living edges: oysters filter, kelp breaks waves, mangroves return where we stripped them. We paid for measurable services—water clarity, nursery function, storm buffering—so restoration crews could make payroll without grant bingo.

Half the nights are starry because we chose that. Low orbit became a utility with bonds for cleanup. Bounties pay per kilo of captured junk. Radio‑quiet rings protect observatories and certain wild places. Commercial skies dim on schedule.

The world beyond your street

We wrote an Open Tools Pact: access to chips and critical tools for verifiable commitments—no mass surveillance of dissent, no autonomous kill chains, open education stacks, audit rights for rights. The carrot is big—finance, training, tools. The stick exists. In parallel, we flooded the world with uncensorable education Companions, open health packages, and satellite links hard to jam. Transparency tech—tiny satellites, authenticated imagery, open analysis—makes hiding atrocities harder and rallying precise pressure easier.

A Lift Fund drew slivers from carbon fees, land leases in hot corridors, spectrum auctions, and the frontier duty. It bought clinic‑in‑a‑crate kits with panels, storage, and satellite links; electrified health posts; paid nurses on time; funded clean water and boring logistics. Malaria and tuberculosis slip toward noise not by miracle but by stubborn logistics that do not flicker. Migration is not a crisis but a system we practice: Welcome Networks designate receiving towns, pre‑permit pads, train school staff, and stand job pipelines before storms. Move First, Return Last programs help families leave floodplains and fire corridors before the worst, then return if risk drops, with cash matched to measured risk.

How we got here

Years 1–3:
- Pass yes‑provided clocks for clean power, transmission, water reuse, and pattern‑based housing that meet posted standards. Agencies post reliability bonds that pay if they blow clocks without documented safety findings.

- Publish the Pattern Library for apartments, corner shops, cottages, and office retrofits; legalize by right statewide where it applies. Set interconnection deadlines with liquidated damages both ways.

- Stand up Public Shops—open labs and studios with governance you can’t buy. Secrecy only by citizen juries on timers. An auditor with subpoena power. Funded from spectrum, land value, and a small duty on frontier compute.

- Install Doorway rooms at transit hubs, clinics, and courts; write strict liability and rotating audits into law; teach the gesture teens will actually use.

- Launch the civic kernel—rules, permit clocks, decision logs, the Civic Observatory; attach the Manual Override Rule to one city and require quarterly dial demonstrations.

- License frontier compute and high‑capability deployments with hazard evaluations, red‑teaming, incident reporting, and an insurer‑of‑last‑resort pool. Ban ad‑subsidized Companions; fund library Companions.

- Publish a Children’s Charter; pilot Wayfaring Terms with Guides and youth benches. Start Common Hours with a few obvious services and grow.

Years 4–7:
- Convert the first coal sites to storage or modular fission within fences. Commission long‑duration storage and daytime desalination. Set vehicle‑to‑grid as a standard.

- Halve drug and device approval times with adaptive trials and registries. Deploy ward‑level measurement loops to public hospitals. Eradicate malaria in one multi‑country region; cut TB incidence by half where it’s worst.

- Turn a third of ghost office stock into homes, labs, and schools via preapproved codes. Stripe night freight lanes. Move daytime buses to guaranteed five‑minute frequency on major routes.

- Slim occupational licensing; institutionalize skill proofs and portable reputations. Normalize four‑day weeks with output steady or better. Equip crews with exosuits and time‑bank unavoidable drudgery.

- Operationalize the Boundary Meter for measurable cross‑fence harms. Throttle bad actors automatically. Publish receipts.

- Open Campuses for Young Minds where synthetic and human novices learn patience together. Codify two‑keys and three‑watchers for irreversible acts.

- Stand up Welcome Networks; designate receiving towns; pre‑permit pads; train staff; practice.

Years 8–12:
- Bring average retail electricity below seven cents per kilowatt‑hour; off‑peak industrial rates below three. Cut power‑sector emissions by seventy percent. Finish continental transmission backbones.

- Cut cancer mortality in half. Reduce suicide and overdose deaths by half. Add five healthy years to the median life.

- Finish clinic electrification and internet links worldwide. Drive TB near zero in several regions. Keep malaria near zero with vigilant monitoring.

- Entrench decision markets and calibration scores in budgets. Publish agency track records. Tie leadership incentives to outcomes. Expand the verification stack with public sensor networks.

- Grow Welcome Networks to receive hundreds of thousands annually with dignity. Expand Move First, Return Last programs for floodplains and fire corridors.

Years 13–20:
- Close the last coal plants. Keep urban rents near mid‑century ratios by saturating pattern construction and retrofits.

- Make four in five civic systems legible under the Manual Override Rule. Move from pilot Null Days to a national calendar. Fund replication and translation as first‑class science.

- Clean orbit with bonds and bounties; enforce radio quiet around observatories; dim skies on schedule.

- Codify, simply: if a mind can suffer, consider it. Apply where it counts: animal welfare, and—if we ever build synthetic agents with stable preferences we can test—ours. Keep machines as instruments, not princes.

- Establish far charters with return‑ticket clauses, audit windows, and Joint Benefit modules that send home open archives, clinic meshes, and watershed models as dividends when productive capacity doubles.

An afternoon you could live

On my service day I start at the clinic. The first patient is a night‑shift janitor who wants sleep back. His Companion—running locally under his key—shows tiny chaos points: late trains, a child’s practice, news that tastes like sugar after midnight. The Studio of the Self offers a reversible three‑week patch that makes doomscrolling taste like stale crackers after ten p.m. He signs a time promise to himself. He cries when he admits no one has watched him sleep with care since his aunt during a fever at twelve. The Night Round catches a drift at 3:11; a nurse adjusts a dose. He goes home before noon and never sees the ICU.

At lunch I walk to the Public Shop. It smells like machine oil and basil. Today is a Try Day. In one room, a team unveils a wastewater alert that trips a day before ER visits rise. In another, a poet, a nurse, and two engineers present a refillable inhaler built without rare metals and licensed in an open pool that pays a slice to the commons wherever it’s made. A seventeen‑year‑old asks why the winter prototype failed. An engineer points to a swollen seal. The Safekeeper writes, “Next time, cold trials,” and signs. We clap for the kid and for the engineer who admitted cold won.

At my other job, a copilot drafts a maintenance spec. I do the judgment. A colleague in Porto and another in Lagos flick into a shared‑focus window. We design a fix in thirty minutes that would have taken a week by email. We log off because busyness no longer earns points.

On the way home my daughter drags me to the Civic Observatory to play with forecasts for a proposed tram. Same budget; either one long line or two shorter lines into ignored neighborhoods. The odds lean toward two. We are not voting. We are practicing next week’s shape.

The Welcome House on our block glows. A family steps in still wearing airline tags and carrying a child’s drawing. Our circle’s elders wait on the steps. Someone who welded pipes in another life brings beans; a child learns to set a table. Later the square fills for a civic game. Two coalitions who disagree on duplexes agreed the first round would be a cook‑off and a build‑off. Both have their forty percent chance in council next week—the odds board blinks above the bandstand—but tonight we get brisket and an alley repaired no one could find a bid for. Kids put googly eyes on a theodolite. I cheer for the side that wants homes within ten minutes of bread. We’ll quibble tomorrow. Tonight we eat and fix.

Walking the river path after dark the sky is punched with stars. Somewhere a shade’s geometry is right. Farther north a sail‑yard’s green ping flickers—an autonomous barge declaring its power budget and promising not to bite the near world. A triangle crosses too steady for a plane. The crew’s charter has been on the board for months. Their consent is on file: their own, a quorum of their street’s, and the return covenant they wrote together. People stand with mugs and don’t pretend it’s normal or not. The sky widened. The floors held.

Why this held

We stopped asking intelligence—ours or our machines’—to dissolve politics by charm. We used it to make frictions visible and to reward competence that compounds. We invested in slow parts—steel, substations, trials, teacher training—so fast parts had something to grip. We wrote objectives down and changed them when they misled us. We put tripwires inside systems that can hurt and made halting them a mark of responsibility, not a sin. We built real opt‑outs: quiet streets without bots, paper‑first clinics, library Companions you can borrow and return.

We set delay costs into budgets. If an agency blew a clock without a safety finding, its bond paid into the office that could unjam the line. Rules got crisp. Procurement published calibration scores. Forecasts learned to blush.

We practiced receiving before storms arrived. Receiving is a skill. We drilled it. We made low‑tech options comfortable: analog sanctuaries where screens stay dark; end‑of‑life houses where care is a craft; respite care that opens in hours, not months.

We normalized procedural honesty. When a permit clock said yes on time, people tried again. When a clinic caught trouble before it screamed, we funded it joyfully. When buildings were pleasant, quick, and safe, we supported the next one. When your child knew how to leave and chose to stay, you trusted your own ways more.

It is not glitter. It is specific. The parts exist. We arranged them. We allied the Lattice and the Doorways and refused to treat either as optional. The result is a country where millions more of us can do useful things without heroism, and where the right to leave is not a threat but a promise we make one another: no place will keep you by force; we will build places worth choosing.

On festival nights the amphitheater glows. We honor those who died and those born, those who stayed and those who left a place that did not love them enough and found one that does. The Public Shop wheels out statues of everyday inventors: a farmer who bred a salt‑tolerant seed, a carpenter who wrote an open pattern for a perfect cheap stair, a teenager who tuned a bus stop so it felt safe after dark. We applaud, not to worship, but because gratitude scales. After the lights settle there’s a breath you can hear—the sound a civilization makes when it stands on a floor it built together, checks its dials, and looks for the next lamp to light.
