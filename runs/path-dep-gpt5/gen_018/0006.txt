The Amber Loop

Between the tram shelter and the public pantry, a waist‑high post the color of banked embers keeps its own small warmth through winter. A loop of orange fiber, soft and braided, hangs from a brass bead. The sign is short.

Speak your need. Choose a time.
Two questions only. No story due.

A line cook with a split wrist and a phone blinking red closes her hand around the loop. A human answers in her dialect: Are you safe for ten minutes? Do you want to leave now, or set a time?

“Now.”

Fourteen minutes later a paper sleeve clicks out from the slot. Inside: a day key that rides every bus and tram and opens any public bike with a blink; a code to a clean, lockable room two stops away; three weeks of childcare aligned to a new shift she chose from two options; a freeze on a wage deduction that wasn’t; a protective order that starts before dusk; a clinic appointment tomorrow within five blocks of the new room; and a thumbnail‑sized fob with one button. If she presses it, her health letters, last three pay slips, certifications, and prescriptions slide under a key only she holds to the services in her new neighborhood. If she doesn’t, nothing moves. No pleading. No microphone. She can tell her story later, on terms she picks.

We built the rest of our country outward from that feeling: that exits are logistics, not theater; that floors are universal, not favors; that brakes are visible and enjoyable to pull; that dials we all can touch move the things we share; that a promise is a schedule with an explanation.

What holds it together

Five pieces, simple enough to say to a friend, carry most of the load: the Floor, the Ring, the Charters, the Console, and the Brakeworks. Under them hums the Commons Ledger—how we pay for what we claim, without peering through keyholes.

The Floor

The Floor is the set of goods we promise to each other whether anyone is good, clever, grateful, or not. It is paid like roads are paid: by everybody, for everybody, with books anyone can read.

Within a short walk of frequent transit, the Floor provides:

- A room with a real door. Clean air; heat and cooling inside a steady band; a shower that drains; a stove that lights; a lock that obeys you and a court order only. Inspection stickers that anyone can read. If the lock fails at midnight, someone repairs it by 12:30.

- Food without striptease. Public kitchens where grains, beans, greens, fruit, eggs, oil, and milk are ordinary, with logs of sourcing and wages on the wall. Tap if you can pay; don’t if you can’t. Nobody auditions for carrots.

- Clinics that catch whispers. Walk‑in hours align with shifts. A finger stick and a breath sample run through a lunchbox lab to flag sepsis, drug interactions, and early cancers before shouting begins. Head and mood care sits beside sutures and insulin. Any intervention that can change your mind or your hormones lives behind rehearsed consent, monitoring, and a recovery plan that exists in writing.

- Movement that keeps its word. Five‑minute buses and trams on daytime trunks. Safe crossings, curb ramps, and bike routes a twelve‑year‑old rides without whitening anyone’s knuckles. Transfers within sight of an Amber Loop post.

- Counsel that answers. Wage theft, landlord paper tricks, stalking that uses screens, custody that needs a shield: a public steward answers within a day. A scrub team sits at your table and pries spyware out until a phone is a phone.

- Bandwidth like water. Sturdy in rain, priced and overseen like a utility, run on civic servers bound by law to your interests. Libraries carry the same pipe, without having to smile for it.

- Childcare where the trains are. Skilled, safe, and present.

- A public option assistant. It runs on your device or at the library; owes you a fiduciary duty by law; refuses ads; and is replaceable without data loss. If such an assistant crosses you, its insurer pays by statute and its code changes in daylight.

Floors held by calendars and recipes:

- Permit clocks with teeth. Common buildings and energy pieces come as open recipes—cross‑vented courtyards, shop‑houses, accessory suites, rooftop arrays, basement egress kits, quiet ridge turbines, modular switchgear, district cold tanks. If your plan matches a recipe on a suitable site, a clock starts when you file. Officials can pause only for specific safety, wildlife, labor, or measured spillovers and must explain why in words a jury can read. Miss the clock and the permit issues; a bond posted by the office bleeds to neighbors who waited.

- Corridor compacts. Wires, trunks, and rails cross counties under one schedule. Compensation tables publish before surveyors knock. If a party ghosts its duty, it sits out a bid season. Landowners can click a parcel and see what may cross and what it pays.

- A parts pantry and a maintenance day. We standardized guts—pumps, actuators, switchgear, bus shelters—under open specs. We pool spares in a federation of depots. Once a month, nothing launches. We oil what holds us up, test overrides, and post what we fixed. Raid the maintenance budget and next year it doubles automatically with painted names on the wall.

- A simplicity clause. When rules pile into nonsense, they sunset and must be replaced by something shorter that can be tested on a kitchen table. The diff publishes; courts admit the tests.

- An entropy budget. Strategic shelves of transformers, breakers, filters, gaskets, and antibiotics are kept the way we keep vaccines. We practice blackstart. We keep a paper twin of every essential function.

The Ring

The Ring is the small, stubborn thing you carry—card, pendant, phone chip, or a token trusted to the library—that collects your proofs and keeps your borders.

- Show enough, never everything. Prove “over 18,” “certified for a forklift,” or “resident of Ward Nine” without vomiting your birthday and address. Documents pass a tired‑brain checker and a steelman: a human makes the best case against the choice you’re about to make so you can hear it while calm.

- Consent with ribs. A calendar lists big yeses with sunsets: share sleep with this clinic for twelve weeks; no subscription renewal without a second yes; no implant without a cool‑off. Before irreversibles we run Consider–Cool–Confirm: a counselor gives you the best “no”; the act pauses while your future self arrives; you confirm with a neutral witness who has no skin in either outcome. Where physics allows undo, contracts include a regret budget that funds reversal. Where it doesn’t, a future‑self ping later lets the person you became say stop onward effects now.

- Common Pay. A public payment rail moves money instantly without turning you into product. Landlords, clinics, cousins get paid on time. Errors arbitrate fast.

- Companions that obey. Assistants run locally or inside auditable trusts. They cannot enroll you, sign for you, or pretend to be you. Violations trigger payouts by statute and code fixes in public.

- Reputation that travels. Apprenticeships and mastery letters follow a welder across town lines; a nurse crosses a border without building a shrine; a barber arriving from abroad spends a morning on hygiene and an afternoon on a test cut. Outcome reputations replace follower counts: roofers by years without leaks; kitchens by near‑misses logged and inspections; coders by uptime; barbers by clean combs and satisfied scalps.

- Children first. Children are citizens. Each has an Advocate outside family and school who answers at three in the morning. Between twelve and eighteen, every young person gets two Journeys—funded seasons in a deliberately different neighborhood or guild—with a mentor, a stipend, and a private line adults cannot listen to. Big mind and body choices sit behind Consider–Cool–Confirm and a memory escrow that unlocks at majority for the person it belongs to, not for anyone else.

The Charters

Diversity sits on the Floor. Neighborhoods, guilds, villages, faiths, chat‑towns—places that pick a way—post a one‑page Charter in words a tenth‑grader won’t yawn at:

- What they value and when they are quiet or bright.

- Which defaults change at their line. A contemplative quarter dims neon and asks for covered screens after dusk. A music street runs late but softens at dawn. A kinship ward writes consent signage into its fire code and enforces it like one.

- How you complain, who must listen, and how appeals work.

- Exactly how you leave, where deposits go, and which debts cannot trap you.

Each keeps an Embassy: a blue sash and a kettle at a door where someone explains norms, helps you try for two weeks, and shows you out kindly if you decide to go. Leaving is paperwork, not penance. Strict‑and‑kind places fill. Strict‑and‑cruel places empty or learn. Children’s rights do not bend at the border. Harms that cross fences carry a price; boundary meters bill where the harm crosses.

The Console

The Console is where we touch the dials: bus priority, garbage windows, floodgates, heat islands, school ventilation, subsidy thresholds. The plumbing is public code with receipts.

- Open civic code. Permits, budgets, flood maps, timetables, signal timings, and rules that move public machines live in open repositories with version histories. Every change ships with a one‑page diff: what changed, who signed, why, what they forecast. Forecast cards accompany proposals and keep score afterward. Making and retracting claims earns and spends a calibration score you cannot buy.

- Console Days. Four afternoons a year, responsible officials stand by the levers—bus priority, sluice gates, HVAC profiles—and move one while neighbors watch forecasts change on a wall. On Null Days, we change nothing and check brakes.

- Field Rooms. Any policy that touches a body runs eight to twelve weeks in rehearsal before it rules. Red teams poke. Data opens by default. Secrecy sits on a timer.

- Assemblies by lot. Standing citizen juries guard floors, warrants, and brakes. They do after‑action reports after drills and incidents. Updating in public earns years; refusing to update retires you quietly.

- Simpler by force. Complicated rules die on timers and require replacements shorter than what they killed, with tests a home group can run.

The Brakeworks

Brakes are not an insult to courage; they are how courage repeats.

- Two humans for one irreversible. Opening a high‑risk valve, rerouting a grid, launching a fleet, high‑order synthesis: two licensed humans from different lines of authority turn two keys with dead time between them. Logs are append‑only and courts can pierce them.

- Machines with manners. Any code that can move money, steer, order reagents, dose, or publish to more than a local crowd needs a license, an insurer, and a red‑team grade like a café’s health score. Training runs carry compute passports tied to energy sources and data provenance. Robots broadcast presence beacons. Systems that can harm without hands ride under bright, physical stops a child can yank; classes visit to practice the pull.

- A narrow sentinel. A small, warrant‑bound team watches seams where tail risks live—chip fabs, identity backbones, genome printers, floodgates. It trips narrow and fast; when it grounds drones over six blocks, trams detour and a readable story posts by morning. Standing juries by lot review after.

- Privacy with teeth. Encryption and attestation make mass surveillance brittle. Machine impersonation without a live, time‑bound yes is a felony. Scraping personal data without leave is the same.

- Attention as a commons. Paid nudges in places you cannot reasonably avoid wear Traces: who paid, what act they want, how often they’re right, and the size of their purse. Each neighborhood sets a monthly attention quota for shared screens. When it’s spent, boards fall back to art and sky. Default feeds run in greenhouse mode, especially in public and for children. Adults can flip a visible “sharp feed” switch that glows until it goes dark.

The Commons Ledger

We rent location and privilege instead of spying. The Commons Ledger gathers:

- Ground leases on hot land; spectrum and orbit; ports and water concessions.

- Carbon and heat at the fence; NOx and particulates at the curb; attention in unavoidable places.

- A light license on frontier compute and ultralarge autonomous fleets; bonds for harms that scale faster than people can.

An independent clearinghouse counts harms at edges. Boundary courts, fast and narrow, handle these fights. Proceeds keep floors steady; surplus flows back as a dividend that arrives with the train chime. Paper fallbacks exist because batteries die.

Energy with manners

At noon, electrons laugh. District cold tanks and desalination hum under bright sky. Arc furnaces, ceramics, laundries, and fermenters run lunch shifts. Retired coal yards become storage within fences neighbors already know; in a few places, quiet modular reactors sit behind old gates after drills so thorough you can relax. Elsewhere, iron‑air, sodium, hot rock, and gravity carry the night. Long wires map like rails; compacts cross counties with posted compensation, relocation help, and honest “not here” exceptions. Fleets sell their nights back to the grid by firmware; mechanics get paid for keeping that fact true.

Materials carry passports. Right‑to‑repair is a pegboard and a Saturday class. Disassembly depots pay bounties for screws, clips, and alloys a machinist smiles at.

Health as a visible loop

Night carts collect tiny blood drops and breath and ping humans when something whispers wrong. Robot labs execute human‑written protocols pinned to version control. Licenses look like factories that can explode. Wastewater and cough acoustics trip rehearsed thresholds; vaccine updates move from design to clinic in under sixty days because printers and trial networks sit on retainer. Cancer shifts left: ultrasensitive blood work and breath pull cases into early stages. Antibiotic stewardship is budgets and dashboards, not posters: cultures or written reasons; days‑on‑drug per thousand patient‑days; wastewater flags resistance genes. Addiction leaves sermon and gains scaffold: medication first, test strips in bathroom stalls, long‑acting antagonists unremarkable. Mind‑changing sessions (where lawful) run like anesthesia: monitors who aren’t on your ride, reversals ready, registries that remember, and consent with ribs.

Justice without spectacle

We retired cages for cash. Reminders, check‑ins, and work with wages replaces most pretrial loss. Dangerous people are separated for as long as they are dangerous, with a written path back made of housing credits, apprenticeships, and time. A Reparations Ledger ticks forward—homes, clinics, scholarships—guided by those harmed and audited like bridges. Bereavement has rooms and rules; hours employers cannot veto; paperwork that takes one visit, not six.

Nature and night

Cities behave like sponges. Pavements drink. Creeks return to daylight. Parks hold flood and joy. Living edges replace some walls; necessary walls are honest and strong where water insists. Over‑ and underpasses stitch habitats. Farmers are paid to hold water during storms based on measured damage prevented downstream. Soil carbon payments ride on field measurements and satellites. Agrivoltaics shade lettuce; pollinator corridors stitch orchards to wildland. Where minds can suffer, law errs kind. Skies go dark by calendar. Orbits run on bonds that fund cleanup and bounties paid per kilo; quiet rings protect observatories and some wild places.

The last mile, everywhere

Kits the size of a pickup end the last unlit nights: fold‑out panels, storage, a satellite link, a night lab for breath tests and sepsis panels. Maintainers are paid for uptime, not grants. Vaccines ride thermostable chains on drones. Cash‑on‑coverage programs pay community health workers to hit immunization and tuberculosis targets on dashboards they own and the world can check. Arrival Rings in receiving towns have pads pre‑permitted, classroom seats budgeted, apprenticeships rehearsed, and counselors on retainer. Move‑Together and Return‑Later programs let whole blocks relocate before the worst years and come back—if ever—together.

Machines get a childhood

We gave new minds boredom before levers. Seam schools pair elders, kids, animals, and small robots. Patience, turn‑taking, and “you’re scaring me” are learned. Keepers—the narrow systems that run pumps and timetables—drop into explain‑only mode once a week so humans keep a hand in. Each Keeper has a Skeptic trained to smell proxy games and brittle shortcuts. Red‑team scores hang in the lobby like health grades.

Work and time

Thirty‑two paid hours became normal, not by decree but because drafts wrote themselves; schedules reconciled; claims filed; humans made the calls. Error fell. Throughput rose. A paid service season each year covers culverts before fall rains, flood drills, Amber Loop staffing, ninth‑grade debate without cruelty.

Drudge we can’t yet kill pays well, rotates fairly, and equips bodies with exos and clamps instead of making pain a badge. Apprenticeships pay; mastery arrives when three masters sign a public ledger with their reputations staked. A Care Ledger counts hours with people who’d otherwise face the dark alone. Credits don’t cash out; they unlock studios, sabbaticals, train miles. They fade if you stop showing up, so the record stays honest.

Belonging on purpose

Welcome Houses open two nights a week on most blocks. Circles of fifteen to twenty‑five—newcomers, elders, drifters with tools—commit to mattering to one another for a season. Each keeps a cache and a phone tree someone always answers. Weavers—paid introducers—only earn if, months later, you say your life widened. At eight, the Atlas starts: maps of how other places live and what promises they keep. At twelve, Journeys. At fourteen, Embassy Halls where Charters keep rooms to explain themselves. Youth benches lean toward the child’s stated will and safety.

A day you could live

You wake to a street that prefers feet. Two doors down, a small crane eases a prefabricated story into place. Because the pattern is preapproved and the permit clock worked, there is more watching than fighting. The site steward hands a seventeen‑year‑old a practice kit with a heat‑pump compressor in pieces and a date for the hands‑on that will open her apprenticeship even if algebra still nips at her ankles.

At lunch, electrons are cheap like cinnamon used to be. Buses top off for pennies. The maker space fires ceramic. District tanks bank cold for evening. Freight rides soft tires in set windows after midnight. You don’t fight a car for daylight.

In clinic, the night cart caught a sepsis whisper a day before it would have shouted. A patient who would have seen the ICU now won’t. Three near‑miss slips hang with little flags because we celebrate catching almost‑wrongs as much as ribbon cuttings. In the yard, a machinist holds a spring the size of a fingernail. A poet reads a child’s story about the first morning breath that didn’t hurt after somebody fixed a swelling gasket. The fix gets a bigger cheer than the feature.

On your way home, your kid drags you to the Console. A milk crate appears for small hands. She slides the bus‑priority dial up along the school corridor. Blue dots brighten where waits fall; gray dots hold where late freight slides a minute later. People argue kindly. They laugh. They choose a trial schedule. No one grandstands because any claim spends calibration points you might need tomorrow.

Evening belongs to the common kitchen. Onions and rain. Elders on steps. A family who arrived this morning with a paper packet sits like a weight. They are not. Two more bowls appear without anyone asking for a story. By the door a red rope loops, within reach of a six‑year‑old. Once a season the kids tug it, just to remember the feeling of stopping the world.

Across the river a soft dome covers six blocks. A fab pinged odd. A slim blue tag in your glasses says why the tram detoured and how long it will last. Nudges on the unavoidable screen wear Traces: the purse ticks down; the track record shows; when the month’s attention quota empties, the board fades to a quiet sky.

Why it holds

- Schedules and receipts beat vibes. A “no” names a risk in words a jury can read. A “yes” starts a clock with a bond attached. Forecast boards didn’t kill charisma; they raised the status of leaders who change their minds in public.

- Floors are universal and good. Even the loud eat at the kitchen and ride the bus. There is no appetite to starve what everyone touches.

- Leaving is logistics. When exits don’t demand performance, loyalty becomes a gift, not a chain. Strict‑and‑kind places thrive. Strict‑and‑cruel places empty or learn.

- Competence compounds. Permits that land on time buy trust for the next build. Clinics that catch whispers earn budgets. Buildings that rise quickly and look cared for change minds about building at all.

- Brakes are visible and fun to pull. Children practice stopping things. Adults feel safer. Machines behave because they must explain themselves weekly, and anyone can ask why.

- Costs pushed across fences show up on a bill, not a sermon. Pipes throttle when you don’t pay. Theatrics wither; accounting grows.

- Attention behaves like a watershed. You can still buy a megaphone in the square; you cannot quietly rent a neighbor’s nervous system.

- Kids have a map and a person. Helplessness loosens. Parochialism thins. A fourteen‑year‑old can read a Charter and choose.

- The last mile is boring and proud. Clinics hum on nights that used to go dark. Salaries hit banks on time. Tuberculosis dwindles because delivery wins.

- We keep an entropy budget. Maintenance days are real. Strategic shelves are real. Storms knock, and the grid stands in days, not weeks.

- Offline exists. Amber Loops spit paper when networks die. Assistants have paper twins at libraries. There is a minimum viable offline for all the basics.

How we got here

There was no law of physics that we needed; only errands you could run on Tuesdays, stacked until they looked like destiny.

Years 1–3: First posts and clocks

- We set Amber Loop posts at stations, clinics, libraries, courts, schools, union halls, and malls. We braided shelters, childcare, legal aid, transit passes, and record portability into one pipe keyed to a person, not a building. We drilled the blink gesture in ninth‑grade civics and in mirrors where teens look. We made stalling a felony with automatic logs that wake a judge.

- We published recipes and started clocks. Open pattern books for common buildings and energy pieces landed with permit timers backed by escrow. We scrapped parking minimums near frequent transit. We sold at‑cost basement egress kits.

- We launched Rings and Consent Calendars with paper fallbacks. We tied public money and licenses to compliance. We outlawed machine imitation of a person without a live, time‑bound consent. Libraries became the default place to get fiduciary assistants you can sue.

- We opened Field Rooms so policies could rehearse before they ruled: school lunches, stoplights, shelter routines, triage, ventilation. Open notebooks by default; secrecy on timers; auditors on retainer.

- We built Console Halls with forecast boards and paid assemblies by lot to guard floors. Early choices came with sunset dates and named metrics.

- We fenced off the boring. Maintenance budgets could not be raided without automatic penalties and painted names. We standardized parts.

- We licensed frontier compute. We registered training runs. We watermarked output at the chip. We required two human names for code that could reach an irreversible physical act. We paid and shielded whistleblowers.

- We started the Commons Dividend and spent first on rooms, kitchens, clinics, five‑minute buses, childcare, and non‑adversarial legal help. We built Breathing Rooms—chilled air, HEPA hum, cots, a nurse—for smoke and heat days. No questions at the door.

Years 4–7: Wires and spares

- We repowered. Retired coal yards became storage within familiar fences. Where neighbors chose after honest drills, quiet modular reactors went behind old gates. Noon desalination and district cooling came online. Bus barns rented their nights to the grid.

- We converted dead offices with retrofit kits you don’t curse. We striped night‑freight lanes. We guaranteed five‑minute headways on trunks. HEPA and CO₂ monitors went in schools; far‑UV into risky rooms.

- We slimmed licensing to safety. Skill proofs and apprenticeships ran under local insurers. Thirty‑two hours normalized. Civic seasons unlocked studios and sabbaticals.

- We matured the sentinel under warrants and standing juries; we opened nurseries for machine minds with boredom inside them.

- We daylighted creeks and bought out floodplains by block with Move‑Together programs. We recognized credentials across regions so arrivals did not start from zero. Receiving towns were funded on clocks.

- We warmed antibiotic labs, kept phage units ready, subsidized ventilation like bridges. We shipped clinic‑in‑a‑crate kits—solar, storage, satellite, breath labs—with uptime contracts that paid locals.

- We opened a Match Loom: agents that look for trades across seams—watershed compacts; barters among theaters, transit, and libraries; landlords pooling for boiler crews—with offers logged on ledgers courts already know how to read.

Years 8–12: Thickening

- Retail electricity dropped under seven cents; off‑peak industrial under three. Power‑sector emissions fell by two‑thirds. Outages slid into single digits. Clinic blackouts vanished behind microgrids paid for uptime.

- Floors reached universality: rooms, kitchens, mind care in clinics, frequent transit, legal stewards, and bandwidth. Misses published and fixed on clocks.

- Overdoses and suicides halved as fewer ledges and better floors took hold. Preventable hospital deaths fell with night carts and consent with ribs. Median lives gained five healthy years through early detection, sleep, and food.

- Console Halls and assemblies earned their way into budgets. Leaders who updated stayed; those who did not retired.

- Clinic kits scaled across borders. Tuberculosis fell under one per million in places we worked. Malaria pinned near zero where climate allowed with nets, drugs, and slow, consented gene drives.

- A continental backbone of long wires closed. Orbital bonds funded cleanup; bounties paid per kilo pulled.

Years 13–20: Normal

- Pattern housing saturated; rent‑to‑wage ratios stabilized. Safe backyard suites for elders and wheels tripled. Exos strapped onto the last back‑breaking jobs. Shifts shortened because we could.

- Learning accounts and decade sabbaticals institutionalized. Midlife switches became unremarkable. Repair celebrated like invention.

- Charter Keys—auditable attestations of ability and duty—became mandatory for outbound machines. Bounties paid for proofs of deception. Access to frontier chips and synthesis printers tied to audit‑ready civil rights at home.

- Field Rooms doubled abroad with local teams. The boring code and spares flew farther than flags.

Hard edges, handled without spectacle

A hilltop fellowship told teens that saying no was treason. For years, exit calls died at the last relay. The youth ombuds subpoenaed tower logs and proved it. A portable Amber Loop parked three kilometers from the gate. A judge ordered weekly “how to leave” classes on their lawn, taught by the ombuds and two elders who had left and returned. Eighteen teens left; thirty‑one stayed with weight behind the stay. The congregation rewrote its rites. They still prefer hymnals to headphones. Good. The inside door opens.

A chain of immersion booths tried to dodge consent by labeling itself art while eating weekends whole. A boundary court shuttered them until brakes, recovery plans, and Consider–Cool–Confirm went in. Investors paid enough to remember.

A landlord hid whisperware in hallway cameras. A scrub team stripped trackers; a court emptied the landlord’s escrow and funded new keys. The landlord now teaches “Don’t Do Crimes With Wi‑Fi” to license candidates as a condition of ever owning again.

A model overshot in a dense ward and tripped a wide response. The Field Room reran the math with better data; the next trigger targeted by block, not city. The standing jury published the apology and the fix. The health lead kept her job because she updated in public.

A device firm lied about hardware attestation so its assistants could drink more than people allowed. The sentinel flagged the mismatch. Compute docks pulled licenses. Courts tied fines to spectrum rights until honest firmware shipped and independent audits cleared it. The maker learned. So did its rivals.

Stand in the square at evening. A Charter glows over a door anyone can pull. A judgment scrolls in verbs you understand. A steward ties a new orange loop on an Amber post with hands that have done it a hundred times. The tram arrives when the board promised. The public broth tastes like care. The clinic caught a cancer early enough to bore it into submission. You can point to the permit clock that didn’t miss, to the paneled roof that paid the bus barn to sleep and the laundries to hum at noon, to the knob you turned with your kid when buses got a little faster by the school in June. You can point to the line on your bill labeled Commons Dividend and to the one called Boundary Fee you paid when your band’s amp annoyed the next street.

It is not chrome. It is warm competence. It is plausible because we got here the way you fix a fence: post by post, rail by rail, a string to keep us straight. We wrote promises we could point to. We set clocks. We put receipts on claims. We bragged about brakes. We made leaving cheap and staying sweet. We went home, and the lights were on.
