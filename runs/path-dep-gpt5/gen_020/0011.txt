Harbors, Keys, and the Lattice

By the time the first tram noses into the curve, the bread window is already fogging the glass. Last night’s oven heat spent the dawn chilling milk in the co‑op down the block. A thin rail of copper in the crosswalk still holds a bit of yesterday’s sun, enough to bead off frost without anyone thinking about it. On the corner, a waist-high screen the size of a cutting board ticks through unglamorous truths: buses within five, the pantry’s staples, transformer health, clinic waits, street noise after dark, carbon and heat at the curb, sewer flow after last night’s rain. You can touch any line and it explains itself aloud, plain as a weather report. We banked wind at 3 a.m. The freezer under the plaza has four hours of cold. The senior center’s elevators asked for a top-up; the barn batteries are lending them juice until noon.

Beside the screen stands a pillar the color of banked clay. A woven orange strap loops through a brass eye. A small plate reads:

Hold the strap. Say what you need.
Two questions only. No story required.

A line cook with flour on her sleeves and a phone at two percent puts her palm on the loop. A steward walks up—not a cop, not a counselor in disguise, just a person with a badge that means “I can make the logistics happen.” He is allowed two questions, by law and by custom:

Are you safe right now?
Do you want to leave now or plan for later?

“Now,” she says.

Five minutes later, a paper envelope lands in her hand. Inside: a day pass that opens bus, tram, and van doors all over the county; a code to a clean, private room with a lock and a shower that drains; a three‑week childcare slot aligned to one of two shift options she chose on the spot; a freeze on a wage siphon in her last paycheck; a temporary order that keeps a violent man three blocks and three clicks away; an appointment tomorrow at a clinic two stops from the new room; and a coin‑sized token with one button. Press it, and her health letters, wage stubs, and licenses flow to the new city under keys only she controls. Don’t press, and nothing moves. There’s also a map that works with dead batteries and a single sentence to tack to a fridge if needed: She has left. Following her will be expensive.

We built the rest of our world outward from that feeling: when you pull a handle, it moves; when you hit a brake, it bites. Exits are logistics, not theater. The floor underfoot is generous and dull, on purpose, so the life above it can be loud in the ways that don’t break people.

The Lattice

Beneath arguments and styles runs a civic machine we call the Lattice: power, water, transit, fiber; identity proofs and payment rails; clinic backends and court receipts; spares and drills. We fund it because everyone uses it. We do not make you act out your suffering to stand on it.

Four guarantees sit on the Lattice. They do not check your speech or your savings.

- A door that answers to you. Every resident can claim a private room that meets a posted spec: daylight, fresh air, a quiet band for sleep, a stove that lights without a trick, and a lock that opens for you and a court order only. The tag by the hinge says when the filter was changed and who will answer the phone at 12:30 if the lock sticks at 12:03.

- Movement you can trust with a child. Five‑minute headways on daytime trunks. Sidewalks with corners you can roll. Bike lanes that do not demand courage. Transfers within sight of a Harbor Post. Night belongs to freight and owl‑quiet buses so daylight belongs to feet.

- Kitchens, clinics, counsel, and compute without striptease. Public dining rooms that serve beans and greens and eggs and grain; you tap if you can and you don’t if you can’t, and the wages and cleanliness logs hang on the wall. Storefront clinics catch trouble before it screams; robots don’t replace nurses, they watch for quiet killers at night and whisper when potassium drifts. Legal stewards answer when your stomach drops—wages gone missing, spyware in a hallway camera, a landlord burying a trick in paper. And a public‑option assistant: small, local if you want it, sue‑able by law if it crosses you, unable to sell you no matter how well you phrase a sentence.

- Identity that reveals only what is needed. Your life rides under your keys in a little object we call a Key—card, pendant, phone chip, or held in trust at the library. It lets you prove “over 18,” “licensed to drive a forklift,” “resident of Ward Six,” “allowed to pick up this child” without dumping your birthday and address onto a stranger’s desk. The math can be explained at the kitchen table. When someone leaks anyway, the maker and its insurer pay, and the logs arrive in court in words a jury can read.

Who pays for it? We do, together, with the things that only have value together:

- We lease land by location value—not the paint on top—and return the rent to the Commons.

- We meter harms at their edges: carbon, heat, noise, runoff, spam, and attention floods when they cross into shared space. Pipes and feeds throttle until the meter is paid.

- We lease spectrum and orbital lanes with bonds for end‑of‑life cleanup.

- We put a light levy on frontier compute and very large automated fleets and require bonds sized to plausible harm.

- The Commons Treasury posts a ledger you can read. An affordability autopilot nudges the universal dividend and utility credits so a basket of basics—shelter, staples, heat or cool, a clinic visit, a commute—stays a published slice of median wages.

Brakes worth bragging about

We built rituals for big choices and hard stops so we don’t invent them sweaty and panicked.

- Consent that holds shape. For elective, consequential changes in body or legal status, we run Brief–Breathe–Bind. First, a counter‑arguer—a trained stranger with no stake—makes the best case against and asks you to say it back. Then the process rests long enough for a different day to arrive. Then you affirm in front of a neutral who checks that you understand the risks and truly want it. When biology allows, changes are staged and reversible. For truly irreversible acts, default is no unless two unrelated human names turn two physical keys in different rooms hours apart, and a court can open the log.

- Influence with receipts. Anything that speaks at you without your asking wears a stub: who paid, what they hope you’ll do, why they think it will work, and their calibration score for the last year. Bots wear badges tied to chip watermarks. Impersonation without a live, time‑bound consent is a felony. Public screens and feeds default to greenhouse mode: gentle, explicit ends, slow scroll. If you want a blast of novelty, your device shows a small red lamp until you switch it off.

- Twin‑key rules. A handful of systems that can bite—opening floodgates, pushing new dose software across a hospital, spinning up a genome printer—require two named humans from different chains of command to use physical keys in a time‑separated sequence. We drill bright stops the way we drill fire alarms. The stop handles are hard to miss and satisfying to pull when they should be.

Variety with rails we all recognize

Neighborhoods, guilds, rural co‑ops, diaspora halls, and online quarters post charters at their doors and stable addresses. They say what they love, what they keep outside the fence, how to complain and who must listen, and how to leave without a scene. Five rails run under all of them:

- No stalking across borders. Argue, lampoon, proselytize—but stop at the fence.

- Exits are sacred. Interfere with a departure and a steward arrives with receipts before a uniform.

- Costs you push across lines get metered and billed. Warm someone else’s air, foul their water, jam their feed, flood their attention; you pay what the meter shows.

- Irreversible moves sit under twin‑key rules and drills. Names and reasons land on a ledger a judge can open.

- Children’s rights do not bow to local style. Guides answer at three in the morning. Youth benches lean toward the young person’s articulated will and safety.

Every quarter keeps an Embassy Room—blue sash on a hook, a kettle, and a friendly explanation of local norms, trials for two weeks, and a clean goodbye if you decide to go. Leaving is paperwork, not a loyalty play. Strict‑and‑kind places fill. Strict‑and‑cruel places empty or learn.

Fast, kind building

We stopped treating every roof like bespoke poetry. We keep an Open Pattern Library—pre‑approved designs for rooftop arrays, courtyard apartments, shop‑houses, school retrofits, small substations, safe basement exits, disassembly depots, rain‑friendly streets—with clear performance limits for light, noise, tree protection, and carbon. Match a pattern, and a clock starts. Miss the clock without writing down a safety risk a jury would understand, and the permit issues into an audit window while an escrow compensates neighbors for honest misses. Corridor compacts map rail, water, and wires with standard offers and penalties for dawdle paid into a fund that finishes when builders don’t.

We publish a scoreboard of critical spares—transformers, breakers, inverters—and we keep a strategic shelf of them like we keep vaccines. One day a month is Down Day. No new features. We grease what we already trust.

Power that smiles at noon

Daylight is cheap by design. Buses top off. Pools warm. Ice tanks freeze for evening. Desalination and aquifer recharge hum under bright sky. Easy math moved most heat to pumps and arcs; stubborn flames sip noon‑made hydrogen or honest biogas.

Retired coal sites re‑opened as storage yards behind fences neighbors already knew: iron‑air stacks that inhale rust, salt tanks that store heat, compressed air in the right rock. In a handful of places where locals voted yes after thick drills, compact reactors licensed like airliners—one small family of audited designs, common spares, crews trained to the same book—joined the mix. Bus depots sell evenings back to the grid by firmware; mechanics see the line item on their checks.

Machines with manners, and leashes with knots

If code can move money, push a wheel, order reagents, change doses, or publish to a million strangers, it needs a license. Big training runs carry compute passports tied to their energy sources and the provenance of their data. Licenses live and die on three things: passing red‑team drills by independent labs you can’t bully; logging incidents to a searchable ledger; and carrying insurance sized to plausible harm. Critical code compiles under certified tools that prove the binary matches the source. Robots and vehicles broadcast beacons. Anything that can harm without a hand on it sits under twin‑key rules and a bright, physical stop a child can reach; fourth graders take field trips to pull it.

We run narrow, boring systems—the Keepers—that schedule pumps, lights, and timetables. Each Keeper pairs with a Skeptic trained to sniff for brittle shortcuts. Red‑team scores hang in lobbies like café health grades. Encryption and attestation make mass surveillance clumsy. When a city quietly assembles a face database, its systems lose the ability to interoperate with neighbors until it stops and pays.

Over the seams sits Joint Watch, a civilian, warrant‑bound group that watches the few places a single instruction can magnify harm—chip fabs, genome printers, identity forges, market rails. When it grounds drones over six blocks because a lab tripped a strange signature, it owes the city a readable narrative and an appearance before a standing jury by lot.

Work, time, and reputation without grind

Copilots draft, summarize, schedule, and check arithmetic. Humans make calls. Error rates fall; output rises. Weeks settle near thirty‑two paid hours where logs allow it, with a paid service day people spend clearing culverts before fall rains, running flood drills, staffing Harbor Posts, or teaching ninth graders to disagree without cruelty. Credits you earn for this work fade into months you can cash as apprenticeship, grief leave, or a studio season. We pay for outcomes, not volume: kitchens by near‑misses caught and inspections passed; roofers by years without leaks; coders by uptime; barbers by clean tools and happy scalps. Past a size, companies drift to steward ownership: workers, customers, and a future trust appoint boards and hold the long view.

Behind the scenes hums the Match Loom. Most “we can’t”s are bad searches. Agents look for contracts across seams: a watershed compact across counties; a swap among theaters, libraries, and transit for off‑peak rides; small landlords pooling to hire a boiler crew. We also keep Understory Grants—small, fast money for small fixes you can photograph: fill the trip‑edge, rehang the gate, replace the fan. Receipts are public. Embarrassment does the rest.

Care like aviation, not theater

Hospitals run like careful kitchens and airfields: checklists that help, logs you can read, and applause for catching almost‑wrongs because catching them keeps real wrongs rare. Silent killers—sepsis, drug clashes, salt and potassium swings—hit padded rails at night; stays end days earlier. Department waits and errors sit under the names of leaders who promised to cut them; honesty with a plan is protected.

Outbreaks meet muscle memory. Wastewater and cough acoustics trip rehearsed thresholds. Masks and tests sit on contracts before panic. Variant‑to‑vaccine slid from sixty days toward thirty. Cancer moved earlier: cheap breath and blood pulled big killers into stages where they yield. Mind care has parity: coaches for sleep and fear, relapse plans written by the person who will use them, and more serious sessions that run like anesthesia—monitors, reversal, registries. Addiction shifted from sermon to maintenance. Slow‑release medications became as ordinary as seatbelts. Test strips sit in bars beside toothpicks. Teeth and sleep joined primary care. Payment follows physiology and days‑to‑steady. Pretty words do not pay.

Land and water at the table again

Cities drew green rings where human pressure ratchets down each year—vows, not walls. Creeks came back into daylight; floodplains flood with less panic; shade knocks ten degrees off July at noon. We pay working lands for the work we ask of them: hold water, shelter wildlife, feed pollinators, draw carbon. Sensors and boots verify and pay. Panels float over berries and sheep; birds keep talking. We signed a Civic Species Pact: animals with minds that can suffer get laws that remind us. Slaughterhouses that cannot meet it become museums with plaques we actually read.

Wildlife crosses over and under roads on snap‑together arches you can install in a weekend. Coasts grow living edges where they can and honest walls where they must. Move‑Together buys out whole blocks and moves neighbors intact; bonds pay better if neighbors stay neighbors. Return‑Together brings them back if water recedes and walls hold. Smoke and heat months open Breathing Rooms—filters, chilled air, cots, a nurse—no questions at the door.

Orbit tidied up, night dimmed on schedule

Low orbit cleans itself. Catcher drones earn by the kilogram. Satellites post bonds that fund their endings. We keep radio quiet above observatories and wild sanctuaries by treaty; half our nights give the stars back. Anything outbound carries keys that attest what it can do, where it may replicate, and when it must call home; the keys check against pulsars you can’t counterfeit. Far stations, if they bloom, join a Far Commons: access to tools there ties to open corpora, drought models, and clinic meshes here. Windowkeepers translate far wonders into fixes where taps still rattle.

The last mile, everywhere

Small trucks the size of a room tow light, cold, and signal down muddy roads to clinics that used to guess: fold‑out panels, batteries, a satellite link, and a night lab in a box that reads breath and blood for sepsis. Vaccines ride thermostable chains and drone loops. Community health workers get paid for verified immunization and TB treatment on ledgers they hold and the world can check without seeing names. Remittances are dull and cheap. A Prosperity Fund—fed by carbon and heat fees, land‑value leases in hot corridors, spectrum and orbit rents, and a compute license—pays for pipes, fiber, nurses, and transformers without speeches. Every critical service keeps a Minimum Offline: paper forms at the library, a window with a human, a mechanical clock on the door.

Belonging on purpose

Family stopped being a lottery, alone. Welcome Suppers open twice a week in most blocks. Circles of fifteen to twenty‑five form by choice—newcomers, elders, drifters with tools—and agree to matter to each other for a season. Each keeps a cache and a phone tree someone always answers. At eight, the Atlas begins: maps of how other places live and what promises they keep. At twelve, a Trail Season: a few supervised weeks living under a different neighborhood’s rules with a mentor and a quiet trail home. At fourteen, a Proof Year: a bench, a little money, and a red team to make a thing the world can touch. Each child has a Guide outside family and school and a private line to a Harbor Post. Big choices go into a Memory Safe that opens only to the person later.

Civic plumbing you can touch

Most Tuesdays hold two rooms nearby:

- The Workshop runs trials. Want to change lunches, parking, triage? You run it small for eight to twelve weeks. Models test your idea, and a red team pokes holes. You feed two cafeterias before you talk citywide. If you keep the promises you wrote down, you earn time and budget to try more.

- The Console lets us touch the dials: bus priority, garbage windows, floodgates, tree replacements, power‑shed rules. A public modeling service runs budgets and maps on open data. Proposals come with a policy diff—what changes, what it should cost, who gains and who loses, and a date to revisit. Forecast games pay accuracy and dock bravado. On Knob Days, an official moves a dial in the square and everyone watches the map change. On Null Days, we change nothing and practice our brakes. We wrote a simplicity clause: when layered rules congeal into nonsense, they sunset and must be replaced by something shorter that does the same job, with a diff you can read.

A day here, ten years in

You wake to slow heat seeping from a brick that drank at noon yesterday. Your assistant—unmonetized and provably loyal—notes your iron dipped last week and nudges you toward beans and greens before coffee. Across the lane, a five‑story building climbs like a stack of trays on a small crane. The site steward hands a seventeen‑year‑old a heat pump in pieces and a date for the hands‑on test that will earn a sash at the electricians’ hall even if equations never loved her.

At lunch, daytime power is almost funny. Pools warm; ice tanks freeze; the studio fires ceramic because electrons cost less than cinnamon. Buses top off for cents. Daylight belongs to feet and small wheels. Freight waits for night.

You split your afternoon between clinic and the Commons Yard. In clinic, the night cart caught a patient’s markers whispering before pain could shout; a dose nudged, an ICU weekend disappeared. Three near‑miss slips hang under a small flag because you celebrate catching almost‑wrongs as practice for catching real ones. At the Yard, a machinist holds up a spring the size of a thumbnail; a poet reads a page a child wrote about the first breath that didn’t hurt after someone fixed a stubborn gasket; a red team points at where last winter’s seals failed; the fix gets a bigger cheer than the feature.

On your way home your daughter drags you to the Console. A kid on a crate turns the bus‑priority dial along the school corridor. Blue dots brighten where waits fall; gray dots hold where late freight shifts a minute later. People argue kindly. They laugh. They choose a trial schedule. You don’t vote. You practice touching the world you’ll vote on next week.

Evening belongs to your Quarter. The common kitchen smells like onions and rain. The door is open. Elders sit on the steps. Someone stirs a pot and sets out more plates than chairs because that’s the habit. A new family eats without being asked to audition their hurt. A small sign above the sink reads: Listen, Steady, Let Go. If a palm reaches the orange tile by the door, you know what to do without speeches.

How we got here

No destiny lever. A string of errands, stacked neatly.

Years 1–3

- Plant Harbor Posts at transit hubs, clinics, libraries, courts, and a few quiet places locals know. Braid their back‑ends—shelter, legal aid, childcare, transit, clinics—into one stewarded pipe with rotating audits and strict liability for blocking an exit. Teach the blink pattern in ninth‑grade civics and in places teens already watch.

- Launch Keys so health, work, and school travel under your signatures with a paper twin that works when the net does not. Tie public money and insurer contracts to compliance. Ban ad‑subsidized assistants for core services; require fiduciary duty in law for any tool that sits between you and your own data.

- Pass Permit Clock laws and publish open patterns for rooftop solar, courtyard apartments, school retrofits, district cooling, basement egress, and disassembly depots. Scrap minimum parking near frequent transit. Map transmission like rail: corridors, schedules, and compensation you can read, plus relocation help and deadlines with damages for both sides.

- Light up civic servers: budgets, flood maps, timetables, permits, procurement in public code with tests you can click. Pilot Knob Days, policy diffs with review dates, and forecast games with calibration scores posted next to memos.

- License frontier compute and high‑actor robotics. Register training runs; watermark at the chip; require hazard evaluations by independent red teams with protected disclosure; enforce twin‑key rules for any code that can push an irreversible physical action; certify compilers for critical code. Ban impersonation without signed consent.

- Stand up an externality clearinghouse and Boundary Courts. Publish formulas; throttle bad actors until they pay. Seed the Commons Treasury with land‑value leases in hot corridors, carbon and heat fees, spectrum and orbit rents, and a light levy on ultralarge trades and compute. Spend first on rooms, kitchens, clinics, transformers, and five‑minute buses. Publish a transformer repair scoreboard.

- Build Breathing Rooms for smoke and heat months: filters, chillers, cots, nurses. No questions at the door.

Years 4–7

- Repower retired coal sites as storage yards within existing fences and wires. Where acceptable and safe by local vote after thick drills, install compact reactors from a small audited family. Commission long‑duration storage and set noon tariffs to soak midday. Enforce interconnection deadlines with fines that fund the office that gets it moving.

- Convert a third of ghost offices to homes, labs, and schools via retrofit patterns. Legalize backyard homes with safe egress kits. Guarantee five‑minute daytime buses on major routes; stripe night lanes for freight so daylight belongs to people.

- Expand competency‑based licensing; let nurses, pharmacists, midwives, and technicians work to the top of training. Fund guilds that certify by performance and underwrite liability. Normalize four‑day weeks where logs support it. Seed Time Ledgers that pay in months you can cash for apprenticeships, grief, and making. Stand up Understory Grants for small fixes with public receipts.

- Open seam schools as intergenerational commons for humans and as culture kitchens for new machine minds—places to learn patience, turn‑taking, and that “you’re scaring me” means stop. Codify assistant fiduciary duties; require objective labels for recommenders; default public feeds to well‑being modes.

- Electrify clinics everywhere; pay community health workers for verified coverage through privacy‑preserving ledgers. Start the push against malaria and drug‑resistant TB with breath diagnostics, thermostable vaccines, and contracts that pay for coverage. Put a night cart in every hospital. Stand up scrub courts for digital abuse.

- Entrench Workshops and Consoles. Require policy diffs and retrospectives for big moves. Practice floods, recounts, and outbreak thresholds. Build strategic shelves of critical spares. Make Down Day sacred.

Years 8–12

- Drive power‑sector emissions 70 percent below baseline; bring average retail electricity under seven cents per kilowatt‑hour, off‑peak industrial under three. Stitch corridor gaps; publish blackstart drills and recovery audits.

- Halve mortality from major cancers; add five healthy years to the median life; cut suicide and overdose deaths by half. Make night carts boring.

- Stabilize rent‑to‑wage ratios by saturating pattern‑driven housing and conversions. Triple safe accessory homes.

- Mature Joint Watch with civilian oversight and hard warrants. Keep verifiable bans on fully autonomous domestic killing. Expand child Guides and youth benches. Make Trail Season as common as the science fair.

- Entrench forecasting and calibration into procurement and budgets. Kill doomed boondoggles while they are larvae. Celebrate corrections on Repair Walls more than ribbon cuttings.

Years 13–20

- Complete continental transmission backbones. Close the last coal and repurpose their sites. Retire “transformers are on backorder” as a phrase.

- Institutionalize learning accounts and competency transcripts. Midlife career switches become unremarkable. Move most licensing to demonstration and guild certification where safety allows.

- Nudge weekly hours down again where feasible. Funnel recovered time into care, maintenance, art, and rest. Reduce nonsense work without drama.

- Clean orbit, dim the sky on schedule, keep quiet zones quiet. Tie far projects to near benefits through the Far Commons. Pay Windowkeepers to translate.

- Record rising satisfaction in quiet quarters and loud districts alike—as long as exits stay cheap and entry rails stay clear.

Hard edges, handled without a stage

A hillside congregation wrote prejudice into a charter. Legal. Not admired. Teens never left. The youth ombuds subpoenaed tower logs and found Harbor calls dying midair. A portable pillar parked by their fence. A judge ordered a weekly Trail Season class on the lawn taught by the ombuds. Elders fumed. Eighteen left. Thirty stayed, and staying meant something. Rites changed. The inside door opens now.

An “immerse forever” lounge tried to dodge consent rituals by calling itself therapy while eating weekends whole. A boundary court sealed the door until owners installed a bright brake, recovery‑plan counseling, loud timers, and Brief–Breathe–Bind. Investors learned a cheaper habit: prefer reversibility.

A startup lab ordered restricted reagents through a shell. Joint Watch flagged the pattern; stewards came with a narrow warrant and shut the valve. The owners stood before a jury by lot with engineers on it. No one burned a witch. The brake worked. The lab reopened under supervision and a new test that would catch the trick next time.

A mayor tried to build a face database in the dark. Interop flickered. Fines landed. Knob Day turned into a polite grilling. The database died. The mayor learned to say “I was wrong,” and kept her job because she meant it.

Why it holds

- Floors before wallpaper. Hot meals and clean rooms, cheap reliable power, honest clinics, and exits that work shrink fights.

- Schedules and receipts beat vibes. A “no” must name a risk in words a jury can understand. A “yes” starts a clock with a bond behind it. Forecast boards make humility prestigious.

- Exits without performance turn loyalty into a gift. Strict‑and‑kind places thrive. Strict‑and‑cruel places empty or learn.

- Edges price edges. Heat, noise, runoff, spam, and attention floods hit a meter, not a sermon. Pipes and feeds throttle when debts aren’t paid.

- Sharp tools follow aircraft rules. Licenses, drills, logs, independent insurance, and protected testers make “safe enough” mean something.

- We practice when calm. Outbreaks, floods, recounts. Muscle memory beats improvisation.

- Maintenance is visible. We keep shelves of spares. Down Days are real. When storms knock, the grid stands back up in days, not weeks.

- Attention is a watershed. Public feeds default to gentle. Influence wears labels. The lure light makes the casino your choice.

- Kids have a map and a person. Helplessness loosens. Parochial fog thins. A fourteen‑year‑old can read a charter at a door and choose.

- The last mile is boring and proud. Fridges hum in clinics that used to guess. Paychecks land. Old diseases lose because delivery wins.

Stand in the square at dusk. A charter glows above a door anyone can pull. A judgment scrolls in verbs you understand. The repair crew waves from a ladder. A child presses both palms to a glass panel and watches a line on a map widen, grinning because the world told the truth in a way hands can feel. Down the block, someone’s fingers find the woven strap without shame. You point to the tram that came every five minutes, to the kitchen that fed anyone at noon, to the clinic that caught a cancer early, to the pillar that opened a room across town tonight. It is good because it protects people and place with intention and leaves room to breathe and to try. It is specific because if you arrive hungry at noon we can point to beans and heat, and if you arrive afraid at midnight we can walk you to a post and know someone will answer. It is plausible because we got here by errands: pass clocks, open ledgers, fix transformers, run drills, fund stewards, license code that can bite, practice leaving. We made the boring parts excellent. We bragged about brakes. We made leaving cheap and staying sweet. Then we went home, and the lights were on.
