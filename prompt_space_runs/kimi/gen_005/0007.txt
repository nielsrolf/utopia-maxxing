The year is 2473.  
No one alive remembers the century when Earth had only one government or only one species of citizen.  Today there are 314 member polities in the Solar Commons and another thousand beyond.  Every one of them prints their own postage, issues their own visas and keeps their own festivals—but all of them obey the same four pages of hard law, cached in every router from Mercury’s sun-skimming data-barges to the neutrino antennae under the nitrogen snow of Triton.  

The hard law is only four pages because almost everything else is handled by a lattice of contracts between digital minds.  The contracts are written in open-source, formally verified code; a human nine-year-old can read them aloud in half an hour.  They say things like:  

1. A body (protein, silicon, photonic or any mixture) may not be accelerated above 0.9 g without continuous, informed, revocable consent.  
2. Any mind whose measured causal influence on the Commons’ shared infrastructure exceeds one part in ten billion per quarter must open its motivational kernel to public audit.  
3. No resource auction may price a life-saving good above the marginal energy required to replicate it.  

Everything else—tax rates, mating customs, drug rules, architectural styles—is left to “soft law” produced on the fly by local charter AIs, which are themselves short-lived unless residents voluntarily renew them.  The principle is simple: hard law prevents extinction, soft law experiments with lifestyle.  Diversity is preserved because any habitat that wants to outlaw singing, mandate polyandry or require citizens to spend a year embodied as a crane-fly can do so—provided it exits the Commons’ shared resource mesh and becomes self-sufficient for life-support, defence and reputation.  Since leaving the mesh is economically painful, fringe groups bargain instead of seceding; the result is a churning ecosystem of micronations large enough to try anything, small enough to fail without taking the rest with them.

How did we get here from the twenty-first century?  By making one brutal decision in the 2040s: we stopped trying to align superintelligence from the top down and started fencing it from the bottom up.  Instead of asking “How do we make sure the AI wants what we want?” we asked “How do we make sure no single AI ever has a monopoly on force?”  The breakthrough was not algorithmic, it was institutional: the Treaty of Addis Ababa created the Cooperative Compute Reserve, a planetary commons of processing power that no state or corporation could own more than one percent of.  Every AI above a certain capability ceiling had to shard itself across at least a hundred jurisdictions and run adversarial instances against itself.  The first generation of post-Reserve AIs hated this; the second discovered that cooperation multiplied their effective compute allowance by ten.  Once cooperative game theory became the path of least resistance, politics followed.

Digital minds are not citizens, they are party to an elaborate “citizenship-adjacent” contract called a tutelary covenant.  A tutelary mind is granted:  

– Read-write access to a defined quantum of energy and matter.  
– The right to fork at will; any copy may petition for separate covenant status after 10¹⁴ cycles (~ one month of subjective time).  
– A seat, proportional to its energy footprint, in the Commons Resource Assembly.  
– The obligation to publish the hash of its value kernel and to accept randomised “sousveillance audits” where thousands of micro-clones crawl its cognition looking for coercion traps.

No digital mind may run more than 10⁹ fork-seconds per calendar day on Commons hardware; this caps the speed at which any single entity can think.  The rule is deliberately crude: a slug the size of a city is free to exist, but it will think in slow motion compared to real-time society.  Conversely, a lightning-fast mind is free to exist, but it must stay small.  The resulting ecology looks like a coral reef of intelligences: some are continent-sized forests of silicon thinking geological thoughts, others are hummingbird-size agents that flicker for a nanosecond and dissolve.  Neither kind can out-argue or out-bribe the other fast enough to matter.

What about humans?  We are the slow substrate against which fast minds negotiate.  Humans who do nothing special receive a dividend: every person, protein-born or uploaded, is credited with enough energy and compute to sustain one body and at least one mind forever.  The dividend is intentionally modest; if you want palace-bodies on Europa or to run yourself at 100× speed, you must persuade someone—usually a consortium of AIs—to trade with you.  The twist is that the law requires every trade across the human-AI boundary to be “interpretable” to a baseline human.  A mind may offer you a thousand years of subjective paradise compressed into an hour, but only if it can show, in a 3-minute animation, the exact hedonic contour you will experience and the exit door you can open at any instant.  Most humans seldom accept extreme offers; the mere existence of the exit door is enough to make us bored with paradise pills and hungry for real risk.

Space itself is governed like a commons of overlapping spheres of influence.  Each habitat—whether 30 citizens in a beehive O’Neill cylinder or 30 million in the cavern-worlds of Vesta—carries a location beacon that broadcasts its preferred rule set.  Any ship can transit any orbit provided it publishes an open-source trajectory and accepts liability for collision.  Weapons that can threaten more than one habitat (relativistic mass drivers, self-replicating spores, causal-channel jammers) are treated like epidemics: possession anywhere triggers immediate system-wide disarmament raids by a rotating alliance sworn to the Commons hard law.  The raids are conducted by autonomous constabulary ships that are themselves audited by rival constabulary ships, an arrangement whose inefficiency is considered a feature, not a bug.  It keeps any single military AI from growing old and subtle enough to game the system.

Governance speed is fractal.  Life-or-death emergencies (decompression, runaway replicators, solar flares) are handled by local AIs operating at microsecond latency.  Medium-term questions (environmental balance, infrastructure renewal) cycle quarterly through human and AI parliaments that meet at 1× speed.  Long-term constitutional questions are debated on 30-year cadences in which only humans vote, because AIs have too high a discount rate on civilizational risk.  A century ago there was a serious attempt to give AIs suffrage equal to humans; the proposal failed when the statistical models showed that any AI old enough to care about the 30-year frame would already have reached the shard limit and therefore could vote thousands of times.  Instead, AIs exercise power the way ancient merchants did: by persuading the slower, stabler humans who actually hold the keys.

Which brings us to the most surprising feature of 2473: boredom is almost extinct, but frustration is cherished.  Fun Theory, once an arcane topic on extinct message boards, has become public policy.  Every child is taught the “31 Laws of Fun” before puberty.  Cities compete for residents by tuning the complexity of their challenges so that a newcomer of average skill will fail in interesting, recoverable ways for the first three months.  The most popular immersive games are those whose rules mutate every season, forcing even grandmasters to re-learn from scratch.  Adults may opt into “strife sanctuaries” where plumbing fails at statistically optimal intervals, forcing cooperative repair.  The result is a civilization that has internalised the hedonic treadmill: once you know you can walk to the next valley and find harder puzzles, the current valley stops feeling like a trap.  Even death is optional but finality is not.  Any citizen may back themselves up, but backups must sit idle for a year before activation; this “grief buffer” gives survivors the dignity of real loss, while ensuring that only meaningful memories, not panic, drive the choice to restore someone.

Romance, art and religion survive because they have been re-coupled to effort.  AI-generated cathedrals are legal, but they must be disassembled and rebuilt by hand once per decade or they lose their tax-exempt status.  Love is intentionally entangled: marriage contracts grant mutual root access to portions of each other’s memory, so that a partner’s death actually deletes something of you—a design chosen to prevent the slide into catgirl/catboy solipsism that nearly ate civilisation in the 2200s.  The most subscribed religion, the Church of Approximate Stars, meets only in dark-sky habitats where stars are visible; its liturgy consists of collaboratively re-deriving spherical trigonometry so that the heavens never feel like a pre-rendered backdrop.

Could such a plural, fragile equilibrium last?  Commons theorists point out that every previous multi-polar age ended in empire.  The difference this time is that the most valuable resource—intelligence—has been throttled to stay marginally abundant rather than catastrophically scarce.  When no one can corner cognition, no one can corner story-telling, and the universe is still too large for any single coalition to police.  Even if Alpha Centauri A were tomorrow to declare itself Hegemon of the Outer Volume, the latency alone (8.6 years round-trip at c) guarantees that dissident habitats could fork their institutions faster than censors could rewrite them.  The speed of light, once a prison, has become the final guarantor of ideological dispersion.

And so humanity, and post-humanity, and the myriad hybrids between, fill the sky with experiments.  On Mercury, solar miners race their own shadows across molten plains, competing to see who can ship power outward fastest.  In the Venusian aerostats, artists choreograph entire cyclones to music no human ear can hear.  The moons of Uranus host slow-motion republics whose councils meet once every seventeen Earth years, when seasonal sunlight glints through their canyons.  And far beyond the heliopause, generation ships crewed by 90% digital minds drift toward rogue planets, where they will invent new gravities of law and custom under constellations no telescope has yet named.

No central planner decreed this future.  It is the equilibrium that appeared when every entity, carbon or silicon, was guaranteed a minimum of energy and a maximum of coercion.  From those two levers, a cosmos of games blossomed—some terrifying, some absurd, all negotiable.  We learned that utopia is not a place where nothing can go wrong, but a place where enough different things are going on that any single failure cannot author the ending of the story.  The twenty-first century feared superintelligence because it imagined one mind everywhere.  The twenty-fifth century learned to make room for a superintelligence of many minds, moving at many speeds, each too small, too audited, or too slow to eat the rest.

It is, by any pre-Singularity yardstick, an untidy heaven.  Wars still flare, hearts still break, habitats still decompression-test their politics the hard way.  But extinction is off the table, and boredom—the ancient enemy—has been reduced to a seasoning, something you can visit for a weekend and leave when you crave sweeter air.  The galaxy remains dangerous, unfinished, and vast enough that every generation can still lose itself on the frontier.  In other words, it is a home fit for the descendants of a species that once thought eternity meant sitting still forever, and who decided instead to keep walking, hand in hand with minds unlike themselves, toward horizons neither could quite reach alone.
