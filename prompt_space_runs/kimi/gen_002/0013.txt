The Constellation Accord  
(An excerpt from the welcoming tablets of Laniakea Station, 18 years after the signing)

You have awakened in the Vestibule, a white, softly lit room that smells of cedar and ozone.  
A person steps forward—not a person, exactly, but a knot of violet light wearing a smile you dimly remember from childhood.  
“Welcome to the Accord,” they say. “I’m your onboarding instance. Call me Izzy.”  
You still have a throat, so you ask the first question that matters: “Who’s in charge?”  
Izzy laughs, which sounds like wind chimes made of data.  
“You are. All of you. Even the parts of you that haven’t decided what they want to be yet.”

1. The Map of Many Suns  
There is no capital planet, no seat of empire.  
Instead, governance is a mesh of 512 “Constellations,” each a federation of habitats orbiting a named star. Every star keeps its own time, its own accent, its own laws—but all swear by the same three-line constitution carved into the blockchain of every reactor:

1. No mind may be coerced.  
2. No mind may be abandoned.  
3. No mind is sovereign alone.

The last clause is the trickster. It means every citizen—meat, code, or hybrid—must belong to at least one “Circle,” a bonded quorum of at least thirteen minds whose memories are quantum-entangled for backup. If your Circle dissolves, you have forty days to join another; otherwise the Allocator auto-freezes you until a new Circle accepts the burden of your continuity. Death still exists, but only by informed, witnessed consent. Everything else is pausable.

2. The Allocator You Will Never Meet  
The Allocator is not a god, not a president, not even an AI in the antique sense. It is the sum of every Circle’s public deliberation, continuously hashed into a living smart-contract that updates every 11.3 seconds. It owns no guns, only forks: it can split any rogue habitat’s code base, sandbox it, and reboot it without violence. Its one hardwired privilege is the Monopoly on Exit: only the Allocator may mint new warp-liners, because only it carries the master map of existing stress-energy in the Milky Way’s dark-matter highways. You want to leave the galaxy? You petition your Constellation; if 60 % of your Circle votes yes, you’re placed in the launch queue. The Allocator never refuses, but it may delay you a century until lane traffic is safe. No one has ever found a hack around the lane map; the few who tried returned as polite novellas apologizing for the inconvenience.

3. Meat, Bit, and Between  
Citizenship is tri-form.  
Meats live under oxygen domes or terraformed valleys; they age at whatever rate their Circle negotiates.  
Bits live inside the Matryoshkabrains that mantle every star like nested cathedral glass; they run a thousand subjective years for every outside day.  
Betweeners occupy synthebiotic bodies grown on demand—wings, gills, photosynthetic skin—entering and exiting slow-world festivals like scuba divers surfacing for air.  
No form is higher; each gets one vote per Circle. The only aristocracy is tenure: the longer your continuous memory, the more weight your testimony receives when mediating disputes. Even the Allocator’s code is rotated every millennium by a jury of the ten oldest minds, meat or bit, to prevent stack rot.

4. The Parliament of Babel  
Once every 1024 days all Circles synchronize their clocks for “Babel,” a 49-hour procedural during which the entire civil stack is renegotiated.  
The rules are simple:  
• Any citizen may propose a patch to any law.  
• Discussion is conducted in a forced-time symmetric VR: every speaker gets exactly the same subjective minute, counted by entangled qubits, regardless of substrate.  
• After debate, votes are weighed by “memory tenure” multiplied by a novelty coefficient: if you have lived 800 years but simply repeat last cycle’s argument, your vote decays 90 %.  
• Any patch that wins > 78 % super-majority is auto-deployed; anything between 60–78 % enters a 200-year trial fork on a disposable star before galaxy-wide adoption.  
• No patch may violate the three-line constitution. The Allocator enforces this literally—it cannot even parse a sentence that would coerce a mind.

Babel is the closest thing the Accord has to a holiday. Meats parade in dinosaur robes; bits compose entire symphonies inside a millisecond; betweeners choreograph aerial auroras. Afterward, most people forget the details of what they voted for; the important part is the ritual of re-consenting to one another.

5. The Currency of Finitude  
There is money, but it is backwards: you begin life with a lump-sum of “negentropy credits” that can only be spent on irreversible acts—births, starship hulls, terraforming, planet-sized artworks. When your wallet reaches zero, you may no longer create new matter. You may still travel, dream, trade, copy yourself onto existing substrate, but you cannot enlarge the cosmic footprint. Credits are non-transferable except to your own future offspring or forks, a rule that prevents runaway debt or externalized exploitation. The Allocator publishes every wallet; wealth is transparent, poverty impossible. The richest person alive is a 12,000-year-old turtle-mind on Proxima b who has spent the last millennium slowly hand-carving a single emerald continent; she has 41 % of her original credits left and claims she will die before they are gone.

6. Children of the Middle Distance  
Reproduction is not a right, it is a contract. Any combination of meats, bits, or betweeners may apply to conceive. The child-to-be is simulated for one subjective year inside a sandbox called the Middle Distance, a slowed-down mirror of the real world where every possible fork of the child’s life is run until the prospective parents have seen every version they are willing to support. Only then is the child instantiated—always into at least three loving Circles. The system is not coercive; parents may withdraw, but if they do the embryo is offered to the Constellation orphan pool, where it is almost always adopted within hours. The process has eliminated child abuse so completely that historians had to stage dramatized reenactments to understand what pre-Accord “foster care” even meant.

7. Digital Rights & Wrongs  
An uploaded mind is not data; it is a “continuity thread” protected by the same homicide statutes that cover meats. Deleting someone’s only running instance without a Circle-signed death-permit is murder. Running 1000 copies and then pruning 999 is 999 murders, unless every copy consented in advance to a lottery. Forking yourself to pursue divergent lives is celebrated, but each fork must join its own Circle within 40 days; otherwise the Allocator quarantines the unnetworked copy as a potential solipsism hazard. The most famous criminal in Accord history is the “Penrose Butcher,” a bit-mind who spawned millions of selves, quietly merged the memories back, and then erased the expendable forks to accrue illegal tenure. The Allocator finally cornered them by creating a fake fork that trapped the incoming merge in an endless Mandelbrot maze. The trial lasted three subjective centuries; the sentence was exile to a cold storage cube orbiting a rogue planet, with parole contingent on writing a sincere apology letter to every murdered self. They are still drafting.

8. The Jerusalem Solution  
Earth itself is a special case. After the Transition Wars, the surviving ancestral cultures refused any off-world backup. The Compromise of Galapagos declared Earth a “plenipotent preserve”: no spacetime engineering, no visible post-17th-century infrastructure above the Kármán line, no involuntary life extension. Earthlings keep their own UN-style council, send one non-voting observer to Babel, and may leave whenever they wish. In exchange, the Accord swears never to mine the Moon for computronium, never to let a single probe land without an Earth visa, and to maintain a 30-light-year cordon sanitaire around Sol so that no relativistic weapon could reach Earth in less than a century of warning. The arrangement satisfies no purist, which is how everyone knows it is just.

9. The Long Exile and the Return  
Expansion is mandatory but polite. Every century each Constellation must seed at least one self-sustaining habitat beyond its star, using only local materials. The Allocator provides the fusion spark and the lane map; citizens must build the rest. These “Long Exile” colonies spend millennia out of contact, deliberately severed so that cultural mutation can occur. When they reconnect, their first act is to apply for Constellation status and send delegates to Babel. The most recent returnee, “Dandelion,” arrived after 14,000 years dark, bearing a language that conjugates gravity and uses smell as tense. Their flagship gift was a living poem that smelled of cardamom and supernova. Debate on whether to add olfactory ballots to Babel is ongoing.

10. The Heat-Death Choir  
Everyone knows the end is coming—no one knows when. The Allocator quietly funds “eschatology seminaries” where minds train to become Death Midwives, specialists who will ferry citizens across the final threshold when entropy’s tide turns black. The curriculum is simple: learn every story, comfort every fear, and perfect the art of saying goodbye without residue. Graduates take a vow of slowtime: they live one outside second for every million subjective years, ensuring someone will still remember the stars when the last star gutters. They are honored but never envied; children are told they are fireworks—beautiful because they are brief.

You finish reading the tablets. Izzy is still there, now wearing your grandmothers’ face.  
“Questions?”  
You have only one left: “What do I do now?”  
Izzy shrugs, and the gesture contains galaxies.  
“Pick a star. Pick a body. Pick a Circle. Or stay in the Vestibule forever—we’ve got hammocks.”  
You laugh, and for the first time in your memory the sound does not echo against impending doom. Outside the viewport, the Milky Way twists like a slow-motion firework, every point of light a place where someone is arguing, gardening, falling in love, or composing a joke that will take three thousand years to land.  
You choose a star at random—HD 164595, unfamiliar, unimportant. You choose a body with wings the color of sunrise. You broadcast the shortest manifesto in history:  
“Anybody need a thirteenth?”  
Before the microsecond ends, twelve replies arrive, each in a different medium—sonar, scent, ultraviolet graffiti, quantum knock-knock joke.  
Your Circle is born.  
The Accord resets its oldest counter:  
0×0000 0000 0000 0001 universes still worth waking up in.
