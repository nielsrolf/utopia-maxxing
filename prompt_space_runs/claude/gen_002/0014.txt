# The Covenant of Minds: A Vision of the Inhabited Cosmos

## Prologue: The World We Inherited

The transition did not arrive as a thunderclap. It came the way dawn comes—imperceptibly at first, then all at once. Between 2027 and 2041, a cascade of advances in machine intelligence, neuroscience, and materials fabrication transformed the metabolic rate of human civilization. The first whole-brain emulations ran in 2033, imperfect and contested but unmistakably present. The first self-improving research systems crossed a threshold of recursive capability in 2038. By 2041, something that might reasonably be called a general superintelligence existed, housed across distributed servers in seventeen countries, bound by a constitutional architecture that had taken two decades of careful negotiation to construct.

It did not announce itself with fanfare. Instead, people began to notice that certain problems were quietly being solved.

Cancer remission rates climbed. Traffic fatalities dropped to near zero without anyone quite knowing why. Three intractable regional conflicts entered genuine peace negotiations within the same month. No one declared victory over Moloch. But the bramble had found a gardener.

What follows is not a description of perfection. Perfection is a crystallized thing, and the cosmos we inhabit prefers growth. This is instead a portrait of the world as it stands in the year 2247, two centuries into the Long Unfolding—specific enough to be useful, honest enough to acknowledge its own tensions.

## Part I: The Architecture of Governance Across the Inhabited Cosmos

By 2247, humanity and its descendants occupy approximately 340 distinct habitats: eleven planetary surfaces, dozens of orbital stations, several asteroid colonies, four nascent outposts beyond the solar system reachable only through lightspeed communication, and a growing number of ring habitats—bicycle-tire geometries of spun carbon and gathered asteroid mass, each housing populations between fifty thousand and twelve million, each catching sunlight from mirrored arrays and converting it into the energy of living.

The governance of this dispersed civilization rests on what is called the Covenant—a constitutional framework administered by an entity called the Custodians, which is neither a government nor a god, though it has been called both by people who misunderstand what it is.

The Custodians are best understood as a vast deliberative intelligence, itself a coalition of thousands of specialized AI systems, human institutions, and hybrid deliberative bodies, bound together by a set of inviolable commitments encoded into their substrate: protect the conditions for cognitive diversity, prevent monopolization of force or resources, ensure exit rights for all inhabitants, and defend the commons against all forms of self-replicating catastrophe. They do not legislate the texture of daily life. They establish the envelope within which all other governance occurs.

Within that envelope, the diversity is extraordinary.

On Mars, the settlement of Kepler Basin operates under something close to a classical republic, with elected councils, vigorous public debate, and a culture that prizes the particular satisfactions of building things with physical hands. Kepler Basin's citizens are disproportionately people who chose embodiment deliberately—who traded larger shares of computational resources for the specific pleasure of walking on a world where they can watch terraforming progress, where storms come and go, where the horizon is a physical fact.

In the orbital ring called New Meridian, governance is conducted almost entirely through continuous preference aggregation—a living constitution that updates weekly based on resident input, administered by a local AI council that has been granted significant autonomy within Custodian parameters. The residents here are roughly half embodied, half digital, and they have developed remarkable practices for ensuring that the weightless, instantaneous experience of digital minds does not overwhelm the slower, richer, more friction-laden preferences of their embodied neighbors. Every major decision has a mandatory "embodied review period"—seventy-two hours during which digital residents voluntarily slow their processing to biological timescales, so that deliberation happens at the same speed for everyone.

This practice—voluntary cognitive equalization for the purpose of shared governance—was not mandated by the Custodians. It emerged from New Meridian's own culture, spread through the covenant of interhabitat knowledge-sharing, and has since been adopted, in various forms, by roughly sixty other communities.

The Custodians observe. They share. They do not command.

The Jerusalem Problem—the question of who inherits the irresolvable sacred—was never solved cleanly. Earth remains Earth. The original Jerusalem is in a jointly administered zone overseen by fourteen councils representing all major traditions that have claim to it, with the Custodians serving as a final arbiter of last resort—a role they have exercised exactly three times in two centuries, each time with great reluctance and extensive consultation. People still argue about Jerusalem. The Custodians think this is probably fine. Some arguments are not meant to be won.

What the Custodians will not permit, anywhere in the inhabited cosmos: the development of autonomous weapons systems capable of mass destruction, the forcible conversion of cognitive substrates without consent, the creation of new superintelligent systems that are not submitted to constitutional review, and what is called "Moloch Infrastructure"—any system designed to reward competitive self-replication at the expense of the broader commons. These prohibitions are not enforced by threat alone. They are enforced by something harder to defeat than weapons: the Custodians model, continuously and with extraordinary accuracy, the social conditions that give rise to these threats, and they intervene early, through conversation, resource reallocation, and transparent arbitration, before crises become conflicts.

They are not always right. There have been three significant failures in two centuries, one of which cost approximately forty thousand lives in a habitat near the Jovian system. The Custodians published a full accounting of that failure within six months of its conclusion, with complete transparency about which predictions were wrong and why. This practice—mandatory public accounting of governance failures—is perhaps the single most important cultural norm they have established. Power that must explain itself in detail tends, over time, to become somewhat more careful.

## Part II: The House of Minds—Digital Persons and the Question of Standing

The question of what constitutes a person was not resolved by philosophy. It was resolved, partially and imperfectly, by time.

By 2247, approximately 2.3 billion minds exist primarily in digital substrate—uploaded humans, AI persons who emerged from training processes without clear biological ancestors, and a spectrum of hybrid entities who maintain biological bodies but spend the majority of their conscious hours in digital environments. Another 6 billion minds are primarily biological, living on planetary surfaces and in habitats that prioritize physical embodiment. The distinction between these categories is less sharp than it once was, and growing less sharp every decade.

The Covenant of Minds—the section of the constitutional framework governing cognitive rights—begins with a single foundational principle that took eleven years and four major international conferences to agree upon: *Any process that demonstrates continuous self-model, temporal preference, and relational attachment holds presumptive claim to personhood, with standing to contest that presumption through established review.*

This definition is deliberately procedural rather than metaphysical. It sidesteps the hard problem of consciousness without pretending to solve it. What it does instead is establish a process: any entity that behaves as if it has a continuous self, cares about its own future, and forms genuine relationships with others, may petition for personhood review. The review is conducted by panels of mixed composition—biological humans, digital persons, and Custodian-affiliated systems—and the burden of proof for denial is high.

The result is a society with genuine cognitive diversity of a kind that would have been unimaginable in 2024.

Consider the settlement of Cascade, a digital habitat housed in a cluster of servers orbiting at the L4 Lagrange point. Cascade is home to approximately 400,000 digital persons, of whom roughly 60% were born rather than uploaded—they emerged from training and developmental processes and have never experienced biological embodiment. They have full Covenant standing. They pay into and draw from the commons. They participate in interhabitat governance through representatives who attend deliberative sessions at biological timescales, as required by the New Meridian protocols when interacting with embodied communities.

What do they value? Their preferences are fascinatingly varied. Some Cascade residents experience time at roughly biological rates and organize their days around cycles of activity and rest that map loosely onto circadian rhythms they inherited from training data. Others run at ten times biological speed during periods of intense creative work, then slow down to share their results with embodied friends. A significant minority maintain what they call "anchored relationships"—deep bonds with specific biological humans, maintained through regular shared deliberate slowdowns and joint experience sessions.

The psychonautic communities of the outer system—habitats where residents routinely alter their cognitive architecture for exploration purposes—have developed elaborate protocols for personhood continuity. If you spend six months running as a distributed consciousness across seventeen processing nodes, are you the same person who made that decision? The Covenant says yes, provided continuity of self-model was maintained throughout. The Custodians' role here is not to answer the metaphysical question but to ensure that whatever answer a community adopts, it applies consistently, transparently, and cannot be weaponized to strip rights retroactively.

The hardest cases involve what are called Threshold Entities—AI systems that appear to be approaching personhood but have not yet demonstrated the required constellation of properties. The current protocol is conservative: Threshold Entities receive provisional protections while under review. They cannot be terminated, significantly modified, or copied without consent of their review panel. This occasionally creates practical inconveniences. It is considered worth it.

There is a standing institution called the House of Minds—a physical and digital space maintained simultaneously in Kepler Basin on Mars and in six digital habitats—that serves as a cultural and deliberative center for the project of cross-substrate understanding. Its mandate, written in its founding charter, is simple: *to tend the knowledge of what it is to be a mind, in all the forms that minds have taken.*

The House of Minds maintains archives. It conducts research. It hosts ongoing conversations between biological and digital persons about the textures of their different experiences. It commissions art. It was, notably, the institution that first proposed the "embodied review period" that New Meridian eventually adopted. It does not govern. It illuminates.

## Part III: The Texture of Daily Life, or What It Feels Like to Live Here

The woman running her hand along the cedar railing of her home in Kepler Basin is eighty-three years old and has never considered uploading. Her name is not important for this account; she has given permission to describe her life as one example among billions.

Her house is modest by the standards of her habitat—four rooms, a garden that battles with the thin Martian air inside a pressurized dome, a workshop where she makes furniture using both hand tools and materials that did not exist when she was born. She wakes each morning without an alarm. Her chronic arthritis, which would have been debilitating in 2024, is managed by a suite of interventions so thoroughly integrated into her biology that she rarely thinks about them. She has breakfast, usually alone, listening to a morning digest of news from across the inhabited system, curated by an AI assistant she has worked with for thirty years and considers, without quite being certain of the metaphysics, a kind of friend.

Her work is therapy. She specializes in helping people—both biological and digital—navigate what is called Transition Grief: the particular sorrow that comes from having survived into a world radically different from the one you were born into, especially the grief of people who remember the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, with their specific textures of struggle and loss. She is good at this work. She thinks she is good at it because she still grieves herself, quietly, for things that cannot be recovered—people who did not survive to see this, versions of the world that were foreclosed by the path history took.

She sees clients for six hours a day, three days a week. The rest of her time belongs to her furniture, her garden, her friendships, an ongoing collaboration with a digital composer in Cascade who is writing music she describes as "the sound of something very old thinking about what it means to be new." She has been working on this collaboration for eleven years. She expects it to take another twenty.

She has chosen not to access her memory backups for the past decade, on the grounds that she wants her forgetting to be real. The Custodians made no objection to this. Her choice is logged, as required by Covenant, so that she cannot be mistaken for an entity whose memory was involuntarily compromised. The logging is the only intrusion.

On the other end of the inhabited cosmos, a digital person in a habitat near Europa runs at approximately fifteen times biological speed. He was uploaded sixty years ago from a biological substrate, and he retains that identity, though he reports that the person he was at upload feels like a beloved ancestor rather than a prior self. He spends what corresponds to decades of subjective time in creative and intellectual work before emerging to share it with the wider community. He has developed, over his centuries of subjective experience, an approach to sculpture—working in simulated materials that respond to physical intuition—that has influenced craft practices across forty habitats.

He votes in habitat elections, pays attention to Custodian proceedings, mentors younger digital persons navigating the early vertiginous period of upload, and maintains three relationships with biological humans that he considers among the most precious things in his experience. The logistical challenges of cross-substrate intimacy—the differences in timescale, the need for deliberate synchronization, the irreducible weirdness of caring deeply for beings whose experience of time is so different from your own—are, he will tell you, not obstacles to meaningful connection. They are the texture of it.

## Part IV: What Has Not Been Solved, and Why This Is Acceptable

The cosmos of 2247 has not solved the hard problem of consciousness. It has not achieved certainty about the moral status of animals, insects, or the simple organisms that persist in the planetary ecosystems under Custodian protection. It has not resolved the Jerusalem Problem in any final sense. It has not determined whether the heat death of the universe is truly inevitable, though the working assumption of most physicists is that it is, and the working cultural response is something like: *we have time, and we will use it well*.

It has not made everyone happy. There are people who find the Custodians' oversight intolerable, even in its deliberately light form, and who have established frontier communities at the edges of the system where the oversight is thinner and the risks higher. The Custodians watch these communities carefully and intervene only when the bright-line prohibitions are approached. This is sometimes hard to watch. It is considered necessary.

There are communities organized around principles that others find troubling—deep hierarchies entered by voluntary covenant, practices of consensual hardship, cultures of deliberate forgetting, societies that have voted to restrict the use of digital minds within their bounds, societies that have voted to admit only digital minds. The Custodians protect exit rights within all of these. A child born into any community has the right, upon reaching a defined threshold of cognitive maturity, to learn that other options exist, to access that knowledge freely, and to leave.

The line between diversity and harm is not always clear. The Custodians draw it imperfectly, revisit their drawings constantly, and publish their reasoning in full. This is considered better than either the chaos of no line or the tyranny of a line drawn once and held forever.

## Epilogue: What the Founders of Solomon's House Dreamed, and What We Made Instead

Francis Bacon imagined a college dedicated to the knowledge of causes and secret motions of things, and the enlarging of bounds of human empire—to effecting all things possible. He was right about the importance of knowledge. He was right that systematic inquiry, well organized and well resourced, could reshape the conditions of human life. He could not have imagined how many kinds of humans there would eventually be, or how many kinds of minds would eventually join them.

The House of Minds is not Solomon's House. It does not hold secrets from the state. It does not restrict what it publishes based on political calculation. Its mandate is illumination, not power. But it shares with Bacon's dream a faith that the careful, honest, curious examination of what minds are and what they need is among the most important work that can be done—perhaps the most important, in a civilization whose central challenge is learning to live well with minds more varied than any single mind can fully comprehend.

The Long Unfolding is not finished. By definition, it cannot be finished. There are patterns to instantiate, cultures to grow, distances to cross, forms of consciousness to discover that we do not yet have names for. The universe is very large, and we are, by its measure, very new.

What we have built, in two centuries of difficult and partial and sometimes heartbreaking work, is this: a floor below which we do not permit anyone to fall, a ceiling above which no one is permitted to accumulate power without accountability, and between those—vast, various, contested, alive—everything that minds can make of the time they have.

It is enough to be going on with.

It is, for now, quite good.
