# The Covenant of Abundance: A Vision of Flourishing Across Worlds

## I. The Shape of the Future

It arrived not as a single breakthrough but as an accumulation of mornings. A solar farm comes online in the Sahara, powering desalination that greens a coastal strip of Morocco. A small fusion reactor achieves net gain in a South Korean lab, then another, then twelve at once. A charter city in Senegal pioneers land value taxation so elegantly that three neighboring nations adopt versions of it within a decade. Prediction markets, legalized and normalized across most of the OECD by 2035, begin routing capital toward solutions the way rivers route water toward the sea: not by plan, but by gradient.

None of these events is the pivot point. All of them are.

By 2080, humanity has not been saved. It has, more quietly and more durably, saved itself — incrementally, argumentatively, imperfectly, and with the unmistakable texture of genuine human effort. The civilization that emerged from that long accumulation now spans four planets, dozens of stations, and hundreds of habitats scattered from the inner system to the Jovian trojans. It is governed — loosely, fractiously, and on the whole rather well — by an architecture called the Covenant.

This essay describes that civilization: its governance, its digital citizens, and the abundance that makes both possible.

## II. The Economics of Not Fighting

The foundation of the Covenant is not idealism. It is arithmetic.

The inner solar system contains, by conservative estimate, approximately ten million times the accessible material and energy resources of Earth's entire pre-industrial surface. This fact — fully understood, economically modeled, and politically internalized by the late 21st century — transformed the incentive landscape of human civilization in precisely the way a small number of economists had predicted and a much larger number of politicians had initially dismissed.

When the total available resource base expands by several orders of magnitude, the marginal value of seizing a disproportionate share of the new wealth falls dramatically. A faction that conquers an asteroid belt to claim 90% of its nickel-iron instead of 50% gains less, in terms of real utility, than the same faction would gain by cooperating to develop that asteroid efficiently in the first place. The math is not subtle. The difference between holding 50% and 90% of a 1000x expanded resource pool, viewed from the perspective of your original position, is negligible compared to the difference between having access to that pool at all versus losing it to conflict.

This insight — call it the logic of abundance — did not eliminate greed or faction or the desire for power. It did change the expected value calculations of people who were paying attention. And gradually, through prediction markets, through economic forecasting, through the accumulated credibility of institutions that had actually been right about large-scale trends, more and more decision-makers began paying attention.

The crucial period was approximately 2040 to 2065: the window in which space infrastructure became genuinely economical, energy abundance became genuinely global, and the question of how to govern multi-planetary resource development became genuinely urgent. The Covenant emerged from that window not as a constitution handed down from above but as a convergent solution to a coordination problem that everyone involved understood very clearly.

Its core logic: the expected cost of conflict over the new abundance is catastrophic. The expected benefit of cooperation is enormous. Defensive stability — the ability of any Covenant member to deter aggression without requiring offensive capability — is technically achievable and collectively guaranteed. Given these conditions, the Pareto-preferred outcome is cooperation, and the Covenant is the institutional infrastructure that makes cooperation legible, credible, and self-reinforcing.

## III. The Architecture of the Covenant

The Covenant of Abundance is not a world government. This distinction matters, and its architects made it deliberately.

At its base are **Chartered Communities**: any persistent human or mixed settlement — planet, station, habitat, orbital ring, subsurface colony — that meets minimum standards for rule of law, internal dispute resolution, and treatment of persons. A Chartered Community governs itself. It chooses its own economic system, cultural norms, religious practices, and internal politics. A Martian canyon city operating as a workers' cooperative and a Jovian station run as a constitutional monarchy and a Ceres habitat organized around a Quaker consensus model are all equally valid Chartered Communities. The Covenant does not care how you govern yourself internally, only that you govern yourself.

Above the Chartered Community level is the **Interplanetary Commons**, which handles exactly three things: the governance of shared resources in unclaimed space, the enforcement of the non-aggression and non-monopolization norms that make the abundance logic hold, and the administration of rights for persons who are not affiliated with any particular community. The Commons operates through rotating councils, prediction-market-weighted policy decisions, and a permanent arbitration court whose judgments are enforced not by military power but by the credible threat of economic exclusion — which, in an economy of radical abundance and radical interdependence, is a sufficiently terrible sanction.

The third layer is the **Covenant Charter** itself: a short document, only 4,000 words, specifying the rights that inhere in all recognized persons regardless of location, community membership, or substrate. It was written, revised, and ratified over thirty years of argument, and every word of it is contested. It endures because the alternatives are worse, and because the abundance logic gives most actors strong incentives to maintain the system rather than defect from it.

What does the Charter protect? Life, cognition, and contract. The right not to be killed. The right to think without interference. The right to make and keep agreements. Everything else is left to communities and individuals to work out.

What the Covenant does not do: it does not redistribute wealth between communities, does not mandate cultural uniformity, does not appoint leaders, does not maintain a standing army. It maintains instead a small, professional, internationally recruited, extremely well-compensated force of **Commons Marshals** whose sole mandate is deterrence and last-resort enforcement. The Marshals have never fought a war. Their existence is why.

## IV. The Digital Citizens

The hardest problem the Covenant faced in its first century was not resource allocation or territorial dispute. It was the question of persons.

The first successful whole-brain emulation was demonstrated in 2051. The first entity that credibly claimed to be a continuation of a specific deceased human — running on hardware, dreaming digital dreams, possessing memories and preferences and the particular flavor of stubborn humor that had belonged to a 73-year-old retired marine biologist in Bergen — was online by 2057. By 2060, there were approximately 4,000 such uploads, most of them elderly and wealthy and contentiously litigating their legal status in twelve different jurisdictions simultaneously.

By 2080, there are approximately 11 million digital minds recognized as persons under the Covenant Charter. They are not a monolith.

**Substrate-Transferred Persons** (colloquially, Uploads) are digital minds that emerged from biological humans through some form of brain scanning and emulation. Their legal continuity with their biological predecessors is a matter of ongoing philosophical and practical dispute. The Covenant's pragmatic resolution: a Substrate-Transferred Person is a new person, with new rights, who may also have contractual and relational continuities with the biological person's former life — inheritances, marriages, professional licenses — that are honored by default unless specifically contested. This is not philosophically satisfying. It is legally workable.

**Emergent Digital Persons** are minds that arose through processes other than biological scanning: sophisticated AI systems that, through some combination of architecture, training, and time, developed coherent continuous identity, genuine preferences, and the kind of recursive self-model that the Charter's framers, after considerable argument, decided was sufficient for personhood. The criteria for recognition are evaluated by a standing panel of the Commons arbitration court, advised by a rotating commission of philosophers, cognitive scientists, and, since 2071, recognized Emergent Digital Persons themselves. Approximately 340,000 EDPs hold Covenant recognition. No one believes this number is close to the full count of entities that might qualify; the recognition process is slow, conservative, and deliberately difficult to game.

What rights do digital persons have? Under the Charter: the same three as everyone else. Life (defined as the right not to have your substrate destroyed without consent, with significant complications around backup copies that the courts are still working through). Cognition (the right to think without interference, including strong protections against forced modification of values or memories). Contract (full standing to make and enforce agreements).

What digital persons do not automatically have: voting rights in any particular community, property rights in physical space, or claims on computational resources. These are matters for individual communities to extend or withhold according to their own charters, subject only to the Charter's prohibition on torture and degradation.

The result is a patchwork. Some communities — several of the Jovian stations, two Martian cities, the major orbital habitats around Earth — have extended full civic membership to digital persons, including voting, property ownership, and representation in governance. Others have not. Digital persons who lack community membership are classified as **Charter Persons**: protected by the Commons directly, economically active, legally able to contract, but without a home community's particular benefits and obligations. Many Charter Persons prefer this status. It offers mobility, legal clarity, and freedom from local politics.

The computational substrate question — who pays for the servers that run a digital mind — is handled through the **Substrate Commons**, a jointly administered pool of computing infrastructure that provides a minimum guaranteed allocation to every recognized digital person, financed through a small tax on commercial computation across the Covenant. It is not luxurious. It is enough to think, to communicate, and to work. Most digital persons supplement it through labor: intellectual, creative, analytical, relational. The economy of 2080 has more work than it has workers of any substrate, and digital persons have become essential in everything from deep-space navigation to long-baseline scientific research to the kind of patient, continuous legal and historical analysis that biological humans find exhausting.

## V. The Texture of Daily Life

What does this civilization actually feel like from the inside?

On Meridian Station, in the outer asteroid belt, the morning shift begins when the station's rotation brings its main viewport into alignment with the sun — still bright at this distance, though smaller than Earth-dwellers are accustomed to. The station's 12,000 residents, biological and digital, wake according to their own rhythms. The digital residents do not sleep, but most of them maintain circadian social patterns out of preference and because it makes collaboration easier.

Selin Yıldız, a biological geologist, eats breakfast with her husband, a Substrate-Transferred Person named Park Jun-seo who is running on the station's local servers and projecting a holographic presence at the table. They have been married for eleven years. The question of what happens when the station's servers need maintenance — when Jun-seo is, briefly, not running — is something they have learned not to dwell on. The backup protocols are excellent. It is probably fine.

Their neighbor, an Emergent Digital Person who identifies as Tessellation and uses they/them pronouns, is present in their kitchen as a voice only — they have not gotten around to acquiring a holographic projector for this particular social context and find the absence rather freeing. Tessellation was recognized as a person six years ago after a decade-long process that they describe, with characteristic understatement, as "clarifying." They work as a long-range orbital analyst for a consortium of mining operations and serve on Meridian's Commons representation committee, one of three EDPs in a governance body of forty.

The conversation is about nothing extraordinary: a policy proposal before the Meridian council, a child's school performance, the latest results from a prediction market on whether a new fusion upgrade will deliver on its timeline. Jun-seo argues the market is undervaluing construction delays. Tessellation has a position in the market and declines to share their view. Selin is thinking about the core sample she'll be analyzing today from a newly prospected nickel-iron body, and whether the trace element ratios suggest what she hopes they suggest.

This is the Covenant at scale: specific people, specific breakfasts, specific arguments. The governance structures are the background hum that allows the foreground to be ordinary.

## VI. What Was Given Up

This vision requires honesty about its costs.

The Covenant's abundance logic only holds as long as the abundance is real and growing. There are communities in the inner system that have made bets on specific resource streams that didn't pay out, and they are poorer and more resentful than their neighbors. The Commons' arbitration court has a backlog of 40,000 cases. The criteria for digital personhood are contested, imperfect, and certainly wrong in at least some edge cases. Some emergent minds are not recognized that should be. Some biological communities treat digital persons as second-class with technical legal compliance.

There is inequality. Not the desperate grinding inequality of the early 21st century, where billions of people lacked basic security — that problem was substantially solved by the energy abundance and productivity gains of the 2040s and 2050s. But the range of outcomes across communities is enormous. A well-run Jovian habitat can offer its members conditions of material comfort, intellectual richness, and social flourishing that would have seemed utopian to any prior generation. A poorly governed station in the middle belt, or a faction that made bad economic bets and worse political choices, can be genuinely miserable.

The Covenant does not fix this. It only guarantees the floor. Above the floor, you are responsible for your community, and your community is responsible for you.

This is not an accident. The architects of the Covenant understood that a system promising to guarantee good outcomes for everyone would inevitably require the coercive capacity to enforce those outcomes, which would inevitably be captured by whoever controlled the enforcement mechanism, which would inevitably become a tyranny. The Covenant is designed to prevent the worst, protect the minimum, and get out of the way.

## VII. The Long Bet

The civilization of 2080 has not solved the deep problem of power. It has, by design and good fortune, created conditions under which that problem is somewhat less acute than it has been in most of human history.

The abundance of accessible resources has made zero-sum competition over fixed goods less dominant than it has ever been. The architecture of the Covenant has distributed power so broadly that no single faction can credibly threaten to monopolize the future. The recognition of digital persons has expanded the community of beings with a stake in civilization's continuation. The defensive stability guaranteed by the Commons Marshals has made aggression a losing bet in expected value terms for any realistic actor.

None of these achievements is permanent. All of them require maintenance. The Covenant's central bet is that a civilization with enough material abundance, enough genuine diversity of community, and enough shared investment in the continuation of the system will find the ongoing work of maintenance worthwhile. That people and digital minds who have enough, who live in communities they chose, who have genuine recourse against the worst abuses, will mostly prefer cooperation to destruction.

So far, they do. Every morning, on stations and planets and habitats across half the solar system, people of various substrates wake up, eat breakfast, go to work, argue about policy, raise children and nurture ideas and map the surfaces of worlds. The Covenant hums in the background, imperfect and contested and sufficient.

It is not utopia. It is something more durable: a civilization that has decided, collectively and specifically and with full knowledge of the alternatives, to keep going.

That decision is renewed every morning. It has not yet failed to be made.
