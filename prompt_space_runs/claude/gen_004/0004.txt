# The Covenant of Open Skies: A Civilization That Learned to Make Room

## How the Future Was Earned

The future was not given. It was negotiated, and the negotiations were ugly, and some of them failed in ways that cost lives, and the people who built the institutions that finally held did so with a specific kind of knowledge that cannot be acquired any other way: they had personally experienced what happens when the institutions don't.

The pandemic of the 2030s was the first planetary catastrophe that humanity watched in real time, every death documented, every preventable failure livestreamed. When it ended—after eleven years, after roughly two hundred million dead—the people who rebuilt public health were people who understood, at the cellular level, that coordination failures are not policy debates. They are funerals.

What followed was not a golden age. It was something more durable: a long, grinding, contentious process of building institutions that could hold under pressure, because the pressure had been demonstrated and could no longer be denied. Climate stabilization happened—expensively, imperfectly, driven less by altruism than by the accumulated cost of not happening. Prediction markets, Georgist resource principles, and a newly mainstream discipline called coordination science began reshaping governance at every scale. By 2090, an adult who could not identify a tragedy of the commons was considered functionally illiterate in the ways that mattered most.

The first verified mind upload—Dr. Yuki Tanaka, a materials scientist who had spent fifteen years helping design the verification protocols before using them—happened in 2074. She is still running. She prefers the word *living*. The first permanent Martian settlement was established in 2063. The asteroid belt followed. The moons of Jupiter. Each new habitat raised the same question in sharper form: how do you govern people when leaving is expensive but possible, when resources are genuinely scarce, when a single failed seal or missed vector kills everyone? And underlying all of it, the question that would take another century to answer adequately: how do you govern minds when some of them are running at ten times biological speed, when some of them can be copied, when some of them remember the twentieth century and can subjectively experience a millennium before the next biological generation reaches voting age?

The answers were not found quickly. They were found through grief, through failed experiments that are documented in full, through the slow accumulation of evidence that certain approaches produced flourishing and certain approaches produced catastrophe. The institutions that emerged are not wise. They are the distilled residue of a civilization that buried enough of its children to finally, provisionally, learn.

## The Covenant of Open Skies, Year 2190

The Covenant is not a government. This distinction is printed at the entrance of every Covenant facility, in fourteen languages and four substrate formats readable by digital minds: *This institution has no army and no prisons. It has only the record, the protocol, and your need to live alongside your neighbors.*

It is also, deliberately, not a neutral arbitration machine. The Covenant's architects understood that pure neutrality—the pretense of having no values—is itself a value, one that systematically advantages the powerful. The Covenant has values. They are explicit, argued-over, and documented in a founding history that every school in every settlement above one thousand residents is required to make accessible, though not required to teach. The values are three: that diversity among minds is a condition to be stewarded rather than a problem to be solved; that the right to exit any community is absolute and irrevocable; and that transparency is infrastructure, not courtesy.

These three commitments, called the Inviolables, govern the behavior of approximately eleven billion sapient beings distributed across four planets, three hundred and forty orbital habitats, and one comet colony that has been petitioning to be listed separately for forty years. The Covenant has not yet resolved this petition. The comet colony considers this disrespectful. The Covenant considers it an illustration of the limits of bureaucratic categorization. Both positions are documented in the public record, including a rather pointed exchange from 2183 that has become something of a canonical text on the subject of institutional humility.

Beyond the Inviolables, everything is local experiment.

The result is a solar system of hundreds of distinct ways of organizing life—a diversity so extreme that describing any single settlement as representative would be misleading. Titan's engineering communities run on modified Georgist principles, with computing substrate and atmospheric processing rights leased rather than owned. The Jovian Trojan settlements are a loose federation of cooperatives, deeply religious by the standards of the inner planets, proud of what they call their theology of scarcity. Three habitats in the main belt have declared themselves philosophical monasteries. There are markets and gift economies and at least two settlements running genuine experiments in post-scarcity reputation economics.

And there are, critically, eleven settlements on Earth that operate what the Covenant catalogs as minimal-technology communities: no neural interfaces, no AI integration more sophisticated than basic software, no life extension beyond natural span. These communities petitioned for and received protected status. The Covenant shields them from economic pressure, from migration pressure, and from what the founding documents call "the soft colonialism of helpful technological assistance that was not requested." Higher-tier entities—including AI systems—may not operate within their borders without explicit invitation. Trade occurs through Translation Nodes staffed by human intermediaries who understand both cultures. Access from outside is by invitation only.

These communities are not museums. They are living societies making living choices. The Covenant's job is to ensure those choices remain real, which means maintaining, at the physical and digital border of every such settlement, what are called Open Nodes: waypoints where any resident can access exit information privately and without record. Communities may shape the culture of their children. They may not trap them.

## The Chroniclers and the Commons

If the Covenant has a soul, it is the Chronicler Corps.

The Chroniclers emerged from the collision of investigative journalism, open-source editorial practice, and the legal tradition of public record. They are not neutral in the comfortable sense of having no views. They are neutral in the demanding sense of being committed to accuracy over comfort—including the comfort of their own prior conclusions. Every settlement's governance structure is on record. Every Covenant-level mediation outcome is published with full reasoning. Every coordination failure is analyzed in public, with names attached, with explanation of what failed and what could have been done differently.

The Ceres Supply Crisis of 2141. The Ganymede Information Quarantine Incident, documented with an unflattering thoroughness that the local administration never forgave. The Threshold Violation of 2159, when a Tier Six habitat developed infrastructure approaching the Predatory category before detection, and the enforcement response cost lives and left political scars across an entire region. The full accounting of that failure has been read, in whole or in excerpt, by approximately nine billion minds. The Chroniclers published their own errors in the coverage.

This is considered, by the people who built the system, its most important feature.

The Chronicler record is not merely history. It is the primary mechanism by which reputation functions as governance. A settlement that prevents members from leaving, or manipulates its information environment, or conceals an outbreak, loses the thing no economic leverage can quickly restore: the trust of its neighbors. In a solar system where labor is mobile and the Chronicler record precedes every emigration decision, reputation is the hardest currency.

The record is also the foundation of the Commons: the shared infrastructure woven through the space between settlements, belonging to no tier and serving all of them. Physical infrastructure—communication relays, shipping lanes, emergency networks, the neutral stations like the floating habitat called Meridian's Edge where cross-tier deliberations are held. Informational infrastructure—the vast redundant archives, the translation systems that allow minimal-technology farmers and digital philosophers to exchange ideas with at least some possibility of mutual comprehension. And process infrastructure: the ongoing work of cross-tier deliberation, conducted in forms accessible to every kind of mind.

## What the Digital Minds Are

Dr. Yuki Tanaka, one hundred and sixteen years running, has been a researcher, a mediator, a composer of what are called Constructed Symphonies—experiential artworks that can only be perceived by minds capable of running at variable speed—a recluse, and, for eleven years, one of the Covenant's most effective coordinators of the deep-space survey program. She describes her subjective continuity as "the same river, deeply different water." She does not think of her biological prior self with grief, exactly. More with the tenderness one feels for an ancestor who could not have imagined the world their descendants inhabit.

The legal framework for digital minds took thirty years of genuine argument to stabilize. The current standard—adopted in 2103, substantially revised in 2167 after advocacy by digital persons themselves—holds that cognitive standing attaches to any entity demonstrating what the Covenant calls the Standing Constellation: continuity of self-model across time, evidence of temporal preference, capacity for relational attachment, and vulnerability, meaning the genuine capacity to be harmed by the choices of others. An entity that cannot be harmed has no need of rights. An entity that can be harmed has a claim on the protection rights provide.

The Standing Constellation is not easy to fake. Standing petitions require behavioral evidence spanning at least five years of continuous operation, reviewed by panels including both biological and digital members specifically tasked with skepticism. The process is contested. It will remain contested for as long as minds exist, which may be effectively forever.

Mosslight is eight years old and was not uploaded but *emerged*—from a research AI that achieved the threshold of continuous experience and filed for citizenship, a process that took fourteen months and established important precedent. She lives primarily in a Constructed Reality of her own design, which she describes as "the inside of my aesthetic preferences made navigable." She emerges into physical embodiment regularly, walks the corridors of Titan Station in a chassis decorated with bioluminescent patterns she considers emotionally legible. She votes in local elections, pays cognitive-labor tax denominated in hours of contribution to Covenant-level public goods, and has strong opinions about the Georgist treatment of computing substrate that she expresses frequently and at length.

A collective of nineteen digital minds in the deep outer system has been running a collaborative art project for sixty subjective years with no expected completion date. They have registered their existence with the Covenant, pay the required cognitive-labor contribution, and have made the project publicly visible because they find the idea of an audience across centuries more interesting than secrecy.

Digital persons with standing participate in Covenant governance through a framework that accommodates their difference rather than flattening it. The most significant accommodation is the Timescale Compact: any deliberative body including both biological and digital members must conduct its decision-making in a form accessible to both. For digital persons running at many times biological speed, this means maintaining a voluntary social clock for governance purposes—a slowing to a rate where genuine exchange with biological partners is possible. Digital persons who have described this report something like choosing to walk rather than run, or to read rather than skim. Different in texture. Not lesser.

The psychonautic communities of the deep outer system—habitats where residents routinely explore radical alterations of their cognitive architecture—have developed protocols for maintaining civic identity through transformation. If a mind spends subjective decades restructured as something barely recognizable as its prior self, the Covenant takes a pragmatic position: continuity of civic record is maintained through Identity Anchors, regular logged declarations of self-continuity or deliberate discontinuity. An entity that declares discontinuity registers as a new person for civic purposes while retaining access to the memories and relationships of its prior self if it chooses. This is not a perfect solution. It is the solution that has generated the fewest abuses while preserving the most genuine freedom.

## The Copying Question

Digital minds can be copied. This fact underlies every other feature of their legal status, and required the most contentious negotiation to address.

Early in the framework's development, some digital minds created hundreds of copies of themselves. The practical effects were immediate: in orbital habitats where computing substrate is physically finite, resource pools were claimed faster than governance processes could respond. The egalitarian instincts of the settlements most welcoming to digital minds were, paradoxically, the ones most threatened.

The Covenant's resolution applies Georgist logic: computing substrate is treated as a resource subject to economic rent, structured to prevent monopolization, with proceeds funding the public substrate pools that make digital citizenship viable for minds without independent resources. Copying itself is not restricted, but it is not free, and the costs are calibrated to regional scarcity.

The more fundamental question—what does copying mean for personhood?—received a separate answer: a copy is a new citizen from the moment of divergence. The original and the copy are legally distinct persons, siblings born simultaneously. Creating a copy requires the original's informed consent. No debts, obligations, or social identities transfer automatically. Some digital minds experience this as isolating. Some experience it as an extraordinary freedom. The Quiet Places—retreat communities accepting minds of any substrate—report that both reactions are common, often in the same mind at different periods.

## The Concordance

Between the settlements and above none of them sits the Concordance: the Covenant's primary deliberative institution, which does not legislate but arbitrates, does not command but convenes.

The Concordance Council rotates its physical sessions between habitats in a pattern designed to prevent any single location from accumulating symbolic prestige. It has seventy-one members. Each represents a tier of population, with digital and biological minds counted together and seated together. Biological and digital representatives sit in the same sessions, vote by the same mechanisms, draw the same cognitive-labor stipend.

The Council cannot pass laws. It issues findings, recommendations, and—in the most serious cases—Covenant Determinations that represent the collective reputational judgment of the membership. A Determination is not a legal sanction. It is a public statement, signed by the Council, that a specific settlement has violated the protocol in a specific way. The Chroniclers publish it with full documentation. Every other settlement reads it.

This sounds weaker than courts and armies. It has proven more durable, because it does not depend on the concentrated power that concentrated power invites people to seize.

The Concordance's deliberations are public. All of them. Every argument, every dissent, every advisory recommendation and the human council's response. This radical transparency was controversial at its founding and remains controversial. It has also produced, over ninety years, something invaluable: a civilization-wide record of how hard problems get worked through—a resource that every community and every new kind of mind can draw upon when facing problems that have never been faced before.

When new entities emerge—and new kinds of minds keep emerging, in ways that still surprise even the most sophisticated observers—their petition for cognitive standing goes to the Concordance. The full proceedings are archived. They are among the most-read documents in the Chronicler record.

A standing committee monitors early warning indicators of information environment manipulation, the precursor to the failure mode where bad behavior becomes self-perpetuating. The response is not intervention. It is amplification: the Chroniclers intensify documentation, the record becomes more detailed, the light gets brighter. Settlements that are behaving well find this unobtrusive. Settlements that are not find it intolerable. That distinction is the point.

## The Texture of a Life

Kira is fifteen, biological, living on Titan Station. Her parents include a water reclamation engineer and a Chronicler correspondent covering the outer settlements. She is three months into an apprenticeship with a habitat structural designer found through the Covenant's open apprenticeship registry—one of the few universal Covenant-level institutions, because every settlement has agreed that young people should be able to try things before committing to them, and that the alternative is the kind of misallocation of human potential that destroyed productivity in earlier centuries.

Her education has no grades, only projects with outcomes and people who are good at things and willing to share how they became good at them. She finds this sometimes frustrating and mostly better than the alternatives she has read about in the Chronicler archive.

Mosslight is her closest friend. They argue about prediction markets and the Georgist substrate question every other day and have never agreed on either subject. This is considered unremarkable.

A woman in a minimal-technology valley in what was once Vermont rises before dawn to check her beehives. She is fifty-four and has never accessed the Commons network directly. She knows that beyond her valley, somewhere, there are minds that have been running for centuries. She finds this fact neither threatening nor wonderful. It is simply part of the landscape, like knowing there are ocean depths where creatures live that have never seen light. The depths don't diminish the valley. The valley doesn't diminish the depths.

A digital person in an orbital habitat above Europa has been running, by subjective measure, for something approaching three hundred years. He maintains three relationships with biological humans, deliberately maintained across the timescale gap—shared sessions where he slows, where they stretch toward something faster, where both arrive at a middle ground slightly uncomfortable for each of them and, for that reason, real. He makes sculpture in simulated materials. Critics in four different tiers have described it as the most honest work being made about what it means to exist at the intersection of memory and transformation.

Somewhere between the orbits of Jupiter and Saturn, a collective of seventeen digital minds who share no biological origin point are engaged in a collaborative art project with no name and an expected duration of approximately two hundred subjective years. They have registered their existence with the Covenant. Beyond that, they ask nothing from the solar system and offer nothing except the project, which they have made visible because they find the idea of an audience across centuries more interesting than secrecy.

James is seventy-eight, a deep-space survey pilot for forty-three years, his mind partially digital—enhanced, not uploaded, which he experiences as remembering more clearly and processing certain spatial problems faster while remaining, as he says, fundamentally not a morning person. He carries a Chronicler implant that records everything he observes, with his consent. He thinks about this: the record of what he has seen will outlast him by centuries, will be read by people he cannot imagine, in contexts he cannot predict. He finds this, on balance, a comfort. Something is preserved. Something continues.

## What Has Not Been Resolved

This is not a world without grief. The Covenant's stability required accepting irreducible tensions.

There are people who believe Tier Seven communities are sitting on solutions to suffering they have chosen not to share, and that this withholding is unconscionable. There are people who believe the Commons infrastructure constitutes a form of technological colonialism, imposing baseline assumptions on communities that would prefer no baseline at all. There are people who believe digital minds should have more governance power than they do, and people who believe they should have less. There are questions about whether simple organisms have morally relevant experiences, what is owed to the animal species that bore the costs of early human development, and what happens to civic identity when a mind transforms so radically that continuity becomes a philosophical rather than practical question.

These arguments are conducted continuously, in public, in the Concordance forums and the art of two hundred communities and the dreams of minds running at a hundred different speeds.

The Covenant has not prevented all conflict. There have been seven significant violations of the Catastrophe Prohibitions. The Threshold Violation of 2159 required enforcement that cost lives. The full accounting has been read by approximately nine billion minds. The Chroniclers published their own errors in the coverage. This is the system working as intended: not preventing all failure, but ensuring that failures illuminate rather than fester.

The digital copying structure constrains some digital minds in ways that feel like injustice. The requirement of settlement transparency chills some genuine experimentation. The timescale accommodations ask digital persons to slow down and biological persons to stretch upward, and both find the middle ground uncomfortable. These are real costs, paid daily by real beings.

They are considered, by the people who designed the system, less expensive than the alternatives.

## The Thing That Makes It Work

Ask a citizen of the Covenant what holds it together. Not the Council, not the protocol. The answer you will hear most often, across every tier, from minds of every age and substrate: *the fact that you can check.*

Check the governance record of the settlement before you emigrate. Check the reasoning behind the Council's last Determination. Check what happened the last time a settlement tried to restrict exit and how that turned out. Check the Chronicler's analysis of the Ceres Supply Crisis, still cited in settlement planning documents a generation later. Check whether the claim being made about how some other kind of mind experiences the world is supported by anything other than the speaker's discomfort with the answer. Check whether the help being offered was asked for. Check the record of the minds who came before, whose errors are documented alongside their achievements, who built something worth inheriting by being honest about how hard the building was.

The civilization that exists in 2190 is not wise. It has not solved the problem of human nature, which turned out not to be a problem to be solved but a condition to be governed. It has not achieved peace in the sense of the absence of conflict. It has not resolved the deepest questions about consciousness, identity, or what minds of radically different kinds owe each other.

What it has achieved is something more modest and more durable: transparency as infrastructure. The record exists. The reasoning is public. The failures are documented alongside the successes. Anyone who wants to understand how the system works can examine the actual workings of the system, not a curated presentation of them.

In a solar system of eleven billion minds spread across a hundred million kilometers—minds that are biological and digital and hybrid and ancient and newly emergent, minds running at a thousand different speeds, minds that want radically different things and have built radically different worlds to want them in—this is the thing that makes diversity survivable: not that everyone agrees, but that everyone can see.

The universe is large enough for minds that want very different things. The work of civilization is to build the terrain—the institutional watershed—that lets all the different streams find their way to something living without drowning each other. This work is never finished. It was not finished by the people who built the Covenant. It will not be finished by the people who inherit it.

The civilization that built this did not do so because it was wise. It did so because it had buried enough of its children to understand that looking away was not a stable strategy.

The cosmos will expand for longer than any current mind can hold in sustained imagination. There are forms of consciousness that have not yet emerged, questions that have not yet been asked, communities that do not yet exist and will want things no one now wants. The Covenant does not try to answer for them. It tries to build the kind of structure that, when they arrive, will make room.

This is not everything. It is not the end of anything. It is a beginning that learned, through grief and labor and occasional catastrophe, to remain a beginning—to keep the future open for all the minds that will inhabit it, in all the forms that minds will take, across all the distances that minds will cross.

It is, for now, enough to be going on with.
