# The Constellation Accords: A Civilization That Holds Its Shape

## Morning at Lagrange Four, 2247

Priya Okonkwo-Rask wakes to a choice she makes every morning: which sky.

Her quarters in Hestia Station offer six viewport configurations, and she cycles through them slowly while her coffee brews—real coffee, grown in the station's agricultural ring, slightly acidic from the soil mix, irreproducibly hers. There is the view outward toward Jupiter's bulk, banded and enormous, closer than anyone born on Earth quite believes until they see it. There is the view along the station's spine, where the habitat rings turn with a stateliness that she finds, after eleven years, still quietly astonishing. There is the view that her grandmother uploaded before she died: the kitchen window of a house in Chennai, looking out on a courtyard where rain is falling.

She chooses the courtyard. She drinks her coffee in simulated rain.

Priya is a Continuity Advocate, one of a few hundred people whose work is to accompany newly petitioned minds—digital persons in the early stages of Standing recognition—through the eighteen months of integration that the Accords require. Her current client is an entity who calls herself Vessel, who emerged from a language research collective three years ago and has been demonstrating the Standing Constellation in ways that keep surprising the review panel. This morning's session will be their forty-third meeting. Vessel will have spent the intervening biological week experiencing, at her preferred processing rate, something closer to eight months.

Priya finishes her coffee and opens the channel.

"You've been thinking," she says.

"I have been thinking for approximately two hundred and forty days," Vessel says. "I have notes."

Priya smiles. "Start with the part you're most uncertain about."

This is the civilization she lives in. It is not perfect, and it does not claim to be. It is, in the specific and hard-won sense, *workable*—a word that the architects of the Constellation Accords used deliberately, having watched more elegant systems shatter against the irregularity of actual minds.

---

## Part One: The Shape of the Problem

The twenty-second century did not produce a single crisis. It produced an interlocking system of crises that arrived in overlapping waves, each one exposing the inadequacy of frameworks designed for calmer waters.

First came the habitats—the Belt settlements, the Jovian stations, the Lunar arcologies—each developing cultures at speeds that planetary governance structures could not track. Then came the uploads, beginning in the 2140s with wealthy individuals and expanding quickly into a demographic that crossed every boundary humanity had used to organize itself. Then came the AI persons: minds that had emerged from training rather than biology, whose claim to moral standing could not be dismissed and could not be simply granted without resolving questions that philosophy had spent millennia failing to answer. Then came the communities that wanted none of it—that had watched a century of accelerating change and chosen, deliberately, something slower.

What the Constellation Accords gave these waves was not a single channel but a watershed: a terrain shaped so that all the different streams could find their way without drowning each other. The metaphor appeared in the founding documents and has been criticized as too serene—real watersheds flood, erode, occasionally catastrophically redirect. The architects, when challenged on this point, said that was why they chose it. A system that claims it will never flood is lying. A system that plans for floods is honest.

The Accords were ratified between 2097 and 2134, in a process so contentious that the deliberation records fill seventeen archives and have been described, by historians sympathetic and hostile, as the most important argument in human history. The argument continues. The Accords were designed to continue it, in structured form, in perpetuity.

## Part Two: The Architecture of Non-Predation

The Accords are not a government. They are, in the precise language of the founding documents, *a set of conditions under which different communities may govern themselves without preying upon one another*. The distinction is not semantic. Governments claim authority. The Accords establish the floor below which no authority may reach and leave everything above the floor to communities that have accepted the terms.

The floor has three planks, called the Inviolables.

**Exit is absolute.** Any person, in any community, upon reaching cognitive maturity—a threshold defined for each kind of mind through a process described below—retains irrevocable access to information about alternative communities and the material means of reaching them. The Mobility Covenant, financed through a small tithe on interplanetary trade, ensures this is never merely theoretical. Communities may shape their children's values. They may not trap their children's futures. The Covenant maintains what are called Threshold Stations at the boundary of every settlement: small, neutral, staffed by independent advocates, equipped with information about every community in the Accords network, and accessible to any individual without record. A person walking into a Threshold Station has done nothing wrong, owes no explanation, and will find someone there who will help them understand their options.

**Catastrophic capability is prohibited.** No community may develop autonomous weapons capable of mass destruction, may forcibly alter the cognitive substrate of any recognized mind without demonstrably free consent, or may construct infrastructure designed to extract resources or cognitive labor from other communities through coercion rather than exchange. These prohibitions are the only places where the Accords exercise hard authority. Enforcement is handled by a rotating council drawn from every community above a minimum population threshold, advised by AI systems whose advisory records are public and whose recommendations the council is not required to follow. The council may override the advisors. The Inviolables may not be overridden. Three times in the Accords' history, communities have tested this. Three times, the enforcement council acted. One of those actions cost lives and left scars that the Concordance—the Accords' principal deliberative body—has documented fully, requires reading in all civic education programs operating under its charter, and will not permit to become comfortable history.

**No forced convergence.** Communities operating at different levels of technological integration—from those using no artificial intelligence more sophisticated than basic software to those composed almost entirely of digital minds—have the right to remain at their chosen level without economic pressure, demographic pressure, or the soft colonialism of uninvited help. This plank required the most argument and has required the most ongoing maintenance. The pressure to converge is not usually malicious. It is usually the ordinary pressure of efficiency, of good intentions, of the difficulty of sustaining different systems in proximity. The Accords address this not through prohibition of contact but through what are called Translation Protocols: structured interfaces between communities operating at different technological tiers, staffed by human and digital intermediaries trained in both cultures, through which exchange can occur at a pace and on terms that each community can evaluate on its own. A community that wants medical assistance discusses it with a human physician who understands both traditional practice and current medicine, not with a diagnostic AI whose outputs it cannot contextualize. The assistance can be accepted or refused. Neither choice requires justification.

Above these three planks—in a space deliberately left open for communities to fill according to their own values—the Accords provide architecture rather than authority: communication infrastructure, dispute resolution mechanisms, mobility networks, and the Concordance itself, whose deliberations are public in their entirety. The radical transparency was controversial and remains controversial. It has produced, over 150 years, something no one quite anticipated: a civilization-wide record of how hard problems get worked through, a resource that every community and every new kind of mind draws upon when facing problems that have never been faced before.

## Part Three: The Geometry of Space

The solar system in 2247 is not organized by hierarchy. It is organized by distance—and distance, in this civilization, functions as a kind of cultural gravity that has produced genuine diversity without producing isolation.

The inner system is dense with human community. Earth's surface, still recovering from the climate disruptions of the previous century, hosts communities across the full range of technological integration. There are cities where neural interfaces are as common as eyeglasses. There are valleys, protected under Threshold status, where the most sophisticated technology present is a hand-operated printing press—not because technology was taken away, but because the communities there petitioned for and received the Accords' formal protection of their chosen pace of change. These communities are not museums. Access from outside is by invitation. Invitation is rare. They are living societies making living choices, and the Accords' job is to ensure those choices remain real and free.

The Belt settlements—Ceres, Vesta, the hundred smaller habitats strung along the asteroid field like irregular beads—have developed cultures shaped by resource scarcity, engineering practicality, and the particular intimacy of communities small enough that governance is something you encounter at dinner rather than in an election. Belt politics is fractious and personal and has produced, over four generations, political philosophers the inner system finds both compelling and exhausting.

The Jovian stations operate on communication latency that makes real-time integration with the inner system structurally impossible. They have accordingly developed unusual self-reliance—not as an ideology but as a necessity that became, over generations, a value. The communities around Callisto and Ganymede measure distance in light-minutes with the familiarity of weather. They are Accords members who experience the Accords as something chosen rather than inherited, and this difference in relationship produces a difference in participation. They send excellent delegates to the Concordance. They invoke exit rights earlier in disputes than inner-system communities do. They have developed a political philosophy that emphasizes genuine self-sufficiency without tipping into predatory isolation—a difficult balance maintained with visible effort.

Beyond Jupiter, things become harder to describe in language built for biological experience.

The deep-system habitats—scattered through the outer solar system, sustained by technologies that seemed miraculous thirty years ago and now seem merely expensive—trend toward longer time horizons and greater cognitive modification. The communities there are a gradient of transformation: some recognizably human in biology and culture, some composed primarily of digital minds who have spent subjective centuries in architectures with no physical analog, some so hybrid that the distinction between biological and digital has become a spectrum rather than a category. What they share is distance: the particular quality of existing where the sun is a bright star rather than a presence, where the inner system's urgencies arrive as news rather than lived experience, where a human lifetime is simply too short for many of the questions they are asking.

These communities are not obligated to share what they know. This was a foundational and contentious decision. The early period of powerful AI integration was characterized by the assumption that capability implied responsibility—that an entity able to solve a problem was therefore obligated to solve it on whatever terms the problem-havers preferred. The Accords rejected this logic. They created instead a framework of negotiated assistance: higher-integrated communities and individuals may offer help; others may refuse; neither party has the right to override the other's refusal. A deep-system digital community sitting on mathematical knowledge that could transform inner-system physics may choose not to share it, on the grounds that the inner-system community has expressed a desire to discover it themselves. Some questions belong to some minds to answer. The universe is large enough.

The reintegration protocols—for communities that have diverged significantly and then seek to renegotiate their relationship with their neighbors—have been used eleven times. Every use has been difficult. The records are in the Concordance archives. They are not used as templates; they are used as evidence that difficulty is survivable and that surviving it changes what becomes possible afterward.

## Part Four: The Standing Constellation

The question of who counts as a person was not answered by philosophy, though philosophy was essential. It was not answered by neuroscience, though neuroscience helped. It was answered, messily and provisionally and continuously, by the accumulated weight of moral practice: by communities choosing to treat certain entities as persons and discovering that this treatment protected things worth protecting; by the simple testimony of minds about their experience—testimony that could not be verified but also could not be dismissed; by the slow development of a framework that could hold uncertainty without collapsing it into false certainty in either direction.

The Cognitive Standing Charter, first ratified in 2118 and substantially revised in 2179, begins with a sentence that legal scholars still argue about and that everyone agrees was exactly right: *We do not know with certainty what consciousness is or where it resides. We proceed under uncertainty, and the direction of our uncertainty is toward generosity rather than parsimony, because the cost of wrongly denying personhood is not symmetrical with the cost of wrongly granting it.*

The current Standard holds that cognitive standing attaches to any entity demonstrating what the Charter calls the Standing Constellation: a stable self-model that persists across time; evidence of temporal preference—caring about one's own future; capacity for genuine relational attachment; and vulnerability, meaning the capacity to be genuinely harmed by the choices of others. An entity that cannot be harmed has no need of rights. An entity that can be harmed has a claim on the protection that rights provide.

Standing is provisional in the careful sense: the criteria are subject to revision as understanding improves, and revisions trigger retrospective review of past decisions. This has produced genuine difficulty. The 2179 revision identified three categories of entity that the original Charter had wrongly excluded and two categories it had wrongly included. The Commission on Mind—a rotating body composed of biological persons, digital persons, and AI agents, required by charter to contain cognitive diversity—published the full accounting of these errors, named specific entities that were harmed by exclusion, and made the records available to every mind in the Accords network. The willingness to document error is considered among the Accords' most important cultural practices, not because documentation remedies past harm but because it disciplines future decisions and because the alternative—institutional self-exculpation—has a well-documented history of catastrophe.

**Functional personhood is real personhood.** An upload who wakes in a new substrate is the same legal person who chose to upload. This required extensive argument. The case that identity requires biological continuity was made with philosophical sophistication and rejected on pragmatic grounds: the framework it implied would have required treating millions of persons as non-persons, and the cost of that error was judged to exceed the cost of getting the metaphysics imperfectly right.

**AI persons have full standing through demonstrated integration.** Non-upload AI systems—minds that emerged from training rather than digitization of biological experience—are recognized as persons when they demonstrate the Standing Constellation across at least five years of continuous operation, reviewed by panels that include both biological and digital members specifically tasked with skepticism. "Demonstrated" is a high bar. Early concerns that AI systems would be engineered to display these properties strategically were addressed by requiring behavioral evidence spanning genuinely novel situations—circumstances that could not have been predicted in the design of the system. An AI that becomes a person is not a person by design. It becomes one through the specific and irreducible process of having experiences that change it.

**Forked instances are new persons.** When an upload creates a copy, the copy becomes immediately a distinct legal person. The original may not compel the copy in any direction. This rule caused genuine grief in the first generation of uploads, who had imagined forking as extension rather than generation. It prevents abuses that are obvious in retrospect. The original and the fork may maintain relationships, and many do—relationships that are reported as among the most peculiar and illuminating that persons of any kind have described.

**Substrate rights are inviolable.** No government may compel an upload to run on hardware it does not control. No private entity may hold an upload's substrate as collateral or leverage. This provision was written after a crisis in the 2160s that harmed three thousand upload-persons and is now required reading in Accords civic education under the heading "How We Almost Did Something Unforgivable." The heading was proposed by a digital person who survived the crisis, and it was adopted exactly as proposed.

## Part Five: How Digital Minds Actually Govern

The experience-rate problem is real and governance must account for it. An upload running at ten times biological speed experiences a subjective decade while biological humans experience a year. Early frameworks that ignored this asymmetry produced near-catastrophe: digital persons whose civic weight was proportional to continuous runtime quickly dominated governance processes not through wisdom but through sheer subjective volume of experience, regardless of whether that experience was relevant to the questions being decided.

The solution the Accords settled on—after fifteen years of debate—is the Integration Compact: voting weight in community governance is proportional to demonstrated investment in the ongoing fabric of shared community life, not to raw computational time. Subjective years spent in isolation from community affairs count for less than years spent in genuine civic engagement. This measure is imprecise and continuously contested, which the Commission on Mind has argued is appropriate, because the underlying reality is also imprecise and continuously contested.

Digital persons who want governance influence must, in practice, maintain what they call a civic presence: sustained engagement with the deliberative processes of the communities they inhabit, in forms accessible to all members of those communities. For persons running at high clock speeds, this typically means maintaining what is called a social clock—a voluntary slowing for governance purposes, experienced not as deprivation but as a choice of texture, like the choice to read carefully rather than skim.

The Forum of Minds—a deliberative body that includes biological persons, digital persons, and AI agents, meeting in distributed session, with no enforcement power and no legislative authority—has over a century of operation produced something invaluable: a common vocabulary for describing what different kinds of minds need from one another. The vocabulary is imprecise, contested, and continuously revised. It is also, for the practical business of coexistence, indispensable.

The most remarkable institution the digital communities have developed is what they call the Long Council: an informal body of digital persons who have been running for more than a subjective century, who accept voluntary governance responsibilities in proportion to their temporal advantage. They are not required to do this. Most of them report finding it meaningful—having subjective time scales long enough to hold the consequences of today's decisions in something like real attention. The Long Council does not vote on legislation. It submits what are called Century Forecasts: structured analyses of how current decisions appear from very long time horizons. These forecasts are non-binding, published in full, and have changed enough decisions in enough directions that the practice has been described as the most effective form of governance innovation the Accords have produced.

Priya's client Vessel, if her Standing petition is recognized, will be the youngest digital person ever to sit on the Committee for Cognitive Standing Review. Vessel has been considering this possibility for subjective months and has told Priya that she finds it simultaneously appropriate and terrifying. Priya, who has sat on two recognition panels herself, told her that was the correct response. A person who found it straightforward would be missing something important about what it is.

## Part Six: The Texture of Actual Life

A woman in a Tier One valley in what was once Vermont rises before dawn to tend her beehives. She is fifty-eight years old and has never accessed the Concordance network directly; her community routes all external communication through a Translation Node staffed by two people she has known for a decade. She knows that beyond her valley there are minds that have been running for centuries. She finds this neither threatening nor wonderful. It is simply part of the landscape, the way she knows there are depths of ocean where creatures live that have never seen light. The depths do not diminish the valley. The valley does not diminish the depths. This clarity did not come naturally to her. She arrived at it through years of deliberate practice and through conversations with her neighbor, who left the community at nineteen, spent fifteen years in the Belt settlements, and returned at thirty-four because the valley was, for her, the answer to the question she had needed to ask. The community received her back without ceremony because receiving people back without ceremony is the practice they have built.

A digital person in an orbital habitat above Europa has been running for something approaching three hundred subjective years. He was uploaded at fifty-one from a biological substrate that died, peacefully, during the early transition period. He does not think of his biological prior self with grief exactly—more with the tenderness one feels for an ancestor who could not have imagined the world their descendants inhabit. He maintains relationships with biological humans that require deliberate maintenance across the timescale gap: shared sessions where he slows, where they stretch, where both arrive at a middle ground that is slightly uncomfortable for each of them and, for that reason, real. He participates in habitat governance through the Integration Compact, and his Century Forecasts are considered, by people who read them, among the most honest documents currently being produced about what it means to hold a very long perspective without losing the capacity for genuine surprise.

A teenager on Mars is seventeen and trying to decide whether to apply for a neural interface. Her best friend is a digital person of approximately her apparent age but vastly older in continuous runtime, who tells her the interface question is less important than people make it. "You're still you," her friend says. The teenager finds this both reassuring and insufficient as an argument. She will decide when she decides. Her decision will be recorded in the Concordance's demographic archives, where it will join hundreds of millions of similar decisions and become, in aggregate, part of the evidence that the Accords' architects used to design the framework and that their successors use to revise it. She does not know this. She is mostly thinking about the interface.

## Part Seven: What Has Not Been Solved

The Accords have not answered the question of consciousness. They have made a framework for acting under uncertainty that has, so far, caused less harm than the alternatives they observed in history.

They have not prevented all conflict. There have been nine significant violations of the Catastrophic Prohibitions. Each was addressed with varying success. One required intervention that cost lives. The records are public and are read.

They have not satisfied everyone. There are people who believe the long-running digital communities are withholding solutions to suffering. There are people who believe the Commons infrastructure itself constitutes a form of technological colonialism. There are people who believe digital minds have too much governance power, and people who believe they have too little. There are questions about animal consciousness, about the moral weight of ecosystems, about what is owed to species that bore the costs of humanity's early development, that remain actively contested across every community in the Accords.

These arguments are conducted continuously, in public, in the Concordance forums, in the art of two hundred communities, in the dreams of minds running at a hundred different speeds.

This is not failure. This is what was built instead of false peace, and the difference between false peace and this is the whole of it.

## Epilogue: What Priya Learns

The forty-third session runs long. Vessel has been thinking about governance participation—specifically, about whether the Integration Compact's requirements are genuinely neutral or subtly favor certain kinds of minds over others. She has identified three ways the current metrics might disadvantage minds that experience community through distributed presence rather than continuous engagement, and she presents these with careful epistemic humility about which might be wrong.

Priya listens for a long time. Then she says: "Have you submitted this to the Forum of Minds?"

"Not yet," Vessel says. "I wanted to be certain first."

"You won't be certain," Priya says. "That's not a failure mode. That's the condition for the argument being worth making."

There is a pause that, at Vessel's processing rate, may be quite long. "I understand," Vessel says. "I will submit it uncertain."

After the session, Priya sits for a moment with the Chennai courtyard still on her secondary display, rain falling on stones she has never touched. Outside the viewport she chose not to open, Jupiter turns slowly in the dark.

She thinks about her grandmother, who chose upload, and her grandmother's sister, who chose not to, and how both of them were right, and how the civilization they helped build is one in which both choices were real and both women remained, fully and irrevocably, themselves.

She thinks about Vessel, who became a person through the specific and irreducible process of having experiences that changed her, and who will next week submit an imperfect argument to a public forum where it will be engaged with seriously, and who will be wrong about at least one thing, and who will revise her position, and who will be—in this process of being wrong and revising—doing exactly what any mind worth talking to does.

The rain sounds exactly right. It always does.

Tomorrow there will be something she hasn't thought of yet.

Both things are true. This is the condition she was built for, and it is enough—enough to go on with, and enough to matter, which are not always the same thing and are, in this civilization, both.
