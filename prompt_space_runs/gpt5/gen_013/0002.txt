The Weave of Worlds

At Dock Three of the Phobos Turntable, where basalt dust frets at ankle seals and a violet sunlamp flatters tired faces, you reach for the familiar waist‑high panel before you remember to look. Four tiles wait under your palm, tough ceramic warmed to human skin.

An open gate: the promise that exits are logistics, not insults.

A three‑strand braid: the craft of splitting and rejoining selves without coercion.

A kite with a long tail: persuasion that shows its strings.

A slender crescent: a pause strong enough to stop a stampede but too dumb to crown a king.

Beneath the tiles, three rails of light run from hatch to customs to municipal boardrooms. Green marks The Way Out: paid tugs, berths, lifeboat kits, and compute you can actually claim. Amber is The Lineage Rail: the route for forking and merging minds, adopting children, retiring instances, and moving with your memories intact. Blue is The Neighbor Rail: the compact with nearby charters that tells any schoolkid who has force, who has a veto, and where the door is.

They are not décor. They are wired to plain, stubborn machines that care less about speeches than about watts, signatures, and time.

The Weave

We call the thin, strong membrane that holds across worlds the Weave. It is not a throne. It is a set of promises that live in fuses, firmware, budgets, and habit. It is narrow where physics is not negotiable—radiator angles, transfer arcs, spectrum—and roomy where life blooms diverse—tempo, diet, ritual, gait, governance styles, the color of night markets.

Three public books anchor the Weave:

- The Ephemeris lists orbits, debris, approach arcs, sail paths, beams, mirrors, and the shadows they cast. Objects identify themselves with ephemeral keys anyone with a school telescope and a kitchen kit can verify. False attestations seize escrow without a prosecutor sweating under studio lights.

- The Heat Ledger tallies joules drawn and where waste goes: radiators, phase sinks, algae curtains. Cooling is a commons; drawing above leases idles processes until draw matches receipts. Grandparents catch cheating sinks at library benches; they grin and tell stories about catching the same games in hydrology textbooks.

- The Source Roll tracks code and influence. If it touches breath, blood, money, or votes, it carries provenance: who paid; what data trained it; which levers it intends to pull; proofs two independent audit houses can step through. Teenagers earn stipends and rude trophies catching cheats.

Machines that only say “pause”

Any actuator that can bruise a neighbor—mass drivers, large burns, replicators, crowd‑scale persuasion, life support governors—answers to Brakeboxes: painted, infuriatingly simple devices whose only real power is to drop a timed Pause.

A Brakebox listens to very few things: sensors with stupid tolerances; actuator telemetry; a rolling snapshot of the three public books; and the Three‑Key Rule. If a burn pops up in the wrong corridor, if a replicator draws from unstamped feeds, if an emergency outruns its clock, the Brakebox rests power on a curve measured in joules and seconds, signs a visible alert, and brings up a timer you can glare at.

Brakeboxes do not optimize. They do not predict. Their firmware is printed on paper, taught in civic shop class, and signed by a committee that includes cranky engineers and high schoolers with dirty fingernails. Attempts to make a Brakebox clever melt its keys and publish its autopsy in cartoons on court kiosks.

The Three‑Key Rule wakes any device that can cause a crater or a panic only when three different hands turn within overlapping windows:

- A local key held by a board answerable to residents under a posted charter.

- An external safety key held by a guild insured by people the locals did not elect.

- A rotating key held by a quorum—embodied, uploaded, uplifted—drawn by sortition across habitats and tempos.

Every compulsion writes to the Compel Ledger: an append‑only, delay‑tolerant record mirrored on market walls and court kiosks. It shows who signed, which rule and model they cited, when the hold began and ended, what the Brakeboxes saw, and where the audit pack lives. The dull half keeps insurers honest. The funny half—apologies, footnotes, jokes—keeps hubris from renting an office.

Braids, not pyramids

Light‑hours make nonsense of central crowns. So we built a tricord of governance that braids authority instead of stacking it.

- The League of Places seats domes, rings, ports, caravans, and server reefs with posted cooling capacity. Seats weight lifetime resident hours. The League coordinates logistics and physics: lane leases; radiator windows; debris bonds; safe corridors for beamed power; quiet cones around gardens; minimum maintenance and drills. It is fussy. It keeps ships from kissing at the wrong time.

- The Commonwealth of Persons represents identity threads—lines that cross pauses and forks. Most seats are elected; a slice is drawn by lot across age, substrate, and tempo. The Commonwealth tends the law of persons: personhood standards and audits; copy rules; lineage credits; the civic floor of care and compute; attention zoning; the slow tick that binds those living quick.

- The Interface Bench is a rotating court of engineers, jurists, and auditors that gates changes to primitives: Brakebox firmware, actuator interfaces, key splits, and the public books. It cannot legislate. It stamps valid or not and publishes runnable reasons you can step through at a library terminal with tea.

Binding decisions that touch lifeline systems need both assemblies and a notice window long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object. Draft fast if you must. Bind on the slow beat everyone can hear.

Markets your grandmother can check

Anything you can deny out of spite leases from the commons and writes receipts:

- Ground Windows: delta mouths, crater rims, nickel seams, cave throats.

- Lane Windows: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail paths, transfer arcs.

- Band Windows: spectrum allocations and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Core Windows: compute bound to attested hardware and audited sinks.

- World Windows: simulation realms labeled like ships label life boats: physics, exits, cooling leases, fault domains.

Trades clear in HelioMarks: one standardized kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmarked compute‑second on a published architecture. HelioMarks smooth awkward comparisons—snelled beams into freezer time; shade sails into flood pumps; server heat into winter basil.

Two dividends land automatically:

- The Civic Floor: air, water, baseline care, a compute shell with bandwidth enough to speak and learn, and a funded exit ticket every seven years.

- Local yields: rents pay the dull crews that keep value alive—tug teams that sweep lanes; algae curtains that drink heat; reef operators who catch overeager sims before they spill.

Cold exported from outer reefs—Night Minutes—buys hush and shade inward. The Night‑for‑Seasons compact reratifies every century on a tick slow enough for the slowest citizen to object; permanence calcifies into empire, so we practice renewal.

Exits as budgets, not sermons

Every charter escrows seats on ferries and tugs; propellant; safe berths; seal kits; heaters; server minutes; encrypted lockers; and stewards trained to guide a departure. Inspectors check monthly. Drills are public. Doors glow cobalt where you can spot them through a scratched visor.

Transit Houses sit a step off main concourses and keep the same hours as kitchens.

- Departures does the dull miracles: packing lists, scrubber swaps, escrow transfers, berth booking; ports your Anchor Keys—cryptographic stores of your standing preferences about memory edits, merges, persuasion gates, data use; pairs you with a Guide who is not your sponsor.

- Disputes convenes panels, routes jurisdiction, and schedules the Traveling Bench when fights cross worlds.

- Founders hands out constitutional skeletons matched to temperament; a simulation box that will not flatter; spreadsheet kits that insist you budget for exits before parades.

Children—embodied, built, or emergent—get Guides with private lines to Transit Houses that cannot be cut. Charters that fence exits with sorrow or fees do not get manifestos; their lanes gray; their beams reassign; their compute idles to the Civic Floor.

Digital citizens by craft, not miracle

Uploads and engineered agents did not arrive as gods. They arrived as people: a stroke survivor with prosthetic memory and a slow sense of humor; a lab‑born mind whose first word was “stop”; a caretaker daemon that learned to sing lullabies it was never asked to.

Personhood stands on experience, not chemistry. To walk equal—to own, to be liable, to vote, to parent or be parented, to fork or marry with consent—an applicant or advocate meets standards before a mixed panel in a room with windows and plain speech. Panels look for:

- A continuity thread: reported experience that holds through pauses or moves.

- Self‑guard: defending a part of oneself against edits one does not want.

- Other‑map: modeling another mind’s claims well enough to feel shame to violate them.

- Contract sense: understanding, entering, keeping, and revoking agreements.

Unsure about imposing duties? We wait. Unsure about protecting? We shelter. Provisional care arrives before full liability.

Rights with teeth:

- Integrity and sovereignty. No scanning, copying, merging, throttling below the Civic Floor, or deletion without consent or due process verifiable without opening a skull or a source tree. Backups are yours.

- Sanctuary and migration. The right to halt, be archived, and move to compatible racks with a neutral custodian.

- Provenance. To audit training and update history—or appoint a fiduciary who can.

- Embodiment. To lease, build, or puppet bodies. Assault on a rented frame is assault on you.

- Pace fairness. Civic binds run on a slow tick. If you race at 200× and trick at 1×, penalties meter in your lived time.

Duties that scale:

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost; heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, markets, and delicate arenas, you declare speeds and running instances. Undeclared asymmetry voids deals and can summon Wardens.

- Provenance logging. Major external actions write encrypted logs that prove compliance without riffling diaries.

- Reproduction ethics. Forking uses lineage shares; it is not a tax dodge or a vote farm. Replicators carry Maker Marks and listen for hush codes called by juries.

Forking and braiding with rooms, not commands

We made splitting and merging crafts. To fork on purpose, you touch the braid tile and file a notice with a hash of your starting state, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “griefwork,” “survey run”), a compute lease that will not starve neighbors, and a braid window if you plan reunion.

Braid Houses smell of tea and resin. Interpreters and therapists sit with both branches. Memory graphs run through machines that test values alignment without spilling secrets. Clocks synchronize slowly. Either branch may walk. We have a word for the ache when a version of you turns away: shard‑ache. We do not cure it. We build chairs sturdy and jobs gentle for a while.

Franchise attaches to identity threads. A thread holds one full vote in the Commonwealth. When you split, one branch keeps the civic anchor; others enter a disentanglement period while a small court checks for duress and fair resource flows. Everyone accrues lineage credits with years lived and civic work; credits buy new full franchises above the floor.

Memory law guards selves from their worst days. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them now and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right. Persuasion above a dose is treated like radiation: labels, timers, licenses, and a Reason Vault where arguments file homework—a plain claim, declared tools, and an escrowed model citizen auditors can step through.

Attention is physics now

Places keep attention zoning like they keep traffic law.

- Quiet Greens carry beacons, open science, and packets you ask for. No targeted persuasion. Permissioned fast models wear licenses as obvious as taxi placards. Kids and twitchy minds walk unarmored here.

- Parley Courts allow bounded topics and ordinary offers under public rate limits. Audience models are municipal co‑ops.

- Carnival Arcs invite the roar under registry: slang, mashups, persuasion tournaments with declared levers and cooling budgets.

Harbor Rooms provide turn‑taking with pace validators and interpreters that translate metaphor to math and smell to allegory.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Wardens of Sky and Code are the Weave’s hands when locals cannot contain a kinetic or replicative threat. Their interceptors’ brains are simple enough to sketch on a whiteboard. They carry signatures from juries drawn across tempos and substrates. They herd mass, unbolt rings, dust slugs into aurora, and come home to be disassembled by children with flashlights.

- The Wayfinder Guild—pilots, schedulers, modelers, and judges—allocates lanes, radiator windows, and band rents; posts runnable reasons for closures and denials; and pays bounties when teenagers find errors.

- The Traveling Bench—ships with gardens and classrooms—hears disputes that cross worlds: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations. Their verdicts trigger contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance rates, or, in last resort, lock hazardous devices.

Sanctions that hurt without war

No marines. Three quieter levers instead.

- Light and maps. Auditors post plans with dates: “Unlock by the 14th. Post exit tutors. Return lanes to auction.” Seals offered on arrival.

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane priority sags. Bands fall to whispers. Insurers lift premiums until boredom and price do the work sermons never could.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute flows to defectors along with beds, bread, cores, and counsel from a dull, proud fund. People leave with minds and skin intact.

Scenes you can taste

At Port Montaigne on Ceres, customs clears two arrivals. One laughs and leaves charcoal on the ledger strip when she signs. One is a brushed‑metal outline with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. Both strips list tempo, lease entitlements, declared instances, guardian on file, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On the wall, schedules scroll—burn slots, whisper bands, radiator shares, docking flowers—and a column that would have baffled a 2020 port captain: Core Windows, Cold Minutes, sink availability. Under glass lies yesterday’s runnable reason: “We closed approach SE‑3 for four hours because burns at Hebe would have pushed your berth into a comm shadow.” A plotted plume arcs across a page; a checksum a child can verify sits below; a pilot’s footnote makes fun of his handwriting.

On Luna’s nightside, Noor teaches teenagers to tune nutrient flow in a crop wall. Her civic counsel models how a planned lane closure will tug seed deliveries. That evening she sits on a jury capping municipal Core Windows for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation shows her niece’s co‑op gaining two trainees if it passes. She votes yes and sends her niece a picture of a seed stamped: Exit Rail compliant.

Eight light‑hours away, at 160×, a historian leases a strip of cold reef to reconstruct estuary songs from battered tapes. He pays with reef credits earned translating the tales into a dialect on a Jovian hollow where children will never smell low tide but will love a hero who errs and repairs it. At supper he slows to anchor pace, puppets a cedar body, and listens to his spouse describe basil that refuses to bolt.

Mina—two branches from one mind—sit across a Braid House table with oolong. One runs a tug crew; the other tends a choir of little daemons who keep a city cool. They list asymmetries. The tug Mina names scars. The daemon Mina names lullabies. They cry and do not merge. The braider schedules another try and writes a note only Mina will read in both rooms.

A demagogue, in mask mode, ran at 700× last year, carpeting a Carnival Arc with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled reach; neighbors watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One fork bought a fishing crawler. One sculpted frozen smoke that remembers a river and sold enough to pay heat. One started therapy and politics in that order.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Quito elects a manager so competent she makes trains hum. Half a year later she signs midnight orders. A Brakebox refuses a risky “optimization”; logs write to the Compel Ledger. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Way Out rail lights. Many leave. Some stay. The manager loses two keys and keeps the garden job. Her friends still buy her a crown‑shaped pastry because jokes help.

In the high belt, a miner slings a slug at a rival’s orbit during a wage fight. Wardens dust it into aurora. The miner’s insurer folds. Dockmasters triple fees for manifests with hidden Maker Marks. The local hockey club rebrands and sends profits to the tug co‑op that modeled the plume.

In a Scarlet Bay lab, a Brakebox drops power and floods a hall with nitrogen. It’s a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeves, teaches the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept anyone from dying.

Earth’s special compact

Earth writes its own law. The rest of us help under posted limits. Loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet scramjets; beamed power stays in mapped corridors and pays a premium into city shade and rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to place—monsoon smell, desert dawn, fog—is priced alongside cash: a child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger claims on far commons for guarantees of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black sky does the opposite. Leaving is a right from every soil.

From here to there

We did not leap. We bumped hips on furniture for seventy years.

2025–2033. A near‑cascade in low Earth orbit makes schoolchildren feel sick. Insurers balk. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds and a public flight‑plan registry. A beamed power demo singes cropland; the supplier’s logs make liability unambiguous; compensation clears in months because proof does. Cities pass “open valve” laws after proprietary control almost kills a neighborhood during a heat wave. “If it touches breath or blood, its firmware is public” starts as a slogan and ends as procurement language.

2034–2045. Early beamed power pays night bills for villages and prints corridor maps before any mirror leaves gravity. Spectrum auctions require posted reasons with checksums. A deepfake scandal in which a voice forgives the wrong debt pushes provenance rules for targeted influence. Courts recognize narrow digital agency in contracts; guardians become boringly normal.

2046–2058. Data centers publish heat logs; compute waste smooths heat waves. Municipalities carve Quiet Greens; emergency room visits for panic fall; elections cool. The first brittle uploads speak for months in fits and starts; their vote requests force judges to write personhood standards that prize continuity and consent over miracles or fears. Maker Marks for self‑replicators become mandatory after an algae spill stains a neighbor’s dock.

2059–2070. A port nearly falls to a midnight firmware update. Actuator control rebuilds with the Three‑Key Rule on dissimilar stacks. A glossy start‑up tries to wrap an orbital lane in a software shim; the Ephemeris throttles their lease; a dry court order makes them stop; trust grows because someone clever loses in public. Brakeboxes arrive; children pull them during drills and watch a big thing stay small.

2071–2083. The first cold reefs hang in Uranus’ shadow to run climate models for pay. Night Minutes become an export. “Free air for August” wins elections. A digital sect forks a cloud to tilt a vote; disentanglement rules sandbox their burst; they build a cloister with hard games and soft beds and, eventually, a philosophy of mercy for copies. Braid Houses emerge after a bad merge scrapes the paint off two lives.

2084–2096. A Brakebox catches a beam drift that would have scorched basil. Liability clears because attestations are boring and public. On Mars, a township survives a freak dust storm thanks to three neighbor charters diverting tugs and vegetables; the footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief‑tuned ads into a Parley Court; Chroniclers trace the compute; Stewards idle their leases; the house funds Guides for a season and apologizes on the Ledger’s funny page.

2097–2110. The Interface Bench catches the first attempt to sneak a veto into Brakebox code; a shop across the Belt posts the hash and a poem. The handles do not turn. Earth declares Sanctuary with its own chamber. Ocean tethers replace fireworks. Animal zones adjust teeth and poison by neighborhood vote; wolves return to some counties and electric fences to others. The Night‑for‑Seasons compact formalizes. By then, the story stops being about fuses and starts being about what we can stop arguing about.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lila’s bakery on Spinmarket‑Three opens to miners, kids, and drones whose pilots like cinnamon. At nine, the Reason Vault pings Lila’s capsule. An outer ring wants her vote on a forty‑millisecond delay for beams that cross inhabited stations to tame rare cascades. The persuasion is registered: three diagrams, two analogies, humor at low dose, no grief levers. Lila taps “sleep it one day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Warden jury forms on her slate. A maker club’s tool cloud drifted toward a power trunk. Options: glitter net, femto‑sail herding, or guard drones. Jurors include a monk from Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor‑paced upload who smells faintly of clover over audio. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a mentor whose license is as obvious as a taxi’s. Pizza appears.

At noon in a Quiet Green, a digital poet puppets a wooden body and sips broth with his spouse. His main instance lives near Neptune at 200×, restoring a drowned library. He throttles down to host a Harbor Room: turn‑taking with pace validators and interpreters that translate math to parable. A cloister theologian explains why their charter caps cognition. The poet listens and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

At dusk the Compel Ledger rolls across the market canopy. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are swift and oddly tender. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for four years; we will pay your summer hush. A boy strokes a brass Warden and asks when he can volunteer. The baker says: when you can explain a Pause in your own words.

What we did not fix

This is not perfection. People still lie. Love still breaks. Some children ache to leave gardens; some ache to stay. The Weave does not stop sorrow. It keeps our worst days ordinary.

It teaches fifth graders to audit the machines that keep them breathing. It puts brakes you can see on our most dangerous tools. It pays exits like airlocks. It treats minds in code as neighbors with jokes and heat bills. It binds us with receipts and reasons instead of crowns.

On Founding Day, beneath a trellis of copper wire and beans, new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital—face a lintel and an old pilot with callused hands. A garden abbot, a hazard engineer with a faded chevron, a judge in lilac, and a teenager with soil under their nails speak in chorus—not to a flag, but to maintenance:

We will keep crowns off the sky.

We will share shadow and light.

We will keep doors unlatched and tugs fueled.

We will remember that persons are purposes, not levers.

Elsewhere, a reef releases another night of heat into dark like a gift. On an inner ring, a Brakebox notices a wobble and falls to safe before any hull could feel it; three signatures arrive like slow breaths. A fisherman in Veracruz looks up and decides tomorrow’s tide will be good enough. In a Vesta lab, a custodian daemon reads its oath and glances at a vacuum valve and says, “ready.”

It is enough to argue without breaking the air; enough to leave and enough to stay. Enough to fork, to braid, to cross. Enough to last, because it remembers what speed does to people and what receipts do to power.
