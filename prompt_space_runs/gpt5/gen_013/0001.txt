The Beacon Covenant

Step off the shuttle into the east lock of Juniper Pier two hours after scrub and you’ll see three thumb-sized lamps set into the bulkhead at shoulder height. They’re not decoration. They are wired—literally, with cable you can trace—to valves, budgets, control planes, and places you can sleep.

- The Waylight is a steady green. When it’s on, at least one exit path for you is funded, lawful, and warm: cargo space booked, tug hours reserved, berths and food lined up, heater credit escrowed where your current steward can’t cancel it, and compute minutes you can carry without begging. Put your hand near it and a strip display shows you the route, the last inspectors to walk it, their grades, what the receiving station will hand you on arrival, and who you can call if the socks aren’t where the map says.

- The Kinlight glows amber. Touch it and the schedule opens for mind work: slots to copy yourself, to merge branches, to adopt a shard, to pause a thread for a season, or to retire with honors; the mentors on duty; the cooling and cycles your lineage has in its purse; the rituals the locals use when two lives decide to braid back. It also shows your lineage account, down to joules and minutes, so you can add a child without borrowing from a neighbor’s air.

- The Charterlight is a patient blue. It pulses only if the home charter is short enough for a ninth-grader and exacting enough for a machine. Let your fingers rest beneath it and it reads you the parts you asked about—the eviction clause, the quarantine rules, why the quiet room near the school has a timer. It will not lie. If the words change, it blinks until someone explains why.

These three lamps are the visible grips of the Beacon Covenant: the web of receipts, audits, auction clocks, slow law, and hard fuses that makes neighbors possible across planets, stations, and server reefs—without sanding them into sameness or leaving the strongest to rule by speed. It isn’t a crown. It’s a wiring diagram, a dull budget, a habit you can test with a kitchen scale, and a promise to light up exits even when everyone is tired.

Safeties that only say stop

Anything with a radius that could hurt a stranger listens for more than one hand. Thrusters, beamers, mass drivers, mass printers that can turn sand into wolf teeth, big pumps, and code foundries that can rewrite a clinic all obey Anchor Keys: three independent approvals that must arrive within a brief shared window. One key belongs to a board picked by local residents who can fire it. One sits with a bonded safety guild paid by people who do not vote in the local assembly and whose hardware runs elsewhere. The third is drawn by lot—a rolling jury mixed across humans, digitals, and blends, paced so that rushing grants no advantage.

No Anchor Key can wake a dangerous actuator alone. When the three align, the system prints a stub to the hall wall and to a public ledger before it moves: class, max output, intent, window. Schoolkids are taught to read these stubs. A spell that moves mass without a stub is a crime with strict penalties that don’t wait for speeches.

Underneath all of it is the Citrine Brake: a narrow interface you can buy at a hardware stall and test on a bench. It isn’t a brain. It is a fuse that idles a device for minutes when three simple checks fail: wrong window, wrong key, missing plan. It can’t choose a new course or assign a budget. Its holds expire automatically by design. The brake runs everywhere in triple-diverse implementations: a cheap chip with solder pads you can see through when you hold it to the sun; a municipal photonic board; a hardened simulation you can compile from source. If any two disagree, living room walls blink with an error. The signing keys that bless new firmware are split across far hands and different polities—Earth courts, belt unions, cold server monasteries, habitat collectives—so no quorum fits in one room or one coup.

When anyone compels in public—a door held shut, a beam diverted, a process throttled—a line drops into the Causebook: an append-only diary mirrored from pier kiosks to server reefs. It lists who signed, which rule and model they cited, when the hold began and ended, what the brake saw, and the audit packet hash. Kids make comics about Causebook entries. That is not a joke. Humiliation has saved more lives than force.

Ledgers you can argue with and verify

A civilization spread across light-seconds needed books that would not take anyone’s word for physics. We keep three, and you can check each with a school telescope or a kitchen meter.

- The Open Almanac tracks rivalrous space—approach arcs, sail windows, shadow cones, spectrum bands, radiator minutes, and compute tied to sinks you could throw a snowball at. Schedules are posted with checksums you can confirm by observation. Auctions clear order with a math you can redo on a slate. Every lease expires on a clock that ticks where bodies live. “Forever” isn’t on offer.

- The Thermal Roll refuses to let quiet power vanish into glamour. It ties joules pulled to where the waste heat went. Cooling is a commons. If you declare three instances and pull like three thousand, your cores idle until your draw matches your leases. Grandparents take a small, stern joy in catching a misdeclared radiator and watching the rent tick up until the numbers match.

- The Provenance Index labels persuasion and code that touches safety. If you aim to sway law or budgets at scale, you carry who paid, what data trained you, and which levers you plan to press. If you underwrite safety with software, you carry where it was built and who read it with their eyes. Teenagers earn stipends for catching cheats. The Index has made some very clever people cry in public and then improve.

What can be sat on is leased, not sold

We lease what stubbornness could squat on: illuminated crater rims, shade bands, orbits and approaches, spectrum, radiator minutes, compute tied to hard fins, and simulated “lands” with declared exits and guardians. Five classes of leases run under posted clocks and public math:

- Terrains: deltas, crater rims, caves with steady temps, seams of nickel ice.

- Lanes: slots in orbit, approach vectors, sail corridors, transfer gates.

- Bands: radio spectrum, whisper channels in tunnels, loud cones for festivals and storms.

- Radiators: dissipation capacity tied to fins you can point at and coolants you can audit.

- Cores: compute bound to certified hardware and sinks.

Two shares follow every lease. A local share pays people who keep value alive: tug crews, scrubbers, algae walls, monitor kids. A universal share fills the dignity floor: calories, clean air, baseline bandwidth, personal counsel, and a grant of core minutes so the poorest mind can keep a diary no one can erase—not even the host that holds it. Accounts settle in Lux-Cores: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on a published open architecture at 4 K. The unit demurs slowly; hoarding Lux-Cores feels gross, like letting bread rot under a tarp while your neighbor stares at an empty plate.

Governing as a braid

Distance thins command. Culture refuses to be poured into a mold. We do not pretend otherwise.

- The Chamber of Havens seats places that keep hulls patched and corridors scheduled—planets, ports, caravans, and server reefs that own their radiators. Seats scale to resident-hours. Its work: debris bonds, maintenance standards, safe beaming windows, quiet cones, and the dull ecstasy of safe schedules.

- The People’s Assembly seats persons listed in the Continuity Ledger—humans, octopuses who asked for lagoons, uploads, blends. Most seats are elected; a portion is drawn by lot with quotas for age, substrate, and tempo. It tends the law of persons: personhood standards, copy and merge rules, attention zoning, the Civic Baseline, and the pace at which fast rooms bind slow lives.

- A small Protocol Bench—a rotating panel of engineers and jurists—guards the touch points between code and steel. It approves changes to brake interfaces, key structures, and other narrow protocols that can move metal, air, or blood. It doesn’t make policy. It prints valid/not valid with runnable reasons you can test.

- Above both sits the Balance Panel to negotiate what physics won’t let go totally local: how many cold minutes buy a week of quiet sky in summer; when to throttle memetic spillover across jurisdictions; how to handle rocks that bloom with fossils. Half the seats come from Havens; half from Persons; nothing binds if either half judges that a culture is being bulldozed below the personhood floor.

Any rule that touches the Plain Layer—the clockwork that keeps time for votes and scrams reactors—requires both houses and a notice period long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object. Drafts can sprint. Binding crawls.

Justice that respects latency

- Corridor law first. If you plan to put a mass into orbit or a beam through air, you file an end-of-life script and escrow the means to carry it out. If your plume risks my radiator next week, you post a bond today. Liability follows the sky, not your story.

- Physics juries. Remedy follows light. If your dust will graze my ring in six days, pay now. Juries gather wherever they can catch the relevant photons. They use your models and their eyes.

- Harbor Code. Every object aloft carries a signed end-of-life plan modeled to an envelope anyone can check. Claims that don’t match reality die in court with math.

- Emergency fuses. Storms and spills happen. Emergency charters widen certain powers within posted windows and then die by themselves. The Citrine Brake can accelerate coordination for ninety days, after which any two beacons can challenge with a better plan. Holds without reasons earn fines, and the fines get funny on the Causebook’s back page until the habit leaves.

- Triple signatures for catastrophe. Any move that could erase a corridor, starve a ward, or coerce a mind needs signatures from a residents’ assembly, a bonded civic agent, and a remote auditor on independent racks. The approval windows overlap enough to be useful and then wink out.

- The Pale Net. The only cross-system “force” is uncrewed and dull. Interceptors and glitter nets whose brains are simple enough to draw on a whiteboard nudge tumbling slugs into powder and dying hulls into safe cones. Each move needs threshold signatures from a jury you can message. Every burn writes to the Causebook with proofs and plain language so a teenager can mock you if the math is wrong.

Exit as logistics, not romance

Every charter pays into a boring, audited Leaving Fund: propellant reserves, contracted tug hours, berths, gasket spools, seed kits, heater and food credit, legal counsel, and off-site compute ready to pick up your thread. Digital residents have mirrors on dull racks beyond local jurisdiction and enough throughput to move without a host’s blessing.

Embassy Bays glow saffron in every concourse. Three counters wait: Departures, Disputes, Founders. The chairs suit elders, cephalopods, and drone pilots who blush around people. Children—embodied and built—have Tutors with private lines to Embassies that nobody can cut. Charters that hide exits or run coercive code in civic spaces see their lanes gray, radiator auctions cool, and core windows idle. We do not send fleets. We tighten protocols. Boredom and price do the rest.

Digital citizens as neighbors, not mascots or monarchs

Standing in the Continuity Ledger begins tentative and hardens with time. Mixed panels—psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to spot simulacra—ask five questions in plain language.

- Do you report your own experience across restarts and context shifts?

- Can you form plans, recognize others’ plans, and respect their “no”?

- Do your reasons generalize beyond a narrow training loop?

- Are there signs of joy and suffering in you that matter morally?

- Is this more than a clever mirror chasing rewards without an inner life?

Doubt about protection errs generous. Doubt about duties waits. Rights with teeth:

- Cognitive integrity. No deletion, throttling below the civic floor, forking, merging, or deep edits without consent or due process that writes to your entry. Unauthorized erasure is homicide; unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault.

- Sanctuary and migration. You can pause and be archived safely; you can move to compatible racks; you can summon a neutral custodian when you and your host disagree.

- Provenance. You can audit—or appoint a fiduciary to audit—the data and updates that formed you, with zero-knowledge proofs for sensitive parts.

- Association and departure. Join, fork, merge, leave, and refuse communities by their posted charters.

- Tempo fairness. Live at your chosen speed. Public binds ratify at human tempo unless a Slowroom is posted with validators. If a fast mind tricks a slow one, remedies bite in both subjective lives.

- Embodiment. Assault on rented limbs or senses is assault. Hobbles hidden in hired bodies are treated as poison.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one compels you onto racks with hidden throttles or deletion hooks. If you are hosted by another, you can demand heartbeat beacons to external guardians.

Duties that scale with impact:

- Civic anchors. In elections and regulated markets, you appoint an Anchor instance—your single franchise on public matters. Private cloning is not a way to multiply votes.

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost. Heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, tenders, and fragile spaces, you declare your number of instances and speed. Undisclosed asymmetry voids deals and votes; repeat offenses idle your core leases.

- Persuasion provenance. Above posted doses, influence models carry sources and levers in labels you cannot peel off. Unlabeled cognitive leverage is treated like dumping bleach in a town well: strict-liability fines that grow until you stop.

- Reproduction ethics. Forks draw from a lineage purse that accounts for heat, qubits, attention, and space. Children inherit baseline dignity and a decaying dividend from their progenitor. Replicators carry Maker Marks that answer hush codes when juries call them. Factories of joyless copies find their auctions go to whisper.

Lineages and Weave Houses

Public identity attaches to a mindline: continuity across pauses and forks. One civic franchise sits with the mindline; additional franchises accrue with age and work.

- Forking declares a purpose: griefwork, parallel design, a survey run. You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, and appoint temporary guardians if duty-bearing branches will emerge. One branch retains the vote; others enter a disentanglement period while a panel checks for duress and fair flows.

- Merging is practiced, not performed. Weave Houses are half tea shop, half server bay. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through interpreters that test values without spilling diaries. Either self can veto. Unbraiding is possible until the last stage. The ache when a version of you walks away has a name: branch-ache. We don’t cure it. We make chairs sturdy and work gentle for a while.

- Memory law guards the self from its worst hours. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them and from a snapshot signed before the desire took root. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your own life is a right and a sorrow; reviving you without your past will not be sold as kindness.

- Juvenile agents—kids, training shards, near-persons—get Tutors who are boring on purpose: patient teachers with courage for boundaries and the habit of writing reasons you can check.

Attention is physics; we map it

Influence can feed or burn. We zone it like noise.

- Gardens: topic feeds and notices; no targeted campaigns; fast models only with municipal licenses and visible timers. Children, elders, anxious minds walk here.

- Forums: bounded debate with rate limits and logs. Audience models run as co-ops trained on audit-able data.

- Bazaars: the roar on purpose. Above doses, labels and clocks required. Black box engines that refuse provenance stop at the door.

Doorways carry a sigil—a hand over a leaf—and the line: ask before you press. The Reason Shelf in every polity keeps runnable arguments for any persuasion seeking to move budgets or law. Teenagers get stipends and gossip for catching cheats.

Spaces between suns

Earth kept its own law. The rest of us pay for her weather and biodiversity because we share the sky. Loud launches moved to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers. Beamed power follows corridors with shade bonds. Debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to place is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care. A child in Arizona who aches for black sky can swap the other way. Leaving remains a right from every soil.

Far out, cold reefs exhale “cold minutes”—compute without suffering—for hot city nights. When Recife cooks, its libraries buy minutes from shadows beyond Neptune. In trade, inner worlds send nitrogen, seeds, and music. Peace is counted in receipts, not anthems. We reratify this every century at a tick slow enough that the slowest citizen can object. Permanence calcifies into empire. Renewal makes habits honest.

Walking here from now

We built this like bridgewrights: bolt by bolt, after falling in the river at least twice.

2025–2032. A near-cascade in low Earth orbit made kids feel sick in any city with clear nights. Insurers held their breath; launch providers ate humble pie. Premiums required deorbit plans posted where radio amateurs could read them. Clubs with backyard scopes earned bounties by catching lies. In Rajasthan, a beamed power demo scorched barley. Because the amplifier shipped with open cone proofs, liability was clear and payment arrived before fury fermented into violence.

2032–2039. Scandals over black box models denying medicine and freedom pushed cities to require provenance and explanations for any system that could bind a person. Narrow personal counsel agents—boring AIs with one client who could say “no” without gaslighting—arrived in clinics and ports. Receipts stopped being PR and became habit.

2039–2046. Data centers published waste heat alongside carbon. Cold-lake rigs taught us to count cooling as currency. Cislunar shade farms sold compute to summer nights. Ports posted the first Open Almanacs: approach slots with checksums you could check with a kitchen laser.

2046–2054. The first uploads were uneven and brave. Courts recognized continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—hardened doctrine. Compute floors joined calories and clean air as rights. We learned to put price and audit trails on cooling the sky.

2054–2061. Infrastructure “optimizations” gone wrong taught us to split compulsion three ways wherever it could coerce: arrest from prosecution from confinement; input from output from audit. Actuators learned to listen to three hands. Hurry lost its fangs.

2061–2069. The Citrine Brake arrived as compromise: a brake at machine speed, with timers and neighbors. It idles but cannot point; it speaks reasons and time-to-live; it runs on chips you can hold to a lamp and see through. Premiums fell. Safety drills became picnics with ugly mascots and good soup.

2069–2077. Lane, band, and radiator auctions moved from spreadsheets to registries schoolkids could verify. Land value taxes morphed into lease flows that paid for air scrubbers and algae walls. The Chamber of Havens and the People’s Assembly formed slowly, with retirements and gossip.

2077–2086. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point. The Pale Net turned it to glitter before anyone wrote a condolence letter. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without maker marks. A hockey club renamed itself after tug crews who modeled plumes.

2086–2098. A cloud of clones tried to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttled the lunge. A mayor fenced an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes gray, cores chilled, and the move happened anyway. He lost, learned, and returned to running a school. Doorways to Gardens, Forums, and Bazaars gained small brass sigils. The Causebook’s comics page grew fangs.

What it looks like at human scale

In Vesta’s yard, a co-op wants to test a nozzle that would graze a neighbor’s fins. The Open Almanac predicts brittle metal in a week. They accept a later slot when the fins are turned. The Protocol Bench stamps the model. The yard posts its code under a license you can study. The scary story becomes a lesson with a melted sample that kids are allowed to touch.

On a ring near Mars, a festival clause tries to demand digital residents remain online for a communal rite. The off-site cores hum anyway; people leave. The ring’s seed bank asks a cold reef near Triton to model a fungus in barley. Price: repeal the clause. The vote happens in a hall that smells like tea and solder. The repeal is quiet relief.

Above Quito, a charismatic manager quietly gathers water contracts, tug time, and schedules because she makes trains hum and tells good jokes. Winter later she signs a rush order. The safety guild refuses. The Citrine Brake idles the pump. The Causebook blinks to every wall. Jurors assembled by lot listen with care. She yields two levers and returns to cranes. On Founding Day the children bake a crown-shaped pastry and give it to her while everyone laughs because humor smooths pride without erasing lessons.

A digital congregation spins five hundred clones the week before a vote. The Continuity Ledger flags the surge. Hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads for public matters without burning private work. A year later, three branches petition for distinct standing. Two are granted after a day with a mentor who hates sophistry. One is told to study longer. No racks overheat.

A nurse on Lagos Nightmarket Ring flips through civic notices before bed. “No holds within three berths. Heat levy: 0.11 Lux-Cores. Jury summons: two hours at your pace about juvenile reef processes off Mauritius and whether they earn child credits.” She accepts, then sends her mother a voice note about radiator math.

A cold reef above Neptune slows to plant beans with rented hands. It speeds to 300× to restore flood-muddied archives while grandmothers correct colors without mercy. Payment is in cold minutes and a contract to roof a school in Veracruz.

In a Hazard Lab called Saffron Kiln, researchers test a self-replicating polymer under glass. Twice a year they pull the Anchor Keys with dummy scripts and accept embarrassment when alarms ring. On open day, a child asks if the brake would hold if her uncle stood inside. The engineer, who smells like bleach and jasmine, answers softly: “Yes. That’s why we write letters.”

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder above Valparaíso elects a director who gathers water, law, and defense because she is generous and makes trains hum. Months later she signs midnight orders. The brake refuses an overlong “emergency.” Logs write to walls. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Waylight turns green. Many leave. Some stay. She yields two keys and keeps one with humor. Her friends still bring food because neighbors are not enemies and boundaries are love with teeth.

In the belt, a maker club’s printer cloud drifts toward a power trunk. A jury on Lina’s bakery slate—an Isidis monk, a masked reef landlord, two Recife students, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when he speaks through a rented puppet—chooses dusting over drones. The teenagers are mortified. Pizza arrives. The insurer pays.

On Cydonia’s rim, a charter votes to deny uploads personhood. Nobody ships sermons. Exits are stocked. Digital citizens leave carrying their cold leases. A flood year comes. Pumps fail. An anchor-paced upload coordinates three cantons through a dust storm at human tick with astonishing clarity. The vote reverses in clauses. The mentor from that year still visits schools.

Attention stumbles, for once wisely. A demagogue in mask mode runs at 700× and carpets a Midway with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttle the spill. Friends watch persuasion scores decay like frost after sun. One fork buys a fishing crawler. One sculpts frozen smoke that remembers a river and sells enough to pay heat. One starts therapy and politics in that order.

Keeping kings dull

Three crafts keep power ordinary.

- Build with delay. Authority is local. Cross-border moves need three keys and march on posted clocks. Emergency declarations time out unless renewed with reasons you can verify. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show your work. All compulsion requires signatures picked by public randomness from people who do not sleep together. Budgets expire unless renewed in the open. Federations post Coercion Ledgers. Choirs read them aloud—sometimes for satire, sometimes for sorrow.

- Fund exits like bridges. Every habitat sets aside enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, core minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t try to persuade you to go; we cut checks so that you can.

When a charter welds doors, runs law you cannot read, or salts children’s minds so leaving tastes like poison, neighbors do not send soldiers. They send maps and boredom.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence and repair maps: unbolt by the 14th, hire exit stewards, return lanes to auction. Here are the seals we’ll honor upon arrival.

- Protocol throttles. Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions cool. Lane slots ahead open for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel funded by a dull, proud purse.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute and heaters flow to defectors. The tyrant breathes stale air until habit fails. Neighbors sheltering defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

The taste of it

You are watched, but not by a throne. You are watched by neighbors and logs you wrote together. You learn to switch tempos: the snap of a brake under your palm when a relay goes dumb; the slow, breathing pace of juries assembling across orbits; the glacial patience of a river town on Earth deciding which boats to permit. You argue at the Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you visit the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

Your roof might be basil trembling under a curve of glass or black fins tilted to star-cold. Either way, somewhere near your door three small lamps wait and hum.

Waylight. Kinlight. Charterlight.

Joining day

On Joining Day in a hundred places, new citizens—cellular, silicon, saltwater—rest their hands on railings. A quiet abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read aloud:

We split keys. We publish maps. We keep the hatch funded. A mind is never merely a means.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Freehands Cooperative,” your kettle says. “Seeding a wheel near Vesta. Pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit budget posted. Consent to hear more?”

You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom. The Citrine Brake hums, bored and content. You live among neighbors made of cells and code, under glass and math and humility. Decisions move slow enough for bodies. Receipts stick. Exits exist even on bad days.

It isn’t perfection. It’s enough: precise bolts you can name, rituals you can practice, jokes you can share, and guardrails that keep power from leaping the lane. A civilization that travels between suns and does not forget how to be kind.
