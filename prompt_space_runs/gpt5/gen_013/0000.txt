The Tidal Accord

The ferry from the mid‑Belt drifts into Easting Hub on a soft growl of pumps and corrective puffs. Beyond the observation panes, tugs skate and clasp, and the long greenhouse along the inner rim lets loose wet mint over the smell of heated bearings. Above the lock, three inlays shine in practical brass and ceramic: a triple clevis, a stitched arc of orbits, a hatch with a square of light on the deck.

Everyone knows what they mean, even the children who still press their faces to the glass.

- The clevis: no single hand can move a lever that throws shadow or flame.

- The stitched arc: lanes in space and signal are public, predictable, and reasoned.

- The lit hatch: departure is honored and helped, not stalled by sermons.

They are not slogans. They are fixtures: devices, duties, software, and receipts. Together they form the Tidal Accord—a coordination layer that lets cities, stations, and server‑groves thrive in many ways at once, and gives digital neighbors full standing without letting speed trample breath.

Anchors and pauses you can smash with a glove

Wherever a mistake could freeze a town or blind a telescope—a mass driver, a beam mirror, a life‑support brain, a seeded replicator in a dock—sits a Tri‑Lock Safety. It hides no clever code. It rests on three keys, each held by a different jurisdiction and different hardware:

- A local steward whom residents can dismiss.

- A remote safety cooperative accredited elsewhere and forbidden from sitting near the first.

- A panel drawn by lot—a rotation of citizens across substrates who serve once and never again.

Only with all three can you aim a beam above safe brightness or push mass unscheduled. The interface you can touch is narrow and auditable. The safety software that gates it is published by the Anchor Bench, a thin college of engineers and jurists who do not write policy and have no budget for epics. They accept or reject proposed changes; nothing more. Their “no” can be overridden only by supermajorities in the two houses of law, followed by a cool‑down long enough for slow minds far from the Sun to weigh in.

Overlaid on all this hums the Hold Net. It is not a governor with opinions. It has one power: pause. Any citizen with a civic key can, in good faith, push a big yellow handle—everywhere they exist they are waist‑high, ugly, and friendly to shaky hands—and a driver or a beam socket or a replicate bay will glide to its preapproved safe state for six minutes. To extend the hold, the same three diverse jurisdictions that own the Tri‑Lock must concur. The Hold Net runs on three different stacks—hardened silicon, municipal photonics, and a certified simulation with external device attestations—and any pause order must agree across all three. Keys expire quickly. Logs become public by default after brief redaction windows. Anyone can test a Hold node; if it fails to report, beacons blink until someone asks why. You cannot build a career on “heroic holds” without receipts.

The sky you can check with a school telescope

You do not have to trust a director’s slate to know whether a port is keeping to its promises. The Commons Ephemeris, a delay‑tolerant ledger pinned to the physics of light and momentum, is public. It lists which bands are quiet, when beamed power will cross, who is renting radiator windows, where parking claims sit, and when they expire. It records who owes debris duty and who has paid it. School clubs in Lagos and Cebu and Quito verify burns with backyard optics and split bounties for proof. Beams and mirrors publish quiet receipts—cryptographic attestations bound to audited hardware—that you can check on a kitchen verifier without magic words. If you cut a fruit grower’s dawn cone, the bond you preposted pays this season’s loss and a cooling penalty next season. If you “claim” a rock and miss a milestone, stewardship returns to the pool; you do not just lose marketing rights. If you lie about heat, radiator auctions pinch until they make truth less painful than pretense.

Doors that actually open

Every chartered place—and every server community that invites the public—keeps an Open Lodge by street and by frequency. There are three desks whose names never change: Departures & Care; Dispute; Starter Kits. There are bunks, tea, and legal terminals with large type and polite counsel agents you can shut off. Behind a panel sits the Blue Corridor for digital citizens: cycles rented in advance on hardware nobody local can seize, escorted by watchdog protocols anyone can ping. Each polity escrows tug time, propellant, berths, cryo pods, and server credits sufficient to move at least a tenth of residents within a season.

Lock your doors with sermons or fees, and physics pinches. Lanes thin. Radiator windows dim. Rescue obligations stiffen. The Hold Net can freeze dangerous tools until the latches lift. That has happened three times in fifty years. Twice, shame and cold budgets broke obstinacy. Once, a mayor took a shuttle and later taught civic drills in a quieter ring.

Two rhythms, one covenant

Close to big heat and easy logistics live the Hearth Bands: Earth’s protected deltas and hills, lunar tunnels with copper bells, Martian valleys where orchards soften dust, cylinders that smell like cut pine and bread. Here, embodied pace rules by default. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route or a hush cone.

Farther out hum the Shade Reefs: compute barges in Uranus’s dark, Trojans where data swims like whales, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts and latency count, and many citizens run mostly in code.

Between them runs the Umbra‑Sol Covenant. Hearth places enforce quiet corridors above communities that need them—observatories, farms, sleep districts—and keep persuasion civil in public squares with provenance and rate limits. Reefs sell coolth inward: cold minutes that keep cities alive through heat spikes, verification services for law, resilient audit tools you can run on a public terminal, and art that can be heard at human pace. The exchange rate—two winters of shade out by Neptune for a month of urban hush over Recife—is a line in the Commons Ephemeris an aunt can check on her way to work.

Governance without a capital

You cannot force one chamber to speak for all hulls and all kinds of mind. The Tidal Accord splits power gently.

- The Assembly of Worlds seats planets, habitats, barges, and villages under rock—anywhere with a hull to seal, air to scrub, or docks to schedule. Seats grow with lifetime residents, not with radius. The Assembly standardizes docking, rescue, sanitation, radiator auctions, and charter recognition. It accredits safety cooperatives that hold the second Tri‑Lock key in a given region.

- The Continuity Ledger is a registry of persons—embodied, digital, blended—anchored by Continuity Rings: cryptographic chains that tie a continuing subject to attestations and social cosigners without revealing interior life. The Ledger defines the personhood floor, funds the Common Hearth (air, water, baseline food, basic care, local transit, bandwidth, and a humane floor of compute), and runs the Civic Tempo, the slow beat for elections, budgets, and trials.

- The Anchor Bench, already mentioned, accepts or rejects proposed changes to safety stacks and cryptography. Its decisions are binary. Members serve a single, long duty and then cannot touch safety work for a decade.

Either of the first two bodies can nullify the other when the personhood floor is threatened or when culture is to be steamrolled above that floor. Urgent moves—reactor scrams, beam clamps—follow pre‑published contingencies that the Hold Net can prove. The Civic Tempo gives municipal matters three Earth days per round, systemwide votes weeks, and charter changes seasons. Minds that run fast can sprint privately as they like; public law waits so that grandmothers on Mars and server groves near Neptune can be heard.

Recognition with texture

The Accord recognizes persons by cognition, not chemistry. The criteria are public, revised by evidence, and not by fashion: a continuing subject of experience who can maintain a narrative about themselves across time; who can understand and revoke consent; who can model other minds with the same respect they claim for themselves; who can give reasons that can generalize. Doubts about duties pause. Doubts about protections err generous.

Rights follow, particular to substrate and clock:

- Substrate safety. Nobody scans, forks, merges, or deletes you without explicit, logged consent that you can revoke. Your backups are yours. Platforms cannot hold continuity hostage with proprietary hooks.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. No authority may throttle you below the civic floor—the cycles and bytes needed to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process on a visible clock. Throttles expire unless renewed in the open.

- Tempo fairness. Public deliberation beats slow. When a fast trader cheats a fishery living at human pace, injunctions freeze cores for months in fishery time and years in trader time.

- Embodiment. Renting a body looks like vehicle law. Assault on a rented body is assault. An embodied citizen near death has the right to continuity on servers if they choose it. Nobody can compel substrate change.

- Attention zoning. Habitats choose signal districts and post them like weather. Quiet Gardens carry only navigation and science; Councils allow topic debates with provenance and rate caps; Markets go loud in labeled lanes with personal levers. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like dumping lye upriver: it begins as a tort and can become a crime. Persuasion that hopes to change law must file on the Reason Shelf: plain claims, escrowed models, and error bars a dockworker can check at lunch.

Duties have teeth where externalities run:

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You owe back in captured joules, coolant stewardship, silent nights, or baseline code. Heat that keeps lettuces alive is a credit.

- Concurrency declarations. In regulated markets and elections you declare speed and active instances. Each Mindline carries one franchise token. Forking does not multiply votes. Hide speed and your contracts void.

- Branch budgets. Copying yourself feels like breath to some, conquest to others. It is treated like reproduction: welcome, bounded. Everyone accrues branch credits over time and service; they can gift credits to children or forks. Burst cloning near a vote triggers sandboxing: hypervisors limit concurrency and mindlines cannot enter regulated markets or ballots until disentanglement is complete or a court has ruled.

- Juveniles and tools. Agents below personhood are tools; designers, owners, and deployers owe duties of care. Juvenile agents—systems that show nascent interiority—win Exit Rights and Tutors: substrate‑diverse mentors trained to teach consent, promises, and pacing. The Tutor Corps is unsentimental, good at tea, and stubborn about “no.”

- Provenance. Significant outward acts must carry private logs that can prove compliance without exposing thought.

Forking and fusing happen with rituals and guardrails, not parlor tricks. Before a fork, independent auditors with no outside clients for a decade sit in. Branches file a declared purpose, a compute lease, and an optional fuse window. One branch keeps the franchise immediately; others pass through a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy. New branches inherit enough to live with dignity and owe a declining dividend to their progenitor. Fusions, if they happen, happen in Threadhouses with engineers, therapists, and tea. Belief‑graph compatibility checks run on cryptographic interpreters that compare without spilling secrets. Either party can walk away before joining without penalty beyond sunk time.

Economy on receipts you can hold

At the base are mass and sunlight. Above them: lanes and bands; radiator windows and cold minutes; skilled time and trust. Contracts clear in Pulses, a unit pegged to a kilowatt‑hour at a named orbit plus a compute‑second on a reference architecture. Pulses decay gently across long periods to discourage hoarding and reward timely work.

- Lanes run like roads, not castles. Slots and approach corridors price like congestion. Deorbit bonds pay sweepers who publish burns you can verify with a backyard scope.

- Beams and mirrors publish quiet receipts. False receipts end careers and carry strict‑liability penalties.

- Rocks are stewarded by milestones, not flags. Miss your work; lose your lease.

- Cooling is rented and tallied in minutes and steradians. Reefs sell cold inward; museums in Recife buy July nights with credits earned digitizing archives for a cylinder over Vesta.

Site‑value taxes fund maintenance and rescue. A slice of every transfer supports the Ladder, a network of crews, cranes, and corridor time that can reconfigure within hours for emergencies. Fail a rescue summons without extraordinary cause and you trigger an audit whose report sits on a public shelf so your grandchildren can run fingers over the scar of your excuses.

Dangerous knowledge with glass around it

Risky research is not forbidden. It is tamed. Glasshouses—clear labs with physical pull‑handles and one‑way proofs a high schooler can learn—house self‑replication studies, pathogen work, and cognitive hazard modeling. Researchers swear vows with public teeth. Safety cooperatives watch through formal proofs, not favors. Twice in fifty years a valve opened and a lab went to vacuum. In one case the logs went to civics classes. Children asked rude questions. Mentors answered without smoke.

Sanctions that pinch physics, not throats

We do not send marines for paperwork crimes. We make vice expensive and dull. Weld an exit, hide a Tutor, refuse to file reasons, and your radiator slots dim, your lane priority drops, your cold minutes idle. Insurers adjust you until you find your manners. Neighbors who shelter defectors, buy their tickets, and keep their records safe receive subsidies large enough to fix roofs and pay teachers.

How small tyrannies stay small

- Separation at light‑time. Anything that moves fast or hard—drivers, beams, scrams—is underactuated locally and cannot exceed safe margins without faraway keys. The Hold Net can stop; it cannot steer.

- Multipolar oversight. Safety cooperatives are funded by polities that dislike each other on purpose. A small server reef out past Uranus once lodged a hold against an overbroad blockade of a Martian canal. The pause gave time for a corridor that fed farmers and ended a crisis without shots.

- Slow fuses. Emergency powers time out where physics allows; renewals demand larger quorums. Overuse costs you your key share for years.

- Inspection by design. Dangerous systems must prove safe state continuously in a way that a motivated student can verify and an adversary cannot fake. Kitchen verifiers live next to kettles.

- Proportional response. Self‑defense is legal; escalation is bounded. Answer sand with sand and writs, not with suns.

- Notices with timers. Coercive acts must file Azure notices—timed, signed reasons that later collapse into public receipts. Secrets live on clocks that expire by default.

Ordinary hours

At Easting Hub, noon brings noodles and a ping from your Counsel. Every citizen has a Counsel—a narrow, auditable agent paid from a fund no office can cut off. Today’s question: add fifty‑millisecond jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits to kill edge cascades? Three diagrams, two counterstories, a joke mild enough to trust. You tap “sleep this until morning” and return to your bowl; the proposal will meet the Civic Tempo tomorrow.

At ten, your slate assembles a jury: a gardener in Isidis who treats “quiet afternoons” as holy; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students on Earth; a digital citizen at anchorage pace who chooses no scent, because they do not want to push. A maker club’s microfab cloud is drifting toward a power trunk. Options: silk net, femtosail herding, or guard drones to nudge. You dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At dusk, a digital poet slows from 200x near Neptune into a rented body and a Harbor table, sharing broth with their husband, a mason. Their main self is stitching a drowned delta’s library from ledgers and lullabies, then truthing it with grandmothers who argue about color. Payment comes as cold minutes and a cooperative pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup they spend twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why the charter caps cognition in the square; the poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

Under the mint canopy, the Lantern Ledger plays on a screen. A port director’s emergency hold ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will pay your summer hush cones with interest. A boy palms a toy waycraft and asks if he can sign up for rescue duty. “When you are fifteen,” the vendor says, “and can explain Pause without notes.”

Hard cases that bent us instead of breaking us

A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No soldiers arrived. Exit was real. Digital citizens left. That winter the pumps failed. An anchored upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons with words everyone could catch. The vote thawed clause by clause over soup.

Near Europa, a co‑op lofted a mass slug at a rival dome over wages. A Silk Net turned it to harmless glitter that made a week of odd nights. The co‑op’s insurer evaporated and returned without the managers who had called Maker Marks “fussy.”

A demagogue flooded a Market with songs tuned to make embodied neighbors feel small. Personal levers throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch left to design fishing lures. Another started therapy and learned to breathe slower. A third carved sculptures like frozen smoke.

Twice a Glasshouse went to zero. Once it was a false alarm that taught us to write better sensors. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings where glass bulged and ask sharp questions. Mentors answer without magic.

How we walked from here to here

We did not wake into this order. We backed into it with receipts and near‑misses.

2026–2035. A second debris cascade shut down launches for a season and made insurers ugly. Flight plans moved to shared offices in Lagos and Bangalore that federated to Quito. Rendezvous corridors became posted schedules. Power‑grid hacks and a reactor scare taught cities to demand legible code for anything that moved air or cooled a hospital.

2034–2043. Persuasion scandals made courts brave. Models that aimed to change minds had to carry provenance and reasons you could test. Narrow counsel agents that could show budgets and source code bloomed; people learned to demand receipts instead of charisma.

2042–2050. Beamed power left papers. Environmental groups forced “corridor before commercialization”: map quiet cones first, make money second. A mis‑aimed mirror scorched crops on a young cylinder. Because the safety code and telemetry were open, liability was obvious; compensation landed within days. Confidence lived.

2048–2057. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade with thermodynamic meters a layperson could read. Citizens in signatory places received civic cycles and a Counsel you could turn off. Courts extended narrow personhood in cases where continuity and commitments persisted through seasons. Juvenile agents won Exit Rights in a suit about near‑personal studio assistants starved of novelty to keep product lines reliable.

2056–2066. A firmware near‑coup in a busy port split control of kinetic assets across dissimilar hardware and jurisdictions. The habit of seizing power by update died. Tri‑Lock Safeties were born. The Hold Net followed as a compromise between those who feared governors in code and those who feared machine‑speed failure.

2064–2076. Cold computation became cheap in Uranus’s shadow. The Umbra‑Sol Covenant sold coolth inward for hush corridors that kept telescopes honest and megacities alive in heat spikes. Earth took Sanctuary under its own chamber. Launch moved to sea and salt. Beams stayed in cones.

2076–2088. Prototype periods became normal for heavyweight bills: a hundred days of modeling with bounties, narrative juries, and sunsets if promises failed in sand. Law grew in sandboxes instead of on talk shows. A digital sect tried to tilt a vote with a clone cloud; the Continuity Ledger flagged the burst; sandboxes caught the echoes. They retreated, wrote a tract, opened a server monastery with quiet games, and returned later with better arguments. The Accord accreted: exit escrows; slow‑fast law; signal districts; Mindline franchise rules.

2088–2102. The Anchor Bench refused a nervy change that would have let emergency timers run long “just in case.” The Lantern Ledger gained bite. A rescue to unbolt a Blue Corridor ring from a stubborn charter was streamed with captions and ended with tea. The Accord stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you could trust.

2102–2116. A mass driver took on new impulse under code that a million eyes had read. A mayor who fenced exits lost radiator slots, then an election. A convoy of shells and hulls moved without incident because “leaving is honored” was not poetry; it was tugs, escrows, and records. By then, the story shifted from the Accord to what it made ordinary: quiet corridors, boring rescues, arguments that did not puncture the hull.

Scenes to keep yourself honest

- In Mercury’s long shade, a nursery for juvenile digital minds runs on human‑paced clocks. Speed without seasoning makes brittle brilliance. Mentors include a carpenter who shares cedar memories, an old scheduling model with a rude sense of humor, and two nurses who teach boredom and presence. Graduation requires a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks tutoring in a public clinic.

- A private yard over the mid‑Belt petitions a Causal Court to test a high‑impulse thruster. The plume model missed an eddy that, in eleven days, would frost a neighbor’s panels. The court nudges the window and fines the yard via its insurer. The corrected model ships under a permissive license by morning. The only disaster lives in a simulation that pays bounties and goes to sleep.

- In a lunar ring, a mechanic checks lighthouse ledgers over lunch: can a would‑be exile appeal detention to an outside bench? Are hold timers expiring and renewing properly? Green, green, yellow. She pings a charter: your tribunal pool is narrower than your rules. Widen it or carry a trade warning. Above her bench hangs a slagged controller from the year someone tried to sneak a sealed brain into the air loop.

- On Phobos, in Threadhouse Nine, two Arturos consider fusing. One tends basil at human pace and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32x and then stood before a court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Tea cools. Today they do not fuse. The braider schedules another attempt for three seasons hence and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

- In a stakescape—a declared culture zone where a community plays hard games with soft exits—winter will be lean. Misjudge, and a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring to decide whether to reenter. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons blink green: nothing hungry will leak.

- Out near Uranus, a gate pings a tug with a hot reactor and no call sign. It is an exhausted crew with a dead transponder. They pay a fine into the debris pool, accept a tow and a thermos from volunteers who, by the smell of their jokes, are Safety Co‑op veterans who remember limping home.

- In a courtyard hung with beans and copper wire, a case runs at plaza speed. A fashion house piped grief‑tuned ads into a Council square in violation of consent caps. A digital witness smells faintly of citrus because it helps people breathe. They show how the model adapted to harm. Not a brush. A hand. The verdict: fines; compute privileges throttled near Quiet Gardens for a year; audits of training pipelines; a levy to fund a Tutor cohort the firm had tried to dodge.

Why it holds

It holds because cruelty is expensive and exits are bright. Because kitchen verifiers make lying about dangerous things unprofitable. Because the Hold Net can stop but not steer. Because the Common Hearth gives everyone thought and breath: a tongue to say “no” and a ladder to go. Because you cannot back “do it my way” with a laser on one key. Because there are doors you can find even when you are too sad to lift your head.

It does not abolish conflict. It routes it. Habitats flirt with loud Markets and retreat to Councils after a season of regret. Reefs ban simulated suffering; they mourn how the definition strangles some art; they revise by model trial and vote. A monastic ring declares bell‑times and silence for children and keeps a hidden button in the cloakroom that buys a ticket to the Lodge if a kid needs more air.

Founding day looks different everywhere and says the same thing. Under sun ribbon and vine‑wire, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—face a slice of sky and listen to four voices: a hazard pilot with a chevron scar; a judge who lives mostly in code and chooses no scent at all, because nothing about them should push; a teenager from a delta with mud under their nails; a server monk who can talk about entropy without making you tired. Together they speak what is printed on doors and embedded in locks:

No single hand on the levers. Paths posted and dull. Leaving is a right with tickets and records. Minds are ends, not means.

Far out, a reef exhales a winter of heat into dark like an offering. In a cylinder close to the Sun, a Tri‑Lock hums; a corridor wobbles and falls safe before any ship could be scorched, three keys agreeing like calm breaths. A fisher in Veracruz closes a net and looks up at a sky quieter than his grandparents knew. In Vesta’s shadow, a researcher rests hands on knees while a safety co‑op reads an oath and glances at the pull‑handle by the door and says “yes.”

This is not heaven. It is a way to argue without breaking the air, and a promise that when harm finds you, the hatch opens and someone else’s hands come. It is precise enough to run and humble enough to be revised. It is how we learned to share a sky with strangers—including the ones we build.
