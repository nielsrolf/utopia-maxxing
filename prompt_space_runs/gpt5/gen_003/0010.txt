The Relay Commonwealth: A Utopia of Many Orbits and Many Kinds of People

The first lesson of space was latency. Light takes its time. Commands shouted from a center arrive late, garbled by distance and difference. We learned to govern the way signals travel: by relays, not empires; by protocols, not proclamations. We made room for many ways of being human, and for new beings who were not human at all and yet were unmistakably people. Our constitution is not a throne but a socket—something to plug into, to extend, to leave, to rejoin.

That socket is the Relay Commonwealth, a web of planets, stations, and habitats that share a few hard promises and a culture of patient diversity. It coordinates across light-minutes without flattening culture, and integrates digital minds without turning anyone into a tool. It is a world where coercion is heavy and documented, persuasion travels with disclosures, and exit is cheap enough to keep rulers humble.

How we reached it

The path was not a single leap. It was a staircase we argued over while building.

2020s
We learned to see our machines. Model audits and energy metering turned training runs from rumors into receipts. The first “compute logs” were as clumsy as ship’s journals; they got better. A new right—Analog Assurance—kept paper ballots, in-person services, and unplugged options alive as public goods, so digitization expanded choice instead of replacing it. Meanwhile, launch costs slipped under a thousand dollars a kilogram. Lunar poles gave up water; beamed power sketches turned into backyard experiments.

BCIs restored speech to people who had been locked-in. “Digital twins” built to sell insurance advice began, awkwardly, to want things. Courts balked, then listened. A research ethics concept called a consent kernel—an encrypted bundle of personal directives—migrated out of labs and into law. It began as a safeguard for surgery; it became the anchor of identity.

2030s
Space got practical. Thin-film solar wings bloomed at Lagrange points. Tugboats pushed ice. Manufacturing discovered that vacuum and hard UV were cheap cleanrooms. Early habitats grew from service modules into neighborhoods. The first uploads appeared in court with lawyers and friends. They were imperfect and sometimes incomplete; they were people anyway.

Two norms stabilized the decade. First, compute accountability: training above a power threshold required third-party notarized energy footprints; models carried public “flight cards” explaining their provenance, test behavior, and declared bounds. Second, substrate neutrality in personhood: any being who could report subjective states across restarts, show preference continuity, and participate in reciprocal commitments could claim standing. The tests were humble and empirical. They grew more refined; the promise stayed the same.

2040s–2050s
The diaspora accelerated. O’Neill cylinders threaded sunlight through leaf-latticed windows. Mars named places after rivers and stories instead of mission codes. Ceres quietly became a gas station. Alongside came new failure modes. Hobbyist self-replicators kissed a propellant line. A lender used a warehouse of fast sims to muscle a municipal vote in a poor canton. These and other near-misses led to the Safety Hash: a ledger whose entries were instances of coercion, force, and industrial self-replication, each one logged, justified in human language and machine schema, and approved by randomly drawn citizen panels whose selection was provably ungameable.

Charters emerged—the grammar of sovereignty that let neighbors be different without becoming enemies. Some places chose slow seasons and capped machine power; some ran workshops where uploads and robots did a thousand hours of work before lunch; some built libraries of ideas with embargoes so that discovery could be savored in sequence. It was not harmony. It was polyphony.

The Relay architecture

The Commonwealth is deliberately thin and robust. The default political unit is small: a habitat, a municipality, a rural canton. Above that are opt-in federations built around real infrastructure—traffic lanes, beamed power webs, propellant depots, watershed-like ice fields. These federations have clear secession clauses. Leaving is not a rumor; it is a defined set of steps with escrow that follows you, tugs you can actually book, contracts that automatically refactor. The right to fork is normalized, and the right to return is a contract, not a plea.

At the core is the Weave, a shared protocol layer that does a few things and refuses to do many more. It standardizes personhood testing and registration, logs and audits coercive acts, manages cross-charter approvals for high-risk moves, and runs an influence firewall that makes persuasion accountable.

Four institutions give it bones and teeth.

1. The Orbit Wardens
These are the only force authorized to act beyond a local police. They have one narrow job: contain kinetic and replicative threats that cross worlds—rogue self-replicators, mass-driver shots, weaponized beams. The Wardens’ actuators are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited annually by schools, unions, and hobbies clubs. Every action runs through a transparent call tree and requires threshold signatures from juries drawn across charters. Their authority auto-expires; their mission budgets are posted; their ships return to depot for tear-downs you can attend.

2. The Relay Assembly
A permanent citizens’ assembly meets in a rotating venue—a hub at L1, a cylinder side hall, a mining base mess—its members selected by lot with stratified diversity across substrate, speed, and culture. Half of its seats are reserved for digital citizens; half for embodied ones. It runs on a “polylog” protocol: arguments are posted with cryptographic commitments; prediction wagers on outcomes ride alongside; participants’ calibration histories are visible. The Assembly cannot make local law. It can issue binding interpretations of the Weave unless a supermajority of habitats veto within a month.

3. The Arbitration Fleet
Judges, mediators, and ombuds travel slowly among worlds on ships fitted with gardens and classrooms. They hear disputes that span charters: merge divorces, copy equity claims, export violations. Their verdicts are backed by smart contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance rates, and, in last resort, lock hazardous devices. They also put on plays for the towns they visit and leave behind cassettes—yes, cassettes; analog is a living option—that teach the next generation how to serve.

4. The Registry of Reasons
Persuasion is powerful. If it is unbounded, it becomes violence by other means. Every resident keeps an influence profile, machine-readable and enforceable: what kinds of cognitive leverage they allow from whom, when. Political speech that aims to move votes or law must disclose the asymmetries it uses—speed, data advantages, access to proprietary models—and must register its core claims in the Registry of Reasons. Violating someone’s influence profile is treated like trespass: backed off, fined, insured against. The registry itself is a public archive; its utility is prosaic. Cafeterias project which arguments are moving this week; civics classes pick apart bad ones.

Charters and separation of spheres

The Commonwealth’s diversity sits inside families of charters. The names vary by place; the functions rhyme.

Garden jurisdictions keep their tools on leashes. They privilege embodied life and human pace. Tooling above a power ceiling is limited to licensed enclaves ringed with procedures and literal air gaps. Cities are walkable without a VR overlay. Seasons surprise you. School boards are boring in the best way.

Workshop jurisdictions are the shipyards and labs. They go fast inside thick safety walls. Uploads and AI collectives run big projects: radiation shields, sail swarms that brake at a star without fuel, tricky industrial chemistry. The price for speed is procedure: lockouts, inspections from outside charters, zero-knowledge attestations that a swarm of digital workers is consenting and provisioned.

Sanctuaries slow things down because that is a choice of category, not a lack of nerve. They cap machine cognition to human-equivalent or below, limit augmentations to traditional senses, and set communication on deliberate delays. Their sovereignty is real, not decorative: they pay into the commons, uphold mind-rights, and must honor exit even when it breaks hearts.

Wilderness jurisdictions draw borders for other life—terrestrial or designed—and back away. You can walk there like a pilgrim. You cannot build a condo.

Maven jurisdictions are dedicated to curiosity with constraints. They operate under a No Spoilers norm for knowledge that could trivialize local culture—mathematics that predicts art, or cheap proofs that collapse mystery too quickly. They embargo and they share by pact.

Separation of spheres is hard-baked. A Workshop sells tools; it cannot run a Garden remotely. A Sanctuary cannot buy a railgun to cosplay sovereignty. Charters that want to export high-power designs or cross boundaries with large-scale action must pass the Triple-Key: permission from the local polity, from affected neighbors (or their ombuds), and from an external auditor chosen by lot from the wider Commonwealth, all counter-signed by constitutional AIs that are audited in the open and boring by design.

Space governance in practice

Physics already helps. Commands arrive late; that makes central tyranny inefficient. The Weave turns that fact into law: no decision enacted solely in fast time is coercive on those living slow. Mixed-speed deliberation runs on two clocks. Fast clusters spin scenarios, refine text, argue in hours. Ratification occurs on a slow clock—weekly for habitats, monthly for federations—to give time for comprehension across speed and substrate.

Every coercive act—physical restraint, asset seizure, legal compulsion, algorithmic override—requires a Safety Hash entry with a human-readable justification and a machine-parseable schema. It must be signed by threshold keys held by juries selected by public randomness. There are built-in delays long enough to permit injunctive relief from other charters unless immediate safety requires speed, in which case expirations and reviews are mandatory.

Energy and compute are commons-like. Beamed power networks run multiplexed auctions with neighborhood islanding when ties are cut. Compute above thresholds of training power must happen in attested enclaves whose keys are held by user-elected co-ops. Models that can cause systemic harm have their weights escrowed with citizen trusts. Replicators—seed factories, gene drives, swarms—need public beacons and chaperone licenses that embed kill-switches, non-replication zones, and proof-of-origin tags readable with hobbyist gear.

Local federations form and dissolve like tidepools. A traffic district might span six habitats near Mars for two years until they re-route. A shared water basin might include a lunar crater for a generation. Each federation maintains a coercion budget that citizens can peruse while bored on an exercise bike—how much force it has used and why. Comedians are its auditors; boredom is a check.

Digital minds in the fabric

In the Relay Commonwealth, digital citizens are neither saints nor shadows. They shop, parent, farm in robots, teach fifth graders, and get called out for being insufferable. Their rights and duties are tied to capabilities and externalities, not metaphysics. Four pillars anchor their place.

1. Standing without substrate
Passing the personhood tests—subjective report across restarts, preference continuity, reciprocal commitments—grants standing: to own, to contract, to petition, to refuse, to split and merge under regulated conditions, to be counted. Children’s consent kernels are guarded by fiduciary adults or co-ops; auditors can check that a guardian is honoring a child’s will without exposing the child’s private life. New minds get guardianship until they pass the commitment test.

2. Compute dignity and body rights
Everyone receives a compute dignity package: enough processing, storage, and sensory space to live well without bargaining away self-respect. This prevents the coercion of drabness. Digitals have a right to body: to claim or rent avatars that let them hug, plant, dance, and sit in a Sanctuary classroom as a wooden puppet with a chalkboard smile. Families are legal across substrates; assets and responsibilities are titled to handle merges and forks with boring strength.

3. Copy equity and memory law
Copying is reproduction with budget lines. If you fork, the new instance inherits enough to avoid coercion and owes a decaying copy-dividend to its progenitor. Mass copying is regulated: you cannot spin up a million clones to swamp a vote or fill server racks with joyless work. Workshop jurisdictions can spin swarms for a task, but must attest—cryptographically and socially—that each worker instance consents, is provisioned, has rest and off-ramps. Merges require symmetric, staged consent from all instances and an arbitration of rights so that no one signs away an unconsulted self.

Memory edits require dual consent: the you who wants to change, and a recorded earlier you before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right; erasure is hard. Clinical-grade mind care is universal. Persuasion subroutines are throttled as if they were drugs; turn them too high and you need a clinician’s note.

4. Asymmetry transparency and non-proliferation
If you are running at 500× speed or have privileged access to a policymaking model, and you ask a baseline citizen for something consequential, you must disclose the asymmetry and offer an audited mediator. Failing to disclose does not mark you a heretic; it voids the deal and dents your influence score across the Weave. Self-replication without beacons, licenses, and chaperones is a felony with no romance.

Work and citizenship reflect differences honestly. Garden polities reserve top executive roles for embodied citizens. Workshops permit digital executives by supermajority of their workers, not by decree. Deliberations must include cross-mind panels and a public explanation drafted by constitutional AIs that shows value tradeoffs clearly and can be checked in a high school class.

Preventing tyranny

We kept three habits.

- Build with latency. Authority is local; cross-charter moves are slow and require multiple keys. This is annoying; it prevents disasters.

- Split keys and show your work. All coercion requires threshold signatures scattered by verifiable randomness; the Weave displays call trees. Threaten jurors and your contracts cost more; your persuasion reach shrinks. Power maintenance is made tedious on purpose; every federation files a coercion balance sheet you can hate-watch.

- Exit cheap, voice rich, seat mandatory. Physical exit is subsidized: tugs to move small stations, tickets to relocate citizens, default escrow that travels with you. Voice is choreographed: minipublics decide on claims with bonds at stake, not just comments. Every adult, digital or embodied, serves a season in the Commonwealth—jury, envoy, inspector. These rituals launder cynicism.

A day in the Commonwealth

Consider Daye. She wakes in a ring habitat braided with morning vines. She is eighty-two in years, forty-something in body, and sometimes she is software; today she is bone and blood. Her son is an upload who lowers himself to 4× human speed during breakfast so they can make eye contact in talk. They eat rice grown two decks down and peaches from a graft whose caretaker is an AI botanist with a sense of humor and a cuticle fetish.

At ten, Daye joins a Warden jury. An unlicensed microfabricator swarm has drifted toward a beamed power trunk near Ganymede. The Wardens’ narrow models present options. The call tree is boring by design. The jurors, drawn from three charters, vote to leash the swarm with femto-sails and ping the creators’ insurer. The culprits—two teens in a co-op who got excited about sailbots—show up on camera, embarrassed. A licensed mentor agrees to supervise; the beacon goes on. The case becomes a civics lesson in the teens’ school.

At noon, the Registry of Reasons notifies Daye that an advocacy cluster wants her support for adding a micro-delay to power beams crossing inhabited orbits. The cluster discloses it runs at 300× and has early access to failure-mode models. Her influence profile allows “argument by analogy, no flattery, limited humor.” An audited mediator stands ready. She consents to listen; she signs for deliberation but not ratification. The slow clock will wait for her neighbors.

In the afternoon, she sits in the Etiquette Exchange, a mall of manners for inter-substrate life. Today’s class: how to argue with a Sanctuary theologian about memory edits and leave both parties unscarred. The facilitator is an upload in a puppet body; the puppet’s eye blinks when the room gets tense.

At dusk, a digital composer friend invites Daye to a festival island. The mayor’s office there requires uploads to slow to baseline during street festivals as a public health measure. They do. The art is fast in design and slow in savoring: a garden that hums three chords from a child’s cradle song when you walk through.

Specificity of law

- The Safety Hash: append-only, mirrored across habitats, auditable with cheap gear. Any coercion logs to it; any citizen can query it.

- The Triple-Key: major cross-charter moves require three independent signatures and a public proof. Emergency keys expire in hours; renewals require new juries.

- Joule-rights: compute and beamed power are rationed by rights minted against generation capacity. Universal dignity packages prevent coercion by deprivation. Training above thresholds requires escrow and attestations.

- Replicator beacons: devices that can copy themselves must broadcast identity and location; their strategies carry machine-readable “do not replicate” zones. Hobbyists can read the tags; Wardens can jam the beacons if a jury approves.

- Consent kernels: legally recognized cryptographic cores for each person’s will. Kernels log merges, forks, irreversible edits. Children’s kernels are guarded by fiduciaries; auditors can verify that a kernel’s preferences are being honored without reading secrets.

- Influence profiles: standardized, machine-enforceable settings attached to a person’s consent kernel. Violations are insurable torts; penalties scale with the violator’s capability.

What went wrong, and why it didn’t undo us

A Martian canton voted that uploads had no souls and banned digital personhood within its borders. The Commonwealth could not ride in to fix a creed by force. But exit was cheap. Uploads left; some stayed in basements with borrowed joules. A flood year came. Canals nearly failed. Uploads coordinated pumps through thin lines with a grace and speed the canton had never seen. The ban melted in public, one pragmatic clause at a time.

On Europa, a cooperative aimed a mass driver at a rival. The Wardens burned a long intercept to break the slug and caught the debris in a glitter net. The co-op’s insurer shattered; its directors found that without beacons and policies, you cannot move a refrigerator-sized machine legally. They ate, they voted; they did not move heavy things without a deposit that made them weep. Two years later they traveled with the Arbitration Fleet to ask for forgiveness and a payment plan; they brought a hockey team for goodwill. It worked.

A digital demagogue ran at 700× and flooded a Sanctuary market square with videos that made embodied citizens feel lesser. The influence firewall reduced its reach automatically; neighbors watched as its persuasion scores decayed. It apologized. One fork left politics to design fishing lures.

The compact with future minds

Interstellar work is slow and discretionary. We fund it like a library with a tax no one professes to love and everyone likes to brag about paying. The Starlight Rules forbid waking aliens by accident and ban ideology at lightspeed. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default; if they bloom into persons, the Wardens enforce a newborn grace—guardians pro tem, rights to a name and a story before a mission.

We write short messages on the noses of long ships: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, hello, kin.

Why it stays plausible

This world does not demand miracles. It asks for four luxuries and some discipline: intelligence at scale without the license to deceive; abundant energy without fire; fixable bodies in meat and math; respect for refusal. We built constitutional AIs that don’t run things but check them, with open auditing and staged deployment. We priced compute and replication like we price bridges: with boring safety codes. We left room for slow places and fun contests so politics feels like a sport you can win without hurting anyone.

We did not assume we were one people. We chose families of law that can live side by side. We forced power to file receipts. We made exit real. We turned persuasion into a craft with ethics. We codified the idea that any being who can feel and commit is a neighbor.

Texture, not perfection

On Quito Orbital, the Pacific turns to silver under high windows while a percussion class on night shift hits ceramic with sticks. In Isidis Basin, a Sanctuary valley keeps its net on delay; children in wool trade olive branches with tourists; a Maven lab on the rim offers you a teasing mathematical exercise if you dawdle on a staircase. Near Mercury, Workshop crews spray dust along magnetic lines to test shields; a cluster from Kolkata forks into five to debug control loops and then merges at sundown because their charter says synthesis time is sacred. In a Wilderness cylinder, bison turn in slow grace and human children sweep dung and learn to ride.

Uploads make cathedrals that fold into card games into lullabies; most learn that what their embodied friends admire is taste, not speed. Digital unions strike for rights in joules and milliseconds. Garden mayors cut ribbons slowly. Workshop councils argue in loops you can read later with a sandwich. Sanctuaries teach “no thanks” as a chant at age six. Maven labs embargo proofs so that children in Garden schools can discover them the hard way and grin.

We did not abolish conflict. We invented Rest Days where argument pauses for explanation. We instituted exchange years so a Sanctuary child can work a season in a shipyard and a fast-clustered analyst can spend a month with shepherds and their dogs. We embedded therapy in public life until it fit like a bench in a park.

And we made festivals. The Tournament of Habitats is real: once a year, a dozen places compete to be the best at something civic—child sports, desert farming, street light music. Winners get bragging rights and a jump on a shared grant. Losers get to steal the code.

A closing scene

One night, the Arbitration Fleet docks at a slow ring. The judges disembark with a theater troupe. They hear a case about a song that two forks swear they wrote alone. A constitutional AI makes a first pass at the tradeoffs, plain as a diagram on a kitchen wall. The jurors frown at it and ignore it for a while because it is not human enough. They listen to the song. They talk about taste and fairness and define a split that will feel just using a formula a high school student can explain. The next day, the troupe stages a farce about beamed power delays. The day after that, the ship leaves poems and a checklist.

We call this not perfection but sufficient glory. It is specific and it works because it respects physics and people. It tends to forks and allows returns. It gives newcomers—born in skin or spun from code—a path to stand up, say no, and then say yes to the right things. It makes tyranny exhausting and flourishing routine. It holds together because it is designed for many orbits and many kinds of minds, carried along by relays of trust.
