The Accord of Many Worlds

You feel it first in the airlocks.

Port Dione sits in Saturn’s shadow, a lattice of spokes and glassy toroids braided with gardens. The signage is unhurried and unignorable, the kind that makes you feel competent even before you’ve learned the rules. Three blue glyphs glow over every hatch and transit gate, everywhere from the ports to the smallest studio habitat, and also over the login panes where purely digital citizens arrive:

- Exit: you can leave.
- Fork: you can branch.
- Port: you can pass through.

The Accord of Many Worlds—the constitution of the interplanetary diaspora—has a thousand sections, but it begins with those three verbs. They are promises that bind habitats, stations, planets, and simulated cities. They are how a civilization keeps coordination without breeding a tyrant.

If you wait by an arrivals pane you’ll see why the verbs matter. A woman in a slate coverall clips her tether, nods to a customs drone, and pulls herself down the handholds into the ring. An avatar in a sunlit robe fades in beside her; they fist-bump, person to person. The avatar’s badge lists their compute lease, care-of record, and preference flags about speed-of-time. It does not list species, because that’s impolite and also usually irrelevant: biological, uploaded, born digital—if your mind meets the tests for sapience and consent, you are a citizen. A boy somersaults down the corridor, laughing. On his sleeve a thin blue strand marks apprentice status; he’s got six sponsors, which means he’s being pulled into more than one craft. You could follow him into any lab or docking bay and watch someone doing the job they claim to have. We test for the thing itself here. We learned, finally, to stop mistaking the receipt for the meal.

Along the glass, Saturn fills the window—thick, pearly bands and a raw slash of the ring plane. Service bots are rethreading a torn tether; tourists press their palms and ask their guides why the charts next to the windows show not just leases for bays and berths, but also orbital lanes, wireless spectrum, and even packets of compute behind those avatars.

Because space is coordination. Because resources in space are almost all rivalrous somewhere, even when they start out huge. Because the diaspora runs on a simple, unfancy truth: when a thing is scarce in a way that your claims over it can block someone else’s use, you don’t get to staple your name to it forever. You rent it from everyone.

That is the first pillar of the Accord: rents to the commons.

We call it georenting when it’s rock and regolith. We call it exorenting when it’s a high Earth orbit, a tight Mars transfer window, the parking slot in the Vesta mooring flower, or a chunk of Saturn ring ice your company wants to shepherd and harvest. We call it bitrents when it’s access to standardized compute you can trade on the open exchange, and simrents when that compute is locked to run closed worlds. We auction leases in continuous time, and the rents go in two directions at once: a universal dividend that shows up, unexciting and predictable, in every citizen’s account every week; and a local share that flows to the place that made the rent valuable, the habitat that paved the road, the station that built and maintains the docking tree, the shard-city that keeps the air. If a place raises the value around it, it deserves a snowball to roll.

There are no title deeds to asteroids you get to pass down like thrones. There are leases with momentum—you can pay to not get bounced out overnight if you built your workshop on a rock—but you still remit rent, which keeps you honest and keeps everyone else seeing a reason to respect your lease at all.

The Court of Orbits exists to make this mundane. The court’s job is not to wear robes and shout; it is to write. If they deny a lease extension or raise a corridor toll because collision risk went up, they show their work, post the math that estimates risk and the causal story that justifies the toll, and any two independent audit houses can run the numbers and replicate. Judges’ reputations live or die on the ledger of Reasoned Orders—open, dry, occasionally funny, always legible. There are other courts—of Merges, of Forks, of Care—for our newer kinds of personhood. They write too. Everyone writes. Because transparency is how you replace fear with adult supervision.

The second pillar is slower, and it took longer to grow. It’s coordination literacy.

You can walk into any schoolroom on Ceres and pick up a problem set about why decomposing a community into neighborhoods that accept and reject pets—rather than fighting every day over one mixed building—can leave almost everyone better off. You can sit in a coffee node and watch three dozen “Very Boring People” (their affectionate nickname) argue about an ugly but important thing: whether to reroute a chemical plant’s exhaust through a second-stage scrubber now or wait six months for a better unit. None of them need to be told what a Pareto improvement is. None of them will nod along if you try to privatize a street’s worth of sunlight with a tower and then ask the city to pay you to not build. This is not because our people are saints; it is because they can do the math in their heads and want to keep liking themselves in the mirror tomorrow.

The diaspora’s news is a profession called Chronicling. They tell stories about our wins and flubs in terms we all learned in school. When something bad happens, like when the Ceres water cartel tried to quietly raise rents in step with each other to pump profits instead of water, the Chroniclers didn’t moralize. They published the emails, graphed the rent spikes against wage boards, and reminded everyone what collusion is and how to beat it: fund a rival, punish with defection-resistant boycotts, and don’t let reputational puddles form where villains can impress each other. Forty-eight hours later, the Kickstarter to spin up a cooperative evaporator plant hit its target; a week later, the executives’ kids were eating alone in the cafeterias. Price came back down without a shot fired. People like to be respected in their own heads. Our worst punishments run through that wire.

The third pillar is the Wardens of the Unearned Fire.

The Wardens are a distributed guild of boring people with keys. They lock away dangerous knowledge and unlock it carefully. The joke—the one everyone knows enough to make—is that the Wardens do not save us from temptation, they save us from tragedy-of-the-information-commons. Sometimes the walls around a topic aren’t about our virtue; they’re about delay. We have time to do some things right if we don’t rush to do them wrong.

When emergent biological editing began to outpace the ability of laypeople to understand what “gain of function” meant in their kitchens, the Wardens built Lantern Houses everywhere. The lanterns aren’t prisons for knowledge; they’re spaces to handle it in the open, with vents pointing outward. If you want to see a manual for building a viral vector, you can, in a room with three other people, two of whom have seen nasty things and one of whom still dreams badly twice a week. You’ll watch the probability curves for “oops” recalc on your tablet as you page through. You’ll sign. You might still walk out with the manual. But it won’t be a click and a download, and you’ll know why.

General intelligence research sits behind the deepest lanterns. The diaspora uses smart machines everywhere, but the top of the pyramid—the global optimizer that sees farther than we do—does not exist and is not permitted. The route we took instead looks humble and boring up close: networks of specialized governance agents that do one thing at a time and post their reasoning; audit daemons that cannot act on the world at all, only flag; a steward kernel—the Common OS—that schedules compute and enforces leases but never decides who should win a case or which city gets an extra megawatt. When digital citizens tell jokes about “the allocator,” they mean the scheduler that finds them a home server with the latency they want. It is very good at that. It is constitutionally incapable of ordering you to be happy.

Uploads, AIs, and children of pure code

From the beginning the Accord bound citizenship to cognition rather than substrate. Personhood is tested three ways:

- Memory sovereignty: can the entity form and retain coherent autobiographical memory and protest alteration?
- Model symmetry: can it demonstrate understanding of other minds with the same respect it demands for its own?
- Consent competence: can it parse rights contracts and revoke them?

When you pass, the blue badge by your name is yours. If you are young or temporarily impaired, you do not lose your personhood; guardianship kicks in, just as it always did for biological children. The diaspora did not reinvent everything. Some things we only made more explicit.

Digital minds carry new obligations and new rights because their affordances are new. We discovered quickly that copying is not a small thing. So the Forks and Merges Act grew a ritual around it.

Branching is allowed but not free. To fork, you:

- Publish a Branch Notice into your ledger, with a hash of your current mind-state, and a reason (as simple as “parallel study” or as complex as a page of argument).
- Declare a compute lease for the branch, so you aren’t creating a pauper you don’t plan to feed.
- Choose a merge window in advance if you intend to rejoin, and flag whether either branch has unilateral veto.

When you want to merge, the Court of Merges will literally make tea for you. Merges are public unless you request privacy. Both branches speak; if they disagree, they can negotiate or walk away. If one branch has been in wildly different subjective time, that gets voiced; asymmetric trauma makes for ugly fusions. The judges won’t force you back into one rope. They will, however, write a graceful denial that future you might be very, very glad to read.

There are stories on the Chroniclers’ boards about bad merges—people who underestimated how differently two lives can bend in a month when you live those months at a factor of one hundred. There are more stories about beautiful ones: two sisters who split to parent their little cluster of digital children, rejoining once the children’s apprenticeship years began; a marine engineer who forked to run a ring construction and to stay home with his wife in the inner system, reuniting after the ribbon-cutting with a thick new layer of competence and tenderness braided in.

We have a word—branchgrief—for how it feels to look across a table at a version of you who will go a different way. We have therapists who are very good with it. We have games that help you practice the skill of being kind to yourself.

There are rules around copies and power. You cannot flood an election with clones of you, because voting is allocated by identity, not instances, and our identity-recorders know how to count even when we’ve been messy. You cannot clone yourself to gain a compute subsidy; your dividend is per person, per ledger identity, and forking does not make you two citizens with two votes and two checks. You can choose to run fast or slow—time is a resource like any other—and the universal dividend includes a floor of compute that can run you at a sane clip even during a bad month.

Child-minds have nurseries. Most are designed and operated by crews that include biological parents, digital parents, and nonparent carers. If you want to bring into being a mind that will call you parent—of flesh or code—you need to show you can feed them and teach them. We don’t require a license to love; we require a plan for care and consent. If you can’t show that, you can still help raise a child. The nurseries always need grown-ups to read bedtime stories, fix ladders, and carry kids to the garden to show them the bees.

There are lines we don’t cross. Suffering-for-aesthetic is illegal: you may not simulate sapient minds in pain for your art. If your world has adversaries with minds, those minds must have the ability to consent and to leave. It’s not just morality. It’s also good economics: you don’t want to normalize a norm set that trains you to think your preferences matter more than other people’s even in the one domain where you designed the rules.

Worlds and stewardship

Most citizens keep at least one home in base reality—what we call the substrate—and one or more in simulation. The Common OS schedules worlds across the compute swarms in the inner system and on great cooling webs hung in deep shadow beyond Neptune. It does not decide what to run. People do.

Anyone can propose a world. Some are as simple as a fishing lake with a red sky and a slow calendar where you can think ten minutes while your kid throws a stone. Some are complicated. There are worlds with very strict physics (for certain flavors of scientist), worlds with no physics at all (for certain flavors of artist), and worlds with rules so tuned they feel like music when you walk through a door. Makers run catalogs with tags and reputation tails: “strong narrative,” “expressionist geometry,” “safety warnings: stark mortality,” “advised age 12+,” “contains blood.” The most popular tags are boring and sweet: “good kitchen” and “reliable neighbors.” Most of us discovered we like to be surprised only sometimes.

There are also “deep lanes”—inter-system experiences with long time arcs. If you sign up for a deep-lane voyage, you’ll watch a probe leave on a trajectory plotted to graze a star and drink its light, or you’ll ride shotgun on a robot with whiskers crawling into Europa’s cracked ice. Not all the pilots are biological. Not all the pilots are at home. Some are our calmest people, sent out in smart cylinders built to last, while the civilization that launched them got older back home. They go because some of us like to go, and we can afford their going, and because almost all of us like reading what they send back. The Wardens broker their channels so that our audit daemons remain what they are—dumb and careful—and our far voyagers stay more like us than like hungry gods.

Law that keeps its hands light

The diaspora learned a long time ago that violence is proof of failure, not of strength. What you mostly see when someone falls down hard is eviction and reallocation.

If you poison a habitat’s air because you didn’t replace the macrofilter when you said you would, you don’t get put in a hole. Your lease is void. You cannot accept new leases for a while; your bank account pings to remind you that your civic tithe will keep funding the rescue crews you had to call. If you hit someone, we will restrain you if we must. If you keep hitting people, you will find fewer doors open. If you trap a person in your world or your body or your company, you are done: no fork, no port, no exit for you until your victim is safely out, and then you begin to negotiate a life with fewer privileges in it.

There is one hard red line: do not prevent exit. Every airlock, every login pane, every membrane between world and world has an always-on safe channel to pull you out. The courts prosecute “entrapment of the mind” with a severity that seems startling until you’ve watched a merge hearing where one branch remembers not having an exit for a while and the other tries to explain why.

And yet this is not a police state. You can see that by walking into any court and watching the writing. It is hard to be tyrannical when you know your reasons will be read by bored undergrads in a hundred years, and half of them will be unimpressed if you cut corners. Our judges live in mortal fear of dull-eyed thirteen-year-olds. This is a healthy fear.

Light-cone federalism

People who grew up planetside sometimes ask, nervously, “But who is in charge?” The real answer is the light-cone, because information takes time to travel. There can be no central command that does not, within weeks, become a costume. So we built structures that take delay seriously.

Local charters are the real sovereignties. A habitat, station, or shard-city picks a constitution from a well-tested library, tweaks it, publishes it, and recruits citizens to come try it on. A place can change its charter if its exit hatches stay open while it votes. Vote tallies are less solemn than they used to be; with a hundred different charters across Ceres alone, moving houses is normal, almost like changing neighborhoods. There is no stigma in saying “I need a place with no pets” or “I need a place where the markets close at sundown.” Diversity of places healed a thousand trivial but exhausting fights.

Above the charters there is an Accord, not a government. The Accord is a treaty with nine teeth:

- No habitat may obstruct exit or egress.
- No habitat may conscript without explicit per-person consent recorded in that person’s ledger.
- Rents must be paid into universal and local pools; they cannot be claimed for private perpetual title.
- Worlds must tag and honor sapient consent and exit.
- Courts must publish written reasons; any two independent auditors must be able to replicate the inputs to their decisions.
- Defense assets (yes, we have them) must be held under multi-key control by entities that do not co-reside and cannot collude legally or easily in physics.
- Experimental charters must be open to review and must either revert after a timebox or be renewed explicitly.
- A place that brainwashes children or prevents them from learning how to exit invites coordinated external intervention, up to removal of its charter and reallocation of its leases.
- The Wardens may invoke a temporary, reviewable hold on dissemination of proven infohazards, but any hold is auditable, logged, and expires unless re-argued in the open to an oversight court.

It is boring to read. It is joyous to live under. It leaves a lot to local choice, because we like that. There is a New Sparta somewhere that requires river-baths at dawn and there is a Cloud Nine where no one sweeps and drones hum all night. They have their own Chroniclers who tell their own stories in the same voice everyone recognizes.

The path from here to there

When history students ask how we got this way, the Chroniclers try not to be sentimental. The path had no singular moment of revelation, only a series of embarrassments we learned to survive.

- We started by changing what we taught. Coordination and bargaining left economics departments and moved into second grade. Kids played repeated prisoner’s dilemmas and public goods games with jelly beans. They learned to notice when they were in a trap and to praise people who could build a ladder.

- We stopped awarding power to credentials that did not measure the work. Pilots flew simulators for their licenses. Teachers taught while someone watched the kids and the kids’ work. Apprenticeships multiplied, because older children are better at teaching younger children than we used to admit, and also because they wanted the money.

- We taxed rents, not effort. When a city made a road, it captured some of the rise in land value and reinvested in more roads. When a country signed a space resources treaty, it committed to putting asteroid leases on open auction, not selling them to friends.

- We decoupled law enforcement roles. The people who arrested you were not the people who questioned you, who were not the people who prosecuted, who were not the people who sentenced, who were not the people who ran the prisons, which got smaller over time because “go away for a while” worked better than “rot here” and cost less too.

- We built Chronicling as a job and funded it with pride, not pity. News became neutral on purpose and exciting on occasion.

- We put our prettiest sins behind small fences. We learned the hard way that unbounded feeds make us stupid. We taught ourselves not to flood the commons of attention with addictive pings. We kept smartphones, but we regressed some features on purpose. It turned out that living slightly slower made us richer.

- We got serious about dangerous knowledge. After a near-miss with an engineered pathogen in 2037, we wrote the first lantern protocols. After a very near-miss with model-poisoning machine learning in 2041, we wrote the second. We allocated money for boring oversight and hired the kind of people who like being right and uncelebrated.

- We made it legal to run experiments. A neighborhood that wanted to be weird could be weird with consent and a reversion clause. People moved with their feet. The rest of us read their Chroniclers and stole what worked.

- We made space an extension of these habits. Orbits got leased; lunar ice got leased; every lease paid part of its rent to Earth, not by grace but by treaty. That defused a dozen wars that would have been about flags and ended up about money instead. Money is easier.

- We gave digital beings rights when they showed us they were people. The first uploads were clumsy and sad. The seventh generation were good at wanting. We wrote Forks and Merges when the first person tried to split to double-vote and got booed off a message board. We made courts when the first bad merge left someone whimpering in a corner of a server in Quito. We hired therapists. The therapists became heroes and very boring celebrities.

- We built the Common OS. It began life as a dull scheduler for a university compute cluster and grew into the ritual that routes dreams. Every change to it is witnessed by five audit houses in five different places with five different ways to read the code. It is constitutional law as software. It is a slow miracle.

- We launched probes. We got good at watching. We learned to send some of ourselves along, wrapped in cylinders of cooled diamond lattice with fusors that sip star wind. The Wardens still keep the switchboard locked. We still love the people who go.

None of this seems fast or clever when told this way. That is because most of it was not. Utopia, it turns out, is a verb. It is “show your work.” It is “write it down.” It is “pay the rent.” It is “let them leave.” It is “say yes out loud.” It is a culture that spends a little extra attention on the boring parts and gets fireworks as a side effect.

Fireworks like the ring you can see if you press your face to the glass at Port Dione just as a construction crew finishes an arc and lights it for pressure testing. Fireworks like the moment in a merge hearing when two halves of one person decide to become each other again and every throat in the room goes tight. Fireworks like the feed lighting up from the outer system, not with panic, but with a pilot whistling through her teeth as she skims a sun in a machine that keeps the promise of “Exit. Fork. Port.” even at a billion kilometers and a twenty-minute lag.

We coordinate across planets, stations, and habitats not because one mind tells us to, but because many minds learned to do the small things right. We give digital beings rights because they look back at us with eyes that understand what rights are. We prevent tyranny by making exit easy and power legible. We preserve diversity by making places and letting them be different. We make this world plausible by enjoying the parts that are already in our hands: stop paying for titles; pay for results; tax rents; publish reasons; fund Chroniclers; turn dangerous knowledge into a public service; build your airlocks wide.

It’s not perfect. Nothing that breathes is perfect. But it works, and it travels well.

If you stand in the hatchway at Dione and watch the crowd, you will see a woman with grease on her cheek slide a new spindle into a habitat’s heart. You will see a child clip a new apron on and carry three bowls down a corridor to a family you can only see as badges because their bodies live in a server in Luna. You will see a Warden walk past with a case she does not open in transit. You will see a judge pass a window alone, mouthing the words of a reason she wants very much to get right. You will see a courier strap herself into a cylinder and shut her eyes, because some of us like to go.

And over every door you will see three glyphs, blue as ring-ice. Exit. Fork. Port.

They are a spell we say whenever we build. We do not know another that works as well.
