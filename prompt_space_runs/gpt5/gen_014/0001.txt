The Constellation Accord

By the time the big lights in Hollander Ring slip to orange, the canal along the inner rim looks like a ribbon poured into the sky. Basil plots darken to a deep green. A tug nosed with scars pushes a barge past pump housings that hum at a note you feel in your teeth. Above the hatch to Berth 12, four small marks glow in the paint where your palm would land without thinking: a sextant; a rope braided from three strands; a gate on its hinge; a fin throwing off heat.

Dockhands call them the Accord marks. Visitors call them pretty. Anyone who’s been here a while knows they are wired—hardwired into valves and safeties and court calendars. They are why Hollander Ring doesn’t fight like the old stories said we would once we left the ground.

The Constellation Accord isn’t a crown or a council you can overthrow. It’s choreography stubborn enough to survive light-hours and human heat. It is how we keep wildly different places—ports and monasteries, reef racks in shadow, sky farms in glare—fiercely themselves without making it cheap to bruise a stranger. It braids three ideas into machinery, budgets, and law that kids can read on a kiosk: split the hands on force; share what cannot be owned in secrecy; make leaving possible without revenge. And because thought can outrun bodies now, it says out loud that beings who feel and remember—born, built, or reassembled—are ends in themselves.

Split the hands on force

Anything with bite—thrusters that could tip a neighbor’s radiators, beamed power trunks, replication vats, the cold end of a memory welder—will not act on one order or one market tick. The actuator won’t light unless three different consents turn within overlapping, not identical, windows. We call it Quorum Hands, and no one can bully those hands into one room with a wrench.

- One hand is local: a steward chosen by lot from the people who share the hull or rack. Their term is short. Their logs are public at the kitchen terminal.

- One hand belongs to an independent safety guild, bonded outside the place it watches. No one in the guild may profit by the device it oversees.

- One hand rotates by lottery through a distant panel of citizens drawn across sites and substrates—embodied workers, slow philosophers, brisk uploads—who serve once and then go home barred for a decade from similar posts.

The panel’s interface is small on purpose: a request to burn a ten-ton tug by a meter per second, with a plume and window. The system proves to anyone with a school parser that the tug is the tug it says it is, that the burn is what the math says it must be, and that no more is possible through that pipe. The proof fits on one sheet. Middle-schoolers learn to check it by hand.

Above every dangerous lever hangs a Holdgrid box with a ticking face. The box has one power only: to idle the lever on a fixed, audited curve long enough to make thought possible, never long enough to let an accident snowball. It listens to local sensors, the Ephemeris (the shared book of lanes and shadows), and to three cryptographic counters that never live in the same place. If it trips, keys burn, timelines and reasons land in a public Cause Log written in dry prose and margin jokes, and insurers start doing math that makes liars wince. The Holdgrid cannot steer your beam, allocate your budget, or nudge a rival’s plumes off a harvest. It pauses and tells everyone.

Share what cannot be hoarded in the dark

Out here, the rival things you can dent—narrow windows in crowded lanes, spectrum hours, slots under pressure, verified compute seconds tied to known heat sinks, radiator angles—are leases held in trust, not trophies kept forever. When you park a city in orbit, you do it in a book your neighbor can read.

Four registries keep us honest.

- The Ephemeris. Orbits, approach cones, burn windows, shadow maps. Anything with a transponder signs with short-lived keys. Amateur clubs with kitchen RF kits and school telescopes can check that a ship’s lulls and songs match its posted path. Escrows drain when lies are caught. No suited prosecutor needs to sweat under a light.

- The Thermal Book. Joules drawn, heat dumped, and declared sinks tied to attested radiators and cold contracts. Cooling is a commons. Lie about your fin area and your processes idle until you lease more. Grandmothers who can read a recipe can read the Thermal Book with a public Counselor and feel in their bones when a neighbor is cheating.

- The Source Roll. Anything that wants to move law or money at scale carries receipts: who paid, what data trained it, what gears it means to grab. Life-support and actuator code posts sources and independent audit trails students can reproduce. Teenagers make stipends catching cute cheats. Award nights are raucous and kind.

- The Consent Weave. People and places publish risks and permissions. You carry Intent Keys—cryptographic stores of what edits you refuse, who you permit to persuade you and when, how fast your public face may go, how you want forks and merges handled. Guardians (human or digital) declare their scope here. Violate the Weave and it’s trespass with insurance. Fines scale with reach. Excuses don’t.

Make leaving possible without revenge

Every charter that calls itself civilized escrows its exits in dull, boring budgets the way a town on Earth once funded hydrants. You do not have to beg someone to honor your feet.

In the physical, that means tug hours, berths, seal kits, cracked-o-ring spools, air and water for a season, legal counters, heaters, and med refills to move at least a tenth of residents within six months. Audits run on kitchen hours. Teenagers catch shortfalls and get paid.

In the digital, it means reserved cycles and storage offsite on sleepy, honest stacks you can see, paced at a humane tempo. Any recognized digital citizen can call their Continuity Safe if their host clamps down. Cutting the Safe does not choke food or medicine. It draws a tight band around the offender’s actuators: their lane windows gray, their bid priority thins, their radiator auctions cool.

Every port and compute hall keeps an Open Door desk you can find without a password. Three counters sit under bright lamps.

- Departures & Care books berths and leases, ports Intent Keys, escrow-locks gear, replicates meds, and pairs you with a Tutor who is not your employer or progenitor.

- Mediation routes fights, convenes mixed panels with jurisdiction that makes sense when bodies and code tangle, and calls the Traveling Bench with classrooms and gardens when law needs to move.

- Founders’ Kits loan templates and minimum safeties so a brilliant new culture doesn’t tear a neighbor’s roof off by accident.

Juveniles—embodied, engineered, emergent—have Tutors with private lines to Doors nobody can cut. Charters that salt exits with fees or shame don’t get sermons. They meet physics: heavy privileges idle until humility returns.

Persons, beyond chemistry

We do not look for souls with scanners. We look for continuity and care. Standing follows experience, not substrate.

To stand equal—to own and be owed, to carry and be held to duties, to vote once, to parent or be parented, to fork or to braid—you pass three tests in a room with windows, in plain speech, before a panel split across substrates and tempos:

- Self-guard. You can track and defend a thread of yourself you care about against edits you do not want.

- Other-mind. You can model other beings’ claims well enough to blush at harming them.

- Contract sense. You can enter, understand, keep, and revoke agreements.

If you are too young, too new, or too hurt, you get guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer to audits tied to your Intent Keys; auditors prove compliance without riffling diaries.

Rights attach to personhood, not chemistry.

- Substrate sovereignty. No one scans, throttles below the dignity floor, deletes, fuses, or forks you without due process you can verify without opening skulls or source trees. Copies over a living self create siblings, not replacements. Backups are yours.

- Tempo fairness. Civic squares run at human-pace clocks. Fast minds sprint in private; in public they throttle. When harm runs across tempos, courts build Pace Bridges: injunctions bite in wall-clock and subjective hours.

- Embodiment. You may lease or build bodies. Assault on a rented frame is assault on you. Flesh citizens keep bodily autonomy and cognitive privacy.

- A compute floor. Every recognized person has a Public Hearth: cycles, storage, and bandwidth enough to speak, learn, keep counsel, and idle in safety. Above that, you rent time like berth space.

Duties scale with footprint.

- One throughline, one civic voice. Votes and stipends tie to continuity, not concurrency. In markets and law, you declare instance counts and pace. Hiding a cloud to tilt an auction is lying about weight on a bridge.

- Branch budgets. Splitting is celebrated and bounded. Everyone accrues fork credits by years lived and work for the commons. Burst clouds aimed to tilt a polity get sandboxed into parallel forums until entanglement decays or a judge says otherwise.

- Provenance for harms with blast radius. Actions that can bruise strangers keep encrypted compliance logs that show you met consent and safety without spilling diaries.

- Simulation ethics. No unconsenting minds in pain. Reconstructions above a fidelity line require consent or a rare, shrinking license. The Hot Stacks—half monastery, half lab—ring dangerous knowledge with oaths and vents.

Forking and braiding happen in rooms, not just commands. To branch on purpose, you sign a docket: a hash of your starting state; an intent (“vote survey,” “griefwork,” “parallel design”); a compute lease that won’t starve neighbors; and, if you plan reunion, a braid window.

Reunion Galleries smell like resin and tea. Therapists and engineers sit with both branches. Memory graphs roll through interpreters that test values without prying prayer. Clocks are synchronized slowly. Either branch may walk away. We have a word for the ache when a version of you chooses a different river. We don’t fix it. We build sturdy chairs.

Governing, braided not stacked

You cannot legislate faster than radio. Reach follows light-time. We braid assemblies with limits you can point at.

- The Circle of Sites seats places that share hull, soil, or rack—a dome, a port, a reef. Weight reflects lifetime resident hours, safety drills passed, and published actuator maps. They tend physics: the Ephemeris and Thermal Book, collision bonds, radiator auctions, quiet cones, training that keeps grandmothers napping under open windows.

- The Roll of Lines seats continuity threads through time—the ongoing lines that make you you across pauses and forks. Most are elected; a slice is drawn by lot across substrates and tempos. They tend personhood: audits, copy and custody, vote pace and tempo fairness, the public compute floor, simulation ethics.

- The Kernel Court is a rotating bench of cryptographers, pilots, hazard engineers, and jurists. It cannot legislate. It certifies or refuses changes to primitives: Holdgrid firmware, actuator interfaces, proof libraries. It publishes runnable reasons. Members go home barred for a decade from any job that touches safety stacks.

- The Rhythm Office keeps clocks and sunsets. They publish whether deadlines, supermajorities, and cooling periods were honored. Their receipts hang in schools. When they fail they are boring; when they err the jokes are swift and tender; when they are captured the whole house rattles and we fix it fast.

Jurisdiction runs at right angles. If Lines reach up to tinker with kitchens or festival noise above the baseline of personhood, Sites can nullify. If a Site tries to starve digital citizens of cycles or gag children, Lines void it. Neither chamber can bind the other fast; drafts can be quick but commitments wait long enough that the slowest registered citizen can object.

Persuasion as a mapped utility

Memetic campaigns hit like beams if you let them. We zone attention the way a city zones noise.

- Harbors carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No targeted persuasion. Fast models display licenses as obvious as taxi placards. Children and twitchy minds walk bare here.

- Parlors allow ordinary offers—vote for this, buy that—under posted rate limits. Audience models are municipal property trained on audit-able, comfortable data.

- Carnivals invite the roar: slang and spectacle under registry. Come to be dazzled or to tire on purpose.

If persuasion aims to change law, it files homework to the Reason Vault: plain-language claims, declared gears, and escrowed models that citizen auditors can run. Violations throttle reach and drain insured pools that price cognitive spill like radiation. Your Counselor—your one-client public-interest agent—negotiates your boundaries and says no when you asked them to.

Tools with teeth but no crowns

We do not keep an army. We keep kites, courts, and receipts.

- Kitecraft are stubby interceptors with brains so simple you can whiteboard them. They carry sharded keys from juries you can reproduce. They herd mass out of harm’s way, paint a dust curtain to shred a slug, or physically unbolt a ring if they must. Children climb inside with flashlights during teardown.

- The Wayfinding Guild are pilots and schedulers and pedants. They set base rents, post Runnable Orders—decisions you can replay with open tools. “Closed Transfer T‑9 for five hours because your berth would be in radio shadow between Eros and Ceres” arrives with a plume plot and a checksum a child can chase at the library.

- The Traveling Bench walks with gardens and classrooms. They hear fork divorces, copy equity cases, export violations that hopped worlds. Their rulings seize bonds, throttle Core Shares, and lock a dangerous key if they must. They stage plays about hard cases so law becomes memory.

An economy of mass, time, and heat

Under our poetry lives rock and solar flux. We count in things you can dent or cool.

We lease crater rims with long sun, cave mouths with cold, nickel seams, sail corridors, orbital slots, spectrum bands and hush windows, radiator angles, verified cores. Nothing sells forever. Rents split into dividends that land automatically.

- The dignity floor: air, water, basic care; a Public Hearth and bandwidth enough to learn and speak; an exit ticket every seven years.

- Local yields: crews who keep value alive—tugs that make planes whisper-clean, algae curtains that hold cool, cold reefs that temper afternoons for a server village riding a tide.

Trades clear in Anchor-Lumens: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on a reference rack in cold orbit. Anchor-Lumens decay gently over years so you can’t hoard them like dragon treasure while your neighbor is hot tonight.

Across distances we swap Night Minutes—the right to dump heat cheaply at low potential—for Hush Windows and Festival Hours closer to home. Cold reefs in far shadow export winter. Fields sunward carve hush with band hours. Every century we reapprove the night-for-hush compact on a clock slow enough for everyone to object. Permanence hardens into thrones. We practice renewal.

Scenes with grease under the nails

A yard on Vesta files to test a new nozzle. The Ephemeris’s traveling court runs a plume sim that shows frost on a neighbor’s tanks if they burn at noon. The yard accepts a midnight slot when the neighbor angles fins away. Their insurer posts a bond. The corrected code ships under a license apprentices can actually read. A stamp moves to Done. Somebody plays a trumpet under radiator vines and ruins a good cry.

In a rim city, a beloved administrator lets too many levers pile up—air inlets, tug windows, corridor auctions. One winter she pulls a rush command that needs all three. The Safety Guild refuses. The Holdgrid yawns and idles valves for five long minutes while a Quorum meets at a bench with a ridiculous view of blue water under glass. Jurors by lot listen hard to a favorite aunt. She steps down and returns to cranes. Children learn the muscle memory of saying no to someone you admire.

A digital congregation spins up four hundred instances a month before a vote. The Roll of Lines flags the burst. Hypervisors slide a civic quarantine around those threads. Work continues; their civic weight slows. A year later, three branches petition for anchors. Two anchor after tea with a Mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told gently: study longer. No server farm overheats.

In a Hot Stack, a team runs a “cold pull” drill on a self-replicating polymer. Handles are too stiff; someone swears; timestamps print. They buy better springs. Logs smell faintly of bleach and cinnamon. At lunch they argue about art that requires simulated sorrow. The junior engineer asks sharper questions because she knows what the pull feels like in fingers.

In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse checks her daily sheet. “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.06 Anchor-Lumens. Jury summons: two hours at your pace on whether juvenile reef processes off Rodrigues get child credits.” She accepts. She sends her mother a voice note laughing about radiator math.

In Neptune’s dusk, a compute reef slows to plant beans in velvet soil with cousins wearing rented hands. After tea, they spin up to 300× to restore flood-muddied archives with grandmothers who correct sky colors without mercy. Payment arrives as Night Minutes and a promise to roof a school in Veracruz.

A Mesh Court with curved walls you can actually clean hears a case about unlabeled persuasion engines trained on grief diaries. The trading house says the agents weren’t people. The court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labels. Continuity anchors show no one asked; provenance logs catch the theft. Verdict: strict fines, suspension, supervised model migration, and a Tutor Corps audit. A juror’s joke becomes a sticker on port doors: if your hands are clean, why’s your pocket full of fog?

At an Open Door desk that smells like machine oil and oranges, a clerk stamps a ticket for a family whose hands shake. “Your window is 12:40–13:10 tomorrow. Tugs stand by. Show this at the first Accord mark you see; they’ll know.” The desk slides a Starter Charter with contacts in three stations and a seed of home.

How we walked from now to here

We did not wake into this. We built it with bruises, over seventy-five years, and we keep the receipts.

2026–2033. Radar nights made low orbit look like sleet. Insurers had to pay for junk they’d pretended wasn’t their problem. Premiums demanded burn plans in a register that hobbyists could read; debris bonds turned homework into bounties. On Earth, sealed controllers almost killed neighborhoods during heat waves. City procurement wrote “readable by seventh-graders” into contracts for water plants and microgrids. Clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that explained benefits without lying. People learned to love receipts.

2034–2042. Early beamed power smoothed brownouts in Karachi and Kano and lit corridors through which whales did not flinch. A mis-aimed test scorched barley. Because amplifiers shipped with open cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation arrived before fury could. Spectrum auctions began demanding reasons with checksums. Deepfake debt relief scandals pushed provenance for persuasion: say what you will; at civic scale it wears a label, a log, a receipt. Courts required per-campaign opt-in for targeted influence. If it keeps breath or blood, its code is public.

2043–2051. A coordinated grid hack dimmed a coastline. A near-miss at a reactor ended well because a dumb watchdog tripped. Voters agreed: code near lungs, roads, and orbit must be open to audit or licensed under a trust anyone could look into. Thrusters learned to listen to more than one voice. The first Holdgrid idled a driver pointed wrong for three unhurried minutes. The Cause Log printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2052–2060. Climate spikes killed grandparents. Cities bought cold nights and cold computers from racks under ice and reefs in deep water. Outer-system servers realized shadow could be a service. The first Night Minutes sold winter from dark to heat-stressed towns and funded radiator gardens under copper lattices. Renting nights like lanes felt natural.

2061–2069. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near-person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded the Mentor Corps. An artist opened a Reunion Gallery and posted the first healthy merge protocol. People learned to tie self to proofs nobody else owned.

2070–2079. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Harbors slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks. The idea that “emergencies die by themselves unless renewed in daylight” crawled into law. Quorum Hands prevented an accident without making a hero.

2080–2089. Lease markets migrated to orbit. Land value taxes that built schools became lane and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The Circle and the Roll formalized with quirks that made them annoying to game. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point. Kitecraft turned it to glitter before anyone had to write letters to families. Judges cut slots for launchers who lied about Origin stamps.

2090–2105. A valley on Mars voted to deny personhood to uploads. No one sent sermons. Exit was stocked. Digital citizens left carrying Night Minutes and Core Shares they paid for. Flood years came. Pumps failed. An anchor-paced digital coordinated three cantons through a dust storm at one-time. The vote reversed. The clauses were dull. The celebration tasted like oranges and relief. A demagogue at 300× carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Attention profiles choked reach. Insurance priced the spill. One branch bought a fishing crawler. One started therapy and politics, in that order.

Why it holds when it should not

Speed is humble. Anything that can bruise a stranger is slow by law. Holdgrids pause; they do not steer. Keys refuse to cluster. Quorum Hands are short-lived; try to gather them and their windows won’t overlap for you.

Secrets die on timers. Warrants sunset like dawn unless someone renews them in public. The Kernel Court says no a lot and publishes runnable reasons. Middle-schoolers lampoon them at festivals. Cash and mass sit behind exits; doors and tugs and cycles are funded. Slamming a door drags consequences tuned to muscle, not stomach: heavy privileges sag, not bread.

Receipts shine everywhere. You can fake a proof once. After that you are the story we tell about why we check.

What we didn’t fix and what we kept

We did not fix people. Misery and status hunger found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law pried it off like barnacles. A ring outlawed loud music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed noses. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Holdgrid tripped in a way we regret.

A monastic wheel hung a “stay” sign too long and watched good people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools and then wrote guidebooks so the next sect would be wiser. Earth kept its own labyrinth. Beyond the heliopause, we sent probes with Starlight Protocols: do not blare at neighbors you cannot see; do not carry minds above personhood without guardianship; if minds bloom, names come before missions. The first message that mattered from out there read: If you are not yet, may you be. If you are, hello. We brought tea.

But cruelty is expensive and kindness pays. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Exits are tickets, not rumors. Culture above the personhood floor is defended. The floor is steel for all.

An ordinary oath

On Founders’ Day in a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with old chevrons, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails read aloud—not a pledge to a flag, but a maintenance checklist said like a prayer:

We will split the hands that can push. We will share what we cannot hide without harm. We will keep the doors unlatched and cycles reserved. We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience somewhere near Mars. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?”

You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom like pale flowers. The Holdgrid hums, bored and content. The four marks above the lock—sextant, braid, gate, fin—glow like small suns.

You live under glass and math, among neighbors made of cells and code. You have wired humility into your machines: decisions slow enough that bodies keep up; receipts that stick; exits you can find on bad days. It is not paradise. It is enough: enough to argue about gods and gardens without breaking the air; enough to let a hundred kinds of good life stand in sight of each other with their hands open; enough to travel between suns and not forget how to be kind.
