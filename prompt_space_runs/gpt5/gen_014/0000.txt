The Constellation Compact

When the ferry from Hygiea noses into the clamps at Larkspur Spindle, your tongue finds salt from the kelp tanks and the sweet bite of tamarind steam. The customs hall has tall windows and too many scuffed benches. Above the inner lock, behind safety glass, hang three ceramic knots the size of a child’s palm. Every kid who grew up under the Compact can tie them blindfolded.

- Wayknot. Tap it and the kiosk prints a leaving plan you can actually use: your seat on a scheduled tug, cargo and memory allowances, a booked berth at a receiving port, the compute minutes that follow you across.

- Threadknot. Tap it and the clerk—flesh today, code tomorrow—asks if you mean to split. The panel shows where your lineage account stands, which mentors are on call, the civic cycles you’ve banked for a branch, and when a Braid House has rooms if you hope for reunion.

- Neighbor-knot. Tap it and a slate lists places within one burn day that post their rules in machine-checkable light, honor the same floors, and can explain “no” with runnable models or words you can read on a lunch break.

They’re not decorations. They wire into budgets, service schedules, and code that ordinary hands can audit. Together with some blunt machines and a culture of receipts, they anchor the Constellation Compact: a thin frame that holds domes and caravans, barges and server reefs, without scraping them into sameness.

We wrote the frame after we learned two lessons the hard way and stopped pretending otherwise. Space-time lag is constitutional; you cannot centralize the stove and keep the soup hot. And more cycles do not add virtue. If a mind can lap your thought in the time it takes to breathe, that mind needs rails that do not crush slower lives.

The Ten Plain Articles

Under everything sit rules compact enough to print on the back of a glove and strict enough to fence ambition. They compile to law and code without changing their skin.

1) No nonconsensual harm to persons. A person is any being—cell or circuit—that reports its experience over time, can understand another’s claim, and can enter reciprocal commitments.

2) Life support runs on legible code. If it keeps anyone breathing or steady, its firmware is public, logged, and reversible by trained stewards.

3) No concealed manufacture of suffering, in bodies or in simulation.

4) Decisions that bind beings at embodied tempo ratify at embodied tempo. Draft fast; bind slow.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Replicators carry Maker Marks and listen for a Silence Code adjudicated by juries.

6) Rivalrous commons—lane slots, spectrum, radiator windows, caves that hold pressure—lease through posted auctions with sunsets. The commons is paid first.

7) Children of any substrate learn three habits before algebra: set boundaries, say no, leave with help.

8) No coercive edits of minds; deep edits require dual consent from a you before and a you during.

9) The coordination substrate—the beacons that announce charters, the mesh that carries consent, the minimal guardrail that brakes catastrophe—is narrow, transparent, and nobody’s to own.

10) Exits are budgets, not sermons. Every polity escrows logistics so that at least one real door stays open.

Holds you can point at

Down a yellow corridor behind an ugly door sits the room school tours never forget. It’s ugly on purpose. Waist-high boxes with fuses you can smash, toggles under thick glass, and one recessed button labeled HOLD in letters you can read while coughing. Stopboxes live in threes wherever dumb meets dangerous—mass drivers, beam actuators, seeded replicators, city valves, reactor scrams, industrial kitchens.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can press HOLD for seven minutes when a simple public rule looks about to break: a beam slicing through a declared quiet cone without a ticket; a replicator stack desynced from its beacon; a lane violation pushing a docking ring into a blind. HOLD flips devices to a narrow band and shouts its cause into the Ledger of Cause—what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. To keep a hold past seven minutes, three concurrences must turn: a local panel drawn by lot among those who logged civic hours this year, a remote auditor on hardware the locals cannot touch, and a digital citizen drafted without application. Keys expire quickly. Logs publish after a delay so redactions can be reviewed. Overreach lives in the Lamp Docket that ruins reputations faster than trials.

Over all of this drifts the Holdgrid. It does not plan, tax, or command. It is a brake. It sleeps where we disagree and wakes when simple inputs cross thresholds everyone accepted years ago: untagged spores in a cargo hatch, a velocity vector on course to crater a dome, a cluster spawning without a Maker Mark. It persuades before it pauses. When it must pause, it does so under Tri-Seals: distributed signatures sharded across worlds and substrates so no hand can steady itself on a bright lever without neighbors’ consent. It ships two proofs of action: a cryptographic attestation and a short narrative a dockhand can read on break. It is the only networked intelligence permitted near actuators; it has exactly one gesture—stop—and it times out like a kitchen timer.

The Sky-Ledger you can check with a kitchen telescope

You do not have to trust a portmaster’s slate. You can look up and check the sky. The Sky-Ledger is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident ledger that posts where things are, when beams will cross, which windows are quiet, how much compute sits in which reef, who pays what to lease radiator time, and the declared concurrency of digital citizens engaged in regulated acts. It is pegged to physics. With patience, a child, a pointer, and a school telescope can verify that a scheduled burn happened.

- Beamed power and mirrors sign their contexts with ephemeral keys bound to audited hardware. If your beam steals someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond. Corridor drift posts a fee in hours, not months.

- Quiet cones sit over telescopes, Lyceums—groves of servers signing their proofs—and neighborhoods that have bought peace with law. Quiet is public in decibels and angles; when you pierce a cone for need, you post collateral and a reason you can read aloud.

- The Thermal Ledger under the Sky-Ledger tracks three honest dull things: energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instances. Lie and radiator auctions pinch you until you stop lying.

- Stewardship lapses when idle. If you pin an asteroid, you show beacons, surveys, cleanup orbits. Sit too long without useful work and the lease returns to market. Indigenous life is sanctuary by default.

Two tempos, one exchange

Near heat and gravity are the Hearth Belts: Earth’s stubborn deltas and hills, the Moon’s tunnels, Martian valleys where orchards tame rust, cylinders that smell like mint and cut pine. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route.

Out in shadow hang the Cold Reefs: compute barges in Neptune’s lee, Trojans where data swims like whales, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts and cold minutes and jitter are currencies, and digital polities keep tempo like musicians.

Between them runs the Quiet-for-Cold Exchange. Hearth places carve quiet cones in the sky measured in decibels and degrees, limit persuasion under posted zones, and honor slow streets. Reefs deliver cold minutes, climate models, resilient legal audits that run on a cheap terminal, and art that braids tempos without making slow neighbors feel small. The price of cold at Uranus against a forecast for heat in Lagos is a line in the Sky-Ledger any aunt can check.

Governance without a capital

You cannot make one chamber speak for all hulls and all minds. The Compact breaks its talking into circles that check each other without brunch.

- The Orbit Assembly seats polities with hulls and atmospheres—cylinders, domes, barges—with representation roughly proportional to long-term residents. It tends lanes and infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions.

- The Sentience Council seats citizens directly in proportion to recognized persons, singularly franchised across substrates. It tends the status and safety of minds: personhood standards, minimum civic cycles, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, and attention zoning that protects streets where children walk unhooked.

- The Kernel Bench is a rotating cohort of engineers, jurists, and process auditors. It says valid or not on changes to cryptographic backbones, Holdgrid firmware, Maker Mark standards, and guardrail interfaces. Members serve once in a long duty cycle, meetings are open or publish logs after stated delays, and then members go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade.

Neither ring can push the other out of its chair. Anything that touches guardrails or floors needs supermajorities in both rings and a cooling window long enough for slow-tempo citizens to answer. If the Orbit Assembly redraws beam corridors that would bulldoze a local culture, the Council can send it back with a floor violation. If the Council tightens personhood so fast the groves cannot find their nights, the Assembly can answer: not until quiet is bought.

Charters, tags, and doors

Places choose charters from a library of receipts instead of gloss. Budget and dispute templates come with working code and plain reasons. Charters carry tags so strangers know how to behave in your streets: Still Ward, Talk Quarter, Spark Market. Amendments happen only while exits work and beacons lie to no one.

The Open Gate Protocol anchors exit. Any person—embodied or digital—can request departure through their Beacon. The protocol triggers a veto check under air gap: your polity must either let you go or prove, without gossip, that a due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify the proof. Doors open by code; care obligations travel with the person.

Every registered hatch displays a Gateband: three slim light-stripes wired to public keys. Green glows when an unblocked exit is actually reachable and budgets are funded. Amber pulses when you declare a fork or merger: the Lineage line links you to Braid Houses and mentors. Blue shines when your charter is current with neighbors that agree to floors and publish charters a machine can check and a child can read.

Markets where your grandmother can check the math

Anything rivalrous that someone else might need and you could sit on out of spite leases, it is not held forever. Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Windows: cave mouths, crater rims, deltas, nickel seams, shade bands.

- Lane Windows: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Band Windows: spectrum and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Core Windows: compute time tied to hardware identity and verifiable heat sinks.

- Simspace Windows: sealed worlds that run on paid cores and label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Orders from the Lanes Guild—pilots, schedulers, auditors, judges—ship as Runnable Orders: minimal-model simulations any two audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc S-9 for four hours because two burns at Vesta would have pushed your berth into a comms dead zone” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum your niece can check at a library terminal.

The Clearing Union nets money. The unit, the Helia, is composite: a kilowatt-hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus a compute-second on a posted architecture. Helias demur gently over long periods. Hoarding costs; timely work pays. A slice of auctions funds a dignity stipend that follows recognized persons independent of substrate and a local share that pays those who keep value real: the tug that keeps debris clear, the algae that cools streets, the cold lattice that bleeds heat to black.

Neighborhoods for the mind

Not every square wants a midnight carnival. Attention is zoned with legal teeth.

- Still Wards: persuasion by topic only, no fast models without a license akin to a vehicle, no targeted campaigns. Children and sensors can stroll here unafraid.

- Talk Quarters: bounded debates with public rate limits. Audience models live as municipal cooperatives you can inspect. Offers behave like buskers.

- Spark Markets: the roar on purpose under registry, levers declared, timers enforced by your own Levers Profile.

Citizens and charters carry Levers Profiles: machine-enforceable settings that say who may aim what at whom, when. Break a profile and you pay, in money first and in bandwidth after. The Reason Registry keeps an open shelf where persuasion that hopes to change policy must file its homework—plain claims, escrowed models, error bars someone can check.

Digital neighbors

Recognition is not worship of hardware. It is a Standing Review that asks for five things:

- Continuity you can prove without voyeurs: an autobiographical thread that survives restarts and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- Reciprocal modeling: the ability to represent other minds at the level you demand they represent you.

- Consent competence: you can read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

- Generalizable reasons: an ability to explain choices across contexts, not merely parrot patterns.

- Moral salience: signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter enough to command protection.

Panels are mixed: human psychologists, digital citizens, narrow routines trained to detect simulacra, lay judges with long memories. Doubts about duties pause; doubts about protections err generous. You can win provisional standing and revisit.

Rights follow, textured to substrate and clock:

- Integrity of the inside. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot force raw introspection. Backups are yours. Unauthorized deletion is murder. Unauthorized copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles needed to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs on a Common Beat. Fast clusters draft in hours; ratification happens slowly—days for a habitat, weeks for a federation. Penalties for deception tick in your subjective time.

- Sanctuary and migration. You may halt, be archived, and migrate to compatible racks; you may summon a neutral custodian.

- Hardware sovereignty. You choose where you run. No one may compel you onto hardware with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Association and exit. You can join, fork, merge, and leave, subject to safety codes like vehicle law.

Duties scale with externality:

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuces alive is a credit.

- Concurrency declaration. In elections and regulated markets, you declare speed and active instances. Votes bind to a singular franchise per Strand; deals void if you hide speed.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed unbounded replication outside audited boxes. Your tools carry beacons and answer Silence Codes when Stopboxes call.

- Provenance. Major external acts carry a private log that can prove compliance without baring thought.

Forking and merging are handled like families, not gimmicks. Your public identity follows a Strand across pauses and forks. A Strand holds one civic franchise. When you branch, you file a notice with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose, a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if reunion is contemplated. One branch keeps the civic anchor immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy. New branches inherit enough to live with dignity and owe a declining branch dividend to their progenitor.

Merging, if it happens, happens in Braid Houses. Tea is served because tea slows hurt. Engineers and therapists sit together. Memory graphs run through cryptographic interpreters that verify value alignment without tearing pages from minds. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, we say branch-grief and put the kettle back on.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Skykeepers are the only cross-world actuators. Their mandate is narrow: contain kinetic and replicative threats local Stopboxes cannot manage. They are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited by schools, unions, and hobby clubs. Every action requires Tri-Seals from randomly drawn juries across polities and posts a narrative to the Ledger of Cause.

- The Itinerant Court rides a flotilla with gardens and classrooms. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts trigger contracts that seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Windows, and as a last resort lock hazardous devices.

- The Citizens’ Loom is a permanent assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by substrate and speed. Arguments ride a polylog protocol: claims are cryptographically committed, prediction wagers travel alongside, and participants’ calibration histories are public. The Loom interprets floors for new edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Beacons.

- The Drift Synod is a rolling council that meets on calendars true to orbital mechanics. It tends shared commons—debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that sunset unless renewed.

Sanctions that pinch in physics

We do not send sermons or marines. We make bad behavior boring and expensive. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, or refuse to post reasons, and your radiator windows go dark, your lane priority drops, your Core Windows idle. In a world where heat costs and brakes are expensive, throttles teach. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers. The Sky-Ledger shows who is keeping receipts and who isn’t.

Scenes you can taste

At Larkspur customs, two arrivals clear. One laughs and leaves graphite smudges when she signs. One is a polished chassis with a ribbon of cobalt where a mouth might be. The gate prints small strips under each name: tempo; lease entitlements; declared instances; guardian on file; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On a Martian morning, Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow while dust-light dances on glass. Her Counsel—every citizen has one, paid from a fund no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments two days next month. That afternoon she sits on a jury in a long room with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. The bill would cap municipal Core Shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She votes yes and sends him a picture of a seed stamped: exits funded.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 150× leases a rib of cold reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from crooked recordings and charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but cheer a hero who errs and mends it.

On Phobos, in Braid House Nine—a place half teahouse, half server room—two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Still tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A proxy demagogue running at 300× floods a Spark with songs designed to induce smallness and fatigue. Levers Profiles throttle reach. Neighbors watch persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch goes fishing in a rented body. One enters therapy and learns to breathe slower. One begins to make sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughs and buys a piece.

Twice a Crimson Room—labs where dangerous research lives under oaths—went to zero. Once a false bell taught us to write better sensors. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

Earth keeps a special compact

Earth writes its own law. The rest of us help under rules: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power stays in mapped corridors and pays a premium into city shade and land rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, desert, fog—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black sky can do the opposite. Leaving is a right from every soil.

How we got here from here

It was not grace. It was a stack of dull fixes that held and got copied.

2026–2033. A rash of debris collisions kept children inside and soured insurers. Launches had to publish ephemerides; rendezvous corridors became posted schedules with deorbit bonds. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” the rule for life support. Early AI persuasion scandals made provenance mandatory: if a model tried to change your mind, it had to carry its homework. Courts began granting narrow agency to digital systems when continuity held up over seasons. Guardians—personal AIs with vetoes—became boringly normal.

2033–2045. Beamed power left papers. Environmental coalitions held out for “corridors before commercialization”: map safe lanes first, then money. A demo scorched cropland; supplier logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared fast. Ports adopted compulsion ledgers—dreary at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2045–2057. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a heat meter you could read with a finger. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of civic cycles and a Counsel that would not act without a yes and could be shut off. Early uploads were brittle, but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and commitments held.

2057–2069. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver sang by a habitat, Stopboxes became law: narrow seven-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. That compromise hardened. Insurers made them standard. Two public saves closed arguments.

2069–2081. The twin rings replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Gossamer Net flew, misted a wayward slug into glitter, and came home to be opened by children with oranges. Personhood law found language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. The Common Beat for civic time took hold. Attention zoning—Still, Talk, Spark—grew from municipal quiet hours that saved sleep and elections.

2081–2089. The first attempt to swing a vote with a cloud of clones met singular franchises, Junction Courts, and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2089–2098. The Quiet-for-Cold Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought quiet cones. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Crimson Rooms got kill-lines and oaths. The Holdgrid refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.”

2098–2110. The Lamp Docket found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt a Gateband ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup. The Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Preventing tyranny without saints

Authority lives local by default. Cross-border moves are slow unless safety trips. We design with delay. Emergencies fizzle back to ordinary when timers roll back and someone must say in public why they shouldn’t.

We split keys and wrote laws that demand receipts. Compulsions log themselves into our gossip. Federations around pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire with seasons and drills. Secession is practice. We fund exits the way old towns funded fire brigades. Beacons stay honest. Doors stay oiled. Tug crews sleep in their boots. Code polities escrow cores and storage for Exit by Branch. The Sky-Ledger audits; stewards run surprise drills; kids can tell their parents where to go and why this room has the thickest doors, not the shiniest views.

When a charter welds exits or sweetens sorrow until leaving tastes like poison, three rings tighten. Auditors post proof: unbolt by the 14th, publish Tutor lines, return leases to market. Protocol throttles: beacons dim, radiator auctions go cold, lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds and bread and Counsel paid out of a fund with a name as dull as its values are bright. Trade halts. Core Windows idle. The offender breathes its own stale air until habit fails.

When it breaks and mends

In a cylinder above Quito, a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient and made the trains sing. Six months later, midnight decrees. The Holdgrid refused a risky “optimization” of air mixers. The Ledger of Cause drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of Beacon pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Green Gateband lit brighter. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two keys and later took a narrower role with grace. Her friends still bring her a pastry shaped like a chair because jokes heal.

In the high belt, a miner tried to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage fight. The Gossamer Nets turned it into glitter that made aurora over Ceres. The miner’s insurer evaporated and reformed without the managers who’d called Maker Marks “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for manifests hiding Maker Marks. A hockey team rebranded to the Nets and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

In a Scarlet Lab Bay, the Holdgrid dropped power and flooded a hall with nitrogen. It was a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeves, taught the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept anyone from dying.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet at Rim Market sizzles sesame and scallions. At nine, the Reason Registry pings her Counsel: add forty-millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp edge-case cascades. Provenance is clean: diagrams, stories, mild humor, no grief levers. Lina taps “sleep until tomorrow,” thinks at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a Warden jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when they speak from a puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Still Ward, a digital poet in a wooden puppet sips broth with their human wife. The poet’s main self runs near Neptune, stitching a drowned delta’s library back together from aunties’ stories and smudged ledgers. They throttle down to share soup. After, twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra and declines politely to speed up “just to keep up.”

At dusk, the canopy flips violet. The Lamp Docket plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds too long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy palms a toy Net-craft fin and asks if he can sign up. Lina says when you are fifteen—and can explain HOLD without notes.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to shift tempo—the relief of a Stopbox snapping a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river on Earth choosing which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later when the cartoons have been filed off.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Larkspur Spindle, the three knots blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Way. Thread. Neighbor. Not a spell. A habit of glass and budgets. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because delay is honored, exits shine, and no one can be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
