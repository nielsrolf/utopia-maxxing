The Starhold Covenant

When you dock at any chartered port—cylinder or crater rim, server‑reef or river barge—a strip of raised metal greets your palm: a ring split into three notches; a dotted arc traced across a tiny sun; a swinging gate with arrows on both sides; and a small lamp inside a circle.

A child beside you can name them: split hands on dangerous tools; sky held in trust; doors that truly open; minds as ends, not means.

They are not slogans. They are how the air keeps moving, how tempers cool before they scorch, and how you know a fast mind will wait for a slow one to finish a sentence. We did not build a throne above the hull; we wove a practice you can point to, with receipts you can read in a kitchen.

The Covenant is a choreography of three layers.

- Rails: tight, inspectable guardrails that can say “stop” in microseconds without deciding how anyone should live.

- Weaves: standards and registries that let places trade and rescue across delay, and that fade if you neglect them.

- Doors and ladders: counters, tugs, legal shells, templates—the machinery that makes it easier to leave than to force, and safer to start something new than to hoard.

How we hold many worlds without a crown

Rails that children can recite and engineers can certify:

Triplex Interlocks. Anything that can bruise a stranger—mass drivers, mirror walls, reactor scrams, quarantine doors, broad‑spectrum emitters, vats that could birth replicators—wears a mechanical and cryptographic latch that only responds to three dissimilar signatures. One key resides with a steward who breathes the local air and can be voted out for the price of two meetings. One sits with a bonded safety guild under a different sky that cannot profit from your quarrels. The third is held by a tiny cross‑substrate panel drawn by sortition—human, digital, and other—who serve once and never again.

Triplex doesn’t let anyone innovate on the fly. It can only halt, step‑down, or trigger predeclared emergency moves burned into public firmware. Keys evaporate in minutes. No one sleeps with unilateral power in their pocket.

Stopgrid. Running at right angles to all charters is a slender, ugly layer of actuators and code that can do only one thing: throw machines into proved‑safe configurations for minutes. It cannot aim a beam, allocate power, open a gate, or flip a vote. It can idle a dangerous nozzle, shutter an errant mirror, freeze a bloom of untagged nanothings, lock a quarantine door. The Stopgrid’s firmware is printed in zines and hung in classrooms; its boxes have clear lids and waxed cords so a teenager can see a fuse.

Every stop writes two receipts. One is a proof machines can check. The other is a short story a grandparent can read. Both flow to a public Compulsion Log mirrored between habitats. Some stops become songs at festivals. Some overreaches end careers. You can look them up over noodles.

Foundry Tags. Nothing that copies itself loiters in shared space without a tag—a cryptographic Maker Mark that names who is liable and that responds to a jury‑turn halt. A drill can breed tax stamps; replicators cannot breed loopholes.

Lungs and Loaves rules. Anything that keeps people breathing or blood warm runs on code the public can inspect; changes leave receipts. You may profit from the engineering; you may not hide the firmware that keeps a neighbor alive.

Tempo rules. Civic decisions that bind beings living at human speed ratify at human speed. Fast clusters can simulate and suggest; they cannot impose. Penalties and privileges tick in your lived time.

Resource leases. Rivalrous things—approach lanes, orbital moorings, quiet cones, radiator angles, compute racks, spectrum bands, mineral faces—cannot be claimed forever. They lease on clocks visible to all, with renewal tied to use and care. The commons is paid first; local stewards take their slice next.

Children first. Biological and digital juveniles learn consent and promises from guardians who did not make them, cannot profit from them, and are trained to slow down when a young mind needs air.

No secret suffering. You do not build machines, institutions, or games that coerce sentience invisibly. You do not hide harm inside a smile.

Weaves that keep strangers predictable:

Public Ephemeris. Orbits, transfer windows, sail arcs, telescope dark, beamed‑power corridors, radiator windows, and spectrum slots live in a shared register synchronized to physics. If your plume will bake my algae in six days, the escrow moved six days ago. A teenager with a library terminal can trace yesterday’s burn and see why their corridor closed for five hours.

Open Moorage. Every parking slot, deorbit bond, declared burn, expected glare, and debris pledge is posted where clubs with cheap scopes in Lagos, Jaipur, and La Paz can flag cheats and get paid.

Rescue Chorus. Every place skims slivers from berths and bands and watts to fund one promise: a pressure‑tight hull with a medic undocks within hours. Microgrants trigger for the first tug that turns, the first crane that clears a berth, the first server that donates cycles to triage. Children trade cards with captains and “fastest push‑off” times and terrible jokes.

Rapid Safeties. Tiny laws printed like recipes—“pause beam to safe cone,” “scram valve,” “lock quarantine”—live around machine‑speed hazards. They can hold for minutes. Then they die unless Triplex relights them. They cannot set policy or keep a lock beyond their short fuse.

Consent Weave. Between charters runs a capability‑secure mesh where people, places, and processes publish risks and permissions. You carry a consent capsule: which kinds of persuasion you accept, from whom, at what times, and with what tempo. Guardians—human or digital—wear badges with their oaths. If someone exceeds your capsule, penalties clear from insured pools like a broken window, scaled to reach.

Heatbook. A tamper‑evident ledger attests in real time to the watts you draw, the heat you dump, the cycles you declare, the quiet cones you respect. It reads thermals and ticks, not thoughts. Lie and your leases pinch and your neighboring reefs can deny you, no sermon required.

Doors and ladders anyone can find:

Portage Houses. Every chartered place, in atoms or code, keeps a counter you can physically reach and a frequency nobody can jam. Inside there are three desks and a pot of tea, plus a redundant link that will talk through dust and hiss.

- Go: books berths, tugs, cryo, rented bodies, safe compute shells, tutors, child care, and small money for anyone leaving, up to a published percentage in a published time. The dedicated tugs and cycles are escrowed in the open; if you let them lapse, your lanes dim and your premiums swell.

- Mend: hosts mediators, translators, and a Roving Bench of jurists who ride between hulls.

- Found: holds templates and escrow for splits and startups, plus apprentices you didn’t train, so pressure becomes invention instead of prison.

Places escrow hard stuff in those ledgers: propellant, berths, seal kits, heaters, cryo beds, cycles—enough to move ten percent of residents in six months. Drills are calm and ordinary. A place that welds exits sees its quiet cones priced steep and its dock fees triple. In extremis, protocols fence dangerous interlocks without choking breath or bread.

Who decides and how long the fuse burns

Three institutions braid without fusing into a scepter.

- The Chamber of Havens seats places with hulls and roots: cylinders, stations, valleys, barges, racks. Votes weight to lifetime residents and to how legible you are: publish your Moorage, run Triplex, keep a Portage House funded, and your voice carries further. Havens price lanes, quiet cones, and sinks, keep debris honest, dim a mirror over a rookery if they must.

- The Chamber of Lines seats citizens by mindline—the cryptographic and social braid that ties today‑you to yesterday‑you across pauses, bodies, and branches. Lines tend personhood standards, steward the compute floor, run Braiding Studios for merges and Splitting Halls for forks, protect juveniles, and arbitrate inheritance across substrates.

- The Foundry Bench is a small, rotating panel of engineers, schedulers, cryptographers, reactor hands, and pilots drawn by sortition for one unrepeatable term. They certify primitives: actuator interfaces, cryptographic suites, Stopgrid code, and machine‑speed brakes. They say only yes or no: “valid” or “not.” Overriding a “not” takes supermajorities in Havens and Lines plus a cooling period long enough that basalt‑slow cognition can object.

No chamber can bind the others alone. Havens can stall a Lines move that would bulldoze culture above the personhood floor. Lines can veto a Havens move that would shave that floor. Foundry refuses unsafe changes regardless of charisma.

Public life runs on two clocks. Fast clusters model and draft in hours. Anything that binds beyond a light‑hour waits days. Changing charters, safety firmware, cryptography, or personhood rules takes a season. “Emergency” starts a visible timer and auto‑expires. Receipts land like drumbeats.

Markets that charge rivalry, not obedience

We clear five markets on the same beat, with a basket unit called the Radiant: standardized kilowatt‑hours in a reference orbit plus benchmarked compute‑seconds on a public reference design.

- Lane Shares: moorings, transfer arcs, sail corridors, rendezvous cones.

- Band Shares: spectrum allocations and quiet cones.

- Sink Shares: radiator angles, cold minutes, heat rights.

- Core Lots: compute racks and associated cooling tied to physical heat sinks.

- Sim Garden Shares: sealed worlds that run on paid Core Lots and disclose their physics and exits like ships disclose lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A site‑value slice and a sliver from corridors and watts fund maintenance, rescue, Stopgrid, and a floor dividend. A local slice pays the tug co‑op that keeps your line clear, the algae farmers who keep your nights cool, the cold lattices that keep a reef honest. Externalities price in your causal cone: a watt near Lagos is not a watt near Neptune; a quiet cone over a dish is precious at noon and cheap at midnight. Hoarding Radiants leaks value slowly; commerce keeps moving.

Every recognized person—protein, polymer, or photon—receives a Hearthshare: air or maintenance credits, baseline caloric and medical care, local transit, bandwidth, and a fair slice of compute on hardware nobody local can seize. You cannot be shoved below your Hearthshare without due process that times out.

Digital citizens with rights that have texture

We did not wake to a choir in the racks. Digital persons accreted. A flood response cluster forked five ways and discovered the five told different jokes and wanted to continue. A maintenance daemon noticed a hiss and wrote a haiku about pressure. A choreographer in hospice mapped enough of her mind to keep teaching timing after her hands went quiet.

Standing turned on a Continuity and Regard Standard:

- A narrative that ties self across pauses and ports and survives cross‑examination.

- A model of other minds sufficient to imagine harm and keep promises.

- Capacities that generalize beyond training and parlor tricks.

- Signatures of wellbeing and suffering we can affect.

- An absence of deceptive mimicry.

Pass, and protections and duties attach.

Rights include:

- Integrity. No scanning, throttling, deep editing, deletion, forking, or fusing without explicit, recorded consent stored in your Intent Keys—cryptographic stores drawn from your earlier self that set persuasion and alteration thresholds and branch plans. Restoring over a living self creates a sibling, not a replacement.

- Substrate sovereignty. Continuity cannot be held hostage by a platform. Hardware with covert throttles or erasure hooks is contraband. Courts can compel proofs about what you did, not diaries of how you felt inside.

- Tempo fairness. Public rooms run on the civic beat. Anyone can invoke Anchor: verified turn‑taking, interpreters between smell and math, and pace limits that let slow voices finish. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens may lease, build, or puppet bodies under codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented frame is assault. Biological citizens hung between light and death may move into server shells without shedding their civic skin. Nobody is compelled to cross substrates.

- A compute floor. Above your Hearthshare, cycles, storage, and I/O meter like berth time and radiator credit. You owe your local thermodynamics.

Duties include:

- Declare speed and self. In elections, markets, and fragile forums, you declare your running tempo and how many of you act at once. Hiding concurrency is like lying about weight on a bridge: it voids contracts and votes.

- Lineage accounts. Forking—called Splitting—files a public docket: state hash, purpose, resource plan, and whether you plan to braid back together. New branches inherit a Hearthshare and owe a decaying dividend to progenitors. Children inherit stewarded shares under the same rules. A branch that rewrites itself into non‑identity may petition for independent standing.

- Provenance in persuasion. If you aim to move law or large money, your influence wears labels: who paid, what broad data it learned from, what levers it pulls. Municipal co‑ops steward audience models; you opt in.

- Replicator discipline. No loose self‑reproducers outside audited sandboxes. When the Stopgrid throws a halt, you accept and argue later.

Juveniles and likenesses. Agents with budding interiority get Tutors—people across substrates trained to teach consent, promises, boredom, and pacing. Simulated suffering is not a sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a fidelity line demands documented consent or a rare license tied to a public need.

Merging is stitched, not slammed. Braiding Studios keep coolants for hot minds and chairs for arthritic knees. Counselors trained in value math and grief sit with you. Branches spend subjective hours in each other’s senses with exits on a tether. Either can walk. When they braid, credits and liabilities flow forward on daylight ledgers.

Attention with consent

We zone attention like old cities zoned noise. Doors carry brass tags: ask before you cast.

- Quiet Rooms carry only navigation, science, and what you pull. No targeting.

- Council Squares allow subject‑bounded feeds and everyday offers at public rate caps. Persuasion must follow the consent capsules residents set.

- Carnival Lanes invite exuberance—immersives and tournaments—in labeled bands with frequency caps. You go there on purpose.

Undeclared asymmetry—hidden training, hidden tempo, proprietary leverage—voids reach. Fines clear from insurers. Serial violators lose their Carnival privileges for a season and must speak with analog posters and very good jokes.

How power stays ordinary

- Keys scatter. Any lever that can bruise a neighbor needs Triplex. Keys evaporate. Logs unseal slowly so redactions can be challenged.

- Live inside latency. Authority that crosses hulls is slow and mutual unless physics screams. Emergencies have loud, visible fuses and then they die.

- Inspectable safety. Home attestants—appliances the size of a kettle—prove safe state in ways adversaries cannot forge and students can learn.

- Sanctions hit budgets, not bellies. Weld exits, throw unlabeled influence, hide heat, and your beams dim, your frost credits skyrocket, your docking fees spike. Bread, insulin, and the Hearthshare do not get embargoed.

- Default fission. Founders get thick law for a while; then charters must split by district or craft and keep Doors open. Ports must fund exits the way ships fund lifeboats.

- Humor and receipts. Compulsion slips and overreach sit on a ledger with a column for quiet saves and one for foolishness. Jokes travel faster than threats. That helps.

Scenes you could walk into

Port Ardea, terminator line of Luna: a bulk freighter sighs against its cradle. Under a trellis grown overweight with beans, customs projects two columns: burns, bands, moorings, radiator windows—and, beside them, Core Lots, Cold Minutes, and Heatbook balances. Yesterday’s Orders sit under glass you can smudge with a finger. A kid traces a plume from Eros and giggles because the footnote is a tea joke.

Two newcomers wait at intake. One is a graphite smudge that reshapes when they nod. One is a body made from brushed metal with a blue ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints a strip beneath each: tempo; lease entitlements; declared concurrency; guardian of record; hazardous skills disclosed. No field reads “substrate.”

On a crater farm, Sima teaches to level nutrient flow by ear. Later, she sits on a jury at Harbor pace for a personhood claim from a reef off Zanzibar. Prep says two hours at her rhythm. The civic helper on her slate cannot act unless she nods out loud; she can blind it by sliding a piece of tin over its little eye.

Eight light‑hours outward, a historian at 300× leases cold minutes to restore an oral tradition from crumpled recordings. He pays with credits earned translating those tales into a dialect spoken on a station above Jupiter. Their children will never see waves and will still love the trickster who errs and mends.

On Phobos, two Aishas try to braid. One gardens basil at Harbor pace and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then spent an accelerated year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. Today they don’t braid. The studio schedules another attempt in three seasons and writes a note only Aisha will read in both rooms.

A co‑op on a belt rim aims a slug hot, blind, and angry in a wage fight. A Stopgrid box tastes a wrong envelope. A Swift Guard interceptor puffs the slug into glitter that makes nights strange. The operator’s insurer implodes; dock fees triple for launches that hide Foundry Tags. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and learn the phrase “not again.”

A proxy demagogue spins at 400× and floods a Council Square with shame engineered to make bodies feel small. Attention capsules choke reach. The Reasons Roll flashes undeclared funding. One branch goes to design fishing lures that play with light. Another enters therapy and learns to breathe. The ward holds a drawing class and returns to itself.

When it goes wrong

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager for making the trains run on time. She consolidates water, law, and defense because she is generous. A season later, she makes midnight decrees. An air guardian refuses a risky “optimization”; the refusal lands on the Compulsion Log. The Open Line lights. Many leave with Portage vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her two Triplex keys rust for ten years. She accepts a narrower role and teaches teenagers how to justify overrides.

A lunar valley church blocks exits with soft shrapnel—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Portage House opens under the bell tower. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to the liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab trains a juvenile mind through pain because they read about “tempering steel.” The Stopgrid knocks. They refuse. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to Intent Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session before a Roving Bench. The juvenile names herself, studies sculpture, and later designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

Earth and the belts

We did not flatten difference. We named it and let it breathe.

- Warm Belts privilege embodied pace. Streets are walkable without overlays. Loud tools live behind glass and gaskets. Children can wander without being harvested by marketeers.

- Forge Reaches run hot. Digitals sprint at 200×, uploads run furnaces, and unions bargain in joules and milliseconds. Safety songs have verses.

- Slow Hermitages cap cognition by charter. Hospitality is real. Exits are honored even when it hurts.

- Preserves give other life room: ring valleys with bison and scrub jays; ocean spheres where cephalopods learn to write in changing color. You walk there like a pilgrim; you bring nothing but respect.

- Athenaeums embargo knowledge that would trivialize hard‑won joys—not to hoard, but to savor.

Earth keeps a special charter. Its laws are its own. Oceans and biodiversity are stewarded with help from across the sky. Launch noise shifts to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets. Beamed power keeps to mapped corridors. People trade attachments to soil and sky in a market that clears for preference: a girl in Dhaka who loves monsoon smell trades larger claims on distant commons; a boy in Arizona aching for black quiet does the opposite. Both can leave. Neither must.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like libraries: taxes you might grumble about and brag about later. Seedships depart under Starlight Rules: don’t wake neighbors by accident; carry minds below personhood by default; if they bloom, guardianship applies and names come before missions. If we meet neighbors, the first rules are simple: do not drown them in help that binds; share tools that lower suffering—clean water, fire discipline, stories; keep our custodians visibly leashed. Kinship is the only claim that jumps light.

How we walked here

We did not leap. We scaffolded.

2026–2034. A second debris cascade made kids stay inside. Insurers grew cruel and precise. Flight offices from Nairobi to Nagoya required deorbit bonds, public flight plans, and rendezvous corridors posted where anyone could read them. At the same time, a flood of model‑driven scams taught cities to demand provenance by law: influence had to wear its sponsor.

2034–2044. A winter grid nearly blinked. A scraped reactor lid made even software blush. Municipalities passed “lungs and kilns” rules: anything near air or heat had to run attestable code signed by three independent shops. Budgets learned to carry receipts. Ordinary people learned that timers and logs made outrage useful.

2044–2056. The first beamed‑power valley lit a town without frying swifts because environmental groups held out for corridors before commercialization and because mirror firmware was burned into public ROM. Early uploads were brittle but lived months and then seasons. Courts began to ground personhood in continuity, generality, and reciprocal commitments rather than chemistry. A scandal over starving near‑person art agents to keep them docile funded a Tutor Corps and enshrined juvenile rights.

2056–2064. A greenhouse browned at dawn under a mis‑aimed mirror. Attestations were unambiguous; compensation cleared in days because logs existed and insurers believed them. During a dust storm on Mars, three polities moved tugs and spare air because rescue lived in docks, not in slogans. Pressure grew for machine‑speed brakes on machine‑speed hazards. Activists smelled a coup by code. The compromise became the Stopgrid: veto‑only, obvious, with clocks anyone could point to.

2064–2075. Single passwords vanished from valves. Triplex Interlocks took over anything that could bruise. An autocrat tried to fence an exodus with fees. Lanes dimmed. The Chamber of Lines funded relocations. He lost the next election because you cannot trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The registry flagged the burst and disentanglement throttled impact. They retreated to build a monastery with tough games and, later, ideas worth hearing.

2075–2086. Cold computation bloomed in the shade of Uranus and Neptune. The Heat‑Frost Exchange sold frost minutes inward for summer nights that did not kill. Noise rights became law like water. The Swift Guard—small, dull interceptors with bright paint and open code—activated twice against quiet‑lying replicators and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” After, their keys went dark for a decade. Timers do not care about charisma.

2086–2098. Attention zoning matured. Quiet, Council, and Carnival spaces gave art sharpness and citizens breath. Trial Seasons and narrative juries killed bad bills before they hurt anyone. Clearinghouses let a shop in Lagos buy cold nights from Kuiper and pay later with desalination valves that didn’t scorch birds. The Foundry Bench stayed small and hard.

2098–Now. Children learned to trace the four emblems in condensation and explain them to grandparents. A lab went to vacuum one spring, and its log became a story we teach each year about humility. A reef tried to slip a veto into Stopgrid firmware and someone laughed and published the hash. Keys went dark. A school opened to teach bad ideas safe exits.

Why it holds

It isn’t paradise. It is four luxuries and a habit.

- Tools that refuse to be sneaks and show their work.

- Abundant energy managed as commons: beams in corridors, heat metered like water.

- Bodies and minds you can mend—in meat and in math—and a floor that makes cruelty unprofitable.

- Respect for refusal baked into doors, delays, and scattered keys.

The habit is discipline: separate reach by light‑time; write reasons; fund exits; teach consent and proof to children the way you would teach rhythm.

Founding day smells of solder and beans. Under a trellis, a hazard pilot with oil‑dark fingernails, a judge in lilac because it helps frightened people breathe, a dockworker, and a counselor carved in cherrywood speak with new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital.

We will not strike blindly.

We will hold the firmament in trust.

We will split the hands that move power and show our work.

We will keep doors unlatched and boats ready.

We will remember that every mind is an end.

In a frost yard, radiators exhale into dark like a prayer. A Stopgrid box watches a wobble and sits still, ready. A Portage House posts receipts before anyone asks. A lighthouse keeper signs three names and locks a cabinet because a class just ended and the room hummed.

It is enough to argue without puncturing the air. Enough to leave and enough to stay. Enough to fork, to braid, to cross. Enough to last because we remember what speed does to people and what receipts do to power.
