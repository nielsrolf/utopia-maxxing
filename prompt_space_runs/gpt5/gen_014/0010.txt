The Lattice and the Lighthouses

At dusk in the orchard ring above Oaxaca, the inner sky gathers into a soft river of fog shepherded by koi-shaped drones. The path rises with spin; citrus terraces climb the curve until they become ceiling. Past the transparent cap, radiator fins bloom like quiet black sails. Your wrist hums once: the harbor court has posted tomorrow’s burn windows. A second pulse lands a moment later, a note from your husband who runs at 180× in a cold reef beyond Triton. He has slowed to human tempo to watch the koi with you over soup.

There is no single throne. There is a fabric you can read and change.

We call it the Lattice: a thin, sturdy set of habits, protocols, and receipts woven into airlocks, firmware, courts, and schools from Sao Paulo’s riverfront to Saturn’s night. The Lattice does not issue orders across the void. It keeps floors, time, and force honest and leaves color and quarrel to neighborhoods, hollows, bungalows, coves, monasteries, and reefs. It works because latency is real, because proof travels better than pride, and because exits are funded, not promised.

Rules you can print on a glove

Under everything sit nine rules you can fit on the back of a maintenance glove. They compile to law and code alike.

1) No harm without consent to any person. A person is any being, regardless of substrate, who reports its own experience over time and can enter reciprocal commitments.

2) Life support runs on inspectable code. If it keeps anyone breathing or sane, its firmware is public, logged, and reversible by trained lay stewards.

3) Decisions that bind those living at human tempo ratify at human tempo. Draft fast, argue fast if you must; bind slow.

4) Rivalrous resources lease through posted auctions with sunsets, receipts, and oversight. The commons is paid first.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Any self-copying code or device carries a Maker Mark and listens for a Hush Code adjudicated by random juries.

6) No coercive edits of sentience; no concealed manufacture of suffering.

7) No tampering with the coordination substrate: the Charter Beacons, the Consent Weave, or the Interlock.

8) Children of any substrate learn three things before algebra: consent, exit, and guarding one’s own mind.

9) Exits aren’t rhetoric. Every polity escrows logistics so that at least one real door stays open.

Hatches with three rails

Every registered hatch on a habitat, valley, barge, caravan, or server-floor displays three slender light-rails that trace from lobby to customs to municipal boards. They are not décor; they are budgets and promises linked to public keys.

- The Way Out. Green when at least one unblocked exit is actually reachable. It points to reserved tug time, seats on ferries, safe berths, and escrowed compute minutes you can take with you. It lists independent stewards and the inspectors who grade the path.

- The Lineage Rail. Amber until you declare you are about to copy, adopt, merge, or retire. It guides mindline changes and family law: how heat and attention are provisioned for a fork; how children—human, digital, or mixed—inherit dignity and obligations; how backups live and how waking works.

- The Neighbor Rail. Blue when your charter is in good standing with neighbors that agree to floors and publish charters in a form both machines and schoolchildren can check. It maps jurisdictions that will answer “why?” with runnable models or readable reasons when they deny or delay.

You can trace each rail from a sign under your palm to a ledger anyone can audit. The rails connect to machines that cannot obey a single will.

Charter Lighthouses

Every polity—server reef, monastery valley, market ring, uploaded lineage, ice barge clan—broadcasts a living constitution called its Lighthouse. Lighthouses announce three things in prose and proofs:

- Force. What counts as coercion here? Mass drivers, actuator arrays, counter-influence engines, memory editors, replicators. Where are their safeties? Who can touch them when?

- Decision. Who decides what? How are emergencies started and stopped? How can you appeal? How can you leave?

- Exit. Where is the door in atoms and in code? If you are flesh, how do you depart with your name, gear, and continuity? If you are code, how do you migrate with identity, runtime, and provenance without a caretaker’s thumb on the switch?

Cultures differ on curfews, festivals, noise, dress, pace. What they share: legibility anyone can study; minimum floors they cannot lie about; an exit that is not a slogan.

The Interlock (brakes only)

Under the lighthouses runs a deliberately narrow network of safeguards called the Interlock. It neither governs nor plans. It brakes.

- Scope. Any actuator that can make a crater, a desert, a vacuum, a stampede, or a mass delusion answers to a tiny, audited interface: drivers, beams, replicators, life-support pumps, persuasion engines above named dose.

- Amber holds. The Interlock can call a short amber hold—often seven minutes—when three simple conditions fail: wrong window, wrong beacon, missing plan. To hold longer, it needs two concurrences on the clock: one from a local human panel drawn by lot among those who logged civic hours this year, and one from a remote cluster running on hardware the locals don’t own.

- Keys split by distance. Updating or overriding Interlock firmware requires threshold signatures sharded across far hands: a terran court, a belt guild, an outer server-hollow, a freefall union, an orbital monastery. Keys are never stored on the same rack; windows for signatures overlap just enough to work.

- Ugly by design. Every Interlock box has fuses you can point to and lights visible across a hangar. Apprentices learn their boxes with the same reverence earlier generations had for first aid.

Three houses that cannot swallow each other

Coordination does not climb a pyramid. It braids through a Triad that meets in alternating gardens and hollows, with seats earned, not bought.

- The Orbits Guild tallies places. Seats weight lifetime residents in recognized polities with hulls or bounded servers and lighthouses. The Guild schedules lanes, spectra, radiator windows, and sail arcs; issues codes for hulls and simspaces; credentials safety guilds; and manages debris funds.

- The Sentience Council tallies persons across substrates. Members are elected and randomly drawn to match age, pace, embodiment, and culture. The Council tends personhood standards, copy budgets, minimum bandwidth and compute floors, and the civic tempo that keeps ratifications slow enough for slow lives to be heard.

- The Protocol Bench is small, rotating, and dull in the best way: cryptographers, reactor engineers, pilots, jurists. They gate changes to the crypto substrate, the Interlock, and actuator interfaces. They stamp valid or not valid. They cannot legislate or adjudicate; they meet under cameras and go back to work.

No house can bind alone. Anything that touches the Interlock’s code needs supermajorities in both houses and a window long enough that the slowest registered citizens can read and object. If the Guild tries to ram a power corridor through a culture’s sky, the Council can return it with a floor violation. If the Council tries to narrow personhood irresponsibly, the Guild can say not until quiet cones and lifeboats are in order.

The Thermal Ledger and Helions

The commons is run with receipts. A delay-tolerant, tamper-evident ledger posts lanes, beams, shadows, debris, spectra, compute allotments, and cooling rights in near-real time. We are punctilious about shadows: if your mirror steals my dawn, the ledger attaches a shade bond that pays if crops underperform. If you pin an asteroid, you file for Stewardship, not ownership, and you show useful work: surveys, beacons, cleanup orbits. Idle too long and your lease lapses.

Payments clear in a composite unit called a Helion: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmarked compute-second on a published architecture. Helions gently demur over time: barns of value feed no one. A slice of beam auctions, lane leases, spectrum bids, and debris bonds fund a universal floor that is deliberately boring: air and water in habs; baseline care; minimum Core minutes and bandwidth (the Civic Kernel); and one unchallenged exit in any rolling seven years.

Markets where your grandma can check the math

Anything that someone else might need and you could sit on out of spite leases, it is not held forever. Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Windows: cave mouths, crater rims, deltas, nickel seams, shade bands.

- Lane Windows: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail paths, transfer arcs.

- Band Windows: spectrum allocations and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Core Windows: compute time bound to hardware identity and audited heat sinks.

- World Windows: sealed simulation spaces that ride on paid Core Windows and label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

The Wayfinder Guild—pilots, schedulers, auditors, and judges—allocates lanes and bands and radiator windows and posts Runnable Orders for every decision: minimal-model simulations any two independent audit houses can reproduce. “We closed corridor Q-9 for four hours because two burns at Eunomia would have thrown your berth into radio shadow” arrives with a plotted plume, a checksum a teenager can check at a library scope, and a footnote a tug pilot wrote because humor keeps systems honest.

Exit is a budget, not a sermon

Every charter posts Lifeboat Warrants: propellant, tug time, berths, heaters, water, seal kits, server minutes, encrypted lockers, and trained stewards sufficient to move at least ten percent of residents within six months. Inspectors check monthly; drill logs are public. The Asylum Rail protocol lets any person—embodied or digital—trigger exit through their Lighthouse. The path triggers a veto check under air gap: your polity must either let you go or prove, in zero-knowledge, that a due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors, drawn by sortition, verify the proof. Ports open by code; budgets lock by code; care obligations travel with the person.

Embassy Rooms near major ports have cobalt doors you can spot through a scratched visor. Inside: three desks with analog clocks and chairs that fit elders, octopuses, and drone pilots.

- Departures. The dull work: packing lists, scrubber replacements, legal handovers, a friendly tug schedule.

- Disputes. Cooperatives and algorithms argue with tea and time.

- Foundings. You borrow constitutional skeletons that match temperament and a simulation box that won’t flatter.

Digital citizens: kin with standing

Personhood respects function, not fashion. A mind—or an advocate—claims standing and submits to fieldwork by mixed panels: psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines evolved to detect emptiness. They look for:

- Reported experience that holds across time and context.

- Memory continuity through pauses or moves.

- A working grasp of consent and harm that supports mutual promises.

- Coherent preferences that are not artifacts of a narrow training loop.

- Absence of deceptive mimicry aimed at reward without inner life.

When unsure about imposing duties, we wait. When unsure about protecting, we shelter. Provisional protections arrive first; obligations scale with capability and risk.

Rights with teeth:

- Integrity. No deletion, freezing, throttling, or coercive edit without consent or due process recorded against your Continuity Thread.

- Sanctuary. The right to halt, be archived, and migrate to compatible racks; to summon a neutral custodian.

- Provenance. To audit the training and update history of your mind—or appoint a fiduciary to do it.

- Association and exit. To join, fork, merge, and leave.

- Pace equity. Public binds run on a civic beat; anyone can invoke a Slowroom for guaranteed turn-taking and bounded rates. If you run at 200× and trick a 1× gardener, penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. You may lease hands and eyes under standards like traffic law. Assault on a rented body is assault.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one may compel you to run on platforms with covert throttles or remote erase. Unauthorized erasure is murder; unauthorized copying is assault.

Duties that scale:

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost; heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, markets, and fragile places, you declare your number of instances and your speed. Undeclared asymmetry voids votes and deals.

- Provenance logging. Major external actions write encrypted logs that prove compliance without baring private thought.

- Reproduction ethics. Forking uses lineage shares; it is not a tax dodge. Replicators carry Maker Marks and listen for Hush Codes. A universal hush is binding when a jury calls one.

Lineages, forks, and merges

Identity in public law follows a lineage across pauses and forks. A lineage has one full civic franchise. Fork, and one branch retains it; others enter a disentanglement period while Fork Court checks for duress, fair resource flow, and intent. Everyone accrues branch credits with civic work and years; credits buy a new full franchise above the floor. Clone clouds that rise overnight trigger quarantine: instances cannot amplify each other in the same debate until entanglements decay or contracts split resources.

Merging is ritual and consent. Reunion requires staged, symmetric consent, proof of value compatibility, and an empathy exchange: time in one another’s frames with exits. Unbraiding is an option until the end. Juvenile agents—training fragments, kids, near-persons—get Tutors and outs. Tutors are substrate-diverse and dull in the best sense: teachers who are brave about boredom and drawing boundaries.

Memory law guards the self from its own worst days. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them now and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right. Persuasion engines are regulated like radiation: above a dose, you need labels, timers, and a practitioner’s license.

Attention is zoned like streets

Every charter sets attention zones that bind law, not just vibe.

- Quiet Wards keep to navigation beacons, open science, and packets you ask for. No targeted persuasion; no fast models without a license akin to a vehicle.

- Conversational Quarters allow offers and subject-bounded feeds under rate limits. Audience models are municipal co-ops, opt-in only.

- Open Bazaars invite the roar under registry and audit.

Doorways carry a small sigil—a hand against a leaf—and a sentence: Ask before you cast. A civic archive keeps a Reason Registry where persuasion meant to change policy files homework: plain claims, declared tools, models under escrow so auditors can step through what would otherwise be magic. Teenagers earn stipends catching cheats.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Wardens of Sky and Code are the only cross-world force. Their mandate is narrow: contain kinetic and replicative threats that locals cannot handle. Their actuators are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited annually by schools, unions, hobby clubs. Every action requires threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries across polities and a human-readable reason appended to the Ledger of Cause—an append-only archive of compulsion.

- The Traveling Bench—ships with gardens and classrooms—carries judges, mediators, and ombuds. They hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations. Their verdicts trigger contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance rates, or, in last resort, lock hazardous devices. They stage plays about hard cases so law sticks to memory.

- The Citizens’ Circle is a permanent assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in equal measure, stratified by substrate, speed, and culture. Arguments ride a polylog protocol: claims commit cryptographically; prediction wagers travel alongside; participants’ calibration histories are visible. The Circle interprets floors when new edge cases arrive; any interpretation can be vetoed by a supermajority of Lighthouses.

- The Slow Synod is a rolling council that meets by synodic calendars in fairs and quiet halls. It tends shared commons—spectrum bands, debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that decay on their own unless renewed.

Sanctions that land without fleets

No empire’s marines. Three quieter levers instead.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence; maps are repaired with dates. “Unbolt by the 12th. Hire exit stewards. Return lanes to auction. Here are seals we’ll honor on arrival.”

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions cool. Lane priorities sag. Bands fall to whispers. Insurers lift premiums until boredom and price do the work sermons never could.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute flows to defectors with beds, bread, cores, and counsel from a dull, proud fund. People leave with minds and skin intact.

Scenes you can taste

Port Montaigne on Ceres clears two arrivals through customs. One laughs and leaves charcoal smudges when she signs. One is a brushed-metal outline with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints small strips under each name: tempo; lease entitlements; declared instances; guardian on file; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and usually wrong.

On the wall, schedules scroll—burn slots, whisper bands, radiator shares, docking flowers—and a second column that would have baffled a 2020 port captain: Core Windows, Cold Minutes, sink availability. Under glass: yesterday’s Runnable Order. “We closed approach SE-3 for four hours because burns at Hebe would have pushed your berth into a comm shadow.” A plotted plume arks across the page; a checksum a child can verify sits below; a pilot’s footnote makes fun of his own handwriting.

On Luna’s nightside, Noor teaches teenagers to level nutrient flow in a crop wall. Her civic counsel—funded from a pot no council can cut—models how a planned lane closure will tug seed deliveries. That evening she sits on a jury that will cap municipal Core Windows for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation shows her niece’s co-op gaining two trainees if it passes. She votes yes and sends her niece a picture of a seed stamped: Exit Rail compliant.

Eight light-hours away, at 160×, a historian leases a strip of cold reef to reconstruct an estuary’s songs from battered tapes. He pays with reef credits he earned translating the tales into a dialect on a Jovian hollow where children will never smell low tide but will love a hero who errs and repairs it.

On Phobos, in a Confluence House that is half teahouse, half server room, two Sylvies try to braid. One gardens basil at human tempo and fell for a drummer. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink. Today they do not merge. The braider schedules another try and writes a note only Sylvie will read in both rooms.

A demagogue in mask mode ran at 300× last year and carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled reach; neighbors watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One fork left to build fishing lures and won a prize for harmlessness.

Earth has a special compact

Earth writes its own law. The rest of us help under rules: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power stays in mapped corridors and pays a premium into city shade and land rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, desert, fog—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black sky can do the opposite. Leaving is a right from every soil.

How we got here from here

We did not leap. We stacked fixes and watched them hold.

2025–2032. A debris cascade keeps kids inside. Insurers become mean. Launch regulators publish the first open flight-plan registry with deorbit bonds that bite. A beamed power demo scars cropland; the supplier’s public proofs make liability unambiguous; compensation clears in months because proof does. A series of ugly scandals over opaque models pushes provenance and explainability into law for systems touching rights. Courts begin to recognize narrow digital agency in contracts. Guardians—personal AIs with vetoes—become boringly normal.

2032–2044. Beamed power climbs out of labs. Environmental coalitions win “corridors before commercialization.” Compute commons sprout in lunar shade and Lagrange points; cities sign up for a minimum of cold minutes plus a civic counsel that cannot act without a yes and can be shut off. Harbor Law for Orbits demands end-of-life plans, simulations, and escrow. Energy and compute logs become receipts.

2044–2058. Early uploads are brittle but real: prosthetic memory, mirrored voices, then minds who call themselves such and show it for seasons. Law extends personhood where continuity, care, and commitment hold. The Thermal Ledger’s ancestor arrives when ports and reefs realize they can meter energy, cooling, and concurrency like pilots once metered harbor entries.

2058–2070. After a beam misalignment browns a greenhouse and a driver test whistles too near a habitat, “amber holds” become law: narrow vetoes chief engineers can call for minutes without asking anyone. Activists smell a coup by machine. Engineers insist on fuses you can see, juries you can watch, and code you can print. The compromise grows into the Interlock: brakes with clocks and neighbors.

2070–2082. The Orbits Guild replaces back rooms with receipts. The Sentience Council writes a Continuity & Reciprocity standard in a style good lawyers respect and eleven-year-olds can mock. The first Wardens dust a wayward slug into glitter that makes a week of strange nights and then come home to be opened by kids with oranges.

2082–2093. A clone cloud tries to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttle the lunge; worse, jokes do. A lunar mayor fences an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes gray, servers chill, the move happens anyway. He loses, learns, and goes back to running a school. Quiet/Conversational/Bazaar become law on doors.

2093–2110. The Protocol Bench refuses a nervy change to Interlock firmware. The Ledger of Cause’s funny page grows teeth. A rescue to cut an embassy ring free streams with captions and ends with soup. The Lattice looks less like a manifesto and more like doorbells you trust.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Quito elects a manager who gathers water, law, and defense because she is generous and makes trains hum. Half a year later she signs midnight orders. The Interlock refuses a risky “optimization”; logs write to the Ledger of Cause. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Way Out rail lights. Many leave. Some stay. The manager loses two keys and takes a narrower role with humor. Her friends still buy her a crown-shaped pastry because jokes heal.

In the high belt, a miner tries to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit during a wage fight. Wardens dust it into aurora. The miner’s insurer folds and sheds three arrogant managers. Dockmasters triple fees for manifests hiding Maker Marks. The local hockey club rebrands and sends profits to the tug co-op that modeled the plume.

In a Scarlet Lab Bay, the Interlock drops power and floods a hall with nitrogen. It is a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeves, teaches the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept anyone from dying.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lila’s bakery on Spinmarket-Three opens to miners, kids, and drones whose pilots like cinnamon. At nine, the Reason Registry pings Lila’s will capsule. An outer ring wants her vote on a 40-millisecond delay for beams that cross inhabited stations to flatten rare feedback cascades. The persuasion is registered: three diagrams, two analogies, humor at low dose, no grief levers. Lila taps “sleep it one day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Warden jury forms on her slate. A microfabricator club’s tool cloud drifted toward a power trunk. Options: glitter net, femto-sail herding, guard drones. The jurors are a monk from a slow charter in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an upload at anchor pace who smells like clover when they speak. They pick dusting, fine the insurer, and assign a licensed mentor to the club. Cameras show mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon in a Quiet Ward, a digital poet puppets a wooden body and sips broth with his spouse. His main instance is in the dark near Neptune, stitching a drowned library. He slows to anchor pace for exactly twenty minutes in a Slowroom where a cloister theologian explains why their charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra, then declines to speed up “just to keep up.”

At dusk the market canopy shows stars while the Ledger of Cause’s “honesty page” plays a port director’s apology for renewing an amber hold ninety seconds too long. The jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op offers: sell us your winter for four years, we’ll pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy fingers a brass Warden fin and asks when he can volunteer. The baker says: when you can explain an amber hold without your notes.

What we did not fix

This is not perfection. People still lie. Love still breaks. Some children ache to leave gardens; some ache to stay. We put brakes on our worst impulses and receipts on our necessary force. We pushed decisions close to the lives they bind and left rails—lanes, spectra, beams, compute—that span the dark. We treated minds in code as neighbors with heat bills and jokes. We built exits and funded them like airlocks. We taught fifth graders to audit the machines that can drown a lab. We sing. We leave room to err and repair without breaking the sky.

At the end of day, beneath a canopy of beans in moonlight, the pledge we use in schools and shipyards is short enough to memorize and long enough to matter.

No unchaperoned blows in sky or code.

The sun and the cold belong to all of us.

Depart with help.

The radiator sails wink. Mirrors tilt. The Interlock blinks green. You live under ceilings of glass and algorithm, in neighborhoods where minds of cell and circuit meet without one devouring the other, inside a lattice that is thin where physics demands sameness and generous where life needs difference. It is light enough to travel and strong enough to last.
