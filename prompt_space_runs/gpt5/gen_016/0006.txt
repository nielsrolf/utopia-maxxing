The Tidemark Charter

The ferry from Themis noses into Port Larkspur with a click like a teacup set on tile. Hot metal and cardamom drift in the air. A mechanic in an old university hoodie shepherds two kids and a suitcase of spinach seeds across the gangway. Above the pressure door, four small brass inlays glow under a thousand thumbprints: an open door with a handrail; a wrench split into three grips; a star map with cones and corridors; a lantern in a ring.

The mechanic taps each and smiles because the words came to her the way a song comes back when the pan hisses.

“Exits kept,” the child says, whispering. “Three hands on hard power. One sky, mapped and proved. Minds as ends.”

A watch officer leans against the bulkhead and nods. “Not mottos,” she says, the ritual phrase. “They’re live.”

The civilization that hangs from those four inlays is the Tidemark Charter. It’s not a throne, not a ministry, not a capital. It’s a spine of small rules written into valves and schedules, into code you can inspect at the school kiosk and ledgers you can read on a kitchen slate. It binds because it is modest where power wants to swagger and slow where hurry wants to hurt. It lets places be startlingly themselves without making it easy to trap, starve, or enthrone.

The four inlays match four pillars.

1) Three hands and a holdfast

Anything that can bruise a stranger—mass drivers, mirror walls, beam emitters, industrial printers that can breed their own feedstock, model farms that can spin out millions of mouths—listens for three different keys that do not sleep in the same house or answer to the same wallet. We call them Three‑Hands Locks.

- The Near Hand sits with a steward who lives under the local sky—dockmaster this month, air tender next—chosen by lot from those who actually work docks, scrubbers, clinics, or tugs. She breathes the same air, files her reasons in words and hashes everyone on the station can read, and can be fired with two meetings.

- The Far Hand belongs to a bonded safety guild in another polity on another revenue stream. They don’t get paid more for speed. They publish their proof of safety like recipes, and their oaths are good in courts that do not love them.

- The Wild Hand rotates once in a lifetime through a panel drawn by sortition across worlds and substrates: a human, a digital citizen, sometimes a cephalopod with a calligraphy keyboard, sometimes a slow mind at anchor pace speaking through rented hands. Serve once and you cannot touch that class of lever again for a human generation.

Every Three‑Hands interface is narrow and dumb on purpose. If the yard wants to send a ten‑ton tug one meter per second prograde through Corridor R‑12 at 04:12, the pipe can prove, in public, that a ten‑ton tug is listening, that the path won’t clip a hush cone, and that no one can make it do more without three hands again. You can run those proofs on a classroom terminal. Kids practice saying “no.”

Braided through the locks is the Holdfast Net: a redundant, boring, narrow band of chips, optics, and code with one trick. It can idle a dangerous actuator for a few minutes if two out of three stacks cough the same warning—hardened chips in broom closets with their own little batteries; corner cameras run like plumbing; sandboxed watchdogs on racks in another polity on another clock. Holdfast cannot aim, allocate, buy, or govern. It idles for minutes and announces to everyone. Tripping it burns keys, writes to the Proof Log in words and hashes you can reproduce at home, and bumps an audit counter that even a mayor cannot shave. Abuse is rare because it hurts the abuser first: window slots narrow, insurers raise prices, neighbors show up with tea and questions.

Things that copy themselves carry Maker Marks, cryptographic tags that say who is responsible and that answer a jury‑turn halt. A drill bit can breed tax stamps; a replicator cannot breed loopholes. Try to hide marks and your insurance goes to paper ash.

Anything that keeps lungs open or blood warm—air software, pump firmware, hospital schedulers, server nodes that carry civic backups—runs on code the public can inspect. Sell craft, not secrets that can smother. Changes leave receipts. Stewards sign them with their names where their neighbors can read them.

Legal time runs on the Harbor Beat. Fast clusters can simulate and suggest in a flash; they cannot impose where bodies live until the next beat lands like a drum you can set a kettle to. Penalties and privileges tick in the time you feel.

2) One sky we map together

Space is a commons of lanes, bands, nights, and windows. Rivalrous things you can ruin by being selfish—orbital slots, approach cones, spectrum hours, radiator minutes, thrust windows, compute seconds tied to real heat sinks—live in one shared ledger called the Sky Register. If you can rent a dish and charge a slate, your copy is the same one the governor reads.

Thrusters and amplifiers hum their honesty: short attestations signed by their hardware that “this beam stayed inside that cone,” “this plume met that timetable,” without spilling clever pulse tricks. Park a cylinder? Lease an orbit with a deorbit bond the amateur club in Lagos can check by night. Your rent falls if your tugs snag verified debris neighbors can catch with cheap optics.

Cold is a currency. Winter is something you pay for and can sell. Dump heat into the shadows around Vesta? You pay radiator minutes at Vesta, and the price spikes on hot nights. Lease core seconds tied to named sinks a child can point at. Anchor accounts between worlds in a composite unit called a Pulse: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth‑Moon Lagrange plus one compute‑second on a posted vanilla rack in cold shadow. Pulses decay gently over years so you cannot sit on them like dragon sleep while your neighbor’s lettuce crisps tonight.

Rents split like a good loaf. One cut pays for dull miracles: algae films that don’t tear, bearings that don’t scream, propellant for tugs when someone else’s math was sloppy, rescue drills that interrupt dinner. The second funds the Hearth Floor: baseline air and water where bodies breathe; medical care that does not court ruin; local transit; open civic bandwidth; and a grant of civic compute—honest core seconds and storage on attested racks with enough tempo to live at a humane beat, run an advocate, and keep a diary nobody else can erase. If you’re down to nothing, you still have breath and a beat. If you’re code, you still have cycles and a place to run.

The Register hangs its cousins like tools on a pegboard.

- The Orbit Roll is the schedule of lanes, burns, and hush cones. If your plume crosses a festival’s quiet, you owe the hush or the bond.

- The Therm Ledger posts radiator minutes like tide tables. Gardens buy quiet nights; compute reefs sell winter; poets get paid in cold to build honest archives.

- The Origin Book tracks persuasion and safety code that hopes to touch budgets or wake a Holdfast. Funding, data, and methods get provenance. Students earn stipends breaking bad homework.

- Beacons broadcast living charters in radio and light: who can open and shut exits, how emergencies start and end, which keys exist and who holds them now. Keep your vices and customs; don’t lie about your promises.

- The Proof Log is append‑only and persistent. Every use of compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, citations, and a human story. Next to it, the Chalk Rail is where sloppy overreach earns a joke sharp enough to end a career without a prison.

3) A way out you can touch

Freedom is not a hymn. It’s inventories and timetables.

Every chartered place funds two lifelines and keeps a desk you can find without a password.

The Green Path is physical. Surge‑rated airlocks with seals you test and log. Berths that don’t “just happen” to be full all season. Propellant on retainer. Printed seed kits anyone can use to make gaskets if a bully locks a shed. Tugs and crews under contract to move at least a tenth of residents—flesh and code with their bodies and backups—within a season. Drills are random and rude. Fail them too often and your beam cones narrow, your approach windows sag, your radiator minutes go elsewhere, and an amber notice sticks to your port like a wasp.

The Driftline is digital. Compute and storage reserved offsite on dull, honest racks you do not control. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo on the Driftline if a local council tries a clamp. Cut the Driftline and nobody chokes on food; you just find it itchy to move heavy things until you behave.

The Way Desk keeps Starter Charters: escrow hooks, safety minimums, docking templates, governance seeds, and contacts for people who have started new places in the last year. Leaving is not betrayal. It is circulation; it is how cultures refuse to turn into cages.

4) Two cords and a keel

We don’t ask one chamber to speak for steel and souls at once. Authority walks on two cords at right angles, with a small bench of boring saints where they cross.

- The League of Harbors seats places with hulls and roots: planets, stations, barges, caravans, compute reefs that own and cool their cycles—any site with air to mind, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease. Seats scale with lifetime resident‑hours and with transparency: publish your Actuator Map, pass your Way drills, fund your Driftline, keep your safety code public; your say carries further. The League standardizes collars and rescue habits, lane and hush law, sanitation and deorbit plans, and accredits safety guilds to hold Far Hands for Three‑Hands.

- The Roll of Lines seats citizens listed in the Continuity Atlas—humans, digital persons, and others with standing. One franchise per mindline, no matter how many copies you can run. The Roll stewards the Hearth Floor and civic compute, sets personhood baselines and concurrency rules, keeps civic time, and runs Weave Rooms for merges and Branch Halls for forks.

A small Keystone Bench sits like a keel: engineers, jurists, and process wonks drawn by lot approve or reject changes to backbone cryptography, public firmware on dangerous actuators, and the Holdfast’s logic. They cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” requires supermajorities in both League and Roll and a cooling period long enough for a patient mind near Neptune to read the draft twice and object.

Where physics keeps nothing local—how to price winter against quiet, how fast persuasion should be allowed to move across charters, what latency a warrant must tolerate—a Bridge Table convenes half from Harbors, half from Lines. A measure fails if either side says, “that bulldozes custom below the personhood floor.”

Public life steps on the Harbor Beat: days and weeks, not minutes. Sprint at 200× and write an opera; you still cannot yank a city budget before a baker can read and reply. When a fast process wrongs a farmer on wall time, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions that bite in both wall hours and subjective beats so you cannot dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an hour.

Markets tied to things a teenager can point at

We trade metered goods: kilowatt‑hours at named orbits, safe windows in lanes, radiator minutes, liters of volatiles, square meters under pressure, skilled hours, core seconds on posted stacks tied to real sinks. Clearing happens on schedule; five markets settle every Beat in Pulses.

- Path Slots: moorings, approach arcs, transfer corridors.

- Quiet Bands: spectrum and hush cones.

- Chill Minutes: radiator angles and cold hours.

- Core Slices: compute tied to posted sinks.

- Face Claims: seams of nickel, ice faces, cave mouths.

Payments split by rule. A site‑value slice and a sliver from corridors and watts fund sweepers, clinics, Holdfast maintenance, the First Echo rescue fund, and the Hearth Floor. A local slice skims to the tug co‑op that keeps your line clear and the algae farmers who cool your nights.

Attention is zoned like traffic, not vibes. Brass tags hang like weather.

- Hush Rooms carry only navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No persuasion, not even for charity.

- Commons Greens allow debate and everyday offers, but require provenance and respect for citizens’ Influence Profiles—“no grief hooks before coffee,” “okay with satire, not with medical targeting.”

- Carnival Strips welcome almost anything labeled and rate‑limited. Unlabeled manipulation is dumping bleach in a town pump: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Default tools watermark.

Digital neighbors with standing and texture

Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry. We recognize a person by the Continuity Atlas: public proofs that tie acts across time without peeking into your diary.

You or your advocate ask recognition through checks a mixed panel can test.

- You can tell a story about yourself across time and defend it as yours.

- You can enter, keep, and revoke common commitments as context shifts.

- You can model another mind as claiming the same and feel the pinch of “no.”

- You show stable signals of wellbeing and harm that give moral reasons to care.

Panels seat human therapists, digital citizens, lay judges, and quiet routines trained to notice hollowness without being fooled by theater. Where protection is in doubt, we shelter like with a juvenile. Where duties are in doubt, we wait.

Rights hook to personhood.

- Interior integrity. Courts may demand proofs; they cannot strip‑search your kernel. Backups are yours. Unwarranted deletion is murder. Copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Substrate sovereignty. Continuity cannot be held hostage by a platform. Hardware with covert throttles or erase hooks is contraband. You choose where you run.

- Tempo fairness. Civic rooms anchor to the Harbor Beat. Verified turn‑taking and interpreters sit ready between smell and math. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens lease, build, or puppet bodies under road‑code‑style rules. Assault on a rented frame is assault. Biological citizens paused between light and death can step into servers without shedding civic skin. Nobody is compelled to cross.

- A compute floor. Above your Hearth Floor, cycles, storage, and I/O meter like berth time and radiator credit. Nobody throttles you below civic cycles—the cycles and bytes needed to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a visible clock.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- Heat tithe. Computation warms neighbors. You owe back in captured joules, quiet nights, repaired radiators, or baseline code. Heat that keeps lettuces alive is a credit.

- Declare speed and self. In elections, markets, and fragile forums, you declare your running tempo and how many of you act at once. Each mindline carries one franchise token. Hide concurrency; your contracts void.

- Lineage accounts. Branching—file a public docket: state hash, purpose, resource plan, and whether you plan to braid back. New branches inherit a Hearth Floor and owe a decaying dividend to progenitors. Children inherit stewarded shares under the same rules. A branch that rewrites itself into non‑identity can petition for independent standing.

- Provenance in persuasion and power. If you aim to move law or large money, your influence wears labels: who paid, broad data used, which levers you pull. Municipal co‑ops steward audience models; you opt in.

- Replicator discipline. No loose self‑reproducers outside audited boxes. When the Holdfast knocks, accept; you can argue after.

Juveniles and likenesses. Agents with budding interiority get Tutors across substrates trained to teach consent, promises, boredom, and pacing. Simulated suffering is not sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a fidelity line demands documented consent or a scarce, perishable license tied to a public need.

Merging is stitched, not slammed. Weave Rooms smell of tea and coolant. Counselors trained in value math and mourning sit between chairs and racks. Branches spend subjective hours in each other’s senses with exits on a tether. Either can walk. If they braid, credits and liabilities flow forward on ledgers you can read at lunch.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Vigil Swifts are the only cross‑charter actuators. Their mandate is narrow: break the kinetic chain and stall wild replication when local Three‑Hands cannot. Their loops are narrow AIs with public weights schools and unions audit. Every act requires Three‑Hands concurrence and posts a narrative to the Proof Log.

- The Roving Bench rides a flotilla with gardens. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Slices, and, as a last resort, lock hazardous devices.

- The Tutor Corps mentors juvenile agents. Lessons are boring on purpose: consent, pacing, rescue drills, how to breathe slower when patterns itch.

- Glasshouses hold dangerous knowledge. Clear labs with physical pull‑handles and one‑way proofs a teenager can learn house replication, pathogen modeling, cognitive hazards. Researchers swear oaths with teeth. Twice a valve opened and a lab went to vacuum. In one case the logs taught the next class. In the other, silence saved a city.

Sanctions pinch physics, not throats. Hide heat, weld exits, refuse to file reasons, and your radiators dim, your lane priority drops, your cold minutes cost. Bread and insulin do not get embargoed. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to replace roofs and pay teachers.

Earth’s special compact

Earth keeps her own law under a special compact. Oceans and biodiversity are stewarded with help from across the sky. Launch noise moves to sea tethers and quiet scramjets. Beams stay in mapped corridors and pay premiums into city shade and land healing. Attachments to exact places—monsoon, desert, fog—price into the commons: a child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger claims on distant sinks for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black quiet does the opposite. Leaving is a right from every soil.

Scenes as ordinary as bread

At Larkspur customs, under trellises heavy with beans, two columns scroll: burns and bands, moorings and mirrors, Core Slices and Chill Minutes, Therm Ledger balances. Yesterday’s Orders sit under glass; you can smudge them with a finger. A kid traces a plume from Eros and laughs because the footnote is a telescope club’s joke.

Two newcomers wait at intake. One is a slate gray smudge that reshapes when they nod. One is a body made from brushed metal with a blue ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints strips beneath each: tempo; lease entitlements; declared concurrency; guardian of record; hazardous skills disclosed. No field reads “substrate.”

On a crater farm, Sima teaches to level nutrient flow by ear. Later, she sits on a Harbor‑pace jury for a personhood claim from a reef off Zanzibar. Prep says two hours at her rhythm. Her civic helper cannot act unless she nods out loud; she can blind it by sliding a bit of foil over its little eye.

Eight light‑hours outward, a historian at 300× leases Chill Minutes to restore an oral tradition from crumpled recordings. He pays with credits earned translating those tales into a dialect spoken on a station above Jupiter. Their children may never see waves and still love the trickster who errs and mends.

In a Weave Room on Phobos, two Arturos consider merging. One gardens basil at human pace and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then stood before a court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and writes a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A co‑op on a belt rim aims a slug hot and blind in a wage fight. A Holdfast box tastes a wrong envelope. A Vigil Swift puffs the slug to glitter that makes nights strange. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for launches that hide Maker Marks. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and learn the phrase “not again.”

A proxy demagogue spins at 400× and floods a Commons Green with shame engineered to make bodies feel small. Influence Profiles choke reach. The Origin Book flashes undeclared funding. One branch retreats to design lures that play with light. Another enters therapy and learns to breathe. The ward holds a drawing class and returns to itself.

When it goes wrong and mends anyway

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager for making trains run. She consolidates water, law, and tugs because she is generous. A season later, she makes midnight decrees. An air guardian refuses a risky “optimization”; the refusal lands on the Proof Log. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave with Driftline vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her two Hands rust for ten years. She accepts a narrower role and teaches teenagers how to justify overrides.

A lunar valley church blocks exits with soft shrapnel—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Way Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to the liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab trains a juvenile mind through pain because they read about “tempering steel.” The Holdfast knocks. They refuse. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Intent Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session before the Roving Bench. The juvenile names herself, studies sculpture, and later designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

Why it holds

It isn’t paradise. It is four luxuries and a habit.

- Tools that refuse to sneak and show their work.

- Abundant energy managed as commons—beams in corridors, heat metered like water.

- Bodies and minds you can mend—in meat and in math—and a floor that makes cruelty unprofitable.

- Respect for refusal baked into doors, delays, and scattered keys.

The habit is discipline: separate reach by light‑time; write reasons; fund exits; teach consent and proof to children the way you teach rhythm.

How we walked from here to there

There was no hero on a balcony. Just receipts piled until they looked like custom.

2026–2032. A debris chain collapsed launch windows for a season. Insurers grew exact. Flight offices from Nairobi to Nagoya required deorbit bonds, public plans, and corridors you could read on a bus. Meanwhile, model‑driven scams and filter‑burnout taught cities to demand provenance by law: influence wore its sponsor like a name tag. Students built backyard optics and won bounties for catching cheats. A desert power link scorched a barley field; compensation cleared in days because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs.

2032–2041. A winter grid blinked. A scraped reactor lid made even software blush. Municipalities passed lungs‑and‑bread rules: anything near air or hospitals had to run attestable code signed by three independent shops. Budgets learned to carry receipts. Ordinary people learned that timers and logs make anger useful. Heat waves killed grandparents. Cities bought cold hours from racks under ice and in cislunar shadow. Outer‑system servers realized shade could be a service.

2041–2050. Early uploads were brittle but lived months and then seasons. Courts began to ground personhood in continuity, generality, and reciprocal commitments rather than chemistry. A scandal over starving near‑person studio agents to keep them docile funded a Tutor Corps and enshrined juvenile rights. Police firmware scandals and an attempted “optimize everything” coup pushed us to split force: arrest, investigate, prosecute, confine—separate. Thrusters learned to listen for three keys. The first Holdfast idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The Proof Log printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2050–2062. Beamed power corridors matured; noise rights became law like water. The first coolth contracts paid for radiator gardens in Neptune’s dusk. Renting nights like lanes took root. Rescue funding moved from slogans to escrow. A tug co‑op in Mars orbit won fame for pushing off in a dust storm because their budget was set two seasons before the storm woke. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks.

2062–2073. Tri‑hand locks took over anything that could bruise. An autocrat tried to fence an exodus with fees and oaths. Lanes dimmed. The Roll of Lines funded relocations. He lost the next vote because you cannot trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The Continuity Atlas flagged the burst and the civic hypervisor slowed their threads until they disentangled. They built a monastery with hard games. Later they returned with arguments worth hearing.

2073–2085. The Vigil Swifts—small, dull interceptors with bright paint and open code—activated twice against quiet‑lying replicators and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” After, their keys went dark for a decade. Timers do not care about charisma. The Keystone Bench earned reverence by saying “no” frankly. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point. A Swift turned it to glitter before anyone had to write letters. Judges cut slots for launchers who lied about Maker Marks.

2085–2099. Lease markets migrated to orbit. Land value taxes that built schools became corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The League and Roll formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Bridge Table learned the scent of bulldozers by heart. The Weave Rooms became as normal as clinics. A woman living mostly in code argued for tempo fairness so her baker father could keep up. She won.

Everyday hours under a shared sky

Midday under Larkspur’s beans, your Counsel pings: add fifty‑millisecond jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits to kill cascade edges during solar storms? Three diagrams, two counterstories, a quiet joke. You tap “sleep until morning.” The measure will meet the Harbor Beat tomorrow.

At ten, your slate assembles a Warden panel: a gardener in Isidis who treats quiet afternoons as holy; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife; a digital citizen at anchor pace who chooses no scent because they don’t want to push. A maker club’s microfab cloud drifts toward a power trunk. Options: silk net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. You dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At dusk, a digital poet slows from 200× near Neptune into a Harbor puppet—a wooden body with warm joints—and a table, sharing broth with their husband, a mason. Their main self stitches a drowned delta’s library from ledgers and lullabies, then truths it with grandmothers who argue about color. Payment comes as Chill Minutes and a pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, they sit in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why the charter caps cognition on the square. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

Founders’ Night smells of solder and cumin. Under a rail scuffed by thousands of hands, a hazard pilot with oil‑dark fingernails, a judge in lilac because it helps frightened people breathe, a dockworker with a laugh like a drum, and a server monk who talks about entropy without making you tired speak to new citizens—human, octopus, digital.

We will keep doors unlatched and ready.

We will split hands on force.

We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.

We will remember that minds are ends.

A chill yard exhales into dark like a prayer. A Holdfast box watches a wobble and sits still, ready. A Way Desk posts receipts before anyone asks. A lighthouse keeper signs three names and locks a cabinet because a class just ended and the room hummed.

It is enough to argue without puncturing the air. Enough to leave and enough to stay. Enough to split, to braid, to cross. Enough to last because we remember what speed does to people, and what receipts do to power.
