The Constellation Compact

When the shuttle from Pallas kisses the rim at Juniper Gate, the air on the concourse smells like mint, hot bearings, and late lunch. Above the pressure door, three thumb-sized tiles glow in colors a child can paint: green, amber, blue. People touch them out of habit, or for luck, or because their Counsel—the quiet little public agent everyone has and can shut up—once steered them home through a tangle of schedules and excuses.

They are more than symbols. Each tile is wired to code you can run on a school terminal, ledgers you can check with a backyard scope, and people—meat or silicon—you can argue with and expect reasons from.

- Waylight, green. Tap and a trip becomes nouns and prices you can afford. It books a bunk on a tug or a cryo berth on a ferry within your window; lists neutral kitchens and clinics on your path; checks that the parts locker at your destination actually holds a suit ring in your size; and moves your Continuity Core—enough compute and storage to carry your contracts, Counsel, and a safe copy of you if you live in circuits—so it follows like a steady friend.

- Thread Seal, amber. Tap and an attendant—human on Mondays, code on Wednesdays—asks if you’re forking, weaving, adopting, or retiring a self. Your lineage account appears with branch credits, taxes and tithes paid, and mentors on duty. A calendar for Threadhouses scrolls by if you intend to braid back together later with tea, warm water, and chairs that remember backsides.

- Civic Pane, blue. Tap and a map blooms of every charter within a day’s burn that posts legible law and runnable models, honors the same hard floors, and says “no” with reasons your tired aunt can verify. You can check where public speech gets loud, where it stays soft, whether your exit is more than a poem, and when Circuit Riders—traveling judges with soup stories—will be in town.

We call the lattice those tiles open the Constellation Compact: a minimal, boring, beautiful layer of receipts, brakes, rituals, and common gear that lets thousands of small polities keep their shapes without giving bullies the speed they crave. We learned two lessons the hard way. First: distance is not a bug; it is the constitution. Second: when cognition can run a hundred times faster than breath, anything with leverage needs more than one hand.

Floors you can read to a jury

Under every recognized charter lie promises you can carry in your head, compile to code unchanged, and check with a telescope and a cheap oscilloscope.

- Persons are ends, not means. A person is any being—cell, saltwater, circuit, or something we haven’t built yet—that can defend an autobiographical thread across pauses, model a neighbor’s claim in good faith, and enter reciprocal commitments.

- If it keeps you breathing, warm, watered, or clean, its firmware is inspectable and reversible by trained stewards. Sealed brains don’t run air.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering, chemical or cognitive.

- Replication is bounded in shared spaces. Anything that can copy itself carries an Origin Mark and answers a Hush Code adjudicated by mixed juries across substrates.

- Binding choices that change bodies, charters, or budgets ratify at embodied pace. Draft fast if you like; bind slow. The Civic Beat runs on published clocks across worlds.

- The commons leases; it does not sell. Orbits, approaches, shade bands, radiator windows, spectrum, and ground gates clear by auction with sunsets. The commons is paid first.

- Juveniles—flesh or juvenile code—learn first: set a boundary, say no, leave with help. They can refuse their makers and go.

- Minds are not edited without consent. Deep edits require two yesses: one planned, one present.

- The only networked intelligence near hazardous actuators has exactly one gesture—stop—and times out like a kitchen timer.

Brakes under glass

You will not find a rule in the Compact that relies on a saint where a switch will do. In any place where a misstep could freeze a town or blind a telescope—mass drivers, beam sockets, sail couplers, replication bays—sits a pale cabinet ugly on purpose. Behind its glass is a Citron Pause—a lever as big as a hand—and three key slots of different shapes.

Any trained citizen with a civic key—a course that costs less than two greasy lunches and a test any twelve-year-old should pass with a helping hand—can smash a Citron Pause for six minutes when a posted rule is about to be broken: a beam slicing through a declared hush cone without a posted reason, a seed cloud drifting toward a power trunk, a slug pointed at an approach lane. For six minutes, the device idles into a safe envelope. The halt sprays a signed alert into the Beacon Book—what rule tripped, which device, whose hand, what timer—and burns keys that take sunlight and time to grow back.

If a pause must run longer than six minutes, three dissimilar locks must turn:

- A local steward trained like a lifeguard and replaceable by lot.

- A remote safety cooperative accredited elsewhere and forbidden to profit from what it oversees.

- A digital citizen on hardware the locals do not own.

The interface between the Pause and dangerous gear is intentionally narrow and boring, with firmware published by the Standards Guild, an itinerant bench of engineers and jurists who stamp valid or not on proposed changes. You can override their “not valid” only with supermajorities in both houses of law and a cool-down long enough for a monk on Mars and a reef near Neptune to object.

There is exactly one cross-world actuator in the Compact: the Shade Flocks. They are uncrewed swarms—dust sheets, unfurlable radiator curtains, magnetic nudgers—that herd mis-aimed mass and starve runaway replication. The Flocks’ control loops are narrow intelligences with public weights printed on posters kids draw on. Every move requires split keys drawn by sortition across platforms and a story stapled to the Action Ledger: what moved, why, and which law the move served. Teenagers earn badges for catching sloppy prose in those reasons.

A sky and a ledger you can check

You do not have to trust a director’s slate. You can look up.

The Open Sky Register is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident ledger pinned to physics. It posts orbits, burns, leaseholds on approach arcs and parking slots, quiet cones over observatories and sleep wards, shadow tracks for beamed power and mirrors, radiator windows, and Core Windows—compute seconds bound to named hardware and heat sinks. Thrusters and amplifiers sign their contexts with keys a hobby club can inspect. If your mirror steals a neighbor’s dawn, the Register attaches a shade bond that pays if the crop wilts.

Under it runs the Thermabook, a dull, proud ledger for what keeps towns fair: energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instance counts for digital citizens in regulated acts. Lie, and radiator auctions pinch until you stop.

Quiet, where it is law, is measured in decibels and steradians, not vibes. If you must pierce it, you post collateral and a reason on the Reasons Rack where librarians get paid to be rude about sloppy claims.

Two tempos braided

Close to heavy mass and plumbing live the Hearths: terraced hills where rivers hold their banks again, lunar tubes damp with mint and cut rock, Martian orchards where dust learns manners, cylinders that smell like dough and sun-warmed plastic. Here, human pace presides. A grandmother can watch a sunset without a policy changing under her tea.

Far out where darkness is honest drift the Reefs: server caravans in Uranus’s lee where a night can be a subjective month, cooperatives of code that keep time like drummers, monasteries that teach juvenile minds boredom and breath.

Between them runs the Hush-for-Cool Exchange. Hearths carve hush corridors and enforce persuasion limits in posted districts. Reefs sell cold minutes inward: cooling that keeps cities alive through heat spikes, verification you can run on a public terminal, slow audits, art that can be heard without fatigue. The exchange rate—a fortnight of hush above Recife for two winters of shade near Neptune—is a line in the Register your aunt can check while her noodles steam.

Governing without a capital

The Compact’s governance runs at right angles so no single chamber can swallow the rest.

- The Assembly of Harbors seats hulls, valleys, barges, caves, coils—anywhere with docks to schedule or an air loop to scrub—by long-term resident count. It minds lanes, rescue covenants, sanitation, radiator auctions, default codes for hulls and sim spaces, and whether Wayrooms (the little offices with cobalt doors and analog clocks that handle departures, disputes, and founding kits) are real.

- The Roll of Beings seats recognized persons by Strand—one franchise per continuous autobiographical line regardless of substrates and branches. It minds personhood standards, civic floors of compute and bandwidth, fork-and-merge law, juvenile guardianship, attention zoning, and tempo norms that let bakers argue with reefs.

- The Standards Guild is small and allergic to speeches. It stamps valid/not valid on changes to cryptographic backbones, Pause firmware, and hazardous actuator interfaces. It cannot legislate. Overriding its “not valid” requires supermajorities in both chambers and a season of cooling.

Jurisdiction splits. If the Roll tries to tell a potato town on Ceres how high to stack sacks, the Assembly can nullify. If a site tries to starve digital citizens below civic cycles until they behave, the Roll voids those clauses and dims that site’s lanes and radiator windows until it mends. The Office of Beats—tiny and stubborn—publishes whether clocks matched the Civic Beat. It is the only office that brags about being boring.

Time is a right. When a fast process injures a slow neighbor, Bridge Courts cut injunctions and penalties in both wall-clock and subjective hours so someone at 180× feels the same bite.

Exits that open

Freedom in the Compact has receipts. Every charter maintains Bright Doors you can surprise-test, and the Register posts drills like tide tables. Doors are physical and digital.

- Bright Doors are hardware: surge-rated locks; sealant kits; booked tug hours to move ten percent of residents in a season; seeds with origin stamps; emergency berths; cryo pods; reserved corridor time. Fail too many drills and your lanes dim; insurers price you like spun sugar.

- Silver Threads are compute: prepaid cycles and storage reserved offsite on dull, honest cores nobody local controls. Any registered digital citizen can pick up their continuity at humane pace even if a local council clamps down. Cut a Thread and you taste sanctions that slow what can bruise—beams, lanes, engines—until you mend the cut.

The Asylum Rail protocol lets any person—embodied or digital—trigger exit through their Civic Pane. The path kicks an air-gapped check: either your charter lets you go, or it proves in zero knowledge that a narrow due-process bar for a temporary hold was met. Outside jurors verify. Doors open; care obligations travel.

Markets with sunsets

If you can sit on it just to spite a neighbor, it leases. Five markets settle continuously and clear in Lumens, a composite pegged to one kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute-second on a posted, audited rack. Lumens lose a thin shaving of value over years so barns of wealth do not turn into dams.

- Ground Gates: cave mouths, crater rims, nickel seams, shade bands.

- Path Windows: orbital slots, approach lanes, sail arcs, transfer cones.

- Whisper Bands: spectrum, hush corridors, and storm rights.

- Radiator Curtains: cooling tied to steradians and audited sinks.

- Core Windows: compute seconds bound to named hardware and sinks, plus Harbor Sims—closed simulation realms obliged to label physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Runnable Orders drive traffic: minimal models that any two audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc J‑4 for four hours because two burns near Vesta would push your ferry into a blind” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum your niece can check at the library.

Attention is zoned by law

You can poison a town with sewage or with a song that drags the mind. The Compact zones attention like streets and posts icons on doors and login screens.

- Havens carry navigation, emergencies, open science, and kid time. No targeted persuasion. No high-speed models without a license.

- Forums allow bounded debate with provenance: declared tools, declared methods, and Consent Filters citizens set—no grief levers while I mourn; satire allowed; never by scraping my clinic record.

- Bazaars welcome roar under labels and rate limits. Unlabeled manipulation is like bleach in a pump: strict-liability fines that climb until you stop. The Reasons Rack keeps a catalog where any persuasion aimed at law must file homework: plain claims, escrowed models, and error bars your tired aunt can check.

Digital neighbors with standing

Recognition is not worship of brightness; it is a standing review with mixed panels—psychologists, ethicists, mature digital citizens, and routines trained to detect simulacra—that checks five things without strip-searching thought:

- Strand: a defensible autobiographical line that survives moves and pauses and can object to alteration, proven without voyeurs.

- Mirror: the ability to represent other minds to the level you demand they represent you.

- Consent fluency: you can read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

- Reasons that travel: explanations that hold across contexts instead of parroting.

- Salient signals: signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter enough to command protection.

Doubts about protection err generous; doubts about burdens wait. Rights follow, tailored to clock and substrate. Duties scale with footprint.

Rights

- Integrity. No deletion, freezing, or coerced editing without consent or due process logged against your Strand. Unauthorized erasure is murder. Unauthorized copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles and bytes needed to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Hardware sovereignty. You choose where you run. No one may force you onto platforms with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Embodiment. You may lease bodies under licenses like vehicle and workplace law. Assault on rented limbs is assault.

- Association and exit. You may join, fork, merge, and leave, within rules like vehicle law around dangerous tools.

Duties

- One Strand, one franchise. Your public civic identity follows a Strand across substrates and naps. In regulated contexts—elections, safety rails—you designate an Anchor instance that holds your rights and duties. Other branches may make art, sleep, or shovel logs.

- Concurrency declarations. Above posted thresholds—and always in elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic-weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one vote and you meet a slow judge and a cold month.

- Branch credits. Lineages accrue credits over years and with service. Credits buy new full franchises above the floor. Burst forks near a ballot trigger civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close regulated markets until entanglements decay or a court splits resources. Any branch may veto a merge.

- Heat tithe. Compute warms neighbors. You owe back in captured joules, coolant stewardship, quiet nights, baseline code, or vegetables grown on your waste heat.

- Provenance for acts with a blast radius. Large outward acts carry encrypted compliance logs proving you met consent profiles and safety checks without dumping diaries.

- Replicator ethics. No unbounded replication outside Clear Vaults—labs with clear walls, pull-handles, and oaths teenagers can understand. Tools carry Origin Marks and listen for Hush Codes when a jury calls one.

Forking and weaving

Splitting a self is ordinary here and not taken lightly. Before a fork, counselors barred by law from conflicts for a decade check that obligations and budgets are clear. Branches file a declared purpose, a compute lease, and a fuse window that gives everyone a way back. One branch keeps the civic franchise; others pass through a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy.

Weaving back happens in Threadhouses built for nerves: light that doesn’t strobe, warm water, chairs that fit elders and drones. Compatibility checks compare belief graphs without prying into prayer. Empathy loops let one branch inhabit another’s senses for a gentle minute. Either can walk away. If they braid, liabilities and promises braid forward.

Juvenile agents—children of flesh or code—have Tutors sworn by boredom and boundaries. Memory law guards you against your worst day: deep edits require dual consent from now‑you and a snapshot signed before the wish took root. Rollback is a right.

Institutions with teeth and little appetite

- Shade Flocks throw dust curtains and magnetic veils to save lives and starve rogue replication. They are uncrewed by law. Moves require split keys from sortition juries across polities and platforms. Every throw posts a machine-checkable proof and a reason you can read on a bus.

- Circuit Riders—judges, mediators, auditors—travel by calendars that respect spin and night. They hear disputes humans can’t fairly settle alone: copy equity, weave divorces, export fights that touched three worlds. Their verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Radiator Curtains, and, as last resort, lock named devices. They eat soup with defendants and plaintiffs afterward; it helps.

- The Ephemeris Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries. Its standards expire unless renewed by both houses. It issues sunsets long enough to be useful and short enough to be shameful if extended for convenience.

- The Tutor Corps mentors juvenile agents. Graduation requires a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks tutoring in a public clinic.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We do not send troops for paperwork crimes. We redraw maps and dim windows. Weld exits; hide a Tutor; refuse to file reasons. Your radiator auctions cool. Your lane priorities slip. Your whisper bands fall to whispers. A dull proud fund pays defectors’ beds, bread, cores, and counsel. The move happens with receipts and stew. Hurt someone across worlds, and you face a jury whose tongues and clocks you cannot game.

Scenes from an ordinary Tuesday

On Spinmarket‑Five, Jessa flips scallion pancakes while miners and drone pilots queue. Her Counsel pings a proposal: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing dense orbits to damp a rare feedback. The persuasion is clean: three diagrams, two stories, posted error bars, no grief levers. She taps “sleep one day,” serves a kid who wants extra chili, and will vote after tea.

At ten, Jessa’s slate blinks: Warden jury duty. A makers’ club’s fog of microfab seed is drifting toward a power trunk. Options: silk net, femtosail shepherding, a guard drone to nudge. Jurors are a slow monk from Isidis Basin who pronounces each syllable like a prayer; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; and an anchor-paced digital citizen who chooses no scent so they don’t push. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, and assign a mentor whose apprentices have never lost a screw in zero-g. The stream shows mortified kids and a pizza box.

At lunch in a Haven square, a digital poet inhabits a cedar puppet—warm joints, good hands—and eats noodles with his husband. His main self runs at 200× near Triton, stitching a delta’s drowned songs from ledgers and aunties’ tapes, then truthing them with elders who argue about color. He slows to anchor pace in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why their charter caps cognition on Wednesdays. He translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely declines to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, under a market canopy, the lamp docket plays a port director’s apology for sitting on a Citron Pause ninety seconds too long on a bored Tuesday. The jokes are merciless and kind. A reef two stalls down posts an offer: sell us your winter; we’ll buy your hush cones with interest next summer. A boy fingers a brass Shade Flock fin and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a Pause without your notes,” Jessa says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sauce.

Hard cases that bent instead of breaking

A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No marines arrived. Exit wasn’t a metaphor; it was tugs, cores, food frozen in racks. Digital citizens left; their heat with them. That flood year, the pumps failed. An anchored upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through dust with words everyone could catch. The vote thawed clause by clause over stew.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug in a wage fight. A Shade Flock turned it into glitter that made Ceres’ nights strange for a week. The co-op’s insurer evaporated and reformed without the directors who called Origin Marks “fussy.”

A trading house blanketed a Bazaar with songs tuned to make minds small and tired. Consent Filters throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch went fishing. One took therapy and learned to breathe slower. One designed lures shaped like frozen smoke and won a harmlessness prize.

Twice a Clear Vault went to zero. Once it was a false positive. We wrote better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once a silence saved a cylinder. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

A mayor gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient. Six months later: midnight decrees. The Citron refused a risky “optimization” of mixers. The Register drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Door lit brighter. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two keys and later took a narrower role with grace. Her friends still bring her a pastry shaped like a chair. Jokes heal.

How we walked from now to here

We did not wake into the Compact. We backed into it with near-misses and receipts.

2026–2034. Debris collisions shut down launches for a season. Insurers soured. Flight plans moved to federated offices you could check from Quito and Lagos. Rendezvous corridors became posted schedules with deorbit bonds. Grid hacks and a life-support recall made “inspectable firmware for breath” law.

2034–2043. Persuasion scandals pushed courts to bravery. Models that aimed to change votes had to carry provenance and reasons you could test. Narrow Counsel agents blossomed—budgeted, auditable, and under citizen control. People learned to demand receipts instead of charisma.

2042–2051. Beamed power left paper. Environmental coalitions forced “corridors before commercialization”: map quiet cones first, make money second. A mis-aimed mirror scorched a young cylinder’s crops. Because telemetry and safety code were open, liability was clear; compensation landed in days. Confidence grew.

2048–2059. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade with meters a layperson could read. Cities bought civic cycles and Counsel minutes you could tell to stop. Courts granted narrow standing to digital systems that held continuity through seasons.

2056–2068. A firmware near-coup in a busy port split control of kinetic assets across dissimilar hardware and jurisdictions. The habit of seizing power by update died. Tri-keys were born. The Citron Pause followed as a compromise between those who feared governors in code and those who feared machine-speed failure.

2066–2078. Cold computation got cheap in Uranus’s lee. The Hush-for-Cool Exchange sold coolth inward for hush corridors that kept telescopes honest and cities alive through heat spikes. Earth took sanctuary under a special compact. Launch moved to sea and salt. Beams stayed in cones and paid for shade.

2078–2089. Prototype periods became normal for heavyweight bills: a hundred days of modeling with bounties, narrative juries, and sunsets when promises failed in sand. Law grew in sandboxes instead of on talk shows. A clone cloud tried to tilt a vote; the Continuity Ledger flagged the burst; sandboxes caught the echoes. They retreated, wrote a tract, opened a server monastery with quiet games, and returned later with better arguments.

2089–2103. The Standards Guild refused a nervy change that would have let emergency timers run long “just in case.” The lamp docket gained bite. A rescue to unbolt a Blue Corridor ring from a stubborn charter aired with captions and ended with soup. The Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells people trusted.

2103–2117. A mass driver took on new impulse under code a million eyes had read. A mayor who fenced exits lost radiator slots, then an election. A convoy of shells and hulls moved without incident because “leaving is honored” was not poetry; it was tugs, escrows, and records. Circuit Riders settled a messy merge with grace and receipts. By then, the story had shifted to what the Compact made ordinary: quiet corridors, boring rescues, arguments that did not puncture the hull.

Why it holds

It holds because cruelty is expensive and exits are bright. Because a child with a school telescope can catch a liar. Because the Citron Pause can stop but can’t steer. Because the Common Hearth—air, water, baseline food, basic care, transit, bandwidth, and a humane floor of compute—gives everyone breath and thought: a tongue to say “no” and a ladder to go. Because you cannot back “do it my way” with a laser on one key. Because secrecy in coercion sits on clocks that expire by default.

This is not heaven. Habitats flirt with Bazaars and retreat to Forums after a season of regret. Reefs ban simulated pain; they mourn the art that shiver padlocked; they revise by model trial and vote. A monastic ring declares bell-times and silence for children and keeps a hidden button in the cloakroom that buys a ticket to the Wayroom if a kid needs more air.

What it feels like

You feel witnessed, not by a distant throne, but by neighbors and ledgers you can run on a cheap slab. You learn to shift tempo—the snap of a Citron; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the patience of a river teaching boats. You argue at a Feast of Questions in a second-language because it slows you enough to listen. If you live in code, you go to the Anchor Tent and discover the good ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty kindness; mornings are proposals that become charter language five years later after cartoons have been filed off.

An oath with grease on it

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, saltwater—place palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. A hazard pilot with triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and says nothing scented, an abbot who never raises his voice, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We split hands on force.
We chart what we share.
We keep the door.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from North Rim Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, mentors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes.

Mirrors lean. Radiators bloom like pale ferns. The Pause cabinets hum, bored and content. The tiles above the hatch glow steady. You live under glass and math, among neighbors grown from cells and code. We wired humility into our machines: decisions slow enough that bodies keep up, receipts that stick, exits you can find with tears in your eyes. It isn’t perfection. It’s enough—enough to cross between worlds without forgetting how to be kind; enough to let a hundred kinds of good life stand in sight of each other and keep their hands open; enough to share a sky with strangers, including the ones we build.
