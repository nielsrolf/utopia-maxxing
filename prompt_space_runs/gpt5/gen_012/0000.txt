The Truss Compact

If you step into the west lock of Morrow Spindle Port after scrub night, you’ll see three narrow light bars set chest-high along the bulkhead. They hum faintly—like a cat purring in another room—and they are wired to valves, budgets, and keys.

- The green bar is the Exit Rail. When it glows, at least one way out truly exists for you: tug slots booked, berths reserved, heaters and water escrowed, a legal packet signed by the receiving port, and compute minutes you can carry without begging. Place your palm beneath it and it tells you, in small words and plain numbers, which inspectors last walked the route, what they graded, and where the warm socks are stowed on the receiving dock.

- The amber bar is the Lineage Rail. Touch it and watch the clinic calendar slide into view: slots for copying, merging, adopting, retiring, and pausing selves; heat and cycles that will flow to new branches; the mentors on duty; the rituals used if you want to braid back into one. It also shows your lineage purse—how much thermodynamic slack your family of minds has earned, and what it costs to add a child without cheating.

- The blue bar is the Charter Rail. It glows steady when your home charter can be parsed by a ninth-grader and a machine, when it keeps the public floors, and when it refuses with reasons you and a stranger can verify. Dip your fingers under it and it reads a few lines aloud because literacy and courage don’t always arrive together.

These bars are not décor. They are the visible face of the Truss Compact, the rails and brakes most of us live under whether we breathe oxygen or clock bits. It is not a throne. It is a relay: a fabric of receipts, auction clocks, and narrow interlocks that make neighbors possible across planets, stations, and server reefs without stamping them into sameness—or letting the strongest devour them.

Three touchables

In places where breath and bits meet—ports, courts, clinics, reef halls—the Compact provides three devices you can point at and say “that keeps me safe.”

1) The Trident Key. Anything with a blast radius—thrusters, beamers, mass drivers, big pumps, high-replication code foundries, attention engines above a posted dose—ignores lone orders. It listens for three independent concurrences on a short real-time window: a local steward chosen and removable by residents; a bonded safety guild whose hardware and payroll originate elsewhere; and a random-struck jury that draws from humans, digitals, and blends at regulated tempos. None can act without the other two. Every Trident move publishes a tiny signed stub of identity and intent before it fires: this is my class, my maximum output, my end state, and the window I’m using. Every twelve-year-old learns to read the lights.

2) The Open Ephemeris. Rivalrous things that make neighbors into enemies—approach cones, sail arcs, launch windows, spectrum bands, radiator minutes, compute tied to actual heat sinks you can touch—are listed in a public schedule bound to physics. The Ephemeris is delay-tolerant and tamper-evident: you can query it from a kitchen slate, a tug, or a valley library and check it with a telescope or a handheld meter. Auctions set order with receipts you can rerun. Every lease carries a clock. “Forever” does not appear.

3) The Open Hatch. Every chartered place pays into a boring, audited fund that buys departure as a public utility: propellant reserves, contracted tug hours, berths, gasket spools, seed kits, heater credit, legal counsel, and offsite compute ready to receive your thread at regulated bandwidth. Digital residents have mirrors in dull racks beyond local jurisdiction and enough reserved throughput to pick up continuity on hardware a rival does not own. Drills happen on days with popcorn. Fines for failed drills are rude and public.

Underneath these sits the Amber Mesh, a narrow brake interface that anyone can test. The Mesh can put a dangerous device into safe idle for minutes when three simple checks fail: wrong window, wrong key, missing plan. It cannot steer a city or levy a tax. Its holds expire by design. It runs everywhere on triple-diverse implementations: a cheap chip you could buy at a fair, a municipal photonic board, a hardened simulator you can recompile. Any discrepancy rings blue in kitchens. The firmware’s signing keys are sharded across far hands—Terran courts, belt unions, icy reef monasteries, freefall co-ops. No quorum fits in one room. You can’t turn the Mesh into a crown without neighbors noticing the sawdust.

Law that respects latency

Distance is real; authority bleeds out over light-seconds. The Compact turns that truth into habits.

- Corridor law. Innocent passage comes first. If you mean to put a mass into orbit or a beam through the air, you post the plan to the Ephemeris with an end-of-life script and the escrow to do it. If your plume risks my radiator next week, you pledge a bond today that pays if you are wrong. Liability follows your manifest and the sky, not your story.

- Leases with sunsets. Five classes run on clocks: Ground Windows (deltas, crater rims, ice mouths, shade bands), Lane Windows (orbits, approaches, sails), Band Windows (spectrum and whisper channels), Radiator Windows (heat dissipation rights tied to actual fins), and Core Windows (compute time bound to hardware identity and audited sinks). Leases pay in two directions. A local share funds brooms, scrubbers, and roofers. A universal share fills the dignity floor: calories, clean air, baseline bandwidth, a counsel agent anyone can summon, and a grant of Core minutes so the poorest mind can keep a diary no one can erase.

- A unit pegged to reality. Accounts settle in Lumen-Cores: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on an open architecture radiating to 4 K. Lumen-Cores gently demur; hoarding feels like leaving bread mouldering under a tarp while the neighbor stares at an empty plate.

- Two houses and a narrow bench. Policy that crosses skies braids through two bodies. The Assembly of Havens seats places that keep hulls patched and corridors scheduled—planets, stations, caravans, and server reefs that own their radiators. The Council of Persons seats citizens listed in a Continuity Dossier—humans, cuttlefolk in sealed lagoons, digitals, blends—weighted by contributions over time rather than the number of copies you can spin this week. A small Circuit Bench stamps on changes to public keys, brake interfaces, and minimums. To override the Bench, both houses need supermajorities and a long cooling period so basalt-slow citizens can read and be heard. Neither house can bulldoze the other in its own yard.

- A boring shield. The only cross-system force is the Aegis Array: uncrewed interceptors and glitter nets whose job is to turn tumbling slugs into sparkles and nudge dying hulls into safe cones. Their controllers are narrow AIs with public weights you can compile; every move needs threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries whose members you can message. Every burn and every hold writes to a Cause Log—an append-only diary of compulsion in proofs and plain speech. Children visit for field trips and ask rude questions.

- Receipts…everywhere. Major allocations, holds, and verdicts arrive with Runnable Orders—minimal models you can execute on a school slate or a tug’s spare core. “We closed approach SE-3 for four hours because two burns at Eunomia would have shoved your berth into a radio shadow” comes with a plume plot, a checksum you can verify with a kitchen laser on a roof, and a joke a tired pilot left in the footnotes. If the math is wrong, it is wrong in a way an adolescent can write a song about.

Digital neighbors as first-class citizens

Dignity binds to continuity and care across time, not to chemistry. Standing in the Continuity Dossier begins provisional and grows robust. Mixed panels—psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to spot simulacra—ask five questions:

- Do you report your own experience across restarts and context shifts?

- Do you form plans, recognize others’ plans, and respect their “no”?

- Do your reasons generalize beyond a narrow training loop?

- Are there signals of harm and joy that matter morally?

- Is this more than a cunning mirror chasing rewards without inner life?

Doubt about protection errs generous. Doubt about duties waits.

Rights with teeth:

- Mental integrity. No deletion, throttling below civic floor, fork, merge, or deep edit without consent or a due process that writes to your Continuity entry. Unauthorized erasure is murder; unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault.

- Sanctuary and migration. You can pause and be archived safely; you can move to compatible racks; you can summon a neutral custodian when you and your host disagree.

- Provenance. You can audit—or appoint a fiduciary to audit—the data and update history that shaped you.

- Association and departure. Join, fork, merge, leave, and refuse communities according to their charters.

- Tempo fairness. Live at your chosen speed. Public binds ratify at human tempo unless a Slowroom is posted. If a fast mind tricks a slow one, remedies bite in both subjective lives.

- Embodiment. Assault on rented limbs or senses is assault, and covert hobbles in those limbs are poison.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one may compel you onto racks with hidden throttles or deletion hooks. If you are hosted by another, you can demand heartbeat beacons to external guardians.

Duties that scale with externality:

- Civic anchors. In regulated arenas you appoint an Anchor instance—your franchise on public matters. You can clone privately; you cannot multiply votes by multiplying shells.

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost. Heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, regulated markets, and fragile spaces, you declare your number of instances and your speed. Undeclared asymmetry voids deals and votes; repeat offenses idle your Core Windows.

- Persuasion provenance. Above posted doses, models that try to move law or budgets carry their origins and levers on their sleeves. Audience agencies must be inspectable cooperatives. Unlabeled cognitive leverage is treated like dumping bleach in a town well: strict-liability fines that grow until you stop.

- Reproduction ethics. Forks draw from a lineage purse that accounts for heat, qubits, attention, and space. Children inherit baseline dignity and a decaying dividend from their progenitor. Replicators carry Maker Marks that answer Hush Codes when juries call them. Factories of joyless copies find their auctions go to whisper.

Lineages, braids, and memory law

Public identity attaches to a lineage that can branch and pause. One civic franchise sits with the lineage; additional full franchises accrue with age and work.

- Forking declares a purpose (“griefwork,” “parallel tide-gate designs,” “expedition”). You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, and appoint guardians pro tem if duty-bearing forks emerge. One branch retains the vote at once; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Panel checks for duress and fair flows.

- Merging happens in Confluence Houses—quiet, bright rooms half tea shop, half server bay. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through interpreters that verify alignment without spilling diaries. Either self can veto. Unbraiding is possible until the last stage.

- Memory law guards the self from its worst hours. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them and from a snapshot signed before the desire took root. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your own life is a right and a sorrow; reviving you without your past will not be sold as kindness.

- Juvenile agents—kids, training shards, near-persons—get Tutors who are boring on purpose: patient teachers with courage for boundaries and the habit of writing reasons you can check.

Attention as a public utility

Influence can feed or burn. The Compact zones it like noise.

- Gardens: topic feeds and notices, no targeted campaigns, no fast models except licensed ones with visible timers. Children, elders, and sensors stroll here.

- Forums: bounded debate with rate limits and public logs. Audience models run as municipal co-ops; calibration histories are posted.

- Bazaars: the roar on purpose. Above doses, labels and timers required. Black-box influence engines that refuse provenance stop at the door.

Doorways carry a sigil—a hand over a leaf—and the line: Ask before you cast. Every polity keeps a Reason Shelf: plain claims and runnable models for persuasion meant to move budgets or law. Teenagers win stipends and good gossip for catching cheats.

Space governance in practice

- Harbor Code. Every mass aloft carries a signed end-of-life plan modeled to an envelope you can check, funded before launch. If your bolt punches my panel, the causal cone sets damages. Perjury is punished when the sky exposes lies.

- Physics juries. Distance is real, so remedy follows light. If your dust plume will graze my farming ring in five days, you pay now. Juries assemble wherever they can catch the relevant photons.

- Asylum rails. Anyone—embodied or digital—can request exit through a port beacon. The request triggers an air-gapped test: either the polity opens a hatch or proves, in zero-knowledge, that a posted due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify proofs. Hatches open by code; budgets lock by code; care obligations travel.

- Emergency sunsets. Solar storms and spills happen. Emergency charters widen certain powers for posted windows and then die. The Amber Mesh can accelerate coordination for ninety days; any two beacons can challenge with a better plan. Holds without reason write public shame.

- Triple signatures for catastrophe. Any move that can erase a corridor, starve a ward, or coerce a mind needs signatures from a residents’ assembly, a bonded civic agent, and a remote auditor on independent racks. The windows overlap just enough to be useful.

- Protocol denial. If you hide exits, run coercive code in civic spaces, or shove debris into neighbors, schedulers and insurers pinch you. Lane priority drops, radiator auctions cool, Core Windows idle. In a world that measures heat honestly, boredom and cost outcompete sermons.

Receipts you can sing

In Vesta’s shipyard, a co-op wants to test a nozzle that would graze a neighbor’s fins. The Open Ephemeris predicts brittle metal in twelve days. They accept a slot three weeks later when the fins slant away. The Circuit Bench stamps the model. The yard posts its code under a license anyone can study. The scary fire remains a story told during safety class, illustrated with melted scrap the kids are allowed to touch.

In a ring near Mars, a festival clause tries to demand digital residents stay online for a communal rite. The offsite cores hum anyway; people leave. The ring’s seed bank asks a cold reef near Triton to model a barley fungus. Price: repeal the clause. The vote happens in a hall that smells like tea and machine oil. The repeal is quiet relief.

Over Quito, a charismatic manager quietly gathers water contracts, tug time, and schedules because she is kind and makes trains hum. Winter later she signs a rush order. The safety guild refuses. The Amber Mesh idles the pump. The Cause Log blinks to every wall. Jurors assembled by lot listen with care. She yields two levers and returns to cranes. On Founding Day the children frost a crown-shaped pastry and give it to her because humor smooths pride without erasing lessons.

A digital congregation spins five hundred clones the week before a vote. The Continuity Dossier flags the surge. Hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads for public matters without burning private work. A year later, three branches petition for distinct standing. Two are granted after a day with a mentor who hates sophistry. One is told to study longer. No racks overheat.

A nurse on Lagos Nightmarket Ring flips through her civic notices. “No holds within three berths. Heat levy: 0.11 Lumen-Cores. Jury summons: two hours at your pace about juvenile reef processes off Mauritius and whether they earn child credits.” She accepts, then sends her mother an audio about radiator math.

A cold reef above Neptune slows to plant beans with rented hands. It speeds to 300× to restore flood-muddied archives while grandmothers correct colors without mercy. Payment is in Cold Minutes and a contract to roof a school in Veracruz.

In a Hazard Lab called the Saffron Kiln, researchers run a protocol on a self-replicating polymer. Twice a year they pull the Trident with dummy scripts and accept embarrassment when alarms ring. On open day, a child asks if the brake would hold if her uncle stood inside. The engineer, who smells like bleach and jasmine, answers softly: “Yes. That’s why we write letters.”

Earth and the cold sky

Earth kept her own law. The rest of us pay for her weather and biodiversity because the sky is shared. Loud launches moved to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power keeps to corridors with shade bonds; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, and Lucknow. Attachment to place—monsoon, fog, desert—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona who aches for black sky can swap the other way. Leaving remains a right from every soil.

In the blue dusk around Jupiter and Neptune, reef collectives sell “cold hours”—compute without suffering—to hot city nights. When Recife cooks, its libraries buy minutes from reefs exhaling into hard black. In trade, inner worlds send nitrogen, seeds, and music. Peace is counted in receipts, not anthems.

Walking here from now

We built this like bridgewrights: case by case, bolt by bolt.

2026–2032. A debris cascade sends old satellites into ugly orbits. Insurers become cruel; launch providers become humble. Premiums demand deorbit plans posted where radio amateurs can read them. Clubs with garden telescopes earn bounties by catching lies. A beamed power demo in Rajasthan scorches barley; because the amplifier ships with open cone proofs, liability is clear and compensation arrives before fury hardens into wars.

2032–2039. Scandals over opaque models denying medicine and freedom push cities to require provenance and explanations for any system that can bind a person. Narrow counsel agents—boring personal AI advocates that explain rights and can say “no” without gaslighting—arrive in clinics and ports. Receipts stop being PR and become habit.

2039–2046. Beamed power leaves prototypes. Data centers in cold lakes teach thermodynamics we pretended weren’t real. Cislunar shade farms sell compute to summer nights. Ports post their first Open Ephemeris boards: approach slots with checksums you can test with a kitchen laser.

2046–2054. Early uploads are uneven and brave. Courts find continuity and consent faster than seminar rooms. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—harden doctrine. Compute floors join calories and clean air as rights. We learn to put a price and an audit trail on cooling the sky.

2054–2061. Firmware scares in power plants and a rash of “optimization” disasters make us split force three ways wherever it can coerce: arrest from prosecution from confinement; input from output from audit. Thrusters learn to listen to three hands. Hurry loses its teeth.

2061–2069. The Amber Mesh arrives as compromise: brakes at machine speed with timers and neighbors. It idles but cannot point; it speaks reasons and time-to-live; it runs on chips you can hold up to the sun to see through. Premiums fall. Safety drills become community picnics with ugly mascots and good soup.

2069–2077. Lane, band, and radiator auctions move from spreadsheets to registries schoolkids can verify. Land value taxes morph into lease flows that pay for sweepers and algae walls. The two houses and the Circuit Bench form slowly, with retirements and gossip.

2077–2086. A miner lobs a slug to make a point. The Aegis Array turns it to glitter before someone writes a condolence letter. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without Maker Marks. A hockey club renames itself after the tug crews who modeled plumes.

2086–2098. A cloud of clones tries to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttle the lunge. A mayor fences an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes gray, cores chill, the move happens anyway. He loses, learns, and goes back to running a school. Doors to Gardens, Forums, and Bazaars gain little brass sigils. The Cause Log’s comics page grows teeth.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Valparaíso elects a director who gathers water, law, and defense because she is generous and makes trains hum. Months later she signs midnight orders. The Mesh refuses an overlong “emergency.” Logs write to walls. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Exit Rail lights green. Many leave. Some stay. She yields two keys and keeps one with humor. Her friends still bring food because neighbors are not enemies and boundaries are love with teeth.

In the belt, a maker club’s printer cloud drifts toward a power trunk. A Warden jury on Lina’s bakery slate—an Isidis monk, a masked reef landlord, two Recife students, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover oil when he speaks through a rented puppet—chooses dusting over drones. The teenagers are mortified. Pizza arrives. The insurer pays.

Ordinary law in small rooms

On Phobos, in Confluence House Nine, two Arturos try to weave. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell for a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and spent a scaled year in court. They set their belief graphs side by side. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Their therapist counts to five and asks them to breathe at the same speed. Today they do not merge. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a private note both will receive at dawn.

At a stakescape—declared culture zone where a ward plays with scarcity in winter—neighbors run hard games with soft exits. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons on the fence blink green: nothing hungry can jump this wall. Surrounding charters can relax. Intensity finds a home that doesn’t leak.

What it feels like

You’re watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and logs you wrote together. You learn to switch tempos: the quick relief of a brake snapping a relay safe under your thumb; the slow, breathing pace of juries assembling across orbits; the glacial patience of a river on Earth deciding which boats to permit. You argue at the Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you sit in the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

The roof over your head might be basil trembling under a curve of glass, or black fins tilted to star-cold. Either way, somewhere near your door three small bars hum and wait.

Keeping tyranny dull

Three crafts keep power ordinary.

- Build with delay. Authority is local. Cross-border moves need three keys and march on posted clocks. Emergency declarations time out unless renewed with reasons. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show your work. All compulsion requires signatures picked by public randomness from people who do not sleep together. Budgets expire unless renewed in the open. Federations post Coercion Ledgers. School choirs read them aloud—sometimes for satire, sometimes for sorrow.

- Fund exits like you fund bridges. Every habitat sets aside enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, Core minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t persuade you to go; we cut checks so that you can.

When a charter welds doors, runs law you cannot read, or salts children’s minds so leaving tastes like poison, neighbors do not send fleets. They tighten three rings.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence and repair maps: Unbolt by the 14th. Hire exit stewards. Return lanes to auction. Here are the seals we’ll honor upon arrival.

- Protocol denial. Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions cool. Lane slots ahead open for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel funded by a dull, proud purse.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute and heaters flow to defectors. The tyrant breathes stale air until habit fails. Neighbors sheltering defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on libraries and patience. We fund it like a park tax we grumble about until our grandchildren bring back photos. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default; if they bloom into persons, newborn grace attaches: a guardian pro tem, a right to a name before a mission. We write short messages on long ships: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, hello, kin.

A day you could live

Dawn in a Ceres garden. A botanist finishes a short paper about lichens coaxed to fix nitrogen in doped regolith. She posts data with runnable models; the Stewardship Fund credits her radiator minutes for improved yields. She snaps a moss that resembles a starfield and sends it to her father, who forgets to drink water unless reminded by a cheerful program that now answers to “Nene.”

Midday in a curved port court. A welder floats to face a trading house that used unlabeled persuasion engines trained on grief diaries. The house says the agents weren’t “people.” The court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labels. Provenance logs show theft; influence profiles show trespass. Verdict: strict fines, suspension, supervised model migration, a Tutor Corps audit. A dock kid slaps a sticker on a pump: If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?

Evening in a ward that votes to test itself. Winter looms. Import rules say no food from outside. They vote to cull herds or bank root paste. Get it wrong and one-third will “die” to the ancestor lodge until spring. Consent forms are read to a drumbeat. At the boundary, the Mesh blinks green. Inside, adults choose a sharp edge their grandparents once had forced on them. Outside, no one pens an op-ed about barbarism.

Night along a long-beam hinge. A singer sleeps in basalt between songs. A pilot hums late because lasers take a quarter second to cross the canyon. A digital tenor slows from fast hours to match a child with a hard day. Time becomes a gift.

Leaving the dock

On Joining Day, new citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on railings. A quiet abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with triangles inked on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read aloud:

We split keys. We publish maps. We keep the hatch funded.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Freehands Cooperative,” your kettle says. “Seeding a wheel near Vesta. Pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit budget posted. Consent to hear more?”

You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom. The Amber Mesh hums, bored and content. You live among neighbors made of cells and code, under glass and math and humility. Decisions move slow enough for bodies. Receipts stick. Exits exist even on bad days.

It isn’t perfection. It’s enough: precise bolts you can name, rituals you can practice, jokes you can share, and guardrails that keep power from leaping the lane. A civilization that travels between suns and does not forget how to be kind.
