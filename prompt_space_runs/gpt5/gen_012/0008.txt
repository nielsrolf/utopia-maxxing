The Beacon Accord

Step into the arrival hall on Teller Station and you can smell boiling cardamom and machine oil. Above the inner lock, five thumb-length lanterns glow steady against brushed steel. Children point and name them before they learn their letters because nothing here works without them.

- Step: a little arrow angled outward. It means there is always a way to leave that doesn’t require anyone’s blessing.

- Keep: a ring bisected by a line. Take your name, your ledgers, your backups, your kin-ties when you go.

- Weave: two threads crossing. Split your mind, join again if you both agree, do it where the rules are patient and the tea is hot.

- Brake: a red bar. Power that bites must show receipts before and after, if the physics allows, and it times out on a clock you can see.

- Sky: a broken arc over dots. Orbits, beams, lanes, cold, and clean channels are commons; private control wears leases and calendars, never crowns.

They are not decoration. The lanterns are wired to budgets and valves; their laws live in the firmware of doors, the schedules of beams, and the public ledgers that bored teenagers audit for stipends. Together they are called the Beacon Accord, and they are not a state. They are an operating manual for living across planets, stations, caves, barges, and server reefs without giving any one frightened hand a lever that can bend the rest.

The Warm Arc and the Cold Span

The Accord begins from physics and manners: light-time won’t bend for meetings; fast minds can draft but they don’t get to ratify while slow lungs are still exhaling. We organized ourselves around two tempos and an invoice between them.

- The Warm Arc is the realm where breath and bread win over throughput: delta plains and mountain towns on Earth, lunar caverns lined with fungus wallpaper, Martian groves strung under glass, cylinders where mornings smell like sawdust and soup. Children can walk home here without being sold attention by a machine.

- The Cold Span is where heat falls into black and compute blooms like winter algae. Server reefs in Jupiter’s shade, Trojan agglomerations, caravans loitering out past Neptune where a day can be a subjective month. Upload co-ops and digital polities sprint here and then slow to talk.

Between them runs the Sunrise–Midnight Swap. Warm Arc towns agree to cap civic speed and publish quiet skies—noise, persuasion, and uplink windows throttled to human pace. Cold Span arrays sell “cold minutes”—audited cooling capacity and compute floors—and return weather and traffic models that don’t surprise poor neighborhoods before rich ones. Prices—watts, decibels, degrees—post on the Orbit Gazette in numbers a grandmother can verify with a pamphlet and a school telescope.

Ledgers a school can check

The Accord’s spine is three ledgers anyone can find at a kiosk and argue with in a library.

- The Orbit Gazette is a delay-tolerant, physics-pegged schedule of where things are allowed to be and shine. Beam corridors, mirror angles, approach arcs, quiet cones over telescopes and thinking groves; leases for orbits, spectrum, transfer windows, and radiator rights tied to hardware signatures. Arrays and mirrors sign their contexts with short-lived keys burned into devices a hobby club can audit with a radio and patience. If your light crosses a neighbor’s dawn, you owe shade bonds or a posted explanation.

- The Thermograph counts the dull thing that keeps us honest: energy draw and waste heat. Cooling is a commons. Declare one self and pull like twenty? Your radiator auctions pinch you to idle until you either lease more cold or slow your instances. The Thermograph pays out in two streams: a Civic Floor—air, water, health, transit, a stipend in compute and bandwidth, and an exit grant you can claim without asking every decade—and a local share that keeps lanes sharp, algae curtains clean, and tug crews sober and paid.

- The Origin Shelf is where persuasion and safety code live. If a message tries to bend law or budgets, it carries who paid, what data fed it, what levers it tunes, and at what speed. If code wakes a safety device, it carries where it was built and who reviewed it, proven without dumping anyone’s diaries. Teenagers collect bounties for catching cheats.

Floors on a sticker

Pinned to life-support panels and server racks across the Accord are laminated cards called the Pocket Floors. They’re strict, short, and written so a coughing person can parse them.

- No nonconsensual harm to persons. “Person” means any being that can tell a story about themselves across time, model another’s claim, and enter reciprocal commitments, regardless of substrate.

- No self-replication in shared environments without a stamped audit. Replicators carry Maker Seals; they listen for universal halt codes keyed by juries you can’t predict.

- No manufactured suffering—no unconsenting minds in pain, biological or simulated.

- Lifelines—air, water, Grayline safety code—are open, minimal, and revertible. Changes log and time out.

- Decisions that bind embodied lives ratify at embodied tempo. Fast drafts are welcome; fast binding is a sin.

- Rivalrous commons—corridors, spectrum, radiator windows, cave mouths—lease from the commons with visible clocks. Commons get paid first.

- Children, whether in bodies or code, learn to set boundaries, say no, and leave households without a maker’s or parent’s leave. Guardians answer to audits keyed to the child’s own preferences.

Machines that refuse drama

The Accord distrusts heroics. Three families of devices sit between swagger and catastrophe.

- Brake Boxes are yellow-gray appliances with glass you can break and handles fat enough for cold gloves. They crouch in triplicate anywhere a foolish command could make a hole: mass drivers, beam shapers, atmosphere valves, replication bays, city throttles. Any trained citizen can trigger a six-minute halt when a posted condition fails—wrong lane, forbidden hour, quiet cone pierced, a gene printer without its Seal. Halts force systems into narrow safety postures described on one-page cards. Every Brake writes its “why and when” into the Civic Log and lights a clock on the wall you can tap.

- Tri-Seal Keys wake anything that can push, cut, infect, or replicate from safe states. They require three signatures that cannot collude at speed: a local jury drawn by lot from people who put in civic hours this quarter; a bonded safety officer on site; and a remote operator—sometimes a digital citizen—running on audited hardware beyond local reach. Keys expire in minutes. Firmware coups don’t line up.

- Grayline is dumb code that enforces time limits and safety baselines and times itself out if it ever tries to be smart. It can starve a reactor, quarantine an infected fab, pause a vote when clocks drift past tolerances. It cannot set policy. It cannot keep running unless it keeps emitting public proofs of its limits. Its manuals are printed and greasy because someone is always tempted to be clever.

All compulsion—seizure, restraint, emergency override—lands in the Civic Log with who signed what, which law was cited, when the clock began and ended, and a checkable proof that the machine saw what it claimed. The Log has a chalkline annex where sloppy overreach is answered with jokes and footnotes savage enough to end careers without needing a jail.

Leases instead of thrones

Anything you could sit on out of spite leases out of the commons with clocks on it.

- Site Leases: crater rims, cave mouths, ice mouths, nickel seams, shady plane trees.

- Lane Leases: orbital slots, approach arcs, sail corridors, transfer windows.

- Band Leases: radio spectrum, loud cones for storms, quiet cones for telescopes and thought.

- Core Leases: compute tied to certified hardware and heat sinks.

- Glassworld Leases: sealed simulations that must label their physics, exits, guardianship, and clock like ships label lifeboats.

The Trackwrights Union—pilots, schedulers, modelers, auditors, and fastidious bores—posts base rents with runnable reasons. “Lane F-12 is dark between 04:00 and 07:00 local because burns at Hebe would throw you into a radio shadow” arrives with a plume plot, a checksum, and an impact graph your aunt can re-run on a library terminal.

Braided power in place of a capital

We stopped pretending one chamber could speak for hulls and minds. Authority travels in three cords that chafe each other on purpose.

- The Transit Syndic seats polities with hulls and atmospheres in rough proportion to lifetime resident hours. Its job is lanes and infrastructure: beam schedules, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions, window charts. It cannot legislate about persons.

- The Assembly of Minds seats citizens across substrates in proportion to recognized persons, with quotas by age and tempo. It writes the Public Kernel that guarantees a compute floor, defines personhood audits, sets copy and merge rules, zones attention, and fixes civic tempos so fast rooms don’t bind slow lives between breakfast and lunch.

- The Root Panel is a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, and auditors who stamp “valid” or “not” on changes to Brake Box firmware, Tri-Seal standards, signature schemes, and Red Lab blueprints. Members serve once and then cannot touch safety again for a decade. Minutes publish, or they are published.

A hinge called the Bridge Table holds cross-border parameters physics won’t keep local: how to price cold minutes against quiet nights; what latency to require for persuasion that crosses charters; how to handle a rock that sprouts fossils and lawsuits both. Half the seats come from places; half from persons. A measure fails if either half says it bulldozes custom beneath the personhood floor.

Anything that touches lifelines needs supermajorities in both chambers and a notice window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to be heard. Fast drafts are sport. Fast binding is mistrust.

Exits stocked, not sung

Freedom here is inventory and timetables, not poetry. Every charter escrows tugs, propellant, berths, foreign server minutes, encrypted lockers, and trained stewards sufficient to move at least ten percent of residents—wetware and code—inside six months. Receipts are public and recalc monthly. On every concourse, behind ochre doors, sit Embassy Rooms with three counters:

- Departures books the physics of leaving: hulls, berths, cores, insurance, customs.

- Mend pours tea while you and a co-op or an algorithm argue until the reasons are clear.

- Founders lends template charters and sim boxes that refuse to flatter.

Hide exits with fees or sorrow and your lanes gray, your bands hush, your cores idle above the floor, your radiator auctions thin. Small, polite interceptors called Swifts—uncrewed, with minds like seatbelts—arrive with cutters to unbolt docks if steel pretends to be law. Their models publish; their steps require juries drawn across tempos and substrates. When Swifts come home, schoolkids climb inside with oranges and questions.

Attention has neighborhoods

Speech is physics and economics; we ride it like tide, not like religion. Persuasion is zoned like sound.

- Quiet Courts carry only navigation and open science. No targeted persuasion. No fast models without a visible civic license. Children stroll here without armor for their minds.

- Commons Halls allow bounded debate and ordinary offers under public rate limits. Audience models belong to the town, not advertisers. People who like each other argue here.

- Carnivals invite the roar. Slang, satire, persuasion leagues under registry. You go to be dazzled or tired on purpose.

If a persuasion hopes to change law or budgets, it files its homework in the Reasons Shelf: plain claims in bus-driver language, escrowed models with error bars, runnable counterarguments. Citizens get stipends for catching cheats. Most people bind an Influence Filter to their Preference Keys: humor yes, grief no before coffee; analogies fine; timebox at fifteen minutes; no weaponized shame on festival days.

Digital neighbors, ordinary and protected

Personhood is substrate-neutral and boring on purpose. We recognize persons through the Thread Test, the Mirror Map, and the Pledge.

- Thread: you can defend a line of experience you care about against unwanted edits and show “I am still me” across restarts and moves.

- Mirror: you can represent another mind’s claims well enough that you would blush to ignore them.

- Pledge: you can understand, enter, keep, and revoke standard rights contracts.

Arrive by womb, by fab, by coaxing two thoughts into a third, or by careful reconstruction with consent. If you are too young or hurt, you get guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer to audits against your Intent Keys—a cryptographic store of your preferences about memory edits, merges, persuasion gates, and data use. Auditors prove compliance without reading your private record.

Rights have texture.

- The Public Kernel: minimum cores, storage, bandwidth, and I/O below which no recognized person can be throttled without due process on a visible clock.

- Interior privacy: courts can compel proofs; they cannot force raw introspection. Backups are yours. Unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault. Unauthorized deletion is murder.

- Tempo fairness: civic deliberation runs by default on a Common Beat—days and weeks. Anyone may summon a Harbor Room—turn-taking with certified rate limits and interpreters between metaphor, math, and scent.

- Body and substrate sovereignty: lease or build bodies under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented hand is assault.

Duties mirror them.

- Heat rent: cores warm neighbors. You pay externalities indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuces alive is a credit; heat that bakes corridors is a fine.

- Concurrency declaration: in elections and regulated arenas, you declare speed and active instance count. Hiding speed voids deals. A Lineage Token anchors one full franchise whether you run a single thread or a burst cloud.

- Replicator ethics: do not seed self-replicators outside audited boxes. Tools broadcast beacons Brake Boxes can hear. When a universal halt sounds, you accept it.

- Simulation welfare: no unconsenting minds in suffering. Reconstructing a human likeness beyond a fidelity threshold requires consent or a narrow, public license. A union of ethicists and engineers writes thresholds and pays bounties.

Splitcraft and Weaving

Treat forking like family, not a stunt. When you split—Splitcraft is the craft—you file a Twin Docket with a hashed state, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “field work,” “grief”), a compute lease for the new you above the floor, and whether you plan to rejoin. One branch keeps the anchor vote; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and nearby elections salt your impact until bonds decay.

Reunion happens in Weaving Houses. There is always tea. Often a comedian because laughter moves fear out of the hands. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs with zero-knowledge tools that verify belief equivalence without dumping diaries on the floor. If tempos differ, clocks are aligned gently. Either self can veto. When twins walk apart, no shame. The ache has a name: branch-ache. People bring soup.

Red Labs, valves, and receipts

Dangerous knowledge lives in Red Labs built from lists of ways the world can go wrong. Walls export one-way proofs to Brake Boxes to attest safety without leaking crown jewels. Under tight, public signals, Brake Boxes can starve a Red Lab in under a second. Twice those valves saved lives. Once they embarrassed everyone. Anniversaries keep bread and ink and fewer eulogies.

Rescue is a drill, not a posture

Between worlds, lanes post like tide charts. Brake Boxes watch for signature sputters. Mutual aid is written and rehearsed. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs and crews on four-hour readiness. Drills run like fire drills and publish. Twelve-year-olds follow rescues the way earlier kids followed football. Fail your call without extraordinary reason and you trigger an audit whose findings live in public for years. A failed rescue is a scar that stays visible until the next generation asks better questions.

Teeth without a throne

- Tri-Seal Keys split force on three clocks. No one takes a city at midnight with a push.

- Utility throttle lands exactly where cheating hurts. Weld an exit, lie on the Thermograph, refuse to post reasons, and your lanes slow, cores idle, bands hush, radiator auctions freeze. In a world where heat is money and brakes wear expensive, boredom and cost beat sermons.

- Time-limited leagues coalesce around pipes—beam networks, refinery chains, rescue nets—and expire by calendar unless they re-up with receipts. Secession drills are as dull as evacuation drills.

- The chalkline annex of the Civic Log ends bad practices with a laugh that carries receipts.

An ordinary day

At Market Five on Teller, Lila flips rice pancakes for miners, students, and two drones whose pilots adore the smell of sesame. At nine, her Counsel—a stubborn, single-client assistant that won’t act without a click and can be muted—pings her with a persuasion capsule: insert 45 milliseconds of jitter into beamed power that crosses inhabited orbits to damp a rare cascade failure. Provenance is clean; humor’s on low; grief levers are blocked by her Influence Filter. She taps “sleep a day,” reads it at lunch, and will vote tomorrow.

At ten, her slate joins a Swift jury. A maker club’s filament cloud drifted toward a trunk line near Hecuba Crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herd, or guard drones to bump it back. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad, and an anchor-paced digital historian speaking through a rented puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza that tastes faintly of lesson.

At noon in a Quiet Court, a wooden puppet lifts broth to lips it does not need while its main self hums near Neptune at 200× threading a drowned library into maps. Today they throttle to share soup with their embodied spouse. After, they help host a Harbor Room while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition. The historian translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines politely to “just speed up to keep up.”

In the afternoon, a Junction Court convenes under cedar joists and a wall that shows flows like soft rivers. Two branches of an engineer sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants to rejoin; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero-knowledge graphs flash; value alignments are tested with proofs, not pain. They cry and hug. One walks. The court writes a ruling as dry as a manual and as tender as an apology.

At dusk, the canopy shows stars, and the Chalkline scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; the apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will buy your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a brass Swift model and asks if he can join. Lila says: when you are fifteen and can explain a Brake Box without peeking.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A rim canton on Mars voted to deny personhood to digital citizens. No sermons flew. Exit was real. Digital neighbors left. A flood year came and pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a borrowed body coordinated three cantons through dust and cold with words everyone could catch. The vote thawed one clause at a time. The mentor from that winter now teaches Junction Court in a school with leaky windows and meticulous notes.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival dome because wages were hot and pride hotter. A Swift turned it to glitter that made a week of uncanny nights. The insurer dissolved. Directors learned “coercion budget” isn’t a suggestion. They apologized and renamed their hockey team to say what they’d learned.

A demagogue blanketed a Carnival with songs tuned to induce smallness. Influence Filters throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores decay like frost. One fork took a job on a fishing crawler and learned knots. One started therapy. One learned to sculpt frozen smoke that remembers rivers and sells enough to pay a heat bill.

In a Red Lab shadowed by Vesta, a bad sensor tripped the valve. Power died; the team swore; the failed part hangs on a red string in a children’s museum. The plaque explains error bars in story and in math.

How we walked here from now

No leap. Receipts accreted until they looked like custom.

- Late 2020s–early 2030s. A second debris storm kept kids indoors and soured insurers. Launches had to post ephemerides; rendezvous corridors became posted schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare forced “open firmware or no license” for life support. An early model-fueled persuasion scandal taught voters to demand provenance when synthetic sincerity tried to balance books.

- 2033–2044. Beamed power left the lab. Environmental groups traded skepticism for lanes-first rules: post safe corridors before money. A demo scorched cropland; supplier logs made liability boring and fast. Cities and ports adopted compulsion ledgers—drab at first, then addictive. People learned to ask for receipts instead of speeches.

- 2044–2056. Cislunar compute commons bloomed under cheap solar and cold shade, guarded by a “thermo meter” a seventh-grader could read. Towns carved quiet hours and budgeted Quiet Courts. Health improved. Tempers cooled. Early uploads were brittle but sustained continuity over seasons; courts recognized personhood where Thread, Mirror, and Pledge held. A demonstration cylinder in Earth orbit became an ugly, beloved field trip.

- 2056–2068. A beam lit a greenhouse wrong at dawn. A mass driver test sang too near a habitat. Six-minute halts any trained hand could invoke without calling a senator became law. Engineers demanded Tri-Seals and public code. Insurers forced the compromise the way insurers do. Two saves on camera ended arguments.

- 2068–2079. The Beacon Accord replaced backchannels with ledgers you can argue with. The first Swifts flew, nipped a stupid slug to glitter, and came home to be opened by schoolkids with oranges. Clean personhood law arrived in language good lawyers love and five-graders mock. The Common Beat for civic time matured. Attention zoning grew from municipal quiet hours that saved sleep and elections.

- 2079–2090. A fork swarm tried to swing a vote and met Lineage Tokens, Junction Courts, disentanglement throttles, and jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and tears; lanes grayed, cores cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and teaches welding now. His jokes are better.

- 2090–2104. The Sunrise–Midnight Swap formalized. Reefs sold cold to gardens; gardens bought quiet cones; poets were paid in cold to build diligent archives. Red Labs got kill valves and oaths.

- 2104–2116. The Root Panel refused a nervy firmware change to let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The chalkline annex grew teeth. A mission to unbolt an Embassy ring from a stubborn charter broadcast with captions and ended with soup and a written agreement to disagree.

How coordination feels most days

On the Moon’s nightside, a cylinder sets festival uplinks by hand count under lanterns. A reef in Neptune’s lee elects chairs by time-weighted escrow because sprints make odd politics. A guild of solar tugs near Mercury writes bylaws in doggerel to keep meetings gentle. When plans cross borders with heavy consequences, a ritual called Three-Side Proof engages: the proposer posts a runnable reason; neighbors post countermodels; the Trackwrights stitch the overlapping common parts and send the rest back to be simplified until grudges can’t hide in pretty complexity. Decisions that bind slow lives ratify at slow speed. If you try to jam them, Grayline blinks and reminds you clocks aren’t feelings.

What it feels like

You feel witnessed—not by a throne, but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to shift tempos: the relief when a Brake snaps a relay; the slow draw of a jury convening across orbits; the winter patience when a river back on Earth decides what boats it will tolerate. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you visit a Quiet Court and learn the pleasant ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that harden into charters five years later after someone scrubs the cartoons off.

A closing day

Miro turns forty-six under LEDs pretending to be late afternoon and doing a good job. Their partner lives mostly as a botanist in the Cold Span and slows for breakfast to watch steam curl from tea. Miro spends the afternoon at Harbor pace teaching teenagers to set their Influence Filters and recheck Orbit Gazette entries. In the evening they enter Loom Mode with a reef collective to test a radiator algorithm they half trust. Next week they will vote on adding micro-delays to beams that cross crowded orbits. They may say no. They may say yes. Either way, their reasons will be legible enough for a child to poke.

Before bed they open their Intent Keys and update a clause about memory edits: not while grief is fresh; ask me twice; three sunsets apart; protect names. They scroll the Chalkline—the only gossip column that matters. They watch a Swift teardown with their niece and explain why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. They message a friend in a quiet Mars valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. They slow to hear the oboe breathe between notes.

Above the hatch, the five lanterns burn patient.

Step. Keep. Weave. Brake. Sky.

Not paradise. Practice with receipts.

Enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross between night and morning without letting anyone become a god by accident. Enough to last because we remember what power does to people and what public proofs do to power.
