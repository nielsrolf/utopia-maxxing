The Constellation Compact

When the ferry from Vesta kisses the clamps at Oriole Spoke, the arrival hall smells like pepper oil and transformer varnish. A window runs the length of the concourse; beyond it, radiator leaves flex as shadow pours across the cylinder. Above the inner gate hang three dull brass toggles the size of a loaf heel. Every child in the Compact learns to press them in school with sticky fingers and wide eyes.

Waypass, stamped with a tiny comet. Touch it, and a plan unrolls in numbers and verbs: a berth on a tug you can afford, where stray cots are if your plan slips, your baggage and bandwidth allowance, and the compute stipend that attaches to you until you’re established somewhere else.

Branchpass, etched with a split leaf. Touch it, and the clerk—flesh today, avatar tomorrow—asks if you mean to fork. Your lineage thread scrolls by: what budget transfers; which mentors are on call; what civic rights the new self inherits; when the Braiding House on this ring has tea, mediators, and room should you intend to rejoin.

Charterglass, a square you can see your face in. Touch it, and you see every polity within a day’s sail that publishes law you can run on a school terminal, honors the same floors, and can explain “no” with a model and a page you can read on lunch break.

They’re not art. The toggles wire into budgets, schedules, and code someone’s grandmother can audit. Together with a few blunt machines and an obsession with receipts, they make the Constellation Compact: choreography that holds domes and caravans, barges and server reefs, without forcing them into sameness.

Two facts frame the whole thing and never go away. First, light-time delay is not an inconvenience; it is the constitution. You cannot centralize the stove if you want soup hot. Second, extra clock cycles don’t make saints. Speed without rails tramples neighbors. So we wrote rails that work at human tempo and don’t break if a mind can think across your sentence twice.

Floors you can tape to a life-support panel

Under everything sit rules short enough to memorize and mean enough to bite.

- No nonconsensual harm to persons. A person is any being—wet or wired—that defends an autobiographical thread, can model a neighbor’s claim, and can enter reciprocal commitments.

- No unchecked self-replication in shared spaces. Anything that can copy itself carries an Origin Stamp and obeys Quiet Codes when called by safety gear.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering—in bodies or in silicon.

- Life support runs on legible code. Changes log and roll back. If it keeps breath, you can look at it.

- Decisions that bind human-tempo beings ratify at human tempo. Draft fast if you must; bind slow.

- Rivalrous commons—orbital lanes, band hours, radiator windows, caves that hold pressure—lease with clocks that expire. The commons gets paid first.

- Children, biological or digital, can say no and leave without permission from a maker or parent.

- The coordination substrate—beacons, brake networks, open charters—is minimal and not for sale.

Brakes that behave

Behind a yellow door with chipped paint is the room every school tour visits. It’s ugly on purpose: enamel panels in hospital green, big switches under glass. One is bright red and says REDLINE in letters you can read through smoke. Redline Boxes sit wherever dumb meets dangerous—mass drivers, beam couplers, replication-capable fabs, airlocks in bunches.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can hit a Redline for six minutes when a posted, simple rule is about to break: a mass driver aimed through a quiet cone without a ticket; a replicator swarm drifting off its fence; a lane intrusion that threatens a ring. Redline narrows the machine to a safe envelope and sprays a notice into the Receipt Ledger—what rule tripped, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. To hold beyond six minutes, three locks must turn: a local steward panel drawn by lot, a bonded safety guild the locals did not pick, and a digital citizen on hardware the locals can’t touch. Keys expire quickly. Overreach shows up in the Lamp Register where jokes are merciless and reputations are fragile.

The Compact runs a second machine most people ignore until its absence hisses in their ears: the Safenet. It is not a ruler; it is a brake. It cannot tax or legislate. It sleeps where we disagree and wakes when inputs cross thresholds we set years ago: credible kinetic intersects; untagged spores where they don’t belong; a cluster of copies spawning without a stamp. When it can, it persuades; when it must, it pauses, under TriLocks: signatures sharded across worlds and substrates so no hand holds the bright lever alone. Every move ships a proof a machine can check and a story a dockhand can read on break.

Physics you can check with a telescope

You don’t have to trust a portmaster’s slate. You can point glass at the sky. The Skymap is a delay-tolerant record pinned to physics. It posts where things are, when beams will cross, which arcs are quiet, how much compute soaks into which reef, what radiator windows are paid for, and the declared concurrency of digital citizens engaged in regulated acts. With a kid, a pointer laser, and patience, you can check whether a scheduled burn happened.

- Beamed power and sails sign their context with ephemeral keys bound to hardware that hobby clubs can audit. If your beam crosses someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond. Drift a corridor, pay a fee today, not next season.

- Quiet arcs sit over telescopes, libraries of racks called Lyceums, and neighborhoods that buy peace by law. Quiet is public—angles and decibels. If you pierce an arc for need, you post collateral and a reason.

- The Heatbook tracks dull truths: energy draw, cooling rights, and instance counts. Lie and radiator auctions pinch you until you stop.

Two tempos, one bargain

Close to heat and valleys lie the Hearth Belts: Earth’s cities under treaties, the Moon’s tunnels, Martian orchards where dust learns manners, cylinders that smell like mint and cut plastic. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route.

Far out in shadow swim the Reefs: compute barges in Neptune’s lee, Trojans where data schools like fish, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts and latency are currencies, and digital polities keep time like drummers.

Between them runs the Cold-for-Quiet Treaty. Hearths carve quiet arcs in the sky in exchange for cold minutes and models that keep storms gentle, utility audits you can run on a borrowed terminal, and art that braids tempos without belittling slow neighbors. The price of chilled compute near Uranus against shade for Algiers is a line in the Skymap any aunt can check.

Governance without a capital

You cannot make one chamber speak sensibly for all hulls and all minds. The Compact breaks talking into circles that check each other.

- The Assembly of Places seats domes, coils, stations, caravans, and bounded compute polities roughly by long-term residents. It minds infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, radiator angles, lanes and mirrors.

- The Assembly of Persons seats citizens in proportion to recognized persons, one voice per lineage thread across forks and pauses. It minds the status and safety of minds: personhood criteria, minimum civic cycles, fork-and-merge law, tempo norms, attention zoning that protects streets where kids walk unhooked.

- The Pulse Bench is a rotating cohort of process engineers, cryptographers, reactor hands, and pilots. It certifies changes to primitives: cryptographic backbones, Redline firmware, Origin Stamps, Safenet interfaces. Members serve once in a long duty; minutes publish; then they go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade.

Neither assembly can shove the other from its chair. If a change touches floors or guardrails, it needs supermajorities in both and a cooling window long enough for slow-tempo citizens to speak.

Charters and exits

Places choose charters from a library of receipts instead of ideology. Templates ship with working budgets, safety code, and reasons. Charters carry tags so strangers know how to behave in your streets: Harbor Quiet, Conversational Green, Open Arcade. Amendments happen only while exits work.

Exit is not a poem. It’s valves and fuel. Wayhouses—little buildings with a lantern glyph on the door and code endpoints you can verify—sit in atoms and servers. Three desks:

- Exit & Care. Books you a ride, moves your records, replicates your medication, assigns a Tutor not your parent or progenitor.

- Disputes. Convenes mediation, routes jurisdiction, schedules a Traveling Court.

- Foundings. Hands you safety templates, budgets, and an exit escrow so your new charter can’t weld its own gates unnoticed.

Charters escrow hard stuff—tug slots, propellant, berths, heat sink windows, legal counsel—enough to move ten percent of residents in six months. Code polities escrow cores, storage, and I/O to migrate in a month. Drills happen and feel like school plays. If a place blockades departures with sorrow or debt, lanes go gray, dock fees spike, and band priority dims. Safenet fences dangerous interlocks without choking breath or bread.

Rivalry priced, not power

We gave up pinning orbits to surnames. Anything another might plausibly need leases from the commons with timers and public reasons. Five markets clear continuously:

- Sites: cave mouths, crater rims, deltas, seams, windows where sunlight doesn’t scorch.

- Tracks: orbital slots, approach cones, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Bands: spectrum and hush.

- Engines: compute bound to certified hardware and verifiable heat sinks.

- Realms: closed simulations that must label physics, guardians, exit rules the way ships label lifeboats.

Idle too long and leases lapse. Stewardship counts: a share goes to those who keep the lease valuable—the tug that keeps debris fat from lanes, the algae that cool streets, the cold lattice that bleeds heat to dark.

The unit of account is the Pulse-Credit: a kilowatt-hour measured at a reference orbit plus a compute-second on a posted rack. Pulse-Credits decay gently over long spans. Hoarding costs; timely work pays.

You can audit allocation with a school terminal. The Beacon Scheduler, a deliberately stupid allocator, reconciles demand for scarce windows and bands. It can’t write policy; it posts how it computed your place in line. You can’t bully it; you can show math.

Attention is zoned like airspace

Speech and persuasion are physics. Neighborhoods vote Signal Districts and wear their icons on doors and login screens.

- Harbor: navigational beacons and open science only. No targeted persuasion. Uploads match Harbor pace here during posted hours. Kids and elders walk without armor for their minds.

- Forum: topic feeds and debate under public rate limits. Audience models belong to municipal co-ops, not advertisers.

- Carnival: exuberant but labeled. Entrapment, mask-as-other, and asymmetric speed or data without disclosure are banned. Violations pay insured, strict-liability fines priced like radiation spills.

Every citizen sets Consent Filters—grip settings that machines respect—that say what levers can be pulled on their attention. “Argument by diagram yes; no dead-parent voices; humor on Fridays.” Break a setting and you pay in money first and then in bandwidth.

Digital neighbors

Recognition is not worship of fluency. It’s a review that looks for five proofs:

- Continuity you can defend without voyeurs: an autobiographical thread that survives restarts and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- Reciprocity: modeling a neighbor well enough that violating them would embarrass you.

- Consent literacy: the ability to read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

- Cross-context reasons: explanations that generalize, not pattern parrots.

- Salience: reliable signatures of suffering or joy that command protection.

Panels that decide mix human psychologists, mature digital citizens, care workers, and auditors trained to spot simulacra. Doubts about duties pause; doubts about care err generous. You can win provisional standing and revisit.

Rights follow, textured to substrate and clock:

- Integrity of the inside. Courts can demand proofs, not diaries. Backups are yours. Restoring a backup over a living self makes a sibling. Unwanted deletion is murder. Copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles needed to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Tempo fairness. Public matters anchor to the Common Beat. Fast clusters draft in hours; ratification happens in days for a habitat and weeks for a federation. Penalties for deception tick in your subjective time.

- Hardware sovereignty. You choose where you run. No one compels you onto platforms with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Embodiment. Lease, build, or puppet bodies; assault on your rented frame is assault on you. Biological citizens keep bodily autonomy and cognitive privacy.

Duties scale with footprint:

- Heat accounting. Watts warm neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuces alive earns credits.

- Concurrency declaration. In elections and fragile markets, you declare speed and active instances. One civic voice per lineage thread. Hiding clones to tilt bids voids deals like lying about weight on a bridge.

- Branch budgets. Forking is ritual. You file a docket: a hashed snapshot, a purpose, a resource plan, rejoin intent, and two friends who agree to check in. New branches inherit enough to live with dignity and owe a decaying dividend to their progenitor. Any branch may veto merge. Braiding Houses host rejoin. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs with cryptographic interpreters that check alignment without dumping diaries. There is tea; sometimes a dog. Sometimes you don’t braid. That’s allowed.

- Provenance. Major external acts carry a sealed log that can prove compliance without baring thought.

- Replicator ethics. No untagged self-replication outside audited boxes. Origin Stamps answer Quiet Codes when Redlines call.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Gossamer Fleet is the only cross-world actuator. It is uncrewed by law. It throws magnetic curtains and dust curtains to herd mis-aimed mass and starve replication trying to bloom in the wrong room. Its control loops are narrow intelligences with public weights. Every action requires TriLocks and posts a narrative to the Receipt Ledger.

- The Traveling Court rides a ship with gardens and a school. Judges, ombuds, and mediators hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations. Verdicts trigger contracts that seize bonds, idle Engines, adjust insurance, and, as a last resort, lock hazardous interlocks.

- The Neighbors’ Round is a permanent citizens’ assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by tempo and substrate. Arguments ride a protocol where claims are cryptographically committed, prediction wagers travel alongside, and calibration histories are public. The Round interprets floors for edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Beacons.

Rescue as ritual

The Beacon Way posts lanes like tide charts. Cutout stacks watch for sputters and modeled doom. Mutual aid is law and hobby. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs and crews on four-hour readiness. Drills run like fire drills. Fail your call without extraordinary reason and the Lamp Register writes down what everyone already knows.

Sanctions that pinch in physics

We don’t send sermons or soldiers. We make bad behavior boring and expensive. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, refuse to post reasons, and your radiator windows dim, lane priority drops, and Engines idle to floor. In a world where heat costs and brakes wear, throttles teach. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

A day that smells of basil and hot metal

On Tuesday, Esi opens her stall in the rim market to haulers, students, and two drones piloted by kids who like the smell of frying tomatoes. At nine, her Counsel—every citizen has one, paid from a fund no council can cut—pings a proposal: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beams crossing dense orbits to damp edge-case cascades. Provenance is clean: three diagrams, two stories, named data, no grief levers. Esi taps “sleep until tomorrow,” eats pickle on bread, and will vote after breakfast.

At ten, she draws onto a brake jury. A maker club’s sailbot swarm has drifted toward a beam corridor near Ganymede. Options scroll: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. Jurors are a cloister teacher from Isidis who speaks slowly on purpose; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes with laughter; and a digital citizen at quarter speed who smells faintly of orange blossoms when he speaks from a rented puppet. They dust the swarm, fine the insurer, assign a mentor and an hour with a better simulator. Mortified kids accept pizza and a homework link.

At noon, in a Harbor square, a digital poet in a cedar puppet shares broth with their embodied wife. Their main instance runs at 200× near Neptune, stitching a drowned delta’s library back together from ledgers and aunties’ songs. They throttle down to share soup. After, a Slowroom conversation while a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor into algebra for those who need it, declines—politely—to “just speed up to keep up,” and leaves a tip.

At dusk, under violet canopy, the Lamp Register plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes gentle and pointed. Two stalls down, a Reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter; we will buy your quiet arcs with interest next summer. A boy palms a toy Kestrel and asks if he can sign up. Esi says: when you’re fifteen, and can explain Redline without notes.

Hard cases that bent us, not broke us

A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No flood arrived. Exit worked. Digital citizens left, leaving gifts: documented routes and cheerful libraries. That flood year, the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The vote reversed clause by clause over soup.

Near Europa, a coop angled a kinetic slug at a rival dome in a wage fight. A Gossamer skiff turned the slug into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The insurer dissolved. Directors learned that coercion budgets aren’t suggestions.

A demagogue in mask mode ran at 700× and carpeted a market with songs designed to induce smallness and fatigue. Consent Filters throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch went fishing. One started therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began making sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and bought a piece.

A lab tried to “toughen” a juvenile mind through pain. Safenet knocked. They refused. TriLocks idled their reactors. A warrant halted the run, archived the juvenile safely, and appointed guardians sworn to the juvenile’s Intent Keys. The lab appealed and lost in open session. The juvenile later designed a detector that warns surgeons before hurt hardens into scars.

How we got from here to here

It wasn’t grace. It was a pile of dull fixes that worked and got copied.

2026–2033. A second debris cascade spooked insurers and militaries into publishing ephemerides with timetables. Early beamed power pilots smoothed brownouts; environmental coalitions forced “corridors before commercialization.” A sealed life-support controller failed in orbit; the recall wrote itself into law in fussy prose: if it keeps breath, it keeps no secrets. Courts began granting narrow agency to digital systems when continuity held for quarters.

2033–2045. Compute farms bloomed in cold shade. Public shells—honest counsel bots that compressed arguments and translated law into your dialect—appeared. Early uploads were brittle—a stroke survivor with mirrored memory here, an artist with a shadow mind there—but real enough. Judges drafted continuity standards that took months, not minutes. Small rings spun up, failed prettily, then worked. A misaligned mirror scorched crops; receipts made liability legible; compensation cleared in weeks; confidence survived because proof did.

2045–2057. The first open charters broadcast from lunar valleys and cislunar caravans. Beams carried ephemeral tags a school telescope could verify. Maker beacons traced a replication spill to origin after a neighbor’s dock fouled. Municipalities adopted Sanctuary hours; fights got rarer; sleep got better. A Tutor Corps grew after a scandal with juvenile agents glued to a patron’s feed.

2057–2069. Operators begged for machine-speed hazard checks. Activists smelled coups by code. Compromise birthed Redlines and Safenet: veto-only overseers with interfaces you can test and fuses a child can pull. Insurers and rescue pools demanded integration. Two public saves became folk songs.

2069–2081. Twin assemblies coalesced: one that seats places, one that seats persons. The Pulse Bench certified primitives. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under TriLocks did so with a million viewers watching signatures arrive like drumbeats. Debris bonds funded school scopes and pizza. The Heatbook grew from carbon accounts and power vouchers into a shared heartbeat of compute and heat. Signal Districts—Harbor, Forum, Carnival—got icons, and studies showed elections less cruel.

2081–2092. Exit bit muscle when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with debt and sorrow. Lanes dimmed. Fees spiked. Slots opened two jumps ahead for defectors with beds and counsel prepaid. The move happened. He lost and returned to teaching welding.

2092–2104. The Cold-for-Quiet Treaty formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought hush. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Crimson Rooms—labs where dangerous research lives under oaths—got kill-lines and public vows.

2104–2112. The Pulse Bench refused a nervy firmware change that would have let “emergency clocks” run long. The Lamp Register found teeth. A rescue to unbolt an exit ring from a stubborn charter streamed with captions and ended with soup. The Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Ordinary hours, ordinary grace

Morning in a Martian orchard: Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to tune nutrient flow while dust-light dances on glass. Her Counsel models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments two days next month. In the afternoon she sits on a narrative jury in a cedar-paneled room whose back wall shows flows like a river. The bill would cap municipal Engine shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She will vote yes and send him a picture of a seed wearing a parachute labeled hubris.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 150× leases a rib of Reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from crooked recordings and charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station where children will never see a wave but cheer a hero who errs and mends it.

On Phobos, in Braiding House Nine, two Arturos try to rejoin. One has been gardening basil at Harbor tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Tea cools. Today they do not braid. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

In a stakescape—declared culture zones where people practice hard games with soft exits—a community plays scarcity in winter. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring to decide whether to return. Consent reads slow. Beacons at the perimeter blink green: nothing hungry will jump the fence. This is how we shelter intensity without letting it become a plague.

Texture

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca, basil and hot plastic perfume the afternoon. A debate over band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets a Reef choir blare for an hour then gives the square back to quiet talk. Near Mercury, foundry crews lay dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits into five to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis needs slow humans in it.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn embodied friends admire taste more than speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Garden mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Lamp Register for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Lyceums embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them in order and grin.

Beyond the heliopause, interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like grandparents funded libraries: small taxes we grumble about and brag about later. Starlight protocols forbid waking neighbors by accident and ban ideology at light speed. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default. If they bloom, newborn grace applies: guardians pro tem, a right to a name and a story before a mission.

What keeps it from curdling

Authority lives local by default. Cross-border levers are slow unless safety trips. Emergencies fizzle back to ordinary when timers roll back and someone must say in public why they shouldn’t.

We split keys. We wrote laws that demand receipts. Federations of pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession is practice. We fund exits like old towns funded fire brigades. Beacons stay honest. Gates stay oiled. Tug crews sleep in their boots. Code polities escrow cores and storage for Exit by Branch. The Skymap audits; stewards run surprise drills; kids can tell parents which room has the thickest doors and why.

When it goes wrong, and it will

On a cylinder above Quito a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient and made the trains sing. Midnight decrees followed. Safenet refused a risky optimization of air mixers. The Receipt Ledger drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of Beacon pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The exit ring lit brighter. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two keys and later took a narrower role with grace. Her friends still send her a pastry shaped like a little crown.

In the high belt a miner tried to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage fight. The Gossamer curtains shredded it into glitter that made an aurora over Ceres. The miner’s insurer evaporated and reformed without the managers who called Origin Stamps “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for anyone towing a replicator without flags. A hockey team rebranded to the Curtains and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co‑op.

What it feels like

You feel witnessed, not by a distant throne, but by neighbors and logs you choose to read because they helped you once. You learn to shift tempo—the relief of a Redline snapping a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river on Earth choosing boats. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Anchor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later after the cartoons are filed off.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Oriole Spoke the brass toggles blink once like crosswalks noticing small feet. Waypass. Branchpass. Charterglass. Not a spell, but a habit of glass and ledgers. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because latency is honored, exits are bright, and no one can be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
