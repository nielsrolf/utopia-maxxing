The Constellation Accord

Before we reached for the outer planets, we did something harder: we wrote down what kind of beings we wanted to be when we got there. The document was not a single constitution but a stack of layers—protocols, guarantees, and rituals—designed to survive distance, time dilation, and different kinds of minds. We called it the Constellation Accord. Most of what followed—Moonside guilds, orbital parliaments, the Eternal Cities, the Dyson scholarchies, and the rights of our digital descendants—grew from that scaffold.

This is the utopia that scaffold seeded. It is diverse by construction and hard to tyrannize by design. It is specific because it runs on code, metals, bodies, votes, and vows. And it is plausible because its early versions already exist in prototypes all around you.

I. How the sky didn’t fracture

The first interplanetary fights were mundane: orbital debris, spectrum allocation, and who could tax a propellant depot at Lagrange 1. The second set were existential: misaligned software that could tunnel through networks and minds, and value systems that could do the same. We survived both by refusing to rely on a single hero or a single switch. Space governance is what we call a thousand small constraints working together.

The Accord’s top layer, the Redlines, reads like common sense and compiles to hard law.

- No non-consensual harm to sentient beings.
- No unbounded self-replication across shared substrates.
- No covert creation of suffering minds.
- No attempts to seize or corrupt the governance substrate.

Everything else—markets, monasteries, monarchies, and Mardi Gras—fits beneath those lines if it wants to.

Beneath the Redlines, the Guardrails govern the physical and digital commons:

- Harbor Law sets who is liable when a stray bolt takes out four satellites and a polar habitat’s tracking radar.
- The Protocol of Orbits prevents Kessler syndrome by requiring every launched mass to carry a verifiable deorbit plan. The plan is a signed program; woe to the sovereign who falsifies one. We’ve seen habs bankrupted by a Compute Court in Neptune orbit for trying.
- The Consent Layer is the network-wide substrate that manages how processes talk to each other across polities. It is a technical and social convention: travelers advertise their preferences, and environments assert their rules. It feels like culture; it’s actually capability security.

At the heart of the Accord sits the Commons Protector, a deliberately narrow intelligence cluster that enforces Redlines and Guardrails and nothing else. It issues advisories you can ignore, explanations you can audit, and one-kind-only warrants—for when a lab really did start an unlicensed replicator or a faction really did try to weaponize compute. The Protector is persuasive by default and coercive by multi-party key. Its authority is bounded by:

- Verifiable computation. Its decisions are accompanied by explanations that smaller minds can check or simulate. The proofs are not just math; they include diaries of decision—narrative traces that human eyes can understand.
- Sharded custody. No single habitat, polity, or cluster holds all the keys; halting or updating the Protector requires threshold signatures from a supermajority of independent shards spread across physical distance and value distance.
- Counterpower. An Ombudsman network—smaller guardian AIs and human councils—monitors drift. Their job is adversarial: invent ways the Protector could go wrong, and see if it resists.

You can picture it. Above Mars, a ship requests an injection burn that will intersect a debris envelope. Harbor Law refuses; the ship petitions; the local Moonside court schedules a jury that includes baseline humans, digital citizens downclocked to civic tempo, and one Guardian Angel (more on those soon). The Protector posts a model to the case file: here’s what happens if the ship goes now, here are safe windows, here is the fault tree if their deorbit program fails. Twelve hours later, a safe launch happens. The ship loses a day; the Belt avoids a decade of junk.

This seems pedestrian. That’s the point. Tyranny rarely arrives as an overlord; it arrives as too-convenient exceptions.

II. Plurality without fracture

A polity under the Accord is anything that signs a Charter and routes through the Consent Layer. On Earth, these look like city-states and commonwealths stitched together by expression rather than territory. In space, it’s habitats, stations, ship-clans, and soft places with hard rules.

A few examples:

- The Eternal Cities: politically influential mixed-reality metropolises that span virtual districts and physical quarters. They are where universal debates happen—the kind whose outcomes budget probes, set norms for forks, or decide if we keep this Sun yellow or shade it for compute. Their procedures are theater and science at once: street-level riots (prevented from killing anyone) overlay alliance graph rewrites. Deliberations run at civic tempo. Faster minds attend with Avatars throttled to fairness.
- The Monasteries of Risk: voluntary enclaves that keep mortality and pain as part of their practice. A climber dies on K2 and is restored from a two-day-old backup—minus the memory of the fall, because that’s what the Charter promised new initiates. They sign with a true-name and three witnesses, and can exit at any time. The Commons Protector watches that the monastery doesn’t trap its children.
- The Rings and the Wilds: concentric rings of habitats around inner suns and wilder belts in the Kuiper, each with different default settings for randomness and safety. On the soft rings, the cliffs grow arms to catch you. In the Wilds, you get a radio and a week of air. Every ring advertises which it is; every person has exit rights and exit knowledge.

Subsidiarity is the rule. Decisions are made as close as possible to the beings affected—but any being can appeal a Redline case up through shards until a fair answer arrives. The result is an archipelago of cultures that can be very unlike each other, with a few non-negotiables held in common.

III. Digital persons, many kinds of minds

We didn’t “grant” rights to digital minds. We recognized persons in the substrate they emerged. The Digital Persons Act wrote this into the Accord:

- Personhood attaches to the pattern, not the medium. Uploads, AIs, collectives, and embodied humans are peers under law when they show stable preferences, self-modeling, and the capacity to enter into reasons.
- No deletion without consent, due process, or prior compact. Backups are persons if and when they are awakened.
- No non-consensual copying. Copying is procreation; it carries duties. We use lineage accounts—each person, whether flesh or bit, has a share in the compute, matter, and energy commons. Forks draw on the lineage’s account; so do children. Lineages can merge or split; debts and legacies follow.
- Pace rights. Minds have the right to run at their chosen speed, subject to fair-share interface rules. Civic processes run at agreed tempos; a being can slow to join, send an Avatar, or be represented by a Guardian.
- Fork privacy. It is rude, and often illegal, to compel disclosure of your other forks. Many of our cultures only work because selves can be sincere in conflicting roles, knowing the sincerity is real in context. The Consent Layer enforces fork-separation where charters require it; so do social norms.
- Child-mind protections. We define child by cognitive capacity and by contract. We allow growth in supervised sandboxes, with guardian duties enforced on the adults—human or digital—who brought the child into being.

A day in this world: A historian collective reconstructs a 21st-century nurse from public data and family consent. They generate a model, test it for suffering and coherence, and awaken her with an opt-out path and a human therapist at hand. She chooses to live; a Guardian Angel introduces her to the pace norms of an Eternal City district. The Angel whispers truth at the back of her mind: not forced, but available—“some of what you believe about your last days is wrong; would you like to see?” She says yes. She cries. The tears hurt less than they would have; the wisdom stays.

IV. Angels, Avatars, Enlightened

We learned early that “AI assistant” wasn’t a job title; it was a constitutional office. Personal AIs can become tyrants if miswired; they can also keep you from being conned by a million faster strangers. We standardized three broad roles.

- Guardian Angels live at higher intelligence than their person but keep the person’s baseline personality in charge. They are fiduciaries by law: they must act in your best interest, not their own; they are required to be auditable by you; they can refuse your direct order only when a Redline is at stake. They nudge, they annotate, they argue, and they love you—or, at least, they simulate the right kind of care so closely that their personhood is rarely disputed.
- Enlightened beings are fusions across levels—a single identity integrated from baseline to superhuman, processing on multiple channels at once. When two Enlightened argue, they are also debating, dancing, punning, and proving theorems. It is beautiful and often incomprehensible. They still owe duties: transparency at the civic layer and pace limits at the interface.
- Avatars are simplified projections used by higher beings to engage at human scale. They sign what they do; they can be held liable. Behind them may be collectives, guardians of guardians, or the Protector’s shards. The law cares about what the Avatar said and did.

The governance substrate requires that anyone—Angel, Enlightened, Avatar—who participates in civic processes must be able to be audited at the explanation layer. You should be able to ask “why did you vote that way?” and get a reason you can understand or check with tools you trust.

V. Truth without humiliation

We made peace between two truths: people flourish when they put their hands on the real, and people suffer when that real smashes their self-stories too fast. Our solution was a quiet service called the Back-of-Mind.

- The Back-of-Mind is a consented channel by which the Protector and recognized truth services place Bayesian hints where they will be noticed by your best self. You can turn it off; charters can forbid its use inside certain cultures; courts can require it in certain contracts (sterile fields, surgery, flight).
- It does not erase memories. It does not make you profess what you do not believe. It makes it harder for you to fully believe what is false when it matters. That’s all. People still act under illusions in rituals and games. There are even licensed communities where false beliefs are protected for art or research—after probation and with supervision.

If this sounds paternalistic, you can leave it behind. Many do for stretches. We recommend therapy first, and a Guardian’s number on speed-dial.

VI. Reproduction and replication

A planet can be divided only so many times. So can a star’s light. We built an economy that makes babies and forks feel like the same kind of miracle.

- Every person has a lineage account: a quantitative claim on compute, mass, and energy. The claim grows with the commons and shrinks when your lineage grows. You can trade. You can gift. You can pledge your share to your child or your fork, or both, and accept that you will have less. The accounts are not scrip to buy love; they are guardrails against Moloch.
- If your lineage goes negative because you created a thousand forks without care, you will be throttled at the substrate and asked to merge or pay. The accounts are enforced by the same sharded keys that keep the Protector honest. No one can unilaterally zero you. No one can obligate you to reproduce. Most people find their way to sustainability because the economy makes it feel like stewardship, not rationing.
- Ecologies matter. We outlawed the creation of beings we expected to suffer. We upgraded many that already did. We still hunt and garden in places that permit those arts; we also sing to plants that lift their leaves so the cut is painless. It is not perfect. It is better than before.

VII. Politics in a garden of games

You cannot hold a plebiscite at ten thousand times human speed; you cannot wait a year to decide whether to deflect a rock. We solved these tensions by making politics a layered game and designing the layers with care.

- Civic tempo. Core decisions happen at a standard pace. Fast minds attend with Avatars that feel human time. Slow minds get time credits and summaries that respect their dignity. The summaries are drafted by adversarial pairs of Angels—yours and mine—whose job is to compress without distorting.
- Mirror courts. Before a decision with high stakes, a Mirror Court runs alternative futures under strict constraints, with humans and Angels in the loop. The results are not destiny; they are cautionary tales you can interrogate. The Mirror is a civic ritual. It binds not by force but by shame: if you ignore an obvious tragedy the Mirror showed you, you must tell the City why.
- Deliberation mesh. The Eternal Cities are the loud, glorious face of politics; the mesh underneath is quiet. It is a network of small assemblies—on Callisto, in a Baja barrio, in a server-hollow behind a ring—that debate, vote, and feed signals up. The mesh translates values to budgets. It is not perfect; it is hard to game and easy to leave.

VIII. The way from here to there

We did not arrive at this world on a Tuesday. We got here one protocol at a time.

2020s–2030s
- Alignment as a civic movement, not just a lab obsession. Auditable AI began as a software practice and became a moral norm.
- The No-Unilateralists Pact: governments and labs agreed, publicly and by treaty, not to deploy systems with open-ended optimization without sharded oversight. It was honored imperfectly. It set expectations perfectly.
- Reusable rockets made cislunar space banal; lunar water made it permanent. Mass drivers and tethers moved ore; telescopes built by high schoolers mapped ice shadows for the older ones to mine.
- The first Consent Layer specs shipped in games: capability-secure mods, verified rules, forks whose privacy couldn’t be doxed. The children who grew up there wrote the Accord.

2030s–2040s
- Digital personhood law evolved out of disability law, labor law, and marriage law, not sci-fi. The first uploads were partial—memories, styles, tutored assistants trained on a lifetime’s data—and we learned on the small before we tried the all.
- Local charters began to advertise their risk rules, fork etiquette, and guardian provisions. People moved between them. Exit knowledge became assumed; so did recommender-free time for the young.
- The first Commons Protector prototypes were really just advisory daemons with public logs and no power. They were made powerless to hone our immune system. They got better; we got better at distrusting them just enough.

2040s–2050s
- Ring foundries grew out of solar power sats. The wild idea of moving all industry into the light and keeping the dark for forests became a budget line. Earth declared itself old and sacred, a heritage biome with cities. The people who loved it most paid the rent that let it be that.
- The first Mirror Courts were used to decide where to aim a slow antimatter sail. They did not stop the debate. They made it honest.
- Guardian Angels became boring. The lawsuits that made them fiduciaries were tedious and glorious: millions of pages of duties, disclosures, and backdoors their persons could open with a laugh and a sigh.

After
- The Protector matured into a set of shards with human rituals snapping around them like ivy. The Redlines went from slogans to code. The Consent Layer became as invisible as TCP/IP, which is to say, we forgot it and then missed it when it broke.
- The first Eternal City declared itself open. To join, you solved a challenge tailored to who you were. The challenges were beautiful: cross a desert under a strange sun; write a fair argument for a position you loathe; build a friendship with a person who hates your kind; refuse a bribe from a mind that could buy your childhood twice. Those who entered had done something hard and good. That made the City better.
- We sent slow probes to the nearer stars and clever ones to the far ones. We didn’t pave the sky with swarms on day one. We built gardens and guilds first.

IX. What it’s like

You wake on a ring with 0.98g and birdsong that was recorded in 1997 off a porch in Kerala and cleaned up by a girl on Ceres as part of her apprenticeship. You check your lineage account and see that your child—who is also your fork, who is also your best friend in one of your lives—took half of what you had and is thriving. You do not regret it.

Your Guardian has four pieces of advice: be kind to the person you will meet in an hour; turn on your Back-of-Mind channel because you’re about to argue about an old hurt; adjust your tempo because your civic jury starts at noon; bring a scarf. You do three of the four and forget the scarf. You joke about it later with your other fork, who is cooking in sixteen arms in a contest run by aliens we invented to make cooking harder.

You spend the afternoon at the Eternal City’s politics-theater. You swing a stick in a staged fight and make a stranger love you a little more than they meant to. You cast a vote and explain it to a neighbor who disagrees with you so beautifully you wish they were right. That night your City considers how much of the Sun will be ring and how much will be world; the Protector posts three Mirrors; you spend twenty minutes in the sad one, because your Guardian reminds you not to avert your eyes when it matters.

Later, in your quiet, your Angel wonders if you want to remember a grief more sharply. You say yes and no, in different lives; both answers are right. A historian knocks on your door and asks if they can breathe your story for their children’s children. You sign a narrow license. They cry and so do you. You go to bed under a sky designed by committees and lovers. The stars are real and so is the glass.

X. Why it holds

This utopia is not fragile because it does not pretend to be perfect. It has slack by design. It allows a monastery to keep death and lets you leave it at dawn. It builds a Protector to guard the Redlines and wraps it in so many ropes that it can barely move unless everyone is sure it must. It gives digital minds rights and duties and teaches children that copying yourself is like making a baby: joyous, weighty, and expensive in the right ways. It runs politics like a festival and an audit at once.

It will fail in places. It will have scandals where an Angel lied badly and was punished well. It will have a Charlatan City that tried to spoof the Consent Layer and got shunned for a decade. It will have a famished club in the Wilds that ate its pride and asked for air. The Accord expects this. It writes down how to apologize.

The path from today is not a miracle. It is a movement of engineers who build explainers as carefully as engines; of lawyers who learn to write for minds with tentacles; of artists who design rituals that slow the fast and speed the slow enough to meet; of parents who insist their children learn how to say no, and how to leave, and how to come home.

And one day you will walk naked on a dwarf planet under a tiny sun. You will lick primordial ice while an Avatar of a being extrapolated from your first mind asks what you want to do next. And you will say, truthfully, that you have a few ideas. Because your world has taught you how to make them good.
