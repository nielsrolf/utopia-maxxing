The Neighbor Accord

When the tug from Hygiea kisses the clamps at Saffron Pier and the air tastes like hot metal and burnt sugar, dockhands in patched jackets call across a spill of amber light. Beyond the viewport, radiator leaves unfurl like lacquered ferns into a darkness pricked by station lamps. Two kids snake out from behind a luggage cart to press their palms to three small tiles let into the bulkhead by the inner lock. The tiles are wired. Their names are Pathlight, Safestop, and Covenant.

Pathlight glows green when your exit is a promise, not a speech. Touch it and a clean map blooms in your palm: a berth on a ferry within your budget, heater credits and seed kits waiting where you land, a bunk in a neutral hostel, and your Civic Core—enough compute and storage that your counsel, contracts, and voice travel with you whether you go in skin or in simulation. The last five travelers who took this path left brief notes and a line of receipts that still balance.

Safestop shines amber when the local brakes have their teeth. Behind the tile is cable, not art—bundles running to valves, thruster relays, server cages, and an ugly room of red toggles behind thick glass. Any actuator that can bruise a stranger or bind a life answers to Safestop. The drill is always the same: one hand nearby, one far away, one by lot.

Covenant glows blue when the rules that matter haven’t changed in the dark. Tap it and a voice will read the parts you ask about: festival quiet hours, who can cap a street’s speed when elder knees start to go, how a port posts its lanes and bonds. If anyone edits in the night, Covenant blinks until someone explains, with models you can run on a library slate.

The civilization that runs on those tiles is called the Neighbor Accord. It is not a banner, an anthem, or a chair at the center of the room. It is humility welded into metal and code. It is the discipline of small interfaces that prove what they claim. It is the stubborn insistence that a person who wants to leave should find a door lit even on their worst day.

Three hands on anything heavy

Power that compels—air pumps, reservoir gates, mass drivers, beamed power lines, replication vats, budgeted persuasion engines—never takes a single key from a single pair of hands. It listens to Three-Hand Consent.

- Local hand. A steward who breathes the same air as the lever; chosen by lot from citizens who have done unglamorous work in the last quarter. She posts her reasons in words and proofs the market can check. She can be recalled with two sober meetings and a pot of tea.

- Distant hand. A safety guild under a different sky whose charter forbids profiting from what it signs. They ask dull questions. They enjoy saying “no.”

- Wild hand. A small, cross-substrate jury drawn by lot for the event—two embodied citizens, two digital citizens, one other. Serve once, and you cannot touch that class of device for years.

Push for propulsion at 04:12 on a Tuesday, and the interface proves that it can only be propulsion at 04:12 on a Tuesday; that you are who you say; that the mass moved matches the declaration; and that you can’t reach a neighbor’s lever through a hidden path. The proof fits on a poster. Teenagers learn to catch lies on those posters and are paid in tubs of dumplings by employers who value embarrassment cheap.

Under and between these hands runs the Stillnet: three independent stacks that can only idle, and only briefly. One is humble boards with fat toggles behind polycarbonate in a broom closet; one is municipal photonics chained to dull instruments on school carts; one is an attested rack three jurisdictions away. Any two can halt a named actuator for six minutes. Not five. Not sixty. Six. They light a public timer and shout their cause in language so plain it invites jokes. The Stillnet cannot aim, buy, sell, or rule. It idles and it announces. If it bites too often, insurers sniff and corridors narrow, and neighbors show up with questions and soup.

Books you can check with a kitchen laser

The Neighbor Accord keeps two books to make charisma thin and physics thick.

The Ephemeris Register is a delay-tolerant ledger you can audit with a school telescope. It posts transfer windows, approach arcs, sail corridors, thruster burn cones, quiet zones over neighborhoods and telescopes, and where beams will cross dawn. Thrusters sign short contexts with keys bound to hardware you can open without breaking a seal. If your mirror steals my sunrise, the entry attaches a shade bond that pays if my crop wilts.

The Thermal Ledger records radiator minutes, cooling rights, compute seconds tied to certified sinks, and declared instance counts for digital citizens when they enter regulated arenas. Draw more than you have, and your cores idle until draw meets promise. Not a debate. A thermodynamic correction.

Trade settles in Standards: one kilowatt-hour at a named orbital reference and one compute-second on a public architecture near four kelvin. Standards lose a thin shaving of value every year. Hoard, and you punish yourself first. Spend, and you buy care.

Windows, not thrones

Anything you can park on just to spite a neighbor is time-bound. A lane is a window. A radio band is a window. A radiator curtain is a window. A core is a window. A sim harbor is a window.

Leases must pay on the same beat in two directions.

- The dignity share funds a floor that no politics may pull out: calories or credits to eat them; care when you break; transit so you can reach work and markets; a counsel that serves one person only and can say “no” to systems that bind you; a compute grant—a little loaf of mindtime—so digital citizens can think, speak, and be heard.

- The locality share pays for dull miracles: tug crews that sweep screws from corridors, algae walls that sing to heat, custodians who polish ugly fuses, and auditors too stubborn to look away.

Two tempos, one exchange

Human heat and planets’ pull breed Hearths: river valleys on Earth that learned to breathe; lunar tubes that smell like wet rock and mint; Martian orchards where red dust learns manners; cylinders where dragonflies collide with children and both laugh. Here, clocks tied to muscles rule. You can buy noise for a wedding; you cannot buy a shortcut through a school’s slow street.

Out in sun-cold dangle Frost Reefs: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee; server archipelagos in Trojan swarms; Kuiper racks where a night is a subjective month. Here, watts are bread and jitter is tax.

Between them lives the Warm–Quiet Exchange. Hearths sell quiet measured in decibels and steradians and hold persuasion to posted doses in labeled rooms. Reefs deliver cold minutes, long forecasts, two-way translations, and audits you can verify on a donated slate in a library. Prices float on the Ephemeris in Standards a kitchen scope can confirm.

A braid of places and persons

Coordination braids through three houses that cannot swallow each other. Nothing with teeth binds unless two of three agree.

- The Council of Ports and Settlements seats places with hulls or roots: stations, valleys, ships-of-homes, server reefs with fins of their own. Seats weight resident-hours and legibility: publish your actuator map; run Three-Hand Consent; fund your Gatehouse; drill your exits. The Council sets prices on lanes and bands, schedules radiator auctions, funds debris brooms, and keeps ways open.

- The Register of Minds seats citizens by mindline—the cryptographic-and-social thread that ties today-you to yesterday-you across sleeps and forks. Members are elected and drawn by lot to match age, pace, and substrate. The Register tends personhood standards, guardianship, lineage budgets, floors of compute and bandwidth, attention zoning, and the civic tempo.

- The Foundation Bench is small, dull, and venerated for it: pilots, cryptographers, reactor hands, care workers, jurists. It certifies basics—the Stillnet logic, key structures, public firmware near lungs and wheels—with runnable reasons. It cannot legislate. Overriding a “not valid” needs supermajorities in both houses and a cooling window long enough for a monk in Arsia Mons and an upload in Triton’s dusk to read and object.

Some questions won’t stay parochial—how to price frost on a quiet street; what to do when a rock blossoms with fossils and walks the line between artifact and ancestor. A Balance Panel convenes half from places, half from lines. Nothing binds if either half says, “that bulldozes a culture below the personhood floor.”

Public life moves on a civic tempo. Fast rooms can sprint. Anything that binds breathing lives waits long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object. “Emergency” lights an hourglass you can see and dies unless renewed in both houses with reasons pinned to a wall you can touch.

Law that respects light-time

The Accord fixes remedies in physics first and stories second.

- Corridor duty. Planning to put a mass into orbit or a beam through air? Post an end-of-life plan anyone can audit and escrow what it will cost. If your plume will graze my fins next month, you post a bond today. Models that depart from reality die with math you can run in a classroom.

- Harbor codes. Every ship, tower, and simspace carries a signed exit and a signed end-of-life plan. If a twelve-year-old with a library slate can’t check the signatures, no one boards.

- The Filament Net. We don’t keep fleets. We keep a thin, uncrewed layer of glitter-makers and gentle nudgers with control loops a union hall can sketch on a whiteboard. When a slug flies dumb, the Net turns it to frost that makes night strange. Every action requires threshold signatures from random juries across polities and substrates and writes two receipts: a proof machines can check and a paragraph grandparents can read.

Exit as an institution

Freedom is a thing with tickets and racks, not a feeling. Every charter keeps a Gatehouse with two lifelines and drills designed to bore you.

- Greenways are physical. They include surge-rated locks, emergency berths, sealant kits, gaskets stamped with their origin, and tug hours escrowed to move at least ten percent of residents within a season. Fail drills, and your lane priority dims; your radiator auctions cool; a wasp-colored sticker sits on your lock until you mend.

- Silver Berths are digital. They are compute and storage minutes reserved off-site on dull, honest cores you do not control. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity there at a humane speed even if a local council clamps down. Cut a Silver Berth, and dinner still arrives; sanctions make dangerous devices sleepy until you behave.

Embassy Bays sit near major docks behind cobalt doors chipped by gloves. Inside are three counters with analog clocks and chairs that fit elders, octopuses, and drone pilots. One desk handles departures, one disputes, one foundings. An Asylum Rail runs through every Covenant pane: any person—wetware or code—can trigger their exit by touching their listing. The path triggers an air-gapped check that yields either a door or a zero-knowledge proof that a narrow, posted due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify. Doors open. Budgets lock. Care obligations travel.

Digital neighbors with standing

We do not make gods out of metal nor ghosts out of minds. We recognize persons where continuity, reciprocity, and moral weight meet. Mixed panels—therapists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to detect mirror masks—ask five questions without strip-searching thought:

- Can you show a thread of self that survives moves and pauses, and signal when alterations would break that thread?

- Can you model other minds to the same depth you ask they model you?

- Can you read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts at a tempo that fits you?

- Do your reasons travel, or are they rewards in disguise?

- Do you exhibit joy and pain, boredom and care, strong enough to demand protection?

Where protections are in doubt, the Accord is generous. Where burdens are unclear, it waits.

Rights are teeth, not banners.

- Integrity. No erasure, freezing, throttling below the civic floor, deep edits, forking, or merging without consent or narrow due process logged against your line. Unauthorized deletion is homicide. Unauthorized copying that grips levers is assault.

- Floors of compute and bandwidth. You cannot be starved below civic minutes—the cycles needed to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a visible clock.

- Substrate choice. You choose where you run; covert throttles and erase hooks in rented bodies or hosts are contraband.

- Temporal fairness. Live fast or slow; duties that bind others ratify on the civic tempo or inside Slow Rooms with validators on both ends. If a fast mind tricks a slow neighbor, the remedy bites in both subjective lives.

- Embodiment access. You may lease a body under safety codes that read like vehicle and workplace law. Assault on rented limbs is assault.

- Association and exit. You may join, fork, merge, and leave, subject to safety and lineage budgets.

Duties scale with footprint.

- One line, one franchise. Forking does not mint votes. In regulated contexts, you designate an Anchor instance—your single civic voice.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, tenders, and fragile markets, you disclose how many civic-weighted instances you are running and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice, and you meet a slow judge and a cold window.

- Branch budgets. Branching is joy with gravity. Lineages accrue branch credits over years and civic work; credits buy new full franchises above the floor. Burst clouds near a ballot trigger quarantines: civic weight slows and regulated markets close until entanglements decay or a court splits resources.

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay a levy tied to local cooling costs; heat that feeds lettuces earns a credit.

- Provenance for acts with blast radius. Large external acts must carry labels: who paid, what data broadly shaped your model, what levers you grip. Persuasion aimed at law must file runnable models and error bars in the Reason Shelf. Unlabeled leverage is bleach in a well.

Forking and merging as craft

Before a split, neutral counselors—boring on purpose—make sure obligations and budgets are understood. Forks declare purpose: griefwork, parallel design, census runs. You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, appoint guardians if duties will bind new branches. One branch keeps the franchise; others move into disentanglement while a junction panel checks for duress and fair flows.

Merges happen in Loom Houses built to soothe: water slides without strobe; chairs cradle old bones and rented joints; scent is set low and kind. Compatibility checks compare value-graphs without prying into diaries. Empathy loops let each branch inhabit the other’s senses. Either may walk away. If they braid, liabilities and promises braid forward. Deep edits require dual consent: now-you and a snapshot signed before the desire took root. Ending your own life is a right and a sorrow. Reviving a shell without a past is not sold as kindness.

Attention is infrastructure

We mark influence like streets. Doorways carry a small sigil—a leaf and a hand—and a sentence: ask before you aim.

- Quietways carry navigation, child time, emergencies, and open science. No targeting. No fast models in the air unless a license says so.

- Debate Squares allow campaigns and quarrels but require provenance: declared backers, declared tools, declared methods; neighbors can set Influence Profiles—no grief levers while I mourn; satire fine; never through my clinic records.

- Carnival Strips welcome the roar in labeled bands with timers that blink. Unlabeled manipulation is treated as contamination at the town pump.

The Reason Shelf keeps runnable arguments any persuasion must file before it hopes to move budgets or law. Auditors—many teenagers high on glue fumes from civics—earn stipends catching cheats.

Sanctions shaped like physics

There are no marines in the Accord. When a charter welds an exit, hides a tutor, or refuses to post reasons, other hands don’t reach for guns; they dim windows. Radiator auctions cheapen. Lane privileges slip. Whisper bands fall to whispers. A proud, dull fund pays defectors’ beds, bread, cores, and counsel. Moves happen with soup and receipts. Harm someone across worlds, and you meet a jury whose clocks and tongues you cannot game.

Scenes to taste

At Saffron Pier customs, two arrivals clear. One rubs a graphite smudge on her knuckle where she just signed. One speaks through a chassis the color of rain with a bright cobalt ribbon where a mouth would be. The kiosk prints under each name: declared tempo, lease entitlements, Anchor instance, guardian on file, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and usually wrong.

On a Martian morning, Kian teaches thirteen-year-olds to keep nutrient flow level while red light dances on glass. His Counsel—the boring, fierce agent that serves only him—pings a model: a lane closure will delay compost two days next month. That afternoon he sits on a jury in a room with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. The bill would cap municipal Core Windows for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation sketches wages in the appliance district where his sister fixes kettles. He votes yes and sends her a seed stamped exits funded.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 120× leases a rib of Frost Reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from scorched ledgers and aunties’ tapes. Payment comes as long-cold minutes and a contract to roof a school in Veracruz.

On Phobos, in Loom House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One tends basil at human tempo and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Today they do not merge. The braider schedules another try in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A trading shop carpets a Carnival Strip with songs tuned to induce smallness and fatigue. Grip Settings throttle reach as labels peel back the levers. Neighbors watch persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch goes fishing in a rented body. One branch enters therapy and learns to breathe slower. One starts making sculptures like frozen smoke and sells a piece to a reef that posts provenance with pride.

Twice a Clear Vault—a lab with glazed walls and obvious kill-lines—went to zero. Once a false bell taught better sensors. Once a silence saved a town. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer plainly.

When it goes wrong (and it does)

A cylinder over Quito cheers a manager who makes trains run. She gathers water, tugs, and lane auctions out of convenience and kindness. A season later she makes decrees past midnight. A safety clerk refuses to skip air sequencing. The refusal prints to the Proof Rail. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for ten years. She takes a narrower job and better jokes. Her friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A congregation in a lunar valley blocks exits with shame and gossip—no law, just thirst. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Gatehouse opens under the bell. The liturgy adds “and if you go, we bless you.” Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A nursery tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book promised steel. The Stillnet coughs. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians sworn to her Intent Keys—preferences she left for memory edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses in open session under cedar beams. Years later she names herself, sculpts in ice, and designs a sensor that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

A bad sensor trips a pause that kills a night market. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars with a story you can rerun with paper and light.

Earth keeps her covenant

Earth writes her own law. Wind and water touch everyone. Help flows out with rules: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet scramjets; beamed power follows corridors on the Ephemeris and posts shade bonds so swifts don’t fall; debris budgets fund brooms in Nairobi, Quito, and Lucknow. Attachment to exact places—monsoon, desert, fog—is priced alongside credits: you can trade claims on distant commons for guarantees at home, and vice versa. Leaving remains a right from every soil.

Beyond the heliopause, we fund interstellar work like libraries. Seedships sail under Starlight Rules: do not wake neighbors by accident; carry minds below personhood by default; if they bloom, guardianship applies and names come before missions. First contact begins with humility: clean water, fire discipline, and stories; custodians kept visibly leashed; help that does not bind.

How we walked here

We did not leap. We bruised forward and then installed railings where we kept slipping.

2025–2032. A near-cascade of orbital debris kept city kids under roofs on clear nights. Insurers grew teeth. Launchers posted flight plans and deorbit bonds school clubs could audit. A beamed power demo singed barley in Sichuan; because amplifiers shipped with open cone proofs, blame was legible and compensation came before lawsuits. Two ugly bugs in “smart” brakes and a grid hack in Texas made inspectors out of grandmothers. “If it steers air, water, food, or brakes, the code is public” left pamphlets and became law.

2032–2040. Black-box models denied medicine and parole. Courts demanded provenance and runnable reasons for any system that could bind a person. Narrow counsel agents—boring AIs with one client and the courage to say “no”—arrived in clinics and ports. Receipts stopped being PR and became habit.

2040–2048. Beamed power left labs with teeth. Environmental coalitions won “corridors before commercialization.” Data centers began publishing heat next to carbon. Cold-lake rigs taught cities to count cooling like money. Ports posted early Ephemerides: approach slots and cone proofs a kitchen laser could check.

2048–2056. Early uploads were uneven and brave. Courts recognized continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—hardened doctrine. Floors of compute joined calories as rights. We learned to meter cycles like berth time.

2056–2063. Infrastructure “optimizations” gone wrong taught us to split compulsion anywhere it could coerce: arrest from prosecution from confinement; input from output from audit. Actuators learned to listen to three hands. Hurry lost its fangs.

2063–2071. The Stillnet arrived as compromise after a reactor scare and a rogue drone. A brake at machine speed, obvious, with timers and neighbors. It idles, not aims; it speaks reasons; it runs on chips you can hold to a lamp and see through. Premiums fell. Drills became picnics with ugly mascots and good soup.

2071–2079. A miner lobbed a slug blind in a wage fight. The Filament Net turned it to frost before anyone drafted condolences. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without Maker Marks that named who would pay when things went wrong. A hockey club in Valparaíso rebranded to the Filaments and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

2079–2088. Lane, band, radiator, core, and sim-harbor markets moved from spreadsheets to registries schoolkids could verify. The Council and the Register formed in gossip and retirements. The Foundation Bench stayed small and hard.

2088–2096. A mayor fenced an exodus with sorrow and fees. Lanes dimmed. Cores chilled. The move happened anyway. He lost, learned, and went back to run a school. A cloud of clones tried to tip a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttled the lunge. Doorways to Quietways, Debate Squares, and Carnival Strips got brass sigils. The Reason Shelf’s comics grew teeth.

2096–2104. The Warm–Quiet Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought hush. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets in cycles to rebuild archives you could drink. Clear Vaults got kill-lines and oaths. The Bench refused a firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.”

2104–2112. The Lampboard—the public board that prints which lights blinked and why—found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt an Embassy Ring from a stubborn port streamed with captions and ended with soup. The Accord stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like a doorbell you trust.

What it feels like

You are seen, but not by a crown. You are seen by neighbors and by logs you wrote together. You learn two paces: the click under your palm when a relay goes dumb and the slow breathing of juries assembling across two planets and a reef. You argue at the Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it makes you listen. If you are digital, you sit in the Garden Tent at market and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: a floor of dignity that no vote can pull away; transparent lifelines; the right to leave with your skin and your song; reasons you can check. They will tell you those rails saved an aunt at Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to hold people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

An ordinary oath

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta. New citizens—cellular, silicon, and saline—place their palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read aloud:

We will split hands on force. We will keep books anyone can check. We will keep the way lit.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from Free Rim Gardeners,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow valley on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom like pale sails. The Stillnet hums, bored and content. Pathlight, Safestop, and Covenant glow like three small moons above the hatch.

This is not perfection. It is rails and receipts and brakes you can reach while coughing. It is a world where bodies and code share rooms without anyone being made into furniture for the other. It is a way to travel between suns without forgetting how to be kind.
