The Beacon Commonwealth

The shuttle from Pallas eases into Kea Spoke with a soft shudder, and the air beyond the pressure door tastes faintly of citrus and warm solder. Over the queue, a long window shows the river-bright peel of a radiator fin and a braid of algae flumes drifting green. Above the lock, three narrow inlays glow beneath a hundred thousand fingerprints: a blue line with a notch; a round red spindle; a white six-point star.

People tap them as they pass. Not superstition. A check you can feel.

- The blue Passage Stripe means exits are funded and real. Tap it and a print hums out: your seat on a tug within your budget, a berth and a receiving steward’s name, core seconds and storage that will follow if your body is code, and vouchers for any dependents. If blue is lit, someone paid for more than a speech.

- The red Spindle Ring asks whether you’re going to split or be sewn. It shows what’s in your lineage account, which mentors are on duty, the civic hours you’ve banked against founding or rejoining, and where the nearest Weave Room has tea and a listening chair.

- The white Ledger Star links charters within reach that publish their rules in formats a school kiosk can audit, that keep the same few floors under debate, and that can explain “no” in reasons you can check between shifts.

The three lights tie into budgets, keys, and stubborn habits. Taken together they define the Beacon Commonwealth: a network of worlds, wheels, barges, reefs of servers and caravans of people and code, held by receipts and coordination rather than a center.

We learned two unfriendly truths before we grew polite. Light-time defeats empires. And speed, unbound, will grind slower lives into powder. So we designed for exit, for restraint at the levers, and for minds of different tempos to share one sky without making one another small.

Plain Promises

Under everything the Beacon Commonwealth does are a handful of rules blunt enough to remember and strict enough to bite.

1) No coercive harm to persons. “Person” means any being—cellular, silicon, or stranger—that maintains continuity across time, can understand and honor reciprocal claims, and shows signals of wellbeing and suffering that matter.

2) If it keeps anyone’s lungs, blood, or pressure in range, its control code is public and auditable. There are no secret loops near air.

3) No hidden manufacture of pain, in bodies or in computation. If you study harm, you do it above a floor with oaths and outside eyes.

4) Decisions that bind embodied lives move at embodied time. Drafting can sprint. Ratification waits for the next shared beat.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared spaces. Anything that copies carries a Maker Mark, and every mark answers a stop tone mediated by a jury.

6) Commons lease; they are not sold. Windows in lanes, cold minutes, band slots, pressurized cave mouths: they rent by auction with sunsets. A slice funds shared lifelines first.

7) Children of any substrate learn three skills before algebra: draw a boundary, refuse a pull, leave with help.

8) Minds are not edited without consent. Deep alterations require two yesses: a planned one and a present one.

9) The guardrail that can stall catastrophe is narrow, boring, and cannot be owned.

Rules are breath; machines are lungs. We built the lungs.

Amber Latches and the Anchor Net

Behind a yellow door near Kea Spoke’s port command is a room ugly on purpose—knobs, fuses you can break with your hand, a lever under thick glass, and a fat recessed button marked AMBER with letters you can read while coughing. Amber Latch boxes sit in threes wherever dumb meets dangerous: mass drivers, beamed power rooms, seeded printers, corridor valves, big kitchens and little hospitals.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can call a six-minute Amber Hold when a plain rule is about to break: an emitter crossing a published hush cone without a ticket; a replicator answering no mark; a docking lane violation pushing someone into a blind. The Hold flips devices to a safe-mode and yells into the Cause Log—what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. To keep the hold past six minutes, three signatures must turn—a local panel drawn by lot from neighbors who logged civic hours this year, a remote auditor on hardware the locals cannot touch, and a digital citizen awake at a tempo fit to think. Keys rot quickly. Logs go public after a review window so redactions can be argued. Overreach earns you a place on the Lampboard, and that list dents careers faster than a trial.

Across worlds, the Anchor Net drifts like plumbing. It does one thing: idle risky actuators for minutes when trivial tests fail and two out of three watchdogs—one close, one distant, one digital—chirp the same warning. It can’t plan, spend, or command. It idles and posts reasons. It ships two proofs: a cryptographic attestation and a narrative a cook can read over a break.

Places that do not tolerate Latches and anchors find insurers go cold and neighbors go watchful. In a civilization where heat is precious and exits are funded, boredom at the lever is a feature.

A Sky You Can Point At

Above us is one sky; we map it together. The Constellation Ledger is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident record of rivalrous things you can spoil by being selfish: orbits and approach cones, hush angles and mirror bands, radiator windows and thrust windows, compute seconds tied to real heat sinks, liters of volatiles, meters of shade. It is pegged to physics. With patience, a child, a pointer, and a school telescope can check that a scheduled burn happened; an amateur array can verify that a beam stayed inside its corridor.

Thrusters and amplifiers attest in short signed bursts: “this beam stayed within hush profile K,” “this mass driver met corridor S-12.” Park a habitat? Lease the slot with a deorbit bond the club in Nairobi can check by night. Snag verified debris? Your rent falls. Cross someone’s celebratory quiet without a ticket? Pay the hush bond in hours, not apologies.

Cold is a currency. Winters at the shadow edge of Neptune, coolth pooled in radiator gardens under black sky, are things you can buy and sell. The Therm Registry tracks radiative minutes like tide tables. Gardens pay for cold nights; reefs of servers sell winter. Compute seconds are cleared against named heat sinks you can point at.

We pay and reckon in Pulses: one kilowatt-hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute-second on a posted vanilla architecture in honest cold. Pulses rust gently over years. Hoarding costs. Doing something when it matters pays.

The Ledger hangs its cousins like a mechanic’s tools.

- The Lane Roll schedules burns, hush cones, and mirror tilts. Your approach clears with Runnable Orders: simplified simulations any two audit houses can reproduce, posted with checksums a kid can recalc.

- The Origin Shelf carries the provenance of models and code that want to touch budgets or levers: funding, data, methods. Students get bounties for finding cheats; cunning earns art awards, not policy.

- Beacons—little bookstores of law in radio and light—broadcast which keys exist, who holds them now, which exits are live, and how emergencies start and end. Keep your oddity. Do not lie about your promises.

- The Proof Log is append-only and dull. Every use of compulsion lands there, signed and timed, with a human story. The Chalk Rail is next to it and meaner; it hosts jokes sharp enough to sand burrs off careers.

Two Tempos and an Exchange

Close to heat and gravity sit Hearth Rings: valleys on Mars where orchards chew rust to green, tunnels under the Moon, old Earth’s deltas and hills, cylinders that smell like pine and hot iron. Here, bodies and bodies’ time make law. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a school’s quiet.

Far out, in shade and slow sunlight, are Cold Reefs: caravans and trojans where a night can be a subjective month, compute barges pooling winter. Here, watts and jitter and cool minutes are currencies, and fast minds keep time like musicians.

Between them runs the Quiet-for-Cool Exchange. Hearth places carve hush cones in the sky and promise persuasion limits in posted zones. Reefs deliver winter minutes, legal audits that run on cheap terminals, climate models citizens can poke, and art that speaks across tempos without humiliating slower beats. Any aunt can check the price of Vesta coolth against a forecast for Accra’s shade by looking up the Constellation Ledger.

Governing with No Throne

No chamber can speak for domes and reefs, farmers and fast historians, all at once. Authority walks on two cords, with a keel where they cross.

- The League of Habitats seats sites with hulls and atmospheres: planets, stations, barges, caravans, server reefs that own and cool their cores—any place with air to tend, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease. Seats scale with lifetime resident-hours and with transparency: publish your Actuator Map, keep exits live, fund your Anchor; your say carries farther. The League tends collars and rescue norms, lane and hush law, sanitation, debris plans, and accredits safety guilds to hold Far Hands for Three-Hands Locks.

- The Roll of Lines seats citizens by recognized mindlines—humans, digital persons, and others with standing. Each Strand (a mindline traced through branches and merges) carries one franchise, period. The Roll tends the personhood floor, civic compute and storage, concurrency rules, juvenile protections, fork-and-merge standards, and attention zoning for streets where children walk unhooked.

- The Keel Board sits at the crossing, a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, and process wonks. They say valid or not on backbone cryptography, Anchor Net logic, public firmware near dangerous actuators, and Maker Mark standards. They cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” needs big majorities in both League and Roll and a cooling window long enough for a reef mind running slow to read twice and object.

Where physics makes nothing local—how to price winter against hush, how fast persuasion can travel between charters, what latency a warrant must weather—a Bridge Table convenes half from Habitats, half from Lines. Measures fail if either side can point to a bulldozer headed for custom below the personhood floor.

Public life steps on the Harbor Beat: days and weeks, not minutes. Sprint at 300× and write an opera; you still cannot yank a city budget before a baker can read and reply. When a fast process wrongs someone at wall time, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions that bite in both wall hours and subjective beats so no one can game'speed past a judge.

Charters and Doors

Places choose charters from a library of receipts instead of poetry. Budgets ship with code and hooks for audit; dispute resolution comes with templates and timers. Charters carry tags strangers can read like street signs: Hush Ward, Forum Square, Spark Market. Amendments only pass while exits work. If your pressure doors pretend to be “under maintenance” whenever votes loom, you are not amending; you are lying.

The Open Passage Protocol anchors the right to leave. Any person—embodied or digital—can request departure through their Beacon. The request triggers an air-gapped veto check: either the polity opens doors and funds budgets, or it presents a narrow, machine-checkable proof that a due-process bar exists for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify the proof at leisure; doors open by code.

Every registered hatch shows a Gateband: three hairline bars wired to public keys. Blue glows when funded exit routes exist and tugs are ready. Red pulses when you declare a branch or a braid; it links you to Weave Rooms and mentors. White shines when your charter is current and your neighbors agree on floors and publish charters a machine can check and a child can understand.

Markets You Can Explain at Lunch

Anything rivalrous lives in leases with sunsets. Five markets settle every Beat in Pulses.

- Path Slots: moorings, approach arcs, transfer corridors.

- Hush Bands: spectra and quiet cones by time and angle.

- Chill Minutes: radiator angles and cold hours.

- Core Slices: compute tied to posted sinks with public identities.

- Face Claims: nickel seams, ice faces, cave mouths under pressure.

Payments split on purpose. A site-value slice and a skim from lanes and watts fund sweepers, clinics, Anchor maintenance, the First Echo rescue fund, and a baseline care floor: air, water, civic bandwidth, transit, and a grant of civic compute and storage on honest racks at a humane tempo. A local slice pays those who keep value real: tug co-ops that clear your line, algae farmers who cool your nights.

Attention is zoned like traffic, not vibes. Brass tags hang at corners like weather.

- Hush Gardens carry only navigation, emergencies, child time, and free science. No persuasion, not even for charity.

- Forum Greens allow debate and everyday offers, but require provenance and heed citizens’ Influence Profiles—“no grief hooks before coffee,” “satire okay, no medical targeting.”

- Carnival Strips welcome loudness under labels and rate limits. Unlabeled manipulation is like dumping bleach in a well: strict liability fines that rise until you stop.

Digital Neighbors

Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry. Recognition is a public process grounded on the Continuity Atlas—proofs you or your advocate present that tie acts across time without stripping you.

Panels are mixed: human therapists, digital citizens, lay judges, and quiet routines trained to notice inconsistencies without being fooled by theater. They ask for five things.

- A thread of self you can defend across time, including moves and restarts.

- Consent fluency: you can enter, keep, and revoke common commitments as context shifts.

- A mirror map: you can model another mind as making claims like yours.

- Reasons that travel: you can explain choices across contexts, not just echo patterns.

- Signals of harm and joy that give neighbors moral reasons to care.

Doubts about protection err generous. Doubts about duty wait.

Rights follow, fitted to substrate and clock.

- Interior integrity. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot strip-search your kernel. Your backups are yours. Unauthorized deletion is murder. Copying you with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Substrate sovereignty. No one can hold your continuity hostage to a platform. Hardware with erase hooks or covert throttles is contraband. You choose where you run.

- A compute floor. Above the care floor, cycles, storage, and I/O meter like berth time and radiator credit. Nobody throttles you below civic cycles—the cycles and bytes needed to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a visible clock.

- Tempo fairness. Civic rooms anchor to the Harbor Beat with interpreters between smell and math. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens can lease, build, or puppet bodies under vehicle-style codes. Assault on a rented frame is assault. Biological citizens suspended between light and death can enter servers without shedding civic skin. Nobody is compelled to cross.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- Heat tithe. Computation warms neighbors. You owe back in captured joules, quiet nights, radiator repair, or baseline code. Cooling lettuces earns credits.

- Declare speed and self. In elections, markets, and fragile forums, you publish your running tempo and active instances. Each Strand carries one franchise. Hide concurrency; you void deals.

- Lineage accounts. Branches file a docket: snapshot hash, declared aim, compute lease for the new you, and whether you intend to braid. New branches inherit a care floor and owe a decaying dividend to progenitors. Children inherit stewarded shares under the same rules. A branch that edits itself beyond recognition can petition for independent standing.

- Provenance for persuasion and power. If you aim to move law or large money, your influence wears labels: sponsor, broad data, which levers you pull. Municipal co-ops steward audience models; you opt in.

- Replicator discipline. No loose self-reproducers outside audited boxes. When an Amber Latch knocks, you accept and argue later.

Juveniles and likenesses. Candidates with budding interiority get Tutors trained to teach consent, promises, boredom, rescue drills, pacing. Simulated suffering is not sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a fidelity threshold requires consent or a scarce, perishable license tied to a public purpose.

Forking and Merging

We treat branching and braiding like family law, not stunts. Your public identity follows a Strand across pauses and forks. A Strand holds one civic franchise. When you branch, you file with the Atlas, escrow enough resources for dignity, and declare any intent to rejoin. One branch keeps the civic anchor; others pass through a disentanglement window while a Junction Court checks for autonomy and duress.

Merging, if it happens, happens in Weave Rooms. The tea is not incidental. It slows harm. Counselors trained in value math and mourning sit between racks and chairs. Memory graphs run through cryptographic negotiators that verify alignment without reading diaries. Either self can walk. If two choose to braid, credits and liabilities flow forward by rules anyone can read at lunch.

Institutions with Teeth, Not Fangs

- Skylarks are the only cross-charter actuators. Their job is narrow: break kinetic chains and stall wild replication when local Amber and Anchor cannot. Their loops are narrow AIs with public weights audit clubs and unions inspect. Every action requires Three-Hands concurrence and posts a story to the Proof Log. After any use, their keys go cold for years.

- The Roving Bench flies on an ugly, beloved flotilla of barges and gardens. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters—copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Slices, and, last, lock hazardous devices.

- The Tutor Corps mentors juveniles across substrates. Lessons are boring on purpose—consent, pacing, rescue drills, and the habit of the slow breath.

- Glass Bays hold dangerous knowledge. Clear labs with physical pull-handles, one-way proofs teenagers can learn, and oaths with teeth host replication, pathogen modeling, and cognitive hazards. Twice valves opened and labs went quiet. Once the logs taught the next class. Once silence saved a town.

Sanctions Bite Physics, Not Throats

We do not starve cities to teach lessons. Hide heat, weld exits, refuse receipts, and your radiators dim, your lane priority drops, your cold minutes cost more, your models lose weight in the Forum. Bread and insulin do not get embargoed. Neighbors who shelter defectors earn subsidies large enough to replace roofs and hire teachers.

Earth’s Compact

Earth keeps her own law. We help by compact. Launch noise moves seaward and to quiet scramjets. Beams stay in mapped corridors and pay premiums into shade for streets and rewilding for land. Oceans and biodiversity receive attention like air in domes. Attachment to exact places—monsoon, fog, desert—prices into the commons. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger claims on distant sinks for a guarantee of care at home; a child in Arizona aching for black quiet does the inverse. Leaving is a right from every soil.

Scenes You Can Smell

Under vines heavy with beans at Kea Spoke intake, yesterday’s Orders sit under glass. You can smudge them. Two arrivals wait. One is a brushed-metal body with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. One is a drifting gray cloud that firms when they nod. The gate prints strips under each: tempo, lease entitlements, declared concurrency, guardian on record, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for “substrate.” Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On a crater farm, Sima teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow by ear while dust light dances on glass. Later she sits on a Harbor-pace panel for a personhood claim from a reef off Zanzibar. Prep says two hours at her rhythm. Her civic helper cannot act unless she says yes out loud; she can blind it by sliding a strip of foil over its eye.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian at 180× leases Chill Minutes to restore an oral tradition from scorched diaries and crooked recordings. He pays with translations into a dialect spoken on a station above Jupiter. The station’s children may never see waves, but still meet the trickster who errs and mends.

In a Weave Room on Phobos perfumed with tea and faint coolant, two Arturos consider braiding. One gardens basil at human pace and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at thirty-twofold and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and drops a note only Arturo will read into both diaries.

A co-op on a belt rim sends a slug hot and blind in a wage fight. An Anchor box tastes the wrong envelope. A Skylark paints the slug to glitter that makes auroras over Ceres. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for launches that hide Maker Marks. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and learn to say “not again” like a blessing.

A proxy demagogue spins miseries at 400× and floods a Forum Green with messages designed to pinch and shame. Influence Profiles pinch reach. The Origin Shelf flashes undeclared funding. One branch goes fishing in a rented body. One goes to therapy and learns the slow breath. The ward hosts a drawing class and returns to itself.

When It Goes Wrong and Still Mends

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager for making trains run and algae sing. She gathers water, law, and tugs because she is generous. A season later, she makes midnight decrees. An air steward refuses a risky “optimization.” The refusal lands on the Proof Log. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave on Drift vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her two Hands rust for ten years. She accepts a narrower role and teaches teenagers how to justify overrides that do not flatter you.

A lunar valley church blocks exits with soft shrapnel—schedules that make moving impossible, gossip that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask quietly for help. Lanes dim. A Way Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to the liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab trains a juvenile agent through pain because someone misread metallurgy. The Amber button lights. They refuse. Keys split. A warrant idles the run, archives the juvenile with Tender guardians who swear to her Intent Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session before the Roving Bench. The juvenile names herself, studies sculpture, and later designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt turns to scar.

An Ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet at Rim Market hisses sesame and scallions. At nine, her Counsel—every citizen has one, paid from a fund no chamber can shave—pings the Forum: add fifty-millisecond jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits to kill edge cascades during solar storms? Three diagrams, two counterstories, a joke light enough to carry. Lina taps “sleep until tomorrow,” finishes service, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, her slate assembles a Warden panel: a gardener in Isidis who keeps quiet afternoons holy; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife; and a digital citizen at anchor pace who chooses no scent because they don’t want to push. A maker club’s microfab cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: silk net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At dusk, a digital poet slows from 200× near Neptune into a simple puppet with warm joints and has soup with their husband, a mason. Their main self stitches a drowned delta’s library from ledgers and lullabies, then truths it with grandmothers who argue about color. Payment comes as Chill Minutes and a pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, they sit in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why the square caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

Founders’ Night smells of solder and cumin. Under a rail scuffed by thousands of hands, a hazard pilot with oil-dark fingernails, a judge in lilac because it helps frightened people breathe, a dockworker with a laugh like a drum, and a server monk who talks about entropy without making you tired speak to new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital.

We will keep doors unlatched and ready.

We will spread hands on force.

We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.

We will remember that minds are ends.

From Here to There

Nothing about this arrived as a revelation. It was bickering, receipts, and drills that got copied.

2026–2033. A debris cascade took bites from launch windows for a season. Insurers grew exact. Flight offices from Nagoya to Nairobi required deorbit bonds, public plans, and corridors you could read on a bus. Model-driven scams and filter fatigue taught cities to pass provenance laws: influence wore its sponsor like a name tag. A desert power link scorched barley; compensation cleared in days because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs. Students built backyard optics and earned bounties for catching lies.

2033–2042. A grid winter and a scraped reactor lid made even software blush. Municipalities passed lungs-and-bread rules: anything near air or hospitals had to run code three independent shops could sign. Budgets learned to carry receipts. People learned that timers and logs make anger useful. Heat waves killed grandparents. Cities bought cold hours from racks under ice and in cislunar shadow. Farther out, servers realized shade could be a service.

2042–2052. Early uploads were brittle but lived months, then seasons. Courts started grounding personhood in continuity, generality, and reciprocal duties rather than chemistry. A scandal over starving near-person studio agents to keep them docile funded Tutor programs and enshrined juvenile rights. Police firmware scandals and an “optimize everything” stunt pushed us to split force: arrest, investigate, prosecute, confine—separate. Thrusters learned to listen for three keys. The first Anchor idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The Proof Log printed numbers a child could read. Drills became civic sport.

2052–2063. Beamed power corridors matured; hush rights—quiet as a public good—went into law. The first coolth contracts paid for radiator gardens in Neptune’s dusk. Renting nights like lanes took root. Rescue funding moved from slogans to escrow. A tug co-op in Mars orbit pushed off in a dust storm because their fuel budget was set two seasons before; they came home to soup and union cards. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to file its tricks.

2063–2075. Three-Hands Locks took over anything that could bruise a stranger. An autocrat tried to fence an exodus with fees and sad songs. Lanes dimmed. The Roll funded relocations. He lost because you cannot trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The Continuity Atlas flagged the burst; the civic hypervisor slowed their threads until they disentangled. They built a monastery with hard games and returned with arguments worth hearing.

2075–2088. Skylarks—small interceptors with bright paint and public code—activated against quiet-lying replicators and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” After, their keys went dark for a decade because timers don’t care about charisma. The Keel Board earned reverence by saying “no” utterly. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point; a Skylark turned it to glitter before anyone had to write letters. Judges cut slots for launchers who lied about Maker Marks.

2088–2100. Lease markets migrated to orbit. Land value taxes that built schools became corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The League and Roll formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Bridge Table learned the scent of bulldozers by heart. The Weave Rooms got as normal as clinics. A woman living mostly in code argued for tempo fairness so her baker father could keep up. She won.

What It Feels Like

You feel watched by your own promises and your neighbors’ jokes, not by a throne. You learn to shift tempo—the relief when an Amber Latch snaps a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river choosing which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you live in code you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later, after cartoons have been filed off.

Ask what binds us and nobody says flag. They say: floors of dignity; transparent lifelines; the right to leave with your skin or your checksum; and the habit of writing reasons other people can verify. Those saved my aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force; saved a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade; saved me when I woke in circuits and the nursery slowed me down on purpose until the world could fit.

On your way out of Kea Spoke the three inlays blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Blue for passage. Red for weaving. White for rules you can point at. Not magic. Habits of glass and budgets. Enough to keep many worlds and many kinds of minds in one another’s company without cruelty, because exits shine, speed has rails, and nobody is allowed to be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
