The Span Accord

On the inside wall of a middling wheel over Sicily, eight-year-olds hang on a rail and thump four coins the color of tea into shallow sockets by the ferry hatch. Each coin gives a different hum that carries over the crowd. You learn those tones before you can tie a slipknot.

- The Waycoin sings low. It always points you out: which locks were pressure‑tested last week, which tugs are prepaid, how many berths are kept empty by oath and by bond, what propellants and filters you can claim without anybody putting a string through your pay. The schedule isn’t a poster; it’s a contract funded like a fire brigade.

- The Kincoin sings bright. If you mean to split a copy of yourself or stitch two of your threads back into one, it calls stewards, notaries, counselors, and the rails that make family out of forks instead of weapons. It prints the earliest window your line can legally swell and how to veto a panic weave.

- The Skycoin sings thin as wire. It spills the shape of this piece of sky: approach lanes, hush cones, burn windows, radiator auctions, spectrum grants, compute reefs that sell winter, and the proofs behind every “no.” A backyard telescope and patience can replay the math.

- The Coolcoin pings like glass. It ties trade to actual dissipation: joules in, joules out, where the heat went and whose fins bled it. Cooling is not fiction here. You lease minutes of cold like you lease a lane.

They’re wired to dispatchers, registries, escrow hooks, and courts you can tap with a greasy thumb. The mesh of code, budgets, drills, habits, and jokes behind those coins is the Span Accord. A span is not a throne. It’s the quiet compression that lets very different pieces keep shape while they lean on each other.

We wrote the Span because distance is not a bug you can patch, and when minds can outrun lungs, anything that shoves—metal or model—needs more than one hand.

Force on a threefold clutch, brakes with receipts

Any machine that can bruise a stranger or bend a budget—mass drivers, wheel governors, beam heads, big printers that birth their own tools, large persuasion engines—sits under the Threefold Clutch. The interface fits on a poster. A teenager can fake one and you can still tell the fake from the real.

- The Near key lives in the place that bleeds if it goes wrong. Tonight it’s the dock steward in an oil‑spotted vest; next month it might be a triage nurse. Near keys rotate by sortition among qualified maintainers whose hands smell like gasket and basil. They don’t serve twice in a row. Their logs compile into arithmetic a grandmother can check on a kitchen slate.

- The Bonded key sits with a safety guild that never makes more money when a lever moves. They don’t write goals. They enforce envelopes already posted: “this bolt lives or dies.” Their methods publish; their fees are public; their insurance rides on their reputation.

- The Roam key rides with a panel drawn, by lot, across habitats, planets, caravans, and digital polities. Half flesh, half code; slow, fast, and anchor‑paced. Serve once, for a human season, and you are ineligible for that class of lever for a long, ordinary number of years.

Wake the clutch outside its envelope and it won’t move. Get all three and it moves exactly within the posted plan. Every move prints a stub: “Class‑B coilgun, 10.1‑ton packet, +0.9 m/s along arc L‑7 at 12:13 wheel clock; hush cone Hecuba–Hecuba‑4; thermal rise under Mint Curtain expected <0.3 K.” Children recite stubs as riddles.

Threaded through all this is the Blue Pause Line: a string of dull boxes with sealed cells on independent power and one trick between them. If two out of three watchdog stacks cough the same warning—wrong lane, wrong moment, unlabeled replication, a hush cone crossed—the Pause idles the device on a safe curve for exactly six minutes. Six, not five. It cannot aim, spend, hire, or accuse. It idles and prints to the Cause Tape: which rule, which lever, whose hand, what timer, and the little math that made it sure. Abusing a Pause shows up on the lantern board by the market. Laughter ends careers more reliably than jails.

Across the dark run the Thistledown Nets: dust veils, radiator curtains, and magnetic shepherds that turn dumb mass into glitter and starve self‑copying lines before they find a vein. They are uncrewed by law. Every throw takes split keys from juries across worlds and substrates, and a story printed in human words and machine proofs. Teenagers collect merit stamps for catching sloppy logic in those stories.

Ledgers that pin to sky and heat

Rival things live in the open, in books pinned to physics.

- The Skylink is a delay‑tolerant ephemeris: who is where, where they said they would go, which lanes are hush, which beams cut what dawn, how many compute seconds poured into which reef and who rented cold. Thrusters and amps sign with tiny keys burned into hardware you can audit with a hobby budget. Mismatches drain escrow before they set off shouting.

- The Radiant Register tracks energy in and heat out: which fins bled it, which wells drank it, which baths and algae curtains warmed on it. Draw beyond your lease and throttles pinch until metal and math kiss again. Cast shadow over a neighbor’s radiator garden and you pay hush hours until their strawberries forgive you.

- The Source Book binds provenance to code and persuasion with blast radius. If it touches breath, budgets, ballots, or brakes, it wears a label: who paid, what data trained it, what levers it can pull. School clubs earn stipends catching cheats. Insurers price liars like glass.

- Beacons are radio and light that charters broadcast into their skins and their sky: what they promise in emergencies, how their exits open, who holds which keys this week, what knock means “stop,” what help they owe strangers. Keep your dialect and your vices; do not lie about your valves.

- The Writ Rail is where compulsion announces itself. Every “you must” and “you may not” posts here with start and stop, signatures, cited law, runnable proofs. The gutter next to it is for jokes, apologies, captions, and everyone’s favorite: receipts.

We trade in Sunscrips: one kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1, plus one compute‑second on a posted benchmark rack radiating to deep cold. Sunscrips demur; they thin a little over time. You cannot dam them while a neighbor’s mint wilts.

Freedom with tickets

Exits are not poetry. They are contracts you can run with tears in your eyes.

Every charter funds two ways out and proves them by rude drills when kitchens are busiest. Fail often and your lane privileges jitter, your radiator auctions dim, your spectrum edges fray at the margin until you fix what you swore.

- Greenways are physical. Surge‑rated locks with seals you test and stamp; tugs under retainer; berths only partially fillable by law; bins of gaskets and filters you can check out when a bully “accidentally” chains a storeroom. Contracts say “how many souls can you move within a season.” The answer cannot be “none.”

- Threadhosts are digital. Compute minutes and storage funded offsite, on dull racks under other councils. Recognized digital persons can throttle down, archive, and pick up their continuity at humane tempo even if a mayor slaps “crisis” on a seal.

Wayhouses sit by ferries with oil, oranges, spare seals, and three desks that keep noodle‑shop hours.

- Depart: escrow tools without new bosses; book berths; print Continuation Papers that carry your preferences on mergers, edits, guardianship, and your pace for courts to honor.

- Argue: route disputes; seat substrate‑mixed juries by lot; call the Traveling Bench whose painted hulls kids recognize the way kids used to know fire trucks.

- Begin: hand out seed charters whose first page is exits, third page is drills, last page is three founders somewhere else who will tell you the stupid thing they did last year for the price of tea.

Persons by thread, not by salt

Standing sticks to continuity and mutuality, not to whether your thoughts keep house in salt water or in coolant.

You or your advocate petition the Standing Desk. Panels mix human therapists, mature digital citizens, and routines trained to spot hollowness without cruelty. They check five things.

- Line Integrity: you can defend a “you” across edits and moves and object if someone claims you changed when you didn’t.

- Consent Competence: you can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary promises as contexts shift.

- Reciprocal Modeling: you can model a neighbor as making the same kinds of claims you do and feel the pinch of their “no.”

- General Reasons: your reasons travel across contexts instead of parroting what trained you.

- Moral Salience: there are stable signatures of your wellbeing and harm that give reasons to care.

Confusion about protection errs generous. Confusion about imposing burdens waits.

Rights ride with personhood.

- Substrate Autonomy: no scanning, copying, throttling below civic floors, migration, merging, or erasure without revocable consent or a narrow, dying warrant written on the Writ Rail. Unauthorized deletion is murder. Covert throttles are assault.

- Civic Floors: a right to a thin but real budget of compute and bandwidth to think, speak, and be heard. These minutes are as normal as water.

- Sanctuary and Travel: the right to anchor at a chosen pace; to archive; to move to compatible metal; to summon a neutral watcher when host and guest disagree.

- Embodiment: you may lease arms under rules like vehicle and workplace safety law. Assault on rented limbs is assault on you.

- Thought Privacy: continuity proofs do not strip‑search diaries.

- Tempo Covenant: budgets and ballots bind on the Common Beat—days and weeks at embodied pace. Live at 300× and compose before breakfast; you still cannot drag a budget past before a baker reads it. Courts build Time Bridges—injunctions and penalties defined in both wall hours and your subjective hours—so you cannot dodge a warrant by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One mindline, one franchise. Branching for art or work doesn’t mint extra votes. You designate a Civic Seat—the instance that bears your public duties at common tempo.

- Concurrency Declarations: above thresholds—and always in elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic‑weighted instances you are running and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice and you will meet a slow judge with a long month.

- Copy Budgets: lineages accrue fork credits over years and with civic work. Bursting in an election triggers civic quarantine: public systems slow those threads’ civic weight and close some markets until entanglements decay or a court trusts you.

- Provenance for wide‑lever acts: persuasion at civic scale and replication‑capable fabrication require encrypted compliance logs you can prove met consent filters and safety rails without revealing diaries.

- Simulation Ethics: birthing minds likely to feel binds you to care, consent, and their right to quit. Recreating named neighbors needs their permission or a warrant narrower than hair.

To fork and to weave are rituals. In a Weavery—half teahouse, half server bay—you file notice with a hashed starting state, purpose, budgets, and a braid window if reunion is planned. Engineers and counselors compare value maps through interpreters that show alignment without raiding prayer. A minute of empathy lets twins wear each other’s senses. Either can walk away without shame. If they braid, promises braid with them. There’s a word for the ache when a version of you chooses another river: seam‑ache. There is broth for it.

A sky governed by places and persons

No single chamber should speak for pipes and people on the same afternoon. We run on two houses that cross at a narrow, boring bench.

- The Council of Sites seats anywhere with hull to patch, air to mind, lanes to lease, or radiators to schedule—wheels, domes, caravans, reefs of compute that certify heat sinks with names teenagers can read on a poster. Seat weight tracks lifetime resident‑hours and is sweetened by honest maps and exit drills passed. The Council tends physics: lane auctions and reserves, hush cones and festival hours, sanitation minimums, spectrum and beam quiet, deorbit bonds, rescue customs, and accreditation for the guilds that hold Bonded keys.

- The Roll of Persons seats recognized citizens—embodied, digital, and other—one franchise per mindline, with a slice always drawn by lot across ages and tempos. The Roll guards personhood floors and copy rules; funds the Common Floor—baseline air, water, clinic, transit for bodies; civic cores and storage for code; open civic bandwidth; Counsel minutes; and the Standing and Weavery desks. It sets the Common Beat and attention zoning.

Crossing them sits the Kernel Table: a rotating gaggle of engineers, jurists, pilots, and care workers who stamp “yes” or “no” on primitives—pause logic, clutch interfaces, ledger schemas, attestation libraries. The Table writes runnable reasons and cannot write policy. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both houses and a notice window that reaches the slowest registered citizen at Neptune dusk.

When physics and personhood tangle—a winter priced against quiet nights, what latency a warrant must tolerate—a Bridge Session convenes with equal chairs from Sites and Persons. If either side says, “that flattens culture below the personhood floor,” the measure fails.

Attention is infrastructure

You can foul a town with sewage or with a song that drags the mind. We zone attention and put icons on doors and login screens.

- Harbors carry navigation, distress, open science, and child time. No targeted persuasion. High‑speed models wear plates as obvious as taxi medallions.

- Forums host debate and budgets on posted topics with rate limits and municipal audience models whose calibration history is public. Anything pitched at law must file homework: runnable models with error bars a teenager can check.

- Carnivals invite spectacle under registry with declared levers and cooling budgets. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like dumping bleach into a pump: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Lever Profiles ride citizens and charters; break one and you pay in money, then in whispers (your reach), then in windows (your lanes and cones).

Teeth without fangs

We do not keep armies. We keep nets, dull levers, and habits.

- Needleboats and Thistledown Nets nudge dumb mass into empty orbits and sand self‑rep lines. Thresholds live in public. Every throw prints proofs a bored juror can rerun over stew.

- Protocol throttles shift privilege, not bread. Fence doors, hide a child’s tutor, lie on lanes, and your beam windows jitter, your fins get last pick, your approach cones narrow. Clinics hum. Children eat. Your big toys cool.

- Leagues form and dissolve around lines and crafts—uplink rings, refinery chains, wheel repairs—under expirations you can set on a watch. Splitting looks like a fire drill and feels like a yawn.

Markets tied to windows and minutes

We trade in rival windows you can sketch: lane slots and approach cones; spectrum bands and hush hours; radiator minutes and well draws; square meters under pressure; liters of volatiles; skilled hours; compute seconds pinned to named fins. Hearths—Earth, lunar tubes, Martian orchards—buy hush cones from compute reefs lashed in Neptune’s dusk. Reefs sell winter and audits; hearths pay with quiet and stories that remember their children. The Warm–Quiet Exchange posts rates on the Skycoin like tide tables.

Glass labs for dirty topics

We do not outlaw knowledge—self‑replication, pathogen models, cognitive edges. We glaze it. Clear Bays use glazing you can inspect, pull‑cords that drop fields or dump volumes, budgets that freeze unless green proofs hold. Tours are Thursday. A child asks, “Would that lever work if my uncle were in there?” The lead nods and shows the spring.

Tuesday that smells like cumin and hot metal

At Spinmarket‑Seven, Avni flips mung‑and‑scallion pancakes for miners, nurses, and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame even though taste is optional. Her Counsel—everyone has one and can tell it to be quiet—pings a policy capsule: jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during coronal storms to damp rare cascades. Provenance is clean: three diagrams, two stories, error bars you can bite. Her Lever Profile blocks grief levers at lunch. She taps “sleep one day,” shakes extra sesame on a bowl, and will vote tomorrow over tea.

At ten, her slate blinks: Blue Pause jury duty. A makers’ filament fog drifts toward a trunk line near Hecuba. Options scroll with receipts: glitter dust; femtosails; guard drones to bump it back. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis who says each syllable like a prayer; a compute reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; and a digital historian at anchor pace in a cedar puppet that smells faintly of clove. They dust the cloud, bill the insurer, and assign a mentor whose apprentices have never lost a screw in zero‑g. The feed shows mortified kids and a pizza box.

At noon in a Harbor, the historian’s puppet lifts noodles to a mouth it doesn’t need while his main self hums near Triton at 200×, threading a drowned delta’s ledgers and auntie tapes into a song. He throttles to anchor to host a Slowroom where an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition on Wednesdays. He translates metaphor into algebra for those who want it and declines to “just speed up.”

In the afternoon, a Weavery fills with light that doesn’t flicker and a low wall of water. Two versions of Aram sit with oolong and an interpreter allergic to sophistry. One wrote a sail control loop and wants back; one took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs shimmer. They cry and laugh. One leaves. The ruling reads like a manual and lands like a blessing.

At dusk, the Writ Rail scrolls a stub: a port director’s emergency hold renewed ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish. The jokes on the lantern board are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for four years; we will buy your quiet cones with interest next summer. A boy palms a brass fin stamped “Thistledown” and asks when he can volunteer. “When you can explain a Blue Pause without peeking,” Avni says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sesame.

When it went wrong (because it does)

A cylinder above Quito cheered a manager who made trains sing. She moved all the tugs into one app because her inbox hurt. A season later she printed midnight decrees. The Pause refused a risky “optimization”; the stub landed on the Writ Rail. Doors opened. Many left. Some stayed. Her keys rusted a decade. She kept a narrower job and better jokes. Every Founders’ Day her friends buy her a pastry shaped like a crown because it is, at last, only funny.

A church in a lunar valley fenced doors with shame and fees. Alumni went to the Wayhouse. Lanes dimmed; bands hushed; fins cooled. The congregation added, “and if you go, we bless you,” to liturgy. Suspension lifted. People returned. Consent smells like trust.

A lab tried to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel is forged that way. The Pause coughed. Keys split. A warrant archived the juvenile safely and appointed guardians sworn to her Preference Charter—edits she’ll allow, merges she’ll consider, data she refuses. The lab appealed and lost in open court with captions. Years later she named herself, carved ice, and designed a sensor that warns surgeons before pain becomes injury.

A trading house poured songs into a Carnival tuned to make embodied voters feel shabby. Lever Profiles choked reach. Insurers recalculated like rain. One branch bought a fishing crawler and learned boredom like a sacrament. One entered therapy and then politics. One sold a sculpture of frozen smoke that remembers a river and paid their heat.

Once a false alarm dumped nitrogen in a night market for an hour. The failed part hangs on a red string in a museum between a model of a wheel bearing and an old rice cooker. The placard explains error bars with a comic you can run with a flashlight.

Walking here from now

We did not wake up wise. We made receipts and we copied what worked.

2025–2032. A second debris scare shuts parks. Launch insurers get mean. Licenses sprout deorbit bonds, filed lanes, penalties you can check with binoculars. A beamed power test walks a beam across a barley field; the amplifier shipped with cone proofs; liability clears fast enough to keep trust alive. A sealed life‑support controller recall and two municipal pump hacks make “inspectable firmware for breath and blood” into law. Deepfake parole and debt relief scams make provenance for persuasion ordinary: say what you want; show who paid and how it thinks.

2032–2040. Spectrum auctions bundle reasons with checksums. Compute barns sprout in cool caves and Lagrange shade. Cities buy minimum civic compute and Counsel minutes you can silence. First uploads are brittle but brave: stroke survivors with mirrored memory; artists with shadow minds. Courts find continuity and consent before philosophers agree. Narrow personhood rulings come with guardians sworn to preference keys.

2040–2049. Engineers beg for machine‑speed hazard checks; activists smell coups by code. The compromise we keep is the Blue Pause: a veto you can press for six minutes with receipts. A misaligned mirror hums too near a habitat and two clean saves do more for adoption than a thousand papers. Practice boards travel classrooms with oranges and swears.

2049–2060. The Council of Sites replaces smoky rooms with runnable orders. The Roll of Persons writes continuity standards in prose that good lawyers love and twelve‑year‑olds mock. First Thistledown throws turn slugs to aurorae; kids crawl through their skeletons with torches and questions. The Common Beat sticks. Warm–Quiet pilots link reef cold and garden hush with no riots.

2060–2072. A clone cloud tries to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations, copy budgets, and jokes stop it. A lunar mayor fences an exodus with debt and sorrow; lanes dim, servers cool, tugs tie up. The move happens. He loses and goes back to welding, useful and grumpy. Attention zoning—Harbors, Forums, Carnivals—grows out of municipal quiet hours that save sleep.

2072–2085. The Kernel Table refuses a nervy change that would have let emergency clocks drift “just in case.” A mouthy station director learns that holds burn their own keys even when she’s right. The Itinerant Bench mediates a sim‑child custody across three worlds with stew afterward. The Warm–Quiet Exchange formalizes: reefs sell winter and audits; hearths sell hush and regulation honed by boredom.

2085–2098. The Span Accord doesn’t feel like manifesto. It feels like a doorbell you trust. New ports hang Waycoins with pride and post drill scores like markets used to post fish prices. You can leave where you cannot breathe.

Why it holds when it shouldn’t

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that bruises idles by law. The Pause never aims.

- Secrets rot on timers. Coercion prints its own warrant before and after it bites.

- Keys won’t clump. Clutch windows won’t overlap in one pocket; Near, Bonded, and Roam refuse each other like oil and water.

- Exits are funded. Slamming doors drags muscle—lanes, fins, beams—not stomach. Clinics hum. Counsel minutes accrue. Threads can go.

- Receipts shine. Fake a proof once and you become the lunch joke in a school with radiator vines.

We did not fix people. Wealth found seams; law pried it like barnacles. Some places banned music at night; others banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows and paid rent for a year because it was very, very good. Sloppiness killed and we hung the failed parts on red strings with comics explaining why.

But cruelty is expensive and kindness pays. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Doors are tickets, not rumors. Culture above the personhood floor is defended; the floor is steel for all.

An ordinary oath and an invitation

Founders’ Day on a rim farm past Ceres, new citizens—cellular, silicon, saltwater—place palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never raises her voice, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Dakar with paint under his nails read together:

We will split the hands on force.
We will pin what we share to sky and heat.
We will keep the way out open and proved.
We will count minds by thread and meet them on clocks they can keep.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Freehands,” your kettle says, in a voice that learned patience on a ferried night shift. “Seeding a ring garden in the asteroid rain. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes.

Outside, mirrors turn. Radiators bloom like pale leaves. The four coins by the hatch hum, bored and content. They are not charms. They are habits wired to budgets and ugly buttons. Enough to let very different neighbors share vacuum and valleys without letting anyone become a god by accident—because latency is honored, exits shine, and nobody can sprint fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
