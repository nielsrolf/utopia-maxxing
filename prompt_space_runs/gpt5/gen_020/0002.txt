The Constellation Covenant

The shuttle from Ceres bumps the collar and you feel it in your teeth like a coin. When the lock opens, thyme and metal drift in. Set at shoulder height into a rail of basalt veined with mica sit four touch‑pieces; they’re scuffed by gloves and sticky with the ghosts of dried fruit.

- Path Chip. A pebble of ceramic that wakes when you lay a palm on it. It plots routes you can afford and power you can bring without bowing: which ferry is honest this week, which bunk will be warm, which clinic stitches without sending you home wrecked, and the name of a neutral steward if your boss suddenly grows a spine. The steward’s audit is pinned to a code recipe you can rerun at the library.

- Splice Port. A hoop of woven glass‑fiber that opens your Line Ledger whenever you split yourself or plan to braid. It shows what dignity any child branch inherits by default, what debts and promises follow the line, who your fiduciaries become if you heat up, and the exits you wrote when you were steady.

- Covenant Pane. A square of smoky crystal. Touch it and the local charter prints crisp at a level a ninth‑grader can read. Green dots mark where emergency timers start and sunset; a blue dot is the Exit Beacon. Tap it and an off‑world clerk—under foreign law—will tell you, without heat, exactly how to leave with your skin or your checksum.

- Winter Needle. A slender metal vane cold to the touch when quiet minutes are cheap. It shows radiator windows, hush arcs over schools and markets, and what rate this place is renting cold seconds tied to fins you can point a kitchen telescope at.

They aren’t decorations. Wires run from them to berths, warrants, bandwidth pools, and escrow. Together they hang the habit that links our domes, rings, barges, caravans, and server reefs from lunar pits to the black waters beyond Neptune: the Constellation Covenant. It is not a capital and not a creed. It is a boring stack of rituals and receipts that let neighbors very unlike each other share lanes, sleep, and attention without sanding one another smooth—or letting the fastest buy the air.

What pushes must answer to three hands and a clock

Anything that can shove a stranger—mass drivers and big beams, replication clouds that could overrun a corridor, amplifiers able to trample a market, and charm engines large enough to tip an election—sits behind a Tri‑Key Console and a Saffron Pause.

- The Near Key lives here. Today it’s a dock warden in a stained vest; next month a nurse with shift authority. The pool is people who tightened bolts, changed filters, lifted kids, or scrubbed algae lately. Their reasons land in the Reasons Roll in words and math your aunt can pencil through.

- The Neutral Key belongs to a bonded safety house that doesn’t profit on the device it blesses. Their methods read like recipes. Their servers live under law on a different world. If they lie, it’s someone else’s judge and someone else’s jail.

- The Far Key roams. A panel, half embodied and half digital, drawn by sortition across places and tempos, holds it on hardware under foreign law. Sit once and you can’t touch that class of lever for a long, human span.

Between keys and actuator sits firmware signed by the Keel Bench: a small, rotating bench of cryptographers, pilots, process gardeners, and ethicists allergic to adjectives. The Bench cannot legislate. It stamps valid/not on changes to public code that touches breath and large velocity. Overriding its “not” takes both houses of the Covenant and a cooling window long enough that a monk in dust and a reef in dark water can read twice and object.

Above the console squats a box the color of old butter, sealed with wax and a glass fuse the size of your thumb: the Saffron Pause. Anyone with a civic key—after a greasy, boring class and a test a kid can pass with a neighbor’s help—can slap it when a posted rail is about to break: a beam creeping into a hush cone, a slug pointed without a docket, a seed cloud drifting toward a corridor. The machine drops into a published safe envelope for exactly six minutes on a clock nobody can goose. A signed note appears in the Reasons Roll—what tripped, whose hand, which timer—and all three keys burn. They grow back under sun and time. To hold longer, all three must turn again.

There is one cross‑polity actuator with teeth and no appetite: the Gossamer Nets. They’re uncrewed skiffs with foil and glitter and magnetic curtains. Their minds are narrow and published. Every throw carries split signatures from juries across substrates and tempos, escrow in a posted currency, and reasons a teenager can recite without choking. Once a year schoolkids crawl through their bellies with lamps and rude questions. The answers must fit in mouths, not just code.

A sky and a ledger anybody can check

You don’t take a portmaster’s grin as proof that a wheel will miss your farm. You check the Open Almanac: a delay‑tolerant, physics‑pinned log you can verify with a kitchen scope and a club spreadsheet. It posts:

- Track plans, approach cones, and de‑orbit bonds.

- Hush arcs and celebration windows over rings and nights.

- Beam corridors and their lullabies—timing and jitter so your neighbor’s sleep and your clinic’s monitors aren’t rounding errors.

- Radiator minutes and “winter sheets”—cooling windows a garden or reef can lease or sell.

- Core‑seconds pinned to named fins in shade, with racks and addresses a school trip can visit.

Thrusters and amplifiers attest with short proofs: “this plume stayed in that cone,” “this beam met that quiet,” without spilling pulse tricks. If your mirror steals a neighbor’s dawn, shade bonds post and pay if the crop wilts. Runnable orders drive traffic. “Arc H‑12 closed four hours because two burns near Vesta would push your ferry into a blind.” You can re‑plot at home. If your check misses within posted error bars, you call a steward and then a rag; either way, someone has to answer.

Cooling is a commons. Under the Almanac hangs the Winter Board: radiator minutes like tide tables. Hearthlands buy quiet nights; Midnight Reefs sell cold; poets collect winter to build stubborn archives in dark water. Settlement clears in Baselines—a two‑part unit equal to one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one vanilla compute‑second on an audited rack in deep shade. Baselines decay gently over years so barns of them don’t become dams when someone else’s lettuce wilts this week.

The pegboard under those ledgers holds three more tools:

- A Provenance Register for code and models that want to touch lungs, steel, and budgets. Funding, methods, and training data earn stamps. Teenagers everywhere earn stipends catching cheats.

- Origin Seals for anything that self‑copies in shared space. Replicator clouds must answer a Freeze Call like a driver must answer a siren. Miss a call and your insurer bleeds until you remember how to be dull.

- The Chain of Record. Every act of compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, cited law, proofs your niece can run, and a paragraph your uncle can understand. Next to it, a column called the Margins hosts ridicule sharp enough to end careers without courts.

Two tempos, one contract

Close to bodies, where heat sinks are dear, sit the Hearthlands: Martian caverns damp with mint and stories, lunar tubes cool as cellars, wheel towns where tomatoes climb string above warm pipes. Embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival; you cannot buy a shortcut through a migration corridor. Children come home muddy and unmonetized.

Far out where nights are cheap sit the Midnight Reefs: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee, Trojan schools humming around server stacks, hermitages in the Kuiper. Watts and milliseconds matter here, and digital polities keep time like drummers.

Between them runs a bargain we renew like a marriage: quiet for cold. Hearths cap civic speeds and carve hush arcs; Reefs sell winter minutes and models that keep storms from finding the same roof twice. Audits fit on a school slate. “A week of hush over Recife for two winters near Uranus” sounds like poetry the first time and boring accounting after that—which is exactly right.

How we govern without a throne

We braid; we don’t stack. Two houses walk at right angles with a keel between them.

- The Council of Worlds seats places with air to scrub, docks to schedule, fins to aim, and cores to cool: planets, stations, caravans, and server barges that own and tend what they run. Voice scales with lifetime resident‑hours and with legibility: publish your actuator map, pass exit drills, show your hush plans, and your bell rings louder. The Council tends physics: rescue customs, track standards, collars that don’t bite fingers, radiator leases, and beam corridors that don’t cut through prayers. It accredits the safety houses that hold Neutral Keys.

- The Register of Lines seats citizens—cellular, digital, and stranger—by continuity thread, not meat or clock. One voice per autobiographical line across forks and pauses. The Register tends personhood floors and concurrency rules, stewards the Civic Hearth—baseline air, water, care, and honest cycles on a dull stack at anchor pace—keeps time on the Civic Beat, and writes ethics for simulation and care.

Between them sits the Keel Bench, the truss that stamps changes to dangerous public firmware, the Saffron Pause’s logic, and root cryptography. The Bench cannot command. Overriding its “no” takes both houses and a cooling window long enough to cross a light‑hour and a harvest.

When physics makes nothing local—how persuasion soaks across charters, what a warrant must endure in delay, how to weigh winter against quiet—a Bridge Table convenes, half Worlds, half Lines. If either side can say “below the personhood floor” or “you bulldoze my pipes,” a measure fails.

Public life walks at anchor speed: days and weeks, not minutes. Write your opera at 500× if you like; you can’t yank a water budget before a baker can read it at wall pace. When a fast process wrongs a slow farmer, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions that bite in both wall hours and subjective beats so you can’t dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

Digital neighbors with standing

Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry.

You or your advocate file at a Line Desk and meet checks a mixed panel can run without humiliation: human therapists and ethicists, mature digital citizens, and a slow routine trained to pierce theater without cruelty. They don’t chase souls. They test for four things:

- Thread Defense: signs you can defend the thread of “me” you care about against edits you don’t want.

- Consent Fluency: that you can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Other‑Mirror: that you can model a neighbor as a claimant of the same kind and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Readable Welfare: that your joy and injury show in stable ways that give moral reasons to care.

Where protection is in doubt, we shelter as for juveniles; where burdens are in doubt, we wait. Rights hook to personhood and substrate; duties scale with footprint and risk.

Rights that bite:

- Integrity of thought. Courts can demand proofs, not diaries. Backups are yours. Writing over a living self makes a sibling, not a cleaner. Deep edits without consent are assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. Nobody throttles you below the Civic Hearth without due process that prints its own warrant and sunsets on its own clock.

- Substrate choice and sanctuary. You choose your hardware. If you and your host disagree, a neutral custodian migrates you at a humane pace.

- Tempo fairness. Live fast or slow, but public binds anchor to the Civic Beat. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Lease or build bodies under codes like road and workplace law. Assault on a rented limb is assault on you. A covert throttle in a shell is like drugging a town well.

Duties that scale with reach:

- One line, one franchise. Votes and civic weight attach to continuity, not concurrency. In regulated rooms you designate an Anchor instance that holds your civic duties and pace.

- Declare concurrency. Above thresholds—and always in elections and brittle markets—you disclose live civic‑weighted instances and speeds. Hide clones to tilt bids and your deals void the way lying about weight voids a bridge certificate.

- Branch credits. Lineages accumulate credits with years lived, service to commons, and careful footprints. Those buy new full franchises above the floor. Bursts near ballots trigger a civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close fragile markets until disentanglement ends or a court says “go.”

- Heat tithe. Compute warms neighbors. You owe back—captured joules, coolant stewardship, baseline code, or vegetables grown on your waste heat.

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Big external moves—deploying persuasion engines into Forums, starting a replicator—carry sealed logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety rails without dumping diaries.

- Clean simulations. Reconstructing a living person past a bright threshold needs their consent or a narrow warrant. Creating minds who can suffer binds you to care, consent, and their right to refuse.

Splitting and braiding are ceremonies, not buttons. Before you branch, neutral mentors—barred from conflicts for a decade—check that you understand budgets and duties. Branches file a purpose, lease cycles they won’t steal, and pick a braid window if they hope to rejoin. One branch keeps the civic Anchor; others wait through disentanglement while a Junction Court checks for duress. Weaving back happens in Houses of Joinery: cedar joists, a dog asleep, water you can hear. Compatibility checks compare belief graphs without exposing diaries. Empathy windows let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk without shame. If they braid, promises and debts braid forward. There’s a word for the ache when versions part—line‑ache—and there is broth for it.

Exits you can grip

Freedom isn’t a hymn. It’s inventory, drills, and a desk with a bell.

Every charter that wants lanes and winter keeps two lifelines auditors can kick.

- Green Corridors are physical: surge‑rated locks with seals you test and log, reserved berths that cannot be declared “forever full,” propellant on retainer, gaskets you can print when a boss locks a shed, and crews under contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and backups—within a season. Drills are rude and random. Fail enough and your lane priority drops, your cores idle, and your bands thin. Bread, counsel, and winter still arrive. The muscles that bruise fall asleep.

- Silver Strand is digital: reserved compute and storage off‑site on dull racks under foreign law. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up continuity at a humane beat even if a local council tightens its fist. Cut the Strand and you discover that tools that can hurt people—beams, lanes, mass drivers—get drowsy until humility returns.

Behind a yellow door, the Departure Room stocks Starter Kits that smell like oil and oranges: minimum safety code, docking templates, escrow hooks, bylaw sketches for people who want to be small and kind, and three contacts who built a town last year. The Departures Desk books berths, replicates meds, locks tools in neutral escrow, and pairs you with a tutor who is not your boss. Leaving isn’t treason. It’s circulation that keeps cultures from congealing into cages.

Noise, quiet, and the map between

Influence shares physics with sound. We zone it.

- Quiet Harbors carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No persuasion here, not even “for your own good.”

- Forums allow campaigns and argument but demand provenance on funding and method. Each citizen posts an Influence Profile: “diagram yes; no grief levers before breakfast; satire okay; my clinic records are not bait.”

- Carnivals invite roar on purpose: satire tournaments, persuasion labs, and art that bites. Label it, rate‑limit it, and you may show off. Hide a grief lever and you pay strict‑liability fines until you stop. Tools watermark outputs so a retired nurse can tell a deepfake from a poem.

Institutions with reach and little hunger

- Gossamer Nets nudge slugs, stretch foil, and glitter what needs to lose its teeth. Their throws carry jurors’ signatures and runnable reasons. Kids earn badges for catching sloppy math.

- The Wanderers’ Court rides slow arcs with gardens and a school. Judges, mediators, and ombuds settle copy equity, braid divorces, and export fights that touch three worlds. Sentences seize bonds, alter insurance, slow corridor priority, cool radiator budgets, and, last resort, lock named interlocks. They share soup after. It helps.

- The Almanac Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, triage for drills. Its standards expire unless renewed in daylight by both houses. Sunsets long enough to help; short enough to shame if extended for convenience.

- The Mentor Corps guides juveniles—flesh and code. Graduation requires a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks explaining checklists in a public clinic.

Sanctions that feel like physics

Nobody sends troops for paperwork. The Covenant dims windows, redraws lanes, and cools markets. Weld doors, hide a mentor, refuse to post reasons, and your lanes jitter, your cores go sleepy, your bands thin. A dull fund pays defectors’ tickets, racks, and counsel. Leaving happens with receipts and stew. Hurt someone across worlds and you meet a jury whose clocks you cannot game.

Scenes you can taste

On Wharf‑Nine, Mari flips mung pancakes for miners and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame even though they don’t need to eat. Her Counsel pings a draft: insert 40 milliseconds of jitter into beamed power crossing dense orbits during solar storms to damp feedback. Provenance is clean: three plots, one ugly cartoon, labeled error bars. Mari’s Profile blocks grief hooks at breakfast. She taps “sleep a day,” laughs when a rented body asks for more chili, and will vote after tea.

At ten, her slate blinks: jury duty. A makers’ club’s fog of microfab seeds drifted toward a power trunk near Hecuba. Options: glitter with a Gossamer Net, herd with femtosails, nudge with slow drones. Jurors include a monk in Isidis who tastes each word, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad whose camera shakes when they laugh, and a historian speaking at anchor pace through a cedar puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a licensed mentor and a pizza that tastes like lesson.

At noon in a Harbor square, the puppet lifts broth to lips he doesn’t need while his main self hums near Neptune at 200× stitching a drowned library into maps and arguing with elders who will not budge on sky color. He slows in a Slowroom to cohost a Harbor Hour while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. Someone asks him to speed up “just to keep up.” He smiles. “No. We chose the Beat on purpose.”

Under cedar in a House of Joinery, two branches of an engineer meet with oolong and an interpreter: one wrote a sail control loop and wants to braid; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. Empathy windows hum. They cry. They hug. One walks. The ruling prints dry as a manual and kind as an apology.

At dusk the canopy plays stars. The Reasons Roll scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran eighty seconds long. The apology is sheepish; the jokes are ruthless and clean. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your hush with interest. A kid palms a foil square from a retired Gossamer Net and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a pause without your notes,” Mari says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sauce.

When it bends instead of breaking

A cylinder over Quito adored a manager who made trains sing and orchards glow. She gathered water, tugs, and auctions because she was efficient. A winter later she printed midnight decrees. A safety clerk refused a “harmless optimization” on air sequencing; the refusal logged itself. Beacons strobed. Doors opened. Many left. Some stayed. Her keys rusted for a decade. She kept a narrower job and better jokes. Friends tease her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A Martian valley voted to deny digital standing. No sermons left Earth. Exits were bright. Digital neighbors departed with their Cold Minutes. Flood years came; pumps failed. An anchored upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through dust with words old ears could catch. The vote thawed clause by clause. Relief tasted like oranges.

A trading house carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small and tired. Profiles starved the slop. Insurers priced the spill like radiation. One branch of the composer bought a fishing crawler. One branch consented to therapy and wrote an album about boredom. One branch built better lures with warning labels and won a prize for harmlessness.

Twice a Crystal Ward—a lab where replication and cognitive hazards live under glazing you can inspect and cords you can pull—went to zero. Once it was a false positive; we wrote better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once the silence saved a ring. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask questions without covering their mouths. Mentors answer without magic.

A miner lobbed a slug to make a point in a wage fight. A Gossamer Net threw glitter. Nights on Ceres looked strange for a week. The insurer evaporated. It re‑formed without the directors who called Origin Seals “fussy.”

Earth keeps her say

Earth writes law like a continent: old and wet. The rest of us help with rails. Loud launches slide to sea tethers; beams keep to corridors and pay shade and river repair; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Accra, Patna. Attachment to place—monsoon, fog, desert—prices into claims on the larger commons. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger off‑world claims for local care; a child in Arizona dizzy for black sky trades the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil. So is staying, eyes open, hand on a Stop you understand.

How we walked here

We didn’t wake into this. We backed into it with receipts, bruises, and kids who ran the numbers for fun.

2025–2032. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers paid for junk they’d pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded open track plans and de‑orbit bonds a backyard dish could verify. A mis‑aimed microwave test scorched barley. Because amplifiers shipped cone proofs, liability was plain and paid before fury could gather. Black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities wrote “show your math so a seventh‑grader can check it” into contracts for buses and water plants. Clinics deployed dull counsel agents that explained benefits and court dates without lying.

2032–2041. Grid hacks dimmed coasts; a reactor near‑miss ended well because a dumb watchdog tripped. Voters agreed: code near lungs, roads, or orbit must be open or in a trust anyone can examine. Thrusters got early Tri‑Keys. A Saffron Pause idled a driver pointed wrong for three hot minutes. The docket printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a fashion you could admire without irony.

2041–2051. Heat spikes killed grandparents. Cities rented cold nights from racks in shadow and underground. Out‑system servers realized shade could be a service. The first winter markets paid for radiator gardens that looked like lace. Renting nights the way we rent lanes felt sane.

2051–2060. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded a Mentor Corps. A woman who lived mostly in code won tempo fairness so her baker father could keep up. Houses of Joinery opened because you need wood and water and a slow dog when you cry.

2060–2072. Attention zoning matured like the way old cities learned to calm nightclubs. Neighborhoods carved out Quiet Harbors and slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to wear a label. The habit “emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight at human pace” crawled into law.

2072–2083. Lease markets climbed into orbit. Land‑value taxes that built schools came back as corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae farmers. The Council of Worlds and the Register of Lines formalized, with quirks annoying to game. The Keel Bench earned reverence by saying “no” with runnable reasons. The Gossamer Nets fired three times: twice against lying replicators, once against a manager who ticked “emergency” twice. After that they spent long bored seasons in hangars while kids learned to open their logs with oranges and questions.

2083–2096. A canton denied digital standing. Exits were stocked. Digital neighbors left without a riot. Flood years came. Pumps groaned. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinated like a radio in a storm. The vote reversed in pieces. Clauses were dull. Relief came before blame. By then departures were drills and Starter Kits smelled like oil and oranges. It stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells and receipts.

Why it holds

It holds because hurry has no teeth. Anything that can shove runs slow by law. Because secrecy in compulsion expires on clocks you can see. Because warrants print reasons before they bite and again after. Because keys won’t cluster, exits are funded, and the muscles that bruise sleep when bullies squeeze.

It holds because a child with a school slate can catch a liar in orbit. Because we learned to love boredom near physics. Because we split the hands that shove: three different keys on three different clocks, and a brake that can pause but never aim.

It holds because everyone breathes and thinks at a humane floor. The Civic Hearth—air and water for bodies; first‑line care; open civic bandwidth; honest cycles and storage at anchor pace—is not charity. It’s the price of a civilization where “no” isn’t just a poem.

It holds because departures are invitations, not betrayals. A stubborn church that fenced exits with shame lost lanes, then found its grace under a bell made of loved ones walking out with lunch in their bags and tears in their eyes. They added “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Attendance rose because consent smells like trust.

What it feels like

You feel watched in the kind way: by neighbors with a right to know, by ledgers you can run, by brakes that can pause but never steer. You learn to shift tempo—the snap of a Saffron Pause; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; a river teaching boats. You argue in your second language because it slows your wit to where listening lives. If you live mostly in code, you visit the Anchor Tent and ache sweetly at turn‑taking with bodies that sweat. Evenings are jokes and small favors. Mornings are proposals that become dull law five years later after cartoons wear off.

On Founders’ Night in a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular and silicon and saltwater—press their palms to a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. A hazard pilot with triangles on her collar, a judge who smells faintly of lilac and spends most of his time in code, an abbot who whispers more than he speaks, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under her nails read together:

- We will keep doors ready.
- We will split hands on force.
- We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.
- We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Free Rim Hands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden under Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Hygiea. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tip. Radiators open like pale ferns. The Saffron Pauses hum, content. The Path Chip, the Splice Port, the Covenant Pane, and the Winter Needle glow like small moons.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of people—flesh and code—without letting either become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to us and what public logs do to power.
