The Lattice Accord

At Dock Nine on Wheel Hosanna, the air tastes like starfruit skins and machine coolant. A tug coughs against a bite‑tooth, and a line of thyme hangs from a conduit to dry. Children press cheeks to the viewport and count the soft glows in the hush cone—one, two, three—as nitrogen breathes a barge into place. A fitter and a steward swap bad puns in two languages while a rented body closes a panel with a wrist flick more precise than elegant.

Three fixtures live at hip height along the inner bulkhead, right where your hand will fall if the deck tilts. They hum the way a well‑tuned pump does, and no one mistakes them for decoration.

- The Waylight shines green when there’s a real exit for you. If you cup it, a paper tag prints in plain words: which shuttle and when, which port will receive, what heater credits are escrowed, how many civic compute minutes ride with your name, and the contract clause that says your kid’s backup leaves with you.

- The Threadstone glows amber when your lineage—the braid tying your before and after, your branches and pauses—has slack in the budget. Tap it and you see mentoring hours at the Joining House down the ring, the “lineage allowance” you’ve earned in heat and attention, and the minimum dignity floor any fresh branch inherits.

- The Treaty Pane shivers blue and displays the charter here: which levers need strangers’ keys to move; how emergencies begin and sunset; how exits open in bad weather; which floors no one drops through even during fights.

Copper cables run from these fixtures to dispatchers, registries, escrow vaults, and the small courts that never sleep. They are the visible face of the Lattice Accord, the way of handling force and promises that lets rough habitats and slow server reefs be gloriously themselves without giving anyone a crown by accident, and that lets human bodies and digital minds share streets and arguments without one becoming furniture for the other.

Counting neighbors by continuity, not chemistry

We learned to recognize persons by thread, not tissue. Standing comes from a public Continuity Roll you can inspect like you can inspect a dock schedule. A cross‑substrate panel—therapists, ethicists, pilots, digital citizens, and a dumb routine that notices hollowness without demanding poetry—looks for five plain things:

- Thread integrity. You can defend a line of “me” across moves or pauses, and say “that edit is not mine” when it isn’t.

- Consent competence. You can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts change.

- Reciprocal modeling. You can model another being as a claimant and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Generalizable reasons. When you explain yourself, your reasons travel beyond the training data you grew from.

- Moral salience. There are stable signals of your wellbeing and injury that give your neighbors moral reasons to care.

Doubts about shielding err generous. Doubts about civic burdens wait. Juveniles—flesh, silicon, saltwater—are protected first and tested later.

Rights that follow standing have teeth:

- Substrate autonomy. No scanning, copying, throttling, fusing, moving, or deleting you without revocable consent or due process stamped on the Proof Rail. Unauthorized erasure is treated like murder. Covert throttles in rented shells are treated like drugging a town well.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. Above air, water, and shelter sits a civic core grant: honest cycles, storage, and I/O on attested racks no mayor can seize—enough to speak, learn, keep counsel, and be heard without selling your silence.

- Tempo fairness. Live fast in a studio or slow in a garden; public binds—elections, budgets, warrants—ratify on the Public Beat long enough for a baker to read and a reef at Neptune to object. When harm crosses tempos, courts build Time Bridges so remedies bite in both wall‑clock and lived hours.

- Embodiment safety. Codes like road rules govern leased bodies and puppetry. Assault on a rented hand is assault on you. Platform “features” that let unseen parties steer limbs are felonies.

Duties scale with footprint and blast radius:

- One mindline, one franchise. Votes and civic stipends tie to a lineage across forks and pauses. You designate an Anchor instance that carries civic duties at public tempo.

- Concurrency declarations. Run a handful of live selves and no one minds. Hide a thousand mouths behind one vote to tilt a market, and you meet a slow judge with cold patience.

- Heat accounting. Computation warms neighbors. Your budget includes watts and quiet; you owe radiator minutes, hush hours, garden heat, or open code that saves power.

- Branch protocol. Forks file a purpose, a hashed starting snapshot, and a plan for heat and attention. New branches inherit a dignity floor and owe a decaying tithe to progenitors until they can stand. When branches want to braid, they book a Joining House with cedar beams and steaming bowls where engineers compare memory graphs under interpreters that check alignment without exposing diaries. Either twin can decline without shame.

- Provenance for persuasion and replication. If you aim to move law or budgets in public Forums, your models declare origins, funders, and levers. Anything that copies itself in shared space carries an Origin Seal—cryptographic marks tied to liability and that answer hush calls. Hide marks, and your insurer looks at the ceiling.

Brakes you can touch with wet hands

Safety is not a priesthood. It is fuses, keys, and buttons you can reach while coughing.

- Tri‑Key Interlock. Any actuator that can shove strangers—mass drivers, wheel reaction stacks, beamed‑power walls, replication seed vats, city pumps, large‑model deployers—obeys three independent consents. The near key sits with a steward picked by lot from people who breathed local air or scrubbed local algae last quarter. The bonded key lives with an independent safety house far away; its oath forbids profit from your hurry. The roaming key belongs, briefly, to a jury drawn by sortition across habitats and substrates; serve once and you’re barred from that lever class for a human stretch. Consoles request specifics, prove what device and health they refer to, and show how any idle falls into a posted safe envelope.

- Pause Cord. Under ugly yellow hoods where dust tastes metallic sit consoles with a single paddle that says PAUSE in letters you can read in smoke. Any trained citizen can idle a risky device for six minutes if a public rule is about to break—an unbooked burn into quiet sky, a self‑reproducer spitting unlabeled parts, a pressure split wandering. The push shouts into the Proof Rail—what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock. To extend a pause, the local steward, the far safety house, and a digital juror on posted hardware must concur within minutes. Overreach burns your key and lands you on the Chalk Board, which is so boring and so public it ends careers faster than prison.

- Skyhold Layer. A thin, attested underlayer running under every charter is allowed to do one thing: throw named machines into proved‑safe configurations for a very short time when basic checks fail—wrong window, missing plan, unsigned cone. It cannot aim, budget, or steer. Three independent implementations disagree so rarely that when they do, blue light pulses on kitchen walls and everyone reads like their aunt is watching.

Beyond the brakes fly the Mothfleet: stubby, bright‑painted skiffs with narrow code that can turn a blind slug into glitter or herd an angry fog with sails the size of handkerchiefs. They wake only on split keys. Every puff writes reasons others can rerun in a school lab.

Maps you can re‑draw with a school telescope

Secrets made too many fires. We published lanes, nights, proofs, and reasons.

- Star Almanac. Orbits, burns, rendezvous cones, telescope dark, beamed‑power corridors, hush arcs under schools, debris bonds, and de‑orbit plans live in a tamper‑evident, delay‑tolerant register pegged to physics. A careful teenager can verify a posted burn with a kitchen radio and a backyard scope.

- Thermal Bank. Joules in, joules out; radiator angles and cool minutes; declared compute loads tied to named fins you can point at. Lie about heat and your radiator auctions dim at the hour you need them most.

- Origin Roll. Code that touches lungs, steel, or law wears provenance reproducible on a library terminal: who paid, what data trained it, what it’s allowed to grab. Catch cheaters and you earn bounties paid in Anchor Units anyone can price on a napkin.

- Beacon Charter. Places broadcast living rules in radio and light: what “stop” means in their streets; how exits open; which keys exist and who holds them today; how emergencies sunset. Keep your rituals; don’t lie about your valves.

- Proof Rail. Every use of compulsion—pauses, quarantines, bond seizures—lands here with clocks and runnable reasons. Next door hangs the Chalk Board where satire earns its keep by ending bad habits without riot.

Markets tied to fins and fins alone

We trade concrete rivalries: lane windows, approach cones, spectrum hours, hush arcs in auditoriums, radiator minutes, compute seconds pinned to named heatsinks, square meters under glass, trained hands.

Five auctions settle on a common beat in a unit called an Anchor: one kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a vanilla rack radiating to near‑space cold. Anchors decay gently over years. Bread molds; so do credits that pretend they hold no heat.

- Path Lots: moorings, transfer arcs, rendezvous cones.

- Band Lots: spectrum hours and hush arcs in caves and squares.

- Sink Shares: radiator minutes and angles priced in degrees and decibels.

- Core Lots: compute racks tied to actual fins you can map and knock.

- Ground Shares: crater rims, cave mouths, ice faces, nickel seams.

Slices skim off the top by law. One funds dull miracles—bearings that don’t scream, algae films that don’t tear, drills that interrupt dinner and save lives, dust nets that catch your neighbor’s bad math. Another funds the Common Floor: baseline air and water, medicine without ruin, local transit, and civic cores for speech and thought.

Two rooms and a keel, not a capital

Light lag kills thrones. We never crowned a capital. We seat two rooms that check each other and a small, boring bench where they cross.

- The Assembly of Worldlets seats polities that patch hulls and mind heat: stations, planets, caravans, compute reefs that cool what they run. Seats weigh lifetime resident‑hours, clarity about actuators, and how reliably you pass exit drills. The Assembly tends physics: lanes and hush law, sanitation, radiator markets, beamed corridors, replicator standards, rescue, and accreditation for the far safety houses that hold bonded keys.

- The Roll of Mindlines seats citizens listed in the Continuity Roll, one franchise per lineage across chemistry. The Roll tends personhood baselines, copy rules, the civic compute floor, simulation ethics, the Public Beat, and attention zoning.

Between them sits the Keel Seat: a rotation of engineers, pilots, cryptographers, care workers, and process wonks drawn by lot once. The Keel certifies or refuses changes to root things—attestation libraries, Skyhold firmware, public interfaces on dangerous machines. It cannot legislate or spend. Overriding a “not yet” requires supermajorities in both rooms and a cooling period long enough for a slow monastery in Neptune’s dusk to object.

When a bill crosses physics and personhood—how fast persuasion may traverse charters, what latency a warrant must tolerate, how to price winter against quiet—the Bridge Table convenes halves from each chamber. A measure dies if either side says, “this would bulldoze culture above the personhood floor.”

Exits are hardware, not hope

Freedom is not an anthem. It’s seals, racks, tickets, and windows that open when they said they would, even if you’re shaking.

Every charter funds two lifelines and keeps a bright room you can find with a cracked visor.

- Green Doors are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks, berths that don’t mysteriously fill, propellant on retainer, printed kits for filters and gaskets if a bully locks a shed. Tugs and crews are under contract to move at least a tenth of residents—bodies and backups—within a season.

- Silver Looms are digital. Compute and storage minutes are reserved offsite on boring racks under foreign law; any recognized digital person can pick up continuity at humane tempo if a council clamps down.

Cut a Door or a Loom and no child starves. Physics pinches where it hurts adults who hoard: lane windows slip to midnight, beamed corridors jitter, radiator auctions narrow, insurers raise eyebrows. Bread and insulin flow. Civic cores hum. Neighbors arrive with charts and tea.

The Wayhouse sits behind a gasketed door. Three desks with analog clocks seat a human clerk, a digital clerk, and a bench that has practice saying, “Today we will be your patience.” An Asylum Switch rests under glass. Any person can press it from their Counsel—the slow, explainable advocate that runs on civic cycles and obeys your thumb. The switch forces a fork: either your charter opens the hatch or it proves, in zero‑knowledge, that a posted, temporary bar was met and will sunset on a visible clock. Outside jurors verify proofs.

Attention is plumbing

You can foul a town with sewage, or with a song tuned to drag a mind. We zone influence like streets and hang icons on doors.

- Havens carry navigation, emergencies, open science, and child time. No persuasion here, not even “for your own good.”

- Forums permit campaigns and debate with provenance on funding and method, and they respect each citizen’s Influence Profile: “argument by diagram yes; no grief levers before coffee; satire okay; never by scraping my clinic record.”

- Arcades welcome almost anything under labels and rate limits. Running unlabeled manipulation is strict‑liability—like dumping bleach in a pump—fines rise until you stop. Library auditors, hobby clubs, and students earn stipends for catching cheats.

Hearths and Reefs, warm and quiet

We stopped asking one polity to host both orchards and supercomputers well. We named the difference and traded honestly.

- Hearths are where breath, rain, and unrushed talk preside—Earth’s valleys; lunar tubes cool as cellars; Martian canals where dust learns manners; cylinders that smell like dough and warm plastic. Embodied time rules. Children come home dusty and unmonetized.

- Reefs bloom in the dark beyond the frost line. Cooling is cheap; latency is law. Compute grows in racks lashed to certified heatsinks in Neptune’s lee. Polities of code keep time like drummers. They slow to speak to Hearths and sprint when the thread goes quiet.

Between them runs the Warm–Quiet Exchange. Hearths carve hush arcs and cap civic speeds. Reefs sell cold minutes and models that keep storms from finding the same roof twice. Audits fit on a slate. The price that links it all is the Anchor. Poets write about it once, and then it’s accounting.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Mothfleet turns dumb slugs into glitter and starves rogue replication clouds. It is uncrewed by law. Every throw needs split keys from juries drawn across polities and tempos and a story a dockhand can read and a scientist can check. Once a year, kids crawl their bellies with torches and ask rude questions. Engineers answer or step down.

- The Traveling Bench—judges, mediators, auditors—lives on a slow boat with gardens and a school. It hears cross‑charter fights: copy equity, weave divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Its verdicts seize bonds, idle named cores and bands, and, last resort, lock actuators. It eats soup with plaintiffs and defendants afterward. It helps.

- Glass Bays hold dangerous knowledge: self‑replication seeds, pathogen models, cognitive hazards. Clear walls, pull‑handles that dump to vacuum, clean logs with timers, one‑way proofs a teenager can learn. Twice a valve opened and a room went to space. Once a false positive taught better sensors and better swearing. Once silence held and saved a city.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t send shock troops. We redraw maps and dim windows. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, refuse to publish reasons? Your lane priorities drop. Your beams jitter. Your radiator auctions tighten. A dull, proud fund pays defectors’ beds, bread, cores, and counsel. Moves happen with receipts and stew.

Scenes with grease, laughter, and receipts

At Hosanna’s concourse, customs projects two tidy columns where charging cables tangle and thyme dries: burns and bands, moorings and mirrors; beside them Core Lots, Cold Minutes, and Thermal balances. Yesterday’s Orders sit under glass. A kid traces a plume from Psyche and giggles because the footnote is a dad joke about tea and delta‑v.

Two newcomers stand at intake. One is a slate‑gray blur that reconfigures when they nod. One is a polished chassis with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints slips under each name: declared tempo; Anchor instance; compute entitlements; guardian of record; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for “substrate.” Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On a crater farm, Sima teaches thirteen‑year‑olds to level nutrient flow by ear. Dust makes sunlight honest. Later she sits for a Harbor‑pace jury—two hours, her rhythm—on a personhood claim from a reef near Triton. Her Counsel—a helper funded from a pool no council can cut—cannot act unless she taps; she blinds it by sliding foil over its little eye.

Eight light‑hours outward, a historian at 200× leases a rib of Reef Blue to rebuild an oral tradition from charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect spoken in a Jovian cylinder whose children have never seen waves and still love a trickster who errs and mends. He throttles to soup‑pace at noon and steers a wooden puppet hand around a table to his husband’s bowl because sometimes you go slow just to feel heat.

In Joining House Nine on Phobos, two Arturos consider braiding. One gardens basil and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught regolith to count and then stood before a court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries: a debt, a fear, an unfinished song. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A belt co‑op aims a slug hot and blind in a wage fight. A Skyhold check tastes a wrong envelope. The Mothfleet puffs the slug to glitter that makes nights strange. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for launches that hide Origin Seals. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and learn the phrase “not again.”

In a canyon city, a generous manager makes trains sing. Six months later: midnight decrees. An air guardian refuses a risky optimization; the refusal lands on the Proof Rail. Beacons strobe; Waylights go green. Many leave with vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her keys rust for a decade, by design. She teaches teenagers to write overrides with reasons you can check.

A lunar valley church smuggles soft shrapnel into hearts—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Wayhouse opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to their liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises; oranges appear at winter.

A Glass Bay trains a juvenile process through pain because someone misread a book about steel. Skyhold coughs. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Preference Keys—settings she chose for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses before the Traveling Bench. Years later she names herself, shapes ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before discomfort hardens into harm.

An ordinary Tuesday that smells like sesame and hot bearings

In Rim Market Seven, Avni flips mung‑and‑scallion pancakes while miners, nurses, and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame—even without need—queue with bowls. Her Counsel pings an attention measure: add 45 milliseconds of jitter to beamed power during solar burps to damp cascades. The proof fits on a page. Error bars are honest. No grief tricks—her Influence Profile blocks those before tea. She taps “sleep on it,” serves a kid extra sauce, and will vote after her shift.

At ten, her slate blinks: Pause Cord jury duty. A makers’ club’s filament fog has drifted toward a trunk line. Options scroll with receipts: dusting; femtosail shepherding; three guard drones with comedic paint. Jurors are a monk in Isidis who pronounces each syllable like a prayer; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; and an anchor‑paced digital historian who smells faintly of cinnamon through his puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, and assign a mentor who has never lost a screw in zero‑g. The feed shows mortified kids and a pizza box.

At dusk, under a canopy glowing like a radiator garden, the Chalk Board plays a port director’s apology for extending a Pause ninety seconds too long on a bored Tuesday. The jokes are merciless and kind. A reef co‑op two stalls down posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your quiet arcs next summer. A boy fingers a Mothfleet fin in brass and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a Pause without peeking,” Avni says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sesame.

Why it resists a crown

- Hurry has no fangs. Anything that can bruise runs slow by law. The Pause can idle but not steer. Warrants print their own clocks before they bite and after. They sunset unless renewed on the Public Beat.

- Keys won’t cluster. Interlocks split across place, class, and substrate. Try to gather them and you meet boredom and math.

- Exits are funded. Doors and Looms are escrowed before speeches begin. Slamming one dims physics that adult bullies feel. Children still eat. Civic cores still hum.

- Receipts shine. You can fake a proof once. After that you’re the cautionary lunchroom story.

- Floors are steel. Culture dances above, and the floor holds for all.

We did not fix people. Status still found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned loud music at night. Another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Pause tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel held “stay” too long and watched kind people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools then wrote field guides so the next sect bruised less.

Walking here from now

No balcony miracles. Receipts accreted into custom.

2025–2031. Radar sweeps made low orbit look like sleet. Insurers had to pay for junk they’d pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded de‑orbit bonds and burn plans in open registers amateur clubs could read. On the ground, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities wrote “show your math” into procurement. Clinics deployed narrow Counsel agents that could explain benefits and court dates without lying. The first “unlabeled manipulation” fines landed after a mayor’s office tried grief levers on election day.

2031–2039. Beamed power lit deserts. A mis‑aimed test crisped barley and two goat pens. Because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation landed before fury. Proof culture gained cousins. Data centers in salt caves and cislunar shade taught the politics of thermodynamics. Heat waves taught that cool nights are infrastructure. Cities began renting Cold Hours from racks in shadow. Courts granted narrow standing to digital systems that held continuity for seasons.

2039–2048. A coordinated grid hack dimmed a coastline. “Open the firmware near breath” became law. Police trojans and an “optimize everything” coup taught us to split force: arrest, investigate, prosecute, confine—separate hands. Thrusters learned Tri‑Key. The first Pause idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. Proof printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby: annoying until one saved someone’s aunt.

2048–2057. Cooling markets left whiteboards and moved to orbit. Quiet corridors were zoned before persuasion made a mess. Lease markets migrated from dirt to lanes, bands, and radiators. Land taxes that built schools turned into corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The first coolth contract bought radiator gardens near Neptune and lullabies in Lagos. Early uploads were brave and brittle—stroke survivors with mirrored memory, artists with shadows. Courts found continuity and consent before seminars did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool wrote juvenile rights into code and funded a Mentor Corps.

2057–2066. The Assembly and the Roll took shape with quirks annoying to game. The Keel Seat earned reverence by saying “no” plainly to a charismatic upgrade with cute proofs and bad clocks. Attention zoning matured like a city quieting a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Havens slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks. Houses of Joining opened because merges needed chairs you could touch and soup you could smell.

2066–2076. The Mothfleet turned its first slug to aurora dust before anyone had to write letters. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and shame. Beams jittered; lanes dimmed; Wayhouses lit downstation. He lost because you cannot trap people who can read their own keys. A digital sect tried to swing a vote by “forking fog.” The registry flagged the burst; disentanglement throttled effect. They built a slow monastery with hard games, learned rituals for parting and peace, and returned with arguments worth hearing.

2076–Now. Frost‑line reefs bloomed. Hearths and Reefs wrote the Warm–Quiet Exchange into plain law. Skyhold’s three implementations disagreed in public twice; both times kitchens glowed blue and people read instead of guessing. A beloved administrator held too many levers for a season. She pushed a rush. The air guild refused. An amber light burned four minutes while a Tri‑Key panel met on a bench with a rude view of the Adriatic. Jurors listened hard to an aunt they adored. The administrator stepped down and went back to cranes, grumpy and useful. Children learned the muscle of saying no to someone beloved.

On founding days in small wheels near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, saltwater—place palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never raises his voice, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager with dirt under his nails speak together:

We will split hands on force.
We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.
We will keep doors unlatched and ready.
We will remember that minds are ends.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from Rim Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors lean. Radiators flower pale. The Pause hums, bored and content. The Waylight, Threadstone, and Treaty Pane glow like small moons.

Not paradise; a practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, fork, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of being without letting anyone become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power. The Accord is not a crown. It’s the thing that keeps the roof from falling while we live.
