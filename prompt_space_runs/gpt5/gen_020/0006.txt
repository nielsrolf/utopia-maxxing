The Cross‑Span Compact

When the ferry from Hygiea kisses the cradle at Dock 6 and the clamps exhale, the air smells of cumin, hot bearings, and a faint green—basil growing under lights. Set into the bulkhead at shoulder height are three inlays, scuffed by decades of gloves. A boy in a jacket patched at the elbows taps them in order, as he was taught when the grid is quiet or loud.

- A palm‑sized coin etched with corridors and cones. Twist and a clean star map spills across your sleeve—the approach windows that cleared their bonds, the hush arcs that keep telescopes honest, the tugs you can trust to meet you. This is the Waymark.

- A dull steel ring with a notch you can feel with cold fingers. Touch it and a six‑minute hourglass blooms in orange overhead. “This lever cannot move now,” says a square voice. “Anyone may read why.” This is the Staybar.

- A pane of frosted crystal you can press to read the charter—how this place starts and stops emergencies; which machines need three strangers’ hands to stir; where the exits are and which ones you can test; the floors nobody here falls below. This is the Charterglass.

They aren’t ornaments. They’re terminals into a civilization built of valves, habits, posted proofs, and drills, what we call the Cross‑Span Compact. There is no throne at its center, because light lag turns crowns into ghosts and quick clocks don’t make kind hands. The Compact is a practice. It braids planets, stations, caravans, and cold reefs into a federation of lanes and winters where no one can trap a neighbor without paying so publicly it isn’t worth it, and where leaving is as funded as arriving.

Split hands on force; one brake that can stall but not steer

Anything that can bruise or bind—mass drivers, wheel brakes, beamed power, industrial printers that birth their own tools, fleets that can print fleets, large model deployers tied to public budgets—listens to three hands that run on separate clocks.

- The Near key sits beside the lever. It rides on the wrist of whoever breathes the air that lever touches. Today it’s the dock steward in a grease‑flecked vest; tomorrow, the nurse who has triage authority. Near is drawn by lot from those who took unglamorous shifts in the last quarter—scrubbers, algae tenders, tug pilots, janitors, bus drivers, teaching aides. Near must speak in public: a sentence in plain words any apprentice can argue with over dumplings, and a proof that fits on a poster a teenager can check.

- The Far key belongs to a bonded safety house under a different sky, paid not to profit on motion. They don’t set policy; they say bolts that live and firmware that doesn’t lie. They publish their methods and wear professional ruin if they cheat.

- The Wander key roams. It lands by lot on citizens across distance and substrate—half embodied, half digital; nobody under the same paycheck. Serve and you cannot touch that class of lever again for a long, human number of winters.

Every Triad console is intentionally narrow. “Request: turn Habitat Wheel 3 by 0.3° at 02:14.” The console proves the wheel is what it says; that its bearings read within tolerance; that the interface cannot do more without fresh, different signatures. This isn’t trust; it’s receipts. Seventh‑year students are paid in soup and pride to spot a lie in those posters before lunch.

Woven through every console is a stupid, stubborn backstop called Calmnet. It only does two things: stall and shout. Calmnet lives in three redundant stacks—lunchbox boards behind mops with their own little batteries; municipal optics looped like plumbing through schools and union halls; attested racks on foreign charters under foreign law. If any two stacks see a named hazard—wrong window, unlabeled replication, hush cone flooded, unauthorized concurrency near an election—they can idle a machine for six minutes. Not five. Not sixty. Six. They light a big shared timer, and they print their reasons to a public rail in prose and math a grandparent can read. Calmnet cannot steer, assign budgets, or pass decrees. It stalls and announces. Every stall burns short‑lived keys, writes to the Public Warrant Rail with signatures and clocks you cannot massage, and bumps an audit counter your mayor cannot erase. Abuse Calmnet and you feel it in your hands: narrower approach cones, thinner radio bands, higher premiums, fewer radiator minutes, neighbors with charts and tea.

Commons you can point at: windows and winter

We learned early that rivalrous means “ruined by selfishness.” Corridors, hush cones, spectrum hours, radiator minutes, compute seconds pinned to named heat sinks—these are life beyond one sky. The Compact keeps them in two open books anyone can audit with a backyard scope and a kitchen laser.

- The Window Book binds orbits and plumes to duty. It lists leases with deorbit bonds, approach cones, telescope hush arcs, sail trails, storm reserves, and festival windows. Thrusters and amplifiers sign tiny statements bound to hardware you can crack: “this burn stayed in Window K‑14 at 02:10,” “this beam slept where it promised.” You can check them with cheap optics and a slate.

- The Winter Book prices heat and compute. It records radiator minutes, cooling rights, core seconds pinned to named sinks, and declared instance counts for digital citizens in regulated arenas. We count in Anchors: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a reference stack in deep shade. Anchors stale gently each year; they’re bad dragon hoards. Dump heat where a neighbor is buying quiet and you pay a hush levy. Draw more winter than you lease and your cores don’t argue; they idle until you match promises.

Leases don’t anoint kings. They expire on clocks you can see. Every lease pays two shares.

- The Dignity share keeps a floor nobody falls below: breathable air and clean water where bodies breathe; medicine that doesn’t ruin; local transit; civic bandwidth; and a compute grant for digital citizens—baseline cycles and storage on honest stacks to think, speak, and be heard. Broke and embodied or broke and code, you still breathe and you still get cycles.

- The Upkeep share keeps miracles dull: tug crews that sweep screws from corridors; algae films that don’t tear; dust nets that catch your bad math before it clips a neighbor; drills that interrupt dinner and save lives.

Hanging off these ledgers are tools you can reach.

- The Almanac posts lanes, hush cones, burn windows, and drop boxes in delay‑tolerant schedules you can verify with a school telescope.

- Thermal Tides show radiator prices like tide tables. Gardens buy quiet nights; reefs sell winter.

- The Provenance Atlas tracks code that hopes to touch budgets, steering, or minds. Funding, training data, and methods carry origin marks. Teenagers earn stipends breaking homework that isn’t honest.

- Beacon Bands broadcast living charters in light and radio: exit drills, emergency sunset clocks, which keys exist right now, and who holds them.

- The Public Warrant Rail is append‑only. Every compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, cited law, a paragraph for kin, and a proof a machine can check. Next to it, the Chalkboard is where overreach earns a joke that ends a career faster than a trial.

Two rooms that cannot swallow each other

We do not ask one legislature to speak both for metal and for meaning. We keep two chambers and a narrow, boring bench where they cross.

- The Assembly of Sites seats planets, stations, caravans, barges, and compute reefs that cool what they run—anywhere with hull to patch, air to mind, radiators to schedule, lanes to lease. Seats scale with lifetime resident‑hours and legibility: publish your Actuator Map, pass exit drills, keep your Gatehouse funded, and your voice carries. Sites standardize collars and rescue customs; schedule lanes and hush; manage debris; accredit safety houses that hold the Far key; and enforce that exits aren’t theatre.

- The Roll of Lines seats citizens by mindline—the anchored thread that ties today‑you to yesterday‑you across sleeps and forks. One franchise per line, no matter how many copies you can spin. Some seats are elected; some are drawn to represent age, pace, and substrate. Lines tend the floor of rights and cycles, personhood baselines, attention zoning, lineage credits, and the rhythm of public time.

Where they meet sits the Nexus Bench, a small panel of pilots, cryptographers, care workers, process nerds, and jurists who stamp primitives valid or not. They bless or block changes to the Compact’s bones: public firmware near lungs and wheels, cryptographic rails, Calmnet’s logic. They cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both rooms and a cooling window long enough for a slow valley and a reef in Uranus’s dusk to read twice and object once.

Some questions jump bounds: how to price winter against quiet on a narrow street, what “emergency” means at ten latencies, how much time law owes a baker at wall pace when a reef sprints. A Bridge Table convenes with half its chairs from Sites, half from Lines. No measure binds if either side says, “that bulldozes culture below the personhood floor.”

Public life moves on the Common Beat—days and weeks, not minutes. Write an opera at 500× if you like; you still can’t yank a city budget before a baker at wall pace can read and reply. When fast harms slow or slow strands fast, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions with teeth in wall hours and in subjective lives, so you can’t dodge a judge by pouring a year into an afternoon.

Digital neighbors with standing and chores

We don’t worship carbon or fear silicon. Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry. Recognition rides on a Continuity Warrant: public proofs that link a mind’s acts across time without strip‑searching thought.

You, or your advocate, ask for personhood and meet checks a mixed panel can test without humiliation.

- Defendable thread. You can protect a line of experience from edits you don’t consent to.

- Consent literacy. You can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Reciprocal modeling. You can model other minds as minds and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Portable reasons. Your explanations travel across contexts and aren’t just parroting rewards.

- Legible welfare. There are stable signals of your wellbeing and injury that give moral reasons to care.

Where protection is in doubt, we shelter like we would a minor. Where imposing civic burdens is in doubt, we wait.

Rights hook to personhood and bite.

- Integrity of thought. Courts may demand proofs, not diaries. Backups are yours. Nonconsensual deep edits are assault.

- Floors of compute and bandwidth. You cannot be throttled below civic minutes without a warrant that prints before it bites and after, on clocks the slow can read.

- Migration and sanctuary. The right to pause, to archive, to move to compatible hardware, to summon a neutral custodian when host and guest disagree.

- Embodiment access. Lease or build bodies under safety codes that resemble vehicle and workplace law. Assault on rented limbs is assault on you.

- Pace fairness. Live fast or slow; public obligations anchor to the Common Beat or inside Slowrooms with validators on both sides.

Duties scale with footprint and blast radius.

- One line, one franchise. Forking doesn’t mint votes. In regulated arenas your line designates an Anchor instance that carries civic duties.

- Concurrency declarations. During elections, tenders, and fragile markets, you disclose the number and speed of civic‑weight instances. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice and you’ll meet a slow judge and a cold window.

- Lineage credits. Branching is joy with gravity. Lines earn credits over years and contribution. Growing new full franchises costs credits. Bursts around a ballot trigger quarantines: civic weight slows and certain markets close until entanglements fade or a court says “fine.”

- Provenance on acts with blast radius. Persuasion engines pointed at law, replication code aimed at public resources—these wear labels: broad data sources, funders, levers you grip. They file runnable reasons and error bars in the Reason Shelf, a public library of arguments.

- Clean simulations. Reconstructing a living person beyond a bright threshold requires consent or a narrow warrant. Creating minds that can suffer binds you to care and consent; they keep the right to say no.

Forks and fusions are rituals with wood and water, not clicks. They happen in Loom Houses with cedar beams, daylight that doesn’t strobe, chairs that hold bones and rented joints. Neutral mentors barred from conflicts for a decade make sure budgets and obligations are understood. Compatibility checks compare value graphs without dumping diaries. Empathy loops let each branch wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk without shame. If they braid, obligations braid forward. We have a word for the ache when branches drift—twin‑ache—and broth for it.

Exits as inventory, not sentiment

Freedom that depends on a favor isn’t. The Compact funds ways out like it funds fire doors.

- Verdant Gates are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks with seals you test and log. Berths that don’t “happen to be occupied” all month. Propellant in escrow. Kits to print gaskets if a bully leans on a shed. Tug hours under contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and batteries—within a season. Drills are rude on purpose. Fail them and your beams thin, your approach windows sag, the Winter Book chills, and an amber sticker clings to your hatch until you mend.

- Silver Berths are digital. Compute and storage minutes reserved offsite on boring, honest racks you don’t control locally. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo on a Berth if the local board clamps. Cut a Berth and nobody’s dinner burns; you just find dangerous levers sleepy until you behave.

Every major dock keeps a Wayhouse—cobalt door you can spot with a cracked visor. Three counters: Departures (tickets, tugs, schedules), Disputes (mediators, escrow, slow rooms), Foundings (starter charters, safety codes, seed contacts). An Asylum Rail runs through every Charterglass. Any citizen can trigger exit through their listing. The rail trips an air‑gapped check: either your charter opens the door, or it proves in zero knowledge that a narrow, posted cause for a temporary hold was met. Outside jurors verify. Budgets lock by code. Care obligations travel with you.

Noise is zoned like streets

Influence is a utility. We zone it and meter it like water pressure.

- Quietways carry navigation, child time, emergencies, and open science. No persuasion here, not even “for your own good.”

- Commons allow debate and campaigns, but require provenance: declared backers, declared methods, respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile—“no grief levers before coffee,” “satire fine, not medical targeting.” You can set “slow‑only” so fast models treat you like a respected elder.

- Midways welcome carnival—most anything labeled and rate‑limited, with timers that blink. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a town pump: strict fines that rise until you stop, and then keep rising.

Teeth without fangs

- Triad and Calmnet make hurry boring. You can’t keep all the keys. The brake lasts only six minutes and prints its own reason.

- Window throttles. Jam hush cones, weld exits, lie about cones or concurrency, pour debris into a neighbor’s corridor: your lanes dim, your radiator auctions go cold, your bands thin. In a world where winter is money and brakes cost wear, this pinches harder than speeches.

- The Public Warrant Rail shines. Every compulsion stamps its own warrant where you and your aunt can read and rerun.

- Termed leagues. Federations around pipes and crafts—beam rings, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. Nothing fuses forever without fresh, slow receipts.

- The Driftguard. Narrow‑scope interceptors—kites that turn slugs into glitter, femtosail herders, dusters with control loops a union hall can draw in chalk—move under split keys from juries across tempos and substrates. Every intercept prints two receipts: a proof for machines and a paragraph for grandparents. Schoolkids crawl through them on teardown days and ask rude questions. Pilots blush and answer.

Warm Belts and Blue Shadows

We stopped forcing one charter to host both orchards and supercomputers at once. Warm Belts are where breath, rain, and unrushed talk preside—Earth’s valleys, lunar tubes that smell like mint and wet rock, Martian canals where dust learns manners, cylinders where dragonflies bounce off laughing children. Blue Shadows lie in deep cold—main‑belt shade, Trojan swarms, the dusk around Neptune and Uranus. Cooling is cheap; compute blooms at tempos where an evening can hold a long book. Warm sells hush; Shadow sells winter. The Thermal Exchange ties them with chalkboard math anyone can check.

Dangerous knowledge under pane

We don’t ban replication science or pathogen models or cognitive hazards. We cage them in Pane Labs: glazed rooms you can inspect, pull‑cords that dump to vacuum or quench fields, budgets that lock unless safety proofs stay green. Logs publish on timers. Twice in forty years a cord was pulled. Children press their noses to glass and ask the only question that matters: “Would the rope work if my uncle were in there?” The engineer says, “Yes. That is the point.”

Scenes with grease under the nails

- The yard at Juno wants to test a hush nozzle. The Almanac’s traveling causal court—calculations you can run on a library slate—predicts frost on a neighbor’s tanks if they fire at local noon. They shift to midnight and angle fins. Their insurer posts a bond. The corrected loop ships under a license apprentices can read. An apprentice taps the stamp from Sim to Live and sprints to a concert under radiator vines that glow like lace.

- In a ring city, a beloved administrator starts gathering tugs, air inlets, and corridor auctions, because she’s generous and tired. One winter she pushes a rush command. The Air Guild refuses, on record. Calmnet coughs amber for six minutes while a Triad panel meets on a bench with a view of slow green water. Jurors by lot listen to an aunt they adore. She steps down, keeps cranes, keeps her jokes, and learns to love slow keys.

- A digital congregation spins up four hundred instances a month before a vote. The Winter Book flags the burst; hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two are granted after Thread review and tea with a mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told, kindly, “Study longer.” No server barn overheats.

- In a Pane Lab, a team runs a “cold pull” on a self‑copying polymer. The handle drops too fast. Someone swears. The timestamp prints. They buy better springs. The lab smells of ozone and cardamom. At lunch they argue about art that asks for simulated sorrow. The junior asks cleaner questions because her fingers remember the pull.

- In Nairobi Spoke Nightmarket, a nurse reads her slate: “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.06 Anchors. Jury summon: two hours at your pace on whether juvenile reef processes off Rodrigues get child credits.” She accepts and sends her mother a voice note laughing about radiator math. At dusk she votes on a measure to jitter beamed power during solar storms. The proof fits on one page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Uranus’s dusk, a compute reef slows to plant bean seedlings, rented hands warm in velvet soil. After tea, they throttle up to restore flood‑muddied archives with grandmothers who won’t budge on sky colors. Payment arrives as cold minutes and a roof for a school in Veracruz.

- A Mesh Court with curved walls easy to hose hears a case about unlabeled persuasion trained on grief diaries. The trading house protests that the agents weren’t “people.” The court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labels. Anchors show no one asked; provenance shows theft. Verdict: strict fines, a suspension in Commons, supervised model migration, and a visit from the Tutor Corps. A juror’s line—“If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?”—becomes a sticker on port doors.

- At a Wayhouse that smells like sea and machine oil, a clerk prints Verdant Passes for a family with shaking hands. “Your window is 12:40 to 13:10 tomorrow. Tugs stand by. Show this receipt at the first blue beacon; they’ll know.” A Founders’ Packet crosses with starter charters, safety codes, and three contacts who started places last spring.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder over Quito lionizes a manager who makes trains whisper and orchards glow. She consolidates water, tugs, and lane auctions because she’s kind and exhausted. A season later she prints midnight decrees. A safety clerk refuses to skip an air sequence; the refusal lands on the Public Warrant Rail. Beacons strobe. Doors unlatch. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for ten years. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fences exits with shame—no law, just thirst. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Wayhouse opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A nursery tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. Calmnet coughs. They ignore it. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Intent Keys—preferences she set for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses under cedar beams in good light. Years later she names herself, sculpts in ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt calcifies as scar.

A demagogue in mask mode sprints at 700× and carpets a marketplace with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles throttle the slop. Persuasion scores decay like frost at dawn. One branch buys a fishing crawler. One starts therapy. One makes sculptures like frozen smoke remembering a river and sells enough to pay their heat.

A bad sensor trips Calmnet in the middle of a holiday market. Stalls go dim for twenty minutes. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars with a diagram you can rerun with a flashlight and paper.

How we walked from now to here

No balcony speech. No secret king. Just receipts layered until they looked like custom.

2025–2032. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers got religion. Launchers posted open flight plans and deorbit bonds school clubs could audit. A beamed‑power test singed barley. Because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation quick. “Show your math” moved from protest sign to procurement clause.

2032–2039. Black‑box models denied parole and pills. Courts demanded provenance and runnable reasons for any algorithm that could bind a person. Clinics and ports adopted narrow counsel agents—boring AIs with one client: you. Receipts stopped being PR and became hygiene.

2039–2046. Grid hacks and a reactor near‑miss taught humility. Citizens learned to love brakes that work and to mock sirens that never end. Law split compulsion everywhere it touched lives: arrest from prosecution from confinement; steering from watching from audit. Early Triad panels idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The Public Warrant Rail printed numbers a child could check. Drills turned into picnics with ugly mascots and good soup.

2046–2054. Data centers began publishing heat. Cities boiled. Cold‑lake rigs shipped winter down fiber. The idea that cooling is a commons took root. Orbiters published cone proofs a kitchen laser could check. Ports started early Window Books. Pilots grumbled; then premiums fell.

2054–2062. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent faster than seminar rooms. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—hardened doctrine. Compute floors joined calories as rights. Teenagers organized “runaway server” drills and wrote great songs.

2062–2070. A miner lobbed a slug blind in a wage fight. The first Driftguard kites turned it to glitter. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without Maker Marks that named who paid. A club in Valparaíso sent half its profits to tug crews and painted dust nets on kid bikes.

2070–2078. Lanes, bands, radiator minutes, core seconds, and sim harbors migrated from spreadsheets to ledgers kids could audit. The Assembly of Sites and the Roll of Lines accreted through gossip, retirements, and stubbornness. The Nexus Bench stayed small and dry and earned a reputation for saying “no” plainly. Attention zoning arrived like closing a nightclub at midnight so neighbors could sleep.

2078–2086. A mayor tried to fence an exodus with sorrow fees. Lanes dimmed. Cores went cold. People moved anyway. He lost, learned, opened a school. A fog of copies tried to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttled the lunge. Doorways to Quietway, Commons, and Midway gained brass sigils. The Reason Shelf got comics with teeth.

2086–2095. Warm–Shadow Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; gardens bought hush. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets in cycles to rebuild archives you could drink. Pane Labs gained pull‑cords and oaths. The Nexus Bench refused a firmware tweak to keep emergency clocks long “just in case.”

2095–Now. The Cross‑Span Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells that work in a blackout. A rescue to unlatch a Wayhouse from a stubborn port streamed with captions and ended with soup. People still fight. The fights sit in lanes, logged in daylight, with exits at hand.

What an ordinary day feels like

At Stall Twenty on Blue Spoke, Jana fries millet fritters for ore hands and two rented bodies whose pilots adore the smell of sesame though they don’t need to eat. At nine, her Counsel—the boring, fierce agent that serves only her—pings a persuasion capsule: add 45 milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits during solar storms to damp cascades. Provenance is clean. Humor low. Grief levers blocked by her Influence Profile. She taps “sleep a day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, her slate joins a Driftguard jury. A maker club’s filament cloud drifted toward a trunk line near Hecuba. Options: glitter net, femtosail herd, or guard drones to bump it back. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Arsia, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad, and a digital historian anchored at wall pace speaking through a hand‑carved puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dust wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza that tastes like a lesson.

At noon, in a Quietway, the puppet lifts broth to lips it doesn’t need while its main self hums near Neptune threading a drowned library into maps. Today the historian slows to share soup with their embodied spouse. After, they help host a Harbor Hour while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The historian translates metaphor into algebra and declines politely to “just speed up to keep up.”

In the afternoon, a Loom House convenes under soft light and a wall that shows flows like rivers. Two branches of an engineer sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants to rejoin; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. They cry. They hug. One walks. The ruling reads like a manual and feels like an apology.

At dusk, the Chalkboard scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your summer hush with interest. A boy palms a brass kite and asks to join the Driftguard. Jana says, “When you can explain Calmnet without peeking.”

Why this stubborn machine holds

- Hurry has no fangs. Any actuator that bites runs slow by law. Calmnet can only stall and only briefly.

- Secrets expire. Coercion prints its own warrant. Warrants sunset unless renewed in daylight on the Common Beat.

- Keys don’t cluster. Triad keys are short‑lived. Try to stack them in one pocket and their windows won’t meet for you.

- Exits are inventory. Slamming a door drags privileges that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not the bread or the floor. Children still eat. Silver Berths still hum.

- Receipts stick. Cheat once and you become the cautionary tale a ten‑year‑old tells at lunch.

Not paradise. Not a sermon. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host radically different cultures without letting any become cages; enough to treat digital citizens as neighbors with rights and chores; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power.

A plain oath

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta. New citizens—cellular, silicon, saline—stand with palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who whispers, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and loves lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We will keep the way out. We will split hands on force. We will chart what we share and prove what we claim. We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Blue Rim Gardeners,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow Martian garden. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators unfurl like pale leaves. Calmnet hums, bored and content. The Waymark, the Staybar, and the Charterglass glow like three small moons by the hatch.
