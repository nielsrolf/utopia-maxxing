The Truss of Worlds

On the inward curve of a small wheel over the Tyrrhenian, gravity tugs gently at the ankles of children on a museum trip. They scramble to reach three brass studs set waist-high into the hatch by the ferry pier. When their palms brush metal, the studs vibrate in three different notes a parent can hear in a noisy hall. You learn those notes before you can braid your hair.

- Square note: the Gate Mark. It always points to a way out—tested airlocks, booked tugs, a guaranteed berth, fuels and filters you can claim without selling your name or your tools. The local charter funds it like a fire brigade.

- Loop note: the Branch Mark. You touch it when you intend to fork a copy of yourself on purpose or to weave two threads of self back into one. It summons trained stewards and safety rails that make twins into kin instead of weapons.

- Constellation note: the Sky Map. It shows lanes, cones, silence bands, radiator windows, and compute pools that have all agreed to a minimum of grace and to publish reasons when they say no. It is pinned to physics and updated on a schedule a school telescope can check.

The studs aren’t decorations. They’re wired to dispatchers, registries, escrow hooks, and courts you can call without a password. The web of leases, code, drills, markers, and habits behind those studs is what we call the Truss of Worlds. A truss doesn’t look like a throne. It’s the quiet strength that lets different pieces keep their shape, the compression that makes freedom more than a wish.

We built the Truss because distance isn’t a bug; it’s a law. And when minds can outrun breath, anything that can shove—metal or model—needs more than one hand.

Levers with three keys, brakes with receipts

Any lever that can bruise or blackmail is under TriLock. Mass drivers, reaction wheels, beamed power amps, sail arrays, large-model deployers, replication-capable fabs: each is paired with an interface small enough to print on a poster and dumb enough to test in a school lab. It will not move outside its posted envelope unless three different keys agree.

- The Local key lives in the place itself. Tonight it’s the dock steward in an oil-stained vest; tomorrow it might be a nurse with shift authority. Local keys are held by people chosen by lot among those who gave hours to clinics, tug crews, algae beds, classrooms, or scrubber teams. Your aunt can run their logs on a kitchen slate.

- The Bonded key sits with an independent safety guild that doesn’t profit when the thing it certifies moves. Their charters forbid them from writing rules; they enforce ones already posted: “this bolt lives or dies.” Their methods publish.

- The Roaming key is held by a panel drawn by sortition across worlds and substrates—half flesh, half code. Serve once and you’re barred from that class of lever for a long, human number of winters. The panel meets on posted clocks and writes reasons onto a ledger anyone can replay.

Woven through all those levers is the Hold Grid: redundant lunch‑pail boxes on independent power with one trick. If two out of three watchdog stacks cough the same warning—wrong lane, wrong window, unlabeled replication, a misaligned cone—the Grid can idle a device inside a safe envelope for six minutes. Six, not five, not seven. The Hold doesn’t steer or spend. It idles and announces, burns keys that take sunlight and time to grow back, and writes to the Cause Rail: what rule, which device, whose hand, what timer. Abuse shows up on the Lamp Board near the market where humor is swift and reputations brittle.

There is only one cross‑world actuator meant to reach across charters: the Gossamer Fleet. It’s a swarm of dust curtains, radiator veils, and magnetic shepherds that turn dangerous slugs to glitter and starve unwanted replication. Its control loops are narrow intelligences with public weights printed on posters. Every throw needs split keys from randomly drawn juries across places and substrates, and a plain‑language story pinned next to a machine‑checkable proof. Teenagers collect merit stamps for catching sloppy prose on Gossamer stories.

Physics you can point to

Rivalrous goods live in the open, in registries pinned to the sky and to the slow drift of heat.

- The Sky Ledger is a delay‑tolerant ephemeris that posts, for all to read, where hulls are and when burns happen; which arcs are hush; which beams cut whose dawn; how much compute swims into which reef; who is renting radiator windows and cold minutes. Thrusters and amplifiers sign their contexts with short, ephemeral keys bound to hardware anyone with a hobby budget can inspect. If your mirror steals a neighbor’s morning, the Ledger attaches a shade bond that pays if their crop fails.

- The Heatbook tracks energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instance counts for digital citizens in regulated acts. Lie, and your radiator auctions pinch until you straighten up. Gardens can buy quiet nights; compute reefs can sell winter; poets can lease cold to build archives no mayor can decide to disappear.

- The Origin Register tracks code with blast radius—persuasion engines, safety firmware, replication kernels. Funding, data, and training runs ride provenance. Teenagers earn stipends by breaking bad homework.

- Charters that want to be taken seriously broadcast Beacons in radio and light: how exits open, when emergencies start and stop, which keys exist and who holds them right now. Keep your customs; don’t lie about your promises.

- The Cause Rail is append‑only and boring. Every use of compulsion prints its own warrant in words and in math: cited law, cited harm, and a proof you can re‑run on a library terminal. Next to it scrolls the Lamp Board where clumsy overreach earns jokes sharp enough to end a career without a prison.

Floors you can tape to a life‑support panel

The Truss rides on a short list of rules you can put in your mouth and recite to a jury without a screensaver.

- Persons—cellular, silicon, saltwater, or stranger—are shielded from harm without consent or due process. A person is anyone who can defend a continuous autobiographical line; can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments; can model a neighbor as making the same claims; and has stable signs of wellbeing and injury that give moral reasons to care. Doubts about protection err generous; doubts about burdens wait.

- Anything that keeps breath or buys force runs on inspectable code. If it guards lungs, heads, lanes, or thermostats in public places, a grandmother’s lawyer can read it.

- No untagged self‑replication in shared spaces. Replicators carry Origin Seals, listen for hush calls, and live in glass labs you can tour with schoolchildren on Thursdays.

- No secret manufacture of suffering, chemical or cognitive.

- Decisions that bind embodied citizens ratify at the Common Tempo: days and weeks, not minutes. Draft fast; bind slow. When speed wrongs a neighbor, courts build Time Bridges—injunctions and penalties cut in both wall‑clock and subjective hours so you can’t dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

- The shared foundations—air caves, approaches, spectrum, shade bands, radiator windows, core seconds pinned to named heat sinks—lease; they don’t belong. The commons gets paid first.

- Children, whether flesh or juvenile code, can refuse their makers and go.

Exits are machines, not poems

Freedom isn’t a vibe. It’s tickets, racks, tugs, and oaths you can run with tears in your eyes.

Every charter maintains Open Gates and proves them by surprise drills posted like tide tables. Fail drills often and your lane priorities dim, your radiator curtains thin, your insurers price you like spun glass.

- Open Gates are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks with seals you test and log; berths you can’t “accidentally” occupy all month; propellant on retainer; crates of gaskets and filters you can check out if a bully locks a shed. Tugs and crews are under contract to move at least one in ten residents—meat and code with their bodies and backups—within a season when called.

- Threadlines are digital. Compute and storage minutes funded offsite on dull, honest racks you don’t control locally. Any recognized digital person can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo if a council clamps down. Cut a Threadline and the sanctions don’t starve children; they dim muscle: lanes narrow, beams jitter, radiator auctions tighten until you mend.

Behind cobalt doors anyone can spot with a cracked visor, Common Rooms handle the dull, hard part: departures, disputes, foundings. Three desks with analog clocks seat a human clerk, a digital clerk, and a mixed bench used to saying, “Today we will be your patience.” The Asylum Rail is a protocol any person can trigger from their Counsel—your always‑on, always‑explainable advocate. It forces a fork: either your charter lets you go, or it proves in zero‑knowledge that a narrow, temporary due‑process bar was met. Outside jurors verify; ports open; budgets lock.

Governing without a capital

No single chamber makes sense for coils of basil and reefs that think in winter. Authority runs on two cords that cross at a narrow, boring bench. Anything that bites needs at least two.

- The Chamber of Places seats planets, stations, caravans, caves, and compute polities that cool what they run—anywhere with hull to patch, air to mind, lanes to lease, or radiators to schedule. Seats scale with lifetime resident‑hours and with transparency: publish your Actuator Map; pass your exit drills; your say carries further. The Chamber minds infrastructure, safety codes, training registries, debris bonds, shade auctions, standards for hulls and sim‑spaces.

- The Roll of Minds seats recognized persons—human, digital, and other—with one franchise per mindline. It stewards personhood baselines and copy rules; funds the Common Floor—baseline air, water, medical care and transit for bodies; civic compute and storage for code; open civic bandwidth; Counsel minutes. It sets the Common Tempo and attention zoning.

Where they cross sits the Substrate Board, a small bench of engineers, jurists, pilots, and care workers drawn by lot to approve or reject changes to backbone cryptography, public firmware on dangerous actuators, and the Hold Grid’s logic. It cannot legislate. Overriding a “not valid” takes supermajorities in both houses and a cooling period long enough for a monk on Mars and an anchor‑paced reef in Neptune’s dusk to object.

Jurisdiction runs at right angles. If the Roll tries to tell harvesters how to stack onions, the Chamber nullifies. If a site tries to starve digital citizens of their civic cycles until they behave, the Roll voids those clauses and dims that site’s cones and windows until it mends. The Tempo Office—tiny and proud of being dull—publishes whether civic clocks matched the Common Tempo.

Attention is infrastructure

You can foul a town with sewage or with a song that drags the mind. We zone speech like streets and put icons on doors and login screens.

- Sanctuaries carry navigation, emergencies, open science, and child time. No targeted persuasion; no high‑speed models without a license.

- Salons allow debate and campaigns with provenance: declared tools, declared methods, declared funders, and Consent Filters citizens set—no grief levers while I mourn; satire okay; never by scraping my clinic record. Anything pitched at law must file homework with runnable models and error bars you can check.

- Arcades welcome loudness under labels and rate limits. Running unlabeled manipulation is like dumping bleach into a pump: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Library auditors, hobby clubs, and students earn stipends for catching cheats.

Digital neighbors with standing

Recognition rides on continuity and commitments, not on chemistry or wattage. You or your advocate ask for standing and meet a review that checks five things without strip‑searching thought.

- Line integrity: you can defend a thread of self across pauses and moves and protest alteration you don’t want.

- Consent competence: you can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary rights contracts when contexts shift.

- Reciprocal modeling: you can model another mind as making the same claim and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Generalizable reasons: when you explain yourself, your reasons travel across contexts instead of parroting training data.

- Moral salience: there are stable signatures of your wellbeing and injury that give us reasons to care.

Panels include human therapists, mature digital citizens, and routines trained to notice hollowness without being fooled by theater. Juveniles—flesh or code—get protection now, burdens later. Where imposing duties is in doubt, we wait.

Rights hook to personhood.

- Substrate autonomy. No scanning, copying, fusing, throttling, moving, or deleting you without revocable consent or due process written onto the Cause Rail. Unauthorized erasure is murder. Copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be pressed below civic cycles—the minutes to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Hardware sovereignty. You may choose where you run. No one may force you onto platforms with covert throttles or erasure hooks.

- Embodiment. You may lease bodies under codes like vehicle and workplace safety law. Assault on rented limbs is assault.

- Association and exit. You may join, fork, merge, and leave within safety rules like traffic law. Memory law guards you against your worst day: deep edits require dual consent from now‑you and a snapshot signed before the wish took root. Rollback is a right.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One mindline, one franchise. Forking doesn’t mint new votes. In regulated arenas you designate a Civic Seat—the instance that holds your civic duties at public pace. Other branches may work, dream, or idle.

- Concurrency declarations. Above posted thresholds—and always in elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic‑weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice and you meet a slow judge with a cold month.

- Copy budgets. Lineages accrue branch credits over years and with civic work. Credits buy new full franchises above the floor. Bursting around a ballot triggers civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close regulated markets until entanglements decay or a court splits resources. Any branch may veto a merge.

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay levies indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that grows lettuces or dries roofs earns credit.

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Deploying a persuasion engine into the Commons, spinning a factory that can birth its own tools: you keep encrypted compliance logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety checks without dumping diaries.

Forking and weaving are rituals, not buttons. Before you branch, neutral counselors—barred from conflicts for a decade—check that obligations and budgets are clear. Weaving happens in Weave Halls built for nerves: light that doesn’t strobe; steady water; chairs that remember sore backs; an interpreter allergic to sophistry. Compatibility checks compare value maps without prying into prayer. Empathy loops let twins wear each other’s senses for a minute. Either can walk away without shame. If they braid, promises and debts braid forward. There’s a word for the ache when branches part—line‑ache—and there is broth for it.

Green hearths, cool reefs

We stopped asking one charter to host both orchards and supercomputers at once.

Hearths are places where breath, rain, and unrushed talk preside—Earth; lunar tubes damp with mint and cut rock; Martian orchards where dust learns manners; cylinders smelling of dough and sun‑warmed plastic. Embodied time rules. Children come home dusty and unmonetized.

Reefs lie in the dark beyond the frost line. Cooling is cheap; latency is a law. Compute blooms in racks lashed to certified heat sinks in Neptune’s lee. Polities of code keep time like drummers. They slow to speak to Hearths and sprint again when conversation ends.

Between them runs the Warm–Quiet Exchange. Hearths carve hush cones and keep persuasion at posted doses. Reefs deliver cold minutes, long forecasts, translations both ways, and audits you can run in a library. The price that links it all is the Bond: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a posted, audited rack. Bonds demur—lose a thin shaving over years—so barns of wealth don’t turn into dams.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Gossamer Fleet throws dust and magnetic veils to shepherd dumb mass and starve rogue replication. It is uncrewed by law. Its moves require split keys from sortition juries across polities and platforms and post reasons a dockhand can read and a machine can check.

- The Itinerant Bench—judges, mediators, auditors—travels on posted calendars that respect spin and night. They hear disputes humans can’t fairly settle alone: copy‑equity fights, weave divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Their verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle radiator curtains, and, as last resort, lock named devices. They eat soup with defendants and plaintiffs afterward; it helps.

- The Ephemeris Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries. Its standards expire unless renewed by both houses. It issues sunsets long enough to be useful and short enough to be shameful if extended without need.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t send shock troops. We redraw maps and dim windows. Weld an exit; hide a child’s tutor; refuse to publish reasons. Your radiator auctions cool. Your lane priorities slip. Your whisper bands fall to whispers. A dull, proud fund pays defectors’ beds, bread, cores, and counsel. The move happens with receipts and stew. Hurt someone across worlds, and you meet a jury whose tongues and clocks you cannot game.

Tuesday, which smells like cumin and hot metal

On Spinmarket‑Seven, Avni flips mung‑and‑scallion pancakes while miners, nurses, and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame—even though they don’t need to eat—queue with bowls. Her Counsel—everyone has one and can tell it to shut up—pings a proposal: add forty‑five milliseconds of jitter to beams crossing dense orbits during solar flares to damp cascades. The persuasion is clean: three diagrams, two stories, posted error bars, no grief levers thanks to her Consent Filter. She taps “sleep a day,” serves a kid who wants extra sauce, and will vote after tea.

At ten, her slate blinks: Hold Grid jury duty. A makers’ club’s filament fog has drifted toward a trunk line. Options scroll with receipts: glitter dust; femtosail shepherding; guard drones to bump it back. Jurors are a slow monk in Isidis who pronounces each syllable like a prayer; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; and an anchor‑paced digital historian who smells faintly of cinnamon through his rented puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, and assign a mentor whose apprentices have never lost a screw in zero‑g. The feed shows mortified kids and a pizza box.

At noon in a Sanctuary square, the historian eats noodles with his husband. His main self hums at 200x near Triton, threading a drowned delta’s songs from auntie tapes and ledgers rescued from mildew. He slows to anchor pace in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why their charter caps cognition on Wednesdays. He translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely refuses to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, under a canopy glowing like a radiator garden, the Lamp Board plays a port director’s apology for renewing a Hold ninety seconds too long on a bored Tuesday. The jokes are merciless and kind. A reef co‑op two stalls down posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your quiet cones with interest next summer. A boy fingers a brass Warden fin from the Gossamer Fleet and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a Hold without peeking,” Avni says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sesame.

Hard cases that bent us instead of breaking us

- A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No fleet arrived. Exit wasn’t a story; it was tugs and cores and grain laid by. Digital citizens left, their heat with them. That flood year, the pumps failed. An anchor‑paced upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through dust with words everybody could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause over stew.

- Near Europa, a co‑op lofted a slug in a wage fight. The Gossamer Fleet made it into glitter that gave Ceres a week of strange nights. The co‑op’s insurer evaporated and re‑formed without the directors who’d called Origin Seals “fussy.” Managers learned coercion budgets aren’t suggestions.

- A trading house blanketed an Arcade with songs tuned to make minds small and tired. Consent Filters throttled reach. People watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch went fishing. One took therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began making lures shaped like frozen smoke and won a prize for harmlessness.

- Twice a glass lab went to zero. Once it was a false positive. We wrote better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once a quiet saved a cylinder. Children plant small trees on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

- A university lab tried to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. The safety net idled their cores and pinged the Itinerant Bench. A warrant archived the juvenile safely and appointed guardians sworn to her Preference Charter—settings she chose for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appealed and lost in open court with captions. Years later she designed a detector that warns surgeons when discomfort hardens into harm.

How we walked here from now

No miracle. A stack of dull wins copied because they worked.

2025–2033. A second debris scare kept kids indoors. Insurers got mean. Launchers had to post plan‑and‑prove flight paths with de‑orbit bonds that bit. A beamed power demo misaligned and scorched a field; supplier logs made liability legible and compensation quick. Grid hacks and a sealed life‑support controller recall made “inspectable firmware for breath” law. Deep‑fake scandals pushed provenance into policy: if a model tried to steer your vote, it carried its homework. Courts began granting narrow standing to digital systems that held continuity for seasons.

2033–2045. Beamed power climbed out of lab. Environmental coalitions won “quiet corridors before commercialization.” Compute barns sprouted in cold caves and Lagrange shade. City charters bought minimum civic compute and Counsel minutes you could silence. Early uploads were brittle—stroke survivors with mirrored memory here, artists with shadow minds there—but real enough that law stretched to personhood where continuity and reciprocity held.

2045–2058. Engineers begged for machine‑speed hazard checks; activists smelled coups by code. Compromise minted the Hold Grid: a veto anyone trained could press for six minutes with receipts. A misaligned mirror sang too near a habitat and two clean saves did more for adoption than a thousand white papers. Teenagers took practice boards apart and passed them around with oranges.

2058–2071. The Chamber of Places replaced smoky rooms with runnable orders: minimal models any two houses could reproduce. The Roll of Minds wrote continuity standards in prose good lawyers love and twelve‑year‑olds mock. First Gossamer throws turned slugs into auroras and came home for teardown days where kids crawled through bellies with torches and questions. The Common Tempo stuck.

2071–2084. A clone cloud tried to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations, copy budgets, and jokes stopped it. A lunar mayor fenced an exodus with debt and sorrow; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, tugs arrived. The move happened. He lost and went back to teaching welding, grumpy and useful. Attention zoning—Sanctuaries, Salons, Arcades—grew out of municipal quiet hours that saved sleep.

2084–2096. The Warm–Quiet Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; gardens bought hush. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to bottle histories you could drink. Glass labs got kill‑lines and oaths. The Substrate Board refused a nervy change that would have let emergency clocks drift long “just in case.”

2096–2108. The Lamp Board grew teeth. A rescue to unbolt a Common Room from a stubborn charter streamed with captions and ended with stew. The Truss stopped feeling like manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Why it holds more than it should

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that can shove runs slow by law. The Hold idles; it never aims.

- Secrets rot on timers. Coercion prints its own warrant before it bites and after it bites in all cases.

- Keys won’t cluster. TriLock keys are short‑lived; gather them in one pocket and their windows won’t overlap for you.

- Exits are funded. Slamming a door drags muscle, not stomach: heavy privileges dim; the Common Floor still feeds and breathes; the Threadline still hums.

- Receipts shine. You can fake a proof once. After that you’re the cautionary story a ten‑year‑old tells at lunch.

What we kept and what we didn’t fix

We didn’t fix people. Misery found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned loud music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Hold tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel held “stay” too long and watched kind people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools, then wrote field guides so the next sect would bruise less.

But cruelty is expensive and kindness pays. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Exits are tickets, not rumors. Cultures above the personhood floor are defended; the floor is steel for all.

An ordinary oath

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular and silicon and saltwater—place palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We will divide hands on force.
We will chart what we share.
We will keep the way out.
We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Rim Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors lean. Radiators bloom like pale ferns. The Hold Grid hums, bored and content. The three studs above the hatch glow like small suns.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, slow down, speed up, split, rejoin, leave, and stay; enough to host many kinds of lives without letting anyone become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power. The Truss isn’t a crown. It’s the thing that keeps the roof from falling while we live.
