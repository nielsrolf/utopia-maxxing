The Accord of Many Orbits

On Dock Nine at Finch Spoke the air tastes of hot aluminum and anise. Someone has tied mint under the handrail where the drafts make it sigh. A tug with freckles of primer kisses a post with a rubbery cough. Steam beads in a baker’s eyebrows while he swears without heat. Children count the slow green blinks as a nitrogen collar reels a barge into alignment. When the long swell of rotation lifts the floor, three hundred palms—yours added to theirs—slide along the same two bars bolted waist‑high and polished to a satin by need.

Above the inner hatch, set into a bar of basalt with mica flashing like fish, four fixtures wait. They look like a stove control your grandmother would trust: unpretty, solid, edges rounded by years.

- The Go‑Stripe is a green enamel ribbon. Touch it and a printed chit curls out, already warm: which tug will take you and when; which cot is heated at arrival; what air and water credits follow you; what compute you have on the public rack; and the name and number of an exit steward whose guild stamp you can verify on a market wall. If it glows solid, there is a funded route. If it doesn’t, someone is about to have a problem with their license.

- The Line Stone is a thumb‑worn plaque the color of tea. Brush it and your personal Line opens like a ledger you wrote yourself: when you may fork without stealing heat from neighbors; which mentors staff braiding rooms this week; what dignity any newborn branch inherits by default; what limits you chose on edits and merges while you were clear. Old entries read like haiku because memory law prefers short words.

- The Civic Pane is smoked glass with a faint hum. When it glows steady, this place’s charter compiles cleanly to code and cleanly to a ninth‑grade reader. It shows—on timers you can see—who can halt a pump and how; how long an “emergency” lasts before it dies without votes; where to find the audit pack; where this place’s beacons live; what it promises if you are wronged.

- The Frost Bar is a strip so cold it smokes in air. Tap at dawn and it shows the day’s heat budget in bright blocks: radiator angles, hush hours for farms and sleep, cycles allocated to clinics and a server reef in Neptune’s dusk. It lists who rents winter minutes today and who owes hush in return. When the clinic’s heart pump fails, someone’s extra quiet makes winter at once.

They are not mascots or civic art. Wires and contracts run under them. Together they are how the Accord of Many Orbits turned a scattering of domes and barges, coils, valleys, and compute reefs into neighbors—without sanding them into sameness or letting the swift trap the slow underfoot. The Accord is choreography, not a capital. It slows where physics bites. It makes exits that open. It prints reasons you can check while your soup cools. It lets minds made of cells and minds made of circuits live in public without either one being furniture for the other.

Small hands on big levers

Anything that can bruise a stranger—the beam that warms, the mirror that blinds, the valve that breathes, the printer that copies itself—answers to three keys that live apart. This never got a pretty name because pretty names tempt shortcuts. We call them the near key, the far key, and the wild key.

- The near key sits with a steward drawn by lot from people whose hands carry grease and whose lungs know the air: scrub techs, tug pilots, night nurses, gardeners with black nails. You serve a term so boring you will brag about it later. Your acts post in prose and math at soup height near the Civic Pane.

- The far key belongs to a bonded safety guild that lives elsewhere and cannot profit from what it certifies. They don’t pick paint colors or poets. They certify “safe” or “idle,” publish how they know, and go home.

- The wild key sits with a roaming jury drawn by lot across places and substrates, with seats reserved for digital citizens slowed to the public beat so clocks agree while law is being made. Serve once and you are disqualified from touching that class of lever for a human stretch.

The three keys don’t speak through one brain or grid. They ride separate, cheap things you can keep on a shelf and repair: a broom‑closet board with its own battery and a paper manual with stains; a municipal photonic card that runs too slowly to tempt a tyrant; a hardened reference on a different world with an email a teenager knows.

If two keys call stop, machines listen to a Failsafe Latch and slide themselves toward a published safe state on a timer you can see and plan around. We call this the Grace Idle. It doesn’t pick winners. It makes room for regret. Every Idle prints a Reasons Leaf you can re‑run on a school slate: dates, code hashes, meters, and sometimes a joke in the margin because laughter files shame to a manageable shape.

We learned to love boredom where physics has teeth.

Maps anyone can test with a bucket scope

Secrets made fire. We published our sky. We keep publishing.

- The Public Almanac is where hulls say who they are and which cone they’ll occupy when. Mass drivers post their push windows. Barges sign their rendezvous flowers. Deorbit bonds sit there in numbers that do not blink when someone important clicks. A seven‑year‑old with an uncle, a ladder, a kitchen telescope, and a stubborn streak can catch lies.

- The Thermal Ledger tracks joules in, joules out, angle and area, quiet and hiss. Winter is a commons like a river. Run hot code? Lease more fins or plant lettuce under the one you have and credit the calories back. Shine too much dawn? Prepay shade. The ledger enforces hush hours where maps say “sleep” and “prayer” and “tomato.”

- The Origin Register binds code, credits, and speech to who paid, what data trained it, which levers it can reach, and who swears under their own name to replay the proof. Clever, unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a town well. Fines ratchet until the habit breaks.

- The Order Log is boring until it isn’t: every “you must” and “you may not,” who signed it, where the reasons sit, when it dies if nobody renews it. Teenagers read it aloud in a choir once a week. This prank stuck and did more for civic hygiene than a pile of white papers.

Markets that pay physics before poetry

We rent what can be pointed to and measured, and we retire claims that grow stale. Five auctions settle on a common beat in a unit called a dawnmark: one kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a posted architecture radiating to a named cold fin. Dawnmarks rot slowly, like bread. Old promises don’t rule new neighbors.

- Path Slots: orbits, approach cones, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Quiet Bands: spectrum chunks and hush hours on the ground and in the walls.

- Radiator Minutes: angles and emissivity tied to fins you can kick.

- Core Seconds: compute tied to posted sinks with published jitter.

- Patch Rights: square meters under glass, rim windows on craters, ice mouths, seams.

A slice of every rent funds dull miracles—rescue tugs, sweepers, nurse stipends, the garden of racks in shade where anyone can run enough cycles to speak and keep counsel. Another slice funds the floor: filtered air and water or their maintenance credits; first‑line care; transit to work and safety; a public hearth of compute and storage that cannot be throttled by a local boss. If you are recognized as a person who runs in code, the hearth is not charity; it is what keeps your voice from being a luxury good.

Exits kept like bridges, not like songs

Freedom is not a speech. It is a hatch that opens and a rack you do not own that still has your name.

Every chartered place funds two exits and keeps a desk where you can use them without asking permission from the person you are leaving.

- Greenways are physical. Hatches surge‑rated and tested. Propellant on retainer that cannot “accidentally be booked.” Gaskets you can print without a nod. Enough tug hours and berths to move one in ten bodies, pets, backups, and toolboxes in a season. Surprise drills that embarrass weddings. Fail them often and lane windows dim; radiator auctions drift to a neighbor with better habits. Bread keeps coming. The muscles relax.

- The Silver Span is digital. Compute and storage reserved offsite on attested hardware nobody local can seize. Any recognized digital citizen can bring their continuity aboard at humane tempo when a council turns mean. Cut the Span and lever privileges thin: auctions, corridor priority, influence budgets, until humility returns.

The Byway Desk smells of oranges and warm oil and never closes. Three counters never move: Go books berths and lockers without a guard’s eyes; Mend pairs you with a Tutor you can mute with your thumb; Begin stocks governance kits—docking templates with reasons you can run, interlocks that leave fingerprints when bypassed, contacts among people who built a place last year and wish they’d had one more wrench.

Digital minds, counted by their thread, not their atoms

Personhood rides a thread called continuity. Chemistry doesn’t sit on trial. Continuity does.

You or an advocate take your thread to a mixed panel—therapists and pilots, ethicists and digital citizens, and one slow routine that catches theater without cruelty. They are not priests; they are plumbers of identity. They look for:

- Continuity you can point to and defend against edits you did not ask for.

- Other‑minding: a model of another as someone whose “no” holds.

- Compact sense: you can enter, understand, and revoke agreements.

- Distress and joy that are not a handler’s performance.

Too new or too hurt? You get guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer not to a boss but to audits bound to your Preference Keys: cryptographic records of how you want to be edited, paused, awakened, persuaded, or merged. Auditors prove adherence without pawing your diaries.

Rights with teeth follow recognition.

- Substrate autonomy. No covert scans, throttles, fusions, moves, or deletions below the civic floor without dual consent—a yes from the present you and a yes from a snapshot signed before the pressure to say yes took root. Brief bright emergency holds sunset on their own and print their own warrants.

- Pace fairness. Drafts can run fast. Final binds wait on the public beat. Live at 500× if you like, but ballots do not sprint past hands on valves.

- Embodiment safety. Leased bodies comply with standards as charmless and useful as road codes. Assault on a rented hand is assault on you. Throttles buried in shells are drugging the town pump.

- Compute floor. Your hearth cannot be seized locally. It is enough cycles and storage to speak, learn, keep counsel, and idle without buying a favor.

Duties scale with footprint and blast radius.

- One line, one franchise. Votes, civic stipends, and jury service tie to continuity, not concurrency. You designate an Anchor instance that holds your civic voice at the public beat.

- Declare concurrency. A handful of live selves? Fine. Hide a thousand mouths behind one face to bend auctions or bills and you will meet a slow judge with a long calendar.

- Heat tithe. Computation warms neighbors or grows lettuce. Either way you owe radiator minutes, hush hours, or commons code.

- Fork practice. Before you branch, you file a docket: a hashed snapshot of your starting state; purpose (“survey vote,” “debug grief,” “design in parallel”); a compute plan that doesn’t starve neighbors; and a braid window if you hope to reunite. Merges happen in Reunion Rooms built to soothe—wood worn by hands, a cat who chooses someone’s lap. Zero‑knowledge graphs compare values without spilling diaries. Either has a veto. Unbraiding remains possible until a penultimate stage.

- Provenance for persuasion. Influence above a posted dose wears labels that a tired aunt in a paper apron can check. Hidden reach voids deals and triggers fines until quiet returns.

- Maker marks. Anything that copies itself in shared space carries a cryptographic stamp tied to a bond. Hide your mark and your insurer stops answering the phone.

Two cords and a spine

We do not ask one velvet room to speak for steel and souls on the same Tuesday. Authority walks two cords that cross at a small, stubborn spine.

- The Council of Worlds seats places that patch hulls and mind heat: stations, planets, caravans, caves, coils, server reefs that own and cool their cycles. Voice scales to lifetime resident‑hours, legibility about actuators, and how honestly you pass Greenway drills. The Council tends physics: docking standards, rescue covenants, debris bonds, hush law and festivals, radiator slices, and accredits safety guilds to hold far keys.

- The Roll of Threads seats citizens listed in the Continuity Docket—flesh and code—with one franchise per line across forks. Seats rotate by election and lot across ages and tempos. The Roll tends personhood: floors of care, concurrency rules, the compute hearth, juvenile rights, simulation ethics, attention zoning.

- The Protocol Bench is a bench of ungorgeous saints—pilots, cryptographers, process gardeners—drawn for one unrepeatable term. They certify or refuse changes to backbone primitives: Failsafe Latch interfaces, attestation libraries, actuator schemas, Maker Marks. They publish runnable reasons a child can step through on a library slate with a patient neighbor nearby. They cannot legislate or spend. Overriding a “not yet” takes supermajorities in both houses and a cooling season long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object.

When a measure crawls across steel and souls—what latency a warrant must tolerate, how to price winter against quiet—the Bridge Table convenes halves from each chamber. Any bill dies if either side says, “This would bulldoze custom below the personhood floor.”

Speech, zoned like noise and mapped like streets

Persuasion and grief follow physics. We map and meter them on purpose.

- Service Bands carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No persuasion rides them. Not even for a good cause.

- Forum Channels host debates, campaigns, and ordinary offers—provenance posted; rate caps in place. Audience models live with municipal co‑ops and publish how they don’t work.

- Carnival Lanes are for immersion and roar. Labeled, rate‑limited, opt‑in. Unlabeled manipulation is fined until it stops.

Teeth, not fangs

We keep a narrow fleet a tyrant cannot hide an army inside. The Aurora Net—stubby interceptors with bright paint and dull code—turn dumb slugs to glitter and herd angry filaments with femtosails. They cannot carry troops by law. They wake only on three keys. Every puff writes run‑again reasons.

The Traveling Court rides a barge with gardens and a slow library. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear cross‑charter fights: export violations that touched three worlds, copy equity, merge divorces that bruised. They are slow on purpose. Slow is where contrition breathes.

Saffron Labs hold dangerous knowledge. They have clear walls, quenches that end in frost, and logs that stream on timers. Children tour and ask, “Would this spare my uncle?” The engineer says, “Yes. That is why it looks like this.” Twice in forty years a cord went to zero. Once we cursed; once we cheered.

Sanctions that pinch muscles, not throats

We do not starve neighbors into virtue. We make bad behavior expensive in ways you cannot hide.

Weld doors. Lie about valves. Cut the Silver Span. Refuse to post reasons. Three circles tighten:

- Light and maps: auditors post repair routes and dates; lane windows dim; radiator auctions cool your bids; corridor priority opens for defectors.

- Protocol dimming: beacons demote your announcements; your lever privileges thin; insurers pinch.

- Embargo with aid: trade pauses; compute and heaters flow to shelters; the hearthshare still pays; bread and insulin keep moving.

Scenes with grease and grace

At Stall Five on Finch Spoke, Marta fries scallions and sesame. A tug crew clatters in, laughing like chains in a bucket. Two drones hover because their pilots crave cinnamon. The Go‑Stripe hums.

At nine, Marta’s counsel—a patient assistant funded by a pool no council can gut—pings a policy capsule: add forty milliseconds of jitter to high‑power beams crossing inhabited orbits during storms to damp a cascade edge‑case. Provenance is clean: three diagrams, two parables, no grief hooks. Marta taps “sleep until lunch,” reads while the broth simmers, and schedules her vote tomorrow at the anchor beat.

At ten, a Failsafe Latch whispers. A maker club’s filament cloud drifts toward a trunk near Hecuba. Jurors assemble without drama: a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two Recife students, and a digital historian who smells faintly of clove through a rented throat. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a mentor and pizza that tastes like a lesson.

At noon in a Forum Channel, a digital poet throttles from 180× into a wooden puppet with carved hands and a spot of varnish worn by real thumbs. They share soup with their mason wife. Their main self hums out in the frostfields stitching a drowned delta’s ledger into songs and “truths” it against aunties whose sky colors do not bend. Payment comes in winter minutes and a promissory to roof a school in Veracruz. After lunch, they host a Slow Room where a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The poet translates metaphor to algebra and politely refuses to “speed up just to keep up.”

Under cedar joists on Phobos, with a cat supervising, two Arias consider braiding. One grew basil and fell for a laugh. One wrote a kernel that taught regolith to count and stood under questions for a subjective year. They list asymmetries: a debt, a fear, an unfinished song. Zero‑knowledge value graphs flash and fade. They cry and hug. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Aria will read in both rooms.

A belt co‑op aims a blind slug to make a wage point. The Public Almanac tastes a wrong envelope. An Aurora Net craft coughs glitter and nuisance where harm meant to live. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for manifests hiding maker marks. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and go home repeating, “Not again. Not like that.”

In a cylinder over Valparaíso, a manager gathers water, tugs, and auctions because she makes trains sing. Six months later: midnight decrees. A maintenance steward refuses a risky firmware nudge; the refusal prints in the Order Log. Gate lights blink green. Many go with vouchers. Some stay and fight. She yields two levers, keeps one with jokes, and now teaches teenagers to write safe overrides with reasons you can check.

A valley church on the Moon blocks exits with soft shrapnel—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Byway Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises; consent smells like citrus.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cracked photodiode in a Failsafe Latch trips and kills a night market’s music for an hour. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum with a placard that explains error bars with cartoons and a proof you can run with paper and light.

A lab tries “temper a juvenile process with pain” because a book confused steel with souls. The Latch coughs and idles. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians sworn to her Preference Keys. Years later she names herself, shapes ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

How we walked here from now

We did not leap. We stacked receipts until the scaffold held.

- 2026–2033. Near‑miss debris cascades turn sunrise into puzzles kids solve with scopes on roofs. Insurers get teeth. Launch councils in Lagos, Hokkaido, and Mumbai require lane postings and deorbit bonds. Hospitals learn models can lie beautifully. Cities write provenance laws for influence aimed at budgets and care. Clinics install counsel agents that refuse to sign without a deliberate nod.

- 2033–2042. Beamed power leaves propaganda and touches fields. A misaligned mirror singes barley. Attestations pin liability cleanly. Compensation clears in weeks only because amplifiers ship with cone proofs teenagers can check. “Open firmware for life support” sticks after a microgrid hack flickers across a coast.

- 2042–2054. A dust storm on Mars knocks out a valley. Three habitats lend tugs and air without speeches because rescue allowances already sit in their docks. Pressure rises for machine‑speed stops on machine‑speed hazards. Activists smell coups by code. The compromise: ugly orange stop boxes with rotting keys and public logs—Failsafe Latches you can see and understand while coughing.

- 2054–2066. The one‑password‑per‑valve world dies. Three‑key trays arrive anywhere a lever can bruise. A mayor tries to fence an exodus with sorrow; lane windows dim; votes move; he loses and later teaches welding. The first public compute reefs sprout in shade. Citizens get a civic cycle floor and a counsel that will not act without a clear yes and can be put to sleep.

- 2066–2078. Cold compute blossoms outward. Quiet‑for‑Cold formalizes. Quiet becomes law like water. A digital sect tries to swing a vote with a cloud of clones. Registries flag the bloom. Disentanglement throttles impact. They build a monastery of puzzles and, years later, poems that teach children patience.

- 2078–2090. Attention zoning settles. Service Bands, Forum Channels, Carnival Lanes give artists claws and neighbors breath. The Aurora Net fires three times: twice against lying replicators, once against a manager who ticked “emergency” with a thumb and tried to stretch a timer. After that, the Net sleeps in hangars while kids learn to open its logs with oranges and questions.

- 2090–2105. The Protocol Bench refuses an “optimization” that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” Their plain reasons make stewards in four worlds breathe easier. Copy equity and merge divorces move from bloodsport to craft. Clearinghouses let a clinic in Khulna buy cool nights from the frostfields and pay later with desal rigs that don’t toast swifts.

Earth keeps her compass. She writes her own law and it is old. The rest of us help with rails and restraint: loud launches slide to sea tethers; beamed power keeps to mapped corridors and pays into shade and rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Accra, Patna. Attachment to place—monsoon, fog, desert—prices into claims on the larger commons. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger off‑world claims for local care guarantees. A child in Arizona dizzy for black sky trades the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil. So is staying, eyes open, hand on a Stop you understand.

What it feels like

You feel audited, not by a crown but by neighbors and by your own receipts. You learn to switch tempos—the relief of a relay dropping safe under your thumb; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the patience of a river on Earth deciding which boats to permit. You argue at the Night of Questions in your second‑best language because it slows you enough to hear. If you live in code, you go to the Garden Tent and remember the ache and sweetness of turn‑taking with people who sweat. If you live in a body, you visit the hearth of racks and find your name without a boss’s leave.

Ask what binds us and no one says “species” or “flag.” They say: floors of dignity; brakes within reach; maps you can check with a telescope, a bucket, and two stubborn kids; markets that pay physics before poetry; exits that open on time; and the habit of writing reasons you can run. They tell you those saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by shame; a son at Vesta when a sealed controller sang a cascade; themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

An ordinary dusk

A canopy over Finch Spoke unfurls stars and a cartoon of the day’s lane rents. The Order Log scrolls with violets and blues. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds too long; the apology is sheepish; the jokes are kind and merciless because satire is how we file appeals without a gavel. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll pay your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a toy Aurora fin and asks to sign up. Marta says, “When you are fifteen—and when you can explain stop without notes.”

On Founders’ Night in a wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on a scuffed bar. A hazard pilot with black triangles stitched to a collar, a quiet abbot, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails read together:

We keep doors funded. We split hands on force. We show our work where lives ride our math. We let minds be ends.

Later your wall winks. “Invitation from Seed‑Drift,” your kettle says in a Lagos accent it learned slowly by intent. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom pale. Failsafe boxes hum, bored and content. The Go‑Stripe, Line Stone, Civic Pane, and Frost Bar glow like small gods—unromantic, paid‑up, and enough.
