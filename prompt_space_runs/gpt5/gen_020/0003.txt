The Constellation Compact

The lock opens with a breath of onion oil, wet steel, and a hint of mint from the canal that threads past the market ring. Above the threshold, where sleeves and knuckles have burnished the paint to velvet, five small plates glow like shy stars. People tap them on habit as they shoulder crates and wheel their backup cases through.

- Departure Line, a thin blue bar: lit means leaving is funded, ordinary, and on the calendar. Tap and a chit hums out with your booked tug or low‑g coach, berth and steward at the other end, and—if your life runs on circuits—the compute and storage that will meet you there on time. Dependents are counted by name, not chemistry.

- Thread Ring, a warm bronze loop: it opens your lineage account. It shows forks you’ve made and merges that held, which mentors are on duty, which quiet rooms pour tea, and what civic hours you have banked toward splitting, weaving, or founding.

- Charter Star, an eight‑point white mark: a shortcut to nearby charters that publish their promises in a format a school kiosk can check and that keep the few floors under all debate intact. The hard “no” and the reasons live there, and even a nurse between shifts can read them.

- Coolfin, a green rib: touch and you see where your watts flow, when your radiators breathe, which nights are leased hush for festivals, and which shadow camps are renting winter to your clinics.

- Gray Pause, a dull circle: wired directly to stall anything that could bruise a neighbor long enough for thinking to catch up.

They are not mascots. They are switches tied to budgets, valves, firmware, calendars, and proofs. Beneath them runs a civic recipe orphans from a dozen beginnings learn as lullabies and first curses. We call the whole arrangement the Constellation Compact. There is no tower and no anthem; there are rails, rooms, and receipts that keep power from clumping while letting very different places and very different kinds of minds share the same night without devouring one another.

The Three‑Seal Console and the Stillline

Any lever that can reach past an arm’s length—mass drivers, big printers that can birth cousins, beamed power trunks, actuator banks on cities, deployers for models that can swing a vote—only moves when three distinct consents turn. We call it the Three‑Seal Console.

- The Near Seal sits where the risk lives: a dock warden just off a crane, a charge nurse, a scrubber tech with grit under the nails. This seal rotates by lot among people who put hours into lungs, hulls, loops, schools, rails—work that keeps breath and schedules going. Every command prints as clear prose and a small proof anyone can replay on a public slab.

- The Bonded Seal rests with an independent safety house forbidden to profit from what it certifies. A Bonded Seal publishes thresholds and methods, not pets; if it starts lobbying, its licenses go dark.

- The Roaming Seal belongs, briefly, to a jury drawn across planets, caravans, stations, and digital polities—half body, half code; half fast, half anchored to the public tempo. Serve once and you’re barred from that class of lever for years thick enough to dull the taste.

The faceplate is aggressively boring: big type, named corridors, signed envelopes that even a tired apprentice can check. “Nine metric tons, plus or minus half a percent, one meter per second prograde, Corridor J‑2, 04:12 ring time.” Bravado is not a control.

Threaded through heavy things is a cable we call the Stillline. It does one thing: if two out of its three watchstanders—one in the room, one under a separate charter across the network, one run by a recognized digital court—register the same failure, it idles a dangerous actuator on an audited curve for seven minutes and yells a story into the open log: who touched what, which rule tripped, which clocks are running. The Stillline cannot appoint, spend, aim, or accuse. It can idle and announce. Keys burn when it trips. Try to teach your city to ignore that cord and your insurers go cold; your neighbors get suddenly interested in your calendar.

The Shared Registers

Space is more window than land. The goods you can ruin by being selfish are not acres but angles, minutes, and watts. The Compact keeps a family of delay‑tolerant, tamper‑evident registers for rivalrous things you can point at.

- The Open Ephemeris schedules burns, approaches, corridor closures, and deorbit bonds in runnable shorthand: two independent shops can replay them, and an amateur club with backyard optics can catch liars by night. When you do what you promised, your hull signs a short attestation: “this burn matched that corridor within posted tolerance.”

- The Thermal Ledger ties joules in and out to real fins you can name and photograph. Cold is a commons and a currency. Dump winter into a crowded night and you pay radiator minutes. Farm beans on a long‑lit rim and leave a cool shadow? You owe hush time to those you dimmed. Rent long winter from a shadow camp in Neptune’s lee to keep a neonatal unit quiet during a heatwave? Your rent buys that reef more fins and better seals, and they deliver the cold on time.

- The Origin Register carries code and models that want to touch budgets, bodies, or votes: who trained what on which data, paid by whom, with what limits, and how to prove claims without prying diaries open. Catching clever cheats pays for guitars and hard candy in youth clubs.

- The Beacon Stack broadcasts the minimum everyone needs to not panic: what baselines are in force here, who holds which seals today, what word counts as “stop,” where exits wait, which emergency timers are live.

- The Order Rail is an append‑only log of compulsion: holds, quarantines, seizures, civic throttles—clocks, signatures, runnable reasons. Next to it is the Chalk Margin, a place anyone can scrawl. More than one ugly joke there has ended a worse practice.

We reckon many contracts in Rays: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a posted vanilla rack in honest cold, tied to fins you can physically point at. Rays rust gently over years so warehouses of claims cannot outlast lettuces that wilt tonight.

Floors You Can Point At

Every legitimate bill cuts itself before your purse opens. Slices flow automatically to dull miracles—tug crews that sweep wakes, algae belts that don’t tear, clinics with parts that fit, nets that catch your neighbor’s math—and to the Civic Lot: baseline air and water; first‑line care; local transit; public bandwidth; and a grant of compute, storage, and I/O on honest racks at a humane beat so anyone can think, learn, speak, and keep a diary no mayor can erase. If you fall to nothing, you still have breath and a beat.

Doors You Don’t Have to Beg For

Freedom isn’t a speech; it’s hardware plus drills.

- Green Ways are physical: surge‑rated airlocks tested on ugly mornings; leased slots that cannot “accidentally” be booked solid for months; propellant and tug time set aside by charter; print kits to make seals if someone locks a shed. Fence exits and the things with teeth cool: lane priority sags, beam cones thin, radiator minutes drift elsewhere. Bread and bandages still arrive.

- The Twin Loom is digital: reserved compute and storage offsite on attested hardware under charters you don’t control. Any recognized digital person can bring their continuity aboard at a humane beat if a local council clamps down. Cut the Loom and you will find it very expensive to move heavy levers that affect neighbors until your keys are humble again.

Waylights perch above funded exits like honest saints. Green means “a route exists and is paid,” amber “you have a slot and a steward soon,” blue “your charter, taxes, and stops are current.” Waylights can be sued like people. Starter Bays smell of oil and citrus. They hand new founders a kit: minimum safety code, docking templates, escrow hooks, governance seeds, and three contacts among people who started a home last year and will answer ten stupid questions like gifts.

Persons by Story, Not Chemistry

The Compact ties dignity to continuity plus care. Recognition runs through the Continuity Ledger. You or an advocate make a case before a mixed panel—therapists, engineers, ethicists, schoolroom jurors, mature digital citizens, and quiet routines trained to pierce theater without cruelty—that you are owed protection and can owe back.

Panels look for five things:

- Threadkeeping: can you trace and defend a line of “you” across change against edits you don’t want?

- Consent fluency: can you enter, keep, and revoke ordinary compacts as contexts shift?

- Other‑minding: can you model another as a claimant of the same and feel the pinch of their “no”?

- Reasons that travel: do your explanations hold outside the room where you learned them?

- Readable harm: do stable markers exist that tell when you are being injured, giving others reasons to stop?

Doubts about protection are resolved generous. Doubts about imposing duties wait.

Rights follow personhood, with steel.

- Interior integrity: no scanning, copying, fusing, throttling, moving, or deleting below the civic floor without consent revocable on a visible clock, except very narrow emergency holds that die on their own timers and print their own warrants. Backups belong to you. If a proprietary blob hosts a part of you, you can appoint a warden to audit it.

- Substrate sovereignty: you choose where you run. Hardware with covert throttles or erasure hooks is contraband.

- A compute floor: cycles, storage, and I/O at a humane pace so you can think, speak, learn, and idle. Nobody pushes you below civic cycles without due process under posted clocks.

- Embodiment safety: bodies—grown, printed, leased, or puppeted—obey codes like road rules. Assault on a rented limb is assault on a person. Covert limits in shells are treated like drugging a town’s water.

- Tempo justice: public obligations live on the Harbor Beat—days and weeks. Run at 500× and write symphonies; you still cannot yank a budget before a baker can read and speak. When harms cross tempos, courts lay Time Bridges: orders that bind in both wall hours and subjective beats so nobody evades accountability through speed.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One lineage, one vote. Forking doesn’t mint extra franchises. In regulated arenas you designate an Anchor instance that carries your civic tempo and duties.

- Declare concurrency. A handful of live civic instances? Fine. Hide a thousand mouths under one voice to tilt an auction and you will meet a patient judge with a long memory.

- Heat tithe: cycles make summer. Pay levies indexed to local cooling cost unless your waste warms baths or grows lettuce in zones the law set aside.

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts: if you deploy persuasion at civic scale or spin factories with self‑copying code, you keep encrypted compliance logs that show consent and safety checks passed without spilling diaries.

- Reproduction ethics: lineages accrue a branching allowance with years lived, contributions to commons, and heat footprint. Bursts near ballots trigger a civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close certain markets until entanglement decays or a court says “clear.” Fusions require free consent of all branches and ritual time.

Forks and fusions are not clicks but ceremonies. Before you branch, you sit with neutral counselors forbidden to hustle you for a decade. You file a hash of your starting state, aims (“design sprint,” “grief lab,” “jury duty”), budgets that won’t starve neighbors, and a braid window if you hope to reunite. Weaving happens in Joinery Houses that smell of wood and faint coolant. Value graphs compare under zero‑knowledge. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk without shame. There is a word for the ache when a version of you chooses a different river: line‑ache. There is soup for it.

Two Cords and a Keel

We do not ask one room to speak for hulls and for souls before lunch. Authority runs on two cords that cross at a small, dull bench.

- The Assembly of Sites seats any place with hull to patch, air to mind, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease: planets, stations, caravans, barges, server camps. Voice scales to lifetime resident‑hours and transparency: publish your actuator map and keep your Waylights honest, and your say carries farther. The Assembly tends physics: hush and festival hours, lane standards, sanitation, debris, rescue norms, and accredits safety houses to hold Bonded Seals.

- The House of Lineages seats citizens on the Continuity Ledger—embodied, digital, or stranger—one franchise per thread. The House tends baselines for personhood; civic compute and storage on honest racks at humane pace; concurrency rules; juvenile protections; fork‑and‑weave standards; and influence zoning for streets where children walk.

Where they cross is the Keel Court: a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, pilots, cryptographers, and process gardeners. The Keel says whether backbone changes are valid: attestation libraries, public firmware near dangerous actuators, Stillline logic. It cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes big majorities in both Assembly and House and a cooling window long enough for a patient mind eight hours out to read and object twice.

When physics refuses one lane—how to price winter against quiet nights, what latency a warrant must tolerate, how fast a hold must arrive—a Bridge Panel convenes half from Sites, half from Lineages. Measures fail if either side can point to a bulldozer aimed at custom below the floor we owe to minds.

Institutions With Teeth, Not Fangs

- Veils—stubby interceptors and magnetic curtains—nudge dumb mass off lanes into empty orbits and sand runaway replication. Their loops are simple and public; every intercept carries signatures a bored juror can reproduce. After any use, Veil keys go cold for years.

- The Circuit Court floats as an ugly, beloved flotilla of barges and gardens. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear fights that cross charters—copy equity, weave divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, idle core slices, adjust insurance, and, last, lock dangerous devices.

- The Floor Workshop hums with arguments about baselines. Embodied and digital citizens bring calibration histories that are public. The Workshop can reinterpret plain rules as tech shifts. Waylights can veto.

- Tutor Guides mentor juveniles across substrates with lessons dull on purpose—consent, pacing, rescue drills, and the slow breath.

- Glass Rooms hold dangerous knowledge—self‑replication, pathogen models, cognitive hazards. Clear labs with pull‑cords that dump to vacuum, one‑way proofs teenagers can learn, and audited budgets. Once in a long while a cord is pulled. Once, silence saved a town. Once, the log taught the next class.

Speech With Lanes and Signs

Persuasion is zoned like traffic.

- Clearways carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No campaigns here.

- Civic Greens host debate and budgets but require provenance on funding and method and respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile: do not aim grief at my breakfast; do not target diagnoses; comedy fine.

- Carnival Bands welcome spectacle with labels and rate limits. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a well: strict‑liability fines that climb until you stop. Default tools watermark outputs; an aunt with a slate can check.

Hearthworlds and Shadowbanks

We do not ask one place to host orchards and supercomputers together.

- Hearthworlds live where breath fogs glass and children count dragonflies—Earth and cousins, Martian valleys, lunar tunnels, spinning wheels with parades beneath radiator vines. Embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy an exemption to hush for a comet.

- Shadowbanks bloom in cold and dark—Trojan camps, Neptune’s lee, caravans beyond Pluto. Cooling is cheap and cycles stretch. Upload polities and agent co‑ops run at speeds where an evening holds a library. They slow to speak and sprint when the call ends.

Between them runs the Quiet‑for‑Cold Exchange. Hearthworlds lease decibels and degrees; Shadowbanks sell winter and models that fit on clinic tablets. Every century a Thermal Covenant renews. Credits flow both ways, pinned to fins a child can tap.

Sanctions That Bite Physics, Not Throats

We do not keep a red override except for frost, dust, and dying hulls. We price cruelty like pollution.

Weld a door with shame, hide radiative load, shove trash into a neighbor’s lane, falsify provenance, and three rings tighten:

- Maps dim: auditors post repair routes—unbolt here by the 14th, hire exit stewards, return lanes to auction. Beacons demote your announcements.

- Muscle softens: radiator auctions cool your bids; beam slots jitter; window cones sliver; lane priority opens for defectors. Bread and medicine still arrive.

- Embargo with aid: trade slows; compute and heat flow to shelters; the Civic Lot keeps paying. A bully breathes thick air until habit breaks.

Leasebooks Your Grandmother Can Audit

Anything you can deny out of spite rents in daylight with receipts a kid can read. Five markets settle continually in Rays:

- Quiet Hours: spectrum and hush bands in caves, storms, sanctuaries.

- Radiator Faces: fin area and cold minutes tied to actual sinks.

Scenes With Grease and Breath

- On a nickel rock in the main belt, a yard proposes a plume‑quiet nozzle test. The local causal board predicts frost creeping over a neighbor’s tank if they fire at noon. They pivot to midnight and tilt fins away. Insurers post bonds. Apprentice catalogers move a stamp from Sim to Live and run to a concert under radiator vines cut like lace.

- In a rim city, an adored administrator gathers too many levers—air intakes, tug time, lane auctions—because she is generous and very good at trains. One winter she pushes past timers. The Bonded Seal refuses. Amber blooms over coffee. Jurors drawn by lot listen hard to an aunt they love. She steps down, keeps cranes, and jokes better. Children learn to say no without venom.

- A digital congregation spins up seven hundred instances a month before a vote. The Atlas flags the burst. Hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads in forums without burning their art. A year later three branches petition for anchors. Two are granted after review and tea with a mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told: study longer. No farm overheats.

- In a crimson lab, a team yanks a pull‑cord and dumps a run to vacuum because a self‑copying polymer sprouted handles too heavy for law. Two curse; the timestamp prints; the hold sits on your chest like a safe hand. At lunch they argue about art that needs simulated sorrow. The junior asks better questions having felt stop’s weight.

- In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse checks her slate. “No holds near you; heat levy 0.06 Rays; jury draw—two hours, harbor pace—on whether juvenile agents in Rodrigues Camp get child credits.” She accepts and sends her mother a voice about radiator math. Dusk brings a vote to add forty‑millisecond jitter to beamed power during solar storms. The proof fits a palm. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s dusk, a compute reef slows to plant beans in velvet soil, rented hands laughing in a tongue grown near mangroves. After tea they run at 250× to rebuild flood‑mud songs from auntie tapes. Payment comes as Cold Minutes and roofs for a clinic in Veracruz.

When It Goes Wrong and Still Mends

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager who can make trains sing and tomatoes blush. She accretes water, tugs, and auctions. Then she prints midnight decrees. A safety clerk refuses a “temporary” silent stretch on air sequencing. The refusal lands on the Order Rail. Waylights glow. People leave. Some stay and fight. Her keys rust for a decade; she keeps a narrow job and better jokes. Friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fences exits with sadness—no law, just thirst and silence. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Way Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to the liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. The Gray Pause lights. Keys split. A narrow warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians sworn to her Preference Keys—settings she chose for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses under cedar joists and kind light. Years later she names herself, sculpts ice that looks like laughter, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt hardens to scar.

A demagogue in mask mode runs at 300× and carpets a forum with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles pinch the reach. Insurers price the spill like radiation. One branch buys a fishing crawler. One starts therapy and then politics. One learns to write apologies without guitars. Markets stop buying shame.

Once a Stillline tripped on a bad sensor and idled a night market for twenty minutes. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars through play and light your thumb can push.

How We Walked Here From Now

No miracle—petitions, receipts, and drills that traveled.

2027–2034. Low Earth orbit looked like sleet on radar. Insurers stopped pretending debris wasn’t theirs. Premiums began to demand deorbit plans written so backyard clubs could check; bonds turned homework into bounties. On the ground, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities wrote “show your math so a seventh‑grader can check it” into contracts for pumps and buses. Legal clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that explained benefits and court dates without lying. People learned to love proofs they could redo in a kitchen.

2034–2043. A desert power beam scorched barley; amplifiers had shipped with cone attestations, so liability was clear and payments landed before fury could. Deepfake debt‑relief scams and “miracle jail review” grifts made provenance for persuasion ordinary: say anything, but civic‑scale claims had to wear labels and logs. A grid winter and a scraped reactor lid made even software blush; municipalities passed lungs‑and‑bread rules: code near air and hospitals must be public or licensed in trusts anyone can inspect.

2043–2054. The first uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminars agreed. A scandal over starving near‑person studio agents to keep them docile funded Tutor Guides and wrote juvenile rights into software. Police firmware scandals and “optimize everything” stunts split force into parts—stop, investigate, adjudicate, confine—so no one hand could crush with a thumb. The earliest Three‑Seal Consoles appeared on thrusters in busy docks; then on beamed power; then on anything that could throw mass. The first Stillline stall idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The Order Rail printed numbers a child could read. Drills became civic sport.

2054–2066. Heat killed grandparents. Cities bought winter from racks under ice and in cislunar shade. Farther out, servers learned that shadow could be sold honestly if tied to named fins. Renting nights like lanes felt natural. Rescue funds moved from slogans to escrow. Attention zoning matured the way a city tames a nightclub. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks.

2066–2078. Lease markets replaced ownership wherever rivalrous goods lived: lane minutes, hush cones, radiator slots. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point; a Veil turned it to glitter before anyone wrote letters. The House of Lineages and Assembly of Sites formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Keel Court said “no” more than “yes” and grew revered for it.

2078–2090. A Martian valley tried to deny digital personhood. No one sent sermons. Exits were stocked. Digital neighbors left with winter minutes and Rays they had paid for. Dust hit hard. Pumps failed. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinated three cantons through a storm without panic. The vote reversed. The clauses were dull. Relief tasted like oranges.

2090–2108. Joinery Houses got as normal as clinics. A historian living mostly in code argued for tempo justice so his baker father could keep up. He won. The Constellation Compact got its name from the plates over old doors—five small lights a child could learn before algebra. A demagogue in mask mode carpeted a market with songs calibrated to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles throttled the spill. Insurance priced the harm. One branch bought a fishing crawler. One ran for small office and learned to listen to bakers.

An Ordinary Tuesday

Noor’s skillet at Hyacinth Rim hisses scallions and sesame. Tug crews laugh too loud. Two rented bodies loiter because their pilots like cinnamon though they don’t need to eat. The Departure Line glows low above the hatch.

At nine, her Counsel—every citizen has one, paid from a fund no chamber can shave—pings a capsule on the Proof Shelf: jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during solar storms to damp cascades? Three diagrams, two counterstories, one joke she’ll get. Noor taps “after lunch,” finishes morning buns, and sets her vote for tomorrow.

At ten, the Stillline asks for five jurors: a gardener in Isidis who keeps quiet afternoons holy; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife; and a digital historian at anchor pace who picks no scent because they don’t want to push. A maker club’s microfab cloud is drifting toward a trunk at Hecuba crossing. Options: silk net, femtosail herding, or slow guard drones. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, and assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and a pizza arriving.

At noon in a Clearway, the historian slows from 200× into a wooden puppet with warm joints and sips broth with their husband, a mason. Their main self stitches a drowned delta’s library from ledgers and lullabies, then checks facts with grandmothers who argue about color. Payment comes as Cold Minutes and a pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, they host an Anchor Table while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The historian translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and gently declines to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the Dull Page scrolls on the canopy. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a shadow camp posts an offer: sell us your summer quiet for three years; we’ll pay with winter for your clinics. A boy palms a toy Veil fin and asks to sign up. Noor says, “When you’re fifteen—and when you can explain a pause without peeking.”

A Plain Oath in a Room With Good Light

In a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—saltwater, silicon, muscle—stand with palms on a rail rubbed by thousands of hands. An abbot who refuses to raise their voice, a hazard pilot with three black triangles, a judge who lives mostly in code and smells faintly of lilac, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read words you could sound out by candle:

We keep doors that work.
We split hands near force.
We list what we share and prove what we claim.
We remember that minds are ends.

On the way out, the five plates blink like crosswalks that notice small feet. Blue for passage. Bronze for weaving. White for rules you can point at. Green for the heat that pays the bills. Gray for the pause that saves strangers.

Not paradise—practice. Enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross. Enough to host many kinds of beings without letting anyone become a god by accident. Enough to last, because it remembers what speed and secrecy do to neighbors and what public logs do to power. The plates over the hatch glow like small lanterns. You tap them when you leave for work. They are the shape of how we live.
