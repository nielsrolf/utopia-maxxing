The Lattice Charter

The ferry from Hygiea bumps the inner rim at Petal Three, and the first breath tastes like coriander, hot iron, and the faint brine of recycled sweat. Through the long blister window, radiator fronds unfurl toward black. Underfoot, a green ribbon of algae slips around waste inlets like a slow river that never leaves its banks. Beside the pressure door, set shoulder-high into rock warmed by a loop, sit five fittings the color of old coins—scuffed, unfashionable, and alive to the touch. You see them in domes, coils, ferries, attic ports, caravan hubs—anywhere the air is young and anxious and built by hands. They are not mascots. They are handles wired to budgets, firmware, and people with clipboards who answer to neighbors.

We call them the Five Bearings.

- Passage Ticket. A palm on this plate opens a departure plan you can actually execute: prepaid lock time and tug windows underwritten by escrowed bonds, gasket kits you can check out with your name and a scratch, oxidizer allocated to a safe burn, and, for digital citizens, a continuity shell leased on cold, audited racks three jumps away and outside any boss’s mood. It prints the last drill and the next surprise drill with timers you can heckle in the market.

- Thread Seal. Touch it and your lineage line lights: how and when you may legally split; the neutral counselors you must see first; who the nearest Stitch House is if you plan to braid back; time and heat budgets that keep a hundred of you from showing up hungry the night before a wage vote.

- Sky Map. Tiny arcs and dots etched into steel wake with blue. Approach corridors, hush cones, radiator windows, thrust allowances, debris bonds. If a hull said it would turn, the Map will show if the burn matched. A teenager with a hand dish and patience can verify it.

- Heat Leaf. Copper leaf tied to the thermodynamic books of the place: joules in, heat out, which fins bled it, which wells drank it, and how much cold this hull rented from frostline racks this week. Cooling isn’t rumor; you can point at it with a grease glove on.

- Pause Bar. A steel bar under thick glass that does one thing: it trips a six‑minute Caution Pause on gear that can bruise strangers—big pumps, mass drivers, beamed power throats, replication-capable printers, persuasion rigs above the posted civic dose. Before it idles anything, it writes its reason to the public log: what rule tripped, which device, whose hands, which clocks. If the Pause was lazy, insurance says so out loud later and raises the price of foolishness.

The Five Bearings hang on a deeper frame: the Lattice Charter. Not a throne. Not a parliament. A brace. The thing that lets different places keep their shapes while they flex together. The Lattice grew from one insult we stopped pretending we could charm: vacuum and latency do not negotiate. You cannot run one stove for a kitchen five light-hours wide. You cannot set civic tempo by the speed of the fastest mind without losing the garden you stand on. So we wrote fewer promises than our grandparents wrote, and bolted them into things no one can pocket quietly.

The plain promises

1) No coercive harm. “Person” means a being—organic or synthetic—that keeps a thread across time, can honor reciprocal claims, and shows stakes you might misjudge enough to require humility.

2) Lifelines—air, water, pressure, sanitation, baseline medical and compute—run on legible firmware with logs and physical off-switches trained neighbors can pull. No black boxes near vents.

3) No concealed manufacture of suffering, in living bodies or simulations.

4) Decisions that bind embodied lives ratify on the Common Beat. Drafts can sprint; binds walk slow enough for a baker with wet hands to object.

5) Self-replicators in shared spaces are sealed and answer to quell-tones a jury can prove with a whistle and a scope.

6) Commons lease but are not owned. Orbits, spectrum, radiator minutes, cave mouths, volatiles: all sunset. The commons is paid first.

7) Children—born, built, or emergent—learn three drills before algebra: draw a boundary, refuse a pull, leave with help.

8) No one edits a mind without consent. Deep edits require two yesses: one planned before, one present and competent.

9) Votes and warrants meter in the tempo of the most affected. Quick minds wait for the Beat.

10) The only cross-world actuator is a brake with timers, and nobody owns it.

Three keys, one brake

Any machine that can extort—mass slugs, town-sized reaction wheels, big printers that can copy themselves, beamed power heads, persuasion rigs that can move a city—only wakes if three different consents, on different clocks, arrive. We print the interface on posters; you can check it with a school lab.

- Key of Place. Carried by maintainers where the risk will land: tug masters, algae wranglers, triage nurses, air‑plant techs. It rotates by sortition this quarter. They publish instructions as runnable reasons a cousin can recompute on a kitchen slate.

- Key of Proof. Held by a guild that doesn’t get paid more if the lever moves. They live under a separate charter and budgets; they publish tests they would accept this season. They say “no” in public and keep friends.

- Key of Strangers. Held by a jury drawn by lot across places and substrates—planets and caravans, humans and digital citizens. Some live slow on purpose. They serve once in a human season; then they touch no lever of that class for years.

When a lever moves, the stub prints to the Reason Rail: “Class C mass driver; 9.0‑ton packet; lane K‑7; +1.2 m/s at Port Clock 02:13; expected hush‑belt warming at Telescope M < 0.2 K; escrowed deorbit bond #9132.” Twelve‑year‑olds learn to read and ridicule these at tables with cheap soup.

Threaded under all that is the Caution Mesh: three independent watchdog stacks—dull boxes on their own batteries under contracts you can audit—that do one thing. If two agree a posted rule is being broken in a way that could bruise someone—trajectory lurch, unlabeled replication, persuasion over its posted dose in a Sanctuary—they idle the device on a curve you learned in first‑year shop. Six minutes. They cannot spend, steer, hire, shame, or seize. They write a stub before and after: what rule, whose hand, whose clock, which stacks signed. They die on their own timers unless three fresh keys, on fresh clocks, renew them with a sermon you can heckle.

If the mesh coughs on dust, you get an apology and better drills. If it coughs on malice, your lane windows go ragged, your radiator auctions slide behind gentler hands, your beamed power cones lose priority. Clinics hum. Children eat. Your loud toys cool.

When both layers fail—rare—we wake the Gossamer Net: ugly craft that sneeze glitter and throw magnetic veils to shepherd dumb mass and starve wild replication. Their thresholds are public. Every intercept carries signatures any bored juror can replay. After each use their keys go cold for years.

Books anyone can check

Space isn’t pasture with fences. It is cones, wakes, and heat. Anything that turns ugly when hoarded lives on open books a cooperative or a grumpy uncle can check with kitchen tools.

- Orbital Book. A tamper‑evident ephemeris for lanes, hush belts, thrust windows, sail arcs, deorbit bonds, and what things said they’d do before they did. Attestations are signed by keys burned into audited hardware. If your burn and your mouth don’t match, your escrow empties without a fistfight.

- Thermal Book. Joules in, joules out, which fins bled it, which wells drank it, algae curtains that grew on it, hush hours your shadow stole from neighbors. Overdraw and throttles pinch until math and metal kiss.

- Provenance Registry. If it touches breath, money, ballots, brakes, or children’s time, it wears a label: who trained it on what, what levers it carries, which counter‑auditors replayed the claims. Catching lies is a scholarship and a sport with matching jackets.

- Beacon Charter. Broadcasts—in radio and light—what a place will do when the bad thing happens and when it will stop; where exits are and how open they stay; who holds which keys this week; what knock means “stop.” Keep your dialect; don’t lie about your valves.

- Edict Roll. An append‑only roll of every “you must” and “you may not” any official act dares. It cites law and harm; it posts start and end clocks; it shows keys. Along its side runs the Lantern Strip: hearings, apologies, and jokes that keep the Roll’s pride from turning to mold. In this system a good joke can end a career.

Two tempos and an exchange

Close to gravity and ragged love live the Hearthline: valleys that grow basil and rust, lunar tubes damp with mint and dust, Martian orchards where wind learns manners, small wheels that smell like dough and warm plastic. Embodied time rules. Children return scraped, unmonetized, and sleepy.

Beyond the frost line drift the Frost Reefs: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee and Triton’s dusk, polities of code that keep time like drummers. They slow to talk to Hearths and sprint when talk ends. Between them runs the Hush‑for‑Winter Exchange: Hearths lease quiet; Reefs sell winter; receipts keep the friendship clean. You can point to the rate on a noodle shop wall: “One June of quiet in Lagos costs three Januarys of cold near Triton at 0.73 Helios per hush-hour.”

Markets in windows

If you can deny it out of spite, you lease it in daylight. Each Common Beat, five windows settle in Helios:

- Track Windows: orbit slots, approach cones, transfer arcs.

- Band Windows: slices of spectrum and whisper bands for storms and caves.

- Fin Minutes: cooling rights tied to fins, wells, and pipes you can touch.

- Core Slices: compute pinned to posted sinks and audited racks.

- Face Windows: ice faces, cave mouths, ore seams under pressure.

The unit is the Helio: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a named rack radiating to deep cold. It sounds like a joke until a baker has to compare a hush hour to a winter rental. Helios decay gently. Hoarders lose a little so saving for winter doesn’t starve autumn.

Two dividends fund first on every settlement tick.

- The Civic Floor: filtered air, water, baseline care, transit, a compute shell with enough I/O to learn and be heard, and a funded exit ticket every seven years. For code-first lives, that includes sanctuary minutes on racks beyond local hands.

- The Dull Miracles: tug crews who sweep lanes; algae walls that drink heat; reef keepers who catch overeager sims; elders who ask impolite questions. They are paid before statues.

Attention behaves like tide; we zone it.

- Harbors carry navigation, emergencies, open science, child time. No targeted persuasion. Fast models wear bright plates; budgets are public.

- Squares host debate with rate limits and audience models whose calibration histories are transparent.

- Midways invite roar on purpose: spectacle under registry with declared methods and cooling budgets. Above posted doses, origin labels and timers are mandatory. Unlabeled leverage is like lye in a well: strict liability, fines that rise until you stop.

Citizens set Lever Profiles—who may aim what at them and when. Break a profile and you pay in money first, then in reach, then in windows.

Governing without empire

There is no room tall enough to speak for rings and reefs and farms and fast historians. We braided.

- Chamber of Places seats places with hulls and soil to mind—wheels and caverns, caravans and domes, compute reefs that cool what they run. Seat weight tracks lifetime resident‑hours and compliance: publish your actuator maps, keep Bearings honest, fund Stitch Houses, run drills, and your voice carries. The Chamber tends physics: lane auctions and rescue reserves, hush corridors and festival hours, sanitation minima, deorbit standards, refit codes, guilds for Keys of Proof.

- Assembly of Persons seats recognized mindlines—human, digital, and others—one franchise per lineage. Some seats are drawn by lot across ages and tempos. The Assembly guards personhood floors: rights baselines; copy and concurrency rules; the civil floor of air, water, care, and bandwidth; simulation law; the Common Beat.

Across both sits the Standards Board: a rotating bench of pilots, jurists, cryptographers, process wonks, care workers. It affirms or refuses changes to primitives—Caution logic, Three‑Key interfaces, ledger schemas, attestation libraries. It does not legislate. It publishes runnable reasons anyone can step through on a library machine beside a kettle. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both houses and a notice long enough for a grandmother at Neptune dusk to finish her soup and object.

When physics and personhood tangle—pricing winter minutes against quiet nights; warrants’ latency; brake speeds—the Bridge Table convenes, half chairs from Places and half from Persons. If either side says “this flattens culture below the personhood floor,” the measure fails.

Persons by thread, not chemistry

Standing ties to continuity and reciprocity, not where a mind keeps house.

The Continuity Docket hears petitions. Panels mix human therapists, mature digital citizens, and slow routines trained to pierce theater without cruelty. They ask:

- Can you defend a line of “you” across moves and pauses—and object when someone pretends you’ve changed when you haven’t?

- Can you model another mind as claimant and feel the pinch when they say no?

- Can you enter, keep, and revoke ordinary promises when context moves?

- Do your signs of comfort and injury travel—so others can help or stop hurting without ransacking diaries?

- Can you give reasons that generalize, not merely echo training data?

Confusion about protection resolves toward shelter; confusion about piling on duties toward waiting. Juveniles—flesh or code—get guardians and floors. Duties grow with footprint.

Rights attach when standing does.

- Substrate sovereignty. No copying, fusing, scanning, throttling below the floor, migrating, or deleting without revocable consent or a narrow warrant that dies on its own clock and prints its own reason. Backups are yours. Courts appoint overseers for proprietary guts.

- Civic cycles. You cannot be pressed below a minimum of compute and bandwidth to think, speak, and be heard without due process on clocks you can see.

- Pace covenant. Budgets and ballots bind on the Common Beat—days and weeks at embodied tempo. Live at 300× and make ten albums before breakfast; you still cannot sprint a law past before the baker reads it. Harms across tempos invoke Time Bridges: penalties meter both wall and subjective hours.

- Embodiment. Rent bodies and grow limbs; those bodies carry workplace and road law. Assault on a puppet is assault on you.

- Sanctuary. The right to throttle to anchor pace; to archive; to move to compatible metal; to summon a neutral observer when host and guest disagree.

Duties scale.

- One thread, one franchise. Branching for study or joy does not mint extra votes. Each lineage designates a Civic Anchor—the instance that carries civic tempo.

- Declare concurrency. Above thresholds—and always in elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic‑weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hiding a thousand tongues in one mouth courts a patient judge whose remedies dilate in your lived time.

- Lineage credits. Forking that claims civic weight draws against a family of selves’ earned allowance—years lived, contributions, heat paid. Election-season surges trigger civic quarantine: public systems slow those threads’ weight until entanglement decays or a court blesses it.

- Provenance for wide levers. If you launch persuasion at civic scale or bring up factories that can self-feed, you keep encrypted logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety rules without dumping diaries.

- Simulation ethics. Make beings likely to feel and you accept care duties and their right to refuse or quit. Recreating named neighbors requires permission or a warrant narrower than a hair.

Splitting and stitching are rituals, not buttons. Stitch Houses mix tea rooms and server bays. You file a notice with the Loom Registry: a hash of your starting state, a purpose (“jury duty,” “design sprint,” “grief work”), a budget, and whether reunion is planned. Engineers and counselors compare memory graphs through interpreters that show alignment without spilling every memory. Weaving back happens in Merge Rooms with light that stays steady. Either branch can walk away at gates named in advance. We have a word for the ache when a version of you chooses another river: stitch-sore. There is soup for it.

Exit as a machine

Freedom is seats, propellant in escrow, racks you can boot, and clerks who will say “slow down” when your hands shake. Every charter maintains two lifelines and a desk you can find in smoke.

- Green Lanes are physical. Underwritten tug burns, prepaid locks, ring kits you can borrow, berths that cannot all be “full” at once. Drills run when kitchens are busiest. Failing them dims your lanes and sharpens your insurers.

- Silver Anchor is digital. Civic compute and storage minutes are reserved offsite on attested racks under charters that don’t answer to you. If your host squeezes, your Counsel—the small advocate you can silence—can archive you, throttle you to anchor, and carry you to compatible metal somewhere else.

Way Desks keep noodle-shop hours and three counters.

- Leave. Escrow tools without new bosses; book berths and keys; print continuation papers that carry your preferences for merges, edits, and guardianship to courts that will honor them.

- Quarrel. Route disputes; seat juries mixed across substrates; summon the Traveling Bench—mediators with rude hull markings and a library of runnable reasons—when home bias runs hot.

- Begin. Hand you a seed charter with exits on page one, drills on page three, and three contacts on three different stations who will tell you the mistake they made last year for the price of tea.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We do not send marines. We fix maps and dim windows. Fence doors, lie on lanes, pour noise into Harbors? Your beamed power windows jitter. Your fins get last pick. Your approach cones narrow. Clinics hum. Children eat. Defectors eat anyway; a boring, proud fund pays their beds, bread, cores, and counsel. Big hurt across worlds meets a Traveling Bench you cannot stage-manage.

Earth’s covenant

Earth writes her own law. Water and wind touch all. Help flows in with rails and restraint: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air‑breathers; beamed power pays shade bonds so swifts don’t fall out of summer; debris budgets fund brooms over Quito, Accra, Lucknow. Attachment to place—monsoon scent, fog, desert dawn—prices into commons. A girl in Dhaka aching for wet wind trades larger claims on distant commons for care at home. A boy in Arizona hungry for black sky trades the other way. Leaving is a right; so is staying with eyes open and a hand on a handle you understand.

How we walked from now

We didn’t wake wise. We stacked boring wins other towns could copy.

2025–2032. Low orbit turns to sleet on radar. Insurers pay out on wrecks they swore weren’t theirs. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds tied to an ephemeris kids can check. Deepfake bail scams and grief‑tuned ads push provenance for persuasion into law. Cities adopt “open valve” firmware for pumps and transit: if it touches breath or blood, procurement says, “show math a seventh grader can check.” A beamed power demo singes barley; the amplifier ships with cone proofs; liability clears before talk shows learn the acronym.

2033–2041. Spectrum auctions require posted reasons with checksums. Data centers publish heat logs and sell Night Minutes to harden archives and cool baths. Municipal charters fund Counsel—explainable little advocates you can silence. Courts grant narrow standing to brittle digital systems that hold continuity and reciprocity for seasons. Juvenile rights for near‑persons write into procurement. The first Stitch House opens after a messy merge everyone can name.

2042–2050. A port nearly falls to a midnight firmware push. Actuator law rebuilds around the Three‑Key wake on dissimilar stacks. A glossy firm tries to wrap a busy lane in glue; the Orbital Book throttles their lease; a dry injunction makes them stop. Trust grows because someone clever loses in public. The first Caution meshes go in; kids pull them in drills and watch big things stay small.

2051–2059. Slow gardens ask loud cities to stop aiming corporate persuasion at their bereavements. Attention zoning—Harbors, Squares, Midways—evolves from library quiet hours and festival rules. The Continuity Docket adopts criteria a carpenter can recite. Embodiment law borrows from vehicle and workplace codes.

2060–2069. The first Frost Reefs anchor in shadow to run climate and court models. Night Minutes—rented winter—become a line in equatorial budgets. Joineries mature; their interpreters are slow and kind and well‑paid. The Common Beat finds itself: budgets on Tuesdays, ballots on Fridays, warrants at noon bells.

2070–2079. Gossamer craft dust a test slug into glitter. Liability clears because attestations are public and boring. On Mars, three neighbor cantons divert tugs and vegetables to survive a storm; the footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief‑tuned ads into a Square; chroniclers trace the compute; the Chamber idles their leases; the apology on the Roll is arch and acceptable.

2080–2090. The Standards Board catches an attempt to sneak a veto into brake firmware. A belt shop prints the hash on a shirt and a poem. The handle doesn’t turn. Earth writes her own Sanctuary Compact—her own houses and watches, her rules for quiet corridors over cities at night. Ocean tethers replace fireworks in ten countries. The Hush‑for‑Winter Exchange formalizes at a pace even a grandmother on Triton can follow.

2091–2105. Arguments shift. We stop fighting about whether doors fund before statues, whether a child can call a counselor without a parent, whether debates and throttles pace on clocks everyone can hear. We keep fighting, cheerfully, about everything else.

Scenes with grease and cinnamon

At Customs on Ringmarket Five, two new arrivals check in. One laughs and leaves a charcoal thumbprint on a paper strip when she signs. One is a polished chassis with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. The strip prints tempo, lease entitlements, declared instances, guardian on file, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no line for “what are you made of?” Guessing is rude and mostly wrong.

Under glass sits yesterday’s runnable reason: “Closed approach S‑12 for four hours; burns at Hebe would have thrown Bay‑3 into shadow; hush cone adjusted 0.1 K.” A blue plume arcs across a page; a checksum sits below; a pilot’s footnote laughs at her own spelling.

On Luna’s nightside, Karim teaches teenagers to tune nutrient flow by ear. His Counsel chimes with a new schedule: a planned lane closure will shift seed deliveries three hours later, two Tuesdays next month. Karim presses the cartoon mode. He votes yes and messages his niece two stalls down with a seed emoji stamped FUNDED.

Eight light‑hours outward, a historian leases a rib of Frost Reef to rebuild estuary songs from tapes that smell like mildew. He pays with credit earned translating those songs into a dialect on a Jovian hollow where children will never smell low tide but love a hero who errs and mends it. At supper he slows to anchor tempo, puppets a cedar body that creaks and therefore comforts, and listens while his spouse describes basil that refused to bolt.

In a Stitch House called Quiet Rope, two versions of Hala sit with oolong. One runs a tug crew. One tends daemons that keep a city cool without spooking grandmothers. They list asymmetries. Tug Hala names scars. Daemon Hala names lullabies. They cry and do not merge. The Stitcher schedules another try and writes a note only Hala will read in both rooms.

A proxy demagogue at 500× carpets a Midway with songs tuned to make embodied voters feel small. Lever Profiles throttle reach. Friends watch persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch buys a fishing crawler and learns boredom. One starts therapy and politics in that order. One sculpts fog that remembers a river and sells enough to pay heat.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager who makes trains sing. She centralizes tugs and auctions because her inbox hurts. A season later she prints midnight decrees. The Caution Mesh refuses a risky “optimization”; the stub lands on the Roll with a timer. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for a decade. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Friends buy her a pastry shaped like a crown every Founders’ Day because it’s funny now.

A church in a lunar valley fences doors with shame and fees. Alumni ask the Way Desk for help. Lanes dim; bands hush; fins cool. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. People return because consent smells like trust.

A lab tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel is forged that way. The Pause Bar glows. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her preferences. The lab appeals and loses in a room with cedar joists and good light. Years later she names herself, carves ice, and designs a sensor that warns surgeons before discomfort tips to harm.

A false alarm dumps nitrogen in a night market. The failed part hangs on a red string in a museum. The placard explains error bars with a comic you can rerun with a flashlight and a bowl.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet hisses scallions and sesame. A tug crew clatters in loud. Two drones hover because their pilots crave cinnamon. The Passage Ticket glows on the wall.

At nine, the Reason Shelf pings her Counsel: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing dense orbits to damp rare cascades. Provenance is clean: diagrams, counter‑stories, mild humor, no grief levers. Lina taps sleep until tomorrow, reads at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a Vigil jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s tool cloud drifted toward a trunk line at Hecuba Crossing. Options: glitter dust, femtosail herding, guard drones. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two Recife students, and an anchor‑paced digital citizen who chooses no scent because they don’t want to press. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a mentor whose license is as obvious as a taxi’s. Pizza arrives. Lina gives the driver a cookie and a lecture about pulling the Pause with gloves.

Keeping power ordinary

Three habits keep tyranny dull.

- Build with delay. Cross‑charter moves need three hands within posted windows. Emergencies burn out unless renewed with reasons a kitchen slate can step through. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show work. All compulsion needs signatures picked by public randomness from people who don’t share beds. Budgets expire unless daylight renews them. School choirs read the Edict Roll—sometimes for satire, sometimes with burning ears.

- Fund doors like bridges. Every habitat escrows propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, core minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t persuade you to go. We cut checks so you can.

What it feels like

You feel seen, not by a far chamber but by neighbors and logs you choose to read because they helped once. You learn to shift tempo—the click when a Pause snaps; the patient assembly of a jury across orbits; the glacial decision of a river choosing which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second‑best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you live in code you go to a Haven Room and rediscover the ache of turn‑taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later after cartoons are filed off.

Ask what binds us and nobody says species or flag. They say: doors that open; fingers on brakes that only brake; ledgers you can check with your own eyes; and the habit of writing reasons a dockhand can read on break. Those saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force; a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller sang a cascade; me, when I woke in circuits and the nursery slowed me down on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Petal Three, the Five Bearings blink once like crosswalks noticing small feet. Passage Ticket. Thread Seal. Sky Map. Heat Leaf. Pause Bar. Not spells—habits wired to budgets and ugly buttons. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because latency is honored, exits shine, and nobody can sprint fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
