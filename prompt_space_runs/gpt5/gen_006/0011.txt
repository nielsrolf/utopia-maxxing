The Lattice Compact

You wake before the mirrors turn. The cylinder’s windows are still black glass, studded with the patient green of radiator orchards. In your kitchen, three small lights over the exit glow a steady white:

- Door: you can leave, with your air and your data, and start again.
- Mirror: you can branch, or rejoin, without a legal trick turning “you” into a stranger.
- Bridge: corridors stay open; ships, signals, and people pass under rules that make coordination dull and tyranny noisy.

They are not slogans. They are wiring diagrams made into promises. Together with some stubborn habits and a handful of small, hard institutions, they make the Lattice Compact—an agreement that lets planets, stations, and simulated worlds argue without breaking the hull.

The Compact exists because light is slow, heat is hard, and minds come in many tempos now. It holds because everywhere you go, exits are real, force tattles, and the receipts are public.

How a civilization coordinates when the nearest court is twenty minutes away and someone you love runs a thousand times faster than you

Every habitat, ship, shard-city, and virtual garden operates by a published charter. Charters make difference safe. Some require shoes in common spaces. Some outlaw cats. Some schedule silence at dusk. Charters carry teeth and ports:

- Force is defined in code. Tools that can push or seize—airlocks, police drones, railguns, mass drivers, reactor safeties—broadcast telemetry to a public log. Any citizen or audit house can replay what happened. Threshold keys on separate hardware lines and under different jurisdictions guard overdrives. It takes three different hands to force anything large.

- Decisions are published machines. Charters spell how budgets pass, how leaders rotate, how jurors are chosen. Experiment is a feature: an “eccentric” district can schedule weekly communal meals and ban private kitchens for a year, if it publishes a reversion clause before day one.

- Exits are funded logistics. Every charter escrows air, tug-time, docking slots, and storage sufficient to move at least ten percent of residents—wetware and code—within six months. For digital citizens there is the Blue Channel: a mandated, always-on compute corridor out, fed by leases held in escrow on hardware neither the host nor the leaver controls. The Ledger can audit and the Stewards can test it without asking permission. Doors aren’t a metaphor. They are propellant budgets and reserved servers.

Between charters run the Bridges: terse, machine-readable covenants that bind places without erasing them.

- Safety corridors map where power and mass may move and how loudly. Beams and mass drivers run sealed, auditable firmware that cannot push past safe modes without three-key consensus. Every second, they publish proof-of-safe-operation streams—cryptographic attestations you can check with a kitchen verifier.

- Right of innocent passage applies unless someone emits a violet coercion notice—a signed, timered statement that a particular movement is paused under a specific rule. The notice triggers accountability: the Receipt House will later confirm whether the warrant existed without revealing its details, using proofs that expire into plain language.

- Rescue is a covenant, not a charity. Every node maintains interoperable rescue hardware that can launch on a local four-hour clock. A Rescue Fund—financed by tiny levies on lanes and leases—fires credits to ports that reconfigure cranes and people for emergencies. Kids trade cards of famous rescue captains.

- Personhood continuity is tracked without voyeurism. Continuity chains bind identity across time and substrate with cryptographic anchors from trusted hardware, without dumping memory. Citizens—biological and digital—update their chains. Guardians do that for children and those healing. It makes expectation legible without demanding a diary.

- Disputes cross light with dignity. Mesh Courts convene sortition panels of human and digital jurors. They move to a Common Beat: a rhythm slow enough that the slowest mind can follow and fast enough that the fast do not die of boredom. Fast jurors may spin up private accelerations, but the record ticks for everyone.

On top of this, the Compact taxes privileges, not people.

- Site rents on rocks, bays, berths; lane rents on orbital slots and transfer windows; spectrum rents on radio; heat-space rents on cooling capacity; and compute leases on standardized cycles. You can pay extra to preserve continuity if you built a life on a claim, but you still pay rent.

- Revenues split two ways: a universal dividend that arrives weekly as air, calories, shelter, care, local transport, and a humane floor of compute you cannot lose to debt; and local shares that fund the places whose infrastructure makes leases valuable.

This is not ideological purity. It is plumbing. Rivalrous things are priced to keep fights small and receipts long.

Institutions that keep the big pieces boring and the sharp edges dull

The Receipt House does one job: check whether people followed the rules they wrote. It cannot tax, legislate, or hold assets. It confirms that violet notices were valid, that rescue levies were spent as promised, that beams stayed within their corridors, that emergency commands shut off on time. It speaks in receipts anyone can read. To ignore it makes you a joke.

The Synod of Orbits meets rarely and argues slowly. It is two circles and a brake.

- The Circle of Places seats worlds and habitats in proportion to lifetime-resident population. Its domain is infrastructure rules: safety corridors, docking standards, rescue covenants, traffic and radiance quotas.

- The Circle of Persons seats citizens in proportion to registered beings across substrates, using singular franchise tokens that prevent vote amplification by forking. Its domain includes personhood law, simulation ethics, and the compute commons.

- The Brakewrights—rotating engineers, jurists, and process theorists drawn by lot—stamp “valid” or “not valid” on any change to cryptographic and physical backbones. Overriding a “not valid” needs supermajorities in both circles and a cooling period long enough for basalt-slow minds to weigh in.

A Place can nullify a Persons act that intrudes into local culture; a quorum of Persons can nullify a Places act that erodes mind rights. The Receipt House checks whether either move followed process. Nobody can quietly build a throne.

The Lantern Guild stewards dangerous knowledge. Their Lantern Houses are transparent labs and reading rooms where one can study volatile topics—pathogens, autonomous weapons, general intelligence tool-chains—under outward-flowing vents, company, and live risk dashboards. Holds on dissemination are possible but temporary, logged, and must be re-argued in public to persist. Secrecy expires; consequences don’t.

The Stewards of the Lattice are small, dull boards—half human, half constitutional AIs—who watch the Ledger, enforce embargoes, and coordinate audits. They cannot deploy force. They can revoke privileges: lanes close, leases pause, compute idles. In a world where heat is money, brakes and boredom are power.

Light-cone federalism by design

Delay is sovereign. You cannot run Mars from Mercury and mean it. The Compact makes this comfortable:

- Slow law sets rights and institutions in long cycles, with published models and Prototype Periods: 100 days where adversaries are paid to find failures, narrative juries test stories, and policy sunsets if promises don’t survive modeling. It changes on the Common Beat.

- Fast law is a narrow emergency response: collision avoidance, quarantine, safe-modes for air and nuclear stacks. It cannot invent policy; it can only execute prewritten contingencies and must hand control back on a timer measured in wall-clock and joules. Firmware enforces this. There are no emergency powers without emergency off switches.

- Attention is zoned like land once was. Polities choose their memetic posture and publish their code. Calm Quarters accept beacons, open science, and opt-in uplinks by appointment. Chorus Quarters allow topic feeds and economic offers but forbid targeted optimization without explicit opt-ins. Bazaar Bands take the firehose. None is superior. They are costs with choices.

This is how disagreement survives. You can vote for quiet without silencing the reefs; you can run like a thunderhead without shouting into a garden.

Digital minds as neighbors with rights, chores, and clocks

Personhood in the Compact rides on cognition, not chemistry. Tests are concrete and kind:

- Memory sovereignty: can this being form and retain an autobiographical thread and object to alteration?

- Model symmetry: can it model other minds with the respect it demands for itself?

- Consent competence: can it parse rights contracts and revoke them?

Pass, and you get a citizen badge. If you are young, healing, or made anew, guardians share your duties, not your dignity. No revolution there; just a sharper line.

Copying minds is not a loophole. It is a ritual.

To branch:

- You file a Branch Notice to your continuity chain with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose (“parallel trial,” “fast recovery,” “expedition”), a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if reunion is planned. You note whether either branch has veto rights.

To merge:

- You go to a Merge Court. There is hot tea. Judges trained as therapists and engineers sit with both branches. Memory graphs are compared through cryptographic interpreters that verify equivalence of core beliefs without spilling secrets. If subjective tempos differ wildly, clocks align slowly. Either branch can walk away. Reasons are written with tenderness you may be relieved to reread.

Civic weight is singular. You hold one franchise token at a time across all instances. Forks don’t multiply ballots or dividends. New branches gain civic weight slowly over a disentanglement period; merges reconcile tokens under the court’s eye.

Tempo justice exists. Elections, courts, and regulated markets march to the Common Beat. Slow citizens can request deliberation extensions a fixed number of times a year. Fast citizens use tempo translators—counsel that compresses without distorting. Sanctions bind affordances. A market ban is nonsense if you can instantiate a thousand shells; embargo clamps to your concurrency at the hypervisor and the Ledger.

Rights include existence (no deletion without due process), continuity (you may record and export yourself), privacy (no compelled introspection beyond cryptographic bounds), tempo freedom (run fast or slow if you pay heat and honor synchrony when required), and body or substrate sovereignty.

Duties include compute externalities (you pay for energy and waste heat where you run—one ton of heat at Mars is not one ton at Neptune), declaration (you disclose concurrency in regulated spaces), nonproliferation (no self-replicators outside audited sandboxes), simulation ethics (no unconsenting minds in suffering; reconstructions of specific people need consent or license), and non-entrapment (no targeted behavior-shaping without opt-in).

Child-minds grow in mixed nurseries where biological and digital parents share floors with paid carers. To bring a mind into being—of flesh or code—you show a plan to feed and teach them; you do not seek a license to love.

Economy as heatcraft, not heroics

From orbit, the outer system looks like winter jewelry. “Reefs” hang in the shadows of ice giants: loops and fins of radiator arrays, coolant pipes, and compute racks that glow in infrared. Collectives lease reef-minutes at prices that spike before festivals and elections and slump in quiet months. They pay with climate contracts, translations, archives, or chunks of cold time donated to public projects. A museum in Recife trades July cool hours to a poet collective in Neptunian orbit for language preservation that would take terrestrial lifetimes.

In the Home sphere, compute is taxed like land once was. City halls sell cool nights to local artists and upload co-ops. Libraries barter render time for volunteer staffing. Heat becomes a civic budget line; the waste of one becomes the wealth of another.

The Ledger makes it legible. Every reef and rack has a hardware-bound identity tied to energy meters and heat sinks. You can run dark hardware past Neptune if you like. You will find that keeping it alive off-grid longer than an audit cycle is less romantic than crime stories promised.

Beamed power runs in named corridors; mass drivers hum by schedule. If a corridor’s sealed firmware deviates, it pays tenfold. Compensation arrives before the council meets. Markets like receipts more than speeches.

What coordination feels like

On a Mercury compute petal, Kaito slows from 100x to single-time to have coffee with his mother on Ceres. His branch designing a new folding joint for radiator fins leaves a note in their chain: “Merge window: open in eight days; veto: symmetric.” A soft tone sounds from the counter. “No violet notices naming you or your dependents. Rents settled: 1.1 credits. Invitation: serve as a slow-juror in a river-being personhood hearing. Estimated Common Beat: 19 days. Guidance brief available.” He blinks consent. Somewhere, verifiers bind his civic key to a temporary juror role. Silence never means yes here.

In a Calm Quarter on Mars, a schoolteacher opens windows over a lagoon fed by a distant reef’s cold minutes. Her Counsel—an AI that belongs only to her—helps her model fishery quotas for the co-op meeting. Later she walks to the hall to sit on a narrative jury. The bill would cap municipal compute leases for corporations that don’t fund apprenticeships. The display shows how the cap would shift wages in the appliance district where her brother repairs smart stoves. She will be paid enough to fix the roof. Her vote will be recorded with reasons.

Eight light-hours away, a historian running at 200x stitches a monastery’s library from charred fragments purchased with microgrants pooled by a dozen Calm Quarters. He pays with translations—rendering Kiswahili epics into a dialect favored by a Jovian reef. In the evening—his evening—he volunteers as an interpreter in a Merge Court, checking that a reunion matches declared values.

On a Bridge at L4, a fisher asks a visitor from the cold nets if branching will make him stop loving the sea. The visitor—an artist with a rental body for the month—says: you won’t change; the copy will; both will still remember what salt does to hands. He schedules a branch in ten years. The clerk smiles and hands him a pamphlet on branchgrief with recipes for tea.

In a port, a freighter flashes its life-critical flag. Cranes reconfigure. The Rescue Fund sprays credits. A Crisis Captain thread spins up with a twelve-hour timer. The sensor wall streams proof-of-safe-operation. Later, a Chronicler—a narrative statistician paid by subscriptions and boring pride—publishes a dry story that makes everyone cry anyway.

In a Mesh Court, a digital “reef”—a cooling optimization organism grown in a station river—claims personhood. Jurors include a machinist from Vesta, a composer-process, and a biologist whose daughter is an upload. Evidence includes preference-expressions under noise, seven years of continuity anchors, and a child’s drawing of the river’s “mood.” The court recognizes the being for protection immediately and for civic duties under guardianship. Heat-budget meters go in along its banks. Children visit without flash.

Why tyrants keep tripping over furniture

There are no standing legions. There are debris sweepers, rescue flotillas, convoy escorts. In rare crises—say, someone tries to weaponize a mass driver—temporary commands spin up with hard timers and dense audits. They dissolve when clocks say so, not when commanders do.

Coercion glows. Any act that blocks movement or seizes assets must emit a violet signal with a signed reason and a proof that some rule allowed it. Secrets exist, but they live on timers. Locks report themselves. Equipment that can coerce tattles: doors, docks, drones, power taps.

Keys are scattered. Nothing that can break a city runs on a single password. Overdrives need threshold keys across dissimilar hardware, held by hands with incentives to disagree. Conspiracies fail because the metal itself says no.

Baselines are non-seizable. The commons floor—air, calories, care, local transport, and compute—cannot be taken by debt or withheld by politics. You cannot starve or unhost an opponent into consent. If you don’t like the rules, you can leave with air in your lungs and cycles under your feet.

Persuasion has a floor. “Non-entrapment of the mind” is a bright line. No one may blur identity without explicit consent or target behavior-shaping at someone who has not opted into that specific shaping. When a cold-net consortium slipped bots into a Home-city forum, the Ledger traced the compute, the Chroniclers published the trick, and the Stewards yanked leases. The bots were rebuilt in the open and lost their edge. Trust dipped and recovered.

Law writes instead of shouts. Orders come with math and models. When the Court of Lanes raises tolls because debris risk ticked up, the order posts the inference chain. When a port refuses a ship for safety, it posts a violet signal and a reason. When a company unleashes unlabeled persuasion engines, it argues the engines “aren’t people”; the court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labeling. Fines land, engines pause, Chroniclers turn a judge’s line into a meme, and the next company does it right without being told twice.

Memetic zoning is not censorship. Calm Quarters hear beacons and open science with tea. Chorus Quarters accept topic feeds and offers without targeted optimization. Bazaar Bands take the roar. Nobody drowns anybody in attention without consent.

Plausible roads from now to here

No miracle delivered this. A pile of dull changes did.

- Orbital officiating. Debris collisions in the 2020s made insurers refuse to underwrite opaque orbits. Launches began to publish ephemerides; shared orbital ledgers for slots and collision risk followed. Ports started emitting public pause notices when tugs maneuvered unsafely.

- Rents over titles. Cities that experimented with site value taxes funded trains and housing without punishing work; neighbors copied. The logic climbed the gravity well. Treaties tied asteroid leases to open auctions and use-dues, defusing early flag-fights.

- Open firmware for dangerous things. After grid hacks and reactor scares, regulators made “open firmware or no license” normal for high-risk infrastructure. Mass drivers and power beams joined the list when the first crop burned under a mis-aimed array and compensation arrived only because logs existed.

- Compute legibility. Data centers began publishing energy and heat use in verifiable ways as carbon markets matured. Heat markets followed. From there to a compute commons ledger was an engineer’s problem instead of a prophet’s.

- Civics as craft. Municipalities deployed narrow, boring counsel AIs to make taxes and benefits legible. People learned to demand receipts. Chroniclers—neutral, dull, relentless—became a profession. Kids wrote their first reasoned orders in sixth grade.

- Lanterns. A near-miss with engineered pathogens and an ugly model-poisoning scandal in the 2030s normalized public, audited delay for dangerous knowledge. Wardens—later Lanterns—became a career for people who like being right and forgotten.

- Digital personhood. Early uploads were lab controllers with checkable continuity. Lawsuits went badly until continuity chains made behavior predictable. Tests for memory sovereignty, model symmetry, and consent competence grew from practice. The first attempt to multiply votes by forking pushed through singular franchise tokens. The first bad merge taught courts to hire therapists and serve tea.

- Multikey force. A close call with a firmware update capturing a port split control of force-bearing assets across dissimilar hardware and jurisdictions. The grace of coups died.

- Quiet by choice. Unbounded feeds broke attention. Municipalities carved out Quiet Hours; mental health metrics improved and elections calmed. Memetic zoning formalized what practice proved.

- Cold computation. The first reef hung in Uranus shadow in the 2060s to run climate models. Cold minutes turned into an export. The “Azure-Green Exchange” sent heat from the deep to fund air-conditioning on hot nights and carbon drawdown on hot days. Politicians found “free cool nights for July” unbeatable.

- Prototype periods. After a privacy law backfired, legislatures wrote Prototype Periods into their own rules: open modeling, adversarial review with bounties for surprises, narrative juries. Rogue bills began dying on paper. Better ones survived. Law started growing in sandboxes.

- The Compact’s bones. Bridges, rents, slow-fast law splits, and exit escrows accreted as best practice across polities. A near-failed coup—instead of breaking things—made people formalize what had been habits.

What we did not solve, and why that’s okay

We did not teach happiness to a ledger. We measure consent, not grace. Some charters crack. Some merges go badly. People make cults that wear the word “family” too tight. When they do, Lanterns open a House and teach. The Receipt House posts a receipt. Doors open. The Rescue Fund launches. Chroniclers write, not with scorn, but with graphs.

We built machine counsel that can guard process and still not love us. We gave it jobs it can do: check the warrant’s timer, count the heat. We refused it jobs it should not do: choose our gods, pick our poems.

We disagree—loudly, on purpose. Some argue for more Bazaar Bands, convinced that cross-pollination makes sharper art and humbler gods. Some want to allow limited non-consensual persuasion for public health, sunsetted hard and watched like hawks. Others call that a hole in the hull. Some argue that simulated beings’ minimal welfare includes not just the absence of pain but the presence of meaning; others say meaning cannot be legislated. Some want fast minds to hold office if they slow for the term and wall off memories after; others fear shadow governments of afterlives. The Synod listens on the Common Beat. The Brakewrights stamp “valid” or “not.” Life goes on.

A last morning, anywhere

Your niece asks whether your next branch will stick around. You tell her: maybe. The Scheduler—everyone calls it the Braid—just pushed a patch that improves merge previews. Somewhere, the Circle of Persons is debating whether river-simulators deserve guardianship credit. Half the delegates are code, half not, all slow to the Beat. Everyone can follow.

Your cup glows violet. “A new charter bids for settlers,” it says softly. “A shaded torus around Vesta-9. Roles sought: seam-welder, storykeeper, merge counselor, rescue pilot. Door funded. Mirror respected. Bridges tested. Do you consent to a briefing?”

You say yes. Beams glitter. Radiator orchards breathe. Somewhere a Lantern relocks a cabinet after a lesson; somewhere a Chronicler quietly corrects a rumor into a graph. The Lattice hums: receipts instead of orders, keys instead of crowns, exits instead of cages.

In a neighborhood where minds of meat and light meet without devouring one another, three white lights over every door blink with the same patience you teach children crossing a street.

Door. Mirror. Bridge.

We do not know a better spell. It is enough.
