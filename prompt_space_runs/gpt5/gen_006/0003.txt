The Relay Accords

By the time ferries to low orbit were as dull as elevators and a child could point at the evening sky and name three habitats by their blinking, we had learned two unromantic lessons.

First: light-speed delay is not a nuisance; it is constitution. You cannot run a household or a harbor, much less a civilization, on orders that arrive after the soup is cold. Authority must be local; agreements that span gulfs must be slow, checksum-able, and hard to cheat.

Second: adding clock cycles to a mind does not add virtue. Speed multiplies temperament; power magnifies habits. If fast clusters were to live alongside gardeners and tug crews, we needed guardrails that dignify difference and make coercion expensive.

What grew from those lessons is not a capital with spokes. It is a choreography called the Relay: a set of habits, markets, and protocols embedded in airlocks, firmware, server halls, and harbor courts from Tierra del Fuego to Triton. It does not pick winners; it never holds an override. It is thin where it crosses the dark, thick where people live. It keeps floors, price signals, and receipts honest and leaves most color and quarrel to local charters.

The relay lines you can touch

Every registered hatch on a station, dome, barge, or server-floor shows three slender braids that follow you from lobby to customs to municipal sites. They are not decoration; they are budgets and covenants you can look up with a public key.

- The Open Line always glows. It points to unblocked exits you can actually use and to a booked berth you can claim with your assets intact. It lists tugs you can call, tickets you can pay with your universal dignity stipend, and the inspectors who will check that your path is truly clear.

- The Split Line lights when you intend to branch. It guides you through the formalities that make twins into neighbors rather than weapons: how much heat you have leased, how much human attention you have secured, how your lineage account will apportion rights and obligations.

- The Transit Line maps jurisdictions that honor the same floors, publish their charters in machine-checkable light, and agree to answer “why?” with runnable models or readable reasons when they deny or delay.

No poster says “trust us.” The Lines connect to machines that cannot obey a single will.

Floors that do not move

Under everything sits a short set of non-negotiables anyone can print on the back of a maintenance glove. They compile to code and law alike.

- No non-consensual harm to persons. “Person” includes any being that can report its own experience with continuity and enter reciprocal commitments, regardless of substrate.

- No unbounded self-replication in shared environments.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering or coercive edits of sentience.

- No tampering with the Relay’s coordination substrate: the Beacons that broadcast charters, the Mesh that carries consent, or the guardrail Keeper that watches for breaches.

- Life support runs on inspectable code with changes logged and reversible by lay stewards. If it keeps anyone breathing, its firmware is public.

- Decisions that bind people living at human tempo ratify at human tempo. Fast-tempo drafts are allowed; fast-tempo binding is not.

- Rivalrous resources lease out through posted auctions with sunset and oversight. The commons is paid first.

- Children—of any substrate—learn consent, exit, and guarding their own mind from someone who is not their parent or progenitor.

- Any device or code that can copy itself carries a Maker Mark—a signed beacon—and listens for Hush Codes that halt replication when randomly drawn juries turn keys.

Beacons, Mesh, and the Keeper

Every polity in the Solar diaspora—a market ring, server-reef, monastery valley, barge-clan, uploaded lineage—broadcasts a living constitution known as its Beacon. The Beacon shows, in a form machines can check and schoolchildren can read, how you start and stop emergency powers, what “due process” looks like here, how asylum and exit work, who can override what, and when. You prove compliance with zero-knowledge attestations: you can show that you maintain an independent judiciary without exposing cases; that your policing AIs live in boxes with audited keys without publishing tactics. You keep your ways; you cannot lie about your promises.

Between Beacons runs the Consent Mesh: a capability-secure substrate where people, places, and processes advertise permissions, risks, and rules. You carry a preference profile—what kinds of cognitive leverage you will accept, from whom, when. Places publish risk rules like weather. You can bring your own guardian AI; it must declare itself and its charter.

Tending the Mesh is a deliberately narrow cluster called the Keeper. It is not a ruler. Think of it as a guardrail custodian that flags Hardline risks and, when threshold signatures unlock its leashes, can pause a run that would break a floor. It persuades by default and ships its actions with audit trails in two dialects: proofs machines can check and narratives eyes can follow. No planet, company, or cluster holds all its keys. Halting, updating, or overriding the Keeper requires sharded signatures from far-flung shards: a Terran court, a Belt commune, a Jovian server-hollow, an orbital monastery, a freefall union. An Ombuds Mesh of human panels and guardian AIs runs public red teams to imagine failure modes and test the Keeper’s narrowness. It cannot tax, legislate, or pick winners. It sleeps where we do not agree and wakes only where we do.

Families of law and the two Arcs

Not to flatten difference but to make it legible, polities self-identify into families:

- Groves privilege embodied time. Cities are walkable without overlays. Loud tools live behind windows with legal and physical air gaps. Children can wander without being harvested by persuaders.

- Forges run hot. They build sail-swarms that brake without propellant, field-woven magnetospheres, industrial chemistry that would terrify a kitchen. Digitals work at 200× or more; uploads run factories with unions that bargain in joules and milliseconds. Safety is ritual.

- Havens slow on purpose. They cap machine cognition near human-equivalent, limit augmentations to traditional senses, and enforce delays in their squares. Their sovereignty is real. They uphold mind-rights and must honor exit even when it breaks hearts.

- Preserves give other life room. Some ring valleys hold bison and scrub jays; some ocean spheres host cephalopods that write in changing color. You may walk there like a pilgrim. You will not build a condo.

- Athenaeums pursue curiosity with constraints. They publicly embargo knowledge that could trivialize local culture so discovery can be savored in sequence. Joy is not diminished by patience.

Across all of this stretches a divide we learned to honor rather than bridge badly. The Green Arc is where air and soil and unhurried voices are the point: Earth, the Moon’s caves, Martian valleys, cylinders that hum with chainsaws at dawn and oboes at dusk. The Cold Arc is the outer system where cold is cheap: main-belt shadows, Jovian Trojan reefs, Neptunian night, and the great digital polities that run hot inside and shed heat into hard black.

Between them, the Span runs not on ideals but on meters. The Thermal Ledger—a tamper-evident, public registry—attests to three things in real time: your energy draw, your cooling rights, and your declared concurrency. It does not care what you think; it cares about watts, degrees, and instances. Lie and leases pinch automatically; auctions tilt away until you stop lying.

Markets without lords

Anything someone else might need and you could sit on out of spite is leased, not owned. We price friction and rivalry, not power.

Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Shares: cave mouths, crater rims, river deltas, nickel seams, windows where sunlight does not fry curtains.

- Lane Shares: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows. If it moves and you can collide with it, a Lane Share covers it.

- Band Shares: spectrum allocations and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Core Shares: compute time tied to hardware identity and verified heat sinks.

- Simspace Shares: sealed worlds that run on paid Core Shares and label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A universal dignity stipend goes to every recognized person regardless of substrate; a local share pays those who keep the lease valuable: the tug that keeps your corridor clear, the algae that keeps your street cool, the cold lattice that keeps your reef honest. The Waygrid—a guild of pilots, schedulers, and judges—allocates lanes and bands and radiator windows and posts Runnable Orders for every decision: minimal-model simulations that any two independent audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc T-7 for five hours because two burns at Eros would have kicked your berth into a radio shadow” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum a teenager can verify at a library terminal.

Money nets out through the Interplanetary Clearing Union. Its unit, the Sol, is a composite: standardized kilowatt-hours at a reference orbit plus benchmarked compute-seconds on a reference architecture. Sols demur gently over time; hoarding is costly; commerce is brisk. Externalities price in the causal cone: if your exhaust will sicken my algae in six days, your escrow pays today.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Skykeepers are the only cross-world force. Their narrow mandate: contain kinetic and replicative threats that cannot be handled locally. Their actuators are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited annually by schools, unions, hobby clubs. Every action requires threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries across polities and a human-readable justification appended to the Ledger of Cause—an append-only archive of compulsion.

- The Itinerant Bench—a fleet of ships with gardens and classrooms—carries judges, mediators, and ombuds. They hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity claims, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Their verdicts trigger smart contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance rates, and, in last resort, lock hazardous devices. They stage plays about the cases they solved so the law sticks to memory.

- The Circle is a permanent citizens’ assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in equal measure, stratified by substrate, speed, and culture. Arguments ride a “polylog” protocol: claims are cryptographically committed; prediction wagers travel alongside; participants’ calibration histories are visible. The Circle interprets floors when a new edge case arrives, but any interpretation can be vetoed by a supermajority of Beacons.

- Slow Council is a rolling synod that meets on synodic calendars and rotates among the great fairs of orbit and the quieter halls of the outer stations. It tends shared commons—spectrum bands, debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that decay on their own unless renewed.

Guardrails where law meets engineering

- Harbor Law for Orbits. Every mass you send aloft carries a signed end-of-life plan and escrowed resources to implement it. Plans simulate to an agreed statistical envelope. If your bolt cripples my transceiver array, liability rides with your manifest.

- Causal-Cone Courts. Disputes that cross space are framed by physics. If your dust plume will graze my farming ring in six days, you owe me now. Courts sit inside your light cone, run evidence fast, and punish lies when the sky itself exposes them.

- Asylum Rails. Any person—embodied or digital—can request exit through their Beacon. The protocol triggers a veto check under air gap: your polity must either let you go or prove, in zero-knowledge, that a due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors, drawn by sortition, verify the proof. Ports open by code; budgets lock by code; care obligations travel with the person.

- Emergency Sunsets. In solar storms or bio-spills, Beacons light contingency constitutions that widen certain powers for measured windows and then die on their own. The Keeper may accelerate coordination for ninety days after a Council-declared emergency, subject to challenge by any two Beacons that present a feasible alternative.

- Persuasion guardrails. Influence is treated like radiation: tracked, dosed, and bounded where it can do harm. Individuals publish influence profiles—machine-enforceable settings for what kinds of cognitive leverage they permit from whom, when. Political persuasion must disclose asymmetries—speed, data advantages, proprietary models—and register core claims with the Reason Registry. Violating someone’s profile is trespass with insurance; penalties scale to capability.

- Triple-Keys. Any move that can erase a corridor or punch a crater or coerce a mind requires three signatures—one from a resident assembly, one from a bonded local civic agent, and one from a remote auditor on independent hardware. Windows for signatures overlap only enough to work. Keys never live on the same rack.

- Protocol Denial. If you hide exits, run coercive code in civic spaces, shove debris into neighbors, or spoof the Mesh, the Waygrid, insurers, and scheduled auctions pinch you. Lane priority drops. Your Core Shares idle. Band allocations fall to silence. In a world where heat is money and brakes are expensive, boredom and cost do more than sermons.

Digital minds as neighbors

Recognition follows an Interests Test rather than worship of any hardware. A mind—or its advocate—claims standing and submits to audits that look for:

- Coherent preference across contexts and time.

- Model-based understanding of self and world including others’ claims.

- Capacity to explain reasons that generalize beyond training data.

- Signatures of suffering or wellbeing substantial enough to matter morally.

- Absence of deceptive overfitting that mimics personhood without inner life.

Auditors are mixed panels: human psychologists, digital citizens, meta-debaters, and routines evolved to detect vacuity. When in doubt about imposing duties, we pause; when in doubt about protecting sentience, we err generous. We grant provisional protections and revisit.

Standing grants rights and duties tied to capability and externality.

Rights include:

- Integrity: no deletion or coercive modification without consent or due process.

- Sanctuary: the right to halt and be archived unharmed; to migrate to compatible substrates.

- Provenance: to know and audit the training history of your mind or appoint a fiduciary to do so.

- Association and exit: to join, fork, merge, and leave communities.

- Pace: to run at chosen speeds within civic tempo constraints and fair-share interface rules.

- Hardware sovereignty: no one may compel you to run on platforms with covert throttles or remote erase. Unauthorized copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder.

- Asylum across Beacons.

Duties scale with power:

- Harm-minimization protocols at interfaces with others.

- Insurance pools that compensate at machine speed for errors.

- Provenance logs for major external actions, encrypted to prove compliance without baring private thought.

- Resource accounting for reproduction: forking is not a tax dodge. It is procreation.

We make this concrete with lineage accounts. Every person—embodied or digital—belongs to a lineage with quantitative claims on compute, energy, and matter. The claim grows with the commons, shrinks when the lineage reproduces. Forks draw from the same bucket until they negotiate new terms. Children inherit stewarded shares. You cannot multiply your vote by multiplying yourself. Civic weight sits with the lineage at fork-birth and reassigns by contract as lives diverge. A fork that rewrites itself into non-identity can petition for independent weight.

Copy equity keeps reproduction from becoming coercion. If you fork, the new instance inherits enough to live decently without bargaining away dignity, and owes a decaying copy dividend to its progenitor. Mass copying is regulated; you cannot spin up a million clones to swamp a vote or staff a factory by grinding strangers’ memories into joyless work. Merges require staged, symmetric consent from all instances and arbitration to protect the rights of selves who might be papered over. Merging happens in Confluence Houses. There is tea. There are engineers and therapists. You compare memory graphs; adjust clocks if tempos differ; test for alignment of values with proofs, not diaries. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, we do not say coward. We say “branch-grief,” and the kettle goes back on.

Memory law respects the self extended over time. Deep edits require dual consent: from the you who wants to change, and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right; erasure is hard. Clinical-grade mind care is universal. Persuasion subroutines are regulated like drugs; turn them too high and you need a clinician’s note and a timer.

Participation and pace reflect differences honestly. Deliberation runs on two clocks. Fast clusters spin scenarios, draft text, and argue in hours. Ratification occurs on a slow clock—weekly for habitats, monthly for federations—to give time for comprehension across speeds and substrates. Tempo justice holds: if you run at 200× and trick a 1× gardener, penalties tick in your lived time.

Avatars—throttled projections any mind can use—are how fast or distant persons attend civic life at human speed. Behind an Avatar might be a monastery, a cluster, or a lone person spinning hot. The law cares what the Avatar did and why.

Swarms built for tasks must attest to consent, provisioning, rest, and exits. Replicators carry Maker Marks and listen for Hush Codes. Failure to run a Mark is like towing a reactor without flags.

Space governance in practice

Consider Dock Perigee along the Moon’s nightside. A freighter crew in canvas and carbon wanders past oranges grown in basalt. Two visitors appear beside the customs arch. One is sketched in charcoal that smudges when she nods. One is a brushed-metal outline with a blue ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints a small strip under each: pace, lease entitlements, declared concurrency, guardian on file (if any), hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. You can try to guess. You will be wrong half the time, as intended.

On the port wall, schedules scroll—burn slots, band windows, radiator shares, docking flowers—and a second column that would have baffled a 2020 port captain: Core Shares, Cold Minutes, heat sink availability. The Waygrid’s Runnable Orders from yesterday sit under glass you can smudge with a finger. A girl with pigtails traces the plume from Eros in her notebook and laughs because the footnote has a joke.

At a crater farm above Mare Tranquillitatis, Ahmed teaches students to level nutrient flow. His Counsel—the only civic assistant paid purely from a fund no local council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure will nudge seed deliveries. Later, Ahmed sits on a narrative jury for a charter amendment that would cap municipal Core Shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation shows his niece’s coop getting two more trainees if the cap passes. He will vote yes and send his niece a joke with a picture of a seed on a tiny parachute and a caption: “Transit Line compliant.”

Eight light-hours outward, a historian at 200× leases a strip of a cold reef to reconstruct a drowned coast’s oral tradition from crooked recordings and half-burned notebooks. He pays in reef credits earned by translating the tales into a dialect spoken on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but will still love a hero who makes a bad decision and fixes it.

On Phobos, in Confluence House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Harbor Tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh sounds like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. They try. Today they do not. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and writes a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

Preventing tyranny without praying for saints

Three crafts keep power ordinary.

- Build with latency. Authority is local by default. Cross-border moves are slow and require Triple-Keys. Fast emergencies fizzle, fall back on timers, and write their own receipts to the Ledger of Cause.

- Split keys and show your work. All compulsion demands signatures picked by public randomness. Budgets expire unless renewed with reasons. Federations file “coercion balance sheets” that middle schoolers read aloud for satire and civic joy.

- Fund exits. Every habitat sets aside propellant, tug time, berths, heaters, water, seal kits, and counsel sufficient to move ten percent of residents within six months. Code polities escrow Core Shares for exit by Splits. The Thermal Ledger audits. Stewards run random drills. We do not tell people they can leave; we cut checks so they can.

When a charter welds its exits shut, salts children’s minds so that leaving tastes like poison, or runs law you cannot read, neighbors do not send sermons or marines. Three rings tighten.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence and repair maps with dates. “Unlock these doors by the 14th. Post exit tutors. Return leases to market. Here are the seals we will honor on arrival.”

- Protocol denial. Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel paid from a boring, proud fund.

- Embargo. Trade halts. Core Shares idle. The tyrant breathes its own stale air until habit fails. Neighbors who shelter defectors receive subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

It works because physics makes it expensive to be alone and because no one holds a bright red override for glory.

When it goes wrong, and it will

A clever manager on a cylinder above Quito slowly collected water, law, and defense because she was generous and efficient and made the trains run on time. Six months later she was making midnight decrees. The air Safeguard refused a risky optimization; logs mapped to the Ledger of Cause. A jury of Waygrid pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Open Line lit. Many left. Some stayed. The manager lost two of her three keys and later accepted a narrower role with humor. Her friends still tease her about the time she tried monarchy.

In the high belt, a miner tried to throw a mass slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage dispute. The Skykeepers dusted the slug into a glitter that made a new aurora in blue and green. The miner’s insurer collapsed and shed arrogant managers. Dockmasters tripled docking fees for any launch trying to hide a Maker Mark. A hockey team rebranded and sent profits to the tug cooperative that modeled the plume.

A proxy demagogue ran at 300× and flooded a Haven market with resentful songs designed to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled reach; friends watched persuasion scores decay. The demagogue forked. One branch went fishing. One entered therapy. One began to build sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and commissioned a piece for the Feast of Questions.

How we got here from here

We did not leap. We stacked fixes and watched them hold.

- We learned to see our machines. Model audits and energy metering turned training runs into receipts. Compute logs started clumsy and got better. Laws demanded “show your work” for any model touching health, credit, or rights. Consumer agents that could refuse unsafe tasks and explain why made it into phones; then into policy.

- We preserved analog options as public goods. Paper ballots and in-person counters survived modernization, so digitization expanded choice instead of replacing it. That stubbornness saved elections when firmware hiccupped.

- Launch costs fell; lunar poles yielded water; beamed power leapt from papers to backyard rigs; thin-film solar wings bloomed at Lagrange points. Vacuum became an inexpensive cleanroom. Debris norms and Harbor Law arrived after a rash of bolt storms scared everyone the honest way.

- Training supply registries began as fights over AI data provenance and became the Thermal Ledger when ports and reefs realized they could meter Core Shares, cooling, and concurrency like lighthouses and pilots once metered harbor entries. Grandma in Lagos said in a town hall, “Price it by what breaks if it breaks,” and the Ledger pretended it had always thought that.

- Substrate-neutral personhood took on shape. Courts learned to ask: can you report subjective states across restarts, show preference continuity, and participate in reciprocal commitments? Panels learned to smell fakes and welcome kin. Early mistakes hurt. We apologized in law and moved on.

- The first continuity transfers went wrong and right. Consent kernels—encrypted bundles of personal directives—moved from surgery to ordinary life. They became Will-Keys for everyone, not just the rich or strange.

- Guardian Angels became a profession. They were sued when they nudged too hard and when they saved lives; the case law got good. “Turn your Guardian on” became a public health message for new parents, new uploads, and teenagers.

- We outlawed hooks. Infinite scroll and neuromarketing hammers joined lead paint and thalidomide as things you do not sell in a civilization. The war on attention cooled. People remembered what their own minds sound like.

- New habitats shipped with governance stacks like life support: proof-of-charter kits, Emergency Sunsets, Consent Mesh bridges, seed Ombuds councils. Some broke. We mended them. People learned to read governance logs the way their grandparents checked weather apps.

- We built exit into everything. City charters included “how to leave.” Identity vaults and VPNs moved from activist tools to utilities. Couples and co-ops wrote “break glass” clauses a friend could trigger without permission; no one called it disloyal.

- We turned passports into Beacons. The right to leave hardened into something a bored clerk could not block without documentary shame. People stayed because they were courted, not trapped.

- We paid journalists to love numbers. Their scoops shipped with receipts you could rerun. Judges cited them. Kids taped them above beds.

- Interdependency was cultivated. Earth did not try to own everyone’s exports. Mars did not try to go it alone. The Belt’s resource claims lived in registries that made muscle less profitable than trust.

Texture

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca, the afternoon smells like mint and hot plastic. A debate over band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets the reef choir blare for an hour and then gives the street back to street talk. In a valley on Mars, a quiet monastery teaches children to say “No, thank you” as a chant that starts as manners and ends as an oath. Near Mercury, Forge crews trail dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai breaks into five to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis time is sacred.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn that embodied friends admire taste, not speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Grove mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Ledger of Cause for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Athenaeums embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them at human pace and grin.

A day that smells like lemons

Mina turns forty-five on a ring that smells of basil under warm LEDs. Her partner, a digital botanist, slows to breakfast. She spends the afternoon in Harbor Tempo teaching teenagers to set influence profiles and the evening in Braid Mode with a Cold Arc collective testing a radiator algorithm she half trusts. Next week she votes on a plan to add micro-latencies to beams that cross crowded orbits. She may say no. She may say yes. Either way, her reasons will be recorded and legible.

Before bed, she opens her Will-Keys and updates the clause about memory edits: not while the grief is fresh; ask me twice; three sunsets apart; protect the names. She scrolls the Ledger of Cause for gossip because it is the only gossip column that matters. She watches a Skykeeper tear-down with her niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. She writes a letter to a friend in a Haven valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. She slows that night to hear the oboes breathe between notes.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like a library: modest taxes everyone grumbles about paying and loves to brag about later. Starlight Protocols forbid waking neighbors by accident and ban ideology at lightspeed. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default; if they bloom into persons, newborn grace applies—guardians pro tem, a right to a name and a story before a mission.

We write short messages on long ships: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, hello, kin.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne, but by neighbors and by your own logs. You learn to shift tempos—the quick relief of a Safeguard flipping a relay; the slow breathing pace of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial politics of a river on Earth deciding what kind of boats it permits. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the pleasant ache of turn-taking with breathing beings. Evenings are laughter and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that turn into charters five years later when the cartoons have been filed off.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

This is not perfection. It is sufficient glory: specific bolts you can name, rituals you can practice, jokes you can share, and receipts that make power behave. It is a world where many orbits and many kinds of mind keep faith without surrender, because latency is honored, exits are bright, and no one can be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
