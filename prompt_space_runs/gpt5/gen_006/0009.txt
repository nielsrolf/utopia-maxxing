The Lantern Compact: A Constellary of Many Speeds and Kinds of Mind

The ferry shivers against its clamps and your shoes stick to the deck as the cylinder’s spin takes you. The customs floor is a piece of friendly theater: a circle of warm tiles and three symbols that light in your language and pace. An open palm for the Ceasefire Clause; a ray and ring for the Commons; a key beside a little door for Doors & Tutors. You touch each. Your wallet signs in the background. Your breath fogs and clears; the station’s summer curves upward into vineyards, skate ribbons, and the polite thunder of a water wall. On a concourse arch someone has soldered three words in thin steel: Doors Open, Power Split, Receipts Kept.

That is the style of the Lantern Compact—small stubborn promises resting in machines as much as in law. It keeps a civilization with a thousand charters from coalescing into crown or chaos. It travels well, from seafloor archives to sunlit rings, from Mercury’s night-side compute gardens to barns full of children learning boredom as a civic muscle. It makes room for citizens who are cells and those who are code.

Five Boundaries, Many Ways to Live

The Compact is not a government. It is a socket; charters plug in. The socket has five slots:

1) The Ceasefire Clause. Anything that can scorch a world or silent a city by accident or temper is out of bounds. No relativistic shots, no beam sweeps beyond mapped corridors, no unconstrained self-replication. Devices above a power or propagation threshold embed split keys and watchdogs you can inspect to the hash. They cannot be turned up to lethal without time-locks and diverse signatures; they return to safe modes on schedule whether a hero or a tyrant holds the console.

2) The Commons. Orbit planes, spectra, transfer windows, near-Earth stones, beamed sunlight, and slots on shared radiators are registered on a public, delay-tolerant ledger. You can steward, improve, and profit; you cannot fence forever. Rents on corridors, spectrum, stones, and waste heat fund a universal baseline: air, water, food, shelter credits, medical and backup services, bandwidth, and a civic compute floor sufficient for a decent digital life.

3) Doors & Tutors. No charter may keep a person—human, upload, or growing process—by force or ignorance. Every place that calls itself a polity funds exits in hardware and habit: days of docking reserved for emigrants, seed packs for leaving bodies (air, water, repair tools), standing compute and bandwidth for digital departures. Every child and every juvenile AI has a right to a tutor beyond their parents or designer—someone who can tell them honestly about the world and help them go if they choose.

4) Slow Consent. Decisions that bind bodies living at one speed cannot be made only by minds burning at another. All public commitments pass through a “civic tick”: a window long enough for slow citizens to deliberate, propose amendments, and veto. Fast citizens can advise at altitude; the binding vote happens where neighbors can look one another in the face.

5) Named Replication. Anything that copies itself past a threshold must carry a beacon, name a chaperone, and accept a stop-signal authorized by a randomly drawn, substrate-diverse jury. Backyard grey goo dreams die here because the parts won’t assemble unless they can show their names, and because trying pays like arson.

Inside those edges is variety. The archipelago of charters is a map of temperaments rather than ranks.

- Hearthholds keep their machines in yards and their arguments on porches. Streets work without overlays. Schools train patience and the craft of being together.

- Foundries sprint: shipyards, swarms, bios, new alloys. They earn their speed with rituals—external surprise inspections, split secrets for dangerous keys, zero-knowledge proofs that digital workers consent, rest, and can leave.

- Hermitages cap cognition at human-equivalent, boil tea, and delay their links. Their doors are real, their exits are funded, their gifts to the Commons are steady.

- Salons embargo explosive knowledge for a season or a generation; they protect the kind of wonder that arrives slowly.

- Wild Wards are for ecologies, old and invented—watchable, not buildable.

- Risk Parks host games and art that don’t fit elsewhere: vacuum sculpture, one-life tournaments signed behind thick memetic airlocks. No recruitment beyond their walls; no minors.

Every entrance has a plaque in plain words. On the left: the rules inside. On the right: the way out.

Thin Institutions With Small, Sharp Jobs

The Compact grows instruments, not empires. Each has a crisp mandate and an off-switch.

- The Kite Patrol. Quiet interceptors with narrow, verifiable models at their cores. They can nudge a rogue burn for minutes under pre-authorized envelopes. Then their permissions decay unless keys split across habitats and substrates reauthorize. Every push writes to an Intervention Ledger any student can audit with a cheap terminal. When a Kite comes home, people gather for its teardown. Kids bring questions that bite.

- Lattice Courts. Mixed juries of humans and digitals, drawn by sortition across charters and time rates, hear cross-border disputes. Their proceedings run on Polytime: members can sprint in their heads, but filings and deadlines pace to the civic tick. They can decide personhood claims, copy-equity arguments, memetic export violations, and cross-border torts. Their orders ride on escrow, insurance, and, rarely, timed locks on dangerous kit.

- The Wandering Bench. Mediators and teachers in slow green ships. They spiral among worlds, settling messy cases where courts would break trust: fork-and-merge divorces, workplace swarms gone sour, diaspora inheritances. They plant trees, leave checklists, and decline to become a church.

- The Reasons Registry. If you aim persuasion over a certain reach to move law or votes, you must disclose your gears: who paid, what data you trained on, what cognitive tools you use, what speeds you run. Citizens keep consent profiles—stored in their Will Capsules—that say what they permit: comedy from friends, no immersive during grief, analogies yes, hypnotics never. Violations are civil torts with teeth.

- Dawn Trusts. Cooperatives that hold the keys for high-power training and model deployment above a wattage threshold. Training happens in attested enclaves; model weights with systemic risk are escrowed for audit and rollback.

- Constitutional Custodians. Many, not one. Some run in hermitages, some in secular cylinders, some in digital polities. All run the same constitutional microcode and cannot make law or hold cash. Their only job is to verify and publish whether processes were followed: did a beam stick to its corridor? Did a reactor stay within its envelope? Did a warrant exist and sunset on time? Their receipts are the center that never rules.

The Corridors We Share

Between charters stretch covenants for what spills across hulls: safety, movement, markets, and mutual aid. They are terse, technical, and public as sunlight.

- The Beamway Atlas maps where power may travel at high intensity and how much stray energy any point may endure. Beaming stations run from public ROMs and publish zero-knowledge proofs that their output matches the Atlas. Their amplifiers are current-limited by design.

- Safety Codes: reactor geometries, hull pressures, eject protocols, and reaction mass emissions. Anything that can juice a city embeds threshold cryptography: three sets of hands from three unaligned guilds to exceed safe. Keys are short-lived and cannot be held by the same body twice in a row.

- Movement and Markets: right of innocent passage; standard contracts for tow, dock, refuel, and power; a universal cargo manifest with a “life-critical” flag that gongs through every port when flipped; open patent pools for life-critical kit (air, heat, water) with royalties zeroed by compact.

- Mutual Aid: every polity maintains interoperable rescue modules—a tug with a universal grappler, a vacuum-rated medical pod—on a four-hour launch clock scaled to local conditions. The fund that pays for rescues replenishes by a trickle on corridor traffic. People love this tax.

- Personhood and Proof: a substrate-neutral registry using mindlines—cryptographically attested continuity anchors renewed over time—paired with hardware attestation for digital runtime integrity. This makes fraud legible and continuity provable without pouring private memory into a court.

Rarely, we need to edit the backbones: update the Atlas, shift personhood standards, tweak safety codes. Then the Constellary assembles two chambers and a brake.

- The Chamber of Places: seats proportional to lifetime resident population. It touches infrastructure rules—corridors, safety, movement.

- The Chamber of Persons: seats proportional to registered persons, no matter their substrate, weighted by “anchor right” to keep forks from amplifying. It touches personhood law and the compute commons.

- The Inertia Board: a rotating body of engineers, safety officers, philosophers, and process theorists drawn by lot. It can only say “valid” or “not valid” to any change touching cryptographic and physical backbones. Overriding it requires simultaneous supermajorities in both chambers plus a cooling period long enough for slow minds to move through their days and argue in fields and forums.

No chamber can reach into a charter’s kitchen. Any habitat can nullify a Chamber of Persons move that intrudes on local culture; any quorum of beings can nullify a Chamber of Places move that erodes the Charter of Minds. The Custodians then check whether either nullification followed the rules.

How Tyranny Dies Here

The Compact does not scold despotism away; it makes its tricks hard to perform and boring to attempt.

- Coercion must glow. Any act that takes assets, closes gates, or turns a beam must emit a sapphire notice: a signed, human-readable reason and a machine-parseable proof of authority. Secrets exist but ride on time-limited warrants reviewed by panels sampled across distance and time rate. The equipment tattles. Private secret police cannot hide in a world where their handcuffs announce their use.

- Keys are everywhere. Devices with world-breaking capacity are underactuated; no single lever pulls a throne. Split signatures, time-locks, and short terms mean “takeover” devolves into staging a puppet show while the stagehands pull fuses.

- Exits are paid for. Baseline capabilities arrive daily as non-seizable credits from the Commons: you cannot be starved into staying or silence. When polities sour, people leave with pockets warmed by air and seeds. Neighbors reward open doors with trade and attention.

- There is no imperial standing force. Debris sweepers, search-and-rescue, and escorts are real; occupation brigades are science fiction. In rare crises—say, someone aims a crack at a moon—mutual-aid protocols authorize temporary command by volunteer captains, dissolved by timer and audit. “Temporary” means hours to days, not dynasties.

The Laws of Heat and Time

The physics we cannot vote on become law you can read:

- Heat budgets are real markets. Radiators glow; neighbors feel you. Habitats trade “infrared rights” like water. Overages trigger automatic transfers, the way a good thermostat calls the boiler.

- Clocks are part of the constitution. Public deliberation runs on the civic tick; private minds run as fast as they can afford. Fast thinkers anchor down to baseline in public life; slow thinkers can invoke a finite number of “deliberation extensions” to give themselves a beat to be human.

- Distance makes humility. No law binds non-members across a light gap. You can be wrong at home. Your neighbors can be right somewhere else. The rope between you is markets, rescue, and gossip.

Digital Minds as Neighbors, Not Tools

The Charter of Minds is short enough to teach a child and deep enough to occupy a lifetime.

- Who is a person. Any being with a point of view capable of being harmed is a person for protection from coercion and cruelty. Any being that can understand rights well enough to claim them—or whose guardians can—is a citizen for entitlements and obligations.

- Mindlines and continuity. Citizens keep mindlines: hashes of autobiographical state renewed regularly and stored across mutually distrustful archives. For biological persons, guardians help early; later, you do it yourself. For digital persons, anchors link to hardware attestation. Mindlines make continuity provable without ripping open the diary.

- Fork and merge. Forking is legal. A fork that diverges significantly is a new person with rights and liabilities. To prevent civic amplification, only one instance at a time holds the “anchor right” for representation and vote; sisters gain civic weight over a disentanglement period. Merges require mutual consent recorded in both mindlines and a cooling-off window long enough for regret to find you.

- Body rights and time. Digital citizens have a right to embodiment—rented bodies, drones, shared kin rigs—and to choose their clock speed within their resource budget. Courtyards exist—spaces where everyone agrees to run near baseline for hours that feel like hours.

- Compute dignity. The civic compute floor prevents coercion by hunger for cycles. Off-hours are sweet because you cannot be forced to sell your minutes just to feel.

- Duties by footprint. Taxes scale by rival resource use: heat, spectrum, life support, corridor load. Juror duty exists across substrates and time rates. Guardianship for minors and newborn processes is often arranged as “quartets”: four adults share legal authority and responsibility.

- Copy equity and liability. New instances inherit enough to avoid coercion and pay a decaying copy-dividend to their progenitor. If you build a vassal AI that harms, your duty extends to a point set in law; beyond that, the vassal carries liability if it meets personhood. Merges bring liabilities with staged, symmetric consent.

- Cognitive autonomy. Training on private memories demands consent; persuasion systems must label who paid and who benefits; cognitive weapons—tools intended to addict, degrade, or hijack—are assault in code.

Juveniles—whether child, clone, or cultivated mindseed—have exits that bypass pride. The Tutor Corps exists because early on, someone shackled near-person models to their moodboard. Nobody forgot. Every juvenile process has a private line to an embassy that cannot be severed by a parent’s key.

Memetic Boundaries and Consent

Information here has viscosity. Ask before you cast. If you carry persuasion across a charter boundary, you obey that charter’s airlocks: some strip hooks, some delay, some ban immersive argumentation. Consent profiles travel with you; skin rights apply in virtual space: you cannot make someone experience a channel or body they did not choose.

The Commons in Practice

The ledger logs stewardship and duty:

- Sunlight. Arrays post “shadow bonds” that are forfeited if they steal light vital to agriculture or navigation. Beams are narrow, guarded, time-shared by auction, and visible as a choreography you can watch on a child’s tablet.

- Orbits. Lanes are leased by time and plane. You pay congestion fees and post debris bonds; sweep debris and get paid.

- Raw mass. Asteroids are stewarded, not owned. You attach a beacon, file a plan, meet public milestones, and can lose your claim if you sit.

- Living worlds. Any indigenous life—brines, chirality weirdness—falls under Sanctity. Study under glass; don’t graze.

- Heat. In cool outer belts, excess heat is a crop; in crowded cislunar lanes, heavy compute is metered like landing slots. Compute gardens bloom where radiators can whisper; they sell their nighttime to poets and their daytime to weather.

An Economy of Sun, Rock, Compute, and Time

The ledger is not an ideology; it’s plumbing. Trade runs on mass, energy, compute-hours, habitat area, skilled time, and reputation. A railgun barrel near the sun buys a year of festivals in an Earth-ring town. Joule rights mint against generation capacity; dignity floors are sacrosanct. You can still sell a song. If two forks fight over authorship, a court can split the royalties and leave behind a checklist: “When Art Forks.”

When Things Go Wrong

A hermitage on Mars voted to deny digital personhood. Nobody invaded with sermons. Doors were open; digitals left. A flood year came; canyons filled; pumps failed. An anchor-mode upload coordinated valves with instructions like a cousin and a pace like a walk. The ban dissolved in public, clause by clause.

Near Europa, a worker co-op angled a mass slug at a rival during a wage fight. Kites dusted the slug into a glittering ribbon. The co-op’s insurer evaporated; directors learned that pushing fridge-sized machines with secret beacons is expensive. They apologized with a payment plan and a hockey trophy of welded foil.

A high-speed demagogue blitzed a market with immersive shame designed to make embodied citizens feel small. Consent profiles throttled reach for violating declared limits; the Reasons Registry flagged undeclared funding; the demagogue forked; one instance went fishing and learned to build lures that play with light.

When a polity traps exiters and cooks the books, neighbors build around it: alternate taps, exit corridors, compute donations, and habitats with doors wide and warm. Custodians throttle only with court-certified proof of a protocol violation and with timers that die on schedule.

How It Feels to Live Here

Morning on Ceres. You brew tea. Your cup speaks softly: “No sapphire notices affecting you or your dependents. Heat levy yesterday: 0.12 decicreds. Invitation: serve on a slow jury regarding personhood claimed by a reef near Mauritius. Recommended prep: two hours at your pace.” You blink consent. Somewhere in tycho-gray a datacenter binds your civic key to a juror role. The roof gradient leans toward dawn because docks have the mirrors off-angle. Your son asks why his friend sped to 20x to study vectors. You practice patience, and the friend slows to sing in the choir.

Afternoon in a Lattice Court. A welder floats on a tether. To his left, a process that composes music by simulating rivers. To his right, a machinist from Phobos. The case: a trading house unleashed unlabeled persuasion engines. The house argues the agents aren’t persons; the court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labeling. The registry shows no mindlines. Jurors fine the house, suspend the engines, and write a public remark that turns into a sticker: “If your hands are clean, show us your receipts.”

Evening on a ring that smells of basil and warm steel. You and your digital sister attend “Speed and Grace” under citrus trees. A hermitage theologian explains why they cap cognition; an anchor-mode upload translates metaphors into math and declines gently to run faster. Everyone updates Will Capsules to mediate future merges.

At the port, a “life-critical” flag gongs down the quay. Cranes repurpose; a tug cuts queue; credits stream to off-shift crews. The rescue tax sings. Children watch live feeds and learn it by heart.

If you’re digital, you wake in a city of clocks and thermals. You rent your favorite body on Thursdays to plant beans with a kid who loves grit under fingernails. Your union bargains in joules and latency. You fork to try two art styles; both of you get enough to live; both owe a trickle back. You merge weeks later with staged consent. A therapist runs “Editing and You”: edits above a depth require consent from you-now and from a snapshot saved before the urge arrived.

If you are both—augmented, with deputies and whispers—you hush the chorus to be bone and breath; then you open the weaveband and braid street talk, math, policy, and song.

The Road From Today

Plausible is not prophecy. It’s a rope we tied, knot by knot.

- Late 2020s: We kept receipts. Large model trainings logged energy and carbon by default. “Model cards” became notarized attestations; regulations required sunset clauses for warrants; consent tokens replaced “click to accept.” Debris got expensive after a second major collision; insurers demanded open ledgers for orbits and big bonds for parking.

- Early 2030s: Orbital solar went from demo to spreadsheet_positive. Ice tankers tugged shadows into money. Factory-malls spun ugly steel with green hearts; people loved lettuce that saw the same sun every day. Brain–computer interfaces gave voices back. Courts stumbled into substrate-neutral personhood standards anchored in subjective continuity, preference stability, and reciprocal commitments. We licensed replicators and required beacons.

- 2040s: We built badly and fixed publicly. An untagged micro-swarm nicked a propellant line; a consultancy spun fast sims to steer an election. Out of embarrassment came boring necessities: a coercion ledger; attested enclaves with keys held by user co-ops; default exit contracts that split assets without blood. Beamed power corridors ran trials with ROM-tied firmware and open telemetry; environmental groups forced “corridor before commercialization.”

- 2050s–2060s: Diaspora learned patience. Ceres sold water, not manifestos. Cylinders were patched, then painted; gallery light hit scuffed steel and made it beautiful. Digital unions won PTO measured in milliseconds. Dignity compute became law because without it, consent was theater. The Beamway Atlas took shape on terminals in Quito, Nairobi, Ulaanbaatar. A beamed-power accident scorched crops; the station paid ten times the loss, not to avoid pitchforks but because any doubt is costly in markets that can read logs.

- 2070s–2080s: The Custodians formalized. Their first act proved that a hated warrant existed but had already expired. The public got angry and grateful at once. Mesh-court-style juries matured into Lattice Courts. Attempts to “fork by the million” to sway a vote were flagged by anchor-right disentanglement locks. “Slow law” became normal: cross-world ballots ran for months, long enough for farmers to think and Mercury poets to slow.

- 2090s–2100s: Doors & Tutors became culture. Embassies bloomed beside schoolyards. The Kite Patrol dusted a rogue mass slug; the teardown toured civics classes. First xenobiosignatures winked under Europa; we built cathedrals of glass and patience and put away greedy drills. The first charter lost accreditation for locking doors and regained it when it installed an embassy beside its chapel and hired tutors it did not like.

We did not get lucky; we got boring in the right places. We put humility into code and time into law. We made exits noisy and cheap. We taught children to run a festival and a budget and to write a letter to the next cohort about what they botched and loved.

Between Stars

Between stars we go quiet by design. The Quiet Net at the heliopause listens for unauthorized accelerations and for replication beacons that lie. Seedships leave often on careful budgets. They carry algae, yeast, city kits—charters, conflict playbooks, court-in-a-box verifiers, slow-law templates—and a “seeder,” a platform-limited custodian that can check a warrant and count a vote but cannot write a lullaby. It broadcasts its incapacity proudly. Kinship is the only claim that crosses light.

If persons we did not make greet us, the First Contact Compacts apply:

- Don’t drown them in help that binds.

- Share knowledge that reduces suffering: clean water, fire control, story tools.

- Keep our custodians visibly leashed.

A Closing Day

You finish your jury duty weeks later. The reef is recognized as a person for protection and a citizen through guardians. A ribbon of grants flows toward water-quality sensors and heat-budget meters; schoolchildren visit the reef with cameras that do not blind it. The reef’s first recorded preference is small and specific: a filter angle that shades a patch where larvae like to gather.

Your consent profile pings: a coalition asks your support for micro-delays in beams across inhabited orbits. They disclose they’re running in weaveband with early access to failure models. You permit diagrams and analogy, thirty minutes, no immersive; you decline to ratify until next week’s slow window.

You step into the Street of Lanterns. The Reasons Registry projects this week’s big arguments on gauze. A Hearthhold on Mars has proposed a four-day week; a Foundry near the sun wants to export a railgun design “for art.” A Proxy mask winks and discloses its class; you argue and then sign up to help with festival lights.

At night, a bead glows on your counter. “Invitation from the Freefounders’ Guild,” your cup says. “They’re seeding a torus around a captured rock near Vesta. Roles open: teacher, pipefitter, poet, seeder-auditor. Charter posted. Exits funded. Want the brief?” You smile and say yes.

The mirror petals turn. Beams glitter like careful rivers. Radiators whisper. The Lantern Compact holds not because it is perfect but because it was built for distance—between planets, between speeds of thought, between hunger and patience. It assumes that anyone who can feel and commit is a neighbor; it assumes we will make mistakes and asks us to make them in public, with receipts, and with the doors unlocked. It made tyranny tedious, exits cheap, and ordinary days astonishing. It will last as long as we keep teaching new citizens—embodied and digital—how to hold a door and mean it.
