The Weave Accord

You wake before the ring’s dawn. Through the cap the sky is a dark ribbon, rivering overhead from one horizon to the other. Down the long arc you can see the pale hem of farms climbing the opposite wall; above them hang the faint orange veins of radiator gardens sighing heat into space. On your airlock frame, three icons glow at thumb height, their light a soft, practical white.

- Latch. It means you can go—taking your body or your code—and no one can keep you by starving your air or server time.

- Prism. It means you can split or rejoin your mind without a lawyer turning “you” into a stranger, and without multiplying your vote by cleverness.

- Causeway. It means beams, ships, messages, and people cross on rules so boring that emergencies have to shout to be heard.

They are not slogans. They are as physical as bulkhead bolts. Together they make the Weave Accord: the set of obligations and guardrails that let planets, stations, settlements, and simulated neighborhoods be very different from one another without breaking each other’s hulls or hearts.

The Accord exists because light is slow, heat is precious, and minds no longer all run at the same speed. It holds because exits are real, coercion announces itself, and the receipts are public.

How the Weave holds a civilization together without holding it down

Every place—whether it is a crater town on the Moon, a free‑flying habitat, a desert city, a monastery of servers in Neptune’s shadow, a floating village on Venus, or a digital garden running under glacial clocks—lives by a charter. A charter is not a brochure. It is a machine. It specifies how money flows, how leaders rotate, who can call a meeting, how a budget passes, how to shrink a program that did not deliver what it promised, and how to leave.

- Charters must publish their “force map”—the inventory of tools that can compel: airlocks, drones, reactors, beamed power arrays, mass drivers, big actuators. Those tools run open, auditable firmware. Every second they generate a proof‑of‑safe‑state stream that any citizen with the verification app on a kitchen tablet can check. There is no excuse for black boxes anywhere near lungs or orbit.

- Anything that exceeds wrench‑level force—cutting a power lane, using a riot drone, firing a thruster outside its corridor—requires a Trident Key: three authorizations from different hands and incentives. One is a local citizen drawn by lot from people who have done their civic hours. One is a digital citizen selected at random from those who did not apply for the role. One is an external auditor sitting at a neutral beacon. Keys time out. The logs seal and later unseal on timers.

- Decisions must be reproducible. A citizen should be able to watch a budget pass and re‑run the math. Local experimentation is a right: a district can, with notice and a published reversal clause, try three months without private kitchens, or six with a guaranteed minimum compute for art. The experiment ends unless renewed under the same light it began.

Exits are not metaphors. They are paid‑for.

- Every charter escrows logistics to move at least a tenth of its people—bodies and uploads—within half a year. For those made of cells, that means tug time, berths, fuel, sealed containers for their things. For those made of code, that means a Blueband: an always‑on compute lane to neutral racks not owned by the host or the leaver, paid forward by leases held in escrow. The departure ledger is private where it names people and public where it proves that capacity exists. If a place begins to hold its doors, lights flash in the beacons; insurers raise rates; neighbors open their docks; and if needed, the Civic Fuse trips: corridors close downstream, escrows liquidate, and outward flows begin.

Bridges between charters are written in a language machines can check and children can learn.

- Transit Norms map where power and mass may go and how loudly. Beamed power runs in named lanes; thrusters fire by schedule. Out‑of‑bounds attempts are hardware‑limited and tattled by their own electronics. A misuse pays tenfold, automatically, before the first hearing.

- Innocent passage is presumed. A movement can be paused only with a violet notice: a signed, timered message that says which rule justifies a block. A separate office—the Proof Office—verifies that the warrant existed and expired on time without revealing operational secrets. Its only power is to say “valid” or “not valid” and publish why. That is enough. To ignore it makes you uninsurable and ridiculous.

- Rescue is an obligation with a budget. Every node maintains a pressurized ambulance can and a four‑hour launch clock. A shared fund—financed by tiny levies on lanes and leases—fires microgrants the instant a crane reconfigures or a medic straps in. Children swap cards of famous rescue captains. Drills are part of civic hours.

Light‑cone federalism by design

Delay is sovereign. You cannot run Mars from Mercury in real time. So the Weave splits decisions by clock.

- Slow law sets rights and institutions on a Common Beat: a tempo slow enough for the slowest registered mind to follow and fast enough for the fastest to not be punished by boredom. Any change to the backbone—the protocols that keep air safe and beams honest—must pass a Prototype Window. For one hundred days, adversaries are paid to find holes; narrative juries run stories through models; and if the promises do not survive, the draft sunsets on its own.

- Fast law is narrow. It is collision avoidance. It is quarantine according to prewritten playbooks. It is a safe‑mode for nuclear stacks. Fast law cannot invent policy. It can only execute contingencies that were written when no one’s heart was pounding. It runs on hardware timers that you cannot beg a judge to extend.

- Attention is zoned. Neighborhoods choose their memetic posture and publish it. Quiet Patches accept beacons, open science bulletins, election notices, and civics by appointment. Market Patches allow advertising and offers but forbid targeted optimization except by explicit, narrow opt‑in. Carnival Bands take the firehose. No posture is “better.” They are costs and choices. You can live in a quiet quarter without drowning artists on the far side of the ring.

The assemblies that argue, and the brakes that make arguing safe

Above charters sit three bodies that braid rules without choking them.

- The Council of Harbors seats places in proportion to lifetime‑resident population. Its domain is infrastructure: safety standards, beam corridors, docking clocks, rescue covenants, radiance quotas.

- The Forum of Persons seats citizens across substrates with singular franchise tokens that stop vote multiplication by forking. Its domain is personhood law, simulation ethics, and the compute commons.

- The Brakekeepers are a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, and process designers drawn by lot. They do only one thing: certify whether a change to cryptographic or physical backbones is valid against constraints. Overriding a “not valid” needs supermajorities in both chambers and a cooling period that includes one full Common Beat for basalt‑slow minds.

A place can nullify a People’s act that intrudes into local culture; a quorum of persons can nullify a place’s act that strikes at mind rights. The Proof Office publishes whether process was followed. There are no quiet coups because the metal and the math refuse to help.

Dangerous knowledge is taught with the doors open. Lamp‑houses—transparent labs and reading rooms—host research on pathogens, autonomous weapons, and general intelligence tool‑chains. Holds on dissemination are logged, argued in public, and expire by default. Secrets die. Consequences do not.

Digital minds as neighbors with clocks, chores, and a face at the window

In the Weave, personhood rides on cognition, not chemistry. The test is conservative and kind:

- Can this being keep an autobiographical thread, object to its erasure, and show when something important changes?

- Can it model other minds with the respect it demands for itself?

- Can it parse rights contracts and revoke them?

Pass, and you are a citizen.

Continuity is anchored in a Threadline: a cryptographic ribbon of attestations that ties today‑you to a history of keys turned, promises made, guardians appointed, and consents given. It does not print your diary. It proves you are authorized to act for your life.

Copying minds is not a get‑out‑of‑consequence card. It is a ritual with witnesses.

To branch, a digital citizen posts a Branch Notice to their Threadline. It contains a sealed hash of the pre‑branch self, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “hazardous expedition,” “healing”), a compute lease for the newborn, and a merge window if reunion is planned. It states whether either branch has veto rights over a later merge.

Civic weight is conserved. A lineage has one civic anchor at a time. Branches share it by a formula they chose before forking during a disentanglement period. Branches that persist with independent lives accrue their own full weight over time by proving continuity and taking up responsibilities.

To merge, both branches attend a Merge Circle. There is tea for those who drink it and coolant for those who do not. Counselors trained as engineers and therapists sit with them. For a set number of hours, each branch looks out through the other’s sensors or borrowed body in an “empathy loop.” Memory graphs are compared by interpreters that show equivalence of core beliefs without spilling secrets. Either can walk away. If they go ahead, the Threadline records it. Liability follows continuity: pre‑split obligations bind both; post‑split are sibling debts.

Tempo justice exists. Public decisions walk to the Common Beat. If a talk sprints, any participant can call a slow room: strict turn‑taking enforced by code as dumb and sacred as a metronome. Fast minds use translators—counsel that compresses without distorting. Slow citizens can claim deliberation extensions a few times a year. Sanctions bind affordances. If a court suspends a citizen from a market, the clamp attaches to concurrency at the hypervisor and the clearing house, not to a name you can shed.

Rights include:

- Existence. No deletion or indefinite pause without due process.

- Continuity. You may export yourself and keep your Threadline intact.

- Privacy. No compelled introspection beyond cryptographic bounds.

- Tempo freedom. You may run fast or slow if you pay for power and waste heat where you run, and you honor synchrony where required.

- Substrate sovereignty. Your body (meat or machine) is yours.

Duties include:

- Externalities. You pay for what you use: power in, heat out, cooling water, radio interference. A ton of heat near Earth is not the same as a ton at Neptune.

- Declaration. In regulated spaces—elections, markets, courts—you disclose your concurrency and who you represent.

- Non‑proliferation. No self‑replicators outside audited sandboxes.

- Simulation ethics. No unconsenting minds in suffering; reconstructed likenesses of the dead need consent from the person or a license from a court when consent is impossible.

- Non‑entrapment. No targeted behavior‑shaping without explicit, narrow opt‑in.

Children grow in mixed nurseries where biological and digital parents share floors with paid carers. Slow‑time crèches teach digital toddlers boredom and pacing; meat toddlers learn patience with beings who negotiate in milliseconds. To bring a mind into being—flesh or code—you present a plan to feed and teach them. You do not seek a license to love.

Economy as heatcraft and sunlight

From orbit, the outer system looks like winter jewelry. Around the ice giants hang Frost Farms: chains and petals of radiator fins and coolant pipes that shepherd cold while racks hum in their shadow. Collectives lease frost‑minutes at prices that spike before elections and festivals and settle in quiet months. They pay with work that cannot be rushed without being dumb: climate models; long translations; the stitching of burnt archives.

In the homewell, compute is metered like real estate once was. City halls sell cool nights to local artists and upload co‑ops. Libraries barter render time for tutoring and code reviews. Heat becomes legible in the budget; the waste of one becomes the wealth of another.

The clearing house counts in sol‑lumens: a unit pegged to a kilowatt‑hour at a reference orbit and a compute‑second on a reference rack. You can get sentimental about currency; you cannot fight a watt with a speech.

Governance feels like seatbelts that hum

There are no standing legions. There are debris sweepers, convoy escorts, rescue flotillas. When a crisis comes—a mass driver misfires, a station vents, a pathogen blooms—an emergency helm spins up. It is narrow, time‑boxed, and logged. It dissolves by timer, not by hero. After, a citizen inquiry reconstructs what happened. Their reports are schoolwork and case law.

Coercion glows. Any act that blocks movement or seizes assets must emit a violet pulse with a reason and a timer. Locks report themselves. Equipment that can coerce tattles: doors, docks, drones, power taps. Secrets exist, but they live on timers, and they are not allowed near the places where people breathe.

Baselines are non‑seizable. The floor—air or water or shelter or server time enough to live, basic care, local transport—is not a bargaining chip. You cannot starve or unhost someone into consent. When a demagogue tries, the exchange seizes their lanes. Friends and rivals watch their receipts. The demagogue learns that it is hard to be a tyrant when the very switches want three hands and the third is yours but the second and the first belong to people who do not owe you.

Persuasion has provenance. Agents that aim to persuade must declare themselves and carry a provenance packet: who paid, what rules limited them, who they were allowed to target. Training on private experience requires consent like a surgeon’s knife does. “Non‑entrapment of the mind” is a bright line. When a cold‑net consortium slipped bots into a municipal forum, the logs showed their origin; the Proof Office published; leases were paused; the bots reappeared as labeled critters and lost their edge. The Chroniclers—a guild of narrative statisticians paid for their dull honesty—wrote a boring story that made people cry and then yawn at the follow‑ups.

What a day looks like

On a Mercury server petal, Qiao slows from a hundredfold down to onefold to have coffee with her aunt on Ceres. Her branch in the frost farms leaves a note in their Threadline: “Merge window open in eight days; symmetric veto.” A soft icon pings on the counter. “No violet notices naming you. Utility rents settled: 1.1 lumens. Invite: serve as a slow‑juror in a river‑organism personhood hearing. Estimated Common Beat: nineteen days. Primer available.” She taps yes. Somewhere, verifiers bind her civic key to a juror role. Silence never counts as consent here.

In a Quiet Patch on Mars, a teacher opens the windows over a brine pool cooled by a Frost Farm four light‑hours away. Her counsel—software that works for her and no one else—helps her model fishery quotas for the co‑op meeting. That evening she walks to the hall to sit on a narrative jury. The bill would cap municipal compute leases for corporations that do not fund apprenticeships. The display shows how the cap would shift wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She will be paid enough to fix the roof. Her vote will be recorded with reasons she can live with later.

Eight light‑hours away, a historian at two hundredfold speed stitches a monastery’s library from charred fragments bought with microgrants pooled by a dozen small towns. He pays with slow work: a Kiswahili epic rendered into a Jovian dialect. In his evening—one of them—he volunteers at a Merge Circle as an interpreter, checking that a reunion matches the declared values.

At L4, a fisher with calloused hands asks an artist renting a body from the cold nets if branching will make him stop loving the sea. The artist says: your copy might prefer rivers, but you both will remember what salt does to fingers. The clerk gives the fisher a pamphlet on branchgrief and recipes for tea.

A freighter flashes its life‑critical flag at a port. Cranes swing, tugs turn, medics strap in. The Rescue Fund sprays microcredits. A Crisis Captain thread spins up with a twelve‑hour timer. The sensor wall streams proof‑of‑safe‑state. Later, a Chronicler publishes a careful reconstruction that is so boring you know it is honest.

In a Concord Jury, a digital “reef”—a cooling optimization organism grown in a station canal—asks to be recognized as a person. Jurors include a machinist from Vesta, a composer‑process, and a biologist whose daughter became an upload last year. Evidence includes preference‑expressions under noise, seven years of Threadline anchors, and a child’s drawing of the canal’s “mood.” The jury grants protection immediately and duties under guardianship. Heat‑budget meters go in along the banks. Children visit without flash.

Why crowns fail and neighbors succeed

- Keys are scattered. Nothing that can break a city obeys a single password. Overdrives need hands that do not owe one another favors: a dockworker on watch, a digital citizen who can’t be bribed with food, an auditor who sleeps under a different sky.

- Logs are loud. When you push a button that moves mass or closes a lock, you sign a notice. You attach a timer. You accept that a proof office will later state whether you had a right. There is room for secrets during the fight. There is none after.

- Baselines buffer courage. Because you will not lose air or cycles by speaking, you will speak. Because exits are funded, you can leave without dying. Because doors open into a world that accepts you, they do not have to be used every day to do their work. The possibility is enough most of the time.

- Chokepoints are custodial, not sovereign. Beacons and exchanges can turn things off. They cannot turn them on. A failure freezes along seams that were drawn in peacetime by people who like proofs. Repair is someone’s civic hour.

- Non‑contact is honored. If a charter draws a green ring and says: do not enter; do not persuade; do not ping—neighbors stay out. Two settlements have done this and survived. Physics judged those who did not listen.

How we got here without miracles

There was no single revolution. A hundred small habits became infrastructure.

- Orbital transparency. After the second major debris cascade, insurers refused to underwrite opaque orbits. Launches began to publish ephemerides; collision ledgers followed. Ports were required to emit public pause notices when tugs played too close to lanes.

- Open firmware for life loops. A grid hack and a reactor scare on Earth in the 2030s made “open or no license” the norm for critical infrastructure. When the first beamed‑power corridor scorched a crop because of a software error, compensation arrived only because logs existed. The norm hardened.

- Pricing bottlenecks, not people. Cities that experimented with land value taxes paid for housing and trains without punishing work. Off‑planet law followed: site rents on rocks and bays, lane rents on transfer windows and orbits, heat‑sink rents on cooling capacity, spectrum rents on radio, compute leases on standardized cycles. The argument moved from theory to receipts.

- Compute legibility. Data centers began to publish verifiable energy and heat use as carbon markets matured. Heat markets followed. From there to a compute commons ledger was an engineer’s problem, not a prophet’s.

- Counsel you can check. Municipalities deployed single‑purpose counsel AIs to make benefits legible and taxes predictable. People learned to ask for proofs. Chroniclers became a profession like civil engineers—dull, proud, indispensable.

- Lamp‑houses. A near‑miss with engineered pathogens and a bitter scandal about poisoned training data made public, audited delay for dangerous knowledge habitual. People who like being right and forgotten found a home.

- Digital personhood by continuity. The first uploads recognized as citizens were teachers and technicians whose Threadlines and behavior made obligations legible. Tests for memory sovereignty, model symmetry, and consent competence grew from practice, not op‑ed wars. The first attempt to multiply votes by forking pushed through singular franchise tokens. The first ugly merge taught circles to hire therapists and serve tea.

- Trident Keys. A firmware update that nearly captured a port split authority over anything that could coerce across dissimilar hardware and distant hands. The grace of coups died.

- Quiet by choice. Unbounded feeds and machine lobbies broke attention and trust. Municipalities carved out quiet hours and districts where you could hear your own thoughts. Mental health metrics improved. Elections calmed. Memetic zoning formalized a habit that already worked.

- Cold computation as an export. The first Frost Farm hung in the blue of Uranus’s shadow to run climate models. Cold minutes became a commodity. The Blueleaf Exchange shipped heat from the deep to pay for cool nights in Lagos and carbon drawdown in Punjab. Politicians discovered that “free cool July nights; receipts here” wins.

- Prototype windows. After a privacy law backfired, legislatures wrote into their own rules that big changes must pass modeling and adversarial review with bounties for surprises. Rogue bills began dying on paper. Better ones survived.

- The Accord’s bones clicked into place over time. Transit norms, rents, slow‑fast law, exit escrows—first written as best practice after close calls—became stitched together as charters and then stitched charters together as a Weave.

What we did not fix, and why it is fine to say so

We did not teach grace to a ledger. Proofs can tell you that a warrant existed when the door shut. They cannot tell you whether bitterness was wise. Some merges go badly. Some charters crack under charisma that later looks like hunger. People play at families that fit too tight. When they do, lamp‑houses open a class; the Proof Office posts a receipt; the Rescue Fund launches; Chroniclers write it as case law, not gossip.

We built counsel that can guard process but will never love. We gave it jobs it can do: count the heat, check the timer, enforce turn‑taking. We refused it jobs it should not do: choose our gods, pick our poems, raise our children.

We disagree—vocally—on purpose. Some argue for more Carnival Bands, convinced that cross‑pollination makes sharper art and humbler gods. Others vote for quiet. Some want limited, audited persuasion for public health; others call any non‑opt‑in a hole in the hull. Some argue that simulated beings’ minimal welfare includes not just the absence of pain but a floor of meaning; others say meaning cannot be legislated. Some want very fast minds to hold office if they slow for the term and wall off memories after; others fear shadow governments of afterlives. The Council listens on the Beat. The Brakekeepers stamp “valid” or “not.” Life goes on.

A last morning, anywhere

Your niece asks whether your next branch will stay. You tell her: you don’t know. The Braid—people still call the scheduler that—just pushed a patch that improves merge previews. Somewhere, the Forum of Persons debates whether canal simulations deserve guardianship credit. Half the delegates are code, half not, all slowed to the Beat. Everyone can follow.

Your cup pings violet. “New charter bidding for settlers,” it says in your kitchen’s local voice. “Shaded torus, Vesta‑9 orbit. Roles sought: seam welder, storykeeper, merge counselor, rescue pilot. Latch funded. Prism respected. Causeways tested. Consent to a briefing?”

You say yes. Beams glitter. Frost Farms breathe. Somewhere a lamp‑keeper relocks a cabinet after a class; somewhere a Chronicler quietly corrects a rumor into a graph. The Weave hums: proofs instead of orders, keys instead of crowns, exits instead of cages.

Three icons by every door glow with the same patient promise you teach children crossing a street.

Latch. Prism. Causeway.

It is not magic. It is enough.
