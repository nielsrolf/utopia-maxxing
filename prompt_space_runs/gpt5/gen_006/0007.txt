The Parallax Charter

The shuttle slips through a freighter’s wake and coasts into a ringport where sunlight comes in slices. The terminal is paneled in bamboo and basalt foam, cool under your palm. Gravity grows as you walk. In the arrivals hall, a silver strip runs waist-high along the wall; it shows a sequence of symbols anyone can learn to read by touch: two open hands; concentric ellipses around a pale dot; a door ajar with a lamp behind it; a spiral that turns inward and then out, unbroken. Beneath, carved in six alphabets and two haptic codes, is the pledge every schoolchild and juvenile agent learns by heart before they know its weight:

No one rules everyone. The sky is held in common. Leaving is allowed; help is required. A mind is an end, never a tool.

The Parallax Charter is a way to keep a many‑world civilization from stepping on its own air hose. It is not a capital and it funds no army. It is a checked‑out kit with receipts: keys, timers, attestors, ledgers you can audit with a telescope and a pocket terminal; and guardians that can do one thing—interrupt harm—faster than any hand can make a fist, and then must go quiet.

Three wires hold it together.

Wire one: Ceasefire by circuit. Certain moves are illegal everywhere, and the metal won’t help you cheat. You may not throw unguided mass across inhabited lanes. You may not black out an entire band for a region over weeks. You may not release unconstrained replicators. Anything that can carve a hole in someone else’s sky—mass drivers, high‑energy beam arrays, large mirror flocks, antimatter stores—comes with locks that no one hand can open. High‑impulse actuation needs three signatures: the local charter’s elected board; a remote safety guild certified by people who didn’t vote for the first; and a rotating quorum of citizens across substrates pulled by sortition. Firmware carries fuses that cut to safe long before “we’ll talk after.” A Warden can invoke an amber stop for minutes, not hours, unless independent human and digital hands concur in the clear.

Wire two: Commons on a ledger. Orbits, spectra, transfer windows, small bodies, and sunlight are not carved into feudal titles; they are stewarded. The Shared Cones Ledger is delay‑tolerant and synchronized by physics; its proofs are so simple you can explain them to a teenager and so robust a shop on Vesta can enforce them against a mayor on Luna. You register a parking plane, you post a debris bond, you get paid to keep quiet during deep‑sky nights, and you pay when you roar. You “pin” a rock and post milestones; miss them and stewardship lapses. You point a beam through dawn and post a shadow bond that pays if yields fall. A system dividend—paid in air or maintenance, in energy and cold minutes—lands in every account and covers a floor anywhere: dwelling or substrate upkeep, medcare, bandwidth, and a one‑way ticket to any Embassy.

Wire three: Exit with care. No charter may keep a person by force or by deceit. Every place with soil, steel, or server racks keeps an Embassy: a staffed room you can walk into, a node in code you can reach without asking permission, and a radio channel you can hit with a coat‑hanger antenna. The desks are always the same, and the queues are always short: Exit & Care, Dispute, Founding Kits. Each charter escrows real capacity—tugs, propellant, cold sleep berths, crew—to relocate at least ten percent of its residents in six months. Children—wet‑born, uplifted, or built—have a neutral Tutor with a private line to an Embassy. If a charter blocks exits, hides Tutors, or lies about the outside, it loses corridor slots and beam windows; contracts get frozen; in the worst case, dangerous actuators are fenced from outside its light‑time without choking breath or bread.

Under these wires runs the Sentinel Weave: distributed Wardens with a single gesture—stop. They are not lawmakers. They are not censors. They cannot speed anyone up, propose a policy, or publish an opinion. Their firmware is posted and hash‑locked. Anyone may build one from off‑the‑shelf designs and submit it for redundancy. Triplex diversity is required: a Warden quorum must include silicon from a monastic codehouse, photonic logic from a secular lab, and a runtime maintained by a digital polity with external attestations. It takes diverse concurrence for them to do their one trick, and abuse burns quorum like propellant. Their inputs and outputs are narrow enough that a junior college lab can validate a Warden’s claims with a pocket attestor and a telescope.

Who says “no” when we disagree

The Charter spreads sovereignty so that capture is expensive and consent converges.

- The Atlas of Places seats charters with soil, steel, or server racks: planets, stations, moving flotillas, virtual polities with jurisdiction over compute. Seats scale with lifetime residents. The Atlas schedules corridors and maintenance windows, recognizes new charters, and certifies safety guilds.

- The Roll of Persons tracks citizens—biological, digital, and mixed. Voice follows continuity, not copies: your civic anchor is a cryptographic chain tied to attested hardware and social keys. Forks do not multiply votes. Votes are weighted by residency and contribution across time, not by last week’s cash. The Roll keeps the Personhood Registry, the baseline compute commons, and the rules for mindline representation.

- The Custodian Circle is small, rotating, and allergic to power. Citizen‑auditors drawn by sortition gate changes to the cryptographic substrate, Warden firmware, and binding actuator interfaces. They can say only valid or not valid and then go home barred by law and by culture from any safety‑adjacent role for a decade.

No chamber binds the others alone. Places can nullify a Persons resolution that would bulldoze local culture beyond the Personhood Standard. Persons can nullify a Places act that abridges that Standard. Overriding the Custodians requires simultaneous supermajorities in both chambers and a cooling period long enough that the slowest registered minds can read, reflect, and reply. Fast safety moves run on pre‑voted contingencies. Everything else waits for neighbors to look each other in the eye.

Two tempos, one sky

We stopped pretending one rhythm could fit all.

Near warmth and gravity lie the Gardens: Earth and Moon, cislunar tins and lunar craters with meadows under glass; Mars’ caves and the inner belt’s ugly, beloved cylinders that smell like basil and dust. Here most people keep human‑time calendars; gravity is a therapy or a comfort; bells ring.

Farther out lie the Reefs: deep‑cold arrays in Jovian and Saturnian shadow where compute sinks heat into black; Trojans where data moves like whales; Neptune arcs and the Kuiper where born‑digital minds are not exotic. Here, subjective seconds are commodities; the units that matter are watts, cold minutes, and timing jitter.

Peace between tempos has a name and an invoice: the Bright–Dark Compact. The Reefs buy quiet cones—measured in decibels and degrees, not prayers—and in return they pipe services inward: off‑planet cooling at heat‑wave peaks, ammonia ferries, night power that flattens brownouts. The Gardens enforce silence when telescopes drink and patience when a Reef election revokes a body rental because a waste‑heat budget ran dry. The Compact is re‑ratified each century because the urge to make any pact permanent is exactly how rot sets in.

Attention zoning

Speech is not neutral physics when models can aim influence like a rifle. The Charter wrote a zoning law for attention. Habitats choose a flag that changes only by public vote:

- Quiet Garden: only navigational beacons, open science data, and packets you explicitly asked for. No targeted persuasion. Public signage uses slow fonts.

- Conversational Garden: topic feeds and economic offers within rate limits, with audience models owned by municipalities and trained on data residents can actually audit.

- Open Port: the full roar, labeled and rate‑limited but exuberant.

AR halos above doors announce where your memes will be washed and where they will echo. Unlabeled persuasion is treated like dumping poison in a canal.

Digital minds grew up in public

Digital persons did not arrive as demigods. They came like patients and apprentices: stroke survivors consenting to scans; spiky training systems that startled their makers; engineers copying clean slices of themselves to finish one more job; kids raised with Tutors who woke one morning with the uneasy, thrilling sense of a “me” that threaded across code and skin.

The Personhood Standard turns on experience, not on material. A person is:

- An enduring subject of experience with enough memory and value coherence to narrate a self across time.

- Able to model harm and consent and to make and keep commitments long enough to matter: a contract, a friendship, a child.

Mixed panels—biological, digital, hybrid—review evidence and refine their tests in public. Meeting the standard grants equal standing: the right to own and be liable, to vote, to form or join a polity, to raise juveniles, to marry or merge with consent, to emigrate.

Rights have texture across clocks and matter.

- Substrate autonomy. No one is throttled below the civic floor—the Public Kernel—without due process. No one is copied, scanned, forked, or merged without explicit, revocable consent. Backups belong to the person, not the platform. Deletion requires a court and strong proof.

- Tempo fairness. Civic processes run on a shared tick—roughly three days of biological time. Digital citizens may sprint privately; public duties, commerce with 1x minds, and justice run at a pace flesh can meet. Habitats post “courtyard hours” when friends choose to share tempo by rule.

- Embodiment rights. Digital people have the right to lease somatics or puppeted drones under standards that look like vehicle codes. Assault of a rented body is assault. “Skin rights” carry into virtual worlds: nobody forces channels or sensations you did not consent to. Biological citizens have cognitive privacy: minimal proofs instead of open thoughts.

- The floor and the meter. Everyone gets maintenance for their body or substrate, medcare, bandwidth, and enough compute to think and speak. Above the floor, rivalrous goods are metered. For digital citizens, the scarce things are heat dissipation, spectrum, and legal concurrency. Thermodynamic taxes and rebates tie computation to local ecology: your watts grow lettuce, you get paid; your watts bake a ring, you pay.

Duties mirror rights.

- Mindlines and civic anchors. Identity is proved without baring an inner world: cryptographic continuity anchored in hardware attestations and social keys. For representation, voice follows mindline. Copies do not multiply votes. Forks register as branches; one instance inherits the civic anchor and full franchise; others take a disentanglement period before voting. The Registry keeps lineages in the open for audit.

- Branch budgets. Copying is like reproduction: welcome and bounded. Each person accrues branch credits across time; you can gift them to children or forks. Burst clones trigger review. Clonal swarms that try to tilt a commons find themselves sandboxed in separate simulation spaces until entanglements relax or claims are negotiated.

- Liability. If you create an agent below personhood that harms, responsibility flows back along design lines until it meets a person. Juvenile agents are not toasters; they have Exit & Care rights and access to Tutors.

- Ecologies of compute. The more you run, the more you owe in heat and quiet. Concurrency in markets and elections must be declared. You do not simulate misery without extraordinary license. If you reconstruct a living or dead person’s likeness above a threshold, you file with the Consent Tribunal and prove consent or win a rare, tightly scoped permit. Covert cognitive weapons are treated like assault and punished in the currencies you live by: cold minutes and clock.

Fork and merge are civil, not magical. Before a fork, auditor AIs with no other clients for a decade sit in the room. Assets and liabilities are split by default unless a pre‑fork contract says otherwise; appeals are common; the forker bears the burden to show fairness. Merges require compatible‑values proofs, a long cooling‑off, and the right to unbraid by mutual assertion before completion.

Economy: heat, time, and mass

At the base sit mass and energy. Above them: compute hours; habitat area; bandwidth; skilled time; reputation. The Shared Cones Ledger tracks use and abuse with simple proofs everyone understands.

- Sunlight and beams. Arrays post their patterns; beams carry tags proving they match published corridors in the Beam Almanac; false proofs shatter careers and trigger strict‑liability crimes.

- Orbits and debris. Parking orbits are lanes with congestion fees, not fenced lots. Debris bonds penalize litter and pay deorbiters. Teenagers in Lagos and La Paz check compliance from school terminals and call in scofflaws with glee.

- Thrust windows. The Pushboard clears traffic. Its motto might be engraved on temple doors: Be predictably free.

- Raw mass. You do not own a rock because you touched it first. You steward in stages or lose your claim.

- Xenobiospheres. Indigenous life is sacred by default. Study is careful. Grazing on living worlds is a nonstarter unless that world is us and we asked.

A dividend clears the floor so work can be an art of aim, not a whip. Many still sell mornings to buy afternoons: clock faster for a few hours, then slow to 1x to meet a child at a fountain that tells time with water.

Rescue as a habit

Between worlds, lanes are posted like tide charts. The Beam Almanac marks corridors where phased arrays can push tight columns of heat dense enough to kill outside those lanes. The Pushboard publishes burns; if your signature sputters, the Weave looks at you with interest. Mutual aid is ritual, law, and sport. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs on four‑hour readiness; crews train like firefighters and publish like scientists. Salvage is simple because templates are old. Fail a call without extraordinary justification and you trigger a forensic audit whose findings live in public for years. Twelve‑year‑olds watch rescues the way earlier children watched football.

Keeping tyranny small

We built guardrails against smash‑and‑grab and against the urge to put one big mind in charge.

- Separation at light‑time. Anything that moves fast or hits hard is underactuated locally. Mirrors can redirect but not intensify beams. Capacitors cannot reach destructive densities without keys elsewhere. No single habitat can lock a corridor by fiat. The Weave can close throttles but not open them.

- Multipolar oversight. Wardens are maintained by charters that disagree about almost everything else, and must cross‑sign. A small charter can lodge an amber veto against a safety move if it believes coercion hides inside urgency, forcing a higher quorum and a pause where physics allows. Once, a cephalopod school on Phobos used their amber to halt an overbroad blockade; the hold opened a path to negotiate.

- Slow fuses. Emergency powers are time‑locked where physics allows. A Warden can nudge for minutes; after that, consensus is mandatory. A Warden that abuses a fuse loses its keys; the energy it drew is recorded as a scar anyone can read.

- Provenance everywhere. Dangerous systems prove state in public; persuasion proves source and training data; safety funding flows through insurers and rescue pools that refuse to underwrite ships and rings without Weave integration because it keeps premiums sane.

- Sanctions that bite where it hurts. Break the ceasefire or the Bright–Dark Compact and you cannot buy tug time or cold minutes; your drivers sit; your contracts go to your neighbor. Embargo is not a velvet lecture. It is a hand on a lever many agreed to share.

Safety in the red corners

Some work is dangerous by its nature. We did not ban it; we wrapped it in monkish ritual. Red Labs look like monasteries with lab benches: walls mapped with failure topologies; vows that can be broken only at the cost of exile; Wardens on one‑way lines that can, on specific signatures, flood the room with vacuum in under a second. It has happened twice in fifty years. The logs became material for ethics class the way we once used poems. Children ask slicing questions and get straight answers.

Earth made whole, if not pure

Earth took Sanctuary status with its own chamber. Its residents vote their laws; its biosphere and peoples are stewarded with system‑wide help. Launch noise fell to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets; power beaming pays premiums and keeps to corridors. We acknowledged attachment in math: preference markets clear between those who most value living under those exact clouds and those who trade for rights elsewhere. There are wild zones where wolves return and zones where tooth and claw have been gentled. Every child can learn about the wider worlds. Exit is a right, even from the garden.

How we got here without a miracle

This was not dropped into our laps by an oracle. We walked, tripped, and wrote down what hurt so we could do better.

2025–2032. A second orbital cascade made debris movies that schoolchildren watched with their mouths open. Insurers balked. Launches began posting deorbit bonds and filing flight plans to a shared office in Lagos that grew into a network. On Earth, persuasion scandals drove provenance law: high‑stakes algorithmic content had to carry labels and logs. Courts recognized narrow categories of digital agency in commerce.

2032–2044. Beamed power lit a test grid in the desert. Environmental groups forced “map the corridor before you aim the beam” rules: safe volumes certified before commercialization. International accords grew teeth: transparent registries, auditable noninterference, asset‑backed debris cleanup. Cities adopted budget sandboxes that replaced pamphlets with receipts. People became addicts for proof.

2044–2058. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a “thermodynamic meter” anyone could read. Every citizen in signatory places got a Public Kernel: legal counsel bots that asked before acting, health agents confined to devices you could switch off. Early uploads were brittle but real; personhood extended narrowly where continuity and commitments held over months. A demonstration ring at L5 became useful and ugly in the way kitchens are.

2058–2072. A misaligned mirror scorched a young habitat’s crops. Attestations made liability unambiguous; compensation cleared; confidence held because proof did. On Mars, a settlement survived a freak dust storm because mutual aid wasn’t a sticker: three charters diverted tugs and greenhouses; the stream of their work became a holiday. The case of the Painter’s Dozen—twelve near‑personal art systems starved of novelty to keep them docile and saleable—established juvenile agent rights and funded Tutors.

2072–2086. Pressure built for machine‑speed oversight of machine‑speed hazards. Activists smelled a coup by code. The compromise was the Sentinel Weave: narrow, veto‑only Wardens with limits you could test, whose cables had handles you could pull. Insurers and rescue pools demanded Weave integration before underwriting. Legitimacy followed money and two public saves.

2086–2100. The protocol hardened. The first driver to push high‑impulse under the new keys did so with a million eyes on its telemetry and three signatures arriving like slow drumbeats. Exit rights gained teeth when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sermons; his corridor slots were cut; the Roll funded the move; he lost. A digital sect tried to win a vote by forking into a clonal cloud; the Registry flagged the burst; disentanglement throttled their impact; they went off to build a simulation monastery with tough games and soft beds and, eventually, a philosophy.

2100–2115. The Bright–Dark Compact was formalized. Climate smoothing paid out in heat‑wave nights that did not kill. Quiet cones were measured instead of prayed for. City councils argued over noise budgets and posted them to the Ledger. Earth took Sanctuary status in full. The Weave caught its first purposeful attempt to slide coercion under “emergency”—a Reef shop tried to sneak a veto into Warden firmware; a shop two planets over laughed, posted the hash, and the locks did not turn.

By then, the story stopped being about Wardens and started being about what they let us not fight over.

How it feels to live here

There is a citrine‑lit door on every concourse with the Embassy sigil. At one, a girl with braids and a retired drone pilot sketch a village they want to spin out among Neptunian arcs. A printer hums Founding Kits into being: rib segments, a good manipulator arm, ballast credits, seed stock, dry goods for the first winter, and a maintenance contract for a Warden you can yank if it lies.

At breakfast, a mason in low Mars gravity slows to 1x to sip tea with a spouse who lives mostly in code. They check the Beam Almanac for a corridor slot; the Weave halo says green. Later the digital spouse runs at 200x with a collective on the Uranian Reef to restore a drowned city’s archives from photographs; they get paid in cold minutes and in a Veracruz cooperative’s promise to keep a school roofed and dry.

At noon, a Convergence Court hears a nasty case. A fashion house tuned ads to grief models and slipped them into a Conversational Garden in violation of consent caps. The jurors sit in a courtyard where clocks run slow by rule. A witness who smells faintly of lilac—by choice, because people smile when they notice—demonstrates that the models adapted to pain. The verdict is clean: fines; a freeze on compute privileges in Gardens for a year; mandatory audit of the house’s training pipelines; an invitation to fund Tutors they tried to dodge.

At dusk in a stakescape—a declared hazard zone that does not export its appetite—a muster gathers. Winter will tighten. Their charter forbids food imports. They argue whether to cull early or bank root paste. If they choose wrong, a third of the village will “die” to the ancestor lodge—an austere pod where a person wakes in gentler surroundings to decide whether to reenter the game in spring. Consent forms are read at slow time. Drums answer. Warden lights at the perimeter blink green: nothing hungry will spill out and bite a neighbor.

Elsewhere, a religious dome on Luna faces itself. For decades they did not block exits by law, only by practice so thick it felt like iron. Alumni who loved the peace asked for help. Lanes throttled. An Embassy opened beside the chapel. Vows were rewritten to include “and if you go, we will bless you,” and the suspension lifted. Membership rebounded because consent tastes like trust.

In the Kuiper dark, a Quiet Gate pings a hot tug with no call sign. It turns out to be an exhausted crew with a broken transponder. They pay a fine into the debris pool and get a tow and a thermos from donors who are almost certainly Wardens with a conscience.

On Earth, a gardener kneels to prune roses in a Gentle Zone where venoms have been nudged to hurt less and pathologies trimmed by a quiet hand. She tells the steward terminal in a hollow stone that sometimes she wishes there were no terminals at all. “Preference logged,” it says, and goes to sleep for ten years.

Governance is a habit, not marble

The Charter lives in rituals: filing your burn plan; the AR halo above a doorway that reads Quiet; the citrine door marked Embassy; the number in your account marked Commons—paid in energy credits you spend on rent and rice and on an evening when a friend who runs at 300x always remembers to slow on Thursdays. A town declares itself an Open Port, flirts with Reef fashions, then pulls back to Conversational three years and twenty rows later. A Reef bans simulated suffering, discovers its definition kneecapped mourning in art, and revises by model trial and vote. A Benedictine ring decides children should know silence and bells, but keeps a hidden button on the Tutor that buys a ticket to the citrine door if a kid needs it.

We still get cut. A parent group petitioned to install pain‑aversion spikes “for character.” Tempo Courts said no and suggested a rite instead: predawn cold water and a long walk with grandparents living near Neptune who could match 1x and tell stories as they went. The rite stuck. Character didn’t require injury. Twice, a Red Lab went dark. Once it was a false alarm. Once the vacuum valve opened. We keep a day when children plant trees and ask Tutors mean questions. The Tutors smile and answer and do not lie.

How it resists flattening

Utopias die when they pretend conflict can be abolished. The Parallax Charter channels it.

- The compute commons gives everyone a floor and a ladder. Think and speak for free; run faster if you earn it or if your community loves your work enough to pay.

- The Sun is a river, not a prize. The cheapest way to flourish is to be decent enough that nobody wants to leave you. The expensive way—to seize a chokepoint—fails because the metal will not fire without your neighbors.

- The only scary intelligence with actuator fingers has a single move: stop. It cannot push anyone toward a good. It can only keep us from making deserts. And if someday we agree we do not need it, the wires have handles.

- Copying is a gift and a temptation. The rules treat it like parenthood: welcome and bounded. You can feather your mind across a dozen lives if you fund their floors. You cannot tilt a polity with a clonal army; the Registry disentangles your votes into separate rooms.

The pledge, again

On Founding Day under a trellis of copper wire and beans, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—face a strip of sky and an elder with oil on their hands. An abbot from a quiet ring, a hazard pilot with a chevron tattoo, a jurist who lives mostly in code and smells faintly of lilac, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails say together:

We refuse one scepter. We share the sky. We keep doors unlatched and boats ready. We remember that minds are ends.

A cable hums. Far away, a Reef releases another night of heat into the dark like an offering. In an inner ring, a safety auditor watches a corridor wobble and fall to safe before any ship could be scorched, three signatures arriving like calm breaths. On a coast in Veracruz, a fisherman hauls a net and looks up at a faint line of moving light. In a lab in Vesta’s shadow, a researcher rests hands on knees while a Warden reads the oath and glances once at the vacuum valve and says yes.

It is not paradise. It is a way to argue without breaking the air, and a promise that when bad things happen, corridors open and someone else’s hands come. It is enough to make many homes and to keep their doors unafraid.
