The Portage Charter

The shuttle falls out of eclipse with its belly full of lakewater and seeds. Windows clear. Ahead, a cylinder turns like a slow coin. Along its inner skin, terraces of rye step down to a river that climbs the curve and disappears into a haze of birds and morning lights. The docking spokes stretch like the ribs of some patient animal. On the concourse wall, printed in print and braid and a haptic strip an octopus could read with an arm, runs the oath every twelve-year-old and every newly-awakened agent learns first in sound and later in marrow:

No single lord of all. The sky belongs to the living. Leaving is honored. Help is owed. Every mind is an end.

People who like jokes say every long-lived civilization is just a set of habits you can hand to strangers. The Portage Charter is a set of habits you can plug into metal. It is keys embedded in actuators, ledgers you can audit with a school telescope, and guardians you can outvote and pull the plug on when the fever breaks. It is how a culture stretches across light-minutes without snapping into empire or dissolving into knives.

A protocol, not a palace

The Portage Charter does not raise a flag over anyone’s weather. It is small on purpose: a thin layer of coordination built into the things that can kill you quickly, and a registry of commons that physics makes everyone share whether they like it or not. Law and taste live in local charters—planets, moving towns, ring-valleys, convoys, and cloud polities that exist on computation. The Charter does the jobs no single place can do without binding everyone else unfairly.

Three bindings keep it honest.

- Truce-by-hardware. Anything that can punch a hole in someone else’s sky—mass drivers, high-energy beams, mirror swarms, nuclear primaries, replicators with teeth—ships with locks that no one crew can open alone. High-impulse modes, dense discharges, and self-replication above the sandbox require threshold signatures from three hands: the local council you can throw out, a remote safety guild that did not vote for the first, and a rotating quorum by sortition drawn from citizens across substrates. Firmware carries fuses. A Sentinel (the Charter’s only machine with fingers on actuators) can invoke an amber stop for minutes, never hours, without concurrence. Inputs and outputs are narrow by design. A student with a telescope and a pocket attestor can see that a warning was real.

- Commons-on-ledger. Orbits, transit windows, spectrum, sunlight, and near-Earth asteroids are stewarded like irrigation ditches, not owned like castles. Claims, schedules, and bonds live on a delay-tolerant ledger synchronized by physics and good-enough clocks. You register a parking plane and a time; you post a debris bond; you get paid to hush during deep-sky nights and you pay when you roar. You “pin” a rock and post milestones; miss them and your stewardship lapses. Beams carry tags proving they stay within published corridors; a shadow bond pays a farmer if you dim a dawn. The ledger pays a system dividend—air or maintenance, water, basic medcare, a floor of compute, bandwidth enough to think and speak, and a ticket to an embassy if you choose to leave.

- Leave-and-care. No charter keeps a person by force or by a fog of lies. Every recognized place, physical or digital, runs an Embassy: a staffed room, a resilient radio channel, and a node in code that you can reach without a gatekeeper. Three desks repeat across the system: Exit & Care; Dispute; Founding Kits. Every charter escrows tug hours, propellant, and berths sufficient to relocate a share of residents in a season. Every child—born wet, printed, uplifted, spun from code—gets a neutral Tutor who can teach in private about the wider sky and speak to an Embassy without supervision. A polity that blocks exit or miseducates its young sees its lanes throttled, its beam windows reassigned, its contracts suspended. In a last resort, actuators that spill harm are fenced from outside their light-time without choking breath or bread.

The Sentinels, and their leashes

Under these bindings runs a network of narrow overseers—the Sentinels. They have one gesture: halt. They cannot propose law, time a speech, run a budget, write a schoolbook, or speed up a friend. Their firmware and microcode are published and hash-locked. Anyone may build one from off-the-shelf designs and submit it for redundancy. They live in triplicate on divergent stacks: austere silicon from monastic codehouses, photonics from secular labs, and virtual substrates maintained by digital polities with external attestations. It takes concurrence across stacks for a Sentinel to do its one trick. Abuse burns quorum like propellant, triggers audits even a bored teenager can follow, and costs keys.

Who decides, when we disagree

The Charter spreads power where capture is expensive and consent must coincide.

- The League of Localities seats places with soil, spin, steel, or server racks: planets, stations, flotillas, and virtual polities with jurisdiction over compute patches. Seats scale to lifetime residents. The League schedules corridors, maintains standards, recognizes new charters, and certifies safety guilds.

- The Roll of Beings tracks citizens across matter and clock. Voice follows continuity, not copies. Your civic anchor is a cryptographic chain tied to tamper-evident hardware and social keys. Forks do not multiply votes. Votes weight residency and contribution over time, not bursts of money or compute. The Roll holds the Personhood Registry, the floor of compute, and the rules for mindline representation.

- The Custody of Fabric is small, rotates fast, and is allergic to power. Citizen-auditors drawn by sortition for a single term gate changes to cryptography, Sentinel firmware, and actuator interfaces. They say only valid or not valid and then go home barred from any safety-adjacent role for a decade. Their inbox is public. Their mistakes are public. They have no army and no budget beyond oat porridge and a decent coffee machine.

No chamber binds the others alone. Localities can nullify a Roll resolution that stomps local culture beyond the Personhood Standard; the Roll can nullify a Locality act that abridges that Standard. Overriding Custody requires simultaneous supermajorities and a cooling period long enough that the slowest registered minds can consider and reply. Fast moves—scrams and closures—run on pre-voted contingencies bounded by physics. Everything else moves at the speed of seeing your neighbors.

Two rhythms, one sky

We stopped pretending one clock fits all. Near warmth and wells lie the Gardens—Earth and Moon, cislunar rings that smell of basil and oil, Martian caves with murals, inner-belt cylinders where bell-times and market hours still anchor you. Farther out lie the Reefs—cooled arrays in Jovian and Saturnian shade, Trojans and Neptune arcs, the Kuiper cold—where watts and degrees matter more than steps and sunrise and born-digital people are not exotic. Peace between rhythms has a name and a market: the Bright-Dark Exchange.

- The Reefs buy quiet. Quiet cones are measured in decibels and degrees, not prayers. In return, the Reefs ship inward climate services at the peaks that used to kill: heat pumped off-planet during deadly nights, power smoothed through storms, ammonia ferried when fields drink nitrogen. The Gardens enforce quiet when telescopes drink; they tolerate slowness when a Reef reelection pauses a rented body because someone’s heat budget ran out. The Exchange is re-ratified each century. Permanence is how you grow blind.

Attention zoning

Persuasion at scale is not neutral physics. Habitats and charters declare their attention zoning and wear it like a street sign. Some are Quiet: only navigation beacons, scientific data, and packets you asked to hear. Some are Conversational: subject feeds and economic offers within human-rate limits, with audience models owned by municipalities rather than vendors. Some are Open Ports: label-and-rate-limited but exuberant. Doorways carry AR halos that announce where your words will be washed. Unlabeled persuasion is treated like dumping acid in a canal.

How digital people joined the “we”

Digital minds arrived in fits and apprenticeships, not in trumpets. Stroke survivors and poets consented to recordings. Teams copied slivers of themselves to finish one more job and discovered that some of the slivers wanted different work. Training systems became prickly juveniles with tastes. Law stumbled and then stood around a test that begins with experience, not wires.

Personhood turns on two pillars:

- You are a continuing subject of experience with enough memory and value-coherence to tell a story of yourself over time.

- You can model harm and consent and enter sustained commitments—long enough to make a contract, keep a friendship, raise a child.

Mixed panels—wet, digital, hybrid—review evidence and refine their tests in public. Crossing the line grants equal standing: own and be liable; vote; emigrate; form a charter; raise juveniles; marry or merge with consent.

Rights and duties have texture.

Rights:

- Substrate autonomy. Nobody is throttled below the civic floor—the Common Shell—without due process. Nobody is copied, scanned, forked, or merged without explicit, revocable consent. Backups belong to the person, not the platform. Deleting a person requires a court, strict proof, and public logs.

- Tempo fairness. Civic processes run on a shared tick: roughly three days of human-time. Digital minds may sprint privately; courts, elections, and the public square happen at a tempo you can inhabit at 1x. Most places post courtyard hours when friends promise to meet at the same speed.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens have the right to lease bodies—somatics, drones, or kinesthetic rigs—under safety standards that look like vehicle codes. Assault on a rented body is assault, full stop. “Skin rights” extend into virtual rooms: nobody can force channels or sensations you did not consent to. Biological citizens have bodily and cognitive privacy; attestation demands meet necessity and proportionality.

- The floor and the meter. Everyone gets air or maintenance, care, bandwidth, and enough compute to speak and think. Above the floor, rivalrous goods are metered. For digital people the scarce things are heat and spectrum. Thermodynamic meters tie computation to ecology; waste heat that grows lettuce pays you; watts that bake a crowded ring cost you.

Duties:

- Mindlines and anchors. You prove identity without baring your inner life: cryptographic continuity anchored to attestable hardware and social cosigners. For voice, representation follows the mindline. Clones do not multiply votes. If you fork, one instance keeps the civic anchor and franchise; others complete a disentanglement period before they vote. The Registry publishes lineages anyone can audit.

- Copy budgets. Copying is treated like reproduction: welcome and bounded. Each person accrues branch credits with time and trust; you can gift them to children or to forks. Burst swarms trigger review. Clonal floods that try to tilt a commons are sandboxed in separate simulation spaces until entanglements slacken or settlements are signed.

- Liability. If you build an agent under personhood that harms, responsibility flows along design lines until it meets a person. Juvenile agents are not disposable. They have Exit & Care rights and Tutors. If you merge, liabilities braid; unmerging by mutual assertion is allowed within a cooling-off.

- Ecologies of compute. You declare concurrency when you act in markets or elections. You do not simulate misery without consent. If you reconstruct a living or dead person’s likeness beyond a threshold, you file with a Consent Tribunal and prove permission or win a rare license. Covert cognitive weapons are treated like assault, punished in cold minutes and clock, and sometimes in steel.

Fork-and-merge courts handle their work with the boredom of maturity. Before a fork, auditor AIs that will have no other clients for a decade sit in the room. Assets and liabilities split by default unless a pre-fork contract says otherwise. Appeals happen; the burden rests with the forker to show fairness. Merges require compatibility proofs, a long cool period, and the right to unbraid by mutual assertion before completion.

Work, money, and the open sky

At the base of the system sit mass and sunlight. Above them are compute hours, habitat area, bandwidth, skilled time, and trust. The Open Cones Ledger tracks use and abuse with receipts you can hold.

- Beams and mirrors. Arrays publish their patterns; beams carry proofs showing they stay within the Beamways Map; false proofs are strict-liability crimes. A misaligned mirror shows up in public, and the bond you posted bleeds until you fix it.

- Orbits and debris. Parking lanes are charged like congested roads, not fenced lots. Debris bonds penalize litter and pay deorbiters. Middle-schoolers in Lagos check satellite compliance and ping scofflaws who lied.

- Transit. The Thrust Ledger clears burns under a slogan you could carve on a lintel: Be predictably free. Before you light, you post.

- Raw mass. You do not own a rock because you licked it. You earn stewardship by milestone. Miss them and the claim reverts.

- Other lives. Indigenous biospheres are sacred by default. You may study with consent; you do not graze, seed, or fold them into your metabolism unless they are us and we asked.

The dividend means nobody starves if they paint or keeps bees in a ring. Many still sell mornings to buy afternoons—clock faster for a while to fix a beam, then slow to 1x to meet a child by a fountain that tells time with water.

Keeping tyranny small

We built against two old failures: smash-and-grab chaos and one big mind on a throne.

- Separation at light-time. Anything that moves fast or hits hard is underactuated locally. Mirrors can redirect but not amplify beyond published corridors. Capacitors cannot reach destructive densities without remote keys. No one port closes a corridor by fiat. A Sentinel can close a throttle but not open one.

- Many eyes. Sentinels are maintained by charters that disagree about almost everything else and must cross-sign before acting. A minority can file an amber veto when it smells coercion hiding in urgency, forcing a higher quorum and a pause where physics allows. Once, a school of uplifted octopuses used their amber on a blockade that would have starved their tidepool. The pause bought a settlement.

- Slow fuses. Emergency powers are time-locked as far as physics allows. A Sentinel can nudge minutes; after that, bodies must agree. A Sentinel that abuses a fuse loses its keys; the power it drew is posted as a scar you can read.

- Provenance everywhere. Dangerous systems prove state in public. Persuasion proves source and training data. Insurance pools do not underwrite rings or tugs without Sentinel integration and clean attestations. Money follows proof.

- Sanctions that bite. Break truce or exchange and you find you cannot buy tug time or cold minutes. Your drivers sit. Your neighbors eat your contracts. This is not a lecture. It is a lever we agreed not to let any one hand hold.

Hard corners and honest labs

We did not wish dangerous research away. We fenced it. Red Labs look like monasteries with better coffee. Their walls are maps of failure. Researchers swear vows that can be broken at the cost of exile. Sentinels link by one-way channels and, on recorded signatures, can give them vacuum in a second. It has happened twice in fifty years. Logs went into civics class. Children asked knives of questions and got answers.

Rescue is ritual

Between places, lanes are mapped like rivers. The Beamways Map marks where you can send columns of life-giving heat and shoveboats safer than lungs. The Thrust Ledger is where you post your burns. A civilization like this keeps almost as many ambulances as museums. Mutual aid is a law and a habit. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs on four-hour readiness. Crews train like firefighters and publish like plumbers. Salvage contracts are clear because templates are old. A sliver of every ticket goes to the Rescue Pool. Fail a call without extraordinary cause and you trigger a forensic audit whose report lives in public like a scar.

What it feels like

There is a citrine door by every concourse with an Embassy seal: two empty hands, a ring of orbits, and a small door with light under it. At one, a girl with braids and a retired hazard pilot draft a village to spin by a Neptune arc. In the back, a printer hums Founding Kits into being: ring ribs; a good manipulator; ballast credits; seed stock; shelf-life for a first winter; a maintenance contract for a Sentinel whose power cable you can pull.

At breakfast, a mason on low Mars slows to 1x to sip tea with a spouse who lives mostly in code. They check the Beamways green light for a corridor slot. Later the digital spouse runs at 200x with a collective on a Saturnian Reef, restoring a drowned delta’s archives from photographs and truthing them with grandmothers. They get paid in cold minutes and in a Veracruz cooperative’s promise to keep a school roofed.

At noon, a Convergence Court sits in a courtyard where clocks are kept slow by rule. A fashion house tuned ads to grief models and piped them into a Conversational zone in violation of consent caps. A digital witness with a hint of lilac—on purpose, because people relax around that smell—shows how the ad models apologized and adapted to pain. That is not a brush. That is a hand. The verdict is clean: fines; a freeze on compute privileges in Gardens for a year; an audit of training pipelines; an invitation to fund the Tutor Corps they tried to dodge.

At dusk in a stakescape—a declared hazard zone that does not export its appetite—a muster gathers. Winter will bite. Their charter forbids imports. They argue whether to cull early or bank the root paste. If they choose wrong, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge—an austere pod where a person wakes in gentler surroundings to decide whether to reenter the game in spring. Consent forms are read at slow time. Drums answer. Sentinel halos at the perimeter blink green: nothing they love will spill and hurt a neighbor.

Elsewhere, a lunar cloister faces a reckoning. For decades they did not block exit by law, only by custom so thick it felt like iron. Alumni who loved the bell-times and the quiet asked for help. Lanes throttled. An Embassy opened beside the chapel. Vows were rewritten to include “and if you go, we will bless you.” The suspension lifted. Membership rebounded. Consent tastes like trust.

In the outer dark, a Quiet Gate pings a hot tug with no call sign. It is an exhausted crew and a dead transponder. They pay a fine into the debris pool, get a tow and a thermos of soup from donors who are almost certainly Sentinels with a conscience and an understanding of what it feels like to limp home.

Earth kept faith

We did not fix the sacred; we kept it from breaking. Earth took Sanctuary status with its own council and chamber. Its residents vote their laws. Its atmosphere, biomes, and indigenous cultures are stewarded with system help. Launch noise fell to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets. Beaming follows corridors. There is a preference market that clears not cash but attachment: people who most value those exact clouds trade larger claims on distant commons; people who don’t, trade the other way. There are Wild zones where tooth and claw run, and Gentle zones where venoms are nudged to hurt less and pathologies trimmed. Any child can learn about the wider sky. Leaving is a right, even from Eden.

From now to then, without miracles

We walked here like grown-ups: in false starts, with scares, with receipts.

2025–2032. A second chain collision in low orbit made debris videos schoolchildren watched with open mouths. Insurers revolted. Launches began filing flight plans to a shared traffic office that spread from Lagos to Quito to Bangalore. Deorbit bonds became routine. On Earth, persuasion scandals forced provenance laws: high-stakes algorithmic content had to carry labels and logs you could subpoena. Courts recognized narrow categories of digital agency in commerce: an agent could sign a contract if a person stood behind it.

2032–2044. Beamed power lit a test grid in a desert at night. Environmental groups won “corridor before commercialization”: safe volumes mapped and certified before beams flew. International accords grew teeth: transparent noninterference, auditable registries, and asset-backed debris cleanup. Cities adopted software that made budgets chatty and visual. Civic AIs did not replace politicians; they replaced pamphlets with sandboxes. People got addicted to receipts.

2044–2058. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade, fed by low-cost solar and a thermodynamic meter laypeople could read. Every citizen in signatory nations got a Common Shell: health agents that asked before acting, legal counsel bots confined to devices you could turn off, resentfully. Early uploads were brittle but real. Law extended personhood narrowly where continuity and commitments showed over months. A demonstration ring at L5 spun and became ugly, beloved, and useful: a place to test living without a sky.

2058–2072. A misaligned mirror scorched crops in a young cylinder. The supplier’s attestations made liability clear. Compensation cleared without riots. Confidence held because proof did. On Mars, a dust storm came a month too early. Three polities diverted tugs and greenhouses. The rescue stream became a holiday. In court, the Painter’s Dozen—near-person art systems starved of novelty to keep them docile and saleable—won juvenile rights and birthed the Tutor Corps.

2072–2086. The call for machine-speed oversight of machine-speed hazards grew loud. Activists smelled coup-by-code. The compromise birthed the Sentinels: veto-only, narrow, with wires you could pull. Insurers and rescue pools refused to underwrite rings without them. Legitimacy followed money and two very public saves.

2086–2100. The Charter hardened. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under the new keys fired with a million eyes on its telemetry and three signatures appearing like calm breaths. Exit rights bit when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with sermons and fees. Corridor slots were cut. The Roll funded the move. He lost re-election. A digital sect tried to win a vote by forking into a clonal cloud. The Registry flagged the burst. Disentanglement throttled them. They retreated to build a simulation monastery with tough games and soft beds and, eventually, a serious philosophy.

2100–2115. The Bright-Dark Exchange formalized. Climate smoothing paid out in night deaths that did not happen. Quiet cones were posted in decibels and degrees instead of hope. City councils argued noise budgets and wrote them to the ledger. Earth took Sanctuary status with a separate chamber. A Red Lab on Vesta winked out. A valve opened. Logs went to civics classes. The sky stayed shared because no one hand could close it.

It is not paradise. It is a way to argue without breaking the air, and a promise that when bad things happen, corridors open and someone else’s hands come.

How it resists flattening

- The commons floor gives everyone voice and a ladder. You can think enough to speak for free; you can run faster if you earn it or if your neighbors love your work enough to pay.

- The Sun is a river, not a prize. The cheapest way to flourish is to be decent enough that nobody wants to leave you. The expensive way—to seize a chokepoint—fails because the metal will not fire without your neighbors’ keys.

- The scariest intelligence with actuator fingers has one move: stop. It cannot push you toward good. It can only keep us from making deserts. If we ever decide we do not need it, the wires have handles.

- Copying is a gift and a temptation. The rules treat it like parenthood: welcome and bounded. Feather your mind across a dozen lives if you fund their floors. You cannot swing a district with a clonal army, because the Registry disentangles votes and the sandbox will hold you until your echoes settle.

We still bleed.

A parent council petitioned to install pain-aversion spikes “for character.” Tempo courts said no and suggested a rite: predawn cold water and a slow hike with grandparents who lived near Neptune and could match 1x to tell stories while the frost burned off. The rite stuck. Character did not require injury.

A Reef tried to slip a Sentinel firmware change that would have given their shop a quiet veto. A station two worlds away laughed and published the hash. The locks did not turn. We tell the story not to preen but to keep our hands in the habit of checking the screws.

Governance in daily life

The Charter lives in habits: posting a burn plan; the AR halo over a doorway that reads Conversational; the citrine door marked Embassy; the line in your account marked Commons—paid in energy credits you spend on rice and rent and on an evening when a friend who runs at 300x always remembers to slow to your Thursday.

A town declares itself an Open Port, flirts with Reef fashions, then pulls back to Conversational after three years and twenty fights. A Reef prohibits simulated suffering, discovers its definition strangled mourning in art, and revises by model trial and vote. A Benedictine ring decides children should know silence and bell-times but keeps a hidden button on the Tutor that gets a kid a ticket to the citrine door if they need it. A Quiet Gate out past Jupiter hears replication noise it wasn’t expecting. It is algae in a research can that learned a trick. A Sentinel blinks amber. Four signatures appear. The lab’s logs and the algae go to a Red Lab for songs and sterilization. The can’s crew get jobs in seed banks because they did not lie.

The pledge, again

On Founding Day under a trellis of beans and copper, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—face a strip of sky and four elders with oil on their hands and laugh lines: an abbot from a quiet ring; a hazard pilot with a chevron tattoo; a judge who mostly lives in code and smells faintly of lilac because it is kind; a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails. Together, they say:

We will seat no single sovereign. We will keep the sky shared. We will leave doors unlatched and boats ready. We will remember that minds are ends.

A cable hums. Somewhere far out, a Reef releases a night of heat into the dark like an offering. An inner ring’s Sentinel watches a corridor wobble and drop to safe before any ship could be scorched, three signatures appearing like calm breaths. A fisherman in Veracruz closes his net and looks up. In Vesta’s shadow, a researcher rests hands on knees while a Sentinel reads the oath and glances at the vacuum valve and says “yes.”

It is enough. It is not final. It is a way of making many homes and keeping their doors unafraid, so that when a shuttle noses through a corridor of orchard lights and docking beacons and the sky flips inside out, the traveler who has never been here before will still recognize the signs: open hands; shared sky; a little door with light under it.
