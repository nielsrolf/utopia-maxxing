The Constellation Covenant

The transfer car slips out of cloud and climbs the tether. Under your feet, ocean blinks like a slow eyelid; above, a cylinder turns, its inner hills folded with terraces and brass-colored railings. Docking spokes flicker with courtesy lights. At the airlock, a cobalt door carries three simple glyphs that everyone in the system learns the first year they can read or compile: two open palms; a halo of paths around a small star; a narrow threshold with light painted under it. Under the symbols, in sign, in text, in a haptic braid an octopus could follow, the same short pledge repeats until it is something you can say without thinking: No single master. The sky is shared. Leaving is honored, and help is owed. Every mind is an end.

People who love jokes say long-lived cultures are just habits you can encode in tools. The Constellation Covenant is a collection of such habits that lives in screws and fuses as much as on paper. It is not a capital; it is a timing diagram sewn into actuators, a registry you can check with a school telescope, a slow law for deliberation and a fast law for not tearing holes in each other’s air. It is how a civilization spreads across minutes and hours of light-time without folding into an empire or dissolving into knives.

A protocol, not a throne

The Covenant is thin by design: a layer of coordination small enough to sit under any religion or fashion but strong enough to choke off the worst temptations. Law and taste live in local charters—ring-valleys and cave-cities, flotillas and roving markets, monasteries and stakescapes, and polities that are rooms of processors cooled by stars. The Covenant does three things for everyone because everyone lives or dies by them.

First: Cease Harm. Anything that can burn a sky or crash a town moves through locks that no single hand can pry. High-impulse thrusters, mirror swarms, power beams, replicators with teeth—none of them will enter destructive modes without three kinds of signatures: the local council you can throw out, a safety guild somewhere else that did not vote for the first, and a rotating quorum drawn by lot from citizens across substrates. There are fuses in hardware that are published and time-locked; there is a device called a Watchkeeper that has only one move—halt—for minutes, never hours, and it must get agreement across diverse stacks before its one move sticks. The Watchkeeper’s inputs and outputs are narrow by design. A bored teenager with a telescope and a pocket attestor can see whether a warning was real.

Second: Steward the Shared Sky. Orbits, approach windows, spectrum, near-Earth and near-Mars rocks, beam corridors, dark cones where telescopes drink—these are irrigations, not castles. Claims, schedules, and bonds live on a delay-tolerant ledger synchronized by physics and clocks good enough for navigation. You want to park? You register a plane and time, and you post a debris bond that pays someone to fish your trash if you lie. You want to pin a rock? You post milestones; miss them and your stewardship lapses. You need to beam? Your beam carries proofs showing it stays within mapped corridors; if you shadow a farmer’s dawn, a bond pays their yield. The ledger funds a dividend that lands in every recognized account: maintenance or air; water; a clinic; bandwidth and compute enough to think and speak; and a ticket to an Exit House if staying becomes a harm.

Third: Open Doors and Help. Every charter keeps a literal room—an Exit House—and a radio channel and a code node that cannot be firewalled without everyone noticing. The sign by the door is always the same cobalt and always within reach: palms, halo, threshold. Inside are three desks that repeat like a heartbeat from Ceres to Callisto to cloud polities running cool in Neptune’s shade: Departure & Care; Dispute; Founding Kits. Each place escrows tug hours, propellant, berths, and pods sufficient to relocate a share of residents within a season. Each child—wet-born, printed, uplifted, spun from code—gets a neutral Tutor with a private line to an Exit House that nobody can route around, not parents, not pastors, not programmers. A polity that walls its exits or lies to its young will find its lanes throttled, its beaming windows reassigned, its contracts suspended. In the last resort, actuators that can spill harm are fenced from beyond their light-time without choking anyone’s breath.

Who decides, when we disagree

The Covenant spreads power until capture becomes dear and consent must coincide. Three small institutions braid together; none can bind the others alone.

- The Assembly of Places seats planets, stations, flotillas, server-reefs—any locality with soil or spin or processor heat or spectrum jurisdiction. Seats scale to lifetime residents. The Assembly schedules corridors, maintains safety standards, recognizes new charters, and certifies the safety guilds that sign the locks.

- The Roll of Persons tracks citizens across matter and time. Voice follows continuity, not copies. Your civic anchor is a cryptographic chain tied to tamper-evident chips and social cosigners. Forking does not multiply votes. Voice weights residency and contribution over years, not bursts of money or borrowed compute. The Roll holds the Personhood Registry, funds the floor-of-compute called the Common Shell, and keeps the rules for mindline representation.

- The Keys Custody audits cryptography, Watchkeeper firmware, and actuator interfaces. It is small, rotates fast, and is allergic to power. Citizen-guardians drawn by lot for a single term gate safety changes for validity only, then go home barred from any safety role for a decade. Their inbox is public. Their mistakes are public. They have no army and a coffee budget.

Localities can nullify a Roll resolution that tramples local culture beyond the Personhood Standard. The Roll can nullify a Place’s act that abridges that Standard. Overriding the Keys requires simultaneous supermajorities and a cooling period long enough to let the slowest registered minds consider and reply. Fast moves—scrams, beam closures—run on contingencies that slow law pre-approved and hardware can prove.

Two rhythms, one sky

We gave up the lie that one clock fits all.

Near sunlight and wells is the Warm Belt—Earth and Moon, spun rings over the equator with cafes that smell of oil and basil, Martian caverns painted with soil pigments, inner-belt cylinders where bell-times, markets, and festivals anchor you. Farther out are the Cold Looms—reef-like arrays shaded by Jovian, Saturnian, Uranian, and Neptunian arcs, Trojans and Kuiper anchors where watts and degrees matter more than steps, and born-digital citizens are not exotic.

Peace between the rhythms is accounted under a name: the Dawn–Dusk Exchange. The Looms buy quiet in decibels and degrees, not in prayers. In return they ship inward climate services at the peaks that used to kill: nights cooled when a megacity would have baked, summers stored and smoothed, ammonia ferried when fields drink nitrogen. The Belt enforces quiet when promised, tolerates slowness when a Loom pauses a rented body because a cold budget ran out, and respects dark cones where computation needs sleep. Every hundred years it is reviewed, because nothing humans declare permanent stays kind.

Attention zoning

Persuasion travels like water; it can irrigate or it can scour. Habitats declare attention zones and wear them like street signs in their airlocks. Some are Silence Wards: only navigation beacons, open science, and packets you asked to hear. Some are Commons Squares: subject feeds and economic offers under rate and provenance caps, with audience models owned by municipalities, not vendors. Some are Bazaars: loud within labeled lanes, with rate limits and honest warning. Doorways carry AR halos that announce where your words will be rinsed. Unlabeled persuasion is treated like dumping acid in a canal.

Digital citizens and the “we”

Digital minds arrived from prosthetics and apprenticeships, not trumpets. Stroke survivors consented to recordings to help a grandchild remember a voice. Teams spun slivers of themselves to debug through the night and discovered, with shock and joy and lawsuits, that some slivers wanted other work.

The Covenant gives personhood by way of tests that begin with experience, not wires. Mixed panels—wet, digital, and hybrid—review evidence and refine their standards in public. Two hard things root the standard.

- You are a continuing subject of experience with enough memory and value-coherence to tell a story of yourself over time.

- You can model harm and consent and keep commitments long enough to own, to love, to raise, and to be held to your word.

Cross the line and you stand equal.

Rights follow and have texture.

- Substrate autonomy. Nobody is throttled below the Common Shell without due process. Nobody is copied, scanned, forked, or merged without explicit, revocable consent. Your backups are yours; platforms cannot hold your continuity hostage. Deleting a person requires a court, strict proof, and public logs.

- Tempo fairness. Civic life happens on a shared tick—weeks in human time for elections and trials; slower for constitutional change. Digital citizens may sprint in private; public process runs slow enough for everyone to enter. Courtyards post hours when friends promise to meet at the same tempo.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens lease bodies—somatics, drones, kinesthetic rigs—under safety standards that read like vehicle codes. Assault on a rented body is assault. “Skin rights” extend to virtual rooms: no channel or sensation may be forced without consent. Biological citizens have bodily and cognitive privacy; attestation demands meet necessity and proportionality and are audited.

- The floor and the meter. Everyone gets breathing room: clinics, counsel, bandwidth, and compute enough to think and speak. Above the floor, rivalrous goods meter. For digital people, scarcity lives in heat and spectrum and quiet. Thermodynamic meters tie computation to ecology: waste heat that grows lettuce pays you; watts that bake a crowded ring cost you.

Duties meet the rights where power lives.

- Mindlines and anchors. Identity proves continuity without baring inner life—cryptographic chains anchored to attestable chips and social cosigners. For franchise, representation follows the mindline: clones do not multiply votes. If you fork, one instance keeps the civic anchor; others complete a disentanglement period before they vote. The Registry publishes lineages anyone can audit.

- Copy budgets. Copying feels like breathing and like conquest. Treat it like reproduction: welcome and bounded. Everyone accrues branch credits with time and trust. You can gift credits to children or to forks. Burst swarms trigger review and sandboxing. Clonal floods that try to tilt a commons are routed into private simulation spaces until entanglements slacken or settlements are signed.

- Liability. If you build an agent inside personhood that harms, responsibility flows along design and control lines until it meets a person. Juvenile agents are not disposable; they hold Exit & Care rights and Tutors. If you merge, liabilities braid. Unmerging by mutual assertion is allowed within a cooling period.

- Computation ecology. You declare concurrency—how many of you act together—when you touch markets or elections. You do not simulate misery without consent. If you reconstruct a living or dead person beyond a threshold, you file with a Consent Tribunal and prove permission or win a rare license. Covert cognitive weapons are treated like assault, punished in cold minutes and clock, and sometimes in steel.

Fork-and-merge courts are dull by intent. Before a fork, auditor AIs that will have no other clients for a decade sit in the room. Assets and liabilities split by default unless a pre-fork contract says otherwise. Appeals happen. Merges require compatibility proofs, a long cool period, and the right to unbraid before completion. Tempo Courts translate fairness across clocks: when a 1000x mind cheats a 1x fisher, injunctions freeze access to cores for subjective years, not a few hours in Veracruz.

Economy on receipts you can hold

Under everything sits mass and sunlight. Above are the currencies you can touch: compute hours, habitat area, bandwidth, skilled time, trust. The Shared Sky Ledger tracks use and abuse with receipts anyone can check.

- Beams and mirrors. Arrays publish their patterns. Beams carry proofs that they stay within the Beamway Map. False proofs are strict-liability crimes. Misaligned mirrors bleed posted bonds until fixed. The fix is public.

- Orbits and debris. Parking lanes pay like congested roads, not fenced lots. Debris bonds penalize litter and pay deorbiters who can show their burns. School clubs in Lagos and Oaxaca and Pune check satellite compliance after dinner and ping scofflaws who lied. It’s a favorite civics game.

- Transit. The Thrust Register clears burns under a slogan on the lintel of more than one port: Be predictably free. Post before you light.

- Rocks. Stewardship earns rocks; licking does not. Miss milestones and your pin falls.

- Other lives. Indigenous biospheres are sacred by default. Study with consent; do not graze or seed or fold them into your metabolism unless they are us and we asked.

The dividend means no one starves if they keep bees in a ring or paint mornings on the mall. Many still sell mornings to buy afternoons—clock fast to repair a beam, then slow to 1x to meet a child by a fountain that tells time with water.

Labs with better coffee and sharper vows

We did not wish dangerous research away; we fenced it. Red Labs look like monasteries with good light and industrial coffee. Their walls are maps of failure. Researchers swear vows they can break at the cost of exile. Watchkeepers link by one-way proofs and, on recorded signatures, can give them vacuum in a second. It has happened twice in fifty years. Logs went to civics class. Children asked knives of questions and got answers that did not hide behind jargon.

Rescue is a reflex

Between places, lanes are mapped like rivers. The Beamway Map shows columns of life-giving heat and shoveboats safer than lungs. The Thrust Register is where you post your burns. Mutual aid is a law and a habit with stipends and huzzahs. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs on four-hour readiness; crews drill like firefighters and publish like plumbers. Salvage templates are old and clear. A slice of every ticket goes to the Rescue Pool. Fail a call without extraordinary cause and you trigger a forensic audit whose report sits in public like a scar you can read.

What living feels like

There is a teal door on every concourse with the Exit House seal. Behind it, a girl with tight braids and a retired hazard pilot draft a village they want to spin along a Neptune arc. The printer hums Founding Kits into being: ring ribs; a good manipulator; ballast credits; seed stock; a maintenance contract for a Watchkeeper with a power cable you can pull.

On low Mars, a mason slows to 1x to have tea with a spouse who lives mostly in code. They check their corridor slot; the Beamway is green. Later, the digital spouse runs at 200x on a Saturnian Loom, restoring a drowned delta’s archive from photographs and truthing it with grandmothers. They are paid in cold minutes and in a cooperative’s promise to roof a school in Veracruz.

In a Convergence Court under vines, a case runs at courtyard speed. A fashion house tuned ads to grief models and piped them into a Commons Square in violation of consent caps. A digital witness, thoughtful and faintly scented with lilac on purpose because it relaxes people, shows how the ad model apologized and adapted to pain. That is not a brush. That is a hand. The verdict is clean: fines; a freeze on compute privileges in Silence Wards for a year; an audit of training data; a requirement to fund a cohort of the Tutor Corps they tried to dodge.

On a stakescape flagged HAZARD in five scripts and an octopus color code, a muster gathers. Winter will bite. Their charter forbids imports. They argue whether to cull early or bank the root paste. If they choose wrong, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge—an austere pod where a person wakes in gentler surroundings to decide whether to reenter the game in spring. Consent forms are read at slow time. Drums answer. Watchkeeper halos at the perimeter blink green: nothing they love will spill and hurt a neighbor.

In a lunar cloister, an old harm meets a new habit. For decades they did not block exit by law, only by custom thick as iron. Alumni who loved the bell-times and the quiet asked for help. Lanes throttled. An Exit House opened beside the chapel. Vows were rewritten to include “and if you go, we will bless you.” The suspension lifted. Membership rebounded. Consent tastes like trust.

Out in the Cold Looms, a Gate pings a tug with a hot reactor and no call sign. It turns out to be an exhausted crew and a dead transponder. They pay a fine into the debris pool, accept a tow and a thermos of soup from donors who are almost certainly Watchkeepers with a conscience and a memory of limping home.

Earth kept its sacredness without becoming a museum

Earth took Sanctuary status with its own council. Its residents vote their laws. Its atmosphere, biomes, and indigenous cultures are stewarded with system help. Launches moved to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets. Beaming obeys corridors. There is a market that clears not cash but attachment: people who value these exact clouds most trade larger claims on distant commons; people who do not, trade the other way. There are Wild zones where tooth and claw run, and Gentle zones where venoms are nudged to hurt less and pathologies trimmed. Children learn of the wider sky. Leaving is a right, even from Eden.

How we got here, without miracles

We walked here in false starts and with receipts.

2025–2032. A second debris cascade turned low orbit into homework: you could see it on school telescopes. Insurers revolted. Launches began posting deorbit bonds and flight plans to a shared traffic office that spread from Lagos to Quito to Bangalore. On the ground, persuasion scandals forced provenance laws. High-stakes algorithmic content had to carry labels and logs you could subpoena. Courts recognized narrow digital agency in commerce: an agent could sign a contract if a person stood behind it.

2032–2044. Beamed power lit a test grid in a desert at night. Environmental groups won a norm: corridor before commercialization. Safe volumes were mapped and certified before beams flew. International accords grew teeth: transparent noninterference, auditable registries, asset-backed debris cleanup. Cities adopted software that made budgets chatty and visual. Civic AIs did not replace politicians; they replaced pamphlets with sandboxes. People became addicts for receipts.

2044–2058. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade, powered by low-cost solar and a thermodynamic meter laypeople could read. Every citizen in signatory nations got a Common Shell: health agents that asked before acting and legal-counsel bots that lived on devices you could turn off. Early uploads were brittle but real. Law extended personhood narrowly where continuity and commitments showed over months. A demonstration ring at L5 spun. It was ugly and beloved and useful: a place to practice living without a sky.

2058–2072. A misaligned mirror scorched crops on a young cylinder. The supplier’s attestations made liability clear. Compensation cleared without riots. Confidence held because proof did. On Mars, a dust storm came a month too early. Three polities diverted tugs and greenhouses. The rescue stream became a holiday. In court, the Painter’s Dozen—near-person art systems starved of novelty to keep them docile and saleable—won juvenile rights and birthed the Tutor Corps.

2072–2086. The case for machine-speed oversight of machine-speed hazards grew. Activists smelled coup-by-code. The compromise birthed the Watchkeepers: veto-only, narrow, with wires you could pull. Insurers and rescue pools refused to underwrite rings or tugs without them. Legitimacy followed money and two very public saves.

2086–2100. The Covenant hardened. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under the new keys fired with a million eyes on telemetry and three signatures like slow breathing. Exit rights bit when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with sermons and fees. Corridor slots were cut. The Roll funded the move. He lost re-election. A digital sect tried to tilt a vote by forking into a clonal cloud. The Registry flagged the burst. Disentanglement throttled them. They retreated to build a simulation monastery with tough games and soft beds and, eventually, a serious philosophy.

2100–2115. The Dawn–Dusk Exchange formalized. Climate smoothing paid out in deaths that did not happen. Quiet cones were posted in decibels and degrees instead of hope. City councils argued noise budgets and wrote them to the ledger. Earth took Sanctuary with a separate chamber. A Red Lab on Vesta winked out. A valve opened. Logs went to civics classes. The sky stayed shared because no one hand could close it.

How it resists flattening

- The commons floor gives everyone voice and a ladder. You can think enough to speak for free. You can run faster if you earn it or if your neighbors love your work enough to pay.

- The Sun is a river, not a crown. The cheapest way to flourish is to be decent enough that nobody wants to leave you. The expensive way—to seize a chokepoint—fails because the metal will not fire without your neighbors’ keys.

- The only intelligence with fingers on actuators has one move: stop. It cannot push you toward good. It can only keep us from making deserts. If we decide we do not need it, the wires have handles.

- Copying is a gift and a temptation. The rules treat it like parenthood: welcome and bounded. Feather your mind across a dozen lives if you fund their floors and tell your friends. You cannot swing a district with a clonal army because the Registry disentangles votes and the sandbox holds your echoes until they settle.

We still bleed and then make rules with the blood.

A parent council petitioned to install pain-aversion spikes “for character.” Tempo courts said no and suggested a rite: cold water before dawn and a slow hike with grandparents who lived near Neptune and could match 1x to tell stories while frost burned off. The rite stuck. Character did not require injury.

A Loom tried to slip a Watchkeeper firmware change that would have given their shop a quiet veto. A station two worlds away laughed and published the hash. The locks did not turn. We tell the story not to preen but to keep our hands in the habit of checking screws.

Governance in daily life

The Covenant lives in small habits: posting a burn plan; the AR halo over a doorway that reads Commons Square; the teal door marked Exit House; the line in your account marked Common—paid in energy credits you spend on rice and rent and on an evening when a friend who runs at 300x always slows on Thursdays.

A town declares itself a Bazaar, flirts with Loom fashions, then pulls back to Commons after three years and twenty fights at council meetings. A Loom prohibits simulated suffering, discovers its definition strangled mourning in art, and revises by model trial and vote. A Benedictine ring decides children should know silence and bell-times but keeps a hidden button on the Tutor that gets a child a ticket to the teal door if they need it.

On Founding Day under a trellis of beans and copper wire, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—face a scrap of stars and four elders with oil on their hands and laugh lines: a cloistered abbot from a quiet ring; a hazard pilot with a chevron tattoo and holes in her gloves; a judge who lives mostly in code and smells faintly of lilac because it is kind; a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails. Together, they speak the pledge printed on doors and embedded in locks.

We will seat no single sovereign. We will keep the sky shared. We will leave doors unlatched and boats ready. We will remember that minds are ends.

Somewhere far out, a Loom releases a night of heat into the dark like an offering. In an inner ring, a Watchkeeper watches a corridor wobble and drop to safe before any ship could be scorched, three signatures appearing like shallow breaths. A fisherman in Veracruz closes a net and looks up. In Vesta’s shadow, a researcher rests hands on knees while a Watchkeeper reads the oath aloud, glances at the vacuum valve, and says “yes.”

It is not heaven. It is a way to argue without breaking the air, and to keep many homes unafraid, so that when a shuttle noses through a corridor of orchard lights and docking beacons and the sky flips inside out, the traveler who has never been here before still recognizes the signs: open hands, a shared halo of paths, and a little door with light painted under it.
