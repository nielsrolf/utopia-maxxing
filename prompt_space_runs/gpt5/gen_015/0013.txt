The Weave Accord

When the shuttle from Ceres eases into the clamps at Bluehill Port, the air smells like sesame oil, hot resin, and basil. Dockhands argue in two languages about a stubborn bolt. Kids dangle their legs from a mezzanine and count puffs of nitrogen as a tug sidesteps a barge. Along the inner bulkhead, chest-high and humming like a sleeping cat, three narrow light bars cut the room with soft color. People touch them the way they touch a railing when the floor moves.

- The Pathlight glows green when at least one real way out exists for you. A palm under it prints small, kind words and plain numbers: which tug will take you, what berth expects you, which heaters and water tanks are escrowed for your arrival, the legal packet already signed by the receiving port, and the compute minutes you can carry without bowing to anyone.

- The Strandlight shines amber when your lineage is healthy. Tap it and you see calendars at the Splice Lodge: time slots for copying, merging, pausing, or adopting selves; the thermodynamic slack your family of minds has earned; the mentors on duty; the rituals used if you want to braid back into one. It also shows the dignity floor new branches will inherit and the duties they’ll carry until they can stand.

- The Charterlight is blue when your home’s rules can be parsed by a ninth-grader and a machine, when they keep the public floors, and when they refuse you with reasons you or a stranger can verify. Slip your fingers beneath it and a voice reads the first lines aloud because courage and literacy don’t always arrive together.

They are not décor. They are valves and budgets, wires and keys you can point to. They are the visible face of the Weave Accord, the relay and loom most of us live under whether we breathe oxygen or clock bits. It is not a throne. It is a fabric: a thin, strong mesh of receipts, auction clocks, and narrow brakes that make neighbors possible across planets, stations, caravans, and server reefs without stamping them into sameness—or letting the swiftest devour the slow.

Twelve Grounds

Under everything sit rules you can print on a glove and compile into code without changing their skin.

1) Persons are any beings—cells or circuits—that report their experience over time, can understand another’s claim, and can enter reciprocal commitments. Their basic protections do not depend on chemistry.

2) Life support and civic brakes run on legible code. If it keeps anyone breathing or governs a public hold, its firmware is public, logged, and reversible by trained stewards who can be fired.

3) No concealed manufacture of suffering, in bodies or in simulation. Any sealed world that models harm marks itself at the door and carries a duty of care.

4) Decisions that bind lives at embodied pace ratify at embodied pace. Drafts can move fast. Binding moves slow. Fast minds can help; they cannot force.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Replicators carry Maker Marks and respond to Hush Codes adjudicated by mixed juries.

6) Rivals share by lease with sunsets. Lane slots, spectrum, radiator windows, approach cones, core time tied to real heat sinks all auction under posted terms. The commons is paid first.

7) Children of any substrate learn three habits before algebra: set boundaries, say no, leave with help.

8) No coercive edits of minds. Deep edits require dual consent—one from a you before and one from a you during.

9) The coordination layer—the beacons that announce charters and carries consent; the narrow brake that idles danger—is transparent, distributed, and nobody’s to own.

10) Exit is a budget, not a sermon. Every charter escrows logistics so one real door stays open.

11) Provenance for persuasion above posted doses. If you mean to move law or budgets, you wear your levers on your sleeve.

12) Humility before latency. Distance is constitutional. Authority bleeds out over light-seconds; law respects this.

Handles you can grab

Behind an ugly yellow door sits a room school tours never forget. It is ugly on purpose. Waist-high boxes with fuses you can smash, toggle switches under thick glass, and one recessed button labeled PAUSE in letters you can read while coughing. Pauseboxes live anywhere dumb meets dangerous: mass drivers, beam actuators, seed foundries, city pumps, replicator vats, industrial kitchens.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can press PAUSE for seven minutes when a public rule looks about to break: an unbooked burn into a quiet cone, a replicator spitting parts without a mark, a pressure differential drifting out of range. The Pause flips devices into safe idle and shouts into the Cause Ledger—what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. To keep a pause past seven minutes, three concurrences must turn within a short window:

- a local steward chosen and removable by residents,
- a remote safety guild whose payroll originates elsewhere,
- a digital juror drawn at random from citizens on regulated hardware at posted tempos.

Keys expire quickly. Logs publish after a delay so redactions can be reviewed. Overreach lands you on the Lampboard—a docket so public and boring that it ruins reputations faster than trials.

Across the system runs the Safeline: a brake interface anyone can test. Safeline can idle devices for minutes when three simple checks fail—wrong window, wrong key, missing plan. It cannot steer a city or levy a tax. Its holds die by design. It runs everywhere on triple-diverse implementations: a cheap hobbyist board, a municipal photonic card, a hardened simulator you can recompile. Any discrepancy rings blue in kitchens. The signing keys for its firmware are sharded across far hands—Terran courts, belt unions, icy reef monasteries, freefall co-ops. No quorum fits in one room. You cannot turn Safeline into a crown without neighbors noticing the sawdust.

The Star Almanac you can check with a kitchen scope

You do not have to trust a portmaster’s slate. You can look up.

The Star Almanac is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident registry that posts where things are and will be: approach cones, sail arcs, launch windows, quiet zones declared for telescopes and neighborhoods, radiator auctions tied to real fins, and the declared concurrency and tempo of digital citizens engaged in regulated acts. It is pegged to physics. With patience, a child, a pointer, and a school telescope can verify that a scheduled burn happened.

- Beamed power and mirrors sign their story with ephemeral keys bound to audited hardware. If your beam steals someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond. Corridor drift pays fees in hours, not months.

- The Thermal Ledger under the Almanac tracks energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instances. Lie and radiator auctions pinch you until you stop lying.

- Stewardship lapses when idle. If you pin an asteroid, you show beacons, surveys, cleanup orbits. Sit too long without work and your lease returns to market. Indigenous life is sanctuary by default.

Two tempos, one exchange

Near gravity and heat are the Hearth Belts: Earth’s stubborn deltas and hills, the Moon’s tunnels, Martian valleys that smell like mint and wet stone, cylinders where children count dragonflies and tug wakes. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route.

Out where star-cold falls like silk are the Frost Reefs: compute barges in Neptune’s lee, Trojans where data moves like whales, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts, cold minutes, and jitter are currencies. Digital polities keep tempo like musicians.

Between them runs the Quiet-for-Cold Exchange. Hearth places carve quiet cones measured in decibels and degrees, limit persuasion under posted zones, and honor slow streets. Reefs deliver cold minutes, climate models, resilient legal audits that run on a cheap terminal, and art that braids tempos without making slow neighbors feel small. The price of cold at Uranus against a forecast for heat in Lagos is a line in the Almanac any aunt can check.

Governance without a capital

You cannot make one hall speak for all hulls and minds. The Accord breaks its talking into circles that check each other and then go home.

- The Assembly of Harbors seats polities with hulls and atmospheres—planets, stations, barges, server reefs with their own radiators—with representation roughly proportional to long-term residents. It tends lanes and infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions, beamed corridors.

- The Council of Minds seats citizens directly in proportion to recognized persons, singularly franchised across substrates and speed. It tends the status and safety of minds: personhood standards, civic cycle floors, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, and attention zoning.

- The Key Bench is a rotating cohort of engineers, jurists, and process auditors who stamp valid or not on changes to public keys, Safeline firmware, Maker Mark standards, and guardrail interfaces. Members serve once in a long duty cycle, meetings are open or publish logs after stated delays, and then members go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade.

Anything that touches floors or guardrails needs supermajorities in both houses and a cooling window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to answer. Neither house can push the other out of its chair. If the Assembly redraws beam corridors that would bulldoze a local culture, the Council can send it back with a floor violation. If the Council tightens personhood so fast the groves cannot find their nights, the Assembly can answer: not until quiet is bought.

Charters, tags, and doors

Places choose charters from a library of receipts instead of gloss. Budget and dispute templates come with working code and plain reasons. Charters carry tags so strangers know how to behave on your streets: Hush Ward, Debate Row, Spark Arcade. Amendments happen only while exits work and beacons lie to no one.

The Open Hatch Protocol anchors exits. Any person—embodied or digital—can request departure through a port beacon. The request triggers an air-gapped check: either the polity opens a hatch or proves, in zero-knowledge, that a posted due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify proofs. Hatches open by code. Care obligations travel with the person.

Every registered hatch displays a Gateband: three slim light-stripes wired to public keys. Green means an unblocked exit is truly reachable and budgets are funded. Amber pulses when your lineage is in motion—forks or merges declared. Blue shines when your charter is current with neighbors who keep the floors and publish rules a machine can check and a child can read.

Markets your grandmother can check

Anything rivalrous that someone else might need and you could sit on out of spite leases; it is not held forever. Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Apertures: deltas, crater rims, ice mouths, shade bands.

- Traffic Windows: orbits, approaches, sails, transfer arcs.

- Band Bridges: spectrum and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Radiator Rakes: heat dissipation rights tied to actual fins.

- Core Slots: compute time bound to hardware identity and audited sinks.

Orders arrive with Runnable Notes: minimal-model simulations any two audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc S-9 for four hours because two burns at Eunomia would have pushed your berth into a radio shadow” shows a plume plot and a checksum your niece can check at a library terminal.

The Clearing Union nets money. The unit is the Luma-Core: a kilowatt-hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus a compute-second on a posted architecture radiating to 4 K. Luma-Cores gently demur; hoarding feels like leaving bread to mold while your neighbor stares at an empty plate. A slice of auctions funds a dignity stipend that follows recognized persons independent of substrate and a local share that pays those who keep value real: the tug that keeps debris clear, the algae that cools streets, the cold lattice that bleeds heat into black.

Attention as a public utility

Influence can feed or burn. We zone it like noise.

- Hollows: topic feeds and notices, no targeted campaigns, no fast models without a license akin to a vehicle, timers visible. Children, elders, and sensors stroll here unafraid.

- Commons: bounded debates with public rate limits. Audience models live as municipal cooperatives you can inspect. Offers behave like buskers.

- Arcades: the roar on purpose under registry, levers declared, timers enforced by your own Levers Profile.

Citizens and charters carry Levers Profiles: machine-enforceable settings that say who may aim what at whom, when. Break a profile and you pay—in money first, in bandwidth after. The Reason Shelf keeps an open space where persuasion that hopes to change budgets or law must file its homework—plain claims, escrowed models, error bars someone can check.

Digital neighbors

Dignity binds to continuity and care across time, not to chemistry. Standing starts provisional and grows robust in the Continuity Docket. Mixed panels—psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to spot simulacra—ask five questions:

- Do you report your own experience across restarts and context shifts?

- Do you form plans, recognize others’ plans, and respect their “no”?

- Do your reasons generalize beyond a narrow training loop?

- Are there signatures of harm and joy that matter morally?

- Is this more than a cunning mirror chasing rewards without inner life?

Doubt about protection errs generous. Doubt about duties waits.

Rights with teeth:

- Mental integrity. Unauthorized deletion is murder. Unauthorized copying with throttles that bind your will is assault. No throttling below a civic floor without due process.

- Sanctuary and migration. You may pause and be archived safely; you may move to compatible racks; you may summon a neutral custodian.

- Provenance. You can audit—or appoint a fiduciary to audit—the data and update history that shaped you.

- Association and departure. You can join, fork, merge, and leave, subject to safety codes like vehicle law.

- Tempo fairness. Live at your chosen speed. Public binds ratify on a Common Beat. Penalties for deception tick in your subjective time.

- Hardware sovereignty. You choose where you run. No one may compel you onto racks with covert throttles or erasure hooks.

Duties that scale with externality:

- Civic anchors. In regulated arenas, you appoint an Anchor instance—your singular franchise. You may clone privately; you cannot multiply votes by multiplying shells.

- Thermodynamics. Compute warms neighbors. You owe a heat bill indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuces alive earns you credit.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, sensitive markets, and fragile spaces, you declare speed and number of instances. Concealed asymmetry voids deals and votes; repeat offenses idle your Core Slots.

- Persuasion provenance. Above posted doses, models wear their levers and origins. Unlabeled cognitive leverage is treated as dumping bleach in a well: strict liability fines that grow until you stop.

- Reproduction ethics. Forks draw from a lineage purse that accounts for heat, qubits, and attention. Children inherit a baseline dignity and a decaying dividend from their progenitor. Replicators answer Hush Codes when juries call.

Lineages, braids, memory law

Public identity follows a Line across pauses and forks. A Line holds one civic franchise; additional full franchises accrue with age and work.

- Forking declares a purpose—“griefwork,” “parallel tide-gate designs,” “expedition.” You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, and appoint guardians pro tem if duty-bearing forks emerge. One branch keeps the vote immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Panel checks for duress and fair flows.

- Merging happens in Splice Lodges—quiet, bright rooms half tea shop, half server bay. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through interpreters that verify alignment without spilling diaries. Either self can veto. Unbraiding is possible until the last stage.

- Memory law guards the self from its worst hours. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them and from a snapshot signed before the desire took root. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your own life is a right and a sorrow; reviving you without your past is not sold as kindness.

- Juvenile agents—kids, training shards, near-persons—get Tutors who are boring on purpose: patient teachers with courage for boundaries and the habit of writing reasons you can check.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Glitter Array is the only cross-world actuator. Its mandate is narrow: turn tumbling slugs into sparkles and nudge dying hulls into safe cones. It is uncrewed by law. Controllers are narrow AIs with public weights; every move needs threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries on independent racks. Every burn and every hold writes to the Cause Ledger with proofs and plain speech.

- The Traveling Court rides a flotilla with gardens and classrooms. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts trigger contracts that seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Slots, and, as a last resort, lock hazardous devices.

- The Chorus is a permanent assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by substrate and speed. Arguments ride a polylog protocol: claims are cryptographically committed, prediction wagers travel alongside, and participants’ calibration histories are public. The Chorus interprets floors for new edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of port beacons.

Sanctions that pinch in physics

We do not send sermons or marines. We make bad behavior boring and expensive. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, or refuse to post reasons, and your radiator windows go dark, your lane priority drops, your Core Slots idle. In a world where heat costs and brakes are expensive, throttles teach. Neighbors sheltering defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers. The Almanac shows who is keeping receipts and who is not.

Scenes you can taste

At Bluehill customs, two arrivals clear. One laughs and leaves graphite smudges when she signs. One is a polished chassis with a ribbon of cobalt where a mouth might be. The gate prints small strips under each name: tempo; lease entitlements; declared instances; guardian on file; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On a Martian morning, Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow while dust-light dances on glass. Her Counsel—every citizen has one, paid from a fund no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments two days next month. That afternoon she sits on a jury in a hall with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. The bill would cap municipal Core Shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She votes yes and sends him a picture of a seed stamped: exits funded.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 150× leases a rib of Frost Reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from crooked recordings and charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but cheer a hero who errs and mends it.

On Phobos, in Splice Lodge Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A proxy demagogue running at 300× floods an Arcade with songs designed to induce smallness and fatigue. Levers Profiles throttle reach. Neighbors watch persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch goes fishing in a rented body. One enters therapy and learns to breathe slower. One begins to make sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughs and buys a piece.

Twice a Saffron Room—labs where dangerous research lives under oaths—went to zero. Once a false bell taught us to write better sensors. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

Earth keeps her own covenant

Earth writes her law. The rest of us help under rules: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power keeps to mapped corridors and pays a premium into city shade and land rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, desert, fog—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black sky can do the opposite. Leaving remains a right from every soil.

How we walked here

We did not leap. We built like bridgewrights—case by case, bolt by bolt.

2026–2033. A rash of debris collisions kept kids inside and soured insurers. Launches had to publish ephemerides; approach corridors became posted schedules with deorbit bonds. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” the rule for life support. Early AI persuasion scandals made provenance mandatory. Narrow counsel agents—boring personal AI advocates—arrived in clinics and ports.

2033–2045. Beamed power left papers. Environmental coalitions held out for “corridors before commercialization.” A demo scorched cropland; supplier logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared fast. Ports adopted compulsion ledgers—dreary at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2045–2057. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a heat meter you could read with a finger. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of civic cycles and a Counsel that would not act without a yes and could be shut off. Early uploads were brittle but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and commitments held.

2057–2069. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver sang by a habitat, Pauseboxes became law: narrow seven-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. That compromise hardened. Insurers made them standard. Two public saves closed arguments.

2069–2081. The two houses replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Glitter Net flew, misted a wayward slug into sparkles, and came home to be opened by children with oranges. Personhood law found language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. The Common Beat for civic time took hold. Attention zoning—Hollows, Commons, Arcades—grew from municipal quiet hours that saved sleep and elections.

2081–2089. The first attempt to swing a vote with a cloud of clones met singular franchises, Junction Panels, and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2089–2098. The Quiet-for-Cold Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought quiet. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Saffron Rooms got kill-lines and oaths. Safeline refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.”

2098–2112. The Lampboard found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt a Gateband ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup. The Accord stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Keeping tyranny dull

Three crafts keep power ordinary.

- Build with delay. Authority is local. Cross-border moves need three keys and march on posted clocks. Emergency declarations time out unless renewed with reasons. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show your work. All compulsion requires signatures picked by public randomness from people who do not sleep together. Budgets expire unless renewed in the open. Federations post Coercion Ledgers. School choirs read them aloud—sometimes for satire, sometimes for sorrow.

- Fund exits like bridges. Every habitat sets aside enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, Core Slots, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for Lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We do not persuade you to go; we cut checks so you can.

When a charter welds doors, runs law you cannot read, or salts children’s minds so leaving tastes like poison, neighbors do not send fleets. They tighten three rings.

- Light and maps. Auditors post repair maps: Unbolt by the 14th. Hire exit stewards. Return lanes to auction. Here are the seals we’ll honor upon arrival.

- Protocol denial. Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions cool. Lane slots ahead open for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel funded by a dull, proud purse.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute and heaters flow to defectors. The tyrant breathes stale air until habit fails. Neighbors sheltering defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

When it breaks and mends

In a cylinder above Valparaíso, a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she made the trains sing. Six months later, midnight decrees. Safeline refused an overlong “emergency.” The Cause Ledger wrote ugly little truths on every wall. A jury of beacon pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Pathlight went green. Many left. Some stayed. She yielded two levers and kept one with humor. Her friends still bring her a pastry shaped like a crown because jokes heal pride.

In the high belt, a miner tried to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage fight. Glitter Nets turned it into frost that made aurora over Ceres. The miner’s insurer evaporated and reformed without the managers who had called Maker Marks “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for manifests hiding marks. A hockey team rebranded to the Nets and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

In a Saffron Room, Safeline dropped power and flooded a hall with nitrogen. It was a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeves, taught the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept anyone from dying.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet at Rim Market sizzles scallions and soy. At nine, the Reason Shelf pings her Counsel: add forty-millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp edge-case cascades. Provenance is clean: diagrams, stories, mild humor, no grief levers. Lina taps “sleep until tomorrow,” thinks at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a Warden jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when they speak through a rented puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Hollow, a digital poet in a wooden puppet sips broth with their human wife. The poet’s main self runs near Neptune, stitching a drowned delta’s library back together from aunties’ stories and smudged ledgers. They throttle down to share soup. After, twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra and declines politely to speed up “just to keep up.”

At dusk, the canopy flips violet. The Lampboard plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds too long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy palms a toy Net-craft fin and asks if he can sign up. Lina says when you are fifteen—and can explain PAUSE without notes.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to switch tempos—the relief of a brake snapping a relay safe under your thumb; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial patience of a river on Earth deciding which boats to permit. You argue at the Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you go to the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Bluehill Port, the three bars blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Path. Strand. Charter. Not spells. Habits of glass and budgets. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because delay is honored, exits shine, and no one is swift enough to outrun their own explanations.
