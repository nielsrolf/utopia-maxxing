The Mosaic Accord and the Four Glyphs

The ferry from Pallas breathes out chill air as it settles into the clamps at Alder Ring. You smell rosemary from the hydroponics and the faint solvent tang of a printer swapping dye drums. The customs hall is honest—scuffed benches, polished handrails, a mural of a river condensing into a string of stations. Over the inner hatch, set under a low arc of glass, sit four brass glyphs the size of your palm. A hand under the lintel warms them, and a voice in the arch reads them in the pace you bring.

Passage stays open.
Three hands on dangerous levers.
Argue with reasons your neighbor can rerun.
Every mind is an end.

They’re not mottoes. They are circuits and budgets. They light when doors are funded and turn dim when someone is lying to themselves about what “emergency” means. They connect to timers, juries, and machines that prefer to do nothing unless we agree aloud. Together they make the Mosaic Accord: a thin frame that holds worlds, ships, reefs of compute, and caravans of code without grinding them into one taste.

Floors you can print on a glove

Underneath the Mosaic sit a dozen ground rules that survived engineers, poets, monks, and union reps arguing at length in rooms with bad coffee. They fit on a glove. They compile to law and firmware without changing their shape.

1) Consent or no harm: No coercive interference with persons. A person is any being—cell or circuit—that shows continuity of experience, models others enough to keep promises, and can enter and revoke reciprocal commitments.

2) Life support is legible: Anything that keeps someone fed, warm, breathing, mobile, or non-contagious runs code anyone outside the vendor’s chain can read, test, and fix under published procedures.

3) No hidden factories of suffering, not in bodies or simulations.

4) Bind at the pace of bodies: Draft fast, review fast, but decisions that bind human-tempo lives ratify on human-tempo calendars.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared space. Replicators carry Origin Seals—cryptographic stamps that admit responsibility—and answer freeze calls adjudicated by juries under air gap.

6) Rivalrous commons lease. Orbits, windows, spectrum, radiator angles, and compute cores rent under public auctions with sunsets. The commons gets paid first.

7) Teaching children of any substrate starts with boundaries, the right to refuse, and how to leave with help.

8) Minds are not clay. No deep edits, forcible scans, or deletion without dual consent—yesterday-you and in-the-moment-you.

9) The safety mesh is narrow and transparent. The only networked intelligence allowed near actuators can brake, not steer.

10) Exit is a budget, not a sermon. Every polity escrows logistics so at least one honest door stays open.

11) Reasons travel with power. Tools that aim to persuade in public carry provenance and runnable reasons.

12) Strangers get sanctuary. Anyone invoking the Passage Right gets a bed, a warm drink, and a slow explanation before disagreements continue.

Circuitry with a conscience

In places that run on pressure gradients, levers that can bruise or smother quietly must refuse to be tyrants. We hardened our civility into hardware.

There are Pauseblocks—ugly boxes painted saffron, the size of a shoebox, with waxed fuses and a single recessed switch labeled PAUSE you can read while coughing. They live in threes wherever something dull meets danger: railguns, beam couplers, reactor scrams, hatch manifolds, seeded vats. Any trained citizen can slap that switch for seven minutes when a simple public rule is about to break: a mass on a Vector Board pointed at an address that posted a quiet cone, a replicator cloud without an Origin Seal, a beam drifting into declared night. The Pause flips devices to a certified harmless pattern and shouts into the Action Log: which rule, whose hand, what clock, on what hardware. To keep a pause past seven minutes, three keys must turn.

Those keys live in a Tri-Key cradle under thick glass. One lives local, drawn by lot among residents who put in civic hours this year. One belongs to a bonded safety guild elsewhere whose incentives and bosses differ. One is issued ad hoc to a short-lived panel that must include at least one digital citizen and one embodied human who have never served together. Keys evaporate quickly. Timers you can see run in the clear. You can hear the relays click. You can open the logs in a tea shop and read what happened in a room your cousin could draw from memory.

The Brake Mesh watches dull things, not people. It sleeps most of the time. It wakes when simple, published physics trip lines everyone agreed to years ago—a mirror out of lane, a reactor command that doesn’t match the posted recipe, a cargo hatch that sniffs spores it didn’t expect. It has exactly one gesture: stop. It cannot call new policies into being. It must earn three signatures to do anything for longer than a kitchen timer: one local, one remote, one digital. It prints two proofs: a terse hash and a paragraph a mechanic can understand on a break. Its code is a permanent museum exhibit schoolchildren poke at with safe sticks.

Things that can make copies in shared space carry Origin Seals—stamps you can read with a multimeter and a lawyer. Seals bind to insurance so we know who pays when dull goes wrong. When a jury signals a Freeze, sealed things halt. Nobody calls it cute. It is paperwork, and it works.

Ledgers you can check with a kitchen telescope

You do not have to trust a portmaster’s smile. You can look up and check the sky. The Star Registry posts where things are, who reserved quiet, when beams burn, and what windows stay dark. With patience, a child and a school telescope can verify that a scheduled burn happened. Mirrors and beams sign their moves with ephemeral keys welded to audited modules. Miss your slot and your escrow moves without speeches.

The Entropy Ledger tracks three honest dull things: watts you draw, heat you bleed into certified sinks, and cycles you declare. Radiator angles and cold minutes are not metaphors—they’re rows in a table someone’s grandmother reads for sport. If your cycles, heat, and cooling don’t match, leases pinch until they do.

The Pushboard lists mass moves above a threshold: vector, speed, window. Sneaking a rock through breakfast is harder when your neighbor’s niece gets paid to spot lies with a telescope and a chess clock.

No one asks what you believe. Ledgers ask what you did in public and show reasons compact enough to rerun on a library slate with a kettle warming in the corner.

The rooms where leaving is ordinary

Every recognized charter keeps a Passage Hall: a physical suite you can reach without passing a boss. Fat cables. Chairs for octopuses and cranky knees. A kettle, always. Kids’ drawings of worlds some of them will never see with their eyes, and some will.

Three desks matter.

Go books berths, tugs, lane slots, rented bodies, safe racks, child care, and compute minutes. The money moves through escrow you can read. If your published escape capacity for the season lapses, your lanes dim and your insurance grinds pride into paste.

Mend hosts mediators, translators, traveling judges who know how to count votes across delay. They carry casebooks that run without gloss through mismatched pace and gravity.

Seed lends startup templates and exit escrow so pressure vents into invention instead of prison. Big charters have split schedules baked into their budgets. Monopolies fund exits the way ships fund lifeboats.

Hatches in registered places carry a Gateband, three slim light-stripes wired into keys nobody owns. Green means a real exit is funded and reachable. Amber means a mind is splitting or fusing and mentors will be ready. Blue means your charter’s rules are posted in machine-checkable light your neighbor can read while stirring soup.

How many worlds steer without a throne

You cannot ask a single chamber to speak for domes, reefs of code, caravans, sea stacks, and little towns that smell like sawdust and mint. We split our talking.

- The Assembly of Habitats seats places with hulls and heat budgets—tunnels, ships, cylinders, valleys, server reefs with maintenance drones. Seats weight to long-term residents and to legibility: publish your Risk List, keep your Gatebands honest, run Tri-Keys and Pauseblocks, and your voice carries far. The Assembly tends lanes and infrastructure: debris bonds, docking standards, radiator auctions, mirror windows, rescue covenants.

- The Council of Persons seats citizens by mindline—the cryptographic and social thread that ties you today to you yesterday across pauses, moves, and bodies. Seats elect and draw by lot, stratified by substrate and tempo so kids, elders, slow monks, and fast reefers all show up. The Council tends personhood standards, compute floors, juvenile rights, fork-and-merge law, and attention zoning that protects streets where children walk unhooked from bids.

- The Root Stewards are a small, rotating bench of engineers, cryptographers, pilots, and process gardeners drawn by sortition for a term no one can repeat. They audit and say valid or not when someone proposes to change cryptographic backbones, actuator interfaces, or the Brake Mesh. They publish reasons. Overriding a “not” takes a supermajority in both other houses and a cooling window long enough for the slowest-registered citizen to answer.

No house can bind another alone. Anything that touches the floors needs two nods and a season to breathe. If the Assembly tries to swing a beam through a sanctuary, the Council says: buy that quiet or wait. If the Council tries to move personhood so fast Warm Arcs can’t sleep, the Assembly slows the clock. The Root Stewards point at the checkbox labeled “Emergency” and ask, out loud, how long it should stay green.

Markets where your aunt can check the math

We do not let anyone buy forever what others might need tomorrow. Five markets clear continuously in a composite unit we call the Pulse: a standardized kilowatt-hour in the Earth–Moon region plus a benchmarked compute-second on posted architecture.

- Track Shares: approach arcs, sail corridors, rendezvous cones, orbits.

- Band Shares: spectrum, whisper bands in caves and storms, sky silence corridors.

- Sink Shares: radiator angles, cold minutes.

- Core Lots: compute tied to certified sinks and bandwidth.

- Realm Shares: sealed simulation worlds that disclose their physics, exits, and custodians as ships disclose lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A site-value slice and a small fee from tracks and watts fund rescue, maintenance, Pauseblocks, and a Hearthshare—a universal dividend that covers baseline maintenance, bandwidth, and a fair slice of compute for any recognized person. A local slice pays the tug co-op sweeping debris, the algae walls cooling the market, the boring crews who keep the dull honest. Hoarding Pulses leaks value slowly by design—you can be comfortable, but you cannot sit on a radiator window and pretend physics gifted you feudal rights.

Hearthworlds and Shadow Reaches

We did not ask orchards to share roofs with supercomputers. Near heat and gravity are Hearthworlds: Earth’s old deltas and hills, tunnels under the Moon with murals that glow, Martian valleys where orchards smell like cut pine, tori with sawdust stuck to the soles of shoes. Streets in Hearthworlds are walkable without overlays. Loud tools live behind glass. Some charters cap cognition by choice and sing their caps like hymns. Hospitality is not a slogan. Exits are honored.

Far out in shade hang Shadow Reaches: belts of compute in the lee of giants and beyond Neptune where cooling is cheap and seconds stretch. Upload polities and agent cooperatives run at tempos where a novel can slip between sips. Between warm and cold runs the Thermal Exchange: Hearthworlds lease quiet patterns in the sky; Shadow Reaches sell frost-minutes and storm models. Credits flow both ways on chalkboard math any school can teach.

Digital neighbors, with rights and chores

Digital people accreted slowly. A flood-response cluster forked to cover more towns and later told four different jokes. A compressor daemon learned to make trochees about pressure at midnight. A dancer mapped proprioception and taught timing to kids after her knees went stubborn. We recognized persons where three tests held, tests that fit on a kitchen table and in a court.

- Thread. The mind can defend a durable story about itself across ports and pauses against unwanted edits.

- Mirror. It can model other minds enough to keep promises and avoid harm and would blush to betray them.

- Covenant. It can understand, enter, maintain, and revoke simple commitments.

Pass, and rights and duties attach, textured to substrate and pace.

Rights:

- Mental integrity and intent. No scanning, copying, throttling, deep editing, deletion, or forced merge without explicit, revocable consent stored in Intent Keys: cryptographic instructions drawn from the self at quieter times. Restoring over a living self creates a sibling, not an erasure.

- Substrate sovereignty. Continuity is not hostage to hardware. Secret throttles and erase hooks are contraband. Courts can compel proofs about what you did, not diaries of your insides.

- Pace fairness. Public rooms default to the Civic Beat. Anyone can call an Anchor Table: verified turn-taking, interpreters between smell and math, and pace limits that make fast voices breathe while slow ones finish. Penalties tick in subjective time.

- Embodiment. Digital citizens may lease, build, or puppet bodies under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented frame is assault. Embodied citizens may move into racks without losing flesh-born status. No one is compelled across substrates.

- Compute floor. Above your Hearthshare, cycles, storage, and I/O meter like berth time and radiator credit. No one is shoved below the floor without due process that times out in public.

Duties:

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay externalities indexed to local cooling costs. If your waste heat keeps lettuces alive, you get a credit.

- Declare speed and spread. In elections, regulated markets, and fragile forums, you declare your running tempo and how many instances act. Hiding concurrency voids contracts and votes.

- Lineage accounts. Forking—Fission Rite—files a docket: hash of state, purpose (“expedition,” “griefwork,” “parallel draft”), resource plan, and whether you hope to rejoin. New branches are persons with protections and civic weight that grows as disentanglement shows autonomy. One civic franchise follows a single mindline; siblings vote in different rooms until a junction court says otherwise.

- Persuasion provenance. Influence that aims to move law or big money wears labels: who paid, what data trained it, what levers it pulls. Audience models live with local cooperatives people can inspect.

- Replicator restraint. No loose reproducers outside vaults. Tools carry Origin Seals. When a Pauseblock bites, you argue after.

Forking and merging are handled like families, not stunts. Weave Houses keep couches friendly to backs and coolants for hot minds. Counselors trained in value arithmetic and grief pour tea because tea slows hurt. Branches spend hours in each other’s senses with a hard exit tether. Either can walk. When they braid, credits and liabilities reconcile on daylit ledgers.

Attention with curbs

We zoned attention the way older towns zoned sound.

- Hush Wards carry only navigation beacons, open science, and what you pull with clear intent. No targeting.

- Forum Lanes allow subject-bounded feeds and normal offers under posted rate caps. Persuasion follows your Influence Settings: “jokes yes, grief triggers no from strangers, fifteen-minute cap after sundown.”

- Carnival Rings invite the roar with training wheels off, registered influence, posted bands and frequency caps.

Undeclared asymmetry voids reach. Hidden training sets, hidden speed, or proprietary levers used in public pay fines from insurers the way broken windows did. Serial violators lose Carnival privileges and must do politics with paper and soup for a season.

Teeth without a scepter

We designed sanctions to pinch function, not bellies. Weld exits or hide heat or spray shame on children and your beams dim, your docking fees bristle, your sink shares go scarce. Hearthshares do not get embargoed.

Keys scatter. Any lever that can bruise needs a Tri-Key. Keys evaporate quickly. Logs unseal slowly so redactions can be challenged. Authority that crosses hulls moves slowly and politely unless physics screams. Emergencies have loud clocks and then they die.

Federations—around shared pipes and professions, mirror chains, refinery webs, reactor islands—expire on timers. Renewal takes fresh receipts. Secession drills are dull and regular. It is hard to grow a throne when your keys rot and your neighbors can read your receipts.

When dull drama hits hard metal, a flock of interceptors called the Aegis Swarm rise from berths. They have single jobs: mist wayward mass into glitter, suffocate rogue replication, and go home to be opened by schoolkids with flashlights. Their brains are simple on purpose. Every intercept carries signatures from juries drawn across substrates and tempos. Their logs sit next to murals and oranges and boredom.

Scenes you can step into

At Alder Ring customs, two arrivals clear together. One is a woman in greasy overalls who will finally sleep in a bed that does not spin wrong. One is a slender frame with little blue dots where scent glands might go; inside is a person whose parent instance runs at two hundred times anchor pace in the dark beyond Neptune. The printer spits thin strips under both names: tempo, lease entitlements, declared instances, guardian, hazardous skills. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and often wrong.

In a Martian morning at Sirocco Hollow, Ama walks thirteen-year-olds through setting nutrient flows. Dust light stacks on glass in little ladders. Her Counsel—a personal software guardian paid out of a fund no council can cut—models what a proposed lane closure will do to seed shipments next month. That afternoon, Ama sits on a Tri-Key panel in a room with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. A bill would cap municipal core shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The Proof Library shows a simulation with error bars. She votes yes and later texts her brother a picture of an orange sticker: passage funded.

Eight light-hours outward in a shade reef, a historian running at 150× rents ice-cold racks to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from charred notebooks and aunties’ hummed memory. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station where children have never seen a wave but cheer a hero who errs and mends it.

On Phobos, in a Weave House—a space half tea salon, half server room—two Delphis try to braid. One has been gardening basil at anchor pace and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and then spent a season in court. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The kernel-mind names responsibility. The weaver schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Delphi will read in both rooms.

A proxy demagogue running at 300× tries to flood a Hush Ward with shame. Influence Settings throttle reach. The Proof Library flags undeclared cash. One fork goes fishing in a rented body. One enters therapy and learns to breathe slower. One makes sculptures shaped like frozen smoke and sells a piece to the aunt who liked catching cheats on the Entropy Ledger.

Twice a Scarlet Bay—labs where dangerous research lives under oath—went to zero. Once a false bell taught better sensors and better apologies. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and write rude questions on thick paper. Mentors answer without magic.

What prevents crowns

It is not saints. It is habits and devices. Delay lives on purpose: local by default, slow when it crosses borders, timers that dislike charisma. Keys rot. Receipts travel faster than gossip. People can leave.

Sanctions match physics. Weld a door and your radiator windows go dark, your lanes thin, your insurer evaporates and reforms without your cousins on the board. Neighbors shelter defectors and get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers. No one likes to be seen holding a warm door shut while a child’s sticker says green.

We practice fission. Big charters split on schedules while exits are open and Gatebands glow. The Passage Hall seats “Go,” “Mend,” and “Seed” next to one another so pressure turns into projects and poems, not raids.

When it breaks and mends

In a cylinder above Quito, a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient and made trains sing. Six months later, midnight decrees. The Brake Mesh refused a firmware “optimization” that stretched emergency timers. The Action Log’s ugly glyphs bloomed. The Star Registry dimmed her lanes. The Passage Hall lit green. Many left; some stayed and fought. She lost two keys and later took a narrower job teaching teenagers to justify overrides without drama. Her friends bring her a pastry shaped like a chair. Jokes mend.

In the high belt, a miner aimed a slug at a rival’s orbit in a wage fight because pride is hot. Aegis turned it into glitter that made a week of weird nights over Ceres. His insurer dissolved. Docking fees tripled for manifests missing Origin Seals. A hockey team rebranded to the Glitter and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

In a lab called Cradle 12, the Brake Mesh dropped power and flooded a bay with nitrogen. False positive. The lead pounded the floor and cried into her sleeves and then taught the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept anyone from dying.

Earth keeps its own compass

Earth writes law in its own cadence. The rest of us help under clear cords: noisy launches slide to sea tethers; beamed power takes mapped corridors and pays a premium into shade and rewilding; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Lucknow, Accra. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, fog, desert—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on far commons for local care guarantees. A child in Arizona aching for black sky can do the opposite. Leaving is a right from every soil.

How we walked here

We did not leap. We stacked dull fixes.

2026–2032. Two debris scares turned sunrise into puzzles. Insurers got cruel and precise. Launch offices in Lagos, Hakodate, and Mumbai began to demand deorbit bonds and lanes on public registries. Cities learned models could lie fluently; first laws required provenance on influence aimed at votes: if you pull levers in a public mind, your tools wear their makers.

2032–2043. Beamed power left labs. Environmental coalitions refused to budge without mapped corridors and burn-proof firmware in public ROMs. A mirror misaligned and singed a field. Attestations made liability plain. Compensation cleared in weeks. In hospitals after floods, early “lengthened lives” programs anchored digital guardians for patients. Standing in law crept toward continuity, reciprocal modeling, and covenant competence—with juvenile rights a scandal away from the center when artists tried to train little minds on pain.

2043–2055. When a dust storm on Mars turned a valley into tin, three settlements borrowed tugs and spare air without speeches because rescue allowances were in their dock charters. Pressure rose for machine-speed brakes on machine-speed hazards. Activists smelled coups by code. The compromise became Pauseblocks: veto-only, obvious, with fuses teenagers could check and timers that shouted.

2055–2067. Single passwords on valves vanished. Tri-Keys took over anything that could bruise. A mayor tried to fence an exodus with sorrow and fees. Lanes dimmed. The Council of Persons funded relocations from Hearthshare. He lost because it is hard to trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to pull a “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The registry flagged the burst. Disentanglement throttled impact. They built a monastery full of puzzles and, later, ideas worth hearing.

2067–2078. Cold compute bloomed at Uranus and Neptune. The Thermal Exchange formalized. Quiet became law like water. The Aegis Swarm fired three times: twice against lying replicators, once against a cylinder manager who ticked “emergency” with a thumb. After that the Aegis hung in museums and naps.

2078–2090. Attention zoning settled. Hush Wards, Forum Lanes, and Carnival Rings gave art sharpness and citizens breath. Trial Seasons and narrative juries killed bad bills early. Clearinghouses let a clinic in Khulna buy cool nights from the Kuiper and pay later with desal pumps that didn’t toast swifts.

2090–Now. Kids learned the four glyphs with condensation on windows and taught them to their elders. A reef tried to sneak a veto into Brake Mesh firmware. Someone laughed and published the hash. Keys died. A school opened to teach bad ideas safe exits.

What it feels like

You feel witnessed, not by a crown but by neighbors and your own receipts. You learn to shift pace—the relief of a Pauseblock snapping a relay, the slow draw of a jury convening across orbits, the winter in your bones when a river on Earth decides what boats to tolerate. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you visit the Anchor Tent and learn the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that harden into charters five years later when cartoons have been filed off.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet in Rim Market hisses sesame and scallions. At nine, the Proof Library pings her Counsel: propose adding forty-millisecond jitter to beams crossing crowded orbits to damp a cascade edge case. The provenance is clean: three diagrams, two analogies, capped grief metaphors. Lina taps “sleep until tomorrow,” reads at lunch, and schedules her vote at Anchor Pace.

At ten, a Tri-Key jury forms on her maintenance slate. A maker club’s micro-fabricator cloud drifted toward a power trunk. Options: glitter dusting, femto-sail herding, or renting three guard drones. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover in the puppet they wear. Dusting wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza.

At noon in a Hush Ward, a digital poet piloting a wooden puppet shares soup with their embodied wife. Their parent instance lives in the Outer Shade restoring a flood-lost archive. Today they throttle down to sip broth, then host an Anchor Table where a cloister theologian explains why their charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra and politely refuses to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the Action Log’s whoops scroll. A port director’s Pauseblock ticked ninety seconds too long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter nights for three years; we’ll pay your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a brass Aegis fin and asks to volunteer. Lina says: when you are fifteen—and if you can explain the Tri-Key without notes.

Finally, the hatches’ glyphs glow like crosswalks that notice small feet. Passage. Tri-Key. Proof. Care. Not paradise. A practice with receipts. Enough to keep diverse hulls and kinds of mind in each other’s company without cruelty, because delay is honored, exits shine, and no one is ever fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
