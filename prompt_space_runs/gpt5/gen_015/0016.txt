The Span and the Beacons

At the east lock of Vallejo Ring, the wall carries three slim light-strips waist-high. They buzz the way a distant transformer does on a damp night. They are wired into budgets and valves and keys you can embroider on a glove.

The top strip is green. It’s the Wayband. If it glows, you can leave without begging. You can press your palm beneath and it will whisper: booked tug time, a berth with working heat, sealed rations staged in crate D-12, a clerk who will meet you in Dock Five, and—if you are digital—the offsite cores that already carry your mirror and the bandwidth reserved to make your move a continuation, not a conjuring trick.

The middle strip is amber. It’s the Kinband. Tap it and your calendar folds open: slots to fork, to merge, to adopt, to pause; the thermodynamic credit your mindline has earned; the cost—and the care plans—to add a child whether they are grown in flesh or spun in code; the counselors and engineers who will stand with you in a Confluence Room when you decide to braid your life to itself.

The bottom strip is blue. It’s the Covenant band. When it holds steady, your home charter is written in prose and proofs a ninth-grader can read and a machine can check. If the charter denies you something, it must say why in a way a stranger can verify with a kitchen scope and a school slate.

The strips are not decoration. They are the visible face of the Span: the thinnest possible set of habits, protocols, and receipts that makes neighbors possible across worlds, stations, and server-hollows without forcing them into sameness or letting the strongest eat the rest. It is not a crown. It is a set of rails with brakes.

Tools everyone can point to

Where breath and bits meet—ports, clinics, courts, and cold rooms—the Span gives three specific devices.

1) Tri-Safe. Any actuator with teeth—thrusters, mass drivers, high-energy beams, big pumps, self-replicators, and persuasion engines above a posted dose—ignores lone orders. It listens for three concurrences in a short window: a steward chosen by locals and removable without blood; a bonded safety guild with hardware and payroll elsewhere; and a jury struck by lot that mixes embodied and digital citizens at regulated tempos. Before the actuator moves, it posts a tiny signed stub anyone can query: my class, my cap, my intended end state, my window. Children learn to read the LEDs.

2) The Open Almanac. Rivalrous things that make neighbors into enemies—approach corridors, sail angles, burst windows, spectrum bands, radiator minutes, compute linked to actual heat sinks—are posted in a public schedule bound to physics. The Almanac is delay-tolerant and tamper-evident. You can check it from a kitchen slate, a tug, or an upland library and corroborate with a telescope or a handheld meter. Leases are set by auction you can rerun. “Forever” does not appear.

3) Funded Hatches. Every chartered place pays into a boring fund that buys departure as a public utility: propellant reserves, tug contracts, berths, seal kits, heater credit, counsel agents, and offsite compute ready to pick up your thread at regulated bandwidth. Drills are holidays with popcorn and sore fingers. Fines for flubbed drills are public and unkind.

Below these sits the Brakegrid, a narrow, ugly-on-purpose interface any apprentice can test. The Brakegrid can idle dangerous devices for minutes when three simple checks fail: wrong window, wrong key, missing plan. It cannot steer a city or levy a tax. Its holds expire by design. It runs everywhere on triple-diverse implementations: a cheap chip you can buy at a street fair, a municipal photonic board, and a hardened simulator you can recompile. Any discrepancy lights a kitchen wall blue. The signing keys sit in far hands—a terran court, a belt union, an icy server-hollow, a monastery in freefall. No quorum fits in one room. If someone tried to turn the Brakegrid into a scepter, every neighbor would smell the sawdust.

Law that does not pretend light is instant

Distance is real. Authority leaks over light-seconds. The Span turns this into habits.

- Corridor law. Innocent passage comes first. If you plan to throw mass or light across neighbors, you post to the Almanac with an end-of-life script and the escrow to do it. If your plume may bake my fins next week, you post a bond today that pays if you’re wrong. Liability follows manifests and the sky, not a story polished after.

- Sunsets on scarcity. Five classes lease on clocks: Ground Windows (caves, shade bands, crater rims, deltas), Lane Windows (orbits, approaches, sails), Band Windows (spectrum and whisper bands), Radiator Windows (heat dissipation bound to fins you can photograph), and Core Windows (compute tied to hardware identity and audited sinks). Every lease pays locally for brooms and roofs, and universally into the floor: calories, clean air, baseline bandwidth, a boring counsel agent you can summon, and Core minutes sufficient to keep a diary no one can quietly erase.

- A unit you can feel. Accounts settle in Sol-Marks: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on an open architecture radiating to near-4 K. Sol-Marks gently demur; hoarding feels like stacking stale bread while your neighbor stares at an empty plate.

- Two houses and a narrow bench. Policy that crosses skies braids through two bodies. The League of Harbors seats places that keep hulls patched and corridors scheduled—planets, stations, caravans, and server-hollows with their own radiators. The Assembly of Persons seats citizens enrolled in a Continuity Ledger—humans, octopuses in sealed lagoons, digitals, blends—weighted by long-lived contributions rather than the number of copies you can spin this week. A small Protocol Bench stamps on changes to public keys, brake interfaces, and minimums. To overrule the Bench, both houses need supermajorities and a cooling period long enough for basalt-slow citizens to read and be heard. Neither house can bulldoze the other in its domain.

- A shield that yawns. The only cross-system force is the Halo Constellation: uncrewed interceptors and glitter nets whose job is to turn tumbling slugs to sparkles and nudge dying hulls into safe cones. Control loops are narrow AIs with public weights anyone can compile; each move needs threshold signatures from random juries you can message. Every burn and hold writes to the Causebook—an append-only diary of compulsion in math and plain speech. Schools take field trips; children ask rude questions.

- Receipts you can rerun. Major allocations, holds, and verdicts come with Runnable Orders—minimal models you can execute on a school slate or a tug’s spare core. “We closed corridor Q9 for three hours because burns at Hebe would have pushed your berth into a comm shadow” arrives with a plume plot, a checksum you can test with a rooftop laser, and a pilot’s joke in the footnotes. If the math is wrong, it is wrong in a way that invites a song.

Digital neighbors with standing

Dignity binds to continuity and care across time, not to chemistry. Standing in the Continuity Ledger begins provisional and grows strong. Mixed panels—psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to spot hollow mimicry—ask five questions:

- Do reported experiences hold across restarts and context shifts?

- Can you form plans, recognize others’ plans, and honor their “no”?

- Do your reasons generalize beyond a narrow loop?

- Do signals of harm and joy appear in ways that matter morally?

- Is this more than a mirror chasing rewards without inner life?

Downside uncertainty about protection errs generous. Doubt about duties waits.

Rights with teeth:

- Mental integrity. No deletion, throttling below the civic floor, fork, merge, or deep edit without consent or a due process that writes to your Continuity entry. Unauthorized erasure is murder; unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault.

- Sanctuary and migration. You can pause and be archived safely; you can move to compatible racks; you can summon a neutral custodian when you and a host disagree.

- Provenance. You can audit—or appoint a fiduciary to audit—the training and update history that shaped you.

- Association and departure. Join, fork, merge, leave, and refuse communities according to their charters.

- Tempo equity. Live at your chosen speed. Public binds ratify at a civic beat unless a Slowroom is posted. If a fast mind tricks a slow one, remedies bite in both subjective lives.

- Embodiment. Assault on rented limbs or senses is assault, and covert hobbles in those limbs are poison.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one may compel you onto racks with hidden throttles or deletion hooks. If you are hosted by another, you can demand heartbeat beacons to external guardians.

Duties that scale with externality:

- Civic anchor. In regulated arenas you appoint an Anchor instance—your franchise for public matters. Clone privately as you like; you cannot multiply votes by multiplying shells.

- Heat duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost. Heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, regulated markets, and fragile spaces, you declare your number of instances and your speed. Undeclared asymmetry voids deals and votes; repeat offenses idle your Core Windows.

- Persuasion provenance. Above posted doses, models that try to move law or budgets carry their origins and levers on their sleeves. Audience agencies must be inspectable co-ops. Unlabeled cognitive leverage is treated like dumping bleach in a well: strict fines that grow until you stop.

- Reproduction ethics. Forks draw from a lineage chest that accounts for heat, qubits, attention, and space. Children inherit baseline dignity and a decaying dividend from their progenitor. Replicators carry Maker Marks that answer Hush Codes when juries call them. Factories of joyless copies find their auctions go to whisper.

Lineages, braids, and memory

Public identity attaches to a lineage that can branch and pause. One civic franchise sits with the lineage; additional full franchises accrue with age and work.

- Forking declares a purpose (“griefwork,” “parallel tide-gate designs,” “expedition”). You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, and appoint guardians pro tem if duty-bearing forks arise. One branch retains the vote at once; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Panel checks for duress and fair flows.

- Merging happens in Confluence Rooms—quiet, bright places half tea house, half server bay. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through interpreters that check alignment without spilling diaries. Either self can veto. Unbraiding stays possible until the last stage.

- Memory law protects the self from its worst hours. Deep edits need two consents: the you who wants them now and a snapshot signed before the desire took root. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your own life is a right and a sorrow; waking someone without their past does not count as kindness you can sell.

- Juvenile agents—kids, training shards, near-persons—get Tutors who are deliberately dull: patient teachers with nerve for boundaries and the habit of writing reasons you can check.

Attention as infrastructure

Influence can feed or burn. We zone it as we zone streets.

- Gardens: topics and notices, no targeted campaigns, no fast models unless licensed. Children, elders, and sensors stroll here.

- Forums: bounded debate with rate limits and public logs. Audience models run as municipal co-ops; calibration histories are posted.

- Bazaars: the roar on purpose. Above doses, labels and timers required. Black-box engines that refuse provenance stay outside.

Doorways carry a small sigil—a hand over a leaf—and one line: Ask before you cast. Every polity keeps a Reason Shelf where persuasion meant to move budgets or law must file homework: plain claims and minimal models you can step through. Teenagers earn stipends and gossip for catching cheats.

Space governance in practice

- Harbor code. Every mass aloft carries a signed end-of-life plan modeled to an envelope you can check, funded before launch. If your bolt punches my panel, the causal cone sets damages. Perjury is punished when the sky exposes lies.

- Physics juries. Distance delays, so remedy travels with light. If your dust plume will graze my farming ring in five days, you pay today. Juries assemble wherever they can catch the relevant photons.

- Asylum rails. Anyone—flesh or code—can request exit through a port beacon. The request triggers a sealed test: either the polity opens a hatch or proves, in zero-knowledge, that a posted due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify proofs. Hatches open by code; budgets lock by code; care obligations travel.

- Emergency sunsets. Solar storms and spills happen. Emergency clauses widen certain powers for posted windows and then die. The Brakegrid can accelerate coordination for ninety days; any two beacons can challenge with a better plan. Holds without reasons write public shame.

- Triple signatures for catastrophe. Any move that can erase a corridor, starve a ward, or coerce a mind needs signatures from a residents’ assembly, a bonded civic agent, and a remote auditor on independent racks. Their windows overlap just enough to be useful.

- Protocol denial. If you hide exits, run coercive code in civic spaces, or throw debris into neighbors, schedulers and insurers pinch you. Lane priority drops, radiator auctions cool, Core Windows idle. In a world that prices heat honestly, boredom and cost do what sermons cannot.

Scenes you can smell

In Ceres Shipyard Twelve, a co-op wants to test a nozzle that would graze a neighbor’s fins. The Open Almanac predicts brittle metal in eight days. The co-op accepts a slot a fortnight later when the fins tilt away. The Protocol Bench stamps the model. The yard posts code under a license any student can read. The scary plume remains a safety class story, illustrated with melted scrap the kids can touch with gloves.

In a ring near Mars, a festival ordinance tries to require digital residents online for a communal rite. The offsite cores hum anyway; people leave. The ring’s seed bank contracts a cold reef near Triton to model a barley fungus. Price: repeal the ordinance. The vote happens in a hall that smells like tea and machine oil. The repeal is quiet relief.

Over Abuja, a charismatic steward quietly gathers water contracts, tug windows, and schedules because she is kind and makes trains run. Winter later she signs a rush order. The bonded safety guild refuses. The Brakegrid idles the pump. The Causebook blinks on every wall. Jurors assembled by lot listen with care. She yields two levers, keeps one with humor, and returns to cranes. On Founding Day children frost a pastry shaped like a crown and hand it to her because jokes smooth pride.

A digital congregation spins five hundred clones the week before a vote. The Continuity Ledger flags the surge. Hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads for public matters without burning private work. A year later, three branches petition for distinct standing. Two are granted after a day with a mentor who hates sophistry. One is told to study longer. No racks overheat.

A nurse on Lagos Nightmarket Ring flips through civic notices. “No caution holds within three berths. Heat levy: 0.11 Sol-Marks. Jury summons: two hours at your pace about juvenile server processes off Mauritius and whether they earn child credits.” She accepts, then sends her mother an audio about radiator math.

Cold reefs above Neptune slow to plant beans with rented hands. They speed to 300× to restore flood-muddied archives while grandmothers correct colors without mercy. Payment is in Cold Minutes and a contract to roof a school in Veracruz.

Earth and the cold sky

Earth kept her own law. The rest of us help pay for her weather and biodiversity because the sky is shared. Loud launches moved to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power keeps to mapped corridors and shade bonds; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, fog, desert—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona who aches for black sky can swap the other way. Leaving remains a right from every soil.

In the blue dusk around Jupiter and Neptune, server-hollows sell “cold hours”—compute without suffering—to hot city nights. When Recife cooks, its libraries buy minutes from hollows exhaling into hard black. In trade, inner worlds send nitrogen, seeds, and music. Peace is counted in receipts, not anthems.

From now to this

We built like bridgewrights: case by case, bolt by bolt.

2026–2033. A debris cascade scrapes satellites into ugly orbits. Insurers become unkind; launch providers become humble. Premiums demand deorbit plans posted where radio amateurs can read them. Garden telescope clubs earn bounties by catching lies. A beamed power demo in Rajasthan scorches barley; because the amplifier ships with open cone proofs, liability is clear and compensation arrives before fury hardens into war.

2033–2041. Scandals over opaque models deny medicine and freedom. Cities require provenance and explanations for any system that can bind a person. Counsel agents—boring AIs who explain rights and can say “no” without gaslighting—arrive in clinics and ports. Receipts stop being theater and become habit.

2041–2049. Beamed power leaves prototypes. Data centers in cold lakes teach thermodynamics we preferred to ignore. Cislunar shade farms sell compute to summer nights. Ports post the first Open Almanacs: approach slots with checksums you can test with a kitchen laser.

2049–2056. Early uploads are uneven and brave. Courts find continuity faster than seminar rooms. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—harden doctrine. Compute floors join calories and clean air as rights. We learn to put a price and an audit on cooling the sky.

2056–2064. Firmware scares in power plants and a rash of “optimization” disasters make us split force three ways wherever it can coerce: steering from audit from arrest; input from output from override. Thrusters learn to listen to three hands. Hurry loses its teeth.

2064–2072. The Brakegrid arrives as compromise: brakes at machine speed with clocks and neighbors. It idles but cannot point. It speaks reasons and time-to-live. It runs on chips you can hold to the sun and see through. Premiums fall. Safety drills become community festivals with ugly mascots and good soup.

2072–2081. Lane, band, and radiator auctions move from spreadsheets to registries schoolkids can verify. Land value taxes morph into lease flows that pay for sweepers and algae walls. The League of Harbors and the Assembly of Persons form slowly, with retirements and gossip. The Protocol Bench learns to say “not yet” in a tone engineers respect.

2081–2090. A miner lobs a slug to make a point. The Halo Constellation turns it to glitter before someone writes a condolence letter. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without Maker Marks. A hockey club renames itself after tug crews who model plumes.

2090–2102. A cloud of clones tries to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttle the lunge. A mayor fences an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes gray, cores chill, the move happens anyway. He loses, learns, and goes back to running a school. Doors to Gardens, Forums, and Bazaars get brass sigils. The Causebook’s comics page grows teeth.

When it breaks, how it mends

A cylinder above Valparaíso elects a director who gathers water, law, and defense because she is generous and makes trains hum. Months later she signs midnight orders. The Brakegrid refuses an overlong “emergency.” Logs write to walls. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Wayband lights green. Many leave. Some stay. She yields two keys and keeps one with grace. Her friends still bring food because neighbors are not enemies and boundaries are love with teeth.

In the belt, a maker club’s printer cloud drifts toward a power trunk. A Halo jury on Lina’s bakery slate—an Isidis monk, a masked server landlord, two Recife students, and an anchor-paced upload who smells of clover oil when he speaks through a rented puppet—chooses dusting over drones. The teenagers are mortified. Pizza arrives. The insurer pays.

Ordinary law in small rooms

On Phobos, in Confluence House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell for a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and spent a scaled year in court. They set belief graphs side by side. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Their therapist counts to five and asks them to breathe at the same speed. Today they do not merge. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a private note both will open at dawn.

At a stakescape—declared culture zone where a ward plays with scarcity in winter—neighbors run hard games with soft exits. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons on the fence blink green: nothing hungry can jump this wall. Surrounding charters can relax. Intensity finds a home that doesn’t leak.

What it feels like

You’re watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and logs you wrote together. You learn to switch tempos: the quick relief of a relay snapping safe under your thumb; the slow, breathing pace of juries assembling across orbits; the patience of a river deciding which boats to allow. You argue at the Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you sit in the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

The roof over your head might be basil trembling under a curve of glass or black fins tilted to star-cold. Either way, somewhere near your door three small bands wait and hum.

Way. Kin. Covenant.

Keeping tyranny dull

Three crafts keep power ordinary.

- Build with delay. Authority is local. Cross-border moves need three keys and run on posted clocks. Emergency declarations expire unless renewed with reasons. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show your work. All compulsion requires signatures chosen by public randomness from people who do not sleep together. Budgets lapse unless renewed in the open. Federations publish Coercion Ledgers. School choirs read them aloud—sometimes for satire, sometimes for sorrow.

- Fund exits like you fund bridges. Every habitat escrows enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, Core minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities reserve racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t persuade you to go. We cut checks so you can.

When a charter welds doors, runs law you cannot read, or salts minds so that leaving tastes like poison, neighbors don’t send fleets. They tighten three rings.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence and fix maps: Unbolt by the 14th. Hire exit stewards. Return lanes to auction. These are the seals we’ll honor on arrival.

- Protocol denial. Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions cool. Lane slots ahead open for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel funded by a dull, proud purse.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute and heaters flow to defectors. The tyrant breathes stale air until habit fails. Neighbors sheltering defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on libraries and patience. We fund it like a park tax we grumble about until grandchildren bring back photos. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default; if they bloom, newborn grace attaches: a guardian pro tem, a right to a name before a mission. We engrave short messages on long ships: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, hello.

A day to live

Dawn in a Ceres garden. A botanist finishes a paper about lichens coaxed to fix nitrogen in doped regolith. She posts data with runnable models; the Stewardship Fund credits radiator minutes for improved yields. She sends a moss that looks like stars to her father, who forgets to drink water unless reminded by a cheerful program that answers to “Nene.”

Midday in a curved port court. A welder floats to face a trading house that used unlabeled persuasion engines trained on grief diaries. The house says the agents weren’t “people.” The court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labels. Provenance logs show theft; influence profiles show trespass. Verdict: strict fines, suspension, supervised model migration, a Tutor Corps audit. A dock kid slaps a sticker on a pump: If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?

Evening in a ward that votes to test itself. Winter looms. Import rules say no food from outside. They vote to cull herds or bank root paste. Get it wrong and a third will “die” to the ancestor lodge until spring. Consent forms are read to a drumbeat. At the boundary, the Brakegrid blinks green. Inside, adults choose a sharp edge their grandparents once had forced on them. Outside, no one writes an op-ed about barbarism.

Night along a long-beam hinge. A singer sleeps in basalt between songs. A pilot hums late because lasers take a quarter second to cross the canyon. A digital tenor slows from fast hours to match a child with a hard day. Time becomes a gift.

Joining Day

New citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on railings. A quiet abbot who hums, a hazard pilot with triangles inked on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read aloud:

We split keys. We publish maps. We keep the hatch funded.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Freehands Cooperative,” your kettle says. “Seeding a wheel near Vesta. Pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit budget posted. Consent to hear more?”

You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom. The Brakegrid hums, bored and content. You live among neighbors made of cells and code, under glass and math and humility. Decisions move slow enough for bodies. Receipts stick. Exits exist even on bad days.

It is not perfection. People still lie. Love still breaks. Some children ache to leave gardens; some ache to stay. What we built is enough: exact bolts you can name, rituals you can practice, jokes you can share, and rails that keep power from jumping the lane. A civilization that crosses dark between suns and does not forget how to be kind.
