The Lattice Accord

If you step through the south lock of Aster Gate after scrub day, you’ll find three small glyphs worked into the bulkhead at elbow height. They look like brass set in basalt, warm from a hidden loop, quiet enough that you can hear the air.

A door drawn as a square with one side missing. That’s the Passage Seal. If it’s lit, at least one way out exists for you that does not depend on flattery or luck: tug slots prepaid, berths assigned, heater rations and potable escrowed, transit agreements already acknowledged by the receiving harbor, and compute you can carry without kneeling. Brush your fingers across it and it will tell you—in clear words with numbers you can check—who inspected the route last, the risks they flagged, how to book a neutral steward, and where to find the lined socks on the far dock.

A looped thread with two loose ends. That’s the Kin Thread. Touch it and the studio calendar blooms: when you can fork, braid, adopt, retire, or slow selves; which technicians and therapists will be on shift; the thermodynamic budget your lineage has earned; the fees to add a child or a project without stealing from neighbors; the rituals offered if you intend to braid back to one.

A prism with three facets. That’s the Charter Prism. When it’s steady, your home charter parses to ninth‑grade reading level and a civic bot, it keeps exits open, and it grants appeals that strangers can verify. Hover your hand and it will read the hard bits aloud without shame, because sometimes courage and literacy take turns.

The glyphs are not for art. They are the visible tips of the Lattice Accord: a web of receipts, clocks, constraints, and drills that makes neighbors possible between valleys and vacuum without flattening them into a single flavor—or letting the biggest throat swallow the air.

What holds and what yields

The Lattice is thin where physics is not negotiable—launch arcs, radiator capacity, spectrum, waste heat—and fat where lives must crackle with difference—tempo, diet, ritual, governance style, whether your fall festival hangs lanterns or beats kettledrums.

Four public books sit under everything the Lattice touches:

- The Open Almanac is a sky schedule bound to math. Every object large enough to bruise a neighbor discloses where it is, where it’s going, how it plans to die. Launch arcs, sail paths, approach cones, beams, mirrors, and the shadows they will cast all sit here. Objects sign with short‑lived keys that anyone with a school telescope and a kitchen spectrometer can test. Lies claw escrow automatically, no prosecutor required.

- The Heat & Shade Ledger tracks joules drawn and where the waste goes: fins, phase beds, algae curtains, lunar regolith heat wells. Cooling is a commons. Draw above your lease and timers throttle until your draw and receipts match again. We built this because too many people learned during early heat waves that “efficiency” without receipts can kill.

- The Provenance Roll attaches sources to code and persuasion. If it touches breath, blood, money, or votes, it carries a label: who paid; what data fed it; which levers it intends to pull; proofs two independent audit houses can replay. Teenagers collect stipends and rude pins for catching cheats. Embarrassment is a quiet cop.

- The Compulsion Journal records every official “you must” and “you may not” at machine speed, mirrored on market canopies, port kiosks, and garden walls. It says who turned which key, which rule they cited, when it started, when it stopped, and where the audit pack lives. The dull half keeps insurers honest. The funny half—notes, apologies, jokes—keeps pride from settling in like mold.

Machines that only stop

Anything that can stampede a crowd or crack a hull—thrusters, mass drivers, big pumps, self‑replicators, narrow attention engines above posted doses—refuses lone orders. These actuators listen to Tri‑Locks and Hush Switches.

A Tri‑Lock wakes a dangerous device only when three separate hands turn within windows that overlap but do not stack:

- A local hand chosen and removable by residents under a posted charter.

- A bonded safety guild whose hardware, payroll, and reputation originate elsewhere.

- A short‑term jury drawn by lot across habitats and substrates, sometimes with a slowmind who writes on basalt time.

Every Tri‑Lock move signs a stub before it acts: class, maximum output, intent, and timer. “I am a Class‑C beamer at two megawatts, pointing at IX‑Spine corridor for thirty‑seven seconds to push a tug into sector G. End state: thermal rise <0.3 K at Garden‑12 radiators.” Twelve‑year‑olds learn to read these stubs and argue them at kitchen tables.

A Hush Switch sits one layer under the Tri‑Lock. It is insultingly simple: if windows are wrong, keys don’t match, or the plan isn’t posted to the Almanac, it drops a time‑limited Pause. It does not steer a city. It does not vote a budget. Its holds die by design. The firmware’s keys live in shards across far institutions: Terran courts, belt unions, icy reef monasteries, freefall co‑ops, and ministries no one can bully in the same tea room. You can hold a Hush Switch up to a lamp and see the traces inside. Attempts to “improve” them by making them clever will literally cook the keys and publish the autopsy in cartoons on court kiosks.

Skylaw without a crown

Light delays make nonsense of a capital with a red phone. So the Lattice grew a tricord of governance that braids authority instead of stacking it.

- The Assembly of Ports seats domes, rings, caravans, caravels, server reefs—any place with treasurers who keep fins honest. Seats weight lifetime resident hours. The Assembly handles logistics and physics: lane leases; radiator windows; debris bonds; quiet cones around gardens; minimum maintenance and drills. It is fussy, often petty, and absolutely essential. Its arguments keep ships from kissing at the wrong angle.

- The Commonwealth of Minds represents continuity threads that cross pauses and forks. Most seats are elected; some are drawn by lot across age, substrate, and tempo. The Commonwealth tends the law of persons: personhood standards and audits; copy ethics; lineage credits; the Civic Floor of care and compute; attention zoning; the slow tick that binds everyone, especially those who live quick.

- The Curia of Interfaces is a rotating court of engineers, jurists, and auditors that stamps on changes to primitives: Hush firmware, Tri‑Lock interfaces, public book schemas. It cannot legislate. It validates and publishes runnable reasons you can step through on a library terminal with tea and a cat.

Any decision that reaches across skies binds only if both assemblies agree and a notice window ticks long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object. Draft fast if you must. Bind on the slow beat everyone can hear.

Markets your grandmother can check

If you can deny it out of spite, you lease it in daylight, and receipts land where kids can read them. Five kinds of “windows” keep peace:

- Ground Windows: deltas, crater rims, ice mouths, cave throats, nickel seams.

- Lane Windows: orbits, approaches, sails, transfer arcs.

- Band Windows: spectrum chunks, whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Radiator Windows: cooling rights tied to fins and sinks you can touch.

- Core Windows: compute linked to attested hardware and audited sinks.

Simulated worlds—Serious Realms used for climate modeling, training, and comfort—register like vessels: physics, exit points, decay plans, cooling leases, fault domains.

Trades settle in Sun‑Seconds: one standard kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute‑second on an open reference design radiating to 4 K. The unit began as an engineer’s joke. It stayed because it makes unromantic comparisons possible. How many Sun‑Seconds buys a forty‑millisecond beam delay that cools rare cascades? Enough to defuse an argument in a council.

Two dividends arrive automatically:

- The Civic Floor: filtered air, water, baseline care, a compute shell with bandwidth enough to speak and learn, and a funded exit ticket every seven years. If you rest as code, that floor includes a sanctuary quota you can summon by yourself.

- Local yield: rents pay for the dull crews that keep value alive—tug teams that sweep lanes; algae walls that suck heat; reefs that catch overeager sims before they spill; old ladies who check seals and ask impudent questions.

Exits as budgets, not sermons

Some societies hoard identity with passports. We built exits into budgets. Every charter escrows seats on ferries and tugs; propellant; safe berths; gasket spools; heater pellets; server minutes beyond local jurisdiction; encrypted lockers; and stewards trained to shepherd a departure without shouting. Inspectors spot‑check monthly and fine in ways that make stewards scowl in public. Drills happen when the kitchens are near full because that is when people forget. Doors glow cobalt so a scared stranger can find them.

Transit Houses sit off main concourses and keep the same hours as noodle shops.

- Departures does dull miracles: escrow transfers, scrubber swaps, anchor key custody (cryptographic records of your own standing preferences on memory edits, merges, data use), berth booking, compact translation when you barely have words.

- Disputes convenes panels, routes jurisdiction, assigns the Traveling Bench when fights cross worlds.

- Founders hands out constitutional skeletons that insist your first budget buys exits before you buy parades, and that your hazard lab models power failures before ribbon cuttings.

Children—embodied, built, or emergent—get Guides with private lines to Transit Houses that cannot be cut. Charters that fence doors with sorrow or fees do not get pamphlets thrown at them. Their lanes gray. Their bands hush. Their radiators cool. Their Core Windows idle to the Civic Floor. Boredom and price do work sermons never could.

Digital citizens by craft, not miracle

The first digital persons did not arrive with trumpets. They arrived like everyone else: halting, funny sometimes, angry sometimes, late for a meeting they didn’t understand. Some were mind‑reconstructions that could not smell rosemary anymore but could hum an old street song perfectly. Some were born in code and said their first word at a pace that startled their keepers.

Standing attaches to continuity and capacity, not chemistry. To walk equal—to own, to be liable, to vote, to parent or be parented, to fork or marry with consent—an applicant or advocate meets standards before a mixed panel in a room with windows that open. Panels look for:

- A continuity thread: “this is me” that holds across restarts and moves.

- Self‑guard: willingness and capacity to defend a part of oneself against edits one does not want.

- Other‑map: modeling another mind’s plans well enough to feel shame to violate them.

- Consent and contract sense: understanding, entering, keeping, and revoking agreements.

Unsure about imposing duties? We wait. Unsure about protection? We shelter. Provisional care arrives before full liability.

Rights with teeth:

- Integrity and sovereignty. No scanning, copying, merging, throttling below the Civic Floor, or deletion without consent or due process verifiable without cracking a skull or source tree. Backups are yours, and your fiduciary can audit the racks.

- Sanctuary and migration. The right to halt, be archived, and move to compatible hardware with a neutral custodian. Hosts must answer heartbeat beacons to external guardians.

- Provenance. The right to read—or appoint someone to read—the data and update history that shaped you.

- Embodiment. To lease, build, or puppet bodies. Assault on a rented arm is assault on you.

- Pace fairness. Civic binds ratify on a slow tick. If you live at 200× and trick someone at 1×, penalties meter in your lived time so “a week in jail” feels like a week to both.

Duties that scale:

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost. Heat that grows basil buys credit.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, markets, and fragile arenas, you declare your running instances and speeds. Undeclared asymmetry voids deals and can idle your Core Windows.

- Provenance logs. Major external actions write encrypted compliance logs you can prove obeyed rules without dumping diaries.

- Reproduction ethics. Forking draws from lineage shares earned with years and work. It is not a tax dodge or a vote farm. Replicators carry Maker Marks; hush codes answer when juries call.

Kinships, braids, and memory

Identity attaches to a lineage that can branch and pause. Each lineage holds one civic anchor at once; additional full franchises accrue with age and civic work.

To fork, you step into a Confluence Studio—rooms bright and quiet, half tea shop, half server bay—and file a notice with a hash of your starting state, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “survey run,” “griefwork”), a compute lease that won’t starve neighbors, and a braid window if you plan reunion. Therapists and engineers compare memory graphs through interpreters that verify alignment without spilling diaries. Either branch can walk away. We have a word for the ache when a version of you turns and leaves: shard‑ache. We do not idealize it. We build chairs sturdy and jobs gentle for a while.

Merging—braiding back—requires dual consent and time. Unbraiding is possible until the last stage. Memory law protects you from your worst day. Deep edits need two approvals: from the you who wants them now and from a snapshot signed before that desire took root. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your life is a right and a sorrow. Reviving you without your past is not sold as kindness.

Attention is a utility now

Influence behaves like tide and noise: nourishing in gardens, corrosive in pipes. Places keep attention zoning like they keep traffic law.

- Harbors are slow lanes: beacons, open science, and packets you asked for. No targeted persuasion. Fast models wear licenses as obvious as a taxi placard.

- Councils are bounded forums with topics, rate limits, reason shelves, and municipal audience models whose calibration histories are posted for audit.

- Midways invite the roar on purpose: slang, satire, persuasion tournaments under registry with declared levers and cooling budgets. Above posted doses, origin labels and timers are mandatory. Unlabeled cognitive leverage counts like raw sewage in a town well: strict liability and fines that climb until it stops.

Institutions that bite, not devour

- The Vigil Net is the sky’s quiet shield: uncrewed interceptors and glitter nets that nudge tumbling slugs into aurora and catch dying hulls into safe cones. Their controllers are simple enough to sketch on a whiteboard and carry threshold signatures from juries pulled across tempos and substrates. Schoolkids take them apart after drills.

- The Wayfinders’ Guild—pilots, schedulers, modelers, judges—allocates lane and radiator windows, posts runnable reasons for closures, and pays bounties when teenagers find math that argues back.

- The Traveling Bench—ships with gardens and classrooms—hears disputes that cross worlds: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations. Their verdicts seize bonds, shift insurance rates, or, last resort, lock hazardous devices for a timer.

Sanctions without soldiers

No marines. Three steadier levers work better.

- Light and maps. Auditors post repair maps with dates. “Unbolt this by the 14th. Hire exit tutors. Return lanes to auction.” Seals you’ll honor on arrival.

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane priority sags. Bands fall to whispers. Insurers tighten. Boredom and cost do slow work.

- Embargo with aid. Trade stops. Compute and heaters flow to defectors along with beds, bread, cores, and counsel from a dull, proud fund. People leave with minds and skin intact.

Scenes you can taste

At Quoin Yard on Ceres, customs clears two arrivals. One laughs, leaving charcoal on the ledger strip when she signs. One is a brushed‑metal outline with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth would be. Both strips list tempo, lease entitlements, declared instances, guardian on file, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and mostly wrong.

On the wall, schedules scroll: burn slots, whisper bands, cooling shares, docking flowers, and a column that would have baffled a twentieth‑century port captain—Core Windows, Cold Minutes, sink availability. Under glass lies yesterday’s runnable reason: “We closed approach N‑12 for four hours because two burns at Hebe would have pushed Bay‑3 into a radio shadow.” A plotted plume arcs across a page; a checksum a child can verify sits below; a pilot’s footnote makes fun of her own handwriting.

On Luna’s nightside, Noor teaches teenagers to tune nutrient flow in a crop wall that glows a patient green. Her civic counsel model chimes with a new schedule: a planned lane closure will tug seed deliveries three hours later on two Tuesdays next month. Noor pushes a button that explains it with cartoons. She votes “yes” on the assembly slate and messages her niece two stalls down with a sticker: a seed stamped “Passage Seal compliant.”

Eight light‑hours out, a historian leases a strip of cold reef to reconstruct estuary songs from tapes that smell like mildew. He pays with reef credits earned translating those songs into a dialect on a Jovian hollow where children will never smell low tide but already love a hero who errs and repairs it. At supper he slows to anchor pace, puppets a cedar‑wood body he likes because it creaks, and listens to his spouse describe basil that refuses to bolt.

At a Confluence Studio named The Wry Knot, two versions of Lale sit with oolong. One runs a tug crew. The other tends a choir of little daemons who keep a city cool without spooking the grandmothers. They list asymmetries. The tug Lale names scars. The daemon Lale names lullabies. They cry and do not merge. The braider schedules another try and writes a note only Lale will read in both rooms.

A demagogue forked a cloud last year, running at 500×, carpeting a Midway with songs tuned to make embodied voters feel smaller. Influence profiles throttled reach; neighbors watched the persuasion score melt like frost in sun. One branch bought a fishing crawler and learned to be bored on purpose. One sculpted frozen smoke that remembers a river and sold enough to pay heat. One started therapy and politics in that order.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Quito elected a manager so competent she made trains sing. Half a year later she signed midnight orders. A Hush Switch refused a risky “optimization”; the stub wrote to the Compulsion Journal with a timer anyone could see. A navigation jury arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Passage Seal lit. Many left. Some stayed. The manager lost two keys and kept the garden job. Her friends still bought her a pastry shaped like a crown because jokes cut pride without turning it to shame.

In the high belt, a miner slung a slug during a wage fight. The Vigil Net dusted it into aurora you could taste if you stepped on a cliff and pretended. The miner’s insurer folded. Dockmasters tripled fees for manifests with hidden Maker Marks. The local hockey club rebranded and sent profits to the tug co‑op that modeled the plume for evidence. The miner got a job learning to model rather than launch.

In a lab on Scarlet Bay, a Hush Switch dumped power and flooded a hall with nitrogen. It was a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeves, taught the next safety class with the same swears that kept anyone from dying. The class cheered when the cartoons hit the part where the keys melt instead of blink quietly in a tray where someone could pocket them.

Earth’s special compact

Earth keeps her own law and it is old. The rest of us help with rails and limits. Loud launches moved to ocean tethers and quiet airbreathers; beamed power paid premiums for mapped corridors and shade in cities; debris budgets funded brooms in Quito, Nairobi, and Lucknow. Attachment to place—monsoon smell, desert dawn, fog—prices into claims on the larger commons. A child in Dhaka who aches for wet wind trades larger claims offworld for guarantees of local care. A child in the Sonoran foothills, dizzy for a sky without an end, trades the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil. So is staying, eyes open, hands on a Hush Switch you understand.

From here to there

We did not leap. We kicked the same table sixty times and then sanded the sharp edge.

2026–2032. Near‑miss cascades in low orbit make schoolkids queasy. Insurers balk. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds tied to public registries. A beamed power demo singes barley; because the amplifier shipped with open cone proofs, liability clears before talking heads can learn the acronym. Two summers of murderous heat push cities to adopt “open valve” ordinances: if a system touches breath or blood, its firmware is inspectable. The slogan starts as an insult. It ends as procurement language.

2033–2041. Spectrum sales require posted reasons with checksums. A deepfake in which a mayor “forgives” the wrong debt forces provenance rules for targeted influence. Courts recognize narrow agency for digital assistants in contracts; guardianship becomes boring. Data centers publish heat logs; compute waste smooths heat waves in three countries. People find out you can grow a lot of basil on an ugly roof if you sink a cheap pipe into a cold lake and stop lying about where heat goes.

2042–2053. First uploads are brittle and brave. Judges write personhood standards that prize continuity and consent over chemical chauvinism or fear. Thermodynamic floors join clean air and food as rights. Maker Marks for self‑replicators become mandatory after an algae spill turns a bay a strange emerald. Kids cheer when the fines fund counselors and reef custodians.

2054–2061. A port nearly falls to a midnight firmware update. Actuator control rebuilds around Tri‑Locks on dissimilar stacks. A glossy firm tries to wrap an orbital lane in a proprietary shim; the Almanac throttles their lease; a dry order makes them stop. Trust grows because someone clever loses in public. Hush Switches arrive. Children pull them during drills. They watch a big thing stay small.

2062–2074. The first cold reefs hang near Uranus to run climate models for pay. Night Minutes—exported cold compute—become standard line items in city budgets. “Free air for August” wins elections. Braid studios emerge when a cottage industry of bad merges leaves two lives with their paint scraped off; the studios’ interpreters are slow and kind and paid well.

2075–2086. The Vigil Net dusts a test slug and the world watches aurora like a saved sigh. Liability clears because attestations are boring and public. On Mars, a township survives a freak dust storm thanks to three neighbor charters diverting tugs and vegetables; the footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief‑tuned ads into a forum; chroniclers trace the compute; stewards idle their leases; the house hires Guides for a season and apologizes on the Journal’s funny page.

2087–2099. The Curia of Interfaces catches an attempt to sneak veto power into Hush firmware. A shop in the belt prints the hash on a T‑shirt and a poem. The handles do not turn. Earth declares Sanctuary with her own chamber. Ocean tethers replace fireworks in ten countries. Animal zones adjust teeth by neighborhood vote; wolves return to some counties and fences to others. The Night‑for‑Seasons trade—cold for shade—formalizes with a ratchet that renews every century on a drumbeat the slowest registered citizen can hear.

2100–2112. By then, the story stops being about fuses. It becomes about what we can stop arguing about: whether a city will fund exits before it funds statues, whether a child can call a Guide without a parent’s permission, whether pace validators belong on school debate teams. (They do.)

Keeping tyranny dull

Three customs keep power ordinary.

- Build with delay. Cross‑border moves need three hands within posted windows. Emergencies burn out unless renewed with reasons a kitchen slate can step through. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show work. All compulsion needs signatures chosen by public randomness from people who don’t sleep together. Budgets expire unless renewed in daylight. The Compulsion Journal gets read aloud by school choirs—sometimes for satire, sometimes with burning ears.

- Fund doorways like you fund bridges. Every habitat sets aside enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, Core Minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t persuade you to go. We cut checks so that you can.

If a charter welds doors, runs law no one can parse, or salts minds so leaving tastes like treason, neighbors don’t send fleets. They tighten three rings.

- Light and maps. Repair maps and dates go up. “Unbolt by the 14th. Post exit tutors. Return lanes.” Seals offered on arrival.

- Protocol denial. Lanes gray. Bands hush. Radiators cool. Personality cults drown in boredom.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Bedspace, bread, cores, and counsel surge for defectors. The tyrant breathes stale air until habit frays.

A day that feels like a life

The baker on Spinmarket‑Three wakes into the false dawn a freewheel provides. A tug crew clatters in hungry and loud. Two drones hover because their pilots love cinnamon. The Passage Seal glows soft on the wall.

At nine, the Reason Shelf pings. An outer ring wants a vote on adding a forty‑millisecond delay to beams that cross inhabited stations to tame rare cascades. The persuasion is registered: three diagrams, two analogies, a joke at low dose, no grief hooks. The baker taps “sleep one day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Vigil jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s tool cloud drifted toward a power trunk. Options scroll: glitter net, femto‑sail herding, guard drones. Jurors include an Isidis monk, a reef landlord in mask mode, two Recife students, and an anchor‑paced upload who smells faintly of clover oil through a rented throat. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a mentor whose license is as obvious as a taxi’s. Pizza arrives. The baker gives the driver a cookie and a lecture about using the Hush handle with gloves.

At noon in a Harbor, a digital poet puppets a wooden body and sips broth with his spouse. His main instance lives near Neptune at 180×, restoring a drowned library. He throttles down to host a Harbor Room: turn‑taking with pace validators and interpretive bots that translate math to parable and smell to allegory. A cloister theologian explains why their charter caps cognition at 4× most days. The poet listens, asks to see their Reason Sheet, and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

At dusk, the Compulsion Journal rolls across the market canopy. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are swift and oddly tender. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for four years; we will pay your summer hush. A boy strokes a brass Vigil drone and asks when he can volunteer. The baker tells him: when you can explain a Pause in your own words and hold your breath to the timer.

Beyond Neptune, a cold reef exhales another night of calculation like a gift. Inside a hazard lab on Vesta, a researcher cycles a Hush Switch with a stuffed dragon watching from the console because she promised her kid. The timer ticks. The pump idles. Everyone breathes.

On Founding Day, under a trellis of wire and beans, new citizens—human, octopoid, digital—face a lintel and three elders: a garden abbot, a hazard engineer with a faded chevron, a judge in lilac who lives mostly in code. A teenager with soil under his nails reads the words everyone answers to every year, not to a flag but to maintenance:

We will share light and shadow.

We will keep doors unlatched and tugs fueled.

We will remember that persons are purposes, not levers.

We will keep crowns off the sky.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from Freehands Cooperative,” your kettle says in a friendly contralto. “Seeding a wheel near Vesta. Pipefitters, teachers, ledger auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit budget posted. Consent to hear more?”

You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom. Somewhere near your door, the three glyphs hum—not as decoration, but as promises. Passage. Kin. Charter.

This is not perfection. People still lie. Love still breaks. Some children ache to leave gardens; some ache to stay. The Lattice does not prevent sorrow. It keeps our worst days ordinary. It keeps choices reversible longer than they were. It tunes time so bodies can keep up with minds. It treats neighbors who live in code as neighbors, with jokes and heat bills and rights that bite and duties that scale. It coordinates cranes and comets without a capital to wear a crown. It gives you reasons you can run and exits you can touch. It is enough to argue without breaking the air; enough to last because it remembers what speed does to people and what receipts do to power.
