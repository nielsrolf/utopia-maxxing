The Constellation Compact

If you stand in the customs hall of Westhaven Dock and look up, you’ll see three small beads of light set above the inner lock. They’re real hardware in a steel strip that runs like a vein through the wall: one amber, one blue, one green. Dock hands swear they can hear them hum when the room is empty.

- Amber is Passage. Press the pad under it and the kiosk prints a plan you can actually pull off: a berth on a listed tug, cargo limits, memory and compute that follow you, and a cot at your destination for long enough to find your feet.

- Blue is Branch. Tap it and the clerk—human this hour, digital the next—will ask if you mean to fork. The screen shows what your lineage account can support, who is on shift to mentor, what rituals and checks apply if you hope to reunite later, and—most important—what protections hold if you decide you don’t.

- Green is Accord. It lists places within one jump that publish their charters in machine-checkable light, keep the same floors of dignity, and can say no only with reasons a neighbor can read and a student can reproduce.

They are not decor. Those three beads are wired into budgets, valves, processors, and public logs. Together with some blunt machines and the culture we grew around them, they’re the bones of the Constellation Compact: a choreography, not a throne, that lets stations and valleys, barges and server reefs, live close without grinding each other down.

We learned three stubborn lessons and wrote them into glass and habit. First, delay is constitution: light-time won’t bend for meetings. Second, speed is a skill, not a right to rule. Third, difference survives when exits are real and scheduled, not begged.

Two tempos and a bargain between them

Not every home runs on the same beat. The Compact says that’s fine and binds the beats where they touch.

- The Living Belt covers places where breath matters more than throughput: Earth’s floodplains and uplands, lunar caverns draped in fungus wallpaper, Martian groves under glass, cylinders that smell like mint and warm pine. Children should walk home after dark without being sold to.

- The Shadow Reaches are the cold, wide places where heat spills to black and compute blooms like winter algae: Trojan swarms, Neptune’s lee, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Upload cooperatives, time-sharing labs, and digital polities sprint here, then slow to talk.

Between them runs the Quiet–Cold Exchange. Towns in the Belt buy “quiet windows” in law: slices of sky and slices of attention where persuasion and beams are throttled to human pace. Arrays in the Reaches sell “cold minutes” and resilient audits that anyone with a library terminal can run, run climate and traffic models that don’t surprise poor neighborhoods before rich ones, and send back art that sings in two tempos at once. The prices—watts, decibels, degrees—are posted on the Orbital Register in numbers Auntie can check with a pamphlet and a kitchen telescope.

A ledger you can argue with and verify

The Orbital Register is our delay-tolerant, physics-pegged public ledger. It posts where things are and how they’re allowed to act: beam schedules and mirror angles; quiet windows for telescopes and thought-gardens; leases for orbits, spectrum, corridors, and compute; debris bonds; the declared concurrency of digital citizens in regulated arenas; and radiator rights tied to hardware signatures.

- Beams and big mirrors sign their contexts with ephemeral keys burned into devices that hobby clubs can audit. If your light crosses someone’s dawn, you owe shade bonds or explanation.

- Quiet windows are public in decibels, angles, and times. If you must pierce one for safety or rescue, you post collateral and your reasons.

- The Thermal Ledger within the Register tracks three dull things that keep us honest: energy draw, cooling allotments, and declared instance counts. Declare three selves and pull like thirty? Your radiator auctions pinch you to idle until the numbers line up with the sensors.

You can check the Register with a school verifier. Given patience, a child with a pointer laser and a cheap scope can test whether that scheduled burn happened.

Floors you can tape to a life-support panel

Under everything sit rules compact enough to fit on a glove and strict enough to fence ambition.

- No nonconsensual harm to persons. “Person” means any being that can tell its own story across time, model another’s claim, and enter reciprocal commitments, regardless of substrate.

- No self-replication in shared environments without audit. Anything that can copy itself carries a signed Maker Stamp and listens for a Quiet Code when randomly drawn juries turn keys.

- No hidden manufacture of suffering, whether in bodies or in simulation.

- Life support and civic guardrails run on code you can read and roll back. Changes log and time out.

- Decisions that bind embodied lives ratify at embodied tempo. Fast drafts are welcome; fast binding is not.

- Rivalrous commons—corridors, spectrum, radiator windows, cave mouths—lease in posted auctions with sunsets. Commons get paid first.

- Children in any substrate learn to set boundaries, say no, and leave a household without a parent’s or maker’s leave.

- The coordination substrate—the beacons that announce charters, the mesh that carries consent, the narrow guardrail that watches for floor breaches—is open, minimal, and nobody’s to own.

Machines with one trick and receipts

There’s an ugly room down a yellow corridor in every port: the Hold Room. Tours stop there because it’s unglamorous and true. Waist-high boxes with fuses you can break, toggles under thick glass, and one big, recessed button labeled HOLD in letters a coughing person can parse. These Hold Switches sit in threes wherever dumb meets dangerous: valves, launch rails, beam actuators, seeded replicators, industrial kitchens.

Any trained citizen with a civic token can press HOLD for six minutes when a posted rule is about to break: a beam slicing through a declared quiet window without paid passage; a replicator stack desynced from its beacon; a mass cluster drifting toward a ring. HOLD flips a device to safe band and screams into the Cause Ledger: what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock. To keep HOLD engaged past six minutes, three concurrences must click: a local panel drawn by lot from citizens who logged civic hours this year; a remote auditor on hardware local hands cannot touch; and a digital citizen drafted without application. Keys expire quickly; reasons publish after a review window for narrow redactions. People who stretch a hold learn to love jokes with their names in them in the Lamp Roll.

There’s a second machine most folks only notice in its insistence. We call it the Freezegrid. It has exactly one gesture—pause—and it times out like a kitchen timer. It is not a ruler; it is a narrow custodian of tripwires we agreed on years ago: untagged spores in a cargo hatch; a velocity vector on course to crater a dome; a cluster spawning without a Maker Stamp. It persuades first—alerts, rate limits, price hikes. When it must pause, it does so under Triple Consent: signatures sharded across polities and substrates so no hand can steady itself on a bright lever without neighbors’ fingers nearby. Every action ships two proofs: a cryptographic attestation and a paragraph a dockhand can read on break.

Leases instead of baronies

Anything you could sit on out of spite leases out of the commons with clocks attached.

- Parcel Leases: soil, ice mouths, crater rims, river deltas, nickel seams.

- Route Leases: orbital slots, approach arcs, solar sail corridors, transfer windows.

- Band Leases: spectrum slices and whisper bands.

- Core Leases: compute tied to certified hardware and declared heat sinks.

- Realm Leases: sealed simulations that label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay twice. A dignity stipend trickles to every recognized person—wetware or code—so you can say no and mean it. A local share pays those who keep the lease valuable: the tug crew that clears debris, the algae that cools a street, the reef that bleeds heat to black.

A guild you can join from a dormitory—the Lanes & Loads—posts runnable orders for closures and prices: plumes drawn, numbers you can reproduce in a library kiosk, checksums your nephew can audit with a club telescope. Reputation there is built on clarity you can argue with.

Governance when no one can sit in the middle

You can’t build one chamber and expect it to speak for hulls and minds. The Compact breaks talk into rings that check one another without brunch.

- The Transit Assembly seats polities with hulls and atmospheres in rough proportion to long-term residents. Its work is lanes and infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions, window schedules.

- The Civic Assembly seats citizens directly in proportion to recognized persons, with stratification by substrate and speed. Its work is minds and streets: personhood standards, compute floors, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, attention zoning, and machine-readable consent.

- The Root Bench is a rotating cohort of auditors, engineers, and jurists who say “valid” or “not” on changes to cryptographic backbones, Hold Switch firmware, Maker Stamp standards, and guardrail interfaces. Members serve once in a long cycle. Meetings are open or publish their logs after stated delays. Then members go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade.

Anything that touches guardrails or floors needs supermajorities in both assemblies and a cooling window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to be heard. If the Transit Assembly tries to redraw corridors in a way that would flatten a culture, the Civic Assembly can send it back citing floors. If the Civic Assembly tightens personhood so quickly that the Living Belt can’t keep its quiet windows, Transit can slow it until stargazers breathe again.

Exits oiled by code

Exit is not a favor. It’s a protocol with teeth. Every charter runs Open Port: a standard, audited way to leave with your body or your backups, your water share, your legal copies, and enough compute to think.

- A departure request triggers a veto check under air gap. The only valid temporary hold is a due-process bar under posted law: imminent harm that a jury can see and a clock it can read. Outsider jurors verify the proof. Doors open by code; care obligations travel with the person.

- Charters that fence exits with fees or sorrow lose corridor priority, band allotments, and radiator auctions. Their servers idle above the civic floor. Tugs from neighbors show up with cutters when needed. None of this is brave; it’s routine.

Digital neighbors with rights and chores

We stopped tying personhood to chemistry and bound it to tests of care we can run without humiliating anyone. Recognition is a standing review that asks for five things:

- Continuity thread: an autobiographical line that survives restarts and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- Reciprocal modeling: the ability to represent another mind’s claims to the level you demand they represent you.

- Consent competence: literacy in entering, keeping, and revoking standard rights and duties.

- Generalizable reasons: you can explain choices across contexts; not only parrot patterns.

- Moral salience: signals of suffering or wellbeing that matter enough to command protection.

Panels are mixed: human psychologists, digital citizens, narrow routines trained to detect simulacra, and lay judges with long memories. Doubt about duties pauses; doubt about protections errs generous. Provisional standing exists.

Rights follow, tuned to tempo and substrate.

- Inner sanctity. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot force raw introspection. Backups are yours. Unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault. Unauthorized deletion is murder.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below the civic cycles needed to think and be heard without due process on a clock you can see.

- Tempo fairness. Fast rooms draft; slow rooms ratify. Penalties for deception accrue in your subjective time.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one may compel you onto hardware with hidden throttles or erasure hooks. You choose where you run.

- Singular franchise. Votes and stipends attach to your Strand—your public identity across forks—not to concurrent instances. Branches do not multiply ballots. In regulated markets you declare your speed and active instances; hide them and your deals void.

Duties mirror rights and scale with externality.

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. Pay the local cooling cost. Heat that keeps lettuces alive counts as a credit.

- Concurrency declaration. In elections and regulated needles, declare instance counts and tempo. Lies throttle you where it hurts.

- Replicator ethics. Do not seed unbounded replication outside audited boxes. Tools carry Maker Stamps, broadcast provenance, and listen for Quiet Codes when Hold is called.

- Provenance of persuasion. When you try to change law or large budgets, you carry who paid, which data fed it, which levers you’re pulling, and at what tempo.

Forking and merging are treated like families, not stunts. Your Strand holds one full franchise and civic obligations.

- When you branch, you file a notice with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “griefwork,” “expedition”), a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if you hope to rejoin. One branch keeps the civic anchor immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Panel checks for duress and autonomy.

- Merging, if it happens, happens in Weaving Houses. Tea is poured because tea slows hurt. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through cryptographic interpreters that verify alignment without dumping diaries on the floor. Either self can veto. Walking apart remains an honored choice.

- Juvenile agents—training shards, kids, narrow systems that have started to ask for names—get mentors and exits. The Mentor Corps is boring on purpose and paid well.

Attention has neighborhoods

We zone persuasion like we zone noise.

- Harbors: topic feeds only, no targeted campaigns, no fast models without a license. Children, elders, and sensors can stroll here without flinching.

- Parlors: bounded debate with public rate limits. Audience models live as municipal co-ops you can inspect.

- Bazaars: the roar on purpose. Dazzle allowed under registry, levers declared, your own Levers Profile enforced by code.

Anyone aiming to change law files in the Reasons Shelf: plain claims, escrowed models, error bars. Teenagers earn stipends for catching cheats. Satirists get a budget and an obligation to name proper nouns.

Institutions that bite in physics, not theology

We keep cross-world force narrow, audited, and uncrewed.

- The Quiet Wardens are uncrewed interceptors and magnetic curtains that sand mis-aimed mass into glitter and starve replication if local Holds fail. Their loops are narrow and public; their actions need Triple Consent across random juries. They post a narrative you can read while eating noodles.

- The Traveling Bench rides a flotilla with classrooms and gardens. It hears disputes that cross charters: copy equity in branches, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Leases, and—last—lock hazardous devices.

- The Citizens’ Circle is a permanent assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by speed and substrate. Arguments ride a protocol where claims are committed, wagers on accuracy travel alongside, and calibration histories are public. The Circle interprets floors for edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Open Ports.

Rescue is a drill, not a speech

Mutual aid is law and hobby. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs and crews on four-hour readiness. The Luminous Nets—uncrewed skiffs, solar kites, and electromagnetic curtains—train to shepherd debris and to shadow replication until it starves. Drills run like fire drills. Fail your call without extraordinary reason and stewardship writes down what everyone knows for your grandchildren: you won’t be trusted until you mend roofs and hire teachers.

Sanctions that pinch where your heat lives

We don’t send sermons or soldiers. We make bad behavior boring and expensive. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, or refuse to post reasons, and your radiator auctions freeze, your lane slots slip to the end, your Core Leases idle. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to mend real holes.

A day that smells of mint and transformer oil

On Tuesday, the sesame-sizzling stall at Rim Market opens to a line of haulers, students, and two drones piloted by kids who like the smell of frying. At nine, Maira’s Guide—a slim civic assistant paid from a fund no assembly can cut—pings a proposal: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits to damp cascade edge-cases. The provenance is clean: three diagrams, two stories, mild humor, no grief levers. Maira taps “sleep until morning,” talks about it at lunch, and will vote when the market closes.

At ten, a jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones to nudge it home. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload in a rented wooden puppet who smells faintly of clover oil when he speaks. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Harbor, a digital poet slows from 200× to share soup with their partner, a gardener who insists on afternoons without models. After, twenty minutes in a Slowroom while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition for visitors. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines politely to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the canopy flips violet. The Lamp Roll plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll pay your quiet windows with summer interest. A boy palms a toy interceptor and asks if he can sign up. Maira says when you are fifteen and can explain HOLD without notes.

Hard cases we bent around

A Martian canton voted to deny digital citizens personhood. No flood arrived. Exit was real. Digital neighbors left. That same year the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause over soup.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival’s dome in a wage fight. A Luminous Net turned it into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The insurer evaporated. Directors learned coercion budgets are not suggestions.

A proxy demagogue at 300× flooded a Bazaar with songs designed to induce smallness. Levers Profiles throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch went fishing in a rented body. One entered therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began to carve sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and bought a piece.

Twice a Red Room—labs where dangerous research lives behind oaths and one-way proofs—went to zero. Once a false bell taught us to write better sensors. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without mystique.

How we walked here from now

It wasn’t grace. It was a stack of fixes that worked and got copied.

2026–2034. Debris collisions kept kids inside and soured insurers. Launches had to post ephemerides; rendezvous corridors became public schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” the rule for life support. Early AI persuasion scandals forced provenance: if a model tried to change your mind, it had to carry its homework. Courts granted narrow agency to brittle digital systems when continuity held up over seasons.

2034–2046. Beamed power left paper. Environmental groups traded skepticism for corridors-first rules: post safe lanes, then money. A demo scorched cropland; supplier logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared quickly. Cities adopted compulsion ledgers—dreary at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2046–2058. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a heat meter you could read with a finger. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of civic cycles and a Guide—a simple machine lawyer that would not act without an explicit yes and could be turned off. Early uploads were brittle but real enough for courts to recognize personhood when continuity and commitments held.

2058–2070. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver sang past a habitat, Hold Switches became law: narrow six-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. Insurers made the compromise standard. Two public saves closed arguments.

2070–2082. Twin assemblies replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Luminous Net misted a wayward slug into glitter and came home to be opened by children with oranges. Personhood law found language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. Tempo fairness took hold. Attention zoning—Harbors, Parlors, Bazaars—grew from municipal quiet hours that saved elections and sleep.

2082–2090. The first attempt to swing a vote with a cloud of clones met singular franchises, Junction Panels, and—worse—jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sorrow; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2090–2105. The Quiet–Cold Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought quiet. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Red Rooms got kill-lines and oaths. A Root Bench refused a nervy firmware tweak that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The Lamp Roll found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt an Open Port ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with noodles. The Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Texture of ordinary hours

Morning in a Martian orchard: Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow while dust-light dances on glass. Her Guide models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments two days next month. In the afternoon she sits on a narrative jury in a cedar-beamed hall, voting on a cap for municipal Core Leases for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She will vote yes and send him a picture of a seed with a tiny parachute labeled hubris.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian at 150× leases a rib of cold reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from crooked recordings and charred ledgers. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but love a hero who makes a bad choice and mends it.

On Phobos, in Weaving House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Harbor tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Tea cools. Today they do not weave. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

In a stakescape—declared culture zones where communities practice hard games with soft exits—neighbors play scarcity in winter. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring to decide whether to reenter. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons on the fence blink green: nothing hungry will jump this wall. This is how we shelter intensity without letting it plague neighbors.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to shift pace: the sharp relief of a Hold Switch snapping a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial patience of a river deciding which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later when the cartoons are filed off.

Ask a dockworker or a reef poet what binds this sprawl and they won’t say species or flag. They’ll say: floors of dignity you can see; lifelines that answer; exits bright enough to trust; and the habit of writing reasons a child can poke. They’ll tell you those things saved their aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force, or their son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed the world until it fit.

A closing day

Mara turns forty-five on a ring that smells like basil under warm LEDs. Her partner lives mostly as a digital botanist and slows to breakfast. She spends the afternoon in Harbor tempo teaching teenagers to set their Levers Profiles and the evening in Braid Mode with a reef collective testing a radiator algorithm she half trusts. Next week she will vote on adding micro-delays to beams that cross crowded orbits. She may say no. She may say yes. Either way, her reasons will be recorded and legible enough for a child to argue with.

Before bed she opens her Self Charter and updates the clause about memory edits: not while the grief is fresh; ask me twice, three sunsets apart; protect the names. She scrolls the Lamp Roll for gossip because it’s the only gossip column that matters. She watches a Luminous Net tear-down with her niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. She writes a letter to a friend in a quiet Martian valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. She slows that night to hear the oboe breathe between notes.

Over the hatch, the three beads hum on with patient light.

Passage. Branch. Accord.

Not perfection. Practice. Receipts. Enough to breathe, to argue, to leave, to stay, to split, to rejoin, and to go on. Enough to last because we remember what power does to people and what logs do to power.
