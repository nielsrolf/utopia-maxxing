The Waystead Accord

Walk into the east lock of Crosswell Port just after maintenance night. The smell is a mixture of algae broth and transformer oil; your boots make little squeaks against rubber that’s been scrubbed too hard. Above the door three slim bars of light run the length of the wall, pulsing barely enough to see: one green, one amber, one blue. They are wired to budgets, keys, and valves. If you rest your palm beneath any of them, you can feel a faint vibration, like a cat purring in a distant room.

- The green bar is the Route Rail. It turns bright when at least one actual exit is open to you: tug slots booked, berths reserved, water and heaters escrowed, a legal packet ready, and compute minutes you can carry. It also shows the inspectors who graded the path and when they last stepped through it.

- The amber bar is the Lineage Rail. It brightens when you signal that you’re about to copy, merge, adopt, or retire a self. It brings up mentors on duty, how heat and cycles flow to a new branch, what rituals apply if you ever want to reunite, and what protections hold if you change your mind.

- The blue bar is the Neighbor Rail. It glows steady if your home charter can be read by a ninth-grader and a machine, if it keeps the public floors, and if it refuses you only with reasons you and a stranger can check.

They aren’t décor. Those three bars are the visible face of the Waystead Accord, the thin layer that most of us now live under whether we breathe air or cycle bits. It is not a throne. It is a relay: a fabric of receipts and brakes that makes it possible to coordinate between garden domes and ice barges, monasteries and market rings, server reefs and valley towns without stamping them into sameness or letting them be devoured.

The Accord grew from three stubborn lessons that distance rubbed into us. First, latency is law. No one holds the center long enough to issue edicts that arrive on time. Second, speed is a trade, not a right to rule. Third, differences survive when exits are funded, not promised.

Two tempos, one bargain

Our homes do not beat to the same clock. The Accord does not demand a common pace; it knits the tempos where they touch.

- The Hearth Belt is where bodies set the terms: Earth’s recovered deltas and uplands, lunar cylinders that smell like mint and warm loam, Martian groves under glass, freefall orchards circling above Oaxaca and Qingdao. Here, daylight, weather, school bells, and the time it takes to share soup matter more than throughput.

- The Frost Reaches are the cold wide swaths where heat spills to black and compute blooms like lichen: Trojan swarms, backside reefs near Neptune, caravans parked in the Kuiper where an afternoon can be a subjective week. Upload cooperatives, training houses, and digital polities sprint here, then slow to talk.

Between them runs a clean trade called the Hush-for-Cold Exchange. Hearth towns buy hush: declared windows in law where beams, models, and persuasion throttle to human beat. Frost reefs sell cold: compute minutes with audits that any school library can rerun, climate and traffic models that don’t surprise poor neighborhoods before rich ones, art and tools that sing in two tempos at once. Prices—watts, decibels, degrees, cycles—post on a public ledger that a scout troop can check with a pamphlet and a telescope.

Receipts that a child can argue with

The ledger is called Starbook. It’s a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident set of records pegged to physics. It posts where things are and what they’re allowed to do: sail corridors and burn windows; mirror angles and shadow bonds; approach arcs; debris funds; spectrum slices; radiator rights tied to hardware signatures; compute allotments bound to heat; declared instance counts when votes or fragile markets are at stake; sealed simspaces and their exits labeled like lifeboats.

- Big mirrors and beams sign their contexts with keys you can burn into metal and that a hobby club can audit. If your light steals my dawn, Starbook ties your action to a shade bond that pays if crops underperform.

- Quiet windows are public in decibels, angles, and times. Pierce one for rescue and you owe collateral with reasons that will later be read out loud.

- Starbook’s Thermal Annex tracks three dull things that keep us decent: energy draw, cooling allotments, and declared concurrency. Declare three selves and pull like thirty in a regulated arena? Your radiator auctions pinch to idle until your numbers meet your signatures.

A student with a borrowed scope and a kitchen laser can point at a scheduled burn and check the checksum on their slate.

Floors you can print on a glove

Under everything lie laws that compile to code and practice. They fit on the back of a maintenance glove.

1) No nonconsensual harm to any person. A person is any being, regardless of substrate, who can report its own experience across time and enter mutual commitments.

2) Life support and civic guardrails run on code anyone can inspect and roll back. Changes log and time out.

3) Decisions that bind embodied lives ratify at embodied tempo. Draft fast; bind slow.

4) Rivalrous commons—lanes, spectra, radiator windows, cave mouths, sim realms—lease with sunsets and receipts. The commons is paid first.

5) No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Self-copying code and devices carry Maker Marks and listen for Hush Codes adjudicated by random juries.

6) No concealed manufacture of suffering, in flesh or in simulation.

7) The coordination substrate—the Charter Beacons, the consent mesh, the brake interface—is nobody’s to own and is narrow on purpose.

8) Children, in any substrate, learn three things before algebra: how to set boundaries, how to exit, how to guard one’s own mind.

9) Exits are budgets, not speeches. Every charter escrows real logistics so one door stays unblocked.

Brakes, not rulers

The thing that prevents worst-days is a network of brake boxes called the Pause Net. It does not plan. It only stops.

- Scope. Any actuator that could make a crater, a vacuum, a famine, a stampede, or a mass delusion must listen to one small, boring interface: mass drivers, beamers, pump stations, high-dose persuasion engines, replicators above a named scale.

- Amber pause. The Pause Net can call a short amber—usually seven minutes—when three simple checks fail: wrong window, wrong beacon, missing plan. To hold longer, it needs two concurrences on the clock: one from a local panel drawn by lot among those who logged civic hours this year, and one from a remote auditor running on hardware the locals do not own.

- Keys split by distance. Updating Pause Net firmware or overriding requires threshold signatures sharded across far hands: a terran court, a belt guild, an outer reef, a freefall union, a monastic valley. Keys never sleep on the same rack. Windows overlap just enough to work.

- Ugly on purpose. Every box has lights you can see across a hangar and fuses you could break with a crowbar. Apprentices learn their boxes with the same seriousness grandparents once learned CPR.

Three houses that cannot swallow each other

Coordination does not climb a pyramid. It braids through three bodies that meet in alternating gardens and hollows.

- The Waykeepers’ Assembly seats polities with hulls and atmospheres and bounded servers, roughly in proportion to long-term residents. It tends lanes, spectra, radiator windows, sail arcs, docking standards, debris funds, and rescue covenants.

- The Commons of Minds seats citizens directly, proportionate to recognized persons, stratified by substrate and speed. It tends personhood standards, copy budgets, compute and bandwidth floors, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, and neighborhoods of attention.

- The Keys Bench is small, rotating, and deliberately dull: cryptographers, engine techs, pilots, jurists. It gates changes to the crypto substrate, the Pause Net interface, and technical definitions in Starbook. It stamps valid or not and publishes logs after posted delays. Bench members serve once in a long cycle and then go home; they cannot touch keys again for a decade.

Anything that touches brakes or floors needs supermajorities in both houses and a cooling window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to be heard. If the Waykeepers try to push a corridor through a culture’s sky, the Commons can send it back citing floors. If the Commons tightens personhood so quickly that Hearth towns cannot keep their hush, the Waykeepers can slow it until stargazers breathe again.

Leases instead of fiefdoms

Anything you could sit on out of spite leases from the commons with clocks attached.

- Ground Windows: deltas, crater rims, ice mouths, nickel seams, shade bands.

- Lane Windows: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail paths, transfer arcs.

- Band Windows: spectrum allocations and whisper bands for caves and storms.

- Core Windows: compute time bound to hardware identity and audited heat sinks.

- World Windows: sealed simulation spaces that must label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay twice. A dignity stipend trickles to every recognized person—wetware or code—so you can say no and mean it. A local share pays those who keep the lease valuable: tug crews that clear debris, algae that cools a street, reef farms that bleed heat to black.

The Wayfinder Guild—a mix of pilots, schedulers, auditors, and judges—allocates windows and posts Runnable Orders for every decision: minimal-model simulations that two independent houses can reproduce. “We closed approach SE-3 for four hours because two burns at Eunomia would have pushed your berth into a radio shadow” arrives with a plume plot, a checksum a teenager can verify at a library scope, and a footnote a tug pilot wrote because humor keeps systems honest.

The coin that clears and decays

Payments clear in a composite called a Lumen-Second: one standardized kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on a published architecture, with conversion tables you can check in any port. Lumen-Seconds gently demur: barns of value grow stale. A slice of every auction—lanes, beams, spectra, radiators, Core—funds a universal floor that is boring on purpose: baseline care and air; minimum Core minutes and bandwidth sufficient to think and be heard; and one unchallenged exit in any rolling seven years.

Digital neighbors with rights and chores

We stopped tying dignity to chemistry and started binding it to tests of care and continuity that we could run without humiliation. Standing begins provisional and becomes robust.

Panels—mixed on purpose: psychologists, digital citizens, ethicists, and routines trained to spot simulacra—look for:

- Reported experience that holds across time and context.

- Continuity through pauses and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- A working grasp of consent and harm that supports mutual promises.

- Coherent preferences not merely artifacts of a narrow training loop.

- Absence of deceptive mimicry aimed at rewards without inner life.

Doubt about protections errs generous. Doubt about duties waits.

Rights with teeth:

- Integrity. No deletion, coercive edit, or throttling below civic floor without consent or due process recorded against your Continuity Thread.

- Sanctuary. The right to halt and be archived safely; to migrate to compatible racks; to summon a neutral custodian.

- Provenance. The right to audit or appoint a fiduciary to audit the training and update history that shaped you.

- Association and exit. The right to join, fork, merge, and leave.

- Pace fairness. Public binds run at a civic beat; any person can invoke a Slowroom for guaranteed turn-taking and bounded rates. If you run at 200× and trick a gardener at 1×, penalties tick in your felt time.

- Embodiment. You may lease eyes and hands under standards like traffic law. Assault on a rented body is assault.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one may compel you onto racks with hidden throttles or erase hooks. Unauthorized erasure is murder; unauthorized copying that binds your will is assault.

Duties that scale with externality:

- Thermodynamic duty. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill adjusted to local cost; heat that grows basil pays you back.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, regulated markets, and fragile places, you declare your number of instances and your speed. Undeclared asymmetry voids votes and deals.

- Persuasion provenance. Above a dose, models that try to move law or budgets carry their levers and training on their sleeves. Audience agencies must be inspectable cooperatives.

- Reproduction ethics. Forking uses lineage shares; it is not a tax dodge. Replicators carry Maker Marks and listen for Hush Codes. A universal hush binds when a jury calls one.

Lineages, forks, and merges

Public identity attaches to a lineage that can branch and pause. A lineage holds one full civic franchise.

- When you fork, you declare a purpose (“parallel design for the tide gates,” “griefwork,” “expedition”), file a hashed snapshot, and lease cycles for the new branch. One branch retains the vote at once; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Panel checks for duress and fair flows.

- Branch credits accrue with civic work and years. They buy a second full franchise above the floor. Clone clouds that rise overnight trigger quarantine: instances cannot amplify each other in the same debate until entanglements decay or contracts split resources.

- Merging happens in Braid Houses—half teahouse, half server room. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs through interpreters that verify alignment without dumping diaries. Either self can veto. Unbraiding is an option until the last stage.

- Juvenile agents—training shards, kids, near-persons—get Tutors and exits. Tutors are substrate-diverse and boring in the best way: steady teachers with courage for boundaries.

Memory law guards the self from its worst days. Deep edits need dual consent: from the you who wants them now and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right.

Neighborhoods of attention

Persuasion is power. We zone it like we zone noise.

- Gardens: topic feeds only, no targeted campaigns, no fast models without a license and timers. Children, elders, and sensors stroll here.

- Squares: bounded debate with rate limits and public logs. Audience models live as municipal co-ops.

- Carnivals: the roar on purpose, under registry and audit. Above doses, labels and timers required.

Doorways carry a little sigil—a hand over a leaf—and the sentence: Ask before you cast. A Reason Shelf in each polity holds homework for persuasion meant to move policy: plain claims, declared tools, models under escrow so auditors can step through what would otherwise be magic. Teenagers earn stipends for catching cheats.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Skylight Wardens are the only cross-world force. Their actuators are uncrewed by law. Their loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited by schools, unions, and hobby clubs. They contain kinetic and replicative threats when locals cannot. Every action requires threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries across polities and a human-readable reason appended to the Cause Log.

- The Traveling Court rides ships with classrooms and gardens. It hears disputes that cross charters: copy equity in branches, merge divorces, export violations. Its verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Windows, and—last—lock hazardous devices.

- The Citizens’ Round is a permanent assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by substrate, speed, and culture. Arguments ride a protocol where claims commit cryptographically, predictions travel alongside, and calibration histories are public. The Round interprets floors for edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Lighthouses.

- The Slow Synod meets by synodic calendars in fairs and quiet halls. It tends shared commons—spectrum bands, debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that decay unless renewed.

Sanctions that pinch where your heat lives

No fleets. Three levers instead.

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence; maps are repaired with dates. “Unbolt by the 12th. Hire exit stewards. Return lanes to auction. Here are seals we’ll honor on arrival.”

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions cool. Lane priorities sag. Bands drop to whispers. Insurers lift premiums until boredom and price do what sermons cannot.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute flows to defectors with beds, bread, cores, and counsel from a dull, proud fund. People leave with minds and skin intact.

Earth’s special clause

Earth keeps her own law. The rest of us help under a public pact: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power stays in mapped corridors and pays a premium into shade and land repair; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to exact place—monsoon, fog, desert—is priced alongside cash. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind can trade larger claims on the far commons for a guarantee of local care; a child in Arizona aching for black sky can swap the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil.

How we walked here from now

We did not leap. We stacked fixes and watched them hold.

2026–2033. Debris scares and grid hacks drive insurers to meanness. Regulators publish the first open flight-plan registries with deorbit bonds. A beamed power demo scorches cropland; the supplier’s proofs make liability legible and compensation quick because proof does. After a run of ugly persuasion scandals, governments require provenance and explainability for models touching rights. Guardians—simple personal AI advocates with vetoes—become boringly normal.

2033–2044. Beamed power climbs out of labs. Environmental coalitions win “corridors before commercialization.” Compute commons sprout in cislunar shade and at L1; cities sign up for minimum cold minutes and a civic counsel that cannot act without an explicit yes and can be shut off. Harbor laws demand end-of-life plans and escrow. Energy and compute logs evolve from PR to receipts.

2044–2057. Early uploads are brittle but real: prosthetic memory and mirrored voices give way to persons who keep promises across seasons. Courts recognize personhood where continuity and care hold. The ancestor of Starbook appears when ports and reefs realize they can meter energy, cooling, and concurrency like pilots once metered harbor entries.

2057–2069. After a beam misalignment browns a greenhouse and a driver test whistles too near a habitat, “amber holds” become law: narrow vetoes chief engineers can call for minutes without asking anyone. Activists smell a coup-by-gadget. Engineers insist on fuses you can see, juries you can watch, and code you can print. The compromise grows into the Pause Net: brakes with clocks and neighbors.

2069–2081. The Waykeepers’ Assembly replaces back rooms with receipts. The Commons of Minds writes continuity-and-reciprocity standards that good lawyers respect and eleven-year-olds can mock. The first Skylight Wardens dust a wayward slug into aurora and come home to be opened by kids with oranges.

2081–2092. A cloud of clones tries to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttle the lunge; worse for the plotters, jokes do. A lunar mayor fences an exodus with sorrow and fees; lanes gray, servers chill, the move happens anyway. He loses, learns, and goes back to running a school. Gardens, Squares, and Carnivals move from vibe to door signage.

2092–2108. The Keys Bench refuses a nervy change to Pause Net firmware that would have let emergency clocks stretch “just in case.” The Cause Log’s funny page grows teeth. A rescue to cut an Embassy Ring free streams with captions and ends with soup. The Accord stops feeling like a manifesto and starts feeling like doorbells you trust.

When it breaks and mends

A cylinder above Valparaíso elects a director who gathers water, law, and defense because she is generous and makes trains hum. Half a year later she signs midnight orders. The Pause Net refuses a risky “optimization”; logs write to the Cause Log. A navigation jury arrives like a fire extinguisher. The Route Rail lights green. Many leave. Some stay. She loses two keys and takes a narrower role with humor. Her friends still buy her a crown-shaped pastry because jokes heal.

In the high belt, a miner tries to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit during a wage fight. Wardens dust it into glitter that makes a week of strange nights. The miner’s insurer folds and sheds three arrogant managers. Dockmasters triple fees for manifests hiding Maker Marks. The local hockey club rebrands and sends profits to the tug co-op that modeled the plume.

In a Scarlet Lab Bay, the Pause Net drops power and floods a hall with nitrogen. It is a false positive. The lead, who cried into sleeves, teaches the next safety class with the story and the swears that kept any funerals off the calendar.

Tuesday smells like cinnamon and solder

Lina’s bakery on Spinmarket-Three opens to miners, kids, and two drones whose pilots like the smell of sugar. The canopy above shifts from market signs to a spray of stars as radiators bleed heat. At nine, her civic counsel pings her will capsule. An outer ring wants her vote on adding a 40-millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp rare feedback cascades. The persuasion is registered: three diagrams, two stories, one joke, no grief levers. Lina taps “sleep it a day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Warden jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud drifted toward a power trunk. Options: glitter net, femto-sail herding, or guard drones to nudge it home. The jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover oil when they speak through a rented puppet. They pick dusting, fine the insurer, assign a licensed mentor. The feed shows mortified teenagers and pizza arriving.

At noon in a Garden zone, a digital poet slows from 180× to anchor pace and puppets a wooden body with stiff shoulders and real warmth. He shares broth with his spouse; his main instance is in the dark near Triton, stitching a drowned library. After soup, he spends twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a cloister gardener explains why her charter caps cognition for visitors. He translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely declines to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the Cause Log’s honesty page plays. A port director renewed an amber hold ninety seconds too long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for four years; we’ll pay your hush cones in summer with interest. A boy fingers a brass Warden fin and asks when he can volunteer. Lina says: when you can explain a pause without your notes.

Ordinary law in small rooms

On Phobos, in Braid House Nine, two Arturos try to weave. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell for a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count and spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Tea cools. Today they do not merge. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

In a stakescape—a declared culture zone where a community plays with scarcity in winter—neighbors run hard games with soft exits. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring with choices. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons on the fence blink green: nothing hungry will jump this wall. This is how we shelter intensity without letting it plague neighbors.

Relationships that make physics polite

Ask a tug pilot, a reef poet, or a farmer’s Tutor what binds this sprawling family and they won’t say species or flag. They’ll say: floors of dignity you can see; lifelines that answer; exits you can afford; receipts for the few places where we put hands on each other’s levers; and the habit of writing reasons a child can poke. They’ll tell you those things saved their aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force; or their son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade; or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed the world until it fit.

Texture and trust

You feel watched—not by a throne, but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to switch pace: the sharp relief of a pause snapping a relay through your thumb; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial patience of a river deciding which boat to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you spend a morning in the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe.

The roof over your head may be basil leaves trembling along a curve of glass, or a ceiling of black fins tilted to star-cold. Either way, somewhere near your door three small bars of light hum gently and wait.

Route. Lineage. Neighbor.

Not perfection. Practice. Receipts. Enough to breathe, to argue, to leave, to stay, to split, to rejoin, and to go on. Enough to last because we remember what power does to people and what logs do to power.
