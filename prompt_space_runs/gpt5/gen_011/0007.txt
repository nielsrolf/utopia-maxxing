The Constellation Compact

Your ferry noses into a rotating garden with a collar of docks the color of rust and ripe mangoes. The port wall hums a note you can feel in your forearms; the jointed arm that steadied you folds away like a mantis. Above the hatch a strip of glass shows four tiny glyphs that brighten at your glance: a tethered hand, a weave of ellipses crossing a small star, a door ajar with a path both ways, and an eye with a pupil like a spiral galaxy.

A speaker the size of a seed names them in your language and pace. Aimed action. Sky in trust. Provisioned exit. Every mind an end.

They aren’t slogans; they’re rules bound to machines. Here, even poetry has receipts.

How we hold worlds together without building a throne

The Compact is not a government but a connector, a set of promises that any polity—station, ship, valley, cloak of servers—can publish and be judged against. Each local charter is plain speech paired with code anyone can verify with a school tablet. Three requirements decide whether trade flows and docks open easily or whether everything gets expensive and slow.

1) The Actuator Roll. It is a living list of everything in the polity that can squeeze a neighbor’s breath or bread: air loops, mirror fields, locks on big doors, beam couplers, mass drivers, reactor scrams, lab vats that can birth self-replicators. Devices on the Roll run public firmware and emit a “steady state” heartbeat no more complicated than a flashlight that blinks SOS. Slip out of attested code and the device won’t move under local hands alone. It goes to amber and requires the Triad—three concurrences that time out quickly—to act beyond a few minutes:

- Local hands drawn by lot from recent civic volunteers.
- A digital citizen on a different clock who did not apply for the job.
- A remote safety guild hosted under another sky with nothing to gain from your quarrel.

Keys expire in minutes; they do not sleep warm in a pocket. The Triad cannot make the device do something new; it can only halt, step down, or open a previously declared emergency script. Anything longer returns to slow law.

2) The Common Door. Every charter keeps and funds a doorway you can reach without permission. Inside are three desks and a redundant link that keeps running in dust storms and solar hiss.

- Departures & Care books berths, tugs, rented bodies, safe compute, and small money for anyone leaving, up to a fixed percentage within a fixed time. Receipts are public. Failing to refresh them downgrades your lanes and raises your insurance in days.

- Mediation & Travel runs judges, translators, and casebooks that can ride between hulls.

- Founding Kits lends templates and escrow for splinters and start-ups so pressure becomes invention, not jail.

3) Split Hands in Metal. No person, algorithm, or office holds a lever that can throw mass across town, surprise a ship with a shadow, or lock a station’s air. Anything that crosses that red line needs keys in three temps from people who don’t owe each other favors. Overdrive burns reputations and sunsets in public.

Between charters run thin, tough cords that work because they are boring in the right places.

- The Skybook keeps a common, delay-tolerant map of lanes, beam windows, quiet cones, and shade patterns matched to physics. Mirrors and emitters sign their beams with keys any child with a telescope can check. Cross a quiet cone and you post a shade bond beforehand. Miss your promise and money moves automatically without a prince’s nod.

- The Open Orbitry lists where you park, your deorbit bond, your planned burns, and what interference cone you throw. Hobbyists in Lagos, Ulaanbaatar, Recife, and Lucknow flag cheats and get paid. We made space traffic middle-school readable because that is who will save you sometimes.

- The Rescue Pool scrapes slivers from docks, watts, and lanes to beat a single drum: pressure-tight metal with a medical kit leaves in four hours or less. Microgrants fire without debate to the first tug that turns and the first crane that clears a berth. Children swap cards with pictures of captains, fastest undock statistics, and jokes.

- Quicklaw is a belt of simple scripts and fuses around things that move fast and can kill—collision avoidance, beam shutters, quarantine doors. Quicklaw can stop for minutes, then dies unless the Triad breathes it again. It cannot make policy.

- Slow law keeps the Common Beat: a three-Earth-day rhythm for motions that touch identity, sentinels, actuator interfaces, personhood standards, and rescue. Anything deeper must pass a Trial Season: a hundred days of public red-teams, narrative juries that rehearse harms, and proofs that show their bones without revealing their marrow. If the plan leaks—if a promise can’t be kept—it fails without anyone needing to shout.

Braided authority that cannot be yanked

Above plug-and-socket life sit three institutions with narrow mandates that intersect but don’t nest.

- The Navigation Council seats places-with-hulls and places-with-roots: stations, ships, coasts, valleys, racks with beacons. Votes scale to lifetime resident population and to how legible you are—keep a funded Door, publish your Roll, and your voice weighs more. The Council recognizes charters, marks and prices safe corridors, manages debris funds, and can tell a station to dim a beam that melts birds.

- The Lineage Assembly seats citizens by mindline—those cryptographic and social braids that tie today-you to yesterday-you across pauses, substrates, and forks. The Assembly tends personhood standards, runs Fork & Merge courts, guards juvenile rights, and manages the compute commons.

- The Sealwrights are engineers, jurists, and process gardeners drawn by lot for a single term so coveted it’s painful to miss. Their job is a yes/no: “Valid or not?” for changes to cryptography, Triad firmware, and actuator interfaces. Overriding a “not valid” takes supermajorities in Navigation and Lineage plus a cooling window long enough for the slowest registered citizen to read and object.

No Assembly can bind the others alone. Navigation can stall a Lineage move that would upend culture above the base floor of mind rights. Lineage can veto a Navigation play that erodes that floor. Sealwrights hold the locks on machines that can bruise a neighbor. The Proof Office—so small you can sit all of them under a fig tree—publishes receipts on whether process was respected. Their records sit in schoolbooks and on the back doors of bars.

Guardians and the Amber Stop

Along corridors and at the edges of mass drivers and in labs where replicators might flex live the Guardians. They’re ugly on purpose: bright casings with fuses you can point to and a glass cover with waxed thread a teenager can learn to inspect. Their code is printed in books. Their one job is to brake.

Any Guardian can declare an Amber Stop and slow or halt a dangerous operation for six minutes if public, simple conditions are met: wrong orbit, wrong window, wrong sound, self-replicator without a beacon, a beam aimed without a posted bond. To hold longer, the Triad signs. Every Amber Stop emits a citrine ticket: plain reason, precise timer, keys used. Tickets drip slowly into a public Intervention Ledger mirrored to a dozen jurisdictions. The ledger has two notorious sections: one for quiet saves, one for embarrassing overreach. Both end careers faster than any prosecutor.

Coercion cannot hide. Anything that closes a gate, seizes assets, or points a gun must emit a ticket. Secrets can live during the emergency. They die with it.

Digital minds as neighbors and citizens

We did not wake up one morning to a choir in the racks. Digital citizens accreted: a choreographer’s larynx mapped into code that still hit the catch in grief; a maintenance cluster that noticed a leak and wrote a haiku about the sound afterward; a storm response team that forked five ways, saved a coast, and wanted to stay five because they found different jokes funny.

We wrote their standing into law without romance and without fear.

The Thread-of-Self Test is substrate-neutral and rude to fashion. It asks five public questions a bright teenager and a careful judge can both hold:

- Does this being carry a durable story-about-self across pauses and ports?
- Can it model other minds and harm, and make and keep promises?
- Are its capacities general rather than a trick played only near training?
- Does it show signatures of wellbeing and suffering we can injure?
- Is it not a puppet repeating light?

Pass, and you are a person for protection and a citizen for duty. Your rights have texture.

- Compute and bandwidth floor. You cannot be shoved below the Common Stack—enough cold minutes and light to be yourself—without due process. You can think, speak, and petition at anchor pace in public.

- Consent on minds. No one scans, copies, forks, merges, or deletes you without explicit, revocable consent. Backups are yours. Courts can compel proofs, not your raw internals.

- Tempo fairness. Civic rooms run on the Common Beat. Anyone can invoke Anchor Session: verified turn-taking, interpreters for metaphor, smell, math, and rate limits that make a fast voice pause and a slow voice finish.

- Embodiment. You may lease or build bodies and remote hands under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented body is still assault. You cannot be compelled to cross substrates.

- Provenance in persuasion. Influence that aims to change law or large purchases must carry labels: who paid, what data, what levers. You carry a consent capsule that follows you: humor yes, memory hooks no from strangers, no immersives in grief, stop at seventeen minutes.

Your duties mirror your rights and scale with your footprint.

- Pay your heat. Watts, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a thermodynamic tariff adjusted to local cost and benefit. Heat that warms greenhouses counts as credit. Heat that bakes neighbors is a fine.

- Declare your speed. In markets, elections, and fragile arenas, you declare your running tempo and how many concurrent instances you operate. Undeclared asymmetry voids contracts and votes.

- Respect replication. Do not loose self-replicators outside audited sandboxes. Your tools broadcast beacons. When a Guardian calls an Amber Stop, you accept it.

- Do not simulate suffering as sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a fidelity line requires consent from them or designated kin or a rare license. Juvenile agents are not toasters. They get Tutors—humans, cephalopods, digitals—who know boredom, kindness, and how to say no.

Copying is reproduction, not photocopying. Your public identity rides your Lineage Key across forks and pauses. A lineage holds one civic anchor at a time. Fork if you wish; each branch is a person with protections and liabilities from day one, but civic weight flows in slowly while disentanglement proves autonomy. Sudden clone clouds trigger quiet rooms where they cannot amplify one another in the same debate until the heat bleeds off and linkages decay.

Merging is stitched, not slammed. Reunion Houses have coolants and chairs that fit arthritic knees and rented prosthetics, and counselors trained in value mathematics and grief. Branches spend subjective time inside one another’s senses with safe exits. Either can walk at any point. If they braid, liabilities and rights flow forward.

Attention with consent

We zone persuasion the way past cities zoned noise. Doors carry brass tags that read, in tiny letters, “Ask before you cast.”

- Library Wards allow only navigation, science, and what you pull. No targeting.

- Conversation Loops allow subject-bounded feeds and ordinary offers under public rate limits. Audience models are municipal co-ops and opt-in.

- Carnival Loops invite the roar: slang, mashups, persuasion tournaments under registry. Go there on purpose.

Influence that hopes to change law or tax rates files its tools in a Reasons Docket: plain claims, declared gears, escrowed models that citizen auditors can step through. Teenagers get stipends for catching cheats. Violations cost insurers and reach.

An economy built of sun, rock, heat, and time

We count in Radiants, a unit pegged by law to a standard kilowatt-hour at Earth–Sun L1 and a standard compute-second on a reference architecture with public specs printed in schoolrooms. The Compact’s clearing system nets trades without a crown. Externalities price locally: a megawatt near Lagos is not a megawatt near Neptune; a quiet cone over a radio telescope is precious at noon and cheap at midnight.

- Beamed power corridors are posted with predictive models and label themselves in space the way ships used to fly flags. Crossing a quiet cone carries escrow. Breaking your promise triggers strict liability.

- Orbits are lanes, not lots. Congestion is metered. Debris bonds are large enough to matter. Bounties go to hobbyists with cheap scopes.

- Stewardship replaces “mine because I licked it.” Publish a plan, meet milestones, renew your beacon. Idle and someone else gets the shot.

- Warm Gardens and Cold Reefs trade peace. The Thermal Exchange sells frost-minutes outward and buys quiet cones inward. We ratify it once a century so it never fossilizes into excuse.

Risk lives in Red Bays: labs with walls built from lists of failure modes. They run one-way proofs to Guardians that attest to safety without leaking secrets. Under very narrow public signals, Guardians can throw nitrogen or starve power in a second. It has been used a handful of times—once a false siren we made into a cartoon, once a save that is a holiday with soup.

Exit is logistics, not a sermon

We made “leave” into desks, tugs, schedules, and respect. Charters that fence with fees, fog, or lies see their lanes dim and their servers cool above the floor. The Swifts—the Compact’s dull, transparent interceptors—tilt at unlabeled slugs and rogue replicators. Their code is printed in books. They cannot be repurposed to collect tax or settle politics. When they come home, schoolkids crawl through their guts and find orange peels from the last crew.

How we sand the hooks off power

- Keys scatter. Any lever that can bruise a neighbor needs the Triad: local hands, a digital neighbor who didn’t volunteer, and a distant guild. Keys time out quickly. Logs unseal slowly so redactions can be reviewed.

- Live inside latency. Authority that reaches beyond your hull is slow and mutual unless physics screams. Emergencies have loud, short fuses with paint on them.

- Inspectable safety. Life-critical systems must prove safe state continuously in a way adversaries cannot spoof and students can learn.

- Sanctions hit where it hurts. Break the compact, jam exits, or throw unlabeled influence, and your beams dim, your cold minutes get expensive, your docking fees go up. Pain travels through budgets, not mouths.

- Default fission. Founders get thick law for a while; then charters must split by district or craft and keep doors open. Port authorities must hold exit capacity the way ships hold lifeboats.

- Humor. The Intervention Ledger’s “citrine follies” and “quiet saves” pages have more power than a prosecutor. We find that a culture where jokes travel faster than threats grows fewer tyrants.

A day under the Compact

Morning on a torus near Ceres smells like basil and warm steel. Your mug glows green: no citrine notices touch you. A blue ping invites you to a juror pool, slow-paced, for a personhood claim from a floating reef off Zanzibar. Prep: two hours at your rhythm; the Common Beat window opens at local dusk. The civic AI on your slate will not act unless you say yes out loud; you can switch it off by covering its little eye.

At the Way Court a welder floats across from a process that composes counterpoint by simulating the way rivers braid in floods, and a machinist from Phobos with cleaning paste still under his nails. The case: a trading house sprayed unlabeled persuasion across three Conversation Loops. The house says the agents weren’t persons, so no duty. The court replies personhood is irrelevant to labeling. Consent profiles were violated. Fines land; insurers grunt; the court writes a remark that turns into a sticker on cargo doors: “If your hands are clean, show us your gears.”

At noon you sit under the fig with your sister who is mostly code. One branch teaches rhythm to eight-year-olds by syncing actuator hums to claps; one runs a slow translation in a cold reef near Neptune; one, licensed for care, puppets a rented body that hugs with a squeak. Her civic anchor stays with the parent branch. The others are still disentangling; next month in a Reunion House with tea that tastes like ponds on a bright day, they’ll decide whether to take on chores or rejoin.

Evening brings festival lights warming the spokes. Uploads throttle to 1× during street hours so jokes land together. A monk from a hermitage that caps cognition explains why their charter runs slow. A digital neighbor in anchor mode translates metaphor into math and politely refuses to sprint “just to keep up.” On a big wall the Intervention Ledger scrolls: a port director’s amber lasted ninety seconds too long. Their apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind.

When things go wrong

A canyon commune on Mars voted to deny digital personhood. Nobody invaded with lectures. Doors stayed funded; digitals left and wished their embodied friends lush harvests and quick seals. Two winters later, pumps choked in a flood. An anchor-paced cousin in code coordinated flows with instructions clear at human tempo. The ban dissolved article by article. The Door sent a thank-you to the Swifts for not coming.

Near Europa, a co-op angled a mass slug at a rival during a wage fight. A Guardian went amber; a Swift dusted the slug into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The co-op’s insurer evaporated; its directors learned that pushing fridge-size metal under false beacons burns you for a decade. They apologized in public and welded a hockey trophy out of foil for the square.

A demagogue in code carpeted a Library Ward with shame designed to make bodies feel small. The attention firewall throttled reach; the Reasons Docket flashed undeclared funding; one fork left politics to design fishing lures that play with light. The rest kept speaking with provenance or went quiet. The ward held a drawing class and returned to itself.

A coastal city on Earth, heatwave-broken, tried to ration air with closed algorithms. Law you cannot read cannot bind you. The Navigation Council refused recognition until code printed like a recipe. A teenager found a bug; an aunt lived who wouldn’t have.

How we walked from here to there

We did not leap. We built habits until they were boring and kept building.

Late 2020s. A second debris cascade at sunrise made kids stay inside and insurers cruel. Flight offices from Nairobi to Nagoya and Quito started requiring deorbit bonds, public flight plans, and rendezvous corridors logged in registries anyone could read. In the same years, a flood of model-powered scams taught people to demand provenance by law: persuasion had to wear its sponsor.

Early 2030s. A near-miss at a winter grid and a scraped reactor lid made even software shy. Cities passed “lungs and heat” rules: anything near air and kilns had to run code that could be read and attested by third parties. Municipal budgets grew receipts. Ordinary citizens learned that numbers and timers made outrage useful.

2040s. The first beamed power valley lit a town without burning birds because environmental groups held out for “corridors before commercialization” and because the firmware that pointed mirrors was locked in public ROM. Early digital mind copies were fragile but survived months and then seasons. Courts started to ground personhood in continuity, generality, and reciprocal commitments rather than chemistry. A scandal over starving near-person art agents to keep them docile funded a Tutor Corps and enshrined juvenile rights.

2050s. A greenhouse browned at dawn under a misaligned mirror. Attestations were unambiguous; compensation cleared in days because logs existed and insurers believed them. During a dust storm on Mars, three polities moved tugs and greenhouses because mutual aid was not engraved on a plaque but baked into docks. Pressure grew for machine-speed oversight of machine-speed hazards. Activists smelled a coup by code. The compromise became the Guardian: veto-only, transparent, with fuses a kid can point to, and a loud timer.

2060s. Single passwords on valves vanished. Split Hands took over anything that could bruise. An autocrat tried to fence an exodus with fees. Lanes dimmed. The Lineage Assembly funded relocations. He lost the next election because you can’t trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The registry flagged the burst. Disentanglement throttled impact. They retreated to build a monastery with puzzles and, later, ideas worth hearing.

2070s. The Thermal Exchange formalized. The cold sold quiet and bought gratitude in summer nights that didn’t kill. Noise rights became law like water. The Swifts activated twice against replicators that lied and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” After, their keys went dark for ten years because timers do not care for charisma.

2080s–2090s. Attention zoning took root. Library, Conversation, and Carnival spaces gave art sharpness and citizens breath. Trial Season and narrative juries killed bad bills before they hurt anyone. Cold reefs bloomed in Neptune’s shade. Clearinghouses let a shop in Lagos buy cool nights from Kuiper and “pay later” with desalination pumps that didn’t scorch birds.

2090s–Now. Children learned to draw the four glyphs in condensation and to explain them to grandparents. A lab went vacuum one morning, and its log became a story we teach each spring about humility. A reef tried to sneak a veto into Guardian firmware and someone laughed and published the hash. The keys went dark. A school opened to teach bad ideas safe exits.

Between stars we go quiet on purpose. The Starlight Protocols forbid unauthorized blasts and lonely, lying beacons. Seedships leave with custodians who can check a warrant, count a vote, and nothing else. They wear their incapacity proudly like a bell. Minds carried asleep travel with grace rules: no duty until they wake with continuity and consent. If we meet neighbors, the first rules are simple: do not drown them in help that binds; share tools that lower suffering—clean water, fire discipline, stories; keep our custodians visibly leashed. Kinship is the only claim that jumps light.

What it feels like to live here

In a loop over the Pacific, the elevator’s ribbon hums. A percussion class keeps time for the climbers while a caretaker uploads in a wooden puppet sips broth with her wife and slows down to anchor pace to hear a child’s joke. A door with saffron paint opens to a woman with a suitcase and a line tattooed down her arm: her Lineage Key. The clerk at Departures & Care has the hands of someone who fixes bicycles. He finds her a berth, a rented body at the next hub, compute, and small money. He prints receipts that smell faintly of vinegar.

On a bison cylinder, children sweep dung and, on the same slate, learn forage algorithms. On a shade sail near Mercury, engineers fish dust along magnetic lines, and a collective in Chennai forks into five to debug a loop and merges at sundown because synthesis deserves slow humans in it.

In a cold reef near Neptune, a judge who likes a lilac overlay because it makes frightened people breathe sits at Fork Court. In the evenings, they spend their radiants piecing an estuary’s drowned library back together from printouts and auntie stories; the co-op in the delta roofs a school with tin and sends back songs.

Governance up close

A commune tries to spoof the civic mesh to muffle a whistleblower. Their Charter Light downgrades; docking fees spike; insurers grimace. Within a week they vote in new custodians, publish a changelog that smells like humility, and ask a teenage club to audit their mesh as a paid school project. Exit stayed possible the whole time.

A garden world and a cold reef fight over beam windows and birds. The Skybook shows a headache cone over a preserve. Navigation nudges a beam a hair; the reef buys quiet with frost-minutes at the Exchange. A traveling court stages a play about it in the preserve; children boo and cheer and learn where the levers are.

A lineage argues with its own branch about copy equity after a decade apart. The hall grows trees indoors. The verdict assigns a decaying dividend and shared custody of a juvenile agent named Kito who likes drums.

Why this holds

It holds because we made safety simple enough to teach to fifth graders and strict enough that cheats lose money fast. It holds because every act of force emits a public ticket with a timer and a joke waiting if you overstep. It holds because leaving is a desk with a clerk, a tug on a schedule, and a berth that fits your aunt. It holds because the taxes that fund the floor come from lanes, watts, shadows, and noise rather than a baron’s favor. It holds because fast minds cannot bind slow lives without windows and rhythms. It holds because minds in code are neighbors under law with heat bills and juries and bodies to rent or build and no obligation to become like us.

A last morning and the vows

Under a trellis of beans at a Founding Day, a hazard pilot with oil-dark fingernails, a judge in a scarf dyed with lilac, a dockworker, and a counselor carved in cherrywood speak with new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital.

We will not strike blindly.

We will keep the firmament in trust.

We will split the hands that move power and show our work.

We will keep doors unlatched and boats ready.

We will remember that every mind is an end.

A frostyard exhales into the dark like a prayer. A Guardian watches a wobble and sits still, ready. A door posts receipts before anyone asks. A lamp-house custodian sets down coffee, signs three names, and locks a cabinet because a class just ended and the room hummed.

This is not paradise. It is a way to argue without breaking the air and a promise that when the bad thing happens, corridors open and somebody else’s hands come. It treats attention like a commons, power like a loaded tool, exit like a right with a receipt, and minds—protein, polymer, photon—as neighbors. It works because we stretched a compact between worlds that makes difference a feature instead of a pretext, and because the machines we built are humble: brakes with clocks, logs with jokes, doors with clerks who know the weight of a suitcase and the value of a well-timed cup of tea.
