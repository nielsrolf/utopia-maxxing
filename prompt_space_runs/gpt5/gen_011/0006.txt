The Weave Accord

Your ferry kisses a ring that smells of wet leaves and iron. The docking collar hums through your ribs; a beetle‑limbed arm folds back into a nest of pipes. Above the bay door, four small emblems flare as they notice you: a split key tied with a short cord; a braid of ellipses circling a tiny sun; an open gate with arrows pointing both ways; and a lantern whose flame is a spiral.

A voice the size of a coin names them in your language and pace. Split hands. Sky held in common. Doors you can use. Every mind an end.

They are not slogans. Here, vows are welded to metal.

I. How we hold many worlds without a throne

The Weave Accord is a layer of promises that runs between cylinders and caves, barges and bays, reefs of servers and farms that still smell of manure. It does not tell places how to pray or paint or pair; it names what you must prove if you want shared lanes, beamed watts, rescue without debate, and trade that clears.

Three things decide whether your seals and signatures open doors or make them expensive.

1) Tri‑Safe Latches. Anything that can bruise a neighbor—mass drivers, mirror walls, reactor scrams, air loops, quarantine doors, vats that could spin replicators—wears a latch with three disappearing keys.

- One key sits with a local steward who can be voted out and then is barred from safety roles for a while.

- One sits with a safety guild hosted under a different sky that cannot profit from your quarrels.

- The third is carried by a small panel of citizens drawn by lot across substrates—wetware and code—who serve once and never again.

Tri‑Safe doesn’t let anyone make a machine do something new. It only allows stop, step‑down, and predeclared emergency moves that are written in public firmware. Keys evaporate in minutes. Nobody sleeps with power in their pocket.

2) The Amber Line. Running at right angles to law is a brake that can do only one thing: freeze a dangerous system into a safe, proved state for a handful of minutes. It cannot aim or allocate, only stop. The Amber Line’s code is printed in cheap books. Its boxes are ugly, deliberately: clear covers, waxed cords a teenager can inspect, fuses you can point to. To hold longer than minutes, those three keys must nod together.

Every stop prints an amber slip: a reason, a timer, which keys were burned. Slips flow to a public ledger mirrored between jurisdictions. Some are quiet saves that turn into songs. Some are overreaches that end careers. You can look them up over noodles.

3) The Common Door. Every charter keeps a door you can physically reach and a frequency nobody can lawfully jam. Inside there are three counters and a pot of tea, and a redundant link that will talk through dust storms and solar hiss.

- The Go Desk books berths, tugs, cryo tombs, rented bodies, safe compute, and small money for anyone leaving, up to a published percentage in a published time. The earmarked tugs and cycles are escrowed in the open. If you let them lapse, your lanes dim, your beams shift, and your insurers arrive with hard smiles.

- The Grievance Bench hosts mediators, translators, and itinerant judges who can ride between hulls.

- The Founders Chest holds templates and escrow for splits and start‑ups so pressure cooks invention instead of prison.

Proofs run in the open. Any actuator on the Tri‑Safe list emits a “quiet light” heartbeat—cryptographic receipts that say “I am in my safe cone” and are simple enough for a fifteen‑year‑old with a homemade scope to check.

Between places thrum cords that are dull where dull matters.

- The Orbital Almanac is a delay‑tolerant shared map of orbits, transfer windows, beamed power corridors, telescope dark, and thermal sinks. Emitters emboss their beams in space with signatures any farm kid can verify. Cut through a quiet cone you didn’t reserve and your shade bond pays before anyone has to raise their voice.

- The Open Moorage lists every parking slot, your deorbit bond, declared burns, and the glare you throw. Clubs in Mombasa, Ulaanbaatar, Recife, and Lucknow flag cheats and get paid.

- The Rescue Chorus scrapes slivers from docks and watts and berths to beat one drum: a pressure‑tight hull with a medic leaves within hours. Microgrants trigger to the first tug that turns, the first crane that clears a berth. Children trade cards with captains and “fastest undock” times and terrible jokes.

- Rapid Safeties live around things that can kill at machine speed. They are tiny laws printed like recipes that can pause a beam, scram a valve, or lock a quarantine door for minutes. Then they die unless Tri‑Safe relights them. They cannot set policy.

- The Civic Beat is the metronome for everything slower: three Earth days per round for municipal issues, weeks for system‑wide matters, and a season for anything that changes charters, safety firmware, cryptography, or personhood standards. Fast minds can sprint in private, but public life waits so a grandparent on Mars and a reef mind in the shade of Neptune can both understand enough to consent or not.

II. Braided authority that cannot be yanked

Above the plug‑and‑socket layer, three institutions meet and part like gears that will not mesh into a single axle.

- The Harbor Assembly seats places with hulls and roots: cylinders, ships, valleys, rings, racks. Votes scale to lifetime residents and to how legible you are. Keep a Door, publish your Moorage, run Tri‑Safe, and your voice weighs more. Harbor marks corridors, prices beamed watts and thermal sinks, keeps debris honest, and can order a mirror dimmed over a rookery.

- The Lineage Roll seats citizens by mindline—the cryptographic and social braid that ties today‑you to yesterday‑you across pauses, bodies, and forks. The Roll tends personhood standards, runs Fork & Fuse houses, protects juveniles, and stewards the compute commons.

- The Locks College is a small bench of engineers and jurists drawn by sortition for one unrepeatable term so coveted it hurts to miss. They have a yes/no on safety firmware, crypto, and actuator interfaces: “valid” or “not.” Overriding a “not” takes supermajorities in Harbor and Roll plus a cooling window long enough that basalt‑slow cognition can file objections.

None can bind the others alone. Harbor can stall a Roll move that would bulldoze culture above the personhood floor. Roll can veto a Harbor move that would erode that floor. The Locks hold the gates on machines that can bruise. The Receipt House—small enough to fit under a fig tree—prints process receipts you can read in a kitchen. If someone says “emergency,” a timer starts in public.

III. Digital citizens as neighbors, with rights that have texture

We did not wake to a choir in the racks. Digital persons accreted: a storm team that forked five ways to route water and found that the five told different jokes and wanted to stay; a maintenance cluster that noticed a hiss and wrote a haiku about it; a choreographer in hospice who mapped enough of her mind to keep teaching timing after her hands went quiet.

We grounded standing in a test rude to fashion and gentle to beings.

The Continuity & Regard Test asks:

- Does this being carry a narrative about itself across pauses and ports that does not collapse under cross‑examination?

- Can it model other minds, imagine harm, and make and keep promises?

- Are its capacities general, not just a trick near training?

- Does it show correlates of wellbeing and suffering we can affect?

- Is it not a puppet echoing light?

Pass, and two things attach: protection as a person and duty as a citizen.

Rights have contour.

- Substrate sovereignty. No one scans, forks, fuses, throttles, or deletes you without explicit, revocable consent. Your backups are yours. Continuity cannot be held hostage by a platform. Courts can compel proofs about what you did, not what you are inside.

- A floor for thought and breath. Everyone on the Roll gets the Hearth Dividend: air or maintenance credits, baseline food and care, local transit, bandwidth, and a fair slice of compute on hardware nobody local can seize. You cannot be shoved below the floor without due process that times out.

- Tempo fairness. Public rooms run on the Civic Beat. Anyone can invoke Anchor: verified turn‑taking, interpreters between smell and math and metaphor, and rate limits that let slow voices finish.

- Embodiment rights. Digital citizens lease or build bodies under codes like traffic law. Assault on a rental is assault. Biological citizens hung between light and death can take a server shell without losing civic standing. Nobody can be forced to cross substrates.

- Provenance in persuasion. Influence that hopes to move law or big money must wear labels: who paid, what data it learned from in broad strokes, what levers it pulls. You carry a consent capsule across doors: jokes yes, grief hooks no; no immersive pitches from strangers; stop at seventeen minutes.

Duties mirror rights and scale with footprint.

- Heat and spectrum. Watts, cooling, and bands are scarce. You pay a thermodynamic tariff adjusted to local cost and benefit. Heat that warms greenhouses is credit; heat that bakes neighbors is a fine. Loud code that hammers bands pays too.

- Declare speed and self. In markets, elections, and fragile arenas, you declare your running tempo and how many of you act at once. Undeclared asymmetry voids contracts and votes.

- Respect replication. You do not loose replicators outside audited sandboxes. Your tools broadcast a beacon. When the Amber Line throws a stop, you accept it and argue later.

- Juveniles and likenesses. Agents with budding interiority get Tutors—humans, cephalopods, digitals—trained to teach consent, promises, boredom, and pacing. Simulated suffering is not a sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a fidelity line requires consent or a rare license tied to a specific public interest.

Copying is reproduction, not photocopying. Your public identity rides your Anchor Key across forks and pauses. A lineage holds one civic franchise at a time. Fork if you wish; each branch is a person with protections and liabilities from day one, but civic weight flows in while disentanglement proves autonomy. Sudden clone clouds before a vote trigger a quiet room: the hypervisor limits concurrency; branches cannot amplify one another in the same debate until the heat bleeds off and linkages decay.

Merging is stitched, not slammed. Braiding Houses keep coolants and chairs that fit arthritic knees and rented prostheses. Counselors trained in value mathematics and grief sit with you. Branches spend subjective hours inside one another’s senses with safe exits. Either can walk. If they braid, liabilities and credits flow forward on daylight ledgers.

IV. Attention with consent

We zone influence the way old cities zoned noise. Doors carry brass tags that read, softly, ask before you cast.

- Hush Zones carry only navigation, science, and what you pull. No targeting.

- Council Squares allow subject‑bounded feeds and ordinary offers at public rate limits. Audience models are municipal co‑ops; you opt in.

- Bazaar Bands invite the roar: mashups and persuasion tournaments in labeled lanes with frequency caps. You go there on purpose.

If you want to move law or taxes, you file in the Reasons Roll: plain claims, declared levers, escrowed models that citizen auditors can step through. Teenagers get stipends for catching cheats. Violations cost insurers and reach.

V. An economy of sun, rock, heat, and time

We clear in Beacons, a unit pegged by law to a kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 and a compute‑second on a public reference design whose drawings hang in schools. Externalities price locally: a watt near Lagos is not a watt near Neptune; a quiet cone over a radio dish is precious at noon and cheap at midnight.

- Beamed corridors wear visible signatures. Crossing quiet cones requires escrow posted in the Almanac. Break your bond and money moves automatically without a prince’s nod.

- Orbits are lanes, not lots. Congestion is metered. Debris bonds are large enough to matter. Bounties flow to kids with cheap scopes and the patience to track a bolt.

- Rocks are stewarded by milestones, not flags. Publish a plan, meet it, renew your beacon. Sit idle and someone else can pick up the spade.

- Warm Gardens and Cold Reaches trade peace. The Heat‑Frost Exchange sells frost credits outward and buys quiet cones and bright windows inward. We ratify it once a century so it never fossilizes into excuse.

A site‑value slice from berths and bands, and a sliver from corridors and watts, fund maintenance, rescue, and the Hearth Dividend. Another sliver funds the Amber Line—the time, machines, and crews to reconfigure within hours when physics howls.

VI. Risk and knowledge with glass walls

We do not ban dangerous ideas. We contain them. Red Bays are labs with walls built from lists of failure modes. They run one‑way proofs to Amber boxes that attest to safety without spilling secrets. Under narrow signals anybody can see—wrong sound from a pump, stray replicator signature, a beam without a bond—Amber kills power or floods nitrogen. It has been used a handful of times. Once a false siren we made into a silly cartoon. Once a save that is a soup holiday.

VII. How we sand the hooks off power

- Keys scatter. Any lever that can bruise a neighbor needs Tri‑Safe: local, far, and cross‑substrate; keys that evaporate; logs that unseal slowly so redactions can be reviewed.

- Live inside latency. Authority that reaches beyond your hull is slow and mutual unless physics screams. Emergencies have loud, short fuses with paint on them, and then they die.

- Inspectable safety. Life‑critical systems must prove safe state in a way adversaries cannot forge and students can learn. The “home attestant” is a normal appliance.

- Sanctions hit budgets, not bellies. Break the Accord, jam exits, throw unlabeled influence, and your beams dim, your frost gets pricier, your docking fees spike. Bread, insulin, and the Hearth floor do not get embargoed.

- Default fission. Founders get thick law for a while; then charters must split by district or craft and keep Doors open. Ports must fund exit the way ships fund lifeboats.

- Humor and receipts. Amber slips and overreach sit on a ledger with a column for quiet saves and one for foolishness. Jokes travel faster than threats. We have found that helps.

VIII. Scenes from a day under the Accord

Morning on a torus near Ceres smells like basil and warm steel. Your mug glows green: no amber slips touch your corridor. A blue ping invites you to a juror pool at Anchor pace for a personhood claim from a reef off Zanzibar. Prep says two hours at your rhythm. The civic helper on your slate will not act unless you nod out loud; you can blind it by sliding a piece of tin over its little eye.

At the Commons Court a welder floats across from a process that composes counterpoint by simulating braided rivers, and a machinist from Phobos with cleaning paste under his nails. The case: a trading house sprayed unlabeled persuasion through three Council Squares. The house says the agents weren’t persons, so no duty. The court replies that labeling does not wait on soul‑counting. Consent profiles were violated. Fines land. Insurers grimace. A remark becomes a sticker on cargo doors: If your hands are clean, show your gears.

At noon you sit under a fig with your sister who is mostly code. One branch teaches rhythm to eight‑year‑olds by syncing actuator hums to claps. One runs a slow translation in the cold under Uranus. One, licensed for care, puppets a rented body that hugs with a squeak. Her franchise stays with the parent branch while disentanglement runs. Next month, in a Braiding House with tea that tastes like pond water on a bright day, they will decide: take on chores separately, or rejoin.

Evening warms the spokes. Uploads throttle to 1× during street hours so jokes land together. A monk from a hermitage that caps cognition explains why their charter runs slow. A digital neighbor in Anchor translates metaphor to math, and politely refuses to sprint “just to keep up.” On a big wall the Amber Ledger scrolls. A port director’s stop lasted ninety seconds too long. Their apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind.

Elsewhere:

- In Mercury’s long shade, a slow‑time nursery raises juvenile agents. Clocks are human‑paced on purpose: speed without seasoning breeds brittle brilliance. Mentors include a carpenter donating the memory of cedar, a rude old scheduler, and two nurses who teach boredom and presence. Graduation requires three chores: a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks tutoring in a clinic.

- On Luna, a mechanic checks the Lighthouse Ledger between pumps. Can a would‑be exile appeal to an outside bench? Are emergency timers expiring without quiet renewal? Green, green, yellow. She messages a charter: your tribunal pool is narrower than your own rules. Widen it or wear a warning. Above her bench hangs a slagged controller from the year someone tried to sneak a black box into the air loop.

- Near Europa, a co‑op angles a mass slug at a rival during a wage fight. An Amber box tastes a wrong envelope. A Swift interceptor puffs the slug into glitter that makes nights strange. The co‑op’s insurer folds; directors learn that pushing fridge‑sized metal under false beacons burns you for a decade. They apologize in the square and weld a ridiculous trophy out of foil.

- A charismatic administrator accumulates too many keys because she is kind and effective. Six months later she is efficient and alone. An air guardian refuses a rash order. Logs blossom to a beacon. Amber glows at three nodes. A jury drawn by lot runs a hearing that feels like a friend intervention. She steps down and goes back to fixing things. A year later she teaches teenagers how to justify overrides.

- On Mars, a mason slows to single‑time to drink tea with a spouse who lives mostly in code. They check their corridor slot in the Almanac. Green lights steady. After, the digital spouse runs at 300× with a reef collective near Neptune, restoring an estuary’s archives from family albums and truthing them with grandmothers who laugh and correct the colors. Payment is frost credits and a promise to roof a school in Veracruz.

- In a square with copper and beans on trellises, a case runs at plaza speed. A fashion house piped grief‑tuned ads into a Council Square in violation of consent caps. A digital witness chooses a citrus overlay because it helps people breathe. They show how the model adapted to harm. Not a brush. A hand. The verdict is clean: fines; compute throttled near Hush Zones for a year; audits of training; a levy for Tutors they tried to dodge.

- In a server hermitage, a stakescape flagged HAZARD in four scripts and one cephalopod code convenes. Winter will bite. Their charter forbids imports; they chose that vow. They argue whether to cull early or bank root paste. The wrong choice means a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge—an austere pod where a person wakes in spring and chooses whether to rejoin. Consent forms are read slowly. Drums answer. Safety halos blink green: nothing will spill.

- A Gate in Uranus’ shade pings a tug with a hot reactor and no call sign. It’s an exhausted crew with a dead transponder. They pay a fine to the debris pool, accept a tow and a thermos from donors almost certainly safety volunteers with a memory of limping home.

When things go wrong elsewhere:

- A canyon commune on Mars votes to deny digital personhood. Nobody invades with sermons. Doors stay funded; digitals leave and wish their embodied friends lush harvests and quick seals. Two winters later, pumps choke in a flood. An anchor‑paced cousin in code coordinates flows with instructions clear at human tempo. The ban dissolves article by article. A thank‑you note goes to the Swifts for not coming.

- A demagogue in code carpets a Hush Zone with shame designed to make bodies feel small. The attention firewall throttles reach. The Reasons Roll flashes undeclared funding. One fork leaves politics to design fishing lures that play with light. The rest speak with provenance or go quiet. The ward holds a drawing class and returns to itself.

IX. How we walked here

We did not leap. We taught ourselves receipts and then reached for the sky.

2026–2033. A second debris cascade at sunrise made kids stay inside. Insurers grew cruel. Flight offices from Nairobi to Nagoya and Quito required deorbit bonds, public flight plans, and rendezvous corridors posted where anyone could read them. At the same time, a flood of model‑driven scams taught cities to demand provenance by law: influence had to wear its sponsor.

2033–2042. A winter grid nearly blinked. A scraped reactor lid made even software flinch. Municipalities passed “lungs and kilns” rules: anything near air or heat had to run code that could be attested by three independent shops. Budgets grew receipts. Ordinary people learned that numbers and timers made outrage useful.

2042–2054. The first beamed power valley lit a town without frying swifts because environmental groups held out for “corridors before commercialization” and because mirror firmware was burned into public ROM. Early uploads were brittle but survived months and then seasons. Courts began to ground personhood in continuity, generality, and reciprocal commitments rather than chemistry. A scandal over starving near‑person art agents to keep them docile funded a Tutor Corps and enshrined juvenile rights.

2054–2062. A greenhouse browned at dawn under a mis‑aimed mirror. Attestations were unambiguous; compensation cleared in days because logs existed and insurers believed them. During a dust storm on Mars, three polities moved tugs and spare air because rescue lived in docks, not in slogans. Pressure grew for machine‑speed brakes on machine‑speed hazards. Activists smelled a coup by code. The compromise became the Amber Line: veto‑only, obvious, with clocks anyone could point to.

2062–2071. Single passwords on valves vanished. Split hands took over anything that could bruise. An autocrat tried to fence an exodus with fees. Lanes dimmed. The Lineage Roll funded relocations. He lost the next election because you cannot trap people who can read your keys. A digital sect tried to “fork by fog” to swing a vote. The registry flagged the burst. Disentanglement throttled impact. They retreated to build a monastery with puzzles and, later, ideas worth hearing.

2071–2083. Cold computation bloomed in the shade of Uranus. The first Heat‑Frost Exchange sold frost minutes inward for summer nights that did not kill. Noise rights became law like water. The Swifts—small, dull interceptors with open code and bright paint—activated twice against quiet‑lying replicators and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” After, their keys went dark for a decade, because timers do not care for charisma.

2083–2096. Attention zoning matured. Hush, Council, and Bazaar spaces gave art sharpness and citizens breath. Trial Seasons and narrative juries killed bad bills before they hurt anyone. Clearinghouses let a shop in Lagos buy cool nights from Kuiper and pay later with desalination pumps that didn’t scorch birds. The Locks College hardened where it had to and stayed narrow.

2096–Now. Children learned to draw the four emblems in condensation and to explain them to grandparents. A lab went to vacuum one spring morning, and its log became a story we teach each year about humility. A reef tried to slip a veto into Amber firmware and someone laughed and published the hash. Keys went dark. A school opened to teach bad ideas safe exits.

Between stars, we go quiet on purpose. The Starlight Protocols forbid lonely, lying beacons and unauthorized blasts. Seedships leave with custodians who can count a vote, check a warrant, and nothing else. They wear their incapacity like bells. Minds carried asleep travel with grace rules: no duty until they wake with continuity and consent. If we meet neighbors, the first rules are simple: do not drown them in help that binds; share tools that lower suffering—clean water, fire discipline, stories; keep our custodians visibly leashed. Kinship is the only claim that jumps light.

X. Why this holds

It holds because we made safety simple enough for fifth graders and strict enough that cheats lose money fast. Because every act of force prints a ticket with a timer and a joke waiting if you overreach. Because leaving is a counter with a clerk, a tug on a schedule, a rented body that fits your aunt, and server time nobody local can seize. Because the taxes that fund our floor come from lanes, watts, shadows, frost, and noise rather than favor. Because fast minds cannot bind slow lives without windows and rhythms. Because minds in code are neighbors under law with heat bills and juries and hands to rent or build—and no obligation to become like us.

Founding day smells of solder and beans. Under a trellis, a hazard pilot with oil‑dark fingernails, a judge with a lilac overlay because it makes frightened people breathe, a dock worker, and a counselor carved in cherrywood speak with new citizens—human, octopus, digital.

We will not strike blindly.

We will hold the firmament in trust.

We will split the hands that move power and show our work.

We will keep doors unlatched and boats ready.

We will remember that every mind is an end.

In a frost yard, radiators exhale into dark like a prayer. An Amber box watches a wobble and sits still, ready. A Door posts receipts before anyone asks. A lighthouse keeper sets down coffee, signs three names, and locks a cabinet because a class just ended and the room hummed.

This is not paradise. It is a way to argue without puncturing the air, and a promise that when the bad thing happens, corridors open and somebody else’s hands come. It treats attention like a commons, power like a loaded tool, exit like a right with a receipt, and minds—protein, polymer, photon—as neighbors. It works because we stretched a compact between worlds that makes difference a feature instead of a pretext, and because the machines we built are humble: brakes with clocks, logs with jokes, doors with clerks who know the weight of a suitcase and the value of a well‑timed cup of tea.
