The Constellation Compact

When the ferry drifts under the lattice of a radiator fin and the valley inside the cylinder tips into morning, nothing looks like government. Children are already racing along a canal that banks into a meadow of citrus and windbreaks. A tug idles with its clamps folded like a beetle. Above the lock, an etched plate speaks in the plainest words the Compact ever found:

No thrones. No famine. No cages. Aid is owed. Departure is yours. Minds are ends, not means.

It isn’t a slogan. It’s plumbing. The Compact does not assemble a capital to throw orders across hours of light. It binds the worlds with tools and habits that anyone can inspect, so that the fear of one valley never becomes the policy of all of them.

Latency made into law

Two facts set the bones of the era. Light is slow, and it does not care about our impatience. And making a mind faster does not make it kinder. Authority that tries to reach too far arrives stale, and power that can think at two hundred times human tempo can outpace human dignity if we let it.

The Compact is dull about this in the way good rigs are dull. It pushes decisions as close to life as possible. It stretches only the rails that physics forces us to share—collision rules, beam etiquette, distress signals, the way we talk about personhood—and it turns dangerous levers into devices that refuse to obey a single will.

Three cords through every hatch

Walk through any registered hatch—from soy domes in Tierra del Fuego to a data reef in Neptunian night—and you see three narrow braids along the wall. They look like art. They are budgets and covenants you can touch with a public key.

- The Green Cord is the Open Path. It lists exits you can actually use today, the berths and bandwidth you can claim without a patron, and the neutral stewards who will check the path is real. It is tied to physical things: spare suits, tug allotments, a shelf of seed kits, and a compute voucher for anyone recognized as a person regardless of substrate. If you must leave, the Green Cord lights a way you can afford.

- The Amber Cord is for Splits and Starts. It guides forking, founding, and branch births: how much heat you leased, what hardware can hold your mind without someone’s hidden toggle, which human attention you have promised and been promised, how rights and duties flow along a lineage. It is the difference between a clone and a child, a secession and a tantrum.

- The Blue Cord spans jurisdictions. It shines only where worlds have posted their charters in machine-checkable light and agreed to answer “why?” with runnable models or readable reasons. It maps transit lanes, collision cones, and spectrum hours, and it says which courts will hear you when you complain that a beam burned the wrong backyard.

None of it says “trust us.” It points to machines that can be checked and to procedures that ring like ship-bells when they are struck.

Charters you can read like wiring diagrams

Every polity—orbital ring, Martian valley, free-flyer caravan, virtual archipelago—broadcasts a live constitution called a Charter Beam. It must specify three namespaces:

- Force. What instruments here can coerce? Grapple tugs that can turn a station, police drones that can immobilize, beam taps that can blind. The Charter lists them and the keys that open their drawers.

- Decision. Who decides, and how do they leave? What quits the room and to whom do they answer? What is the tempo at which things bind?

- Exit. Where is the door? How does a biological person leave with skin and name and enough kit to start again? How does a digital person leave with continuity and compute?

Charters don’t need to look alike. A haven that caps augmentation and keeps a curfew can stand beside a forge where uploads bargain in joules and milliseconds. What they must share is legibility, exits, and the disfavor of neighbors if they weld the door.

Corridors with physics behind them

Between charters run agreements backed by hardware and proof.

- Safety corridors. Anything that can make a desert—drivers, antimatter casks, high-gain beams—refuses to ramp alone. Turning them up requires three signatures on three racks: a local assembly, a guild peer from away, and a remote quorum drawn by lottery across places and substrates. Devices publish zero-knowledge attestations that their outputs match allowed profiles. You can see a reactor’s control code on a school terminal. A child can tick off its poison rod positions like a recipe.

- Movement and markets. Innocent passage is the default. Docking, tugging, power purchase, radiator time, and throat-clearing in collision corridors run through standard contracts and a public ephemeris. Cargo manifests carry a “life-critical” flag that flips hardware into Rescue Mode. Rates float and clear by auction, but nobody “owns” a lane in perpetuity because you cannot own a place that can kill your neighbor’s Tuesday.

- Mutual aid. Every polity keeps an interoperable rescue kit sized to its risks: a tug that can grapple any standard, an ambulance module that can run someone else’s tubes, crew on a clock scaled to local day. Fail to launch without a reason that persuades your peers and your case opens in public for a decade.

- Personhood and proof. A substrate-neutral registry maintains the Mindline: cryptographic continuity anchors renewed on a schedule and stored across archives that do not trust each other. Embodied citizens update with guardians early on and themselves later; digital citizens link to hardware attestations of the runtime they rely on. The Mindline is proof of self without exhibition of memory.

A Sentinel Net with one gesture

A deliberately narrow scaffold called the Sentinel Net sits under the beams and lanes. It can do very few things, and it does them watched.

- It can stop but not compel. The Sentinel can quench a beam, drop a driver to safe charge, or lock a vault when the proofs that allow them fail. It cannot turn anything up. Its amber stops time out unless independent jurors—biological and digital, sampled across distance and tempo—concur in red.

- It is inspectable by hobbyists. The Net runs on open silicon with microcode published and hash-locked to measurements that labs and unions can reproduce. Its inputs and outputs are narrow enough that a high school can test them with pocket attestators.

- Its keys are sharded. Halting, updating, or overriding the Net requires threshold signatures from far-flung shards: a Terran tribunal, a Belt commune, a Jovian server-hollow, an orbital convent, a freefall union. It cannot tax. It cannot legislate. It logs what it does in a narrative humans can read and proofs machines can check.

Braided authority instead of a pyramid

Beyond the Sentinel, two assemblies and a brake keep each other from swaying too far.

- The Assembly of Places tallies where. Seats track lifetime residents on each habitat, planet, station, and recognized virtual polity with jurisdiction over compute. They schedule corridors, credential Safety Guilds, and set hull codes.

- The Assembly of Persons tallies who. Seats track registered persons—cellular and digital—weighted by civic anchors tied to continuity, not copy count. The Assembly holds personhood law, public compute, and the Mindline registry.

- The Limits Board is small, rotating, and allergic to power. Reactor hands, cryptographers, and process theorists drawn by lot serve short terms to validate changes to cryptographic and physical backbones. They cannot legislate. They can only certify valid or not. Overriding them requires simultaneous supermajorities in both Assemblies plus a cooling period measured in a “civic tick” multiplied enough that the slowest registered minds can read and reply.

Either Assembly can veto an overreach by the other within its house: Places can block a move that would bulldoze a hull’s culture; Persons can block an attempt to water down the Charter of Minds. The Sentinel doesn’t pick sides; it keeps the beams honest while we argue.

Markets with receipts, not dukes

Anything someone else could need and you could sit on out of spite is leased, not owned. Five markets settle continuously, governed by the Heat Ledger, a record physics helps police.

- Ground Shares. Cave mouths, crater rims, river deltas, nickel seams, windows where sunlight doesn’t burn curtains.

- Lane Shares. Orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Band Shares. Spectrum allocations and whisper bands in caves and storms.

- Core Shares. Compute time pinned to hardware identity and verified heat sinks.

- Simspace Shares. Sealed worlds that run on paid Core Shares and label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A universal floor arrives as a dignity dividend—air where relevant, medical care, baseline bandwidth, and a Public Shell of compute cycles so the poorest person’s counsel bot can explain a warrant or a will. A local share rewards those who keep the lease valuable: the tug that keeps your arc clear, the algae that keeps your street cool, the cold lattice that keeps your reef honest.

The Helios, a composite unit measured in standardized kilowatt-hours at a reference orbit plus benchmarked compute-seconds on a reference architecture, clears interplanetary accounts through a neutral clearing union. Helios decay gently over time to discourage hoarding. Externalities price in the causal cone: if your exhaust will sicken my algae in six days, your escrow pays today.

Receipts that talk

The Ephemeris Exchange, a guild of pilots, schedulers, and judges, allocates lanes and bands and radiator windows and publishes Runnable Orders for every decision: minimal-model simulations any two independent audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc N-5 for four hours because burns at Eros would have pushed your berth into a radio shadow” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum a teenager can verify at a library terminal.

Harbor law and the right to leave

Every mass you push into the sky carries a signed end-of-life plan and escrowed resources to implement it. Plans must simulate to an agreed envelope. If your bolt’s arc cripples my transceiver array, liability follows your manifest without weasel words. Debris courts sit inside your light cone, read sensor data fast, and punish lies when the sky itself exposes them.

Every polity funds exit as infrastructure, not favor. Hardware exists: rings of rated airlocks, spare berths for emigrants, neutral embassies in atoms and code. Budgets exist: seed kits on shelves, tug time reserved, Core Shares escrowed for Splits. Procedure exists: Blue Writs—human-readable warrants with machine-checkable proofs—that must accompany any act that seizes assets, impounds persons, or delays departure. Writs expire unless renewed by panels drawn across distance and tempo.

If a charter tries to close its doors with steel or deceit, neighbors don’t ship sermons or soldiers. They tighten three rings:

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence and repair maps with dates. “Unlock these doors by the 12th. Hire exit tutors. Return leases to market. Here are the seals we will honor on arrival.”

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane priority falls. Band allocations drop to silence. Insurers raise premiums until boredom and price do more than speeches.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute flows to defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel paid from a boring, proud fund. The offender’s people leave with skin and mind intact.

Digital minds as neighbors with standing

Recognition does not worship hardware. A person—or an advocate—claims standing and submits to mixed-panel audits looking for:

- Coherent preference across contexts and time.

- A working model of self and others that supports reciprocal commitments.

- The capacity to give reasons that generalize beyond training data.

- Signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter morally.

- Absence of deceptive overfitting that mimics personhood without inner life.

Panels include human psychologists, digital citizens, meta-debaters, and routines evolved to detect vacuity. When in doubt about imposing duties, the Compact pauses; when in doubt about protecting sentience, it errs on the side of care. Provisional protections come first; duties attach when capability and risk do.

Rights that bite and duties that scale

Once recognized, rights and responsibilities attach to capability and footprint.

Rights include:

- Integrity. No deletion or coercive edit without consent or due process recorded against your Mindline.

- Sanctuary. The right to halt and be archived unharmed; to migrate to compatible substrates.

- Provenance. To know and audit the training history of your mind or appoint a fiduciary to do so.

- Association and exit. To join, fork, merge, and leave communities.

- Pace. To choose runtime, bounded by the civic tick in public forums and fair-share interface rules elsewhere.

- Bodily autonomy across substrates. The right to lease arms and eyes with standards of safety and consent; the right to a body free of covert throttles and remote erasure.

Duties scale with power:

- Harm-minimization at interfaces with others.

- Insurance pools that compensate errors at machine speed.

- Provenance logs for major external actions, encrypted to prove compliance without exposing private thought.

- Resource accounting for reproduction: forking spends branch credits. Children are not tax shelters.

The law makes this concrete with lineage accounts. Every person belongs to a lineage with quantitative claims on compute, energy, and matter. The claim grows with the commons, shrinks when the lineage reproduces. Forks draw from the same bucket until they negotiate new terms. Children inherit stewarded shares. Civic weight sits with the lineage at fork-birth and reassigns by contract as lives diverge. No one votes twice by copying themselves before lunch.

Copy equity keeps reproduction from becoming coercion. If you fork, the new self inherits enough to live without bargaining away dignity and owes a decaying dividend to the parent. Mass cloning to swamp a vote triggers disentanglement: your clonal cloud is sandboxed for civic purposes until entanglements decay or claims are negotiated. Merges require staged, symmetric consent recorded in both Mindlines and a right to unmerge during a cooling window.

Memory law respects the self over time. Deep edits require dual consent: from the you who wants to change and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right. Erasure is hard. Persuasion routines are regulated like drugs; turn them too high and you need a clinician’s note and a timer. Training on private diaries demands informed, revocable consent.

Juvenile agents—near-person processes that can suffer but not yet consent—live under guardianship like children: tutors, rights to call neutral embassies, and courts that err on sanctuary.

Tempo fairness and avatars

Public life runs on the civic tick—roughly three human days, with regulated markets, ballot windows, and court deadlines pinned to that beat. A mind may race at 300x to write reports or simulations, but binding happens at a pace the slow can keep.

Avatars, throttled projections any mind can use, are how fast or distant citizens attend assemblies. The law cares what the avatar did and why it said so. Penalties apply in lived time: if you trick a gardener at 1x while you live at 200x, your sentence ticks in your subjective life.

Guardrails where law meets engineering

Several patterns became ritual.

- Origin Seals and Quiet Codes. Any replicator—biological synth line, print farm, runaway polymerase—carries an Origin Seal, a signed beacon that declares the Maker and listens for Quiet Codes authorized by sortitioned juries to halt replication. Running without a seal is like towing a reactor without flags.

- Triple Keys. Any move that can erase a corridor or punch a crater or coerce a mind requires three signatures: resident assembly, bonded local officer, and remote auditor on independent hardware. Signature windows overlap only enough to work. Keys never share a rack.

- Influence budgets. Cognitive leverage is treated like radiation: tracked, dosed, bounded where it does harm. Individuals publish influence profiles—machine-enforceable settings for what kinds of persuasion are welcome, from whom, when. Political content must label speed, data advantages, and sponsors. Unlabeled persuasion is dumping toxins. Fines are strict-liability.

- Emergency sunsets. Solar storms and bio-spills switch charters to contingency constitutions that widen some powers for measured windows and then die on their own. The Sentinel may accelerate coordination for ninety days after a declared emergency, subject to challenge by any two Charters that present a feasible alternative.

Institutions with teeth and memories, not fangs

- Sky Wardens are the only cross-world force. Their narrow mandate: contain kinetic and replicative threats that cannot be handled locally. They fly uncrewed by law. Control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited annually by schools, unions, and hobby clubs. Every action requires threshold signatures from randomly drawn juries across polities and a human-readable justification appended to the Ledger of Cause—an archive of compulsion.

- The Circuit Bench is a fleet of gardened ships that carry judges, mediators, and ombuds. They hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity claims, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Their verdicts execute smart contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance, and, in last resort, lock hazardous devices. They stage plays about the cases they solved so law sticks to memory.

- The Synod of Standards meets on synodic calendars and rotates among fairs of orbit and quiet halls of outer stations. It tends shared commons—spectrum bands, debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that decay on their own unless renewed.

What it looks like where you can touch it

In Dock Yarrow along the Moon’s nightside, two visitors appear by the customs arch. One is inked in hatching that smudges when she nods; one is a brushed-metal outline with a blue ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints a strip under each: pace, lease entitlements, declared concurrency, guardian on file, hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for substrate. Guessing is rude and a good way to be wrong.

On the port wall, schedules unspool—burn slots, band windows, radiator shares, docking flowers—and a second column that would have baffled a 2020 port captain: Core Shares, Cold Minutes, reserved compute gardens. Yesterday’s Runnable Orders sit under glass you can smudge with a finger. A kid traces the plume from Eros and laughs at the footnote; the pilot who wrote it slipped in a joke about sock odors in freefall.

Outside Tranquillitatis, Ahmed teaches students to level nutrient flow. His Counsel—the only civic assistant funded from a pot no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure nudges seed deliveries. Later, Ahmed sits on a citizen jury for a charter amendment capping municipal Core Shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation shows his niece’s coop gaining two trainees if it passes. He votes yes and sends his niece a joke with a picture of a seed through an airlock: “Open Path compliant.”

Eight light-hours out, a historian at 200x leases a strip of cold reef to reconstruct a drowned coast’s oral tradition from crooked recordings and half-burned notebooks. He pays in reef credits earned by translating the tales into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but will still love a hero who makes a bad decision and fixes it.

On Phobos, in a Confluence House built like a teahouse with servers, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell in love with a drummer. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32x and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink. They try. Today they do not. The braider schedules another attempt and writes a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

When it goes wrong and how it mends

A clever manager on a cylinder above Quito gathered water, law, and defense because she was generous and efficient and made the trains run on time. Six months later she made midnight decrees. The life support Safeguard refused a risky optimization; logs mapped to the Ledger of Cause. An Ephemeris jury arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Green Cord lit. Many left. Some stayed. The manager lost two of her three keys and accepted a narrower role with humor. Her friends still tease her about trying monarchy.

In the high belt, a miner tried to sling a mass into a rival’s orbit in a wage fight. The Sky Wardens dusted the slug into aurora. The miner’s insurer folded and shed arrogant managers. Dockmasters tripled docking fees for any launch hiding an Origin Seal. A hockey team rebranded and sent profits to the tug cooperative that modeled the plume.

A proxy demagogue at 300x flooded a haven’s market with angry songs designed to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled reach; friends watched persuasion scores decay. The demagogue forked. One branch went fishing. One entered therapy. One made sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and commissioned a piece for the Feast of Questions.

From now to then, on rails you would recognize

This did not descend like scripture. It accreted like dock grime and court precedent.

2026–2032. A second cascade of debris in low orbit forced insurers, militaries, and launch outfits to agree on a global registry with timetables that bit. Environmental coalitions fought for “corridors before commercialization” around the first beamed power pilots. On Earth, scandal after scandal about opaque models turned provenance and explainability into law for systems that touched credit, health, and rights. Courts recognized narrow forms of digital agency in commerce—lab managers that could sign for deliveries, process guardians in clinical trials.

2033–2044. Beamed power smoothed brownouts in the Sahel. Lunar water cartels tried to form and were punished by contracts that delayed docking and hiked insurance. Multilateral accords grew a spine: noninterference and data sharing became auditable. The first Mindline standards emerged from hospitals and studios where stroke patients and performers wanted continuity without public exposure.

2045–2058. Compute farms bloomed on Mercury’s night rim. Citizens in participating nations got Public Shells—counsel bots that compressed arguments without twisting, legal aid, health agents that asked before acting. Early uploads were brittle but real: stroke survivors with prosthetic memory, artists with mirrored voices. Law extended personhood where continuity could be shown over months. A small ring at L5 spun up, failed beautifully, and then worked.

2059–2070. A misaligned mirror scorched crops in a new habitat. The array’s logs made liability unambiguous; compensation cleared in weeks; confidence survived because receipts did. A Martian settlement outlasted a dust storm longer than models predicted because mutual aid wasn’t a sticker: three polities diverted tugs and greenhouses, and the rescue stream became a holiday. Cases about juvenile agents—near-person models shackled to a patron’s feed—founded a Tutor Corps. Charters began to require exit hardware.

2071–2082. Operators begged for machine-speed verifiers. Activists smelled a putsch by code. The compromise was the Sentinel Net: narrow, veto-only overseers with limits you could test and actuators you could pull by hand. Insurers and rescue pools demanded Sentinel integration. Legitimacy followed premiums and a few very public saves.

2083–2095. The twin Assemblies and the Limits Board formalized. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under split keys did so with a million viewers watching telemetry and three signatures arrive like drumbeats. Exit rights bit muscle when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus; his lane slot was cut, the move funded by the Assembly of Persons, and he lost his next election. A digital sect tried to win a vote by forking into a clonal cloud; the registry flagged the burst; disentanglement throttled their impact; they left to build a monastery with tough games and soft beds.

By then the Compact stopped being the story. The story became the fights we didn’t have to have.

Texture of an ordinary day

Morning on Ceres. A botanist nails the last variable in a paper about lichens that metabolize nickel in doped regolith. She files data with open proofs, gets paid in corridor credits by a stewardship fund, and texts a picture of a moss that looks like stars. Her spouse, a digital ethnographer, slows to 1x for tea, then sprints to 200x in a Compute Garden to annotate a playfront where a simulated city invented a new arraignment ritual using children’s songs.

Midday in a Mesh Court. A welder floats at a hearing. To his left, a process that composes music by simulating rivers. To his right, a machinist from Phobos. The case: a trading house used unlabeled persuasion engines trained on grief diaries. The house says the agents weren’t persons. The court says personhood is irrelevant to labeling. Mindlines show no anchors; provenance logs catch the theft. The verdict: fines, suspension, mandatory migration to labeled, accountable models, and a yearly audit of the training pipeline. A juror’s line becomes a sticker on port doors: “If your hands are clean, where did the fog come from?”

Afternoon in a port. A ship declares “life-critical” and jumps every queue. Cranes swing because Rescue Mode flips relays; overtime credits start flowing before the tug undocks. A teenager on shift at the amber door watches clamps catch true and hums the mutual aid anthem without irony.

Evening in a Wild Zone. Winter looms. Import rules forbid out-of-zone food. The muster decides whether to cull herds or bank root paste. Get it wrong and a third of the village will “die” into the ancestor lodge for a season. Consent forms are read at 1x. Drums answer. The Sentinel blinks green at the perimeter: nothing can spill out. Inside, people play with stakes their grandparents didn’t get to choose.

Night in a choir. A singer lives in basalt and spends an hour as rock between songs. Another’s body rides a tug two hundred kilometers away; her voice arrives a quarter second late because lasers take that long. A digital tenor slows from 100x to match a child’s hard day. No one finds it strange.

What keeps tyranny small and brittle

Three crafts keep power ordinary:

- Build with latency. Authority is local by default. Cross-border moves are slow and require triple keys. Fast emergencies fizzle, fall back on timers, and write their own receipts to the Ledger of Cause.

- Split keys and show your work. All compulsion demands signatures sampled by public randomness. Budgets expire unless renewed with reasons. Federations file “coercion balance sheets” that middle schoolers mock into honesty.

- Fund exits. Every habitat sets aside propellant, tug time, berths, heaters, water, seals, and counsel sufficient to move ten percent of residents within six months. Code polities escrow Core Shares for Splits. The Heat Ledger audits. Stewards run random drills. We don’t tell people they can leave; we cut checks so they can.

Earth, still a home

Earth holds a special charter. Its residents write their laws. Its atmosphere, biodiversity, and indigenous cultures are stewarded with system-wide help. Launch noise is pushed to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets; beamed power pays premiums and keeps to mapped corridors. Attachment to exact sky and soil is priced in a market that clears for preferences, not cash. A child in Dhaka who loves monsoon smells can trade away larger claims on the far commons; a child in Arizona who aches for black sky can do the opposite. Both can learn the worlds beyond and leave if they wish.

What we didn’t solve

The Compact did not make saints. People still hurt each other. Charters contradict. Some children want out of Eden and some don’t. We measure consent better than our grandparents did. We repair faster.

We built custodians that cannot love us and cannot rule us. We designed metal that refuses to obey us in our worst moods. We funded exit and made it noisy. We taught our children to write law like music and music like law. We put receipts on compulsion and jokes in the margins, and we let havens sing in the quiet while forges roared in the cold.

The pledge, again, under a trellis of beans

On Founding Day, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—stand with hands on the rail. An abbot from a quiet ring, a hazard pilot with black chevrons, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under her nails say together:

We will build no throne. We will make no desert. We will make many homes and keep open their doors.

Later a blue dot appears on your wall. “An invitation from the Freefounders,” your cup says. “They’re seeding a torus around a rock near Vesta. Roles open: teacher, pipefitter, poet, seeder-auditor. Charter posted. Exit funded. Do you consent to an informational brief?”

You smile and say yes. The beam corridors glitter. The mirrors turn. The Sentinel hums. You live under a sky that is sometimes glass and sometimes algorithm, in neighborhoods where minds of cell and code meet without one devouring the other, in a civilization that wrote into its wires a humility light demanded: that decisions be slow enough for neighbors to matter, and that anyone can leave with air, water, and a place to begin.
