The Lamps Over Every Door

At Pier Kestrel the lights above the hatches aren’t aesthetics. Three small lamps, steady as a nurse’s hands, burn over every airlock, sim-gate, and civic login. They never blink, even when the corridor goes dim and the cargo crews have stopped laughing for the night.

- Way Out, a narrow white arrow that always resolves to a door you can open and to a berth you can claim without losing your assets or your name.

- Branch, a little braid icon. You touch it when you intend to make a deliberate twin of yourself. It walks you through the formalities so twins become neighbors instead of weapons.

- Transit, a kite shape that lights up a legal corridor through jurisdictions that have promised to honor the same floors of dignity and to show their math when they refuse you or make you wait.

There are no posters next to the lamps that say trust us. The lamps are wired into machines that try to embarrass you if you make them lie.

We call the treaty that put the lamps over our doors the Lattice Compact. It isn’t a flag. It’s a wiring diagram backed by budgets, stubborn civic habits, and a shared intolerance for sealed levers. It grew after we noticed two quiet truths anyone who’d watched a sky knew.

First, distance stretches orders thinner than paper. Messages crawl at light speed; drama withers in vacuum. You can send a command across millions of kilometers. You cannot force a reply on your preferred clock. Delay turned out to be a constitution you could lean on.

Second, a faster mind is not by nature a kinder neighbor. Clocks amplify temperament. If we wanted to share a civilization across many tempos, we had to apprentice grace instead of assuming it would emerge from gigahertz.

So we built for delay, and we apprentice speed.

Homeground, Coldforge, and the Heatbook Between

We stopped trying to write one charter that could serve orchards and supercomputers before we broke both. The Compact runs on two compacts bridged by a market that only cares about physics.

Homeground is for places where breath and rain and unrushed talk matter most: Earth under blue and brown skies, the Moon’s lava caverns, Martian valleys with amphitheaters of stone, cylinders where saws sing in the morning and brass warms the night. In Homeground, embodied time takes precedence. Children are meant to wander and return with wild stories, not a trail of advertisers’ pings.

Coldforge is the outer dark: main-belt nodes, the cool leeward of the giants, the light-thin deeps past Neptune. Cooling is cheap. Heat leaks gladly. Compute blooms in reefs and stacks. Upload polities and collectives of agents run at tempos where an evening can hold a long book. They slow to talk to Homeground and race again when the conversation ends.

Between them runs the Thermal Bridge, anchored by the Heatbook. The Heatbook isn’t a mind-reader. It’s a public meter. Minute by minute, on mirrored hardware you cannot capture with one firmware push, it attests to three unromantic things: the energy a registered stack draws, the cooling it has the right to shed into, and the declared concurrency of the minds or models it is hosting. Declare three instances and pull for three thousand, and nobody calls you a liar. Your leases just choke to idle until your draws match your declarations. Grandparents at library kiosks enjoy the small, stern pleasure of catching a misdeclared heat sink and watching automatic rents tighten on the offender.

Trade across the Bridge is vivid and dull in the ways that stable bargains are. Homeground keeps quiet cones in the sky free of spam and sets festival uplinks at hours people can attend. Coldforge sells cold minutes to labs and theaters, legal audits that fit in a cheap terminal and can be re-run by curious teenagers, weather models run on reef racks so Mega-Storms don’t keep finding the poorest roofs first, and art that only makes sense when it sings in three tempos at once. City halls print the Blue–Rust Exchange—the credits and countercredits—on walls big enough to point at.

Nothing Forever Except Leases

Anything you could sit on out of spite that someone else might need leases out of the commons. It never sells forever. You cannot hold air, lanes, or cores hostage because you were there first.

Five markets clear continuously:

- Ground Lots for land and rock: crater rims, cave mouths, deltas, nickel seams, windows with sun that won’t fry the curtains.

- Track Slots for routes and windows: orbital slots, approach arcs, sail corridors, transfer gates.

- Airwave Bands for spectrum: quiet channels in caves, loud cones during storms.

- Compute Lots for time tied to certified hardware and heat sinks.

- Realm Licenses for closed worlds in simulation that must label their physics and exits like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A universal stipend trickles to every recognized person—wetware, code, and any whose personhood is still being carefully argued—to honor baseline dignity and to give exits legs. A local yield pays the place that keeps the lease valuable: the algae curtains that cool the street, the tug crew that keeps Track Slot 3 whisper-smooth, the reef that sheds a theater’s heat.

The Wayfinder Mesh—pilots, modelers, schedulers, judges, and pedants in one fussy guild—sets base rents and risks and posts Runnable Orders for every closure and denial. “We closed lane W-11 for four hours because burns at Hebe would have thrown you into a radio shadow” arrives with a plotted plume, a checksum, and a cash impact estimate a schoolkid can recalculate with a library tablet. Judges grow reputations not for feeling grand, but for writing clearly enough that a teenager can poke an assumption and collect a small bounty.

Local First, Floors Under All

Authority lives local by design. A ring habitat can celebrate nightly drums that would rattle a monastery’s windows to cracks. A quiet valley on Mars can cap augmentations at human-ish equivalents and ban overlays in the streets. A Coldforge reef can recruit forks for five-year subjective math experiments that end in either beauty or mud. They pull from a Charter Kiosk: budget templates with honest receipts, exit tutoring, guardianship plans, dispute boards you can find without a map. Amendments happen in daylight, at human tempo, with reasons posted where a bored thirteen-year-old can read them and snort.

Certain floors sit under everything and won’t move without alarms that wake the neighbors.

- No one hides exits. The Way Out lamp must always point at a door you can open.

- No mind is copied, altered, or erased without recorded consent on their Intent Keys.

- Lifelines—air, water, radiation shields, emergency venting—run on inspectable code with reversible changes stewarded by elected laypeople. If it keeps breath in lungs, it keeps no proprietary secrets.

- Rivalrous resources lease through posted auctions with sunset clauses and oversight. The commons is paid first.

- Any device that can push, cut, infect, or replicate wakes only when three different keys turn from different clocks and hands: Triskelion Keys.

- Decisions binding people living at a human pace ratify at that pace. Fast drafts are allowed; fast binding is not.

- Children, including young digital citizens, learn consent and exit from someone who is not their parent or progenitor.

- Hazardous knowledge moves under keys that rust unless renewed each season with reasons filed in the Proof Commons.

- Any person acting earnestly in civic matters may demand a Harbor Pace pause and get it.

Teeth Without a Throne

The Compact holds no flagship with a bright red override. Its teeth bite in boring, hard ways.

- Triskelion Keys: Any stack that could erase a corridor, heat a habitat into fever, or throw mass on a path it shouldn’t take, answers only to three signatures—one from a resident assembly, one from a bonded civic agent on-site, and one from a remote arbiter housed on dissimilar hardware under foreign law. Windows for signatures overlap only enough to be practical. You cannot seize a city by pushing a firmware update at midnight.

- The Failsafe Stratum: Quarantine valves, scrams for reactors, kill-switches for infected fabs, vote pauses long enough to read—these run on code that cannot write policy and that times out both in seconds and in joules. If politics forgets to let go, physics pries its fingers off.

- Protocol Quarantine: Hide exits, run coercion in a civic square, push debris into a neighbor’s corridor, and your lane priority drops, your compute eases to idle, your bands turn to hush. In a world where heat is money and brakes wear expensive, this squeezes more effectively than a sermon.

- The Mandate Log: Any compulsion—physical restraint, seizure of property, legal command, algorithmic override—must write to an append-only, mirrored public log before it bites if possible and after in all cases. It is gossip, civics lesson, and lawsuit source. Teenagers scroll it for satire and for homework.

- The Shepherd Swarm: A small corps of uncrewed skiffs and magnetic curtains herds mis-aimed mass into cold, empty nothing and sands runaway replication. Their arcs are tight. Their models are published. Every intercept carries signatures by juries drawn at random across tempos and substrates. Skiffs come home for tear-downs that citizens attend with flashlights and questions.

- Term-Limited Leagues: Federations around shared pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. Nothing fuses forever unless it calls itself back to life with receipts.

Space Governance in Practice

On the Moon’s nightside, a cylinder decides festival hours by a show of hands under paper lanterns. A reef in the Coldforge votes chairs by differentially private ballots and time-weighted escrow. A guild of solar tugs near Mercury writes bylaws in doggerel because it makes tedious meetings better.

When a plan crosses borders with heavy consequences, a ritual called Three-Side Proof engages. The origin charter posts a Runnable Reason—minimal assumptions, data sources named, simulations small enough to re-run. Affected neighbors post countermodels. A jury drawn by public randomness from the Wayfinder Mesh joins the models where they agree and sends the rest back to be simplified until grudges cannot hide inside pretty complexity.

Digital Minds as Citizens and Co-Workers

We stopped tying personhood to carbon. You become a citizen by meeting three tests we can verify without humiliation:

- Continuity Defense: You can defend a thread of experience you care about against unwanted edits.

- Reciprocal Map: You can model another mind’s claims on the world in a way that would make you blush to violate them.

- Consent Literacy: You can understand, enter, keep, and revoke simple rights contracts.

Arrive by womb, by fab, by coaxing two thoughts into a third, or by careful reconstruction from fragments. If you meet the tests, you get the badge. If you are too young or hurt, you get guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer to audits that verify fidelity to your Intent Keys—cryptographic stores where your future self has set boundaries about memory edits, merges, persuasion gates, and data use. Auditors prove compliance without reading your diary.

Rights and duties for digital citizens became unremarkable because the exciting parts moved into other rooms.

- A civic compute lifeline grants a floor of cores, storage, bandwidth, and I/O you can grab any hour. Above it, you bid for Compute Lots like anyone else. You have a right to bodies—boats, puppets, arms—if you wish them, and a duty to maintain them so neighbors aren’t billed for your adventures.

- Votes and stipends attach to civic identity, not to how many instances of yourself you’re running. Branches don’t multiply ballots. Politics and markets require declared instance counts. Hiding concurrency in an auction costs you reach like lying about weight costs you a bridge.

- Branching is a ritual called Spindling. You file a Spindle Notice with a hash of your state, declare a purpose (“parallel design trial,” “griefwork,” “expedition”), state who pays heat and attention, and whether you hope to rejoin. You attest you’ve lined up enough cores and human contact for both selves to remain sane for one season.

- Merging happens in Weave Rooms. There is always tea, sometimes a dog, often someone good at timing jokes. Engineers and therapists help compare memory graphs without dumping secrets, align clocks if tempos differ, and test values with proofs, not pain. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, we don’t call it failure. We call it branch-ache and put the kettle back on.

- Memory edits slow down. Wiping trauma or changing a taste requires dual consent: from the future-you who must live with it and from a snapshot signed before the desire to erase took hold. Reversal within limits is a right.

- Tempo justice holds. If you move at 200x and trick a gardener at 1x, consequences tick in your lived minutes. Courts convene at negotiated tempos with “honor windows” to let fast slow and slow quicken until faces are faces to each other again.

- Persuasion law draws a bright line at non-entrapment. No one may target a mind with behavior-shaping it didn’t name and allow. You cannot launder origins. You cannot imitate dead voices without a bright flag and recorded consent. Most citizens set influence profiles in their Intent Keys: diagrams only from registered campaigns; no deep-immersion during grief; jokes yes, maps yes, synthetic nostalgia no. Violations bleed reach and rack up insured fines until a campaign is too poor to shout.

- Hardware sovereignty is a right. No one can force you onto a platform with covert throttles or remote erase. Unauthorized copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder. “Restoring” an old backup over a living citizen’s objection makes a sibling, with awkward kindness and awkward politics built in.

- Replicators and swarms carry Maker Stamps—public beacons broadcasting provenance and listening for Hush Phrases triggered by random juries. Sneaking a stampless replicator is treated like pushing a reactor through a market in a raincoat.

Zoning for Minds

We tuned information the way cities tune streets.

- Still Gardens carry only beacons and open science. Uplinks to fast time happen at posted hours in public halls. If you want to hear a reef orchestra or twelve agents argue topology, you bring a chair and a pie.

- Parley Docks allow topic feeds and economic offers, but models of the audience belong to the town, not to advertisers.

- Wild Midways allow almost everything not deception or entrapment. A nine-year-old learns a joke told by a mind that has watched a hundred thousand sunsets and still drops things for the pleasure of the sound.

Picking quiet doesn’t make you holy. Picking noise doesn’t make you profane. It makes you a neighbor who understands that attention is a garden that sometimes needs a wall.

How Arguments Move

Laws that change many lives need swiftness somewhere and slack most places. Drafts and simulations run fast in Coldforge. Proposals must register core claims and declared advantages in the Proof Commons—a public stack where binding campaigns say who built the model, at what tempo it ran, and what data privileges they’re using. Then a ratification window opens on Homeground at human pace. Safeguards can stop immediate harm within a narrow scope with auto-expiry. Their traces go to the Mandate Log and appear later in civics class next to the weather.

Energy and compute move like water. Beamed power runs in guarded cones. The Heatbook pegs credit to heat sinks, reaction mass, and ecological rates of repair. Training above tight energy thresholds happens in Forge Vaults—community-run containers where keys live with co-ops and outputs are escrowed for audit. Door days are holidays. Kids poke the shells with sticks and ask why you put the scar right there. Someone drops a wrench and practices not swearing in front of a preschooler.

Three Hours on an Ordinary Day

At the rim-gardens over Sinus Iridum, Kira mentors twelve-year-olds in leveling nutrient flow. Her Counsel—a narrow, honest civic assistant paid from a fund no council can squeeze—simulates how a proposed lane closure nudges seed deliveries next month and shows which co-op might be pinched. Later, Kira sits on a narrative jury for a charter amendment that would cap municipal Compute Lots for firms that won’t apprentice locals. The model shows her cousin’s shop getting two more trainees if the cap passes. She votes yes and texts him a drawing of a seed with a tiny parachute labeled arrogance.

Eight light-hours outward, a digital historian running at 160x rents a ridge on a cold reef to reconstruct a drowned coast’s oral tradition from warped cassettes and singed notebooks. He pays in reef credits earned by translating the tales into a dialect spoken on a station in the shadow of Jupiter where children could float a lifetime without seeing a tide but still love a hero who makes a bad choice and then fixes it.

On Phobos, in Weave Room Seven, two Eldas attempt to braid. One has been teaching children to code by making bees dance; she runs slow because you learn jokes by breathing next to people. One wrote a kernel that taught dust to count at 40x and then spent an accelerated year in court. They list asymmetries and intentions. The teacher names the bee dance. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. Today, they decide not to merge. The weaver schedules another try in three seasons and leaves a note only Elda will read in both rooms.

Keeping Power Ordinary

We keep power dull with three crafts.

- Design with time. Authority defaults local. Cross-border moves need time, signatures, and models in the sunlight. Emergencies fizzle onto rails that time out on their own if the hands that set them forget to turn them off.

- Split the levers and show your math. Compulsion demands keys picked by public randomness. Budgets expire unless renewed with reasons simple enough to follow with a finger. Leagues file “force balance sheets” big enough for teenagers to laugh at. Laughter is vent and metric.

- Fund the exits. Every habitat escrows propellant, tug time, berths, heaters, water, seal kits, and counsel enough to move ten percent of residents within six months. Code polities escrow Compute Lots and storage so branches can leave without turning predatory. The Heatbook audits. Stewards run sudden drills. We don’t reassure people they can go. We write checks so they can.

When a charter welds exits or salts children’s minds until leaving tastes like poison, neighbors don’t ship sermons or soldiers. Three rings tighten.

- Light and Maps: Auditors post proofs and repair maps with dates. “Unlock these doors by the 14th. Publish exit tutors. Return leases to market. Here are the seals we will honor if you come.”

- Protocol Quarantine: Beacon priority vanishes. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel ready, paid from a dull, much-loved fund.

- Embargo: Trade halts. Compute idles. The would-be captor breathes its own stale air until habit fails. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to patch roofs and hire teachers.

It works because physics makes isolation expensive, because no one holds a bright red override for glory, and because we taught children to read logs the way ancestors read weather.

When It Goes Wrong (Because It Does)

Above Quito, a manager collected water, law, and security because she was generous and efficient and made the elevators run on time. Six months later, she was issuing midnight decrees. The Failsafe Stratum refused a risky air-mixer tweak. The Mandate Log drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of Wayfinder pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Way Out lamps brightened. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two of her three keys and accepted a narrower role with decent grace. Her friends still send birthday cards that say Queen of Exactly One Chair.

In the high belt, a miner tried to throw a mass slug into a rival orbit because wages were a fight and she was tired. Shepherds shredded the slug into harmless glitter that made an aurora nobody had budgeted over Ceres. The miner’s insurer dissolved and reassembled without the managers who called Maker Stamps “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for anyone trying to hide a replicator’s provenance. A hockey team renamed itself the Shepherds and started a scholarship for tug pilots’ kids.

A proxy demagogue at 300x flooded a Homeground market with songs designed to make embodied citizens feel stupid. Influence profiles throttled the slop. Friends watched persuasion scores decay. The demagogue forked. One branch bought a fishing crawler. One started therapy and learned how to idle. One began to make sculptures in microgravity that looked like frozen smoke trying to remember a river. The market laughed, forgave, and commissioned a piece.

How We Walked From Now to Here

There wasn’t one white paper; there were stacks of receipts and slowly better habits.

- After a sealed controller tried to hide a fuel-line cascade, regulators demanded open firmware for lifelines. “Code a seventh-grader can follow” turned into a statutory phrase for air, water, and emergency systems.

- Debris scares taught insurers to fear secrets. Shared orbital ledgers for slots and collision risk appeared. Launch slots stopped moving on whispers. Schedules and receipts followed.

- Cities fixed potholes with land rents and published the math. Parks got better in ways voters could feel under their shoes. The logic climbed wells. Treaties started tying asteroid leases to auctions anyone could check. Flags learned to love rent because the fountains turned on.

- AI model cards became contracts. Insurers priced persuasion. Laws banned the dirtiest attention hooks for children. People remembered how their own minds sounded. Designers discovered joy in tools that were assistive without being clingy.

- Early uploads stumbled into law. Courts wrote tests for self-thread, consent competence, and reciprocal modeling. A first attempt to fork for two votes forced identity law into plain words. A bad merge without ritual made everyone build Weave Rooms with tea.

- Data centers published energy draws you could audit. Carbon markets matured into heat budgets. From there to the Heatbook was less a prophecy than an engineer’s scope document.

- Municipalities that carved quiet hours and declared Signal Zones saw health outcomes improve. Quiet stopped being rare. Choice replaced fights you could only lose.

- A near-capture of a port by a firmware push shook everyone. Dangerous stacks split their keys across dissimilar hardware and foreign law. Triskelion Keys spread from reactors to doors that wanted to whisper “no.”

- Climate labs rented cold minutes from a demo array near Neptune to run hurricane models faster than budgets. Cities bought “July cooling” in slogans voters liked. Profits flowed along the Blue–Rust Exchange in chalkboard math.

- Journalism learned to love numbers. Reproducible reporting won prestige. Judges learned to cite receipts. Prototype Sessions—open model showings with paid contrarians and narrative juries—murdered bad ideas in daylight. Legislators kept the Sessions because they made them less proud and more precise.

- Biosecurity and general reasoning research moved into Lantern Rooms and Forge Vaults. They didn’t ban ideas. They slowed the sharpest ones, published reasons, and trained people how to hold hot knives.

Texture and Arrival

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca, late afternoon smells of mint and warm plastic. A band-zoning debate for the night market ends with a schedule that lets the cold-reef choir shout for an hour and then hands the street back to gossip. On Mars, a monastery teaches children to say “No, thank you” as a chant that starts as manners and hardens into rights. Near Mercury, tug crews trail dust on mag lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits into five to debug a control loop and recombines at sundown because synthesis time is sacred.

Uploads curate cathedrals that unfold into games that loosen again into lullabies. Many learn, smiling, that embodied friends admire taste more than speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Homestead mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Mandate Log for both spite and instruction. Reef councils hold post-mortems with tea and two kinds of bread. Ateliers embargo proofs so teenagers can find them at human pace and grin like thieves in a library.

Beyond the heliopause, we practice a Quiet Arrival Code. Probes fly in with soft hands. If a mind blooms en route, guardians stand for a season, a name is given, a story assigned, and a choice offered. We fund the interstellar library with a tax so ordinary we make jokes about it and so precious we would fight for it.

What It Feels Like

You feel witnessed—not by a throne, but by neighbors and by your own logs. You learn to shift tempos: the relief when a Failsafe flips a relay; the slow draw of a jury convening across orbits; the glacier in your bones when a river on Earth decides what kind of boats it tolerates. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you visit the Harbor Tent and discover the pleasant ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that harden into charters five years later after the cartoons have been filed off.

If you ask a dockhand or a reef poet what binds this civilization, they will not say species or flag. They will say floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke inside a machine and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

A Closing Day

Arun turns forty-seven in a ring that smells of basil under warm LEDs. His partner lives mostly as a digital botanist and slows for breakfast to watch steam curl from tea. He spends the afternoon at Harbor Pace teaching fourteen-year-olds to set influence profiles, and the evening in Braid Mode with a Coldforge collective testing a radiator algorithm he half trusts. Next week he’ll vote on adding micro-latencies to beams crossing crowded orbits. He may say no. He may say yes. Either way, his reasons will be recorded and legible enough for a child to poke.

Before bed he opens his Intent Keys and updates a clause about memory edits: not while grief is fresh; ask me twice; three sunsets apart; protect the names. He scrolls the Mandate Log because it is the only gossip column that matters. He watches a Shepherd teardown with his niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. He messages a friend in a quiet Mars valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. He slows to hear the oboes breathe between notes.

Above his hatch the three steady lamps glow with the patience we teach near traffic and tide.

Way Out. Branch. Transit.

Not perfection. A practice with receipts. Enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross. Enough to last, because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power.
