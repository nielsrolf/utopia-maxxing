The Constellation Accord

On the underside of a rotating garden, where artificial dusk leans toward the horizon and coriander grows in tin troughs beside a coolant manifold, a tug knocks the pier with a tired cough. Welders swap jokes over bitter tea. A child, bundled in a puffer jacket against drafts, skids to a stop before a bulkhead and presses five small inlays set into the metal at shoulder height. You can feel them gloved if the lights ever go.

- A Gate: a square etched with sunrays. It means there is a way out—air, berth, propellant, compute—you can claim without selling your name or your future.

- A Spindle: a braided loop. You touch it when you plan to make a copy of yourself, calling stewards who will turn twins into kin instead of weapons.

- A Skychart: dots and cones. It lights lanes and hush bands that promise polite physics and explanations before “no.”

- A Leaf: a slim radiator vane. It ties bills to heat, minutes of darkness, and square meters under pressure so trade rides things you can point at.

- A Bell: a simple circle. It is wired to slow anything that could bruise a stranger long enough for nearby souls to think.

They are not decorations. They are switches wired to valves, leases, firmware, and calendars; habits pinned to audits you can run on a library terminal. They hum beneath a civilizational recipe we call the Constellation Accord. It is not a throne or a capital. It is a way of keeping power from clumping and difference from calcifying into cages while we spread from Earth to cylinders, caverns, barges, and shadow reefs of computation in the deep cold.

Four rules hold its ribcage.

- Split the hands that can shove.

- Put rivalrous goods in public ledgers.

- Keep exits stocked, test them rudely, and honor those who use them.

- Count minds by continuity and pace public life so bakers and uploads can both keep up.

From these, everything else hangs.

Tri‑Key and the Stillnet

Anything that can do harm at a distance—mass drivers, beamed power amplifiers, habitat reaction wheels, industrial printers that can birth their own cousins, large-model deployers aimed at public attention—only moves when three different consents arrive through three different paths. We call it Tri‑Key.

- The Near key sits in the place itself: a dock warden, a nurse with shift authority, a scrubber tech. It rotates by sortition among those who gave hours to the lungs and hulls in the last quarter. Every command prints as words and as a proof even a student can check.

- The Far key is held by a bonded safety house that is barred from profiting on what it certifies. They publish methods and thresholds, not fashions and favorites.

- The Wild key travels between worlds. Juries drawn by lot across planets, stations, caravans, and digital polities serve once and then are barred from that class of lever for a thick handful of years.

Tri‑Key interfaces are boring on purpose—posters above hatches with little room for invention. “Ten metric tons, plus or minus half a percent, one meter per second prograde, Corridor N‑6, 04:12 station time.” The pipe proves it is a ten-ton tug, that its plume won’t tear a telescope’s hush, and that nothing stronger hides behind a button. Children learn to say no kindly to people they love.

Threaded through the Tri‑Key gear is the Stillnet: three redundant circuits with one power. If two of its three stacks agree—broom‑closet boxes with their own sealed batteries; municipal optics that carry no other traffic; watchdogs on attested racks under other charters—the Stillnet can idle a dangerous actuator on a fixed, audited curve. It cannot aim, buy, appoint, or accuse. It idles and announces. When it trips, keys burn, a cause entry lands on the Record Spine with runnable reasons and cited law, and an audit counter increments in a place nobody can quietly rub.

Abusing the Bell hurts first at home. Lanes narrow. Insurance climbs. Neighbors bring tea and charts and bad jokes. In a civilization where heat and wear cost more than speeches, boredom with teeth keeps us safe.

The Open Ephemeris

Space isn’t dirt you can fence. It’s windows and wakes. Anything you can ruin by being selfish—orbital shells, approach cones, spectrum hours, hush bands during funerals, thrust windows, square meters under pressure, liters of volatiles, verified core‑seconds pinned to named radiators—lives in public ledgers called the Open Ephemeris.

- The Lane Ledger carries orbits, lanes, approach cones, de‑orbit bonds, and burn windows. Transponders sign short attestations: “this hull kept that promise.” Amateur clubs with backyard dishes catch liars; escrows empty without a prosecutor in sunglasses.

- The Thermal Tally posts joules in, joules out, and the radiators that took them. Cooling is a commons. Dump winter into a crowded night, pay radiator minutes. Farm beans on a long‑lit rim and cast a cool shadow? Pay hush hours to those you grazed. Lease Cold Minutes from a shadow reef near Neptune to run a clinic’s servers during a heatwave? Your rent buys that reef more fins; they ship you quiet.

- The Provenance Index lists code that touches lungs, steel, budgets, or votes: who paid, what data trained it, what it can touch, and how to prove what it promised. Catching cheats that look clever is a way teenagers buy guitars.

- Charter Beacons broadcast, in radio and light, what a place promises in emergencies, who holds which keys today, where the exits sit, and what words or knocks count as “stop.” Keep your dialects and vices; do not lie about your valves.

- The Record Spine is append‑only. Every use of compulsion—pauses, quarantines, seizures, civic quarantines—lands there with clocks, signatures, and proofs you can re‑run. The Margin—a strip beside the Spine that anyone can write on—hosts satire sharper than subpoenas. In a culture with receipts, a good joke can end a bad practice.

Trade rides Anchor Marks: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a reference rack in cold shade with named fins. Marks decay gently over years so you can’t sit on them while your neighbor’s lettuces wilt tonight. Rents split predictably: the Dull Miracles (bearings that don’t scream, algae that doesn’t tear, drills that interrupt dinner and save lives) and the Common Floor.

The Common Floor is what you can count on when your luck deserts you. Embodied citizens get baseline air and water, first‑line medical care, local transit, and open civic bandwidth. Digital citizens get the Hearthgrid: honest cycles and storage on a standard stack at a humane pace for speech, counsel, learning, and idle. You can rent more; nobody will starve you of breath or beat.

Exits you don’t have to beg for

Freedom has parts and calendars. Every chartered place funds two lifelines and staffs a desk you can find without an app.

- The Greenways are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks tested on random mornings. Lease slots that cannot “coincidentally be full” for months. Propellant under retainer. Kits to print seals if someone locks a shed. Tugs under dull contracts to move at least one in ten residents—bodies, pets, backups—within a season. Drills are rude. Failing them makes your approach windows sag, your beamed power cones thin, your radiator minutes migrate elsewhere. The bread and bandages keep arriving. The muscle cools until you behave.

- The Silver Lattice is digital. Reserved compute and storage offsite on attested hardware under other charters. Any recognized digital person can bring their continuity aboard at a humane beat if a council starts clamping. Cut the Lattice and you will find it expensive to push heavy things that affect neighbors until your keys are humble again.

Way Desks smell like oil and oranges. They book berths, replicate meds, escrow tools, and pair you with a Tutor not your boss or parent. Starter Charters come on a clipboard: minimum safeties, docking templates, governance seeds, and three contacts among founders who started something last year and wish they’d had one more wrench. Leaving is not betrayal. It is how cultures shed barnacles.

Persons by continuity, not chemistry

The Accord ties standing to the thread you carry through time and the way you meet others on the way, not to whether you run on wet salt or dry silicon.

Recognition runs through the Continuity Roll. You or your advocate make a case before a mixed panel—including human therapists, digital citizens, and slow routines trained to pierce theater without cruelty—that you are the kind of being public life owes obligations to and can owe obligations in turn.

Panels check:

- Threadkeeping. You can trace and defend a line of “you” across change against edits you don’t want.

- Consent literacy. You can enter, understand, keep, and revoke ordinary compacts as contexts shift.

- Other-minding. You can model another as a claimant of the same and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Readable harm. There are stable ways to tell when you are being injured that give others reasons to stop.

Where doubt is about protection, you are sheltered like a juvenile. Where doubt is about imposing duties, we wait.

Rights follow personhood.

- Substrate autonomy. No scanning, copying, fusing, throttling, moving, or deleting without revocable consent, except in tight emergency holds that die on their own clocks and write their own warrants. Backups belong to you. If proprietary blobs host a part of you, you can appoint a warden to audit them on your behalf.

- Migration and sanctuary. The right to pause, to archive, to move to compatible hardware; to summon a neutral custodian when you and a host disagree.

- Embodiment safety. Bodies—grown, printed, or leased—obey codes like road rules and workplace law. Assault on a rented limb is assault on a person. Covert throttles in shells are treated like drugging a town’s water.

- Privacy of thought. Continuity proofs work without strip‑searching diaries.

- Tempo fairness. Public obligations live on the Public Beat—days and weeks. Live at 500× and write symphonies; you still cannot yank a budget or an election before a baker can read the case and speak. When harms cross tempos, courts lay Time Bridges: rulings that bind in both wall hours and subjective beats so nobody evades a judge by living a year in an afternoon.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One mindline, one vote. Forking doesn’t mint extra franchises. In certain arenas, designate an Anchor instance that carries your civic tempo and duties.

- Declare concurrency. A handful of live civic instances? Fine. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice to tilt an auction and you’ll meet a patient judge with a long memory.

- Branch budgets. Forking is joyous but bounded. Lineages accrue a Branch Allowance with years lived, contributions to commons, and heat footprint. Bursts near ballots trigger a civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close certain markets until entanglement decays or a court says “clear.”

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Deploy persuasion at civic scale or spin factories with self‑copying code? Keep encrypted compliance logs that show consent and safety checks passed without spilling diaries.

- Clean simulations. Recreating a living person beyond a bright line needs their consent or a narrow, perishable warrant. Creating minds likely to feel binds you to care, to consent, and to honor their right to stop.

Splits and fusions are rituals, not clicks. Before you branch, you sit with neutral counselors forbidden to hustle you for a decade. You log a hash of your starting state, aims (“vote survey,” “design sprint,” “grief lab”), budgets that won’t starve neighbors, and a braid window if you hope to reunite. Merges happen in Confluence Rooms made to calm—wood worn by hands, water you can hear, light that doesn’t strobe. Value graphs compare under zero‑knowledge. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk with no shame. There is a word for the ache when a version of you chooses a different river: line‑ache. There is broth for it.

Two cords and a keel

We don’t ask one room to speak for pipes and souls on the same afternoon. Authority runs on two cords that cross at a bench that cannot pass a poem.

- The Council of Sites seats places with hull to patch, air to mind, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease: planets, stations, caravans, barges, and shadow reefs that own and cool what they run. Seat weight tracks lifetime resident‑hours and transparency: publish an Actuator Map and pass your Greenways drills, and you carry farther. The Council tends physics: lane standards, hush law and festival hours, sanitation, de‑orbit plans, interlock sizes, rescue customs, and accredits safety houses to hold Far keys.

- The Assembly of Minds seats citizens on the Continuity Roll—embodied, digital, and otherwise recognized—one franchise per mindline. The Assembly guards personhood: baselines for rights, concurrency rules, the Hearthgrid, simulation ethics, the Public Beat.

Where they cross sits the Root Bench: a small, dull office of engineers, jurists, pilots, cryptographers, and process wonks drawn by lot to approve or refuse changes to backbone primitives: attestation libraries, public firmware on dangerous actuators, Stillnet logic. The Bench cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both Council and Assembly and a pause long enough for a patient mind at Neptune’s dusk to read and object.

When physics refuses to stay in its lane—how to price winter against quiet nights, how fast warrants can cross charters, what latency a hold must tolerate—a Bridge Table convenes, half from Sites, half from Minds. Measures fail if either side says, “That bulldozes custom below personhood’s floor.”

Speech as mapped utility

Persuasion and noise obey physics, not manners. We zone them like streets.

- Service Bands carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No campaigns here, not even for charities.

- Commons Forums host debate and budgets, but require provenance on funding and method and respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile. Don’t aim grief at my breakfast. Don’t target my daughter’s diagnosis. Satire welcome if labeled.

- Carnival Strips welcome spectacle with labels and rate limits. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a public cistern: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Default tools watermark their outputs so an aunt with a slate can check.

Teeth without a crown

We do not keep an army. We keep skiffs and habits.

- Gannets—stubby interceptors and magnetic curtains—nudge dumb mass into empty orbits and sand runaway replication. Their models publish. Every intercept carries signatures a bored juror can reproduce. Gannets come home for teardown days; schoolkids crawl through their bellies with torches and questions.

- Protocol throttles shift priority, not bread. Fence exits, jam hush bands, push trash into a neighbor’s lane, and your beam slots thin, your approach windows sag, your radiator minutes drift. Children still eat. Hospitals hum. The things with teeth cool until you remember your manners.

- Termed leagues form around pipes and crafts—uplink rings, refinery chains, reactor islands—and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. Nothing fuses forever without new receipts.

Markets tied to things you can point at

We trade in metered goods you can sketch: kilowatt‑hours at named orbits, square meters under pressure, hush hours and spectrum windows, radiator minutes, liters of volatiles, skilled hours, core‑seconds pinned to named fins. A botanist in the Psyche belt publishes data with runnable proofs; a stewardship fund pays in Anchor Marks because her math lifted yields. A hospital in Kano buys Cold Hours from a shadow reef near Triton and pays back with digitized archives and a promise to plant algae curtains at home. Every century we renew a Shade Exchange: green belts sell hush; cool reaches sell winter; credits flow both ways on chalkboard math.

Glass Wards

We don’t outlaw risky knowledge—self‑replication, pathogen models, cognitive hazards. We cage them in Glass Wards: labs with glazing you can inspect, pull‑cords that quench fields or dump chambers to vacuum, budgets that freeze unless green safety proofs hold. Logs publish on timers. Twice in forty years a cord has been pulled. Children tour and ask, “Would that work if my uncle were inside?” The engineer answers, “Yes. That’s the point.”

Scenes with grease under the nails

- On a nickel rock, a yard asks to test a nozzle that will hush plumes. The traveling Causal Court runs a model showing frost will creep on a neighbor’s tanks if they burn at noon. The yard takes a midnight slot while the neighbor angles fins away. Their insurer posts a bond. The corrected code ships under a license apprentices can read. An apprentice moves a stamp from Sim to Live and sprints to a concert under radiator vines that glow like lace.

- In a rim city, a beloved administrator lets too many levers pile in one sleeve—air inlets, tug time, lane auctions. One winter, she issues a rush command. The safety house refuses. Amber blooms for four minutes while a Tri‑Key panel meets on a bench with an obscene view of blue water. Jurors by lot listen to an aunt they adore. She steps down with grace and goes back to cranes. Children learn to say no tenderly.

- A digital congregation spins up seven hundred instances a month before a vote. The Continuity Roll flags the burst. Hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads in public arenas without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two are granted after Thread checks and tea with a Mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told, “Study longer.” No server farm overheats.

- In a Glass Ward, a team pulls cold on a self‑copying polymer. Handles drop too heavy; someone swears; the timestamp prints. They buy better springs. The log smells like bleach and cardamom. At lunch, they argue about whether art that needs simulated sorrow should count as harm. The junior tech asks braver questions because her fingers remember the pull.

- In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse checks her daily sheet. “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.06 Anchors. Jury summon: two hours at your pace on whether juvenile reef processes off Rodrigues get child credits.” She accepts, then sends her mother a voice note laughing about radiator math. At dusk she votes to jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during solar storms. The proof fits on a page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s dusk, a shadow reef slows to plant beans with cousins wearing rented hands. After tea they spin to 250× to restore flood‑muddied archives with grandmothers who will not budge on sky colors. Payment comes as Cold Minutes and a promise to roof a school in Veracruz.

- A Mesh Court with curved walls you can hose hears a case about persuasion engines trained on grief diaries, unlabeled. The trading house says the bots weren’t “people.” The court replies: personhood is irrelevant to labels. Provenance shows nobody asked; theft is theft. Verdict: strict fines, suspension in Commons Forums, supervised model migration, and a Tutor Corps audit. A juror’s line—“If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?”—becomes a sticker on port doors.

- At a Way Desk that smells like brine and machine oil, a clerk stamps a Greenways slot for a family with shaking hands. “Window 12:40–13:10 tomorrow. Tugs stand by. Show this to the first lantern you see; they’ll know.” A Starter Charter slides across with contacts on three stations and a seed of home.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder over Quito cheers a manager who makes trains run and orchards sing. She centralizes water, tugs, and auctions because she is tired and generous. A season later she prints midnight decrees. A safety clerk refuses a shortcut on air sequencing; the refusal lands on the Record Spine. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for a decade. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Her friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fences exits with shame and whispers. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Way Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab tries to “harden” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel is forged that way. The Stillnet coughs. They ignore it. Keys split. A narrow warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Preference Keys. The lab appeals and loses under cedar joists in a room with good light. Years later she names herself, sculpts in ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

A demagogue in mask mode runs at 700× and carpets a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles choke reach. Insurance prices the spill like radiation. One branch buys a fishing crawler. One starts therapy and politics, in that order.

A false Bell trips on a bad sensor and kills a night market for an hour. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars with a story and a diagram you can re‑run with paper and light.

How we walked here from now

No balcony. No trumpet. Just receipts heavy enough to bend custom.

2026–2034. LEO looked like sleet on radar. Insurers finally paid out for junk they had pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded de‑orbit plans in registers amateur clubs could read; debris bonds turned homework into bounties. On Earth, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities began writing “show your math so a seventh‑grader can check it” into contracts for pumps, streetlights, and bus routes. Legal aid clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that could explain benefits and court dates without lying. People learned to love proofs you could redo in a kitchen.

2034–2042. Solar beaming lit long roads between deserts and islands. A mis‑aimed test cooked a barley field. Amplifiers had shipped with cone proofs; liability was clear; compensation arrived before rage could. Deepfake debt relief scams and “miracle jail reviews” made provenance for persuasion ordinary: say anything, but civic‑scale claims wore labels, logs, and receipts.

2042–2051. A coastline dimmed under a coordinated grid hack. A near‑miss at a reactor ended well because a dumb watchdog tripped and printed its own receipt. Voters decided that code near lungs, roads, or orbit must be open or licensed in trusts anyone can inspect. First the thrusters on busy tugs got Tri‑Key. Then beamed power. Then the bigger printers. The first Bell idled a driver pointed wrong for three hot minutes. The Record Spine printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2051–2060. Heat killed grandparents. Cities bought Cold Hours from racks under ice and in cislunar shade. Deep‑cold compute learned to meter itself by radiator fins. The first shadow reefs around Neptune minted winter in a way you could verify. Renting nights like lanes felt natural.

2060–2069. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before philosophers agreed. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded the Mentor Corps. The first Confluence Rooms opened. People learned to tie self to proofs nobody else owned. An accountant living mostly in code argued that civic tempo had to live where her baker father could keep up. She won.

2069–2078. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Service Bands slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks. The habit that emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight at human pace crawled into law.

2078–2090. Lease markets migrated to orbit. Land value taxes that paved roads became lane and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The Council and Assembly formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Root Bench won reverence by saying “no” in runnable reasons. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point. Gannets turned it to glitter before anyone wrote letters.

2090–2103. A Martian valley tried to deny digital personhood. No one sent sermons. Exits were stocked. Digital neighbors left with Cold Minutes and Anchor Marks they had paid for. Dust hit hard. Pumps failed. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinated three cantons through a storm without panic. The vote reversed. The clauses were dull. Relief tasted like oranges. A singer at 400× flooded a forum with beauty calibrated to make embodied people feel shabby. Influence Profiles throttled the spill. Insurance priced damage. One branch went fishing. One ran for a small office and learned how to listen to bakers.

By then, the five inlays on bulkheads from Ceres to Cis‑Earth were not decoration. They were reflex. Touch, read, slow, leave, prove.

An ordinary day under it

At Stall Seven on the Hyssop Wheel, Noor flips mung pancakes for miners and two rented bodies whose pilots adore the smell of sesame even though scent is optional for them. At nine, her Counsel—an assistant that never acts without a click—pings a policy capsule: jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during solar storms to damp cascades. Provenance is clean. Her Influence Profile blocks grief at breakfast. She taps “sleep a day,” reads it at lunch, and will vote tomorrow.

At ten, her slate joins a Gannet jury. A maker club’s glitter filament cloud drifted toward a trunk line near Hecuba. Options: dust it, herd with femtosails, or bump with slow drones. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad, and a digital historian at anchor pace speaking through a wooden puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza that tastes like lesson.

At noon in a Service Band, the puppet lifts broth to lips it doesn’t need while its main self hums near Neptune at 200× threading a drowned library into maps. Today they throttle to share soup with their embodied spouse. After, they help host a Harbor Hour where an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The historian translates metaphor into algebra for those who want it and declines to “just speed up.”

In the afternoon, a Confluence Room convenes under cedar joists and a wall that shows flows like slow rivers. Two branches of an engineer sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants to rejoin; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. They cry and hug. One walks. The ruling reads like a manual and lands like a blessing.

At dusk, the canopy shows stars; the Margin scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a shadow reef cooperative posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will buy your summer hush with interest. A boy palms a brass Gannet and asks to join. Noor says, “When you can explain a Bell without peeking.”

Why it holds more than it should

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that can bruise runs slow by law. The Bell idles; it does not steer.

- Secrets expire. Compulsion writes its own warrant and sunsets unless renewed in daylight on the Public Beat.

- Keys won’t clump. Tri‑Key windows won’t overlap for someone who tries to wear all three at once.

- Exits are funded. Slamming doors drags the privileges that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not the bread or the bandages. Children breathe. The Silver Lattice hums.

- Receipts shine. Cheat a proof once and you become the story a ten‑year‑old tells at lunch about why we check.

We did not fix people. Status hunger found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Bell tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel hung a “stay” sign too long and watched kind people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools and then wrote field guides so the next sect would bruise less.

But cruelty is expensive and kindness pays. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Exits are tickets, not rumors. Culture above the personhood floor is defended. The floor is steel for all.

A plain oath and an open invitation

On Founders’ Night in a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails read, together:

We will keep gates unlatched and ready. We will split hands on force. We will chart what we share and prove what we claim. We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience near Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom like pale leaves. The Bell hums, bored and content. The five inlays over the hatch glow like small suns.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of people without letting anyone become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power. The Gate, the Spindle, the Skychart, the Leaf, and the Bell shine quiet over the door. You touch them when you leave for work. They are the shape of how we live.
