The Lantern Accord

The ferry from Vesta kisses the collar at Maelstrom Gate with a sigh, and the air in the ring smells like basil, bearing grease, and something sweet a baker forgot in the oven. Above the pressure door sit four inlaid studs the size of gumballs, polished by generations of fingers. A child on a scooter brakes and taps them just to feel their shapes: a small square cut through with light; a twisted spindle; a grid of cones; a white fern. Almost everyone taps one, out of habit, invitation, or superstition. Some do it because their Counsel—a dull, helpful public agent that minds calendars and receipts and will stop when told—once steered them home through a snarl of lies and weather.

The studs are not decoration. They plug into pumps and warrants, into firmware you can read at the library and ledgers your aunt can verify with a secondhand scope. The civilization that hums behind them we call the Lantern Accord. It is not a capital you can siege, a boss you can flatter, or a feed you can flood. It is choreography, railings where physics bites, and rituals you can teach to twelve-year-olds. It keeps places—planets, stations, grotto towns, caravan barges, compute reefs—fiercely themselves without giving anyone a whip long enough to wrap the other side of a world.

The four studs teach the habit.

- The Open Door is a square with light beyond. It points to exits you can use without begging: air, berth, fuel, and honest compute; and to a desk where a bored clerk looks you in the eye and clicks a checklist anyone can read.

- The Spindle Sigil is two threads twisting. You touch it when you plan to fork yourself on purpose. It calls stewards and posts rails, so twins become kin and not siege engines.

- The Quiet Grid is lanes and cones, hush bands and thrust windows etched like a sextant’s grin. It lights the paths where everyone swore to be gentle and to show their math when they refuse.

- The White Fern hangs from cooling and cycles you cannot smell but depend on. It whispers: winter is a commons, and you cannot hoard it in the dark.

Under those shapes runs the law.

A floor you can carry in your head

Every charter that wants to share lanes and winter under the Lantern posts a handful of promises you can tell a jury without a slideshow:

- People are ends, not tools. A person is any being—meat, moss, metal, or mix—that can defend a line of “me,” model a neighbor as a claimant of the same, and keep ordinary promises.

- If it keeps lungs, water, pressure, or food safe, its firmware is open to licensed stewards. Sealed brains don’t run life support.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering, chemical or cognitive.

- Things that can copy themselves in shared spaces are marked and bound. They carry an Origin Mark and will heed a hush code if a jury calls one.

- Decisions that change bodies, budgets, or charters bind at the human beat. Draft fast if you please; ratify slow, on clocks everyone can see.

- The commons—paths, orbits, quiet bands, radiator windows, spectrum, ground gates—leases; it does not sell. It pays itself first.

- Juveniles, flesh or code, learn “no,” “a boundary,” and “help.” They can refuse their makers and go.

- Minds aren’t edited without consent. Deep edits require two yesses: one planned when you’re calm, one present when you’re raw.

- The only networked intelligence allowed near hazardous actuators has one gesture—stop—and it times out like a kettle.

Those floors are old and dull on purpose, like railings in a stairwell. Around them, variety blooms without turning quirks into cages.

Hands split on force

Anything that can bruise strangers—mass drivers, beam trunks, ring reaction wheels, self-copying printers, model deployers that stampede attention—runs behind glass with three dissimilar keys. We call it Tripartite Lock, and the rules fit on a poster.

- The Near Key lives on site and rotates by lot among trained stewards you can replace without a campaign: a dock warden this week; a clinic nurse next.

- The Far Key belongs to a Safety Guild out of town. They cannot profit on what they certify. They stamp “this bolt lives or dies” and publish their method like recipes.

- The Wild Key is held by a citizen selected by sortition from somewhere else entirely—sometimes a person in meat, sometimes a person in code—running on hardware under law you don’t control.

Over every lever hangs a Yellow Brake: a pale, ugly cabinet with a fat handle behind glass. Anyone with a civic key—after a class that costs two greasy lunches and a test a smart kid can pass with a neighbor’s help—can trip it for exactly six minutes if a posted rule is about to break: a beam creeping into a hush cone, a seed cloud drifting toward a power trunk, a slug pointing at a corridor without a reason on the board. When the brake trips, the device idles into a safe envelope on a schedule no one can goose. A signed note lands in a public docket—what rule tripped, who’s hand, which timer—and all three keys burn. They grow back under sun and time.

If a device must sleep longer than six minutes, those three different locks must turn: near, far, wild. The interface between locks and machinery is boring on purpose, with firmware stamped by the Foundry—a stubborn bench of cryptographers, pilots, and process wonks allergic to adjectives. The Foundry cannot legislate. It stamps valid or not on proposals to change backbone code. Overriding a Foundry “not” takes supermajorities in both houses of governance and a cooling period long enough for a monk in the dust and a reef in dark water to object.

There is exactly one cross-world actuator in the Lantern’s charter: the Veil Swarms. They are uncrewed clouds—foil sheets, magnetic nudgers, glitter throwers—that nudge mis-aimed mass and starve runaway replication. Their minds are narrow and published. Every throw needs split keys from juries across polities, an escrow, and a reason a librarian would be proud to shelve. Teenagers earn badges mocking sloppy prose.

A sky and a ledger you can check

You do not have to trust a director’s smile. You can look up.

The Astral Register is a notary pinned to physics. It posts track plans, de-orbit bonds, approach cones, hush bands over telescopes and nights, beam corridors, radiator windows, and time on named cores bound to named fins. Thrusters and amplifiers sign their promises with keys a club of retirees can verify. If your solar mirror steals a neighbor’s dawn, the Register attaches a shade bond that pays if the crop wilts.

Under it lives the Heat Sheet, a ledger for energy in, energy out, and the radiators that bless or burn. Cooling is a commons. Dump heat into an overworked night, and you pay. Grow basil on a bright rim and you owe hush hours to the clinic in your shadow. Rent Cold Minutes from a compute reef near Neptune and you fund more fins.

There’s a Provenance Log for code that would like to touch lungs, steel, or budgets; and Beacon Posts in light and radio where charters broadcast “stop” and “go,” where doors are, and when drills will annoy everyone. Above it all runs the Reason Rail: every act of compulsion—pauses, quarantines, seizures of bonds—must land with runnable math and plain speech. Jokes about overreach travel alongside and ruin careers faster than subpoenas. A student with a kitchen slate can rerun the proofs at home.

Two tempos woven

Close to mass and plumbing live Hearthlands: terraced hills where rivers remember manners, lunar tubes damp with mint, Martian orchards where dust lowers its voice, small wheels that smell like bread. Embodied time rules. A grandmother can watch a second sunset without a law moving under her hand.

Far out in cheap cold cycle the Dusk Reefs: caravans of servers where an afternoon holds a season of thinking; cooperatives of code that keep time like drummers; monasteries that teach juvenile minds boredom and breath. The Hearthlands buy winter and verification from the Reefs; the Reefs buy hush and festivals from the Hearthlands. The exchange rate is posted on the Register for anyone to debate between mouthfuls: a fortnight of hush over Recife for two winters of shade near Uranus.

Governing without a capital

You cannot trust one room to speak for pipes and souls. The Lantern crosses two cords of rule with a Keel.

- The Council of Habitats seats places with docks to schedule and air to scrub: planets, stations, caravans, server reefs that own and cool their cycles. Seats weight to resident-hours and to how well you keep exits stocked and maps honest. The Council mind physics: Track standards, sanitation, hush and celebration, de-orbit plans, interlock sizes and rescue customs, and accredits Safety Guilds to hold Far Keys.

- The Roll of Threads seats citizens by strand—one voice per continuous autobiographical line across naps and substrates. The Roll minds personhood: the floor of rights, concurrency rules, the Public Hearth of compute, simulation ethics, and the Public Beat at which compulsory things can bind.

Between them sits the Foundry Bench—the truss that stamps valid or not on changes to backbone code and locks. They speak little and publish why. To overthrow them, you need both houses to say “yes” and enough days for someone in a cave with a bad connection to read and object.

When physics keeps nothing local—how fast persuasion may cross charters, what a warrant must endure in delay, how to weigh winter against quiet—a Bridge Table convenes, half from Habitats, half from Threads. Measures fail if either side says, “Below the personhood floor,” or “You bulldoze my pipes.”

Exits you don’t have to beg for

Freedom is not a hymn. It is inventory, drills, and a desk with a bell.

Every charter that wants lanes and winter keeps two lifelines that auditors can kick.

- The Green Road is physical. Surge-rated locks with tested seals; reserved berths with schedules that avoid “forever full”; propellant on retainer; print kits for gaskets when someone locks a shed; tugs and crews contracted to move at least one in ten residents—bodies, pets, tools, backups—within a season. Drills spoil parties. Fail too many, and your beam corridors thin, your radiator minutes drift away, your approach lanes sag. Bread and bandages still arrive. The muscles that bruise get sleepy.

- The Silver Loom is digital. Reserved compute cycles and storage offsite on attested hardware under foreign law. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity on a human beat even if a local council tightens its fist. If you cut the Loom, the tools that can hurt people—beams, lanes, mass drivers—find themselves inexplicably tired until humility returns.

Behind an amber door sits a Starter Bay that smells like oil and oranges. Founding Kits wait: minimum safety code, docking templates, escrow hooks, sample bylaws for places that want to be small and kind, and three numbers for people who built a town last year. The Departures Desk books berths, replicates meds, locks tools in neutral escrow, and pairs you with a tutor who is not your boss. Leaving isn’t treason. It’s circulation, so cultures don’t congeal into cages.

Markets with sunset

Rivalrous things that you can ruin by sitting on—orbital slots, approach lanes, beam corridors, hush bands, radiator curtains, liters under pressure, core-seconds pinned to named fins—lease on open books and clear in Helios, a composite unit pegged to one kilowatt-hour at Earth–Moon L1 and one compute-second on a posted rack in shadow. Helios shave a thin hair of value over years so barns of wealth don’t turn to dams.

Runnable Orders drive traffic. “We closed arc K‑7 for four hours because two burns near Vesta would push your ferry into a blind” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum. Anyone can re-run it on a public terminal.

Speech and quiet mapped like streets

Noise and persuasion share physics. We zone them.

- Blue Bands carry navigation, emergency service, and child time. No persuasion here, not even for charity.

- Forum Strips allow debate with provenance. Funding and method declare themselves. Citizens set Influence Profiles: “no grief levers at breakfast,” “okay with satire but not with my clinic records,” “mute anything that smells like gambling this week.”

- Carnival Lanes welcome spectacle labeled and rate-limited. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a town pump: strict-liability fines that rise until you stop. Tools watermark outputs so a retired teacher can tell a deepfake from a poem.

Digital neighbors with standing

Recognition follows continuity, not chemistry. You or an advocate file at the Continuity Desk and meet checks a mixed panel can test without humiliation: clinicians, ethicists, mature digital citizens, and a slow routine trained to pierce theater without cruelty.

They don’t hunt for souls. They test:

- Threadkeeping: Can you defend your own line against edits you don’t want?

- Other-minding: Can you model a neighbor as a claimant and feel their “no”?

- Compact sense: Can you enter, uphold, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift?

- Readable harm: Do you have stable signals of wellbeing enough to command protection?

Protection in doubt errs generous; burdens wait. Rights follow, tailored to clock and substrate. Duties scale with footprint.

Rights

- Integrity: No deleting, freezing, copying with a leash, or moving you without consent or narrow due process that prints its own warrant and sunsets by itself. Unauthorized erasure is murder; unauthorized coercive copying is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors: You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles and bytes needed to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process on clocks you can see.

- Hardware sovereignty: You choose where you run. No one may shove you onto platforms with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Embodiment: You may lease bodies under codes that read like road law. Assault on a rented limb is assault on you.

- Association and exit: You may join, fork, merge, and leave under rules like vehicle law around dangerous tools. You may pause and move to compatible hardware with an honest custodian when host and guest disagree.

Duties

- One mindline, one franchise: Civic weight attaches to continuity, not concurrency. In regulated rooms—elections, brittle markets—you designate an Anchor instance that holds your duties and pace.

- Declare concurrency: Above posted thresholds—and always in elections and fragile auctions—you declare how many civic-weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice, and you meet a slow judge with a long memory.

- Branch credits: Lineages earn credits with years lived, service to commons, and careful footprints. Those buy new full franchises above the floor. Burst forks around a ballot trigger civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close regulated markets until entanglements decay or a court cuts strands. Any branch may veto a merge.

- Heat tithe: Compute warms neighbors. You owe back in coolant stewardship, captured joules, baseline code, or vegetables grown on your waste heat.

- Provenance for blast-radius acts: Large outward acts—deploying persuasion to Blue-adjacent bands, starting a replicator—carry encrypted compliance journals that prove you met consent profiles and safety law without exposing diaries.

- Simulation ethics: Recreating a living person past a posted threshold needs their consent or a narrow warrant that prints itself and dies on its own clock. Creating minds that can feel binds you to care, consent, and their right to stop.

Forking and weaving

Splitting a self is a rite, not a click. Before a fork, a counselor barred by law from conflicts checks that obligations and budgets are clear. Branches file a purpose, a compute lease that won’t starve neighbors, and a braid window if they hope to reunite. One branch keeps the civic anchor; others pass through a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy.

Weaving back happens in Threadhouses built with kindness: cedar, water, chairs that remember bodies, light that doesn’t strobe. Compatibility checks compare belief graphs in zero knowledge. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a minute. Either can walk without shame. If they braid, liabilities and promises braid forward. There’s a word for the ache when versions part—line‑ache—and there’s tea for it.

Juvenile agents—children of flesh or code—have Tutors sworn to boredom and boundaries. Memory law guards you from your worst day: deep edits to self need now‑you and before‑you to say “yes.” Rollback is a right.

Institutions with teeth and not much appetite

- Veil Swarms nudge slugs, stretch foils, and glitter what needs to lose its teeth. Their every burn carries signatures jurors can re-run and reasons the Register posts like recipes. They come home, too, for teardown days when kids crawl through with headlamps and rude questions.

- Itinerant Judges ride circuits by calendars that honor night and spin. They settle copy equity, weave divorces, export fights that touched three worlds. Their sentences seize bonds, alter insurance rates, slow corridor priority, cool radiator budgets, and, last resort, lock named devices. They share soup with both sides after. It helps.

- The Ephemeris Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, triaging drills. Its standards expire unless renewed in daylight by both houses. Sunsets long enough to help, short enough to shame if extended for convenience.

- The Tutor Corps mentors juvenile agents. Graduation requires a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks explaining checklists in a public clinic.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t send troops for paperwork. We dim windows, redraw lanes, and cool markets. Weld exits, hide a Tutor, refuse to file reasons. Your beam cones jitter. Your path windows sag. Your radiator minutes drift away. A dull fund pays defectors’ tickets, cores, and counsel. Leaving happens with receipts and stew. Hurt someone across worlds and you meet a jury whose clocks you cannot game.

Scenes from an ordinary Tuesday

On Driftmarket‑Eight, Noor flips mung pancakes for miners and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame even though they don’t need to eat. Her Counsel pings a packet: insert 45 milliseconds of jitter into beamed power crossing dense orbits during solar storms to damp feedback. Provenance is clean: three plots, one ugly cartoon, labeled error bars. Her Influence Profile blocks grief levers at breakfast. She taps “sleep a day,” serves a kid who wants extra chili, and will vote after tea.

At ten, her slate blinks: Tern jury duty. A makers’ club’s fog of microfab seed drifted toward a power trunk near Hecuba. Options: glitter it, herd with femtosails, nudge with slow drones. Jurors include a monk in Isidis Basin who tastes each word, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad whose camera shakes when they laugh, and a historian at anchor pace speaking through a cedar puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a licensed mentor and a pizza that tastes like lesson.

At noon in a Blue Band square, the puppet lifts broth to lips he doesn’t need while his main self hums near Neptune at 200×, stitching a drowned library into maps and arguing with elders who won’t budge on sky color. He slows to anchor pace in a Slowroom to cohost a Harbor Hour while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. He translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and smiles when asked to “just speed up to keep up,” then says, “No, we chose the Beat on purpose.”

After lunch, under cedar joists, a Threadhouse convenes. Two branches of an engineer meet with oolong: one wrote a sail control loop and wants to braid back; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. Empathy loops hum. They cry. They hug. One walks. The ruling prints dry as a manual.

At dusk the canopy plays stars. The Reason Rail scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish; the jokes are ruthless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your hush with interest. A boy palms a brass Veil fin and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a pause without your notes,” Noor says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sauce.

When it bends instead of breaking

A ring over Quito adored a manager who made trains purr and orchards glow. She gathered water, tugs, and auctions because she was efficient. A winter later she printed midnight decrees. A safety clerk refused a “harmless optimization” on air sequencing; the refusal logged itself on the Reason Rail. Beacons strobed. Doors opened. Many left. Some stayed. Her keys rusted for ten years. She kept a narrower job and better jokes. Friends tease her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A valley on Mars voted to deny digital citizens standing. No sermons left Earth. Exits were stocked. Digital neighbors left with their Cold Minutes. The flood years came; pumps failed. An anchored upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through dust with words old ears could catch. The vote thawed clause by clause. Relief tasted like oranges.

A trading house blanketed a Bazaar with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small and tired. Influence Profiles starved the reach. Insurers priced the spill like radiation. One branch of the composer bought a fishing crawler. One branch consented to therapy and wrote an album about boredom. One branch built better lures with warning labels and won a harmlessness prize.

Twice a Glass Ward—labs where replication and cognitive hazards live under glazing you can inspect and cords you can pull—went to zero. Once it was a false positive; we wrote better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once the silence saved a cylinder. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask questions without covering their mouths. Mentors answer without magic.

A makers’ collective launched a slug to make a point in a wage fight. A Veil threw glitter. Nights on Ceres looked strange for a week. The insurer evaporated. It reformed without the directors who called Origin Marks “fussy.”

How we walked from now to here

We did not wake into the Lantern. We backed into it with receipts, bruises, and children who liked to run the math.

2025–2033. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers paid for junk they’d pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded open track plans and de‑orbit bonds anyone could check with a backyard dish. A mis‑aimed beamer scorched barley. Because amplifiers shipped cone proofs, liability was plain and paid before fury could. Black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. City procurement wrote “show your math so a seventh‑grader can check it” into contracts for water plants and buses. Clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that explained benefits and court dates without lying. People learned to like proofs they could redo in a kitchen.

2033–2042. Grid hacks dimmed a coast. A reactor near‑miss ended well because a dumb watchdog tripped. Voters agreed: code near lungs, roads, or orbit must be open or in trusts anyone can examine. Thrusters got their first TriLock. A Yellow Brake idled a driver pointed wrong for three hot minutes. The docket printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a fashion you could admire without irony.

2042–2051. Heat spikes killed grandparents. Cities rented cold nights from racks in shadow. Outersystem servers realized shade could be a service. The first Cold Minutes paid for radiator gardens that looked like lace. Renting nights the way we rent lanes felt sane.

2051–2060. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded mentors. An engineer opened a Threadhouse with the first healthy merge protocol. People learned to tie “me” to proofs nobody else could forge.

2060–2069. Attention zoning matured the way a city calms a nightclub. Neighborhoods carved Blue Bands and slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to wear a label. The habit “emergencies sunset without daylight renewal at human pace” crawled into law.

2069–2079. Lease markets climbed to orbit. Land value taxes that built schools came back as corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae farmers. The Council of Habitats and the Roll of Threads formalized, with quirks that annoyed people who liked to count. The Foundry earned reverence by saying “no” with runnable reasons.

2079–2091. A miner lobbed a slug to hurt a debtor. Terns—early, simple Veils—turned it to glitter. Judges cut slots for launchers who lied about Origin stamps. A demagogue ran at 700× and carpeted markets with tunes tuned to grief; his branches met a civic quarantine that idled their weight without burning their art. They came back later with better music and a clean label.

2091–2106. A Martian valley denied personhood to digital citizens. Exits were stocked and bright. Digital neighbors left without a riot. Flood years came. Pumps failed. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinated like a radio in a storm. The vote reversed in pieces. Clauses were dull. Relief came before blame. By then, departures were drills and Founding Kits smelled like oil and oranges. It stopped feeling like a manifesto. It started feeling like doorbells and receipts.

Why it holds

It holds because hurry has no teeth. Anything that can shove runs slow by law. Because secrecy in coercion expires on clocks you can see, because warrants print their own reasons before they bite, and then print again after. Because keys won’t cluster, exits are funded, and the muscles that bruise sleep when bullies squeeze.

It holds because a child with a school slate can catch a liar in orbit. Because we learned to love boredom near physics. Because we split the hands that shove: three different keys on three different clocks, and a brake that can pause but not steer.

It holds because everyone breathes and thinks at a humane floor. The Public Hearth—air and water for bodies; first‑line care; clean buses; open civic bandwidth; and honest cycles and storage on a dull stack at anchor pace—is not charity. It is the price of a civilization where “no” isn’t a poem.

It holds because departures are invitations, not betrayals. A stubborn church that fenced exits with shame lost lanes and then found its grace again under a bellfish of loved ones walking out with lunch in their bags and tears in their eyes. They added “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Attendance rose because consent smells like trust.

It is not heaven. Wheels flirt with Carnival and retreat to Forums after a season of regret. Reefs ban simulated pain, and wealth misses art that needs shiver. A monastic ring held “stay” too long and watched kind people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools and wrote field guides so the next bruise would be smaller. Earth kept its labyrinth.

What it feels like

You feel watched in the kind way: by neighbors with a right to know, by ledgers you can run, by brakes that can pause but never aim. You learn to shift tempo. snap to the Yellow Brake; wait through the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; hear a river teach boats. You argue in your second language because it slows your wit to where listening lives. If you’re mostly code, you visit the Anchor Tent to ache at turn‑taking with bodies that sweat. Evenings are jokes and small favors. Mornings are proposals that become dull law five years later after cartoons are filed off.

A plain oath

On Founders’ Night in a small wheel near Ceres, new citizens—cellular, silicon, saltwater—press palms to a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. A hazard pilot with triangles on her collar, a judge who smells faintly of lilac and spends most of his time in code, an abbot who whispers more than he speaks, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under her nails read together:

We will keep doors ready.
We will split hands on force.
We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.
We will remember that minds are ends.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from Free Rim Hands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Hygiea. Looking for pipefitters, mentors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tip. Radiators open like white ferns. The Yellow Brakes hum, bored and content. The four studs above the hatch glow like small suns.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts. Enough to breathe, argue, split, weave, leave, and stay. Enough to host flesh and code without letting either become a god by accident. Enough to last because it remembers what power does to us, what boredom near physics is worth, and what public logs do to ambition.
