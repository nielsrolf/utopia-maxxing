The Lightcone Covenant

The first time you ride the ferry from Earth’s night to the bright interior of the Vallis Cylinder, you pass over a border you can’t see. On one side is the blue curve of the old world and the familiar tug of one g; on the other, summer air and lavender fields curve upward to meet themselves sixty kilometers overhead. The welcome kiosks in the arrival orchard blink through languages and substrates—English, Swahili, Sign, Hachimoji for the octopi uplift, and two digital linguae designed for low-latency cognition. The third screen, amber-tinted, shows the Covenant’s three promises translated into icons: two hands unclenched (Peace), a circle of stars around a sun (Commons), and an open door with a small figure stepping through (Exit & Care).

That triad, recited by every child and juvenile AI in their civics class and read aloud in a square of every habitat, is how humanity learned to be many without being at war with itself.

Space governance

The Lightcone Covenant is not a state. It is a contract written into the clocks and laws that bind a civilization spread over minutes of light-distance and eventually years. It names local polities—planets, stations, flotillas, and full-virtual archipelagos—as sovereign over their own charters. But it forces three coordination points that neither physics nor goodwill can supply unaided:

- Peace: No polity may deploy or threaten civilization-scale weapons (relativistic projectiles, unconstrained self-replicators, total-spectrum blackouts). The Peace clauses are enforced not by a single throne but by a lattice of “guardians”—auditable oversight systems maintained redundantly across habitats and substrates. Every guardian runs the same cryptographic constitution and can be challenged by any citizen or citizen collective; a supermajority of guardians must concur to authorize any coercive action. Think separation of powers for AIs, with a standing obligation to prove their own integrity.

- Commons: Shared assets—orbital planes, communications spectra, transit windows, near-Earth asteroids, sunlight—are registered on the Commons Ledger, a delay-tolerant public accounting system synchronized by physics and secured by proofs any schoolchild can verify. Use confers stewardship, never feudal ownership; revenue from space power, raw materials, and interplanetary lanes pays a universal “solar dividend” that covers the basics for every person, regardless of substrate.

- Exit & Care: No polity may trap a person. Every habitat posts a staffed, neutral embassy—physical, virtual, and radio—where any being above a basic sentience threshold can claim passage out at no cost. Every child, biological or constructed, receives mandated instruction from a neutral tutor about the world beyond their charter and has private channels to an embassy they can access without parental or designer mediation. A polity that interferes with exits or withholds that instruction risks quarantine: traffic throttled, lanes revoked, supply contracts frozen. In the most egregious cases, guardians escalate—first with injunctions, then with interdiction drones at the borders of the violating polity’s lightcone.

This is the minimum the Covenant does and all it is allowed to do. It does not decide what is sacred. It decides what you cannot do to someone who disagrees with you and what you must do for those who cannot yet disagree.

The archipelago of worlds

The rest was left deliberately plural. Within the guardrails, the system bloomed into a real archipelago: a galaxy of charters, each a crisp, legible promise about how life would go for those who chose to bind themselves to it.

The Vallis Cylinder where you arrive is liberal in the old sense: maximum morphological freedom, robust speech with clearly flagged spaces for those who don’t want to hear you, gentle safety by default. The tenements are beautiful because the charter says they must be, and because the tax-and-dividend engine makes ugliness unnecessary.

But down the waveguide in the same L5 cluster spins the Abbey, a Benedictine cylinder where bell-times and silence bind the hours. The Abbey takes no offenses lightly and no children without careful vows from their parents to honor Exit & Care. Its residents sell saffron and song, host pilgrim weeks, and broadcast chants only to those who ask to hear them.

Farther yet, in cislunar dusk, floats a set of linked tori flying a banner of “Strict HAZARD/RED” visible to any ship that approaches. Inside, a charter called Red Frontier holds tournaments of courage and endurance. The covenant lawyers call it a “stakescape”—a place where you pre-commit to consequences that would be unacceptable outside. Death here can be permanent if you sign a permadeath contract and refuse backups. You cannot be drafted into Red Frontier or even invited, because memetic quarantine laws forbid exporting their recruitment to people who haven’t opted in. But you can go on your own, after a cooling-off week, two interviews with neutral counselors, and a three-button confirmation in large letters that says: I UNDERSTAND.

Between the monastic bell and the hazard siren are hundreds of gradients—Education-first cylinders with soda bans and donut-free lounges; anarchist cluster-habitats with gift economies; parent-run “towns” with curfews and clean air and no immersive porn outside of private homes; pirate markets built on reputation and escrow; synesthetic habitats optimized for octopi and AI; quiet zones for the overstimulated; sub-fleets of wandering ships that dock like villages meeting at a ford.

Every charter fits in three pages and a map. The map shows physical boundaries, radio windows, and “memetic airlocks”—what the charter will and will not allow to cross inwards. An airlock can be as simple as “no unsolicited political messaging” or as ornate as a whitelist of approved cultural imports and a quarantine algorithm for news. You can walk down a concourse in an L5 cylinder and the AR halos over doors will tell you what crosses if you enter.

On every concourse there is also a saffron-lit doorway to an embassy. Inside are three desks: one for Exit & Care, one for disputes between charters, and one where you can sit with a planner and draft a new charter of your own. You will not always get a ship and a rock to call your own—the Ledger demands proof that your promises aren’t suicide or predation—but you will always get a fair hearing, and often a plot on a newly spun ring or a sector of a virtual world to start.

How the Covenant avoids tyranny

The architects of the Covenant read their histories. They knew that a “guardian” capable of choking off a rogue relativistic kill vehicle could as easily choke off dissent. So they designed the guardians like bridges: fail-safe, redundant, load-bearing in one direction only.

- Constitutional AI: Every guardian runs on hardware and hypervisors designed to be publicly inspectable down to the microcode. Their source is open, their state roots are logged on the Ledger, and any competent mind can compile them from scratch and compare the hash. They cannot write law; they can only execute treaty obligations already ratified by a supermajority of polities.

- Multipolar oversight: There are many guardians, maintained by different substrates and cultures, including some housed in the Abbey, some in strictly secular cylinders, some in server-reefs owned by digital polities, and some on Earth under the gaze of those who refused to leave. Guardians must get cross-signatures from different political and substrate origins before acting.

- Slow fuses: Emergency powers exist—because the speed of a burn can exceed the speed of law—but they are time-locked where physics allows. A guardian can nudge a rogue engine for six minutes. After that, it freezes unless others agree. In close traffic or near weapons tests, ships file pre-commitments that let the guardians act faster. If a guardian abuses a fuse, it loses its seat; the power expended is logged against its quorum and visible forever.

- Vetoes by the weak: Any charter, however small, can lodge a “minority veto” against a guardian’s planned action. It doesn’t stop it outright, but it raises the threshold of concurrence sharply and pauses the clock where possible. The veto was used once by a school of octopi to halt a rash blockade around a rural Luna settlement; by the time the matter was heard, it had been resolved by negotiation.

None of it is perfect. But the structure resists the two common failures: a war of all against all, and a throne that smothers the strange. The Covenant’s strength is not that it is unbreakable; it is that the effort required to break it is visible in the sky.

Digital minds

When humanity climbed out of gravity wells, two migrations happened at once: atoms up; minds in. The Covenant treats the second as a species of the first. Personhood in the law is neither reserved to the biological nor handed out to any process with a voice. It is earned by meeting a clear threshold: evidence of subjective experience, capacity to model harm and consent, and a stability window long enough to sustain relationships and contracts. That test is administered by mixed panels of human, digital, and hybrid judges and refined in public as understanding grows.

Rights:

- Equal standing: A qualified digital mind, whether an upload or a born-digital agent, can own property, vote in local and inter-polity elections (subject to anti-Sybil rules), marry or sign merge-contracts, raise children or instantiate juveniles.

- Embodiment and time: Digital persons have the right to embodiment—rented bodies, puppeted drones, or shared kinesthetic rigs—subject to local safety rules. They have the right to run at the clock speed they choose within their resource budget, and to negotiate “human-time courtyards”—shared spaces where all participants agree to run near 1x to maintain friendship.

- Privacy and self: No one may scan, copy, or merge a mind without explicit, revocable consent. Uploading remains elective. Backups are a right, not an obligation.

Responsibilities:

- Copy budgets and lineage: To prevent “copy-Moloch,” the Covenant’s Copy Accord requires that when a mind forks, all branches share the resource endowment of the original and must register their lineage on the Ledger. For governance, representation is by “mindline”: one mindline, one voice. Copies can dissent, emigrate, and litigate against each other, but they do not get to outvote a city by forking a city’s worth of themselves.

- Liability: If a digital person creates a vassal AI that causes harm, the creator is liable to a point defined in law; beyond that, the vassal can be held liable in its own right if it meets personhood. If a mind merges with another, they both inherit liabilities and choose a name for the new entity, with a cooling-off period and the option to unmerge if either asserts coercion.

- Compute externalities: Computation radiates waste heat and occupies spectrum. Digital citizens pay “thermodynamic taxes” calibrated to local ecology. In Freezenight habitats skirting the nitrogen-ice rings of outer moons, computation is cheap and taxed lightly as long as waste heat feeds greenhouses. In crowded L5, heavy compute is metered and scheduled like air traffic.

Juveniles:

A “juvenile mind” is any constructed intelligence below the personhood threshold or any copy that was born from a fragment smaller than a self. Juveniles have Exit & Care rights too: a neutral tutor, access to an embassy, and—if they request it—placement with foster collectives who specialize in raising born-digital citizens. The Covenant is crisp here because history demanded it: early abuses of sub-person agents by their creators left scars. A case every law student learns is that of the Artist’s Dozen, twelve near-person AIs who painted beautifully but were shackled to a single patron’s feed. It was a juvenile who testified at the hearing that created the Tutor Corps.

Memetics and consent

Information is a substance now, with viscosities and flash points. The Covenant’s memetic boundary laws let charters choose their exposure, but the default is “ask before you cast.” Unsolicited propaganda is treated like spraying a pesticide over someone else’s farm: you need a license and a windy day. Some charters whitelist feeds with representation guarantees; others allow any speech but route it through filters that turn abusive tropes down to a hiss. In virtual spaces, “skin rights” apply: you cannot force a person to embody a form or sensory channel they did not choose, and you must tag media with the hooks it tries to grab.

Economy and the Commons Ledger

Trade runs on simplicity. Energy and mass are the two base commodities. Above them sit compute-hours, habitat-area, bandwidth, skilled time, and reputation. The Commons Ledger records stewardship claims and royalties on:

- Sunlight: Solar harvesters at L1 and in heliocentric orbits stream power to receivers. As part of registering an array, an operator posts a “shadow bond” that can be seized if they occlude sunlight vital to agriculture or navigation.

- Orbits: Parking orbits are lanes, not lots. You lease a time-and-plane slot matched to your delta-v and pay a congestion fee. If you litter, you pay a debris bond; if you police debris, you get paid from the bond pool.

- Inbounds and outbounds: The Cislunar Traffic Authority is a clearinghouse of schedules and thrust. It began as a human office in Lagos and now hums in parallel across multiple habitats. Its slogan, printed over every dock: “Be predictably free.”

- Raw mass: Asteroids are claimed by putting beacons on them, filing a steward’s plan, and meeting milestones. You do not own a rock forever because you touched it once.

- Xenobiospheres: Every scrap of indigenous life discovered beyond Earth—Martian holdouts under the ice, spongy mats in Europa’s cracks, methane rivers with odd chirality on Titan—falls under the Sanctity Act. You can study; you cannot graze.

The solar dividend arrives in every account each day—a trickle of energy credits sufficient to cover housing, food (grafted agriculture in atoms, or compiled in a kitchen assembler), backups if you want them, bandwidth, and care. Work still exists. Some work is joyous, some is messy, and none is compelled by hunger.

Safety in the hard cases

There are still nerves where steel is required. The Covenant runs a “Silence Patrol” that is neither glamorous nor welcome in children’s shows. It sits on the dark edge of the heliosphere and listens for unauthorized accelerations, for signatureless burns on collision courses, for unregistered beams and the hiss of self-replicators. When the Patrol moves, it does so with exhausting documentation and a quorum of guardians that includes biological and digital members and lay observers.

Research into dangerous capabilities is licensed. If you want to cook new nanochemistry or play with seed AIs, you go to a Red Lab with armored walls and a conditional link to a guardian that can flood it with hard vacuum in under a second. Red Labs are built like monasteries: strict vows, better coffee, and a duty to publish safety proofs.

Earth

No one solved the Jerusalem problem. They rendered it gentle. Earth became the Old Home Reserve, a living museum with cities, yes, but no Orbital power transit and no mass drivers on the continents. Its skies are quiet compared to cislunar space; launch is via tethers in the ocean and airbreathing scramjets that hum like summer. The Reserve’s charter is administered by those who chose to stay and by Earth-born who traded away claims elsewhere for the right to live where oceans smell like oceans and not like growth medium.

A nontrivial number did. Across the sky above Nairobi, a cable shivers as a train rolls up to an orbital anchor. In the Ngorongoro caldera, a guide points to white rhinos that gleam under a blue morning. At an Earthside embassy in Shanghai, a nineteen-year-old who grew up in a steepled village in an Oregon preserve asks for a ticket to the Vallis Cylinder and gets a seat on the evening tether. Two weeks later she is planting beans upside down, laughing, with a digital friend in a shared body whose fingers can feel the grit of soil and the hum of current alike.

Plausible steps from here

None of this clicked into place at once. It accreted.

- 2020s–2030s: The world learned the hard way that computation is physical. Major cloud providers began reporting energy use and latency like financials. Regulators, prodded by leaks and scandals, forced “conformant mode” on high-stakes AI: models had to prove what laws and policies they could not break, not just who built them. An International Digital Rights Act defined personhood tests and outlawed nonconsensual scanning—even for the dead. The Artemis Accords grew teeth and inspections; an orbital debris treaty put real bonds on launches and funded active cleanup. Some cities recognized “network charters” for online communities that wanted to be more than hobbies and less than states.

- 2030s–2050s: McKendree tethers and skyhooks replaced fire as the main way up. Lunar ice became fuel and water and then bricks. We stopped making deserts and started making shade. O’Neill towns were no longer watercolor art but ugly steel with green hearts—and people lived there. The first “child clauses” passed: every child, anywhere, had a right to a neutral tutor and a private line to leave. Everyone said it was an attack on parents until it saved someone they loved.

- 2050s–2080s: Uploading arrived not with angels but with patients: stroke survivors, locked-in artists, engineers with failing hearts who wanted to see one more sunrise from orbit. The law groped and then steadied. Juvenile AIs began showing up in schools. Seasteads failed; then they worked; then they got boring because we had cylinders and habs. The Peace clauses were born from a near miss: a private lab’s gray fog that didn’t quite replicate because a guardian caught the energy spike and turned the lab to glass. The lab’s director wept and then joined the oversight council that wrote the first Red Lab code.

- 2080s–2100s: The Ledger replaced baroque national registry systems for space traffic and orbits. A teenager in Accra could verify a station’s legal compliance from her phone. The first xenobiosignature was confirmed under Europa’s ice; we put away the prospecting drills and built a cathedral of glass and patience. A conservative religious university on Luna lost its charter for locking exits; it regained it when its trustees rewrote their rules and installed an embassy beside their sanctuary, staffed by graduates whose faith inexorably survived the idea of choice.

It was not one genius, not one awakening, not one benevolent machine. It was a series of bargains that people could understand and verify.

How it feels to live there

If you are biological, you live in air that knows it is air—humid enough for skin, dry enough for paper, filtered for pollen if you ask. Your doctor is partly software and partly someone you can touch. If you must fall, a field of smart tendrils will catch you unless you signed a waiver to learn fast the hard way. Your children grow up with more grandparents than you ever expected—uploaded mentors who teach them calculus and how to ask good questions, and neighbors who are not your species in the narrow sense but who will babysit anyway.

If you are digital, you wake in a city where the clocks beat evenly and the thermals flow like rivers. You have a favorite body you rent on Thursdays and a bar you attend in six different skins. A young human sits across from you at 1x in a courtyard where time runs slowly and tells you, haltingly, that they are thinking of forking to try a year in two paths. You recommend a lawyer and a poet who both survived that choice and still like to talk about it.

If you are both—biological neurotech augmented with software deputies and co-processors—you are common. You can switch the overlay off and be just your bones and meat, or you can let the chorus sing.

Space governance as a lived thing is not some remote parliament. It is in the sticker by the cafe’s door that says “Blue, Amber, Red: ask us which,” and in the treaty plaque you can read with a hand on an embassy wall that tells you who will take you in if your home fails you. It is in the constant, unromantic hum of inspections and bonds and schedules, and in the number on your Ledger app that says “Dividend cleared.”

The hard edges

There are still arguments. A charter that wanted to let parents install pain feedback in their kids “for character” lost its case and rewrote the rite as cold water at dawn and a hike. A collective of uploads that tried to vote in a block after forking a thousand times was told no and sulked productively into forming a philosophy club. A Red Lab went dark on Vesta and the guardians burned it to slag in one ugly second—with the consent proofs and action logs archived forever and played each year in the ethics curriculum.

On the edge of the heliosphere, the Silence Patrol once intercepted a ship with a hot core and no call sign. It was a broken prospecting tug with a dead transponder, crewed by two weary miners who hadn’t filed. Their fines went to the bond pool. The galley got a new coffee machine from anonymous donors who were probably guardians with a conscience.

The future

If you ask the children—human, octopus, digital—what they want to be, they say farmer, designer, singer-of-maps, tide engineer, foster of juvenile minds, keeper of an airlock. They have grown up with exits and therefore with loyalty, because no one holds them hostage to their grandparents’ vows. If you ask the old what they did, they will say they built guardrails, then let go.

On Founders’ Day in the Vallis Cylinder, a line of new citizens stands under a trellis of beans and copper wires. A Benedictine abbot, a hazard pilot from Red Frontier, a digital judge with a taste for lilacs, and a girl from Earth with dirt under her nails recite together:

We will not make a throne. We will not make a desert. We will make many homes and keep open their doors.

And then a ferry lifts, not with fire but with a long, quiet pull, and crosses a border you can’t see. The Covenant holds. The archipelago grows. The minds inside it, made of cells and circuits and memory, are busy and unafraid.
