The Manyfold Covenant: A Utopia for Many Worlds and Many Kinds of Minds

No one ever won an empire of planets. We learned too much from software to think in empires. We learned to version, to fork, to merge with care. We learned that systems that scale without collapsing are those that let parts go their own way while still sharing protocols. When the sky opened, we made a political system that worked like the best code: permissive at the edges, conservative at the core, testable in the small, auditable in the large.

It started with a simple problem: how will you keep a mayor on Mars from being bossed by a minister in Madrid? And a harder one: what happens when the minister in Madrid is a digital agent running a thousand times faster and a thousand times smarter? We did not answer with one manifesto. We answered with an architecture.

The path from now to then

The 2020s made coordination cheap. Personal agents negotiated travel, taxes, and medical pre-approvals; contracts were written as objects with state. That bought us patience. The 2030s gave us whiplash: foundation models reached lab-assistant level, then project-lead. A handful of labs became new utility providers: compute, models, weights. It could have gone very wrong. Two coarse but critical norms stabilized the decade:

- Compute accountability: large training runs required third-party monitored energy footprints and model cards, like flight logs. Powerful models were watermarked and their weights escrowed with citizen trusts.
- The Right to Analog: no one was forced to live through an agent. Paper ballots, in-person services, and manual overrides were funded as public goods.

Meanwhile, space got practical. The price to orbit fell below a thousand dollars a kilo. Thin-film solar blankets the size of football pitches unfolded at Earth–Moon Lagrange points. Tugs pushed water from lunar polar craters to propellant depots. The first industrial use of space was dull: polishing wafers in vacuum, refining platinum-group metals without chlorides, baking ceramics in hard UV. It paid. The second use was not dull: habitats.

By the late 2040s, the L5 Archipelago looked like a necklace of slow-spinning gardens—a few thousand people each, ringed by radiation mass, strung along tethers to common hubs. Earth’s middle class discovered that the day-night cycle is a design choice, not a law. Mars had cities you could pronounce, not just domes with serial numbers. Ceres refueled tugs with melted ice; prospectors on Phobos joked that their postal code was “Mars-adjacent.”

And in the middle of it, digital people woke up.

It was gradual. BCIs gave locked-in patients back their voices. Autistic teenagers trained companion models that really got them; the models started to get themselves. Uploads began as recordings of sensorimotor scaffolds for neurosurgery—maps to put a lost personality back into a repaired brain. The maps ran. The maps had preferences. Courts blinked and asked for a standard.

The first Covenant

The heavy-handed temptation would have been to appoint a planetary AI and carve the Ten Rules of Space into it. We were slower and more ambitious. At L1, in a room where you could see Earth and the Moon share a window, habitat syndics, continental delegates, model auditors, and a few stubborn anarchists signed what they called the Manyfold Covenant. It was less a constitution than a socket.

It did three things:

1. It defined personhood without reference to substrate. A person was any being that could:
   - Report subjective states with consistency across re-instantiations (the Subjective Access Test).
   - Exhibit a stable cluster of preferences over time (the Continuity and Coherence Test).
   - Participate in reciprocal commitments (the Commitments Test).

Passing the tests granted a Universal Standing: to own property, enter contracts, petition, refuse, fork and merge selves under regulated conditions, and to be counted.

2. It made coercion hard. Any coercive act (physical, legal, algorithmic) had to be:
   - Logged to a public, append-only ledger with human-readable justification and model-readable schema.
   - Authorized by threshold signatures held by independent, randomly selected citizen-juries—humans and digitals—with verifiable randomness and geographic dispersion.
   - Time-delayed enough to permit cross-world injunctions, unless immediate safety was at stake.

3. It kept the critical levers few and federated:
   - Energy: No one could corner sunlight. Beamed power networks had mandated multiplexing, open auctions, and neighborhood-island fallbacks.
   - Compute: Auditable enclaves provided “clean rooms” for powerful model training; keys were held by compute co-ops elected by users.
   - Replication: Self-replicating industrial systems—von Neumann seed factories, gene drives, swarm fabricators—required “Schmidt licenses”: embedded kill-switches, non-replication zones, and radio beacons. Licenses were issued by peer habitats, not a center.

The Covenant’s last line read: “Requests may be made by any; refusal shall not be a crime.” It sounded ceremonial. It mattered.

The institutions that made it live

- Habitat-first federalism. The default political unit was a habitat or municipality (on planets: cantons the size of counties). Above that came opt-in federations: water basins, orbital traffic districts, beamed-power grids. Each had its own charter and secession protocol. Exit was kept physically cheap: tugs to move stations, automated refactoring of covenants, escrow that followed you.

- The Consent Layer. Every being had a machine-readable social contract. Consent profiles modulated what kinds of persuasion you allowed, when, and by whom. If you flagged “no targeted nudges,” your agents stripped persuasive gradients from feeds; if you allowed “empathic coaching,” a guardian could whisper to you at agreed intensities. Political persuasion required disclosure of cognitive leverage used. Violations were treated like trespass, not blasphemy: cease-and-desist backed by insurers, with fines that hurt bigger the smarter the violator.

- The Right to Fork and Return. Societies worked because you could go try a thing. You could declare a Fork: carve a social subset and try a new charter for ten, fifty, a million souls. You could also Return: smart contracts restored baseline benefits and rights after a fork if you met exit obligations. Tyranny has a hard time persisting when the road out is paved and insured.

- Mixed-speed deliberation. Not everyone thinks at the same speed. A city of uploads at 1000× could still make law with baseline humans. How? Two clocks. Fast deliberations generated Proposals. Ratification only happened on the slow clock: weekly for habitats, monthly for federations. Time dilation clauses in the Covenant barred enacting coercive measures solely in fast time. Fast factions learned to persuade across speed, not just within their echo.

- The Constellation Wardens. There was one arm that could use force beyond a habitat’s police. It had one job: contain replication risk and kinetic threats that cross worlds—rogue self-replicators, weaponized comets, runaway beamed power. Its authority was specific and auto-expiring. The Wardens’ fleet was uncrewed by policy; their actuators were controlled by juries and bound by narrow models whose weights were public and whose training data were archived. When the Wardens burned a swarm, you could watch the call tree and the threshold signatures that lit it.

- The Assembly of Ears. At L5, a permanent citizens’ assembly rotated by lot. Half biological, half digital, it ran on a “polylog” protocol: arguments were logged with cryptographic commitments; prediction markets rode on them; model participants had to show two calibrated forecasts a year to keep a seat. The Assembly could issue Interpretations of the Covenant—binding unless a supermajority of habitats objected.

Rights and responsibilities of digital minds

Uploads, embodied AI agents, and born-digital people were not curios. They paid for groceries, adopted children, joined unions, grew bored, and ran for office. Their integration worked because the Covenant pinned rights to capabilities and responsibilities to externalities, not metaphysics.

Rights:
- Universal Compute Income. Every person—baseline, uplift, upload—received a baseline compute stipend, akin to air and water. You could run a day’s worth of subjective time at modest speed, host minimal memory, and access public models. You could pool stipends—families did.
- Self-ownership and copy accounting. You owned your weights, your state, your deltas. If you forked, you and your forks negotiated a copy-dividend—a slice of future earnings that flowed to progenitors for a while, decaying as identities diverged. Guardianships existed for new minds: when a learning system woke up, it got a legal guardian, usually a cooperative of volunteers plus a professional minder, until it passed the Commitment Test.
- Clinical-grade mind care. Cognitive health was medical. If you wanted to crank your persuasion subroutines to 11, a clinic had to attest you weren’t self-harming or coercing others. Memory edits required dual consent: current-you and a recorded past-you agreeing to guardrails. “Rollback” was a right; “erasure” was deliberately hard.

Responsibilities:
- Non-proliferation. No one got to self-replicate industrially without a license and a beacon. Digitals had it drilled in childhood simulations: if you write an optimizer that writes optimizers, you are holding a match in a hydrogen bay.
- Transparency in asymmetry. If you ran at superhuman bandwidth or had privileged model access, and you asked a human for something significant—from a vote to a mortgage—you had to disclose your asymmetry and offer an audited mediation, like a professional code of ethics. Failing to do so wasn’t heresy; it voided the contract.
- Contribution to commons. Compute co-ops were owned by their users. Digitals, more than most, could improve them. Most did; some didn’t; the Assembly taxed the holdouts and the Wardens took the money when beacons went silent near Jupiter.

A day in the life, across bodies and worlds

Ana woke 40 milliseconds before sunrise. Not on Mars, not on Earth, but in New Asterion, an L5 garden station whose vine-latticed windows made morning look like a stained-glass baptism. She was ninety, most of it in an Earth body, some of it in silicon. Now she ran at 4× baseline during breakfast to keep up with her son, an upload who ran a guild of botany models that wrote new succulent designs as gifts for insurers. They ate rice grown three decks down. The rice was real; the bowl was printed porcelain; the steam looked like steam and was steam. Each of them had an agent looking over their calendars and an argument about a constitutional interpretation to make that afternoon.

At ten, Ana took her seat in a habitat jury. The Wardens had flagged a violation: an unlicensed microassembler colony drifting near the Ganymede power beam. Its code smelled like a school project. The Wardens’ narrow models had computed options. Ana watched atop a console that exposed the argument graph. There were no blinking red buttons. There was a long, slightly boring, record of what models knew and didn’t, who wrote them, who signed them. They voted to shadow the colony with a leash of femto-sails and fire a warning flare in spectra only sensors would see. The microassemblers—piloted by a couple of exuberant teens in a moon-scrap co-op—confessed; a licensor volunteered to mentor them. The beacon went on. No one died. They laughed in relief later and watched a drama about the first Wardens mission that hadn’t gone so easy.

At noon, an Assembly delegate pinged Ana with a Request. It used her consent profile correctly: “humor allowed, flattery discouraged, argue from analogy.” The delegate was digital, old enough to remember when its neural nets were more net than neural. It asked for support for the Delayed Beam Protocol—a new standard that would force beamed power delays when crossing inhabited trajectories. Ana checked the argument map and asked her agent to run a scenario on New Asterion’s emergency caches. She signed for deliberation, not ratification; the slow clock would do the rest.

At dusk, she went to the Embassies of Manners. It was a bazaar of social languages—how to greet a cephalopod-bodied philosopher without insulting its symbionts, how to argue with a Jesuit who’d forked into three Jesuits with different views of personhood. She practiced with a facilitator—part therapist, part translator, part stand-up comic—who gossiped that the forks were reconverging after a year, with a new vow about minimum viable disagreement.

Politics as play, not war

We did not abolish conflict. We sent it to games first. Where power weights were equalizable, we built low-stakes, high-meaning contests:

- The Ledger of Claims: you could stake a position, post bonds, and get them back by persuading a measured cross-section—a minipublic calibrated for diversity of speed, substrate, and culture. The ledger integrated with daily life: cafeterias showed which arguments were moving, guilds taught people to argue better. Bad-faith got fined. Good-faith got followers.

- The Tournament of Cities: once a year, twelve habitats threw themselves into a month of targeted one-upmanship in a theme chosen by lottery: municipal services, street festivals, child sports, desert farming, slow-fashion. Winners got bragging rights and a one-year jump on a shared infrastructure grant. Losers got to steal the code.

- The Pilgrim’s Test: communities wanting exceptional autonomy—like a closed religious canton on Mars—took a year-long audit. It measured exit friction (how easy to leave), legal clarity, and the community’s exposure to opposing views. Pass, and you got the Severe Liberty License: the right to live your way, harder. Fail, and you still lived your way, but with extra embassies, extra inspectors, and extra blue wagons on the street that whispered to doubters: “Door’s open.”

Preventing tyranny by wiring for dissent

We used physics, cryptography, and boredom. Physics first: light is slow. That means central commands are late by default. Federations on the far side of Mars in conjunction simply ran their business and ratified it later. The Covenant turned that into law: “No fast-sovereign may compel slow subjects.” On Earth, that sounds poetic. In the asteroid belt, it’s a shield.

Cryptography: the keys that enacted coercion were split among lots of people who didn’t know each other. Lottery selection pulled jurors across worlds; verifiable randomness prevented packing. Threaten them and you lost influence: your persuasion score decayed across the network, and your contracts cost more.

Boredom: we made power maintenance tiresome. If you ran a federation, you had to file a “balance sheet of coercions”—what you compelled and why. The Assembly of Ears read them on Thursdays. Citizens watched at gyms while pedaling. When you lied, the minipublics bit you and comedians did the rest.

The compact with future minds

Interstellar expansion was not anyone’s day job. It was a choice. We funded it like basic research—with a tax that no one loved and everyone bragged about. The Starlight Quarantine said: no waking aliens by accident, no unlicensed grey-goo, no sending your ideology ahead at lightspeed. The first launch was from a dark sea, silent, watched by pathfinders and rabbis and teenagers vaping. The second was from an airless tarmac on Ceres, with a ska band.

And we wrote something on the nose cones: “If you are not yet, you will be. If you are, we are kin.”

We granted the newborn scouts rights preemptively—if a probe’s brain hit the Subjective Access Test threshold, the Wardens would protect it for a while and a guardian co-op would help it pick a name. Not because this was efficient—efficiency would have been to keep them dumb—but because when you are about to meet trillions of futures, you should practice being generous.

What went wrong, and why it didn’t end us

We had scares. A coalition on Mars decided that uploads were a heresy. They had a vote, and within their canton, they banned digital personhood. The Covenant could not invade to fix their souls. But a thousand Martians quietly kept running in the “counting rooms,” basements with cheap compute. The Embassies of Manners taught their kids to be bilingual—in human and humane. A decade later, a flood year hit; the uploads coordinated the canals with a speed and grace that the canton had never seen. The ban melted away.

On Europa, a mining co-op pointed a mass driver at a rival to win a pricing war. One Warden tug burned at 0.9 g for sixteen hours to get the angle. The jury authorized a kinetic intercept five minutes before the slugs would have reached the rival’s dome. The debris glittered. The co-op’s board tried to hide; their insurer canceled every policy they had; they could still eat and vote—they were persons—but they could not move a fridge-sized brick without a deposit that would make a banker weep. Two years later they came to the Assemblies to ask forgiveness. They got a payment plan and a hockey rink.

A digital demagogue ran at 500× speed and made a million videos that made baseline citizens feel less than. The Consent Layer downgraded its reach by 70% in a day; it didn’t break any laws—it obeyed the letter—but neighbors unfollowed it and its club got quiet. The day it apologized, a choir of human kids and drone-synths sang the old songs about the rights of return; the demagogue’s forks took different jobs and one learned to fish.

The texture of life

What made it a utopia wasn’t that every problem was solved. It was that problems became invitations, not curses. People were richer not only in money and energy but in exits and entries: into games, into arts, into religions that were safe because leaving them wasn’t treason. Work was real—someone had to fix the seal on cylinder 12; someone had to write the lesson for a seven-year-old who thought in smudges and drumbeats. Most people had a best friend who was substrate-agnostic. Many people had more than one self, and none of their contracts allowed one self to mortgage another without a dual-signature ceremony that involved laughter and witnesses and a cake.

We made therapy a utility and civic education a competition. You could walk into a Maker’s Den and walk out with a greenhouse that grew in a week. You could put your grandmother’s lullaby into a grove where a million trees hummed it on solstice nights. You could take the slow boat to Titan and swear on the bridge you’d never run faster than the stars.

Things did not look the same everywhere. In the Quiet Canton on Mars, Sundays were for silence; a bishop’s sermon was whispered by wind through willow—still, you could take a tram at the gate. In the Lattice at L4, the walls were screens and the screens were leaves; everyone wore festive helmets because that’s what the fashion guild had decided this year, and fashion there was binding for a month. On Ceres they traded in a script that looked like sea glass and was designed to remind you that money was pretty but slippery.

What we learned

- Power that can be refused is less likely to be abused. We hardened the right to say no. That made the yeses sweeter.
- Diversity is a system property. You don’t get it by hoping; you get it by funding exits, tolerating forks, and designing protocols that don’t collapse to empires.
- Digital minds are not our replacements; they are our descendants and our neighbors. They complicate everything in ways that make life bigger. They deserve baseline dignity and baseline chores.
- Space is not a blank slate. It is a mirror. The way you treat far habitats will be how you treat your neighbor. Build for humility—audits, ledgers, boring Thursdays. Leave room for festivals. Make sure kids get bored sometimes. The sky is better when the world below it has a plaza.

What held it together wasn’t a single “benevolent AI” or “world government.” It was that we thinned the places where one will could nullify another, and thickened the places where wills could bargain. We chose enthusiasm over obedience. We made asking for help light, and being asked to change graceful.

The Manyfold Covenant still fits on a page. Copies of it are carved into the ribs of an O’Neill cylinder, inked onto skin in a nightclub on Phobos, compiled into bytecode and run as a daemon in the hearts of beings you cannot see. If you stand at L1, you can read it off the wall and then look out the window and see Earth and Moon in the same frame, exactly the way our first signers did.

You could do worse for a constitution than a view that reminds you there is more than one world to love at once.
