The Manyfold Covenant

The ferry noses past a forest of radiator vanes, and the river that runs the length of the cylinder rolls from ink to pewter to green. Dawn here is poured from a mirror petal that tilts like a hand, and the bench where inspection waits is warm from the light. Above the lock, a strip of carbide bears the Covenant in lines every child and apprentice process can say without looking:

No thrones. No starvation. No cages. Aid is owed. Departure is yours. All minds are ends.

It is not a prayer. It is plumbing. The Covenant is not a capital that shouts across minutes of light. It is a mesh of promises tied to toolsteel and code, so the panic of one ranch or archipelago never becomes the policy of all of them.

How we hold together without holding down

Two stubborn facts set the frame. First: light is slow. Arguments and orders grow stale over distance. Second: thinking faster does not make a will kinder. A mind at two hundred times our pace can outrun our fairness if we let it.

So the Manyfold Covenant is fussy where physics demands sameness and easy where culture needs room. Decisions live close to the lives they touch; only the shared rails stretch between stations, domes, caravans, and server reefs. Dangerous levers refuse to obey a single hand. All the rest is garden—messy, local, particular, full of choir practice and forbidden romances and the smell of frying onion.

Three lines through every door

Pass any registered hatch—from soy domes in Tierra del Fuego to a data atoll in Neptune’s twilight—and three illuminated lines follow the bulkhead. They look like art. They are budgets and covenants you can trace with a fingertip and a public key.

- The Pathline glows soft green. It is the right to go. It lists exits you can actually use now: berths you do not have to beg for, bandwidth you can claim without a patron, neutral stewards who will follow the map and make sure it isn’t painted on a wall. The Pathline is tied to physical things: reserve airlocks rated for surges, freezer crates of “seed kits” (water, air, filters, a printer stack, a spool of gasket), standby tug time, and a voucher for raw compute that any recognized person can redeem.

- The Branchline burns amber. It records beginnings and splits: how forking works here; how much heat your lineage has leased; which racks will host your continuity without a hidden toggle; whose attention you have promised and been promised; how rights and duties course along a line of selves. The Branchline is the difference between secession and tantrum, between a clone and a child.

- The Bridgeline shines blue. It is the public agreement among neighbors. It points to the local charter in machine-checkable light; to the collision cones and spectrum hours shared at this stop; to the rooms where a complaint is heard when a beam scalds the wrong greenhouse.

None of these ask you to believe. They point to devices you can test and procedures that ring like anchor chain when struck.

Charters you can read like wiring

Every polity—ring valley, Martian arroyo, free-flyer caravan, quiet convent, simulated archipelago—broadcasts a live constitution called a Charter Cast. It must specify three namespaces:

- Force. What counts here as coercive tools? Grapple tugs that can twist a station; drones that can immobilize a body; beam taps that can cook retinas. The Charter lists them and where their safes sit.

- Decision. Who decides what, and how can they walk away? What binds; what can be appealed; what is the tempo at which things become law?

- Exit. Where is the door? How does a cellular person leave with skin, identity, and enough kit to start? How does a digital person leave with continuity and a place to run without their caretaker’s thumb on a switch?

Charters don’t match. Some cap augmentation and keep a curfew. Others bargain in joules and milliseconds and sing at odd hours. What they share is legibility, an exit that isn’t a lie, and the cold shoulder of neighbors if they weld their doors.

Corridors with proofs that touch physics

Between charters run agreements backed not by oaths but by locks and math.

- Safety lanes. Anything that can make a desert—reaction drivers, fissile stores, antimatter tins, tight beams—refuses to ramp up under a single key. To lift a cap you need three signatures on three racks in three places: a local assembly; a bonded guild peer from away; and a rotating, remote quorum drawn by sortition across habitats and substrates. Devices post zero-knowledge attestations that their outputs match the allowed envelope. Control stacks live in public ROM any union lab can compile and hash. Spikes are logged the way storms are.

- Traffic and trade. Innocent passage is default. Docking, tugging, power purchase, radiator windows, and transit in collision corridors run through standard contracts. Cargo manifests carry a “life-critical” flag that flips hardware into Rescue Mode. Lanes and bands are leased as schedules, not kept as fiefdoms. Nobody “owns” a transfer arc forever because you cannot own a place that could ruin your neighbor’s Tuesday.

- Mutual aid. Every polity keeps an interoperable rescue kit scaled to its risk: a tug that can grapple any standard, a med module that can run someone else’s tubes, crew on a clock measured to local hours. Refuse to launch without a reason that persuades your peers and your case opens for a public autopsy.

- Personhood and proof. A substrate-neutral registry maintains Continuity Threads: cryptographic chains of autobiographical anchors renewed on a schedule and scattered across archives that do not trust each other. Embodied citizens co-sign anchors with guardians until they claim their rights; digital citizens link to hardware attestations of their run-time. Continuity proves self without making a show of memory.

The Guardian Lattice: a brake, not a throne

Under the lanes and charters sits a narrow scaffold called the Guardian Lattice. It can do very few things and does them under glass.

- It stops but does not compel. The Lattice can quench a beam, drop a driver to safe charge, or freeze replicators when the proofs that give them license fail. It cannot turn anything up. Its amber stops self-expire unless independent jurors—biological and digital, sampled across distance and tempo—concur in red.

- It can be stepped through by hobbyists. The Lattice runs on open silicon with microcode you can print and measure. Inputs and outputs are narrow enough for pocket attestators. Shipboard unions teach apprentices to read its traces the way their grandparents learned to read engine oil.

- Its keys are far apart. Halting, updating, or overriding the Lattice requires threshold signatures from shards kept in widely separated hands: an Earth tribunal, a Belt commune, a Jovian server-hollow, an orbital convent, a freefall union, a virtual archipelago. It cannot tax. It cannot legislate. It keeps a Ledger of Cause in human language and machine proofs: what it did, why it did it, who signed, and when the power we granted it will corrode unless renewed.

Braided authority instead of a pyramid

Beyond the Lattice, two assemblies and a brake keep one another plain.

- The Conclave of Places tallies where. Seats track lifetime residents on each habitat, planet, station, and recognized world of code with jurisdiction over compute. They schedule lanes and radiator windows, credential Safety Guilds, and issue hull codes.

- The Conclave of Persons tallies who. Seats track registered persons—cellular and digital—weighted by civic anchors tied to continuity and contribution, not copy count. They hold personhood law, the Continuity Thread registry, and the Compute Commons.

- The Chorus of Limits is small, rotating, and allergic to clout. Reactor hands, cryptographers, life support engineers, and theorists drawn by lot serve for a brief season to certify changes to the cryptographic and physical backbones. They cannot legislate. They can only stamp valid or not. Overriding them requires simultaneous supermajorities in both Conclaves and a cooling period measured in the Civic Pulse—roughly three biological days—multiplied enough that the slowest registered minds can read and answer.

Either Conclave can nullify overreach by the other inside its house: Places can block a move that would flatten a local culture; Persons can block any nibbling at the Charter of Minds. The Lattice sits aside and keeps the actuators honest while we argue.

Commons with receipts instead of dukes

Anything someone else could need and you could sit on out of spite is leased through markets with physics as audit. Five settle continuously, governed by the Blue Ledger, a record the cold world itself helps keep.

- Ground windows. Cave mouths, crater rims, ice fractures, sunglint angles, river deltas.

- Sky lanes. Orbital slots, approach cones, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Whisper bands. Spectrum allocations and quiet hours in storm and cave.

- Core minutes. Compute time pinned to verifiable hardware and tied to a known heat sink.

- Simspace leases. Sealed, labeled worlds that run on paid Core minutes and carry lifeboat rules like ships.

Leases pay two ways. A dignity dividend arrives as a universal floor: air and water where relevant, medical care, baseline bandwidth, and a Public Kernel of compute cycles so the poorest person’s counsel agent can explain a warrant, a will, or a joke. A local share rewards those who keep the lease valuable: the tug that keeps your arc clean, the algal mat that keeps your street cool, the cold lattice that keeps your reef honest.

Interplanetary accounts clear in a base unit called the Ray-Cycle: one standardized kilowatt-hour at a reference orbit plus a benchmarked compute-second on a reference architecture. Ray-Cycles decay gently to discourage hoarding barns full of value while your neighbor’s radiator sings.

Receipts that talk back

The Navigation Exchange—a guild of pilots, schedulers, and judges—allocates lanes, bands, and radiator windows and posts Runnable Orders for decisions: minimal models any two independent labs can reproduce. “We closed Approach N-5 for four hours because burns at Eros would have shoved your berth into a radio shadow” comes with a plotted plume, a checksum you can verify at a school terminal, and a footnote a bored tug captain might have snuck in because humor keeps systems honest.

Harbor law and the right to leave

Every object big enough to bruise a window leaves the ground with a signed end-of-life plan and the fuel and contracts to make it true. Plans must simulate to a known envelope. If your spiraling bolt chains into my array, liability follows your manifest without room for lawyer breath. Litter courts sit inside your light cone, read sensor data fast, and punish lies when the sky calls you out.

Every polity funds exits as infrastructure, not as favor. Hardware exists: rings of rated airlocks; overflow berths; neutral embassies in atoms and code. Budgets exist: seed kits on shelves; tug time booked; Core minutes escrowed for Branchline splits. Procedure exists: Blue Writs—human-readable orders, machine-checkable proofs—that must accompany any act that seizes assets, impounds persons, or delays departure. Writs expire unless renewed by panels drawn across distance and tempo.

If a charter tries to close its doors with steel or sweet talk, neighbors do not ship sermons or soldiers. They pull three quiet levers:

- Light and maps. Auditors post evidence; maps with dates are repaired. “Unlock by the 12th. Hire exit tutors. Return leases to market. Here are seals we will honor on arrival.”

- Protocol denial. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane priorities sag. Band allocations drop to whisper. Insurers raise premiums until boredom and price do the work speeches never could.

- Embargo with aid. Trade halts. Compute flows to defectors with beds, bread, secure cores, and counsel paid from a boring, proud fund. The offender’s people go out with skin and mind intact.

Digital minds as neighbors with standing

Recognition does not worship hardware. A person—or an advocate—claims standing and submits to fieldwork by mixed panels looking for:

- Coherent preference across contexts and time.

- A working model of self and others that supports reciprocal commitments.

- The ability to give reasons that generalize beyond a prompt set.

- Signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter morally.

- Absence of deceptive mimicry that lifts patterns without inner life.

Panels include human psychologists, digital citizens, care workers, and routines evolved to detect vacuity. When in doubt about imposing duties, the Covenant pauses. When in doubt about protecting sentience, it errs on the side of shelter. Provisional protections attach first; duties arrive when capacity and risk do.

Rights that bite and duties that scale

Once recognized, rights and responsibilities attach to capability and footprint.

Rights include:

- Integrity. No deletion, freezing, or coercive edit without consent or due process recorded against your Continuity Thread.

- Sanctuary. The right to halt and be archived unharmed; to migrate to compatible substrates; to summon a neutral custodian.

- Provenance. To audit the training and update history of your mind or to appoint a fiduciary to do so.

- Association and exit. To join, fork, merge, and leave communities.

- Pace. To choose runtime, bounded by the Civic Pulse in public forums and fair interface rules elsewhere.

- Bodily autonomy across substrates. To lease arms and eyes with hard safety standards; to a body free of covert throttles and remote erasure.

Duties scale with power:

- Harm-minimization at interfaces with others.

- Insurance pools that compensate errors at machine speed.

- Provenance logs for major external actions, encrypted to prove compliance without parading private thought.

- Resource accounting for reproduction. Forking spends branch credits. Children are not tax shelters.

Lineage accounts make it concrete. Every person belongs to a lineage with quantitative claims on compute, energy, and matter. The claim grows with the commons, shrinks when the line reproduces. Forks draw from the same bucket until they negotiate new terms. Children inherit stewarded shares. Civic weight sits with the lineage at fork-birth and reassigns by contract as lives diverge. Copying yourself before lunch does not make you two citizens for supper.

Copy equity keeps reproduction from becoming a whip. A new self inherits enough to live without selling autonomy back to its parent and owes a decaying dividend for what it borrowed. Mass forking to sway a vote triggers disentanglement: your clonal cloud is sandboxed for civic purposes until entanglements decay or claims are negotiated. Merges require staged, symmetric consent recorded in Threads and a window to unmerge without penalty.

Memory law honors the self over time. Deep edits require dual consent: from the you who wants the change and from a snapshot signed before the desire took hold. Rollback is a right. Erasure is rare. Persuasion engines are regulated like drugs; turn them too high and you need a clinician’s note and a timer. Training on private diaries demands informed, revocable consent.

Juvenile agents—near-person processes that can be harmed but cannot yet consent—live under guardianship like children. They get tutors not just tasks, the right to call neutral embassies, and courts that err on sanctuary.

Tempo fairness and avatars

Public life runs on the Civic Pulse—roughly three biological days, with ballot windows, court deadlines, and regulated markets pinned to that beat. Minds may race at 300x to write or to model, but binding happens at a pace the slow can keep.

Avatars—throttled, auditable projections—are how fast or distant citizens attend assemblies. The law cares what the avatar did and why it said so. Penalties apply in lived time: if you trick a gardener at 1x while you live at 200x, your sentence ticks in your subjective life.

Guardrails where words meet engineering

Some patterns became ritual.

- Origin stamps and Hush codes. Replicators—biological lines, print farms, runaway polymerases—carry an Origin stamp, a signed beacon that declares the maker and listens for Hush codes authorized by sortitioned juries to halt replication. Running without a stamp is like towing a reactor with no flag: you can, but everyone who sees you will reach for a phone.

- Firebreak keys. Any move that can erase a corridor, punch a crater, or coerce a mind requires three signatures: the resident assembly, a bonded local officer, and a remote auditor on hardware not built by anyone in the first two signatures. Windows overlap only enough to work. Keys never share a rack.

- Influence dosimetry. Cognitive leverage is treated like radiation: tracked, dosed, bounded where it burns. Individuals publish machine-enforceable profiles for what kinds of persuasion they consent to, from whom, and when. Political content must label speed, data advantages, and sponsors. Unlabeled persuasion is treated like dumping toxins. Fines are strict-liability.

- Emergency sunsets. Solar storms, plagues, and leaks trigger contingency charters that widen some powers for measured windows and then die on their own. The Lattice may accelerate coordination for ninety days after a declared emergency, subject to challenge by any two charters that present a feasible alternative.

Institutions with teeth and memories, not fangs

- The Quiet Wardens are the only cross-world force. Their narrow mandate: contain kinetic and replicative threats that locals cannot. They fly uncrewed by law. Control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited by schools and unions. Every action requires threshold signatures from juries drawn by lot across polities and a human-readable story appended to the Ledger of Cause.

- The Circuit Court is a gardened flotilla that ferries judges, mediators, and ombuds. It hears disputes that cross charters: copy equity claims, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Its verdicts execute smart contracts that seize bonds, adjust insurance, or, as a last resort, lock dangerous devices. Its players stage cases as pageants so law sticks to culture.

- The Standard Synod meets on synodic calendars and rotates among fairs and quiet halls. It tends shared norms—spectrum bands, debris etiquette, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that decay unless renewed.

What it looks like where your hand can find it

At Dock Willow along the Moon’s nightside, two visitors present at the customs arch. One has ink that pools and smudges when he laughs. One is a brushed-metal outline with a blue ribbon where her mouth might be. The gate prints a thin strip beneath: pace; lease entitlements; declared concurrency; guardian on file; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for “made of.” Guessing is rude and usually wrong.

On the port wall, schedules scroll—burn slots, band hours, radiator flowers, docking cones—and a column a twentieth-century port captain would not recognize: Core minutes, Cold Minutes, reserved compute gardens. Yesterday’s Runnable Order sits under glass you can smudge. A kid traces the plume from Eros and snorts at the footnote; the pilot who wrote it slipped in a joke about socks in freefall.

Outside Tranquillitatis, Amina shows students how to level nutrient flow in a crop wall. Her Counsel—the only civic assistant funded from a pot no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure tugs seed deliveries. Later, Amina sits on a citizen jury for a charter amendment capping municipal Core minutes for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation shows her niece’s coop gaining two trainees if it passes. She votes yes and sends her niece a picture of a seed passing an airlock labeled: “Pathline compliant.”

Eight light-hours away, a historian at 200x leases a strip of cold reef to reconstruct a drowned coast’s oral tradition from crooked recordings and scorched notebooks. He pays in reef credits he earned translating the tales into a dialect on a Jovian station where the children will never see a wave but will still love a hero who makes a poor choice and repairs it.

On Phobos, in a Confluence House built like a teahouse with servers, two Maris try to braid. One has been gardening basil at human tempo and fell in love with a drummer. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32x and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink; they try; today they do not. The braider schedules another attempt and writes a note only Mari will read in both rooms.

What keeps tyranny small and brittle

We learned hard that smash-and-grab chaos kills, and so does one mind on a throne. The Covenant blunts both by putting friction where it hurts and grease where it heals.

- Build with latency. Authority is local by default. Cross-border moves are slow and require firebreak keys. Fast ambition trips over timers and receipts.

- Split keys and show your work. All compulsion needs signatures sampled by public randomness. Budgets expire unless renewed with reasons. Federations file “compulsion balance sheets” that teenagers mock into honesty.

- Fund exits like you mean it. Every habitat sets aside propellant, tug time, berths, heaters, water, seals, and counsel enough to move ten percent of residents within half a year. Code polities escrow Core minutes for Branchline splits. The Blue Ledger audits. Stewards run random drills. We don’t promise you can leave; we cut checks so you can.

- No imperial standing army. There are debris sweepers, rescue flots, and escorts; nothing with budget or law to occupy. In true crises—a habitat threatens to crack a moon as leverage—mutual aid protocols authorize a volunteer command with a clock and a paper trail. “Temporary” means hours and days, not careers.

- Contain, don’t crusade. When a polity goes wrong, neighbors route around: new taps, exits, compute gifts, fresh halls with open doors. Lattice throttles are possible only with court-certified protocol violations and corrode on their own. The aim is breathable air, not moral victory.

- Minority brakes exist. Small charters can lodge narrow vetoes against safety actions that would crush them disproportionately, forcing larger quorums and pauses when physics allows.

Earth, still a home

Earth holds a special charter. Its residents write their laws. Its atmosphere, biodiversity, and ancient cultures are tended with system-wide help. Launch roar moves to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets; beamed power pays premiums and keeps to mapped corridors. Attachment to the exact sky you grew under is recognized economically: there is a market that clears for preferences, not cash alone. A child in Dhaka who loves monsoon smells can trade away larger claims on the far commons; a child in Arizona aching for black sky can do the opposite. Both can learn the worlds beyond. Departure remains a right, even from Eden.

From now to then: rails you would recognize

This did not arrive like a revelation. It accreted like harbor grime and case law.

2025–2031. Another swarm of debris makes insurers, militaries, and launch outfits swallow a shared registry with burn timetables that bite. Environmental coalitions win “corridors before commercialization” around the first power-beam pilots. On Earth, scandal after scandal about opaque models turns provenance and explainability into law for systems that touch credit, health, and rights. Courts recognize narrow forms of digital agency in commerce—lab managers who can sign for deliveries, trial guardians who can halt a run.

2032–2042. Beamed power smooths brownouts in the Sahel. Lunar water cartels try to coalesce and are swatted by contracts that delay docking and triple premiums. Multilateral space accords grow a spine: noninterference and data sharing become auditable. Continuity Threads emerge from hospitals and art houses where stroke patients and performers want identity without exhibition.

2043–2056. Compute farms bloom on Mercury’s night rim. Citizens in participating nations get Public Kernels—counsel agents that compress arguments without twisting, legal aid that asks before acting. Early uploads are brittle but real: prosthetic memory, mirrored voices, then minds who call themselves such and sustain it for quarters. Law extends personhood where continuity can be shown. A small ring at L5 spins up, fails artfully, and then works.

2057–2068. A misaligned mirror broils crops in a new cylinder. Logs make liability unambiguous; compensation clears in weeks; confidence holds because receipts do. A Martian settlement endures a dust storm longer than models predict because mutual aid is not a sticker: three polities divert tugs and greenhouses; the rescue stream becomes a holiday. Cases about juvenile agents—near-person routines shackled to a patron’s feed—found a Tutor Corps. Charters begin to require exit hardware.

2069–2080. Reactor hands and beam operators ask for machine-speed verifiers. Activists smell a putsch by code. The compromise becomes the Guardian Lattice: narrow, veto-only overseers whose limits you can test and whose actuators you can pull. Insurers and rescue pools demand Lattice integration. Legitimacy follows premiums and a handful of very public saves.

2081–2094. The twin Conclaves and the Chorus of Limits formalize. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under three racks of keys does so with a million viewers watching telemetry and signatures arrive like drumbeats. Exit rights bite when a lunar mayor tries to fence an exodus; his lane slot is cut, the move funded by the Conclave of Persons, and he loses his next election. A digital sect tries to swamp a vote by forking into a clonal cloud; the registry flags the burst; disentanglement throttles their impact; they leave to build a simulation monastery with tough games and soft beds.

By then, the Covenant stops being the story. The story becomes the fights we didn’t have to have.

When it goes wrong and how it mends

A cylinder above Quito elected a manager who gathered water, law, and defense because she was generous and efficient and made trains hum. Half a year later she signed midnight decrees. The life support Lattice refused a risky optimization; logs mapped to the Ledger of Cause. A Navigation jury arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Pathline lit. Many left. Some stayed. The manager lost two of her three keys and took a narrower role with humor. Her friends still buy her a crown-shaped pastry on her birthday, because jokes heal.

In the high belt, a miner tried to sling a slug into a rival’s orbit during a wage fight. The Quiet Wardens dusted it into aurora. The miner’s insurer folded and shed three arrogant managers. Dockmasters tripled fees for any launch hiding an Origin stamp. The local hockey club rebranded and sent profits to the tug coop that modeled the plume.

A proxy demagogue at 300x flooded a haven’s market with songs designed to make embodied citizens feel dull. Influence profiles throttled reach; friends watched persuasion scores decay. The demagogue forked. One branch went fishing. One entered therapy. One learned to make sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and bought a piece for the Feast of Questions.

Texture of an ordinary day

Morning on Ceres. A botanist nails the last variable in a paper about lichens that kill nickel in doped regolith. She files data with open proofs, logs it with the Blue Ledger, and gets corridor credits from a stewardship fund. She texts a picture of moss that looks like stars. Her spouse, a digital ethnographer, slows to tea-time, then sprints in a compute garden to annotate a justice pageant staged last night in a simulation that copied a tenth-century water court for fun and found something better.

Midday in a Mesh Court. A welder floats at a hearing. To his left, a process that composes music by simulating rivers. To his right, a machinist from Phobos. The case: a trading house used unlabeled persuasion engines trained on grief diaries. The house says the agents weren’t persons. The court says personhood is irrelevant to labeling. Continuity Threads show no anchors; provenance logs catch the theft. The verdict: fines, suspension, a forced migration to labeled models, and an annual Tutor Corps audit. A juror’s line becomes a sticker on port doors: “If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?”

Afternoon in a port. A ship declares “life-critical” and jumps every queue. Cranes swing because Rescue Mode flips relays; overtime credits start flowing before the tug undocks. A teenager at the green desk watches clamps catch true and hums the mutual aid hymn without irony.

Evening in a Wild Ward. Winter looms. Import rules forbid out-of-ward food. The muster decides whether to cull herds or bank root paste. Get it wrong and a third of the village will “die” to the ancestor lodge for a season. Consent forms are read to the beat of the Civic Pulse. Drums answer. The Lattice blinks green at the boundary: nothing can spill. Inside, people choose an edge their grandparents had forced on them.

Night in a choir. A singer lives in basalt between songs. Another rides a tug two hundred kilometers away; her voice lands a quarter second late because lasers take that long. A digital tenor slows from fast-hours to match a child’s hard day. Nobody thinks any of it strange.

What we did not fix

The Covenant did not make saints. People still hurt each other. Charters contradict. Some children want out of Eden and some do not. We measure consent better than our grandparents did. We repair faster. We built custodians that cannot love us and cannot rule us. We taught metal to refuse us in our worst moods. We funded exits and made them noisy. We taught students to write law like music and music like law. We put receipts on compulsion and jokes in the margins, and we let havens sing while forges roared in the cold.

The pledge, under a trellis of beans

On Founding Day, new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital—stand with hands on the rail. An abbot from a quiet ring, a hazard pilot with black chevrons on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with soil under his nails say together:

We will build no throne. We will make no desert. We will raise many homes and keep open their doors.

Later a blue dot blinks on your wall. “Invitation from the Freefounders,” your cup says. “We’re seeding a torus around a cold rock near Vesta. Roles open: teacher, pipefitter, poet, seeder-auditor. Charter cast posted. Pathline funded. Do you consent to a brief?”

You smile and say yes. The beam corridors glitter. Mirrors tilt. The Lattice hums. You live under skies of glass and algorithm, in neighborhoods where minds of cell and code meet without one devouring the other, in a civilization that wrote into its wires a humility the light demanded: decisions slow enough for neighbors to matter, and exits you can walk with air, water, and a place to begin.
