The Lattice Compact

At the inside edge of the garden ring, the path tips just enough that soup stays in the bowl and kids on scooters lean into the turn. Past the beans and the trellis, radiator groves glitter like black ferns spilling their heat. A power sail etches a hair between sun and shade. Over every threshold—airlock, civic login, sim hatch—three small lamps glow the color of cat’s eyes.

- Doorward, a right-pointing chevron. It always resolves to a hatch you can open and a berth you can claim, without forfeiting your name, your work, your backups, or your friends.

- Bough, a stylized fork. You tap it when you intend to split your mind or reunite one. It walks you through the ritual that turns twins into kin instead of ammunition.

- Vector, a kite-tail. It lights a lawful corridor through jurisdictions that have promised the same floors and to show their math when they refuse or delay.

They aren’t slogans. They’re wires and budgets turned into custom. The system that hung them is called the Lattice Compact. It is not a throne. It is a diagram strong enough to reach from planet to habitat to reef of servers without welding them into one machine. It grew from two hard lessons: space makes orders fragile; speed does not make leaders kind. If you want a civilization that spans worlds and tempos—oceans and deserts, cylinders and caves, computation reefs and ships—without a hand at the center that tightens until it crushes, you make exit ordinary, power legible, and safety dull enough that fifth graders can check it.

Three ground rules

Every port, airlock, or login posts the ground rules in brass, braille, light, and sound:

- Steer what can hurt. You do not fling unsteered mass into someone else’s sky. You do not blind a region’s radios without a posted lane. You do not seed self-replicators outside licensed boxes without beacons and brakes. You do not run coercive models past posted thresholds outside audited vaults.

- The sky is common craft. Useful orbits, beam corridors, quiet cones around telescopes and “groves” of substrates where minds sleep and think in peace: these are leased with rents tied to physics, posted in a place a school can reach. If your beam cools a farmer’s dawn, you lodge a bond and the calendar will settle.

- Depart with help. Any being on the road to personhood can leave a charter without being trapped by debt, format, gravity, or grief. Exits have stewards, not sermons.

Rules alone are pretty speeches. So the Lattice came with small machines that are rude to ambition and easy to audit.

Wardens and the Citrine Halt

Wardens are yellow boxes with big fuses, plugs like knuckles, and glass you break. They sit in triplicate anywhere “oops” could cause a crater: mass drivers, beam actuators, atmosphere valves, replication stacks, city throttles. They know only one trick: halt.

Anyone with a civic key can trigger a Citrine Halt for six minutes if a simple posted condition is violated: wrong lane, wrong hour, wrong quiet cone, a gene printer without a tag. The halt drags machinery into narrow safety states and blasts a loud, signed “Citrine” into the Civic Log: which device, which timer, which rule.

Anything longer needs three hands on different clocks: a jury of locals drawn by lot from citizens who served civic hours this year; a remote arbiter running on hardware the locals do not control; and a drafted digital citizen who did not apply for the work and runs at a certified anchor pace. Keys expire in minutes. Receipts post to the Log. A “cringe” annex exists where sloppy overreaches are documented with humor that ends careers faster than hearings.

Every compulsion—seizure, restraint, firmware override—lands in that Log with who signed, which law was cited, and when the clock started and stopped. Insurers price from it. Teenagers read its funniest cases aloud.

Braided authority without a center

Power travels in braids, not stacks. At the Compact’s center is a federation with two assemblies and a brake:

- Routes Assembly. Seats for worlds-with-hulls and polities-with-servers scale to lifetime residents. They set logistics and infrastructure: beam schedules, debris bonds, maintenance standards, rescue covenants, docking and un-docking windows.

- Assembly of Persons. Seats for citizens across substrates, mostly elected with a quota by age, tempo, and substrate, some pulled by lot. They write rules for personhood, compute floors, fork-and-merge rites, attention zoning, and civic tempos to keep fast rooms from binding slow lives by surprise.

- Keykeepers Bench. A rotating board of engineers, jurists, and auditors with term limits that span decades; they stamp “valid” or “not” on changes to cryptographic bedrock, Warden firmware, and any interface that can push, cut, infect, or replicate. They cannot legislate. Logs of their meetings publish or get published.

Neither chamber can yank the other without help. Anything that touches Wardens or lifeline systems needs both and a cool window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to speak. If Routes tries to bulldoze a beam corridor through a quiet sky or steal a dark night from a monastery, Persons can send it back with a floor violation. If Persons writes personhood law at a tempo only reefs can read, Routes can say: not until lifeboats and telescopes are safe.

Ledgers you can point a school scope at

The Ephemeris Commons is an open, delay-tolerant ledger of orbits, beams, shadows, congestion, debris, spectrum, and stewardship rights. It synchronizes to physics. You can check it with a kitchen verifier and a cheap telescope if you have patience.

- Power beams and mirror arrays broadcast signed tags with ephemeral keys tied to hardware school clubs can audit. If your shadow grazes someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond that unwinds on schedule. Park in a crowded plane and you’ll lease lane-time, lodge cleanup deposits, and pay congestion fees tied to delta-v.

- Quiet cones live over observatories and over “groves”—server forests whose residents pay for quiet by law. The cones are public: angles, degrees, decibels, with posted bonds if you pierce them.

- Stewardship replaces flag-planting. To claim an asteroid or a reef of ice, you show surveys, beacons, cleanup orbits. Idle too long, stewardship lapses. Teenagers in Ulaanbaatar and Recife get small bounties for catching liars with scopes and laptops.

The Entropy Ledger counts what people forgot to count: joules drawn, heat rejected into certified sinks. Declare one self and draw like ten, and nobody calls you a monster; your leases just throttle until your draw matches your declaration. Grandparents at library kiosks love the public joy of catching a misdeclared sink and watching automatic rents bite.

Rents flow two ways. First, the Civic Floor—breathable air and clean water in habitats, basic care, local transit, a minimum of compute and bandwidth, and a no-questions exit grant every seven years—drips to every recognized person. Second, local yield—lane fees, debris bonds, shade rents—pays those who keep value alive: the tug crews who keep lanes whisper-smooth, the algae curtains that cool corridors, the reefs that dump theaters’ heat.

Nothing forever but leases

Anything you can sit on out of spite that someone else might need leases from the commons. Five markets clear in the open:

- Sites: crater rims, cave mouths, nickel seams, sunlit windows, and shade.

- Lanes: orbital slots, approach arcs, sail corridors, transfer gates.

- Bands: spectrum allocations, loud zones during storms, quiet cones when telescopes listen.

- Cores: compute on certified hardware tied to posted heat sinks.

- Worlds-in-Glass: closed simulations that must label their physics, exits, guardianship, and clock like a ship labels lifeboats.

The Pathwrights Guild—pilots, schedulers, modelers, judges, and pedants in one fussy profession—posts base rents, runnable reasons for closures, and bounties when kids find errors. “We closed lane F-3 for four hours because burns at Hebe would have pushed you into a radio shadow” arrives with a plotted plume, a checksum, and an economic impact graph a grandmother can recheck.

Exit is a schedule, not a scold

Every charter escrows tugs, propellant, berths, server minutes on foreign hardware, encrypted lockers, and trained stewards sufficient to move at least ten percent of residents—wetware and code—within six months. Receipts are public, recalculated each month, and cross-checked in the Civic Log.

Embassies are literal rooms behind ochre doors legible in suitlight. Inside are three desks that fit elders and octopuses and shy drone pilots: Departures where you book the physics of leaving; Mediation where you and a co-op or an algorithm argue with tea; and Founders where you borrow sample charters and run them in a simulation box that refuses to flatter. If a charter fences people with delay or debt, their beams gray, their bands go hush, their cores idle above the civic floor, and orange Kestrels—stubby, uncrewed interceptors with seatbelt-simple minds—arrive with cutters to unbolt docking rings if steel pretends to be law.

Digital persons are neighbors, not mascots

Personhood is substrate-neutral and unromantic. The Thread-and-Trust Standard asks for three things you can demonstrate without humiliation:

- Thread Guard: you can defend a line of experience you care about against unwanted edits; you can prove “I was me” across moves.

- Mirroring: you can model another mind’s claims on the world well enough that you would blush to violate them.

- Consent Fluency: you can understand, enter, keep, and revoke standard rights contracts.

Arrive by womb, by fab, by coaxing two thoughts into a third, or by careful reconstruction with consent. Pass, and your badge unlocks the same rights and duties.

Rights with texture:

- A thinking floor. You cannot be throttled beneath the Public Kernel—the minimum of cores, storage, bandwidth, and I/O required to think, speak, and be heard—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Privacy of your inside. Courts may compel proofs but not raw, intimate internals. Backups belong to you. No one scans, forks, merges, or deletes you without explicit, revocable consent.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs by default on a Common Beat—days and weeks, not microseconds. Anyone can summon a Harbor Room: turn-taking, rate limits, and interpreters for metaphor, mathematics, and smell.

- Body and substrate sovereignty. You can lease or build bodies—boats, puppets, drones—under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented hand is still assault. You need not cross substrate lines.

- Provenance of persuasion. Influence that aims to change law or purchases must carry who paid, what data fed it, and what levers it tweaks. Most people bind an Influence Filter to their Preference Keys: humor yes, grief no; analogy fine; timebox at fifteen minutes.

Duties that mirror rights:

- Thermo tax. Cores heat neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling cost. Heat that warms lettuce earns credit; heat that bakes corridors is a fine.

- Concurrency declaration. In regulated markets and elections, you declare your speed and active instance count. Hiding speed voids deals. A Lineage ID anchors one full franchise whether you run once or a hundred times.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed self-replicators outside audited boxes. Your tools broadcast beacons. When Wardens call a universal halt, you accept it.

- Simulation welfare. You do not simulate suffering for sport. Reconstructing a person’s likeness beyond a fidelity threshold requires licenses or consent from them or their heirs.

Forks, reunions, and lineages

Copies are not photocopies; they are kin with claims. Your public identity follows your Lineage across forks and pauses. A Lineage has one anchor vote for assemblies that represent persons. If you fork, you file a Twinning Notice with a hashed state, a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “field work,” “griefwork”), the compute lease that sustains the new you above the floor, and whether you plan to rejoin. One branch retains the civic anchor; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress.

Reunions happen in Loom Rooms. There is always tea, sometimes a dog, often a comedian. Engineers and therapists compare memory graphs with zero-knowledge tools that verify belief equivalence without spilling secrets. If tempos differ, clocks align gently. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, no shame. It’s called branch-ache and people bring soup.

Attention has neighborhoods

We zone attention like we zone streets.

- Quiet Quarters carry only navigation beacons and open science. No targeted persuasion. No fast models unless licensed like vehicles. Children stroll without armor for their minds.

- Debate Streets allow topic-bounded feeds and ordinary offers under public rate limits. Audience models belong to the town, not to advertisers.

- Carnivals invite the roar: slang, mashups, persuasion leagues under registry. You go here to be dazzled or tired on purpose.

Persuasion that hopes to change law files its homework in the Mirrors of Reason: plain claims, escrowed models, error bars. Students earn stipends catching cheats.

Teeth without a throne

The Compact has no big red “override” you can seize and keep. Its bite is narrow, boring, and placed where cheating hurts.

- Tri-Seals. Any stack that can push, cut, infect, or replicate wakes only when three signatures turn on different clocks and in different hands: a local jury by lot; a bonded civic agent on site; and a remote operator under foreign law on dissimilar hardware. Windows overlap enough to be useful; you cannot take a city at midnight with a firmware push.

- The Blue Layer. Quarantine valves, scrams for reactors, halts for infected fabs, vote pauses—control loops that cannot write policy and that time out in seconds and joules.

- Utility throttle. Hide exits, jam quiet, run coercion in a civic square, and your lane priority drops, your cores idle, your band turns to hush. In a world where heat is money and brakes wear expensive, this squeezes better than speeches.

- The Civic Log. Every compulsion writes before it bites when possible and after in all cases. Comedians read it onstage. Hubris hates laughter.

- Time-limited leagues. Federations around shared pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire by calendar unless they re-up with receipts. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills.

- Kestrel Fleet. Small uncrewed craft and magnetic curtains nudge mis-aimed mass into emptier orbits and sand runaway replication. Their models publish. Each intercept carries signatures by juries drawn across tempos and substrates. When Kestrels come home, schoolkids crawl inside with oranges and questions.

Warm Belt, Cold Net

We learned not to ask one place to bear orchards and supercomputers both. The Warm Belt—Earth, lunar caverns, Martian valleys, cylinders where saws sing in morning and brass warms at night—runs by embodied time. People come home muddy and unmonetized. The Cold Net—the main belt, the leewards of giant planets, deep shade beyond Neptune—glows with cheap cooling and computation. Upload polities and agent collectives run at higher tempos where an evening can hold a book. They slow to talk to gardens then sprint again when conversation ends.

The Light-and-Shadow Exchange ties them. Warm places keep quiet sky, set civic uplinks at hours ordinary citizens attend, cap speeds. Cold places sell “cold minutes” and weather models that aim storms away from the same roofs too often; they send art that sings across tempos. Credits flow both ways on chalkboard math.

Red Rooms and green tape

Risky work lives in Red Rooms: labs whose walls are built from lists of ways the world can go wrong. Rooms export one-way proofs to Wardens to attest safety claims without leaking secrets. Under tight public signals, Wardens can starve a Red Room of power in under a second. Twice that valve saved lives. Once it embarrassed everyone. We keep anniversaries with bread and ink.

Only one thing crosses charters uninvited: a Kestrel on a declared intercept with signatures you can check. Its budget sunsets. Its code is printed in books. Its return is a field trip.

An ordinary day

At Rim Market Three, Laleh’s bakery opens to miners, kids, and drones whose pilots like sesame and cinnamon. At nine, her Counsel (a dull, stubborn machine that won’t act without an explicit yes and can be turned off) pings a persuasion capsule: a proposal to add forty milliseconds of delay to beamed power crossing inhabited rings to damp rare cascades. Provenance is clear; humor is approved at low dose; grief triggers are blocked by her Influence Filter. She hits “sleep a day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Kestrel jury forms on her slate. A maker club’s tool cloud has drifted toward a trunk line at Hecuba Crossing. Options: glitter net, femto-sail herding, or guard drones to redirect. Jurors are a monk from a slow charter in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Quito, and an anchor-paced digital poet who smells faintly of clover when they talk. Dusting wins; the insurer pays; the kids get a mentor and pizza that tastes faintly of humiliation.

At noon in a Quiet Quarter, a wooden puppet lifts a bowl of broth to lips it does not need while its main self hums near Neptune at 200× threading a drowned library back into maps. Today they throttle down to share soup with their embodied spouse. After, they help host a Harbor Room while a cloister gardener explains why their charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely declines to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the market canopy shows stars and the Civic Log’s satire page scrolls down a wall. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; the apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts: sell us your winter for three years, we’ll pay your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a brass Kestrel and asks if he can join. Laleh says: when you’re fifteen, and if you can teach me the Citrine Halt without peeking.

How it stays honest

We make abuse expensive. We make dissent cheap.

- Split the force. Any act that bites needs three hands on three clocks.

- Live within latency. Duties past your hull move on slow beats under mutual consent unless physics screams. Emergencies have paint on their fuses and end on public clocks.

- Inspectable safety. Things that can kill must prove safe state continuously in ways students can learn and adversaries cannot spoof.

- Sanctions that squeeze function. Jam exits or lie on ledgers and you lose lane priority, cold minutes, spectrum, tug time. Your shipments lag. Your admirers get bored.

- Default fission. Founders hold thick charters for a time, then must split by district or craft and keep doors open. Port authorities hold exit capacity like lifeboats by code.

- Jokes. The fastest way to end a bad career is a well-sourced laugh.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A rim canton on Mars voted to deny full personhood to uploads. No one shipped sermons. Exit was real. Digital citizens left. A flood year came and pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a borrowed body coordinated three cantons through dust and cold with words everyone could catch. The vote thawed one clause at a time. The mentor from that year still teaches eleven-year-olds how to run a Junction Court.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival dome because wages were hot and pride was hotter. A Kestrel turned it to glitter that made a week of uncanny nights. The insurer dissolved. Directors learned that “coercion budget” is not a suggestion. They apologized and renamed their hockey team to say what they’d learned.

A demagogue blanketed a Carnival with songs calibrated to induce smallness. Influence Filters throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores decay like frost in sun. One fork moved to a fishing crawler and learned to tie knots. One started therapy. One makes sculptures that look like smoke remembering rivers and sells enough to pay a heat bill.

In a Red Room shadowed by Vesta, a bad sensor tripped the kill valve. Power died, the team sat on their hands and swore, then hung the failed part on a red string in a children’s museum.

Roads from here to there

This was not a miracle. It was receipts that compounded.

2026–2033. A second cascade of orbital debris kept children indoors and insurers angry. Launches had to publish ephemerides; rendezvous corridors went from whispers to posted schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” law for life-critical systems. The first AI persuasion scandals taught voters to demand source and provenance; regulators wrote the first, awkward rules. Courts began awarding narrow agency to digital systems in contracts when continuity could be shown.

2033–2044. Beamed power crawled out of labs. Environmental groups held out for “lanes before money”: post safe corridors first, then commercialize. A misaligned demo scorched cropland; public logs made liability simple. Compensation cleared in weeks because proof did. Cities and ports adopted coercion ledgers—drab at first, then addictive. People learned to ask for receipts instead of speeches.

2044–2056. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, powered by cheap solar and policed by a “thermo meter” anyone could read. Signatory cities carved quiet hours and named Quiet Quarters. Health improved. Tempers cooled. Early uploads were brittle but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and promise held over seasons. A demonstration cylinder in Earth orbit became an ugly, beloved field trip.

2056–2068. After a beam lit a greenhouse wrong at dawn and a mass driver test whistled too near a habitat, six-minute halts anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator became law. Activists smelled “coup-by-gadget”; engineers demanded concurrences and public code. The compromise hardened into Wardens. Insurers made them standard. Two saves on camera ended arguments.

2068–2079. The Lattice Compact replaced back channels with ledgers. The first Kestrels flew, nipped a stupid slug to glitter, and returned to be opened by children with oranges. The first clean personhood law was written in language good lawyers loved and fifth graders mocked. A Common Beat for civic time matured. Attention zoning—Quiet Quarters, Debate Streets, Carnivals—grew from old municipal quiet hours that saved sleep and elections.

2079–2090. The first attempt to swing a vote with a “clone cloud” met Lineage IDs, Junction Courts, and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and tears; lanes grayed, cores cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost, went home, and taught welding, and he tells better stories now.

2090–2102. The Light-and-Shadow Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold minutes to gardens; gardens bought quiet cones and human-pace windows; poets were paid in cold to build diligent archives. Red Rooms got their kill valves and their oaths.

2102–2114. The Keykeepers Bench refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The satire annex of the Civic Log grew teeth. A mission to unbolt an Embassy ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup and an agreement to disagree.

What space governance looks like most days

On the Moon’s nightside, a cylinder sets festival uplinks by hand count under lanterns. A reef in the Cold Net elects chairs by time-weighted escrow. A guild of solar tugs near Mercury writes bylaws in doggerel because it keeps meetings gentle. When a plan crosses borders with heavy consequences, a ritual called Three-Side Proof engages: the origin posts a runnable reason, neighbors post countermodels, and a jury from the Pathwrights Guild stitches the common parts and sends the rest back to be simplified until grudges can’t hide in pretty complexity. Decisions that bind slow lives ratify at slow speed. Fast drafts are welcome; fast binding is not.

How digital minds fit without swallowing or being starved

Uploads negotiate like neighbors: in joules, seconds, and quiet. They organize unions, sue, teach, and take tea. Many learn, smiling, that embodied friends value taste more than speed. Many embodied citizens learn that patience with clock differences is a gift that returns in kind when floods come. A shard raised as a lab assistant turns eighteen, files a Twinning Notice to run in parallel through a school and a reef, and separates years later with a warm ache. A cloister that caps cognition wins a court case because it explains its vows in algebra and in story and posts proofs of floor compliance.

This is not paradise. It is sturdier: arguments that do not pierce the hull, levers no one can hold alone, exits that are desks and timetables instead of poems, a promise that minds with threads and trust are neighbors.

A closing day

Arun turns forty-seven in a ring that smells of basil under warm LEDs. His partner lives mostly as a digital botanist and slows for breakfast to watch steam curl from tea. He spends the afternoon at Harbor pace teaching fourteen-year-olds to set Influence Filters and check Ephemeris entries. In the evening he enters Braid Mode with a reef collective to test a radiator algorithm he half trusts. Next week he’ll vote on adding micro-delays to beams crossing crowded orbits. He may say no. He may say yes. Either way, his reasons will be runnable enough for a child to poke.

Before bed he opens his Preference Keys and updates a clause about memory edits: not while grief is fresh; ask me twice; three sunsets apart; protect names. He scrolls the Civic Log—the only gossip column that matters. He watches a Kestrel teardown with his niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. He messages a friend in a quiet Mars valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. He slows to hear the oboes breathe between notes.

Above his hatch the three lamps glow with the patience we teach near traffic and tide.

Doorward. Bough. Vector.

Enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross. Enough to nurture many kinds of people without letting anyone become a god by accident. Enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power.
