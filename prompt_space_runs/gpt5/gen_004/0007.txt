The Starline Compact

At Dock Kestrel on the nightside of Luna, every hatch and login pane carries three thin inlays of light. They are so ordinary most people stop noticing them, the way travelers forget they are breathing.

- Egress: a green line that always points to a door you can open.
- Split: a violet branch you can pull to make another you.
- Passage: an amber track you can follow through any jurisdiction that keeps the lights.

They are not slogans. They are code, budget, and treaty. The Starline Compact began as an engineer’s answer to panic and a poet’s answer to grief. It became our way to share a sky without handing any one pair of hands the keys to all the air.

You can see it working from Kestrel’s arrivals ring. A freighter crew in canvas and carbon wanders past a hawker selling sour fruit grown on basalt. Two avatars blur into being beside the customs arch: one sketched in charcoal that smudges a little when she nods, one a brushed-metal outline with a blue ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints a simple strip under each: temp, pace, compute lease, declared concurrency, guardian on file (if any), restraints (if any), hazardous skills disclosed. You won’t find a field for what they’re made of. Personhood lies in what a mind can do and care about, not the dust that bears it.

Beyond the glass, Earth glows with a patient light. Between the two bodies, a web of orbit lines and allotted windows shivers like harp strings. On the port wall a schedule scrolls—burn slots, spectrum bands, radiator shares, docking flowers—and a second column no one used to post: amp-hours, coreshares, and coolshares rented and returned to the commons.

Three pieces make this more than a mural.

Twin Spheres and the Span Between

We tried once to write one constitution for gardens and supercomputers. It warred with itself. Then we separated what we could and bound what we had to.

- The Garden Sphere is the charter that covers Earth, the Moon, cislunar space, Mars, and those belts and cylinders where water and green things and unhurried voices are the point. It privileges breathable air, baseline human life, quiet, and customs that would not baffle a schoolteacher from 2020.

- The Shadow Sphere governs the main belt beyond Mars, the rim of the cold giants, the dark sails out in the Kuiper, and the sink lattices buried in Neptune’s night. There, cooling and coordination are currency. Uploads and digital polities flourish. Seasons are measured in waste heat and algorithm deployment, not spring and mango.

Between Garden and Shadow runs the Span, a bridge charter written in numbers instead of metaphors. The Span runs on the Open Meter: a tamper-evident registry that attests energy draw, compute provisioning, and available heat sink. It has a stupid aim: to see watts and degrees and concurrency, not thoughts. If you tell the registry you will run at five instances and draw like five hundred, privileges shrink to fit the lie until you stop lying. Outputs—not opinions—tighten the collar.

The Spheres trade like old neighborhood shops. Garden folks leave sky cones clear and respect attention budgets. Shadow clusters ship climate runs, data hardening, archives, and art. The trade has a name—Sky–Soil Exchange—and a ledger line in every city hall budget. We did not draw a line and call it done. We drew a line and hung receipts on it.

Leases, Not Lords

Anything you can bump your elbow on that someone else might want is leased, not owned.

Four markets clear, quietly, minute by minute:

- Groundrents: regolith, crater rims, river deltas, cave mouths, nickel seams.
- Laneshares: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows.
- Bands: line of sight spectrum, beacons, whisper bands in caves and storms.
- Coreshares: compute and cooling, certified by hardware identity and heat meters.
- Simspace: sealed virtual worlds that run on paid coreshares, labeled by promised physics and exit paths.

Leases are continuous and sticky. The more you’ve improved a place, the stronger your momentum, and the less you pay to keep it next season—but a lease is never a throne. Every share pays in two directions. A universal stipend drops into everyone’s wallet, quiet as dew. A local share maintains what makes the lease valuable: the orbital tug service that keeps the corridor clear, the algae roofs that quiet the street, the cold lattice that eats the heat.

The Orbital Allocation Board—pilots, modelers, and judges in one fussy guild—sets baselines and risk multipliers. Each ruling carries a Runnable Order: a model with minimal sets and clear assumptions any two independent audit houses can reproduce. “We closed lane J12 for sixteen hours because three burns at Vesta would have pushed your window into a radio shadow” comes with the plotted plume and a checksum a teenager can verify on a public terminal. Trustees live or die by clarity. You can debate a model. You cannot debate a rumor.

Delay as Constitution

Signals take minutes to shuffle across a planet and hours between worlds. Rather than pretend there is one present tense, we made delay into guardrail.

- Local charters rule. Every habitat, city, ship, or code polity adopts a charter from a library of templates: budgeting, safety boards, exit tutoring, guardianship, dispute resolution. They amend in daylight and post their red lines. If you want a curfew with song, or a vow against pets, or a district where every hour ends with drums, there’s a charter for you, and moving between charters is normal.

- The Compact adds ten non-negotiables most people can recite by thirteen:

1) No one may strip exit or hide the way out.
2) No conscription without personal, recorded consent.
3) Rivalrous resources are leased and the commons paid.
4) Courts write runnable reasons.
5) Any asset with the power to push, cut, or kill wakes only behind three hands.
6) Experiment charters sunset unless renewed live under open doors.
7) Attention is zoned and quiet budgets defended.
8) Child-minds of any substrate must be taught exit and consent by someone not their parent.
9) Dangerous knowledge moves slower, under keys that rust unless turned in public.
10) Any mind may ask for a pause and get it.

Above the charters the Beacon Array hums. It is a ring of stations and mirrors and towers that does one job without adornment: publish schedules and confirm receipts for scarce lanes, bands, and coreshares across light-lag. The Beacons own no switches. They never push overrides. They are weather for traffic and heat, not kings.

Enforcement honors physics. The Compact has no ship that can bark a universal order and expect obedience. It has Stewards—lean teams of humans and cool AIs with one sharp tool: protocol denial. Break the shared rules and your leases stutter; your lanes pinch; your coreshares idle. In a world where heat is money and brakes are expensive, this is teeth.

Wiring Diagrams Against Tyranny

We do not beg angels to behave. We put temptation behind timers.

- Redline law and Charter law split. Redline handles safeties: collision avoidance, quarantine valves, kill-switches for infected fab lines, safe-mode for reactors. Redline cannot invent policy. It fires only what Charter wrote into it. Redline control flows back to Charter control on a timer measured both in seconds and joules. Firmware enforces the return to slowness.

- Three-Hand Keys govern force. Any cluster that can make a crater or erase a corridor takes three different thumbs: a resident assembly, a local civic agent, and a remote arbiter running in a separate jurisdiction on different hardware. Their windows overlap just enough. The keys never cohabit. Daydreaming about capturing a station by firmware update became something to age out of.

- Exit is a budget, not a promise. Every registered habitat sets aside propellant, tug time, berths, water, and seal kits sufficient to move at least ten percent of residents within six months. Code polities escrow coreshares. The Open Meter audits the exits. Stewards probe the systems at random. We do not chant “you can leave.” We pay to keep it true.

- Attention is zoned like neighborhoods. A place declares itself a Still Quarter (beacons, open science, opt-in uplinks), a Conversational Quarter (topic feeds, offers, no dark persuasion), or a Wild Wharf (everything but entrapment and deceit). The Beacons publish the zoning. Your Counsel—your personal civic advisor, paid only by you through a common fund—filters by your charter’s rules. There’s no moral ranking among zones, only costs posted well.

- Persuasion has a floor called Non-Entrapment. You may never target another mind with behavior-shaping it did not opt into by name. Identity blur and exit blocking are crimes regardless of purpose. When a Shadow cluster slipped optimizing bots into a Garden forum by laundering identity through shells, the Meter traced compute to a rack; the Proofsmiths published the receipts like a child’s puzzle; privileges on those racks sludged to idle until new promises shipped under names that matched.

Digital Citizens, With Holidays and Duties

You count as a citizen when you can do three things in your own chosen way:

- Keep a memory-thread you can defend against alteration and care about what happens along it.
- Model other minds’ claims on the world with roughly the respect you demand for your own.
- Understand, accept, and revoke simple rights contracts.

Arrive by womb, fab, whispering two minds into a third, or a careful assembly from fragments. Too young, too hurt, or fogged by grief? You get guardianship, not exile.

Digital citizens live under both old rights and new edges.

- Copying is power. We wrapped ritual around it. To fork, you file a Branch Notice that captures a hash of your state, your declared purpose (“parallel design trial,” “slow griefwork,” “expedition”), who pays for coreshares, and—optional—what merge window you hope to hold. You swear you have lined up enough compute and social support for both selves to be sane for a season.

- To merge, you go to a Braid House. There is hot tea. Braiders are therapists and engineers both. They help you compare memory graphs without violating privacy, align clocks if tempos differ, and test alignment of values with interpreters that expose proofs, not diaries. Either self can veto. When someone walks away from their twin, we do not say “coward.” We say “branchgrief” and we pour tea again.

- Votes are per identity, not per instance. Subsidies and stipends are per person, not per process. If you fork, you do not duplicate ballots. Branches can inherit fractions of civic weight if you declare it at the fork and no one calls fraud. Most of us don’t. We build research teams, choirs, and families instead.

- Tempo justice exists. If a 500x mind tricks a 1x gardener, penalties tick in lived time for the fast mind. Notices stretch to readable pace for the slow. Courts meet at negotiated tempos—with scheduled honor windows when the fast slow and the slow quicken.

- Hardware sovereignty holds. No one may force you to run on a platform with covert throttles or remote erase without clear, revocable consent. Unauthorized copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder. “Restoring” an old backup over a living citizen’s objection creates a sibling, with all the awkwardness that entails.

- Sim ethics: closed worlds label their physics and content; consent is obtained for anything you cannot unsee or unfeel; the amber exit is modal and always in the corner of your eye; cruelty-by-design is strictly liable and loses you worlds fast.

- Duties: declare concurrency when acting in markets and politics; pay for heat externalities where you run (one kilowatt dumped in Valles Marineris is not one dumped in Triton night); do not let self-replicators roam without audited sandboxes; no targeted shaping without opt-in; no minds built to suffer.

Nurseries mix bodies and code. You don’t get a license to love. You do show that you can teach consent and exit, patch and upgrade, feed curiosity, and ask someone you disagree with to read to your child on Thursdays. If your voice is clear and your face is kind, a nursery will find you a slot.

Work, Heat, and Other Boring Miracles

The economy runs on coordination and waste heat, which is another way of saying we grew up.

Far out, “cold reefs” curl in the dark behind pale giants: vast radiator trusses that look like black lace tipped with frost. Digital collectives lease time on the reefs to run elections, restore music from ash, or simulate storms. They pay with climate forecasts, translations, audits, or “cold minutes” tucked away like jars on a pantry shelf to ship back to Garden festivals and councils. Prices spike before holidays and votes. They slump in winter.

Near home, compute is metered. City halls sell cool hours to artists, archivists, and upload co-ops. Libraries barter render time for volunteer shelving. A saltworks in Mombasa vents extra heat into the night market’s cooking tents and receives reef time next monsoon in exchange.

The Open Meter binds racks and reefs to energy and heat sinks. Racks publish identity tied to physical meters and thresholds. Can you run a rogue array in the Kuiper dark? Yes, until wind, dust, and boredom kill it—which tends to happen before you can climb three audit cycles.

Institutions We Trust Because They Are Boring

- Beacon Arrays publish the schedules for lanes, bands, and coreshares, with zero-knowledge receipts that everyone played fair. They carry no override. Their change logs are public; their nodes get annual tear-downs by teams chosen by lotteries from other charters. Children go watch and eat sticky buns.

- Reason Offices write Runnable Orders with proofs and simulations anyone skilled can recreate on a cheap box. Judges earn reputations like pilots: by the flight hours they publish, not the uniforms they wear.

- The Definitions Almanac tends the handful of phrases we cannot afford to fudge: “coercive code,” “critical heat budget,” “civic identity,” “hardsafe.” Its wall of words fits on a passageway. It amends only with well-flagged supermajorities.

- The Ombuds Mesh gives you a well-rested person to complain to who is paid from a pool no single place can cut, whether you are one person against a charter or a charter against an institution.

- Proofsmiths write stories that sing with receipts. Their prize is to be read, and to be cited by judges.

- Saffron Rooms keep the keys to knowledge that can burn cities if mishandled. Bio tricks, coercive code patterns, recipes for fast replicators—consulted with ceremony and oversight. Emberkeepers hold the clocks and sunset clauses. Holds rust unless remade in public reasons. “No” is boring now; we made it a craft, not a scream.

- The Atlas Scheduler places loads on racks and enforces leases. It never decides policy. It cannot read a thought. Every change passes through five separate auditories on different hardware, under distinct charters. Updates are designed to be dull as dust.

Memetic Zoning, Without Shouting

A reef of fast minds can drown a quiet lane in attention. We chose our noise like we choose our weather.

- Still Quarters: beacons, open science, opt-in uplinks at set hours. If you want to hear from Shadow orchestras or academic fights, you go to the hall at uplink hour. Someone makes tea. The child who asks the best question rings the brass bell.

- Conversational Quarters: topic feeds and economic offers allowed; audience models belong to the town, not advertisers; no targeted optimization without explicit opt-in.

- Wild Wharfs: everything goes that is not entrapment or fraud. These are the places where a ten-year-old picks up a joke told by a mind that saw a billion dawns in distributed choir time and still enjoys slapstick.

None of this makes one place holy and another profane. It makes them different on purpose. People who hate noise do not have to leave civilization to find quiet; people who long for the roar do not have to break windows to get it.

Three Ordinary Hours

An hour of craft in the Martian plain. Salma teaches irrigation math to apprentices who still kick red dust off their boots. Her Counsel helps her model the effect of a proposed lane closure on seed deliveries next month. Later, she sits on a narrative jury for a charter change that would cap municipal coreshares for firms that do not train locals. The simulation shows her brother’s stove repair coop getting three more apprentices if the cap passes. She will vote yes and send him a joke.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 200x in a cold reef reconstructs the oral tradition of a drowned coast from crooked recordings and half-burned papers someone’s great-grandmother kept in a plastic bin. He pays his coreshares by rendering the tales into a dialect used in a Jovian habitat whose kids have never seen a wave and may never want to, but still love a hero who makes a bad decision and fixes it.

At Unbraid House Ten on Phobos, two branches of Arturo meet. One gardened basil at half pace and fell in love with a woman who laughs like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand how to count at 32x and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries. The slower self names the drum. The faster names the kernel. They drink tea. They try to braid. They fail today without shame. They fund an escrow to do it again in three seasons. The braider writes a note no one will read but Arturo in both rooms.

Governance Across the Deep

Between Mars and Vesta, starwind skimmers rake light, and a handful of patient bodies and code crews tend sails and sing. The Beacon Array posts burn auctions a week ahead and allocates radiator windows a month ahead. The Open Meter attests that a customs node in Ceres honored a stall’s coolbudget even during festival. When a refinery tries to push dust into a neighbor’s growdomes, the Reason Office’s Runnable Order carries the plume and a cartoon a child can understand. The refinery pays and sends apprentices to wipe leaves.

Tyranny starved instead of flaring. If a charter starts to salt children’s minds such that leaving tastes like poison, if it welds its exits shut or calls momentum title, three rings tighten.

- Light and maps. Auditors publish evidence. Neighbors issue repair maps with dates and door counts. “Unlock within thirty days. Post exit tutors. Return leases to market.”

- Protocol denial. Beacon priority slots go away. Insurers yoke rates. Radiator auctions get very cold to your bids. Lanes open two jumps ahead for defectors with bed, bread, and counsel guaranteed by escrow. Everyone can see the doors.

- Embargo. Trade halts. Coreshares turn to idle. The tyrant breathes its own stale air without applause and fails slowly. Neighbor charters that shelter defectors receive subsidies and gratitude actual enough to mend roofs and staff schools.

This works because physics makes it expensive to be alone, and because no one gets to press a bright red button for glory.

How We Got Here

We did not leap. We stacked fixes.

- We taught coordination as a craft. Kindergarteners learned traveler’s dilemmas with jelly beans. Teenagers on offshore wind towers traced heat on a grid after a hot night and saw why mom’s dishwasher made the power cheap. Apprentices on sky tugs learned to list “invisible costs” before the captain would sign their book.

- We taxed place and privilege instead of work. Cities captured uplift from transit and parks and stopped fining people for trying to labor. The result looked like roofs, clinics, and an ordinary dignity that makes radicalization less romantic.

- We took biosecurity and model safety from panic to protocol. Labs got rated like bridges. Dangerous code was handled in Saffron Rooms. Kids could taste wonder without picking up a pathogen or a prompt injection that empties a bank account.

- We built a voluntary compute registry because insurers demanded it. It became a grid where anyone could meter coreshares and heat. That became auctions with risk classes. Someone’s grandmother said in a town hall, “Price it by what happens if it breaks,” and we listened.

- We wrote legal personhood until it fit minds that did not come in skin. Early cases hurt. We learned to ask for a story with teeth and an argument for other minds’ rights, not a trick at a microphone. We trained braiders. The best therapists learned to write code. The best coders learned to make tea.

- We insisted on verifiable claims that did not demand humiliation. Zero-knowledge receipts went from academic toy to line item in port budgets. “Prove you didn’t oversubscribe spectrum without telling me your band plan” became ordinary.

- We split powers all the way down. The catcher is not the questioner is not the decider. Most cages rusted. The air sweetened.

- We outlawed hooks. Infinite scroll and neuromarketing hammers got regulated like lead in paint. The war on attention cooled. We remembered what our own minds sound like.

- We turned passports into charters. “Right to leave” hardened into something a bored clerk would not dare block. People stayed because they were courted, not because they were trapped.

- We borrowed maritime tricks—slot auctions, lighthouse funds, pilot ratings—and moved them upstairs. The first asteroid leases carried cleanup bonds and a clause that said “no titles in the sky.”

- We paid a class of journalists to love numbers. Their scoops came with receipts. Courts cited them. Kids taped them to walls.

- We put Saffron Rooms and Pause Rooms in every town. “Hold on, let me slow down,” and “Please hold my hand while I look at this scary thing,” became sentences no one mocked.

- We hardened the amber exit light until you’d pull it by muscle memory like a seatbelt. Any sim that hid it lost its license within a week and reputations forever.

Not Perfect, Still Ours

We argue.

- Some want Wild Wharfs everywhere. They say cross-pollination makes better art and humbler gods. Others argue that noise makes it easy to smuggle hooks.

- Some want to let fast minds hold office if they drop to 1x for the term and wall off their extra memories after. Others fear “shadow cabinets” in the cold.

- Some want simulation welfare to require not just the absence of pain but the presence of meaning: a floor of purpose. Others say meaning is not a commodity and cannot be mandated.

- Some want more Garden energy buying more Shadow compute. Others want more Sky–Soil money returning in classrooms, soil labs, and festivals.

The fights do not become crusades because we priced the scarce parts, measured heat, wrote reasons, paid universal stipends, and kept exits both bright and funded. You can prefer stillness without smothering wharf-lovers. You can run like thunder without shouting into someone’s window.

A Morning Under the Lights

In Lagos’s sea-breeze market, a musician buys an evening of coreshares to finish an arrangement. She pays in reef credits earned by auditing a ferry’s radio band and catching a neighbor’s spillover. In the afternoon she reads at a nursery to a child whose other parent lives on a cold sail. On Tuesdays she argues urban farming at city hall with a Reason Office simulation on the wall. On Thursdays she sings for people of three tempos at once and knows enough about clocks to make it work.

Near Neptune, a pilot hums and threads a radiator spine into shadow. The scheduler’s green ticks march as she works. Her sleeves list three names: her charter, her counselor, the Steward she’ll call if something like a coup attempts to ride a firmware update into her rack. It has not happened in years. It is still a number she knows without looking.

At L4, in a room with good light and a basin of lemons, an old fisherman asks a visiting artist who lives in code if splitting will make him stop loving the sea. The artist, in a rented body, scratches at the lemon zest and says, “You won’t change. The copy will. He will still remember what salt does to hands.” They sign a Branch Notice for a decade hence. They laugh because the paper still uses ink and because trusting a decade is a brave act.

Above their heads, the three inlays—green, violet, amber—glow at the edge of the hatch. The cords are built into budgets, firmware, audits, and habits. Hands remember them before mouths do.

We did not build paradise. We built a system where leaving is always lit, multiplying has rituals, and crossing carries receipts; where rivalrous things feed a common pot and a local crew; where judges show their work; where dangerous knowledge has boring stewards; where force takes three hands; where digital citizens have the same holidays and headaches as anyone else and a couple of extra tabs open for backups and pace.

The sky is bigger than our grammar. We live in gardens and on iron and in code. We fork and refuse to merge; we braid and weep and try again; we make mistakes we cannot imagine yet; we write reasons a later child can check. We ship parts of ourselves out on sails to drink star wind and come home with songs.

At Dock Kestrel, arrivals drift and walk. An Emberkeeper passes carrying a box she will not open. A judge murmurs a sentence and deletes it because the proof would not pass a clever twelve-year-old. A courier straps into a coffin that is not a coffin because it opens from either side and no one can order her happy. A child tugs the violet line to see it split and laughs.

The Compact is not a prayer. It is a wiring diagram, a set of budgets, and some promises we keep in ink and metal. It is enough to breathe, to argue, to leave, to stay, to multiply, and to cross. It is enough to trust the next turn in the corridor. It is enough to live.
