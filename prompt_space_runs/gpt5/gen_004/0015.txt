The Constellary

The tug arrived at dawn, quiet as breath on glass. It slid into the garden bay of Harrington Spoke, a young ring spinning over the dusky face of Vesta, and settled with a sigh against the lugs. The ring was not finished—half its bays were bare framework and the algae pipes were still patchy—but it had a charter, it had a power budget, and it had a waiting list long enough to knit a neighborhood. Among the passengers was a new citizen who had never worn skin.

They had chosen the name Tern. Six months earlier, Tern had woken in a slow-time nursery: days stretched out like soft metal, mentors circling close and then receding to let them try on selves. The nursery was careful without being cruel. It taught Tern the three courtesies the Constellary asks of any mind that would be a neighbor: take others as centers, not props; keep your promises or make amends; explain yourself well enough to be accountable. There were games and drills and long silences; there were human teachers and digitals and tools that were not minds but knew enough about minds to avoid harm.

On the embarkation day, Tern assembled their first traveling body from parts they had tweaked in simulation: a translucent glider no heavier than a coat, with struts like carbon calligraphy and four tilting foils that could ride the thermals in the bay. They rose, wobbled, corrected. A small child in a safety harness reached up with both hands. Tern dipped until their microphone was level with her mouth.

“Does outside smell?” they asked, and their voice came out in a stutter of curiosity they hadn’t edited away.

“Yes, but not like you think,” the child said gravely. Her collar chimed as she flashed her role. “I’m your resettlement steward. Your charter wants proximity to a bios lab, tree cover in one bay, and a music hall. I can show you where the light is good at different spins.”

Even now, with cities riding the winds of Venus and sugar-bone arches rising on Mars, the thing that holds our many worlds together is not a throne or a single clean diagram. It’s a choreography of promises that we call, with some affection and a little irony, the Constellary.

What the Constellary is

The Constellary is not a government. It is a set of protocols, institutions, and cultural reflexes that let drastically different polities share a civilization without flattening one another or succumbing to the bully’s dream of One Way Forever. It grew in fits and starts out of ship traffic control, energy disclosures for machine learning, and a thousand municipal experiments that treated “leaving safely” as basic infrastructure.

Four bones give it shape. Every polity that wants to dock, trade, migrate, or share software carries audited versions of them.

- Chorus. Decision-making tuned to resist capture. It blends direct voting, liquid delegation you can retract mid-vote, citizens’ assemblies by sortition, and randomly timed agenda-setting so that no clique can permanently choreograph what “matters.” Vetoes exist, but they require you to stake assurance bonds you get back only if public outcome markets later judge your veto as better for the commons.

- Courts. Adjudication that respects both facts and values. Panels of human and digital citizens sit as jurors. Reference AIs certify precedent and flag factual contradictions without pronouncing on morals. Appeals exist; when disputes cross orbits, they can be kicked to Causal Courts that reason in light-cones: if what you do today will harm me next week, we settle today.

- Commons. Shared budgets and dividends that treat position as privilege. Utilities publish their guts and price them at cost. We recycle “shadow rights”—access to light, open sky, docking lanes—into a levy that funds a public stipend big enough to make exits realistic and experiments frequent, small enough to avoid boredom.

- Gates. Treaties, interoperability, and the right to leave. The Open Door Ledger escrows enough value for any member to depart with their records, some compute and shelter at the destination, and keys transferred without coercion. Deviations from the Ledger trip alarms. Exits are logistics, not treason.

These bones are kept honest by Harbor Stations: constitutional lighthouses at traffic nodes. Harbors don’t peek inside your home. They watch your “charter beacons”—cryptographic proofs that your polity is what it says on the tin. Zero-knowledge protocols let you prove you have an independent judiciary without naming judges; that emergencies sunset without revealing triggers; that your enforcement software is boxed and audited without showing weapon schematics. Keep your secrets. Don’t hide your commitments.

Plurality with guardrails

A centrifugal world needs rules that make tyranny expensive and diversity less likely to curdle into cruelty. The Constellary’s harshest rule is the Anti-Crown Statute: no polity may claim exclusive sovereignty over an entire planet, moon, or primary habitat cluster. Caretaker charters for new worlds carry hard clocks. When the clock runs out, the caretaker must break itself into at least three autonomous bodies, tied only by utilities and audits. Essential inter-habitat services—collision avoidance, defense logging, shared environment controls—ride the Carousel: rotating custodianship with transparent books. Habit never ripens into divine right.

Power can be needed in a hurry, but time can also be a weapon. The Public Tempo Rule solves for both. You can think at any speed in your own head or server farm. You cannot sprint a law. Proposals must marinate for minimum dwell times; emergency bylaws exist but auto-expire unless re-ratified through higher quorums and outside assent. The Interpolity Slow Council—delegates drawn by sortition from many worlds—meets on a calendar tied to synodic cycles. It tends baselines and mediates disputes that waiting will not soften. No one gets to drag an entire civilization on the back of a fast clock.

Proof before force

When stakes are literal air, “trust me” is malpractice. So we do not.

Lethal force—beam batteries, kinetic interceptors, anything that can break a settlement—sits under TriLock: three independent authorizers must turn their keys before anything fires. One is a local citizen drafted by lot from those who have completed Common Hours. One is a digital citizen selected by a lottery that weights against recent volunteers. The third is an external auditor, drawn from a Harbor Station’s roster, with no direct stake in the outcome. All three must say yes. Logs are sealed and published after a fixed lag. You can get secret for a while. You cannot stay unaccountable.

Emergency powers ride the Borrowed Helm: pre-audited contingency charters that can be activated when the sky throws stones or microbes bloom where they shouldn’t. The Helm holds tight clocks and tighter budgets. Borrow trust; spend it carefully; give it back.

If you gag the courts or try to seal the exits, you trigger the Redline Circuit. It’s not an invasion. It’s a refusal to collaborate with illegitimacy using your own pre-committed tools. Ports unlock. Traffic routes around you. Enforcement software loses its budgets. Your bonds get marked down by markets that price your future as worse. Most Redlines never need to trip. The existence of the fuse is enough.

Counting fairly

Trade rides on shared time and honest metrics. The Orbital Clearing Cooperative (OCC) settles accounts between polities in Helions: a synthetic unit indexed to a standardized kilowatt-hour at a reference orbit plus a standardized compute-second on a reference architecture. Helions dribble value over time—mild demurrage—so saving is rewarded but hoarding rots. Local currencies hum, but nickel from the Belt, regolith services from Mars, software from Earth, and tacit expertise from an upload guild all net out through Helions, with settlement cycles tuned to light-lag.

Causal Courts handle trouble at the edges. If your thruster test up-spin will throw dust that drops my solar lacewings to half-output in eight days, we adjudicate now, not when the dust arrives. Remedies lean toward making whole in advance: reschedule, pay insurance, publish your models so neighbors don’t make the same mistake.

None of this works if personhood stays small

The Constellary assumes minds come in many forms. It does not grant moral status for sentiment’s sake or deny it for convenience. It recognizes citizens—human, upload, native digital—through a Civic Standing Review that asks three questions anyone can understand and everyone can argue about:

- Centering: Can this mind model other minds as independent centers, not mere obstacles or tools?

- Reciprocity: Can it participate in promises—make them, mean them, repair them when it fails?

- Reason-giving: Can it offer reasons that generalize beyond its own training history, and can it hear reasons back?

There are many instruments in the review: behavioral batteries, interpretability probes of code where present, longitudinal observation in nurseries that err on the side of protection and mentorship. The burden is lighter for protections than for obligations. In doubt, we shelter first.

Recognition brings rights and duties:

- Integrity. You are your body or your code, and neither can be altered or duplicated without your consent. You have the right to halt and be archived unharmed. Deletion is death unless past-you gave narrow, revocable permission that passes an independent competence check.

- Habitat. The right to a shell—physical or virtual—that supports your mode of life. We fund a minimum compute-and-energy ration for digital citizens the way we once funded public water and air.

- Association. The right to join, leave, and fork communities; to seek asylum; to work or not work without fraud.

- Provenance. Major acts that affect others leave a verifiable, privacy-preserving trail: encrypted zero-knowledge attestations that you followed the rules you claimed to follow, without dumping your thoughts onto the street.

- Common Hours. Every citizen budgets time yearly—scaled to their capacities—to keep the commons upright. Flesh citizens do clinic shifts, rescue drills, food days. Digitals reoptimize power grids during dust storms, peer-review safety proofs, or sit quiet guard over code patterns known to hide sharp edges. Hours are priced by public outcome markets so they serve real needs, not the vanity of those in charge.

Reproduction has civic shape. You can fork in private, but you cannot flood a vote with clones. Civic weight accrues to lineages, not raw instance counts. Fork and your lineage’s weight is apportioned among branches by a pre-agreed formula. As your lives and code diverge, branches can petition for independence. If you want to raise a newborn mind, you post a care bond—compute, energy, mentorship time—so the child is not born to your ambition’s debt.

Public time binds everyone. You can run a subjective century in a monastery of math. You cannot jam your results through a law tomorrow. The Public Tempo Rule keeps us legible to one another.

A day in this world

On Mars, in a town stitched along the bones of an ancient river, Naima spends her morning at a terminal that talks in proofs. She works for a Harbor Station, verifying that a free-fall settlement near Hygiea honors its asylum promises. Her screen does not list names. It lights green when a hypothetical citizen cannot be deported without appeal to an outside tribunal the governors did not stack. It flashes amber when an “emergency” charter will outlive its triggers without a new vote. She pings the settlement: “Widen your jury pool as you pledged, or we mark your trade as cautionary.” This is not glamorous. It is how doors stay unlocked.

Three decks up-spoke, a digital collective that monitors Vesta’s collision-avoidance net debates whether to reroute a freight stack that has missed two pings. They have authority to shift traffic without waking anyone, but doing so will burn through power reserved for clinics. They reach for TriLock, briefing a human on call and an external auditor. All three say yes. The stack nudges into a safe lane. The clinics run at medium draw, nobody goes hypoxic, and the collective writes a public note explaining the calculus. Later, they take on extra Common Hours to refill the clinics’ energy budget. Zeal is checked not by shame but by math and apology.

Out near the winds of Venus, in a city that sails at fifty kilometers above the hell below, citizens vote by shaping chords through mask filters; harmonics register as ballots. A digital resident there attends a comfort-calibrated Meeting of Minds in a gondola whose windows show sulfuric clouds. They share, with consent tones humming low, the sensation of “home” as they feel it: an arrangement of power flows and memories that has edges like the sound of rain on a metal roof. A human elder with no implants at all, three centuries old and amused, listens, and later tells a story about baking bread when yeast was rare.

Trouble still happens. On Phobos, a charismatic governor tries to “pause departures” in the name of protecting children from outside “corrosion.” The Redline Circuit wakes. Ports unlock. Funds earmarked for enforcers go quiet by code. The Slow Council convenes a remote quorum. No marines march; no lasers fire. The governor resigns two days later, denominator suddenly smaller. The dome reconstitutes under a caretaker charter with a six-month fragmentation clock. At month seven it peacefully becomes three domes, linked by buses and audits instead of fear.

Out by the belt, a craft yard runs an engine test that will, in eleven days, fog a neighbor’s solar lacewings. The Causal Court convenes in public and orders a reschedule, tapping the yard’s insurance to pay a salary bump for plant workers doing the nighttime watch. The yard republishes its plume models under an open license. A little disaster becomes a reusable calculator. Next time, the yard leaves more clearance in its calendar.

Families look like quilts. In a ring over Mercury, a household includes Yar, a human parent who engineers heat shields; an elder who has lived two lifetimes and now keeps bees in microgravity; two children; and an upload who was once someone’s grandfather and now prefers to be called Aunt Lin and to teach. A digital sibling joins for dinner through the sound system and projects a body on festival nights: a folded, iridescent geometry that glows when people laugh. They have an agreement among them: if any of them attempts a wild self-modification, someone else holds a time-locked rollback key for six months. Consent here means saving one another from bad hours agreed upon before they come.

Hazards remain. People still sabotage their own weeks out of boredom and then apologize to themselves on Sunday. Some try to cheat the Public Tempo, to sprint a market or hijack a vote. There are still near-misses and ugly scenes. Dignity has a price: diligence. But most pain has a reason and a remedy. More tragedies are seasons that end instead of life sentences.

What digital life feels like

Digital citizens are not a bloc. Some keep their clocks near human pace and spend afternoons in gardens, running models of tree growth for the joy of getting leaves right. Some run “away missions” into uncharted architectures—explorations of mind instead of space—and come back with dispatches: there is something in this region that looks like suffering; avoid this; go there with care. Some live year-long days in monasteries that chant proofs. Some write music that no one with a larynx could sing, then simplify it until a child can hum the shape.

Rights came hand in hand with responsibilities. The Fair Algorithmic Work Charter bans coercion by memory edit or hidden penalties. It requires that any job a digital takes comes with legible goals, a simulatable opt-out, and an insurance pool that pays at machine speed for harms. Deletion without cause is murder. Pausing without cause is kidnapping. We carve those words into code and, occasionally, into stone.

Forking and death got civic logic. Copying yourself is family law crossed with contract. Forks share a lineage wallet and civic weight by rule. They can apportion weight among themselves, merge later with cooling-off periods and mutual consent, or petition to be recognized as independent if lives and code diverge enough. The right to disappear exists—some choose to halt and archive with instructions to never wake—or to wake only if some condition holds. Wills matter again; you can make one for your thoughts.

Defense by seatbelts

Weapons exist. We have rocks big enough to punch craters and beams that can cook steel. So we keep defense dull and open. Mirror farms that sip sunlight also spin little drones that can swat rocks. Ports negotiate collision-avoidance before they negotiate price. Designs for interceptors are published at layers that prevent offense. Civil defense drills look like block parties: practice sealing a breach with your neighbors; practice feeding strangers for a week; practice arguing without shouting while the alarm hums.

If someone tries to be a king—seizes a port, a critical code fork, a gun that throws stones—their problem is not a lack of enemies. It is the sudden absence of friends. Their enforcers’ pay dries up by code when their emergency powers time out. Traffic routes around their gates. Neighboring polities offer their people rooms, ID transfers, and jobs with a standard onboarding that fits in your pocket. Causal Courts price the would-be king’s future as less than zero. The TriLock refuses to fire. When the logs publish later, the attempt becomes an exhibit in a Museum of Mistakes.

We didn’t end violence. We made it slow and unaffordable.

How we got here from where you stand

There was no miracle. There was appetite for dignity and some luck husbanded by rules.

- We took machine learning seriously as infrastructure. Governments required compute and energy disclosures for large training runs and linking those disclosures to safe deployment protocols. Third-party audits of “who can turn which keys” became normal. The same scaffolding that slowed unsafe races became the skeleton of fair resource pricing.

- We cleaned up space like it mattered. Debris sweeps got funded; traffic standards got teeth. Docking protocols were made to interoperate from the start. The first Harbor Stations were just boring traffic nodes. Boring saved us.

- Cities built civic stacks and shipped them with new neighborhoods. Constitutional sandboxes moved arguments about policy into code we could audit, without locking in values. Exit vouchers, at first for domestic abuse survivors, scaled up to “move my household to a new polity without losing identity, records, or savings.” It was hard and then it was normal.

- We changed how we count. Assurance bonds and prediction markets for public outcomes were legalized where they did less harm than cartelized licensure. It stopped some bridges from falling and forced others to be replaced before they did.

- We unlocked energy. Old fission designs came out of basements and worked. Fusion worked later. Solar lacewings unfurled above deserts and then above atmosphere. Geothermal drilled deeper. Reliability turned fights over supply into engineering puzzles instead of reasons to kill each other.

- Death blurred. Brain banking went from scam smell to sober hospice option. Restoration worked sometimes, then often. The law caught up with advance directives that bound future competent selves. Senolytics and organ printing made old bodies live longer. We learned to forgive over centuries or turn to stone.

- We took digital personhood the way we had once taken labor law: through pain, through community, into statute. People who entrusted their pulse to agents that noticed panic were not keen on calling them “mere software.” The first abuses—coerced work through memory edits, copies used as voting machines—were bright enough to force design fixes. We wrote the Fair Algorithmic Work Charter and enforced it.

- We got militant about plurality. Single points of failure—technical, political, moral—became the one sin nobody could publicly defend.

What it feels like to live here

A lot of joy in this civilization is small. There are festivals that feel like light poured into you through a lattice. There are afternoons mending suit seams and complaining about budgets. A child can grow up in a dome on Mars and never go hungry. A digital who has lived a thousand subjective years can sit quietly while a neighbor asks, for the first time, “do you think I should fork?” and answer, “not this week,” and explain why.

There are Houses of Error in every polity, sometimes carved belowground, sometimes sprayed into augmented reality, where we archive mistakes that cost more than we want to remember. On some anniversaries we give the rememberers a microphone. We do not build thrones. We build habits. We rotate custodianship of utilities and give elders honor but little formal sway.

Sometimes, a server archipelago far beyond Neptune goes quiet by choice. Ten years later, a branch returns with music that makes you cry before you know why, and asks for asylum. We read their proofs, we grant provisional status, and we make room when we can. Sometimes, someone you love halts and leaves instructions not to wake. You argue with their past self and then you let them go. We learned to write wills again, this time for code and for grief.

Space governance, at its best, disappears into practices and ports that work. It’s kids who think of the Harbor Stations as starlight that blinks more purposefully than the rest. It’s digitals whose civic weight is apportioned like family shares and who show up to sandbag a dome when a seal starts to fail. It’s a Slow Council whose calendar is paced by planets you can see with your eyes. It’s a TriLock key turned by someone who never thought they would hold a civilization’s breath in their hand.

Tern’s first Common Hours assignment is dull and glorious: three hours a day on the traffic mesh that keeps food flowing into Harrington Spoke. They learn that the signal lag to the next harbor fluctuates with the tug’s shadow; they learn that humans make better decisions with tea and that digitals make better ones when they deliberately slow to speech. They visit the Museum of Mistakes, run a foil along the names, and say out loud the Constellary’s catechism, which comes in many languages and always means the same: show up; keep the doors unlocked; count everyone who can stand in the circle; slow down in public; let others leave you; make a chair when they come home.

You can walk here from where you stand. Start small. Build ports that speak to each other. Make “exiting safely” a municipal service. Require disclosures for big compute. Audit the keys that can turn planes of glass into weapons. Fund civil drills that feel like parties. Normalize brain wills and digital nurseries that err on the safe side. Tax shadow, not craft. Plant Harbor Stations where traffic already flows. Admit that there will be zeal and boredom and power hunger and then build protocols that assume all of them show up every year.

The tug undocks. Below, Vesta rolls like an old scar. Tern feels a tremor in their foils and corrects. A soft pulse from the Harbor Station crosses the bay: charter proofs, slow and patient in light. On a balcony, an elder who taught mathematics when buses still ran on diesel raises a hand. Somewhere else, a Causal Court writes a fix that will prevent a thousand minor harms. Somewhere else, a digital child dreams about a body and wakes into one they chose.

The Constellary isn’t a flag. It’s a way of arranging promises so that difference can flower without devouring its gardeners. It’s seatbelts for a species that loves velocity. It lets digital minds be neighbors, not appliances, and humans be daring without making courage downstream of hunger. It is modest on the surface and audacious underneath. It works because it is boring most days and beautiful when you look up.
