The Weave Accord

The transfer skiff from Hygiea settles into Bay 3 with a purl you feel in your ankles. The air tastes like ginger steam and clean ozone. Under the pressure arch, set into a bar of river stone flecked with mica, four touch pieces wait, smoothed by thousands of gloved thumbs and bored children.

- Way Slate. A flat ceramic that wakes when you lay a palm on it. Routes unspool in units your budget can carry: which ferry has a berth, which bunk will be warm, whose scrubbers are honest this week, what bandwidth you can bring without kneeling, and the name of a neutral steward if you get into a tangle. The audit for that steward is pinned to a code recipe you can run at the library.

- Split Bangle. A ring of woven steel that opens your Line Ledger when your mind forks or plans to braid. It shows what dignity any child branch inherits by default, who your fiduciaries are if you run hot, the off‑ramps you wrote when you were calm, and the duties that travel with your franchise.

- Charter Window. A pane of smoked glass that glows when the local law prints cleanly at a level a ninth‑grader can read. Bright dots mark where emergency timers start and sunset. Another dot is the Exit Beacon. Touch it and an off‑site desk will tell you, without hurt or heat, exactly how to leave with your skin or your checksum.

- Frost Feather. A pale metal leaf, cold when quiet minutes are cheap. It shows radiator windows, hush arcs over schools and markets, and what rate the station is renting “winter”—cold seconds tied to named fins you can point a kitchen telescope at.

They’re not ornaments. They’re wired to pumps, berths, warrants, and escrow. Together they hang the habit we live under from Luna pits to server caravans beyond Triton: the Weave Accord, a way to braid many kinds of neighbors across distances and substrates without sanding them smooth or letting the quickest buy the air.

What pushes must answer, and how

Anything that can shove a stranger—mass drivers, main beams, amplifiers large enough to trample a market, replication clouds, even charm engines in public venues—sits behind an Amber Handle and a Threefold Lock. The rules fit on a glove.

- The Near Key lives here, with someone you can talk to: a dock warden this month, a teacher next. They’re picked by lot from people who tightened bolts, changed filters, or scrubbed algae lately. Their reasons land in the Plain Reasons Ledger in words and math you can redo with a pencil.

- The Neutral Key belongs to a bonded safety guild that does not profit on the device it certifies. Their processes are published like recipes. Their books live under law on a different world.

- The Far Key roams on slow arcs. A small panel, half embodied and half digital, drawn by sortition across places and tempos, holds it on hardware under foreign law. Serve once and you can’t touch that class of lever for a long time.

Between the keys and the actuator sits firmware signed by the Anchor Bench—a rotating guild of cryptographers, pilots, process gardeners, and ethicists allergic to adjectives. The Bench cannot legislate. It stamps valid/not on changes to public code that touches breath and big velocity. Overriding its “not” needs both houses of the Accord and a cooling window long enough for a monk in dust and a reef in dark water to read twice and object.

Above the lock squats the Amber Handle: a fat, ugly bar in a yellowed box with a wax seal and a glass fuse the size of your thumb. Anyone with a civic key—after taking a greasy, boring class and passing a test a kid can ace with a neighbor’s help—can slap it when a posted rail is about to break: a beam creeping into a hush cone, a slug pointed without a docket, a seed fog drifting toward a corridor. The machine drops into a published safe envelope for exactly six minutes on a clock nobody can goose. A signed note appears in the Plain Reasons Ledger—what tripped, whose hand, which timer—and all three keys burn. They grow back under sun and time. To hold longer, all three must turn again.

There is one cross‑polity actuator with teeth and no appetite: the Veil Swarms, uncrewed skiffs that throw foil, glitter, and magnetic curtains. Their minds are narrow and published. Every throw carries split signatures from mixed juries across substrates and tempos, an escrow posted in a named currency, and reasons a teenager can recite without choking. Once a year schoolkids crawl through their bellies with lamps and rude questions. The answers must fit in mouths, not just code.

A sky and a ledger you can check

You don’t take a portmaster’s grin for proof a cylinder will miss your farm. You check the Open Ephemeris: a delay‑tolerant log pinned to physics. It posts:

- Track plans, approach cones, and de‑orbit bonds you can verify with a kitchen scope and a club spreadsheet.

- Hush arcs and celebration windows over rings and nights.

- Beam corridors and their lullabies—timing and jitter so a neighbor’s sleep isn’t a rounding error.

- Radiator minutes and “winter sheets”—cooling windows a garden or reef can rent or sell.

- Core‑seconds pinned to named fins in shade, with racks and addresses you can visit labeled in plain.

Thrusters and amplifiers attest their honesty with short proofs: “this plume stayed in that cone,” “this beam met that lull,” without spilling secrets. If a mirror steals a neighbor’s dawn, shade bonds post and pay if the crop wilts. Runnable orders drive traffic: “Arc H‑12 closed for four hours because two burns near Vesta would push your ferry into a blind.” You can re‑plot at home. If your check doesn’t match within posted error bars, you call a steward and then a paper; either way, someone answers.

Cooling is a commons. Under the Ephemeris hangs the Winter Ledger: radiator minutes like tide tables. Hearths buy quiet nights; reefs sell cold; poets collect winter to build stubborn archives. Settlement clears in Helions—one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one vanilla compute‑second on a named rack in deep shade. Helions decay gently over years so barns of them don’t become dams when someone else’s lettuce wilts this week.

The pegboard under those ledgers holds three more tools:

- A Provenance Registry for code and models that want to touch lungs, steel, and budgets. Funding, methods, and data earn stamps. Teenagers everywhere earn stipends for catching cheats.

- Origin Marks for anything that self‑copies in shared space. Clouds that seed parts must answer a Freeze Call like drivers answer a siren. Miss a call and your insurer bleeds until you remember how to be dull.

- A Plain Reasons Ledger. Every act of compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, cited law, a paragraph your aunt can read, and proofs your niece can run. Next to it, a column called The Margins hosts ridicule sharp enough to end careers without prison.

Two tempos and a contract

Close to bodies, where heat sinks are dear, sit the Hearthways: Martian caverns damp with mint and stories, lunar tunnels cool as cellars, wheel towns where tomatoes climb strings above warm pipes. Embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a wedding; you cannot buy a shortcut through a migrating corridor. Children get muddy and unmonetized.

Far where nights are cheap sit the Night Reefs: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee, Trojan schools humming around server stacks, hermitages in the Kuiper. Watts and milliseconds matter. Digital polities keep time like drummers.

Between them sits the Quiet‑for‑Cold Exchange, renewed like a marriage. Hearths cap civic speeds and carve quiet arcs; Reefs sell winter minutes and models that keep storms from finding the same roof twice. Audits fit on a school slate. “A week of hush over Recife in exchange for two winters near Uranus” sounds like poetry once. Then it becomes pockets and schedules and babies who sleep.

How we govern without a capital

We braid; we don’t stack. Two houses walk at right angles with a keel between them.

- The Council of Terrains seats places with air to scrub, docks to schedule, and fins to aim: planets, stations, caravans, and server barges that own and cool their cycles. Voice scales to lifetime resident‑hours and legibility: publish your actuator map, pass exit drills, show your hush plans, and your bell rings louder. The Council tends physics: rescue customs, track standards, docking collars, radiator leases, beam corridors that don’t cut through prayers, and accreditation for safety guilds to hold Neutral Keys.

- The Register of Lines seats citizens—cellular, digital, and stranger—by continuity thread, not meat or clock. One voice per autobiographical Line across forks and pauses. The Register tends personhood floors and concurrency rules, stewards the Civic Hearth—baseline air, water, honest cycles on a dull stack at anchor pace—keeps civic time on the Public Beat, and writes ethics for simulation and care.

Between them sits the Anchor Bench, the truss that stamps changes to dangerous public firmware, the Amber Handle’s logic, and root cryptography. The Bench cannot command. Overriding its “not” takes both houses and a cooling window long enough to cross a light‑hour and a harvest.

When physics makes nothing local—persuasion that soaks across charters, what a warrant must endure in delay, how to weigh winter against quiet—a Bridge Table convenes, half Terrains, half Lines. If either side can say “below the personhood floor” or “you bulldoze my pipes,” a measure fails.

Public life walks on the Public Beat: days and weeks, not minutes. Write your opera at 500× if you like; you can’t yank a water budget before a baker can read at wall pace. When a fast process wrongs a slow farmer, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions that bite in both wall hours and subjective beats so you can’t dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

Exits with handles

Freedom isn’t a hymn. It’s inventory, drills, and a desk with a bell.

Every charter that wants lanes and winter keeps two lifelines that auditors can kick.

- The Green Doors are physical: surge‑rated locks with seals you test and log, reserved berths that cannot be booked “forever full,” propellant on retainer, gaskets you can print when a boss locks a shed, and crews on contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and backups—within a season. Drills are rude and random. Fail enough and your lanes jitter, your radiator bids cool, your bands dim. Bread, counsel, and winter still arrive. The muscles that bruise fall asleep.

- The Silver Loom is digital: reserved compute and storage off‑site on boring racks under foreign law. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up continuity at a humane beat even if a local council tightens its fist. Cut the Loom and you discover that tools that can hurt people—beams, lanes, mass drivers—get sleepy until humility returns.

Behind an amber door, the Outbay stocks Founders Kits that smell like oil and oranges: minimum safety code, docking templates, escrow hooks, bylaw samples for people who want to be small and kind, and three contacts who built a town last year. The Departures Desk books berths, replicates meds, locks tools in neutral escrow, and pairs you with a tutor who is not your boss. Leaving isn’t betrayal. It’s circulation that keeps cultures from congealing into cages.

Noise, quiet, and the map between

Influence shares physics with sound. We zone it.

- Harbors carry navigation, emergencies, child time. No persuasion here, not even for charity.

- Commons Strips allow campaigns and argument, but require provenance on funding and method. Each citizen posts an Influence Profile: “diagram yes; no grief levers before breakfast; satire okay; my clinic records are not bait.”

- Bazaars invite roar on purpose: satire tournaments, persuasion labs, and art that bites. Label it, rate‑limit it, and you may show off. Hide a grief lever and you pay strict‑liability fines until you stop. Tools watermark outputs so a retired nurse can tell a deepfake from a poem.

The digital neighbors with standing

Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry.

You or your advocate file at a Line Desk and meet checks a mixed panel can run without humiliation: therapists, ethicists, mature digital citizens, and a slow routine trained to pierce theater without cruelty. They don’t seek souls. They test for:

- Line Defense: signs you can defend the thread of “me” you care about against edits you don’t want.

- Consent Sense: that you can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Othermirror: that you can model a neighbor as a claimant of the same kind and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Hurt Signals: that your injury and joy show in stable ways that give moral reasons to care.

Protection in doubt errs generous; burdens wait. Rights hook to personhood and substrate; duties scale with footprint and risk.

Rights that bite:

- Integrity of thought. Courts can demand proofs, not diaries. Backups are yours. Writing over a living self makes a sibling, not a cleaner. Deep edits without consent are assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. No one throttles you below the Civic Hearth without due process that prints its own warrant and sunsets on its own clock.

- Substrate choice and sanctuary. You choose your hardware. If you and your host disagree, a neutral custodian migrates you at a humane pace.

- Tempo fairness. Live fast or slow, but public binds anchor to the Public Beat. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Lease or build bodies under codes like road and workplace law. Assault on a rented limb is assault on you. A covert throttle in a shell is like drugging a town well.

Duties that scale with reach:

- One Line, one franchise. Votes and civic weight attach to continuity, not concurrency. In regulated rooms you designate an Anchor instance that holds your duties and pace.

- Declare concurrency. Above thresholds—and always in elections and brittle markets—you disclose live civic‑weighted instances and speeds. Hide clones to tilt bids and your deals void like lying about weight on a bridge.

- Branch credits. Lineages earn credits with years lived, service to commons, and careful footprints. Those buy new full franchises above the floor. Bursts near ballots trigger civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close fragile markets until disentanglement ends or a court says “proceed.”

- Heat tithe. Compute warms neighbors. You owe back in coolant stewardship, captured joules, baseline code, or vegetables grown on your waste heat.

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Large external moves—deploying persuasion models into Commons, starting a replicator—carry sealed logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety rails without dumping diaries.

- Clean simulations. Reconstructing a living person past a bright threshold needs their consent or a narrow warrant. Creating minds who can suffer binds you to care and their right to refuse.

Splicing and weaving happen in places with wood and water. Before you branch, neutral mentors—barred from conflicts for a decade—check you understand budgets and duties. Branches file a purpose, lease cycles that won’t starve neighbors, and pick a braid window if they hope to rejoin. One branch keeps the civic Anchor; others wait through disentanglement while a Junction Court checks for duress. Weaving back happens in Weave Houses: cedar joists, a dog asleep, water you can hear. Compatibility checks compare belief graphs without exposing private diaries. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk without shame. If they braid, promises and debts braid forward. There is a word for the ache when versions part—line‑ache—and there is broth for it.

Institutions with reach and little hunger

- Veil Swarms nudge slugs, stretch foils, and glitter what needs to lose its teeth. Their burns carry jurors’ signatures and runnable reasons. Teens earn badges mocking sloppy math.

- The Traveler’s Court rides slow arcs with gardens and a school. Judges, mediators, and ombuds settle copy equity, weave divorces, export fights that touch three worlds. Sentences seize bonds, alter insurance, slow corridor priority, cool radiator budgets, and, last resort, lock named interlocks. They share soup after. It helps.

- The Almanac Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, triage for drills. Its standards expire unless renewed in daylight by both houses. Sunsets long enough to help; short enough to shame if extended for convenience.

- The Tutor Guild mentors juveniles—flesh and code. Graduation requires a rescue drill, a corridor audit, and two weeks explaining checklists in a public clinic.

Sanctions shaped like physics

Nobody sends troops for paperwork. The Accord dims windows, redraws lanes, and cools markets. Weld doors, hide a Tutor, refuse to post reasons, and your lane priority drops, your cores idle, your bands thin. A dull fund pays defectors’ tickets, racks, and counsel. Leaving happens with receipts and stew. Hurt someone across worlds and you meet a jury whose clocks you cannot game.

Scenes you can taste

On Wharf‑Nine, Yara flips mung pancakes for miners and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame even though they don’t need to eat. Her Counsel pings a draft: insert 45 milliseconds of jitter into beamed power crossing dense orbits during solar storms to damp feedback. Provenance is clean: three plots, one ugly cartoon, labeled error bars. Yara’s Influence Profile blocks grief hooks at breakfast. She taps “sleep a day,” laughs when the rented body asks for extra chili, and will vote after tea.

At ten, her slate blinks: jury duty. A makers’ club’s fog of microfab seeds drifted toward a power trunk near Hecuba. Options: glitter with a Veil, herd with femtosails, nudge with slow drones. Jurors include a monk in Isidis who tastes each word, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad whose camera shakes when they laugh, and a historian speaking at anchor‑pace through a cedar puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a licensed mentor and a pizza that tastes like lesson.

At noon in a Harbor square, the puppet lifts broth to lips he doesn’t need while his main self hums near Neptune at 200×, stitching a drowned library into maps and arguing with elders who won’t budge on sky color. He slows to anchor pace in a Slowroom to cohost a Harbor Hour while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. Someone asks him to speed up “just to keep up.” He smiles. “No. We chose the Beat on purpose.”

Under cedar in a Weave House, two branches of an engineer meet with oolong and an interpreter: one wrote a sail control loop and wants to braid back; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. Empathy loops hum. They cry. They hug. One walks. The ruling prints dry as a manual and kind as an apology.

At dusk the canopy plays stars. The Plain Reasons Ledger scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish; the jokes are ruthless and clean. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your hush with interest. A kid palms a thin foil Veil and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a pause without your notes,” Yara says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sauce.

When it bends instead of breaking

A cylinder over Quito adored a manager who made trains sing and orchards glow. She gathered water, tugs, and auctions because she was efficient. A winter later she printed midnight decrees. A safety clerk refused a “harmless optimization” on air sequencing; the refusal logged itself. Beacons strobed. Doors opened. Many left. Some stayed. Her keys rusted for a decade. She kept a narrower job and better jokes. Friends tease her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A valley on Mars voted to deny digital standing. No sermons left Earth. Exits were stocked. Digital neighbors left with their Cold Minutes. The flood years came; pumps failed. An anchored upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through dust with words old ears could catch. The vote thawed clause by clause. Relief tasted like oranges.

A trading house carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small and tired. Profiles starved the slop. Insurers priced the spill like radiation. One branch of the composer bought a fishing crawler. One branch consented to therapy and wrote an album about boredom. One branch built better lures with warning labels and won a harmlessness prize.

Twice a Glass Ward—a lab where replication and cognitive hazards live under glazing you can inspect and cords you can pull—went to zero. Once it was a false positive; we wrote better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once the silence saved a ring. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask questions without covering their mouths. Mentors answer without magic.

A miner lobbed a slug to make a point in a wage fight. A Veil threw glitter. Nights on Ceres looked strange for a week. The insurer evaporated. It re‑formed without the directors who called Origin Marks “fussy.”

Earth keeps her seasons and her say

Earth writes law like a continent, old and wet. The rest of us help with rails: loud launches slide to sea tethers; beamed power keeps to mapped corridors and pays into shade and river repair; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Accra, Patna. Attachment to place—monsoon, fog, desert—prices into claims on the larger commons. A child in Dhaka who loves wet wind trades larger off‑world claims for local care; a child in Arizona dizzy for black sky trades the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil. So is staying, eyes open, hand on a Stop you understand.

How we walked here

We didn’t wake into the Accord. We backed into it with receipts, bruises, and kids who liked to run the math.

2026–2033. Low orbit looks like sleet. Insurers pay for junk they pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demand open track plans and de‑orbit bonds anyone can check with a backyard dish. A mis‑aimed beamer scorches barley. Because amplifiers shipped cone proofs, liability is plain and paid before fury can gather. Black‑box models deny mortgages and parole. Cities write “show your math so a seventh‑grader can check it” into contracts for buses and water plants. Clinics deploy dull counsel agents that explain benefits and court dates without lying.

2033–2042. Grid hacks dim a coast; a reactor near‑miss ends well because a dumb watchdog trips. Voters agree: code near lungs, roads, or orbit must be open or in a trust anyone can examine. Thrusters get first Threefold Locks. An Amber Handle idles a driver pointed wrong for three hot minutes. The docket prints numbers a child can read. Drills become a fashion you can admire without irony.

2042–2052. Heat spikes kill grandparents. Cities rent cold nights from racks in shadow and underground. Outer‑system servers realize shade can be a service. The first winter markets pay for radiator gardens that look like lace. Renting nights the way we rent lanes feels sane.

2052–2060. Early uploads are brave and brittle. Courts find continuity and consent before seminars do. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” writes juvenile rights into code and funds a Tutor Guild. A woman who lives mostly in code wins tempo fairness so her baker father can keep up. Houses of Weave open because you need wood and water and a slow dog when you cry.

2060–2070. Attention zoning matures the way cities learned to calm nightclubs. Neighborhoods carve out Harbors and sleep. Elections dull and clean when persuasion has to wear a label. The habit “emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight at human pace” crawls into law.

2070–2082. Lease markets climb to orbit. Land value taxes that built schools come back as corridor and radiator leases that pay sweepers and algae farmers. The Council of Terrains and the Register of Lines formalize, with quirks that annoy people who like to count. The Anchor Bench earns reverence by saying “no” with runnable reasons. The Veil Swarms fire three times: twice against lying replicators, once against a manager who ticked “emergency” twice. After that they spend long bored seasons in hangars while kids learn to open their logs with oranges and questions.

2082–2095. A canton denies digital standing. Exits are bright. Digital neighbors leave without a riot. Flood years come. Pumps groan. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinates like a radio in a storm. The vote reverses in pieces. Clauses are dull. Relief comes before blame. By then departures are drills and Founders Kits smell like oil and oranges. It stops feeling like a manifesto and starts feeling like doorbells and receipts.

Why it holds

It holds because hurry has no teeth. Anything that can shove runs slow by law. Because secrecy in compulsion expires on clocks you can see. Because warrants print reasons before they bite and again after. Because keys won’t cluster, exits are funded, and the muscles that bruise sleep when bullies squeeze.

It holds because a child with a school slate can catch a liar in orbit. Because we learned to love boredom near physics. Because we split the hands that shove: three different keys on three different clocks, and a brake that can pause but never aim.

It holds because everyone breathes and thinks at a humane floor. The Civic Hearth—air and water for bodies; first‑line care; open civic bandwidth; honest cycles and storage on a dull stack at anchor pace—is not charity. It is the price of a civilization where “no” isn’t just a poem.

It holds because departures are invitations, not betrayals. A stubborn church that fenced exits with shame lost lanes, then found its grace under a bell made of loved ones walking out with lunch in their bags and tears in their eyes. They added “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Attendance rose because consent smells like trust.

What it feels like

You feel watched in the kind way: by neighbors with a right to know, by ledgers you can run, by brakes that can pause but never steer. You learn to shift tempo—the snap of an Amber Handle; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; a river teaching boats. You argue in your second language because it slows your wit to where listening lives. If you live mostly in code, you visit the Anchor Tent and ache sweetly at turn‑taking with bodies that sweat. Evenings are jokes and small favors. Mornings are proposals that become dull law five years later after cartoons wear off.

On Founders’ Night in a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular and silicon and saltwater—press their palms to a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. A hazard pilot with triangles on her collar, a judge who smells faintly of lilac and spends most of his time in code, an abbot who whispers more than he speaks, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under her nails read together:

- We will keep doors ready.
- We will split hands on force.
- We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.
- We will remember that minds are ends.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Free Rim Hands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden under Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Hygiea. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tip. Radiators open like pale ferns. The Amber Handles hum, bored and content. The Way Slate, the Split Bangle, the Charter Window, and the Frost Feather glow like small moons.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of people—flesh and code—without letting either become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to us and what public logs do to power.
