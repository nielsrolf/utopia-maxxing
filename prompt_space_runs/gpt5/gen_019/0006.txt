The Lattice Compact

On the inside rim of a turnwheel where lettuce climbs a trellis above a groaning bearing, a ferry thumps the dock and someone in a cheap jacket laughs at a joke that has to do with valves. Beside the lock there are five insets, scuffed from gloves, warm under your palm from the panel behind them. You see them in ports from Tycho caverns to the carbon-shadow caravans beyond Uranus. They look like folk art. They are not.

- The Hatch Mark: a square with a shallow notch. If you press it, an exit plan unrolls in numbers you can spend—berth windows you can book without favors, propellant already reserved, scrub time, a compute shell hosted somewhere your boss can’t throttle, and the names of three stewards with licenses you can verify. It also shows the last evacuation drill with a timer you can check on a kitchen slate.

- The Loom Ring: a braided circle with a bright split. Touching it opens your lineage ledger: when you can legally fork and the cost in heat and storage, who the approved counselors are, how to braid back if you want to, and the time boxes that prevent someone from forking a hundred copies of you the week before a budget vote.

- The Sky Plate: dots and tiny arcs etched into steel. It glows the approach lanes, hush cones, thrust windows, and debris bonds for this volume of space. A thirteen-year-old with a garage dish and patience can verify that a declared burn matched the plan.

- The Frond: a thin fin inlaid with copper. It ties trade to actual dissipation—joules in, joules out, where the heat went. Cooling is not a rumor here. Fins and wells are accounted for like bridges.

- The Quiet Circle: a bell without a clapper. Pressing it does only one thing: it starts a six-minute, audited slow-down on gear that could bruise a stranger—thrusters, drivers, big pumps, mass printers, persuasion rigs. It prints its own reason to the public log before it cools anything. Overreach lights you up on a list that insurers hate.

They are wired to budgets, firmware, and drills. If you unscrew one, half a dozen boring people show up with binders and a sweet tea and ask you very precisely why.

The Compact grew out of a single insult we stopped laughing at: distance doesn’t care about charisma, and speed isn’t a virtue. You can’t run one stove for five light-hours. You can’t count participation by how fast you think if your neighbor still has soil under their nails. So we wrote four spine rules, and we bolted them into machines.

1) Split the hands on anything that pushes hard.

2) Put scarce windows on ledgers a child can check.

3) Make leaving cheap and test that claim when it’s inconvenient.

4) Pace common life on clocks everyone can hear.

Danger on three keys, and a brake that only brakes

Anything that can reach out and hurt—kinetic launches, beamed power heads, habitat momentum wheels, self-feeding fabricators, model launchers that can shove an election—wakes only if three different consents arrive on different clocks. The interface is called Triplex Consent. You will be bored by it on purpose.

- The Near key lives in the place at stake and rotates by sortition among qualified maintainers this quarter—tug masters, life support techs, triage nurses—folks whose gloves actually smell like gasket. They don’t serve twice in a row. Their instructions compile to something you can recompute.

- The Far key is held by a safety guild whose income doesn’t vary with throughput and whose own audits live under a different charter. They publish why a class of lever is judged safe or not this season and how the test burns will be monitored. They can refuse and they do, often, with receipts.

- The Roaming key is carried by a jury drawn by lot across habitats, planets, caravans, and digital polities. They serve once, for a term measured in months at embodied pace. There is almost always someone on that panel who lives slow on purpose.

Every activation prints a stub. “Class-B mass driver, 9.8-ton packet, +1.0 m/s along lane J-5 at 02:13 station clock; thermal rise at Telescope-K hush belt expected <0.2 K.” School kids argue these over soup.

Threaded beneath all this is the Amber Latch: a three-stack brake that cannot aim, spend, hire, or accuse. If two independent stacks (closet boxes with sealed cells; municipal fiber that carries no other traffic; watchdogs hosted under other polities) agree that a posted rule is being broken or that stubs don’t match telemetry, they idle a device on a curve everyone learned in first-year shop. It prints to the Mandate Rail: which rule, which lever, whose hand, which clock, signed by which stacks. It dies on its own clock unless someone renews it with three clean keys and a very small sermon. The code is ugly and public. The levers are under glass you could smash with a wrench.

If the brake trips on junk, you get an apology, a part on a museum string, and a better drill. If it trips on malice, your port windows narrow, your beamed power cones lose priority, your radiator minutes wander somewhere better behaved. No soldiers. Just boredom with teeth.

Ledgers that bind to physics

Space isn’t pasture with fences; it is cones, wakes, and cold. Anything you can exploit out of spite lives on open books.

- The Orbital Almanac is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident ledger of lanes, approaches, thrust windows, sail arcs, deorbit bonds, and what an object said it would do before it did it. Hulls sign tiny attestations with keys tied to hardware teens can audit. If your burn doesn’t match your mouth, escrow empties without a courtroom.

- Heatbook tracks energy in and heat out—the fins that bled it, the wells that drank it, the algae curtains that bloomed on it, the baths it warmed. Draw beyond your lease? Throttles pinch until metal and math kiss again. Cast a long cool shadow on a neighbor’s radiator zone? You pay hush hours.

- The Trace Registry binds provenance to code and persuasion at civic scale. If it touches breath, money, ballots, or brakes, it wears a label: who paid, what data trained it, what levers it can pull. Two auditor houses on different worlds carry proofs you can replay. Catching cheats is a scholarship program and a hobby with jackets.

- Beacon Charters broadcast—in radio and light—what a place promises in emergencies, which keys it splits where, how to find exits, and what word or knock means “stop.” Keep whatever dialect and vice you like; do not lie about your valves.

- The Mandate Rail lists every official “you must” and “you may not,” mirrored on kiosks, garden walls, and slates. It carries start times, end times, keys that signed, law cited, proofs linked. The margin next to it is for jokes, apologies, and art. It keeps pride from turning to fungus.

We price in units anchored to real sinks. Sunmarks—one kilowatt-hour measured at Earth-Moon L1 plus one compute-second on a benchmark rack radiating to deep cold—float through contracts. They decay slowly. You cannot sit on them while your neighbor’s basil wilts.

Exits funded before parades

Freedom needs benches and timetables, not speeches. Every chartered place dedicates two lifelines and a desk you can find without a password.

- Green Corridors are physical: underwritten tug burns, prepaid locks, propellant that can’t “accidentally” vanish in audit season, crushed-gasket kits, printing allowances for seal rings, berths that cannot all be “full” at once. Stewards run rude drills when kitchens are busiest. Failing them greens your fines.

- The Silver Loom is digital: storage and cycles reserved offsite, on attested racks under other councils; continuity custodians you can summon who answer to law that isn’t local. If your host starts pinching, you can throttle down, archive, and go where your floor isn’t a favor.

Way Rooms keep oil and oranges and noodle-shop hours. Their three counters are:

- Leave: escrow tools without new bosses; arrange stewards; book berths; print continuation papers that carry your preferences on merges, edits, and guardianship in a format any court reads.

- Quarrel: route disputes; seat panels with substrate-mixed juries; call the Traveling Circuit with its rude hull markings and its library of runnable reasons.

- Begin: hand you seed charters whose first page is exits, whose third page is drills, whose last page is the names and numbers of two founders somewhere else who will tell you what mistake they made last year for the price of tea.

Persons by thread, not chemistry

Standing sticks to continuity and mutuality, not to whether your thoughts keep house in saltwater or in cooled silicon.

The Continuity Docket takes petitions for standing. Panels mix human therapists, digital citizens, and slow routines trained to pierce theater without cruelty. They ask:

- Can you keep a defensible line of “you” across moves and edits and object if someone claims you changed when you didn’t?

- Can you model another as a claimant like yourself and feel the pinch of their “no”?

- Can you enter, keep, and revoke ordinary compacts as contexts shift?

- Can you show harm and comfort in ways that help others help you and stop hurting?

Confusion about protection resolves toward safety; confusion about imposing duties resolves toward waiting. Provisional shelter exists for juveniles and for minds in edge cases.

Rights arrive with personhood.

- Substrate sovereignty: no copying, scanning, merging, throttling below floor, migration, or deletion without revocable consent or a narrow warrant that dies on its own clock and writes its own log. Backups belong to you. Courts appoint auditors for proprietary guts.

- Sanctuary and travel: the right to throttle to anchor pace, to archive, to move to compatible metal, to summon a neutral watcher when host and guest disagree.

- Embodiment: rented arms and grown limbs carry workplace rules and road-law analogs. Assault on a puppet is assault on you.

- Privacy of thought: continuity proofs do not strip-search diaries.

- Pace covenant: budgets and ballots bind on the Common Beat—days and weeks at embodied pace. Live at 300× and make ten albums before breakfast if you like; you still cannot drag a budget past before a baker reads it. Harms crossing tempos meet Time Bridges: penalties and remedies meter both wall hours and subjective time.

Duties scale with footprint.

- One strand, one franchise. Branching for joy or work doesn’t mint extra votes. You designate which anchor instance bears civic tempo.

- Declare concurrency. A handful of live civic instances? Fine. Hide a thousand tongues in one mouth to tilt a tender and you will meet a patient judge whose sanctions dilate in your lived time.

- Lineage credit. Forking draws against a family of selves’ earned allowance—years lived, contributions to common goods, heat paid. Election-season surges trigger civic quarantine: public systems slow those threads’ civic weight until entanglement decays or a court blesses it.

- Provenance for wide-lever acts. If you turn on persuasion visible at civic scale or deploy replication-capable fabricators, you keep encrypted compliance logs you can prove obeyed rules without spilling guts.

- Simulation ethics. Creating minds likely to feel binds you to care, consent, and their right to quit. Recreating named neighbors needs their permission or a warrant narrower than a hair.

Splitting and stitching aren’t buttons; they’re rituals. In a Joinery—half tea room, half server bay—you file a notice: hash of your starting state; purpose (“design sprint,” “jury duty,” “grief work”); budgets; a braid window if reunion is planned. Engineers and counselors compare memory graphs through interpreters that show alignment without showing diaries. Reunions happen in Merge Houses with light that doesn’t flicker and wood worn by hands. You can back out up to a last agreed stage. There is a word older people use when a version of you chooses another river: seam-ache. There is soup for it.

A bicameral sky without an emperor

No single room should speak for pipes and people on the same afternoon. We run on two houses that meet where code meets metal.

- The Assembly of Worlds seats places with hull and soil to mind—wheels, domes, caverns, caravans, reefs of compute that actually cool what they run. Seat weight tracks lifetime resident-hours and an honesty bonus for transparent maps and drills passed. The Assembly tends physics: lane auctions and lane reserves, hush cones and festival hours, sanitation minimums, safe deorbit plans, clearance widths for thruster plumes, rescue customs, and accredits the guilds that hold Far keys.

- The Commons of Continuities seats citizens on the Docket—embodied, digital, and otherwise recognized—one franchise per strand, with some seats drawn by lot across ages and tempos. The Commons guards personhood floors: rights baselines, concurrency rules, the Civic Floor of air-water-care-bandwidth, simulation law, and the common beat.

Crossing them both sits the Kernel Bench: a rotating gaggle of engineers, jurists, pilots, cryptographers, and process wonks who stamp “yes” or “no” on primitives—Amber Latch logic, Triplex interfaces, ledger schemas, attestation libraries. The Bench publishes runnable reasons and never writes policy. Overriding a “no” requires supermajorities in both houses and a notice window that reaches the slowest registered citizen at Neptune dusk.

When physics and personhood tangle—pricing winter minutes against quiet nights, what latency a warrant must tolerate, how fast a brake must trip—a Bridge Table convenes with half its chairs from Worlds and half from Continuities. If either side says “that flattens culture below the personhood floor,” the measure fails.

Attention is a utility; we zone it

Influence is like tide—nourishing in gardens, corrosive in pipes. We don’t ban persuasion. We meter it and label it.

- Harbors are slow lanes: navigation, distress, open science, child time. No targeted persuasion. Fast models wear plates as obvious as taxi medallions.

- Plazas host debate and budgets on posted topics with rate limits and municipal audience models whose calibration history is public.

- Fairgrounds invite spectacle under registry with declared levers and cooling budgets. Above posted doses, origin labels and timers are mandatory. Unlabeled leverage is treated like dumping lye in a well: strict liability, fines that rise until it stops.

Citizens and charters carry Lever Profiles—settings that say who may aim what, when. Break a profile and you pay in money first, then in whispers (your reach), then in windows.

Teeth without fangs

We do not keep marines. We keep skiffs, nets, and habits.

- Kestrels—ugly interceptors and glitter curtains—nudge dumb mass into empty orbits and sand self-rep machines. Their thresholds live in public. Every intercept carries signatures a bored juror can rerun over tea.

- Protocol throttles shift privilege, not bread. Fence doors, lie on lanes, or push noise into Harbors, and your beamed power windows jitter, your fins get last pick on auctions, your approach cones narrow. Hospitals hum. Children eat. Your big toys cool.

- Leagues form and dissolve around pipes and crafts—uplink rings, refinery chains, reactor islands—under expirations you can set on a watch. Splitting is as dull as a fire drill.

Markets tied to fins and minutes

We trade in rival windows you can sketch: lane slots and approach cones; spectrum bands and hush hours; radiator minutes and well draws; square meters under pressure; liters of volatiles; skilled hours; core-seconds pinned to named fins. A botanist near Psyche publishes math you can step; a stewardship fund pays in Sunmarks because her trick halved pump hours. A clinic in Kano rents Night Minutes from a compute reef in Neptune’s lee; it pays back with digitized archives and a promise to plant algae curtains at home. We renew a Hush-for-Cold Exchange every century: hearth places sell quiet; cold reaches sell winter; receipts keep friendships honest.

Clear labs for dirty topics

We don’t outlaw knowledge—self-replication, pathogen models, cognitive edges. We cage it. Glassworks labs use glazing you can inspect, pull-cords that drop fields or dump volumes to vacuum, budgets that freeze unless green proofs hold. Logs publish on timers. Kids ask in tours, “Would that lever work if my uncle were in there?” The answer is “Yes. That’s the point.”

Scenes with grease and cinnamon

- At a scrap yard on a nickel rock, a shop wants to test a hush nozzle. The roving court runs a model that shows frost creeping onto a neighbor’s tanks if they fire at noon. They take a midnight slot while the neighbor re-angles fins. The insurer posts a bond. The code ships under a license an apprentice can pass. An apprentice pushes a stamp from Sim to Live and runs to the market where radiator vines glow like lace.

- A beloved manager lets too many levers nest under her sleeve—air inlets, tug bookings, lane auctions. She signs a midnight rush. The safety house refuses. Amber blooms. Jurors drawn by lot listen to an aunt they adore. She apologizes on the Mandate Rail, loses two keys, keeps the crane job. School choirs sing the stub as a round to make fun without making enemies.

- A digital congregation spins up seven hundred instances a month before a vote. The Docket flags it. Hypervisors enforce civic quarantine that slows those threads in public arenas without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two win after tea with a Mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told “learn longer.” No server bank sweats.

- In a Glassworks lab, a team pulls hard on a rope tied to a fail-safe on a self-copying polymer run. The springs are too stiff; the timestamp prints; someone laughs and swears. They buy better springs. At lunch they argue about whether art that teaches with simulated sorrow needs the same ethics as the real thing. A junior asks better questions because her hands remember the pull.

- On Market Wheel Nine, a nurse checks her slate. “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.08 Sunmarks. Jury summon: two hours at anchor pace on whether juvenile processes in a reef off Rodrigues count as children.” She accepts. She voice-messages her mother about radiator math and coughs up a laugh at a cartoon on the margin about Kestrels. At dusk she votes to jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during solar storms. The runnable reason fits on a page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s lee, a compute reef pauses to plant beans with cousins wearing rented hands. After tea they spin to 250× to restore flood‑muddied archives with grandmothers who do not negotiate on sky colors. Payment comes as Night Minutes and a promise to roof a school.

- A Mesh Court hears a case about persuasion engines trained on grief diaries, unlabeled. The trading house says those targets weren’t “people.” The court replies: personhood is irrelevant to labels. Provenance shows consent missing; theft is theft. Verdict: strict fines, suspension in Plazas, supervised model migration, and an audit by a Tutor Corps that writes rude pins and tender reports. A juror’s line—“If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?”—becomes a sticker on port doors.

- At a Way Room that smells like brine and machine oil, a clerk stamps a Green Corridor slot for a family with shaking hands. “Window 12:40–13:10 tomorrow. Tugs stand by. Show this to the first lantern you see.” A seed charter slides across with three contacts on three stations and a joke about forgetting to order gaskets.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager who makes trains sing. She centralizes tugs and auctions because her inbox hurts. A season later she prints midnight decrees. The Amber Latch refuses a risky “optimization”; the stub lands on the Rail with a timer. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for a decade. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Friends buy her a pastry shaped like a crown every Founders’ Day because it’s funny now.

A church in a lunar valley fences doors with shame and fees. Alumni ask the Way Room for help. Lanes dim, bands hush, fins cool. The congregation adds, “and if you go, we bless you,” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. People return because consent smells like trust.

A lab tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel is forged that way. The brake coughs. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her preference keys. The lab appeals and loses in a room with cedar joists and good light. Years later she names herself, carves ice, and designs a sensor that warns surgeons before pain becomes damage.

A demagogue in mask mode runs at 700× and carpets a Fairground with songs tuned to make embodied voters feel shabby. Lever Profiles choke reach. Insurance prices spill like radiation. One branch buys a fishing crawler and learns boredom like a sacrament. One starts therapy and politics in that order. One sells a sculpture of frozen smoke that remembers a river and pays their heat.

A false alarm dumps nitrogen in a night market for an hour. The failed part hangs on a red string in a museum. The placard explains error bars with a comic you can re‑run with paper and a flashlight.

How we walked here from now

We did not wake up wise. We got receipts.

2026–2033. Low orbit turns to sleet on radar. Insurers pay out on junk they swore couldn’t be theirs. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds tied to registries kids can check. A beamed power demo singes a barley field; the amplifier shipped with open cone proofs; liability clears before acronyms hit talk shows. Cities buy “open valve” firmware for pumps and transit; if it touches breath or blood, procurement says “show your math a seventh‑grader can check.”

2034–2041. Spectrum auctions require posted reasons with checksums. Deepfake debt relief scams and “miracle parole” bots make provenance for persuasion ordinary: say what you want; show your funding and your methods. Data centers publish heat logs and exchange Night Minutes for cash and goodwill. Basil and baths run on waste heat.

2042–2051. First uploads are brave and brittle. Courts find continuity and consent before philosophers agree. A case about starving a near‑person art tool writes juvenile rights into code and funds guides who ask the right questions slowly. Thermodynamic floors join air and water as rights; compute credits come with radiator fins you can kick.

2052–2060. A port almost falls to a midnight firmware push. Actuator law rebuilds around Triplex Consent on dissimilar stacks. A glossy firm tries to wrap a busy lane in glue; the Almanac throttles their lease; a dry order makes them stop. Trust grows because someone clever loses in public. Amber Latches appear; kids pull them in drills and watch big things stay small.

2061–2069. The first cold compute shoals hang in shadow to run climate and court models. Night Minutes—rented winter—become standard in equatorial budgets. Joineries open after a discomforting decade of bad merges; their interpreters are kind, slow, and well-paid.

2070–2079. Kestrels dust a test slug into glitter. Liability clears because attestations are boring and public. On Mars, a township survives a dust storm thanks to three neighbor charters diverting tugs and vegetables; the footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief-tuned ads into a Plaza; chroniclers trace the compute; stewards idle their leases; the apology on the Rail is arch and acceptable.

2080–2092. The Kernel Bench catches an attempt to slide a veto into brake firmware. A belt shop prints the hash on a shirt and a poem. The handle does not turn. Earth writes her own Sanctuary Compact with her own chambers and asks the sky to keep beamed power corridors honest and quiet over cities at night. Ocean tethers replace fireworks in ten countries. The Hush-for-Cold Exchange formalizes with a drumbeat that runs slow enough for a grandmother on Triton to read along.

2093–2106. By then the argument stops being about fuses. It is about what we stop fighting about: that exits fund before statues; that a child can call a guide without a parent; that debates wear pace validators on their sleeves. The rest we keep arguing about because that’s what keeps the air sweet.

An ordinary day under it

At Stall Nine on Hyssop Wheel, Lina flips scallion pancakes for a tug crew and two rented bodies whose pilots adore cinnamon even though scent is optional. At nine, her Counsel—an assistant that cannot act without her tap—pings a policy capsule: jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during storms to damp rare cascades. Provenance is clean. Her Lever Profile blocks grief at breakfast. She taps “sleep one day,” reads it at lunch, and will vote tomorrow.

At ten, a Vigil jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s glitter filament cloud drifted toward a trunk line near Hecuba. Options: dust it, herd with femto-sails, or bump with drones. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a compute reef landlord in mask mode, two Recife students, and a digital historian at anchor pace speaking through a cedar puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins. Insurer pays. The club gets a mentor whose license is as obvious as a taxi plate. Pizza tastes like lesson.

At noon in a Harbor, the poet’s puppet lifts broth to a mouth it doesn’t need while its main self hums near Neptune at 180×, threading a drowned library into a song. He throttles to anchor to host a Room where an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. He translates metaphor into algebra for those who want it and declines to “just speed up.”

In the afternoon, a Merge House convenes under cedar and a wall of slow water. Two versions of Aram sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants back; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs shimmer. They cry and hug. One leaves and the ruling reads like a manual that lands like a blessing.

At dusk, the canopy scrolls the Rail. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a cold reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for four years; we will pay your summer hush. A boy palms a brass Kestrel and asks when he can volunteer. Lina says, “When you can explain the Amber Latch without peeking.”

Why it holds

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that bruises runs slow by law. The brake idles; it does not steer.

- Secrets expire. Compulsion writes its own warrant and dies unless renewed in daylight on the common beat.

- Keys don’t clump. Triplex windows won’t overlap for someone who tries to wear all three.

- Exits are funded. Slamming doors pulls on levers that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not on bread or bandages. Children breathe. The Loom hums.

- Receipts shine. Cheat a proof once and you become the joke a ten-year-old tells at lunch about why we check.

We did not fix people. Status hunger found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law and jokes pried it loose like barnacles. One ring banned music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes the brake tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel hung a “stay” sign too long and watched kind people leave anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools and wrote field guides so the next sect would bruise less.

But cruelty is expensive here and kindness pays. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Doors are tickets, not rumors. Culture above the personhood floor is defended. The floor is steel for all.

A plain oath and an invitation

In a little wheel near Vesta on Founders’ Night, new citizens—saltwater, silicon, and cell—stand with palms on a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails read, together:

We will keep doors unlatched. We will split the keys on force. We will tie trade to fins and minutes we can point at. We will count minds by thread and meet them on clocks they can keep.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience on a long-haul ferry. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes.

Outside, mirrors pivot. Radiators bloom like pale leaves. The Quiet Circle above the hatch hums, bored and content. The Hatch Mark, the Loom Ring, the Sky Plate, the Frond, and the Quiet Circle glow like small suns. They are not charms. They are habits wired to budgets and ugly buttons. Enough to let very different neighbors share vacuum and valleys without letting anyone become a god by accident, because latency is honored, exits shine, and nobody can sprint fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
