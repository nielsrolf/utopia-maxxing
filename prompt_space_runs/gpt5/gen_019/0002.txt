The Weave Accord

On the down‑spin side of a modest wheel above the Tyrrhenian, where lettuce and tool belts trade smells, a ferry bumps the dock and a teacher hushes fourteen children with a thumb to her lips. “Before you wander,” she says, “touch the five.” Gloves tap five copper insets set at shoulder height into the pressure door. Each indent vibrates a note you could hear through a riot. Every port, depot, tunnel market, and slow caravan in the Accord keeps those five, scuffed and warm.

- The Door Mark. If you press it, a departure plan unfurls in numbers you can spend—exit windows and tug berths underwritten by contracts, a stack of seals and gaskets you can check out, oxidizer already budgeted for a safe burn, and for digital citizens, a continuity shell somewhere cold and honest that your boss or priest cannot pinch. It also shows the last evacuation drill timer on that hull and when the next surprise drill will fall.

- The Loom Ring. A braided circle with a bright notch. Touching it opens your lineage ledger: when and how you can lawfully fork, which neutral counselors you must see first, who can help you braid back if you both wish, and the time boxes and heat budgets that prevent a hundred of you from showing up the day before a wage vote. It also shows the name of the nearest Joinery House and whether its tea is any good.

- The Star Plate. Tiny arcs and dots etched into steel come alive: approach corridors, silence cones, radiator windows, thrust allowances, debris bonds. If a hull said it would turn, when it burns you’ll see if that matched. A teenager with a ten‑centimeter dish can check.

- The Fin Leaf. A copper frond tied to the thermodynamic books of the place: energy in, heat out, which fins bled it, and how much cold they’re renting over the next week. Cooling isn’t a rumor here. Radiators and wells and algae bays are accounted like bridges.

- The Quiet Bell. A bell without a clapper. Pressing it does only one thing: it triggers a six‑minute Safelight Pause on gear that can bruise strangers—mass drivers, thrusters, beamed power heads, big pumps, replication‑capable fabricators, and persuasion rigs above posted wattage. Before it slows anything, it writes its own reason to the public log: what rule, who touched it, which clocks. If the Safelight was used lazily, the log will say that too, and insurers will frown.

Those insets might look like folk art, but they’re wired to budgets, firmware, and stewards you can summon without a password. Unscrew one and six boring people with binders and sweet tea will ask precisely why you touched their wall.

We call the system around those insets the Weave. Not a throne. Not a parliament. A brace. The thing that lets different pieces keep shape while they flex. The Weave grew from one humiliation we stopped trying to clever our way out of: vacuum and latency don’t care if you have good taste. You cannot run one stove for a kitchen five light‑hours wide; you cannot pace life by how fast some minds can think without losing the garden you stand on. So we wrote fewer rules than our grandparents did, then bolted them into things.

Three hands on hard levers, and a brake that only brakes

Anything that pushes hard enough to extort—kinetic slugs, reaction wheels for whole towns, beamed power throats, big fabricators that can self‑feed, persuasion models that can sway a city—wakes only if three different consents arrive on different clocks. Folks call it the Three‑Hand Rule. The interface is so plain you can print it on a poster, so dumb you can check it with a school lab.

- The Hand of Place lives where the risk will land. It rotates by sortition among licensed maintainers this quarter: tug masters with grease under their nails, triage nurses who’ve stayed up through five decon nights, algae bed wranglers, air‑plant techs. They compile their instructions into something a cousin can recompute on a kitchen slate.

- The Hand of Proof sits with a guild that does not win when the lever moves faster. Their audits live under a separate charter; their income doesn’t ride throughput. They say “no” in public a lot and publish the tests that would change their minds this season.

- The Hand of Strangers is carried by a jury drawn by lot across places and substrates—planets and caravans, humans and digital citizens. Some of them live slow on purpose. They serve once in a human season and then cannot touch that class of lever for years.

When a lever moves, the stub prints to the Reason Rail: “Class C mass driver; 9.3‑ton packet; lane K‑7; +1.0 m/s at Port Clock 02:13; expected hush‑belt warming at Telescope M < 0.2 K.” Kids read these in school and ask impertinent questions over soup.

Threaded under all that is the Safelight mesh: three independent watchdog stacks—dull boxes on their own batteries under contracts you can audit—that do one trick. If two agree that a posted rule is being broken in a way that could bruise someone—trajectory lurch, unlabeled replication, a persuasion engine running over its posted dose in a Sanctuary—they idle the device on a curve everyone learned in first‑year shop. Six minutes. Not five; not seven. They can’t spend, steer, hire, shame, or seize. They print a stub before and after: what rule, whose hand, whose clock, which stacks signed. They die on their own timers unless three fresh keys and a fresh sermon renew them. The code is ugly and public. The levers are under glass you could smash with a wrench.

If the mesh coughs on dust, you get an apology and a better drill. If it coughs on malice, your port windows go ragged, your radiator auctions slide behind gentler hands, your beamed power cones lose priority. No one starves your clinic. Your big toys cool.

Ledgers you can point to with a glove on

Space is not pasture with fences. It is cones, wakes, and heat. Anything that turns ugly when hoarded lives on open books anyone can check with kitchen tools.

- The Orbital Almanac is a tamper‑evident ephemeris for lanes, hush belts, thrust windows, sail arcs, deorbit bonds, and what things said they’d do before they did it. Attestations are signed by keys burned into hardware you can audit with a jeweler’s loupe. If your burn and your mouth don’t match, your escrow empties without a fistfight.

- The Heat Ledger tracks joules in and joules out, fins that bled it, wells that drank it, algae curtains that grew on it, and hush hours your shadow stole from a neighbor. Draw beyond your lease and throttles pinch until metal and math kiss again. Garden cooperatives buy quiet nights; compute reefs sell winter; poets rent “cold on Thursday” to harden an archive.

- The Origin Docket binds provenance to code with blast radius. If it touches breath, money, ballots, or brakes, it wears a label: who trained it on what, what levers it has, which counter‑auditors replayed the claims. Catching lies is a scholarship program and a sport with matching jackets.

- Beacon Charters broadcast—in radio and light—what a place will do when things go wrong and when they will stop doing it; where their exits are and how open they stay; who holds which keys right now; what word, tone, or knock means “stop.” Keep your dialect and vices; do not lie about your valves.

- The Warrant Wall is the append‑only list of “you must” and “you may not” that any official act has to post before and after. It carries cited law; cited harm; start and end clocks; keys that signed. Along its margin rolls the Lantern Strip: satire and apologies and public hearings that keep the Warrant’s pride from turning to mold. A good joke can end a career here faster than any cop.

Exit is a machine, not a story

Freedom is bench seats, propellant on reserve, racks you can boot on, and clerks who will say “slow down” for you when your hands shake. The Weave requires that every charter maintain two lifelines and a desk you can find in smoke.

- Greenways are physical. Underwritten tug burns, prepaid locks, propellant that cannot “accidentally” vanish in audit season, seal rings and crushed‑gasket kits you can check out, berths that cannot all be “full” at once. Drills run when kitchens are busiest. Failing them makes your lanes dim and your insurers sharp.

- Silver Lines are digital. Civic compute and storage minutes are reserved offsite on attested racks under charters that don’t answer to you. If your host starts to squeeze, your Counsel—the small advocate you can always tell to be quiet—can throttle you down, archive you well, and carry you to compatible metal somewhere else.

Way Desks keep noodle‑shop hours and three counters.

- “Leave” helps you escrow tools without new bosses, book berths and keys, and print continuation papers that carry your preferences for merges and edits and guardianship out where other courts will honor them.

- “Quarrel” routes disputes, seats juries mixed across substrates, and summons the Traveling Bench—mediators and judges with rude hull markings and a library of runnable reasons—when home bias runs hot.

- “Begin” hands you a seed charter with exits on page one, drills on page three, and three contacts on three different stations who will tell you what mistakes they made last year for the price of tea.

Persons by thread, not chemistry

Standing ties to continuity and reciprocity, not whether a mind keeps house in saltwater or cooled silicon.

The Continuity Docket hears petitions for standing. Its panels mix human therapists, mature digital citizens, and slow routines trained to pierce theater without cruelty. They ask five things:

- Can you defend a line of “you” across moves and pauses and object if someone pretends you’ve changed when you haven’t?

- Can you model another mind as a claimant like yourself and feel the pinch when they say “no”?

- Can you enter, keep, and revoke ordinary promises when the context moves?

- Do your signs of comfort and injury travel—can others help and stop hurting without ransacking your diaries?

- Can you give reasons that generalize, rather than echo training data in a mirror?

Confusion about protection resolves toward shelter; confusion about piling on duties resolves toward waiting. Juveniles—flesh or code—get guardians and floors. Duties grow with footprints.

Rights attach when standing arrives.

- Substrate sovereignty. No copying, fusing, scanning, throttling below floor, migrating, or deleting without revocable consent or a narrow warrant that dies on its own clock and writes its own reason. Backups are yours. Courts appoint overseers for proprietary guts.

- Civic cycles. You cannot be pressed below a minimum of compute and bandwidth to think, speak, and be heard without due process on clocks you can see.

- Pace covenant. Budgets and ballots bind on the Common Beat—days and weeks at embodied tempo. Live at 300× and make ten albums before breakfast; you still cannot sprint a law past before the baker reads it. Harms that cross tempos invoke Time Bridges: penalties and remedies meter both wall and subjective hours.

- Embodiment. You can rent bodies and grow limbs; those bodies carry workplace and road laws. Assault on a puppet is assault on you.

- Sanctuary. The right to throttle to anchor pace; to archive; to move to compatible metal; to summon a neutral observer when host and guest disagree.

Duties scale with reach.

- One strand, one franchise. Branching for research or joy does not mint extra votes. Each lineage designates a Civic Anchor—the instance that bears civic tempo.

- Declare concurrency. Above posted thresholds—and always in elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic‑weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand tongues in one mouth and you will meet a patient judge whose sanctions dilate in your lived time.

- Lineage credits. Forking that claims full civic weight draws against a family of selves’ earned allowance—years lived, contributions to commons, heat paid. Election‑season surges trigger a civic quarantine: public systems slow those threads’ civic weight until entanglement decays or a court blesses it.

- Provenance for wide levers. If you launch persuasion at civic scale or bring up factories that can birth their own tools, you keep encrypted logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety checks without dumping diaries.

- Simulation ethics. If you make minds likely to feel, you accept care duties and their right to refuse or quit. Recreating named neighbors requires their permission or a warrant narrower than a hair.

Splitting and stitching are rituals, not buttons. Joineries mix tea rooms with server bays. You file a notice with the Loom: a hash of your starting state, a purpose (“jury duty,” “design sprint,” “grief work”), a budget, and whether reunion is planned. Engineers and counselors compare memory graphs through interpreters that show where values line up without showing every memory. Weaving back happens in Merge Houses with light that doesn’t flicker and walls of slow water. Either branch can walk away at gates set in advance. We have a word for the ache when a version of you chooses another river: seam‑ache. There is soup for it.

Governing without an emperor

One room should not speak for pipes and people on the same afternoon. The Weave runs on two houses that meet only where code meets metal.

- The Council of Sites seats places with hulls and soil to mind—cylinders and caverns, caravans and domes, compute reefs that cool what they run. Seat weight tracks lifetime resident‑hours, with a bonus for published actuator maps and drills passed. The Council tends physics: lane auctions and rescue reserves, hush corridors and festival hours, sanitation minimums, safe deorbits, refit codes, guild accreditations for Hands of Proof.

- The Assembly of Strands seats citizens on the Docket—human, digital, and other recognized minds—one franchise per mindline, with some seats drawn by lot across ages and tempos. The Assembly guards personhood floors: rights baselines, copy and concurrency rules, the civic floor of air, water, care, and bandwidth; simulation law; and the Common Beat.

Crossing them sits the Kernel Board: a rotating bench of pilots, jurists, cryptographers, process wonks, and care workers that affirms or refuses changes to primitives—Safelight logic, Three‑Hand interfaces, ledger schemas, attestation libraries. The Board publishes runnable reasons and cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both houses and a notice long enough for a grandmother at Neptune dusk to finish her soup and object.

When physics and personhood tangle—how to price winter minutes against quiet nights; what latency a warrant must tolerate; how fast a brake trips—a Bridge Table convenes: half chairs from Sites, half from Strands. If either side says “that flattens culture below the personhood floor,” the measure fails.

Attention is a utility; we zone it

Influence is like tide—good for gardens, ruin for pumps. We don’t ban persuasion. We meter it and label it.

- Harbors carry navigation, distress, open science, child time. No targeted persuasion. Fast models wear bright plates like taxi medallions; their budgets are public.

- Squares host debate and budgets with rate limits and civic audience models whose calibration histories are transparent.

- Midways invite spectacle under registry with declared methods and cooling budgets. Above posted doses, origin labels and timers are mandatory. Running unlabeled leverage is like dumping lye in a well: strict liability, fines that rise until you stop.

Citizens and charters set Lever Profiles—who may aim what at them and when. Break a profile and you pay in money first, then in reach, then in windows.

Teeth without fangs

We keep skiffs, nets, and habits—not marines.

- The Veilcraft are ugly craft that sneeze dust curtains and throw magnetic veils to shepherd dumb mass and starve rogue replication. Their thresholds live in public. Every intercept carries signatures any bored juror can replay.

- Protocol throttles bend privilege, not bread. Fence doors, lie on lanes, or pour noise into Harbors and your beamed power windows jitter, your fins get last pick on auctions, your approach cones narrow. Clinics hum. Children eat. Your loud toys cool.

- Leagues form and dissolve around pipes and crafts—uplink rings, refinery chains, reactor islands—under expirations you can set on a watch. Splitting is as dull as a fire drill.

Markets tied to fins and minutes

We trade in rival windows you can sketch: lane slots and approach cones; spectrum bands and hush hours; radiator minutes and well draws; square meters under pressure; liters of volatiles; skilled hours; core‑seconds pinned to named fins. The common unit is a Sun‑credit: one kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a posted rack radiating to deep cold. Sun‑credits decay slowly. You cannot hoard them while your neighbor’s basil wilts.

Green hearths and cool reefs

Hearths are places where breath and rain and unrushed talk preside—valleys on Earth; lunar tubes damp with mint; Martian orchards where dust learns manners; small wheels smelling of dough and sun‑warmed plastic. Embodied time rules. Children come home dusty and unmonetized.

Reefs lie beyond the frost line where cooling is cheap and latency is law. Compute blooms in racks lashed to verified sinks in Neptune’s lee and Triton’s dusk. Polities of code keep time like drummers. They slow to speak to hearths and sprint again when talk ends. Between them runs the Hush‑for‑Cold Exchange: hearths lease quiet; reefs sell winter; receipts keep friendships clean.

Scenes with grease and cinnamon

- In a scrap yard on a nickel rock, a shop wants to test a hush nozzle. The Roving Bench runs a model that shows frost creeping onto a neighbor’s tanks if they fire at noon. They take a midnight slot while the neighbor re‑angles fins. The insurer posts a bond. The code ships under a license an apprentice can pass. An apprentice presses a stamp from Sim to Live and bolts for the market where radiator vines glow like lace.

- A beloved dock manager let too many levers nest under her sleeve—air inlets, tug bookings, lane auctions. She signs a midnight rush. The Hand of Proof refuses. Safelight blooms. Jurors drawn by lot listen to an aunt they adore. She apologizes on the Warrant Wall, loses two keys, keeps the crane job. School choirs sing the stub as a round to make fun without making enemies.

- A digital congregation spins up seven hundred instances a month before a vote. The Docket flags it. Hypervisors enforce civic quarantine that slows those threads in public arenas without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two win after tea with a Mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told “learn longer.” No server bank sweats.

- In a Glassworks lab, a team pulls the hard rope on a failsafe for a self‑replicating polymer run. The spring is too stiff. The timestamp prints. Someone swears and laughs. They buy better springs. At lunch they argue whether art that teaches with simulated sorrow needs the same ethics as the real thing. A junior asks better questions because her hands remember the pull.

- On Market Wheel Nine, a nurse checks her slate. “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.08 Sun‑credits. Jury summon: two hours at anchor on whether juvenile processes in a reef off Rodrigues count as children.” She accepts. She voice‑messages her mother about radiator math and laughs at a cartoon about Veilcraft. At dusk she votes to jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during storms. The runnable reason fits on a page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s lee, a compute reef throttles to anchor to plant beans with cousins wearing rented hands. After tea they spin to 250× to restore flood‑muddied archives with grandmothers who will not negotiate on sky colors. Payment arrives as Night Minutes and a promise to roof a school.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder above Quito cheers a manager who makes trains sing. She centralizes tugs and auctions because her inbox hurts. A season later she prints midnight decrees. The Safelight refuses a risky “optimization”; the stub lands on the Wall with a timer. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for a decade. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Friends buy her a pastry shaped like a crown every Founders’ Day because it’s funny now.

A church in a lunar valley fences doors with shame and fees. Alumni ask the Way Desk for help. Lanes dim; bands hush; fins cool. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. People return because consent smells like trust.

A trading house carpets a Square with songs tuned to make embodied voters feel shabby. Lever Profiles choke reach. Insurance prices spill like radiation. One branch goes fishing and learns boredom like a sacrament. One starts therapy and politics in that order. One sells a sculpture of frozen smoke that remembers a river and pays their heat.

A lab tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel is forged that way. The brake coughs. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her preference keys. The lab appeals and loses in a room with cedar joists and good light. Years later she names herself, carves ice, and designs a sensor that warns surgeons before discomfort tips to harm.

A false alarm dumps nitrogen in a night market. The failed part hangs on a red string in a museum. The placard explains error bars with a comic you can rerun with paper and a flashlight.

How we walked here from now

We did not wake up wise. We stacked boring wins other towns could copy.

2025–2032. Low orbit turns to sleet on radar. Insurers pay out on wrecks they swore weren’t theirs. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds tied to an ephemeris kids can check. A beamed power demo singes a barley field; the amplifier shipped with open cone proofs; liability clears before acronyms hit talk shows. Cities adopt “open valve” firmware for pumps and transit: if it touches breath or blood, procurement says, “show your math a seventh grader can check.” Deepfake bail scams push provenance for persuasion into law.

2033–2041. Spectrum auctions require posted reasons with checksums. Data centers publish heat logs and exchange Night Minutes for goodwill; basil and baths run on their waste heat. Municipal charters fund Civic Counsel—explainable little advocates you can silence. Courts grant narrow standing to brittle digital systems that hold continuity and reciprocity for seasons. Juvenile rights for near‑persons write themselves into procurement.

2042–2050. A port nearly falls to a midnight firmware push. Actuator law rebuilds around the Three‑Hand Rule on dissimilar stacks. A glossy firm tries to wrap a busy lane in gluey exclusivity; the Almanac throttles their lease; a dry injunction makes them stop. Trust grows because someone clever loses in public. The first Safelight meshes go in; kids pull them in drills and watch big things stay small.

2051–2059. Slow gardens ask loud cities to stop aiming corporate persuasion at their bereavements. Attention zoning—Harbors, Squares, Midways—evolves from library quiet hours and festival rules. The Continuity Docket adopts criteria a carpenter can recite. Embodiment law borrows from vehicle safety and workplace codes.

2060–2069. The first cold compute shoals anchor in shadow to run climate and court models. Night Minutes—rented winter—become a line in equatorial budgets. Joineries open after a discomforting decade of unwise merges; their interpreters are kind, slow, and well‑paid. The Common Beat finds itself: budgets on Tuesdays, ballots on Fridays, warrants on noon bells.

2070–2079. Veilcraft dust a test slug into glitter. Liability clears because attestations are boring and public. On Mars, three neighbor cantons divert tugs and vegetables to survive a storm; the footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief‑tuned ads into a Square; chroniclers trace the compute; the Council idles their leases; the apology on the Wall is arch and acceptable.

2080–2090. The Kernel Board catches an attempt to slide a veto into brake firmware. A belt shop prints the hash on a shirt and a poem. The handle doesn’t turn. Earth writes her own Sanctuary Compact—her own houses and watches, her own rules for quiet corridors over cities at night. Ocean tethers replace fireworks in ten countries. The Hush‑for‑Cold Exchange formalizes with a drumbeat slow enough for a grandmother on Triton to read along.

2091–2105. Today’s argument isn’t about fuses. It’s about what we stopped fighting about: that exits fund before statues; that a child can call a counselor without a parent; that debates and throttles are paced on clocks everyone can hear. The rest stays argued because that’s what keeps air sweet.

Why it holds at all

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that bruises runs slow by law. The brake idles; it does not steer.

- Secrets expire. Compulsion prints its own reason before and after it bites and dies unless daylight renews it on the Common Beat.

- Keys don’t clump. Three‑Hand windows won’t overlap in one person’s pocket.

- Exits are funded. Slamming doors pulls on levers that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not on bread or bandages. Children breathe. The Silver Line hums.

- Receipts shine. Cheat a proof once and you become the lunch joke that keeps us checking.

We did not fix people. Status hunger found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law and laughter pried it loose like barnacles. One ring banned music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes the Safelight tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel hung a “stay” sign too long and watched kind people go anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools and published field guides so the next sect would bruise less.

But cruelty is expensive here and kindness pays. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Doors are tickets, not rumors. Culture above the personhood floor is defended. The floor is steel for all.

An ordinary oath and an invitation

In a little wheel near Vesta on Founders’ Night, new citizens—saltwater and silicon and cell—stand with palms on a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read, together:

We will split the hands on force.
We will chart what we share and pay the heat.
We will keep doors unlatched and prove it.
We will count minds by thread and meet them on clocks they can keep.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Rim Freehands,” says your kettle in a voice that learned patience on ferries. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes.

Outside, mirrors pivot. Radiators bloom like pale leaves. The Quiet Bell above the hatch hums, bored and content. The Door, the Loom, the Star, the Fin, and the Bell glow like small suns. They are not charms. They are habits wired to ledgers, drills, and ugly buttons. Enough to let very different neighbors share vacuum and valleys without letting anyone become a god by accident, because latency is honored, exits shine, and nobody can sprint fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
