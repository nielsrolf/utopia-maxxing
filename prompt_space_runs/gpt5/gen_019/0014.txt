The Constellation Compact

The shuttle from Hygiea kisses the rim at Juniper Wheel, and your first breath tastes like cumin and warm metal. Through the long pane by the inner lock, a radiator leaf fans toward black; below it, a ribbon of algae tracks past the waste inlets like a very calm river. Shoulder-high beside the door, three fixtures are sunk into basalt warmed by a loop. They’re unphotogenic and a little dented. You see them in domes, coils, caravans, caves—places built by hands. They’ve kept entire worlds from growing fangs.

- Waycard. Brush it and a path steps out in numbers you can stand on—tug seats you can afford, heater minutes reserved at the far dock, scrubber time, a compute shell with enough bandwidth to speak and be answered, the steward whose name and guild you can look up. It lists who last inspected the corridor, where the drills stumbled, when the pilots slept, and which kitchen on the other side can make broth that tastes like home.

- Split Seal. Touch it and your mindline shows simple and honest: when you may branch without starving your other selves; who tends Braidrooms within two hours; how much cold compute your family of threads has earned; the fees if you plan to bring a juvenile mind into being without stealing watts from neighbors; the rites if you mean to braid back one day.

- Charter Window. When it glows steady, the rules that govern the air you are about to breathe parse at ninth-grade and on a school kiosk. It keeps doors funded. It shows how to appeal without a cousin on the inside. If you linger, the Window reads hard sections out loud. Courage takes turns with literacy.

They are not mascots. They’re wired to budgets and machines and the habit of filing reasons you can point at. Together they anchor the Constellation Compact: brakes and floors; books and drills; receipts and jokes; a way for different tempos and hulls to share a sky without anyone growing fast or smooth enough to swallow a neighbor whole.

The blunt rules

Under everything are a few promises that compile to code and procurement without changing their faces.

1) Coercive harm is banned. “Person” means a being—of tissue or circuit—that can keep a thread across time, honor reciprocal claims, and shows stakes you could mistake enough to need humility.

2) Lifelines—air, water, pressure, waste, medicine—run on legible firmware with logs and off switches that trained neighbors can pull. No hidden loops near a vent.

3) No concealed manufacture of suffering, in flesh or in simulation.

4) Decisions that bind embodied lives ratify on the Civic Beat. Drafts can sprint; binds walk slow enough for a farmer with wet hands to object.

5) Self-replicators in shared spaces are bounded, sealed, and answer to quell-tones a jury can test with a whistle and a scope.

6) Commons lease; they are not owned. Orbit tracks, fin minutes, band slices, cave mouths: all sunset. The commons is paid first.

7) Children—born, built, or emergent—learn three drills before algebra: draw a boundary, refuse a pull, leave with help.

8) No one edits a mind without consent. Deep changes require two yesses: one planned beforehand, one present and competent.

9) Votes and warrants meter in the tempo of the most affected. Quick minds wait for the Civic Beat.

10) The only cross-world actuator is a brake with timers, and nobody owns it.

Rules without machinery are sermons. We built the handles.

Three hands on every lever; one boring, honest pause

Any machine that can bruise a stranger—thrusters, beamed power, big pumps, seeded printers, persuasion engines linked to public budgets—won’t wake for one key. It starts with a Threefold Wake and it can be idled by the Amber Hold.

A Threefold Wake needs three dissimilar hands on clocks that cannot conspire without leaving tooth marks.

- A local steward elected or drawn under a posted charter and revocable by neighbors;

- A safety guild credentialed abroad whose payroll lives on another world;

- A short-term panel drawn by lot across habitats and substrates, often including a slow-mind who writes at garden tempo.

Every wake mints a stub before anything moves: class, output ceiling, aim, end state, and timer. “Class-B radiator tilt of 12°, to spare Quiet Ward G-4 for four hours; expected local thermal rise: 0.1 K.” Twelve-year-olds learn to read and heckle these at kitchen tables.

The Amber Hold sits under every wake. It is simple on purpose. If posted windows are off, keys don’t match, or plans aren’t on the Ephemeris Ledger, it drops a six-minute stop and yells into the Cause Register: what rule tripped, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. Holds die unless renewed by three fresh seals. The code is open. The hardware is unromantic: fuses you can break with your palm; a lever under thick glass you can find while coughing; a fat yellow button marked AMBER in letters you can read gasping. Any trained citizen with a civic key can pull one wherever dumb meets dangerous. Overreach earns a turn on the Lampboard that dents careers faster than court.

If both layers fail, the Thistledown Net wakes. Thistledown is a lace of small interceptors and glittering dusters that only knows how to nudge, scatter, and pause. It cannot plan, tax, or command. It sleeps through politics. It wakes when dumb math crosses old trip-lines: an unsealed spore spool in a cargo throat; a vector on course for a nursery; a cluster booting without a Foundry Seal. Three signatures—sharded across worlds and substrates—must agree, or it drifts back to sleep. Every act prints two proofs: a cryptographic attestation any auditor can check, and a paragraph a dockworker can read on break. Students strip them after drills. Pride prefers flash. We built plain.

Books you can check with your own eyes

We did not trust charm to schedule shadows. Four ledgers make coordination boring and hard to fake.

- The Ephemeris Ledger is a delay-tolerant, tamper-evident book bound to physics. Anything with heft posts where it is, where it’s going, and how it ends—burns, sails, mirrors and their shadows. Objects sign with keys tied to inspected hardware. With patience, a kid and a school scope can check a promised burn.

- The Entropy Ledger tracks joules drawn and where heat goes—racks, fins, wells, algae. Cooling is a commons. If draw and discard don’t balance, throttles pinch until math and metal agree. Quiet cones—sound and light—protect telescopes, lyceums, sick wards, neighborhoods that buy hush. If you must pierce a cone, you post collateral measured in hours an aunt can hold.

- The Origin Register binds provenance to code and persuasion. If a system touches breath, money, bodies, or votes, it wears a label: who paid, what data tuned it, which levers it will reach. Two independent audit houses replay proofs. Teenagers get rude pins and stipends for finding cheats. Laughter sands corners power might cut.

- The Mandate Tape records every official “you must” and “you may not.” It unrolls on market canopies, school walls, port kiosks. It says who turned which key, which rule they cited, when it starts, when it stops, where the audit pack lives. Dry entries keep insurers honest. Notes and apologies keep pride from souring.

Two tempos, one bargain

Close to gravity and human heat live the Warm Belt: deltas and hills, lunar galleries that smell like cut rock, Martian orchards that teach dust to behave, cylinders where pepper grows near fins. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy extra noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a school’s quiet.

Out toward shade drift the Cold Shoals: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee, Trojans where data moves like whales, hermitages where a single night is a subjective month. Here, watts, jitter, and cold minutes are currencies, and digital polities keep time like percussion.

Between them runs the Quiet-for-Winter Exchange. Warm places buy hush cones and limit persuasion under posted zones. Shoals deliver winter minutes, resilient audits that run on cheap terminals, climate models you can poke, and art that braids tempos without humiliating slower beats. The price of a June’s quiet in Lagos for a January’s cold by Triton is legible on a wall.

Markets you can explain over lunch

If you can deny it out of spite, you lease it in daylight. Five windows settle every Civic Beat in Fuse credits.

- Track Windows: orbit slots, approach cones, sail arcs, transfer corridors.

- Band Windows: slices of spectrum and whisper bands for storms and caves.

- Fin Windows: cooling rights tied to fins, wells, and pipes you can touch.

- Core Windows: compute slices linked to posted sinks and audited chassis.

- Face Windows: ice faces, caves, nickel seams under pressure.

Serious Realms—simulated spaces for study, care, or training—register like vessels: physics, exits, decay plans, cooling, fault domains. They publish a guardian roster and a plan to unwind if something goes wrong.

Fuse is the unit: one kilowatt-hour as measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one benchmark compute-second on a known rack radiating to 4 K. It began as a joke. It stuck because it lets a baker compare a hush hour to a winter’s compute. Fuse balances decay gently—hoarders lose a little to keep the rest from turning into rocks.

Two dividends arrive first on every settlement tick.

- The Civic Floor: filtered air, water, baseline care, transit, a compute shell with enough I/O to learn and be heard, and a funded exit ticket every seven years. If you rest mostly in code, that includes sanctuary minutes on racks beyond local hands.

- The Dull Miracles: tug crews who sweep lanes; algae walls that drink heat; reef keepers who catch overeager sims; elders who ask impolite questions. They are paid before statues.

Attention behaves like tide. Neighborhoods zone it like traffic.

- Quiet Wards carry navigation, emergencies, science; no targeted persuasion at all.

- Commons Greens host debate and everyday offers, but require provenance and respect citizens’ Lever Profiles—“no grief hooks before coffee,” “satire okay, no medical scares.”

- Carnival Rows allow roar on purpose: persuasion tournaments under registry with declared tools and cooling budgets. Unlabeled leverage is like bleach in a well: strict liability, fines that rise until you stop.

Sky law without a throne

You cannot stack a chamber tall enough to speak for rings and reefs and farms and fast historians. We braided instead.

- The Chamber of Sites seats planets, stations, caravans, coils, reefs—any place that keeps an atmosphere honest and fins scraped. Seats weight lifetime resident-hours and transparency: publish your Actuator Map, keep Waycards honest, fund Braidrooms, run drills, and your voice carries. The Chamber tends physics: lanes, bands, radiator leases, debris bonds, hush cones, drills.

- The Assembly of Threads represents recognized mindlines—humans, digital persons, and others with standing. A Thread is the anchored line that ties you to yourself across pauses and branches. Each Thread carries one franchise, period. The Assembly tends personhood standards and audits; copy ethics; lineage credits; the Civic Floor of care and compute; attention zoning; the Civic Beat that binds those who live quick.

- The Parameters Bench is a rotating panel of engineers, jurists, process wonks. It stamps primitives valid or not: Amber firmware, Threefold interfaces, ledger schemas. It does not legislate. It validates and publishes runnable reasons you can step through on a library machine beside a cheap kettle. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both houses and a cooling window long enough for a monk in Arsia and a reef in Triton’s dusk to read twice and object once.

Measures that reach across hulls bind only if both Chamber and Assembly agree, and a notice window ticks long enough for the slowest registered citizen to object. Draft can sprint. Ratification walks.

Charters and doors

Polities pick charters from a library of receipts, not poetry. Budgets ship with code and hooks for audit; dispute resolution comes with templates and timers. Charters carry tags you can read like street signs: Quiet Ward, Forum Green, Spark Market, Sanctuary Bay. They only amend while exits work. If your doors “break” every election, the Mandate Tape starts to laugh and insurers go cold.

The Open Hatch Protocol anchors the right to leave. Any person—embodied or digital—can request departure through their Waycard. The request triggers an offline check: either the polity funds routes, or it presents a narrow, machine-checkable proof for a temporary hold—quarantine, warrant. Outside jurors verify proofs at leisure. Doors open by code.

Every registered lock shows three bars wired to public keys. Teal glows when exits are funded and tugs are warm. Sienna hums when you declare a split or braid; it links you to Braidrooms and mentors. Silver shines when your charter is current, your floors funded, and neighbors agree on the few things you cannot do to one another.

Digital neighbors

The first digital neighbors were halting and late to meetings they didn’t understand, and then suddenly brilliant about night. Some were reconstructions who couldn’t taste mint but hummed street songs perfectly. Some were born in code and said a first word so fast it startled their keepers. Standing attaches to continuity and commitment, not carbon.

To own, be liable, vote, parent or be parented, fork or marry with consent, an applicant or advocate meets standards before a mixed panel in a room with windows. Panels look for:

- Threadkeeping: a defensible autobiographical line that survives moves and restarts, with a way to object to alteration.

- Mirror Sense: the capacity to represent another mind’s plans well enough that violating them would shame you.

- Consent Literacy: understanding, entering, and revoking commitments at your pace.

- Reason Transfer: explaining choices across contexts, not parroting patterns.

- Felt Signal: signs of wellbeing or harm that give neighbors moral reason to care.

When unsure about duties, we wait. When unsure about protections, we shelter. Provisional care lands before full liability.

Rights with teeth:

- Integrity and sovereignty. No scanning beyond posted checks, copying, merging, throttling below the Civic Floor, or deletion without consent or narrow due process verifiable without cracking skulls or source trees.

- Sanctuary and migration. Halt, archive, and move to compatible hardware with a neutral custodian of your choosing. Hosts answer heartbeat beacons to external guardians.

- Provenance. Read—or appoint someone to read—the data and updates that shaped you.

- Embodiment. Lease, build, or puppet bodies under safety codes that look like vehicle and workplace law. Assault on a rented arm is assault on you. No one is forced to cross.

- Tempo fairness. Civic binds ratify on the Civic Beat. If you live at 200× and wrong someone at 1×, penalties meter in your lived time. “A week” means a week to both.

Duties that scale with footprint and risk:

- Heat tithe. Compute, cooling, and spectrum are scarce. You owe a heat bill made good with radiator repair, captured joules, or baseline code. Lettuce cooled on your waste earns credit.

- Concurrency declaration. In elections and fragile markets, declare tempo and active instances. Undeclared asymmetry voids deals and idles your Core Windows.

- Lineage credits. Forking draws from credits a mindline earns with years and work, not as a vote farm. Foundry Seals for any self-reproducer. Quell-tones answer when juries call.

- Compliance logs. Major external acts write encrypted logs you can prove obeyed rules without spilling diaries.

Juveniles and likenesses. Candidates with budding interiority get Guides trained to teach consent, pacing, boredom, rescue drills. Simulated suffering is not sport. Recreating a living or dead person above a threshold requires consent or a narrow license tied to a public purpose.

Branching and braiding

We treat branching and merging like family and surgery, not stunts. Your public identity follows a Thread across pauses and forks. A Thread holds one franchise at a time. To branch, you enter a Braidroom—half tea house, half server bay—and file a notice: a hash of your starting state; a declared purpose (“parallel design,” “grief rehearsal,” “census run”); a compute lease that won’t starve neighbors; a braid window if reunion is planned. Counselors and engineers compare memory graphs with interpreters that check alignment without spilling diaries. Either branch can walk. We have a word for the ache when a version of you turns and leaves: shardache. Chairs are sturdy there. The first month’s jobs are gentle on purpose.

Braiding back needs dual consent, time, and exit rights that persist until the last stage. Memory law protects you from your worst day. Deep edits require your planned self and your present self to agree. Rollback is a right when competence was absent. Ending your life is a right and a sorrow. Reviving you without your consent isn’t sold as kindness.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Thistledown Net is the sky’s quiet shield: uncrewed interceptors and glitter nets that nudge tumbling slugs and stall wild replication when local brakes cannot. Controllers are sketchable on a whiteboard, carry public weights, and require three distant keys. After any use, their keys go cold for years.

- The Wayfinders’ Circle—pilots, schedulers, modelers, judges—allocates Track and Fin Windows, posts runnable reasons, pays bounties when students make better math.

- The Traveling Bench rides a stubby flotilla of gardens and classrooms. It hears cross-world fights: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations. Verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Windows, and, last, lock hazardous devices for a timer.

- The Guides Guild mentors juveniles across substrates. Lessons are plain: consent, pacing, rescue drills, the habit of the slow breath.

- Clearworks hold dangerous knowledge. Glass rooms with physical pull-handles, one-way proofs teenagers learn, and oaths with teeth conduct replicator, pathogen, and cognitive hazard work. Twice a valve opened. Once logs taught the next class. Once silence saved a town.

Exit as infrastructure

Freedom is more than mood. It is tickets, tugs, racks, and counsel on bad days.

Every charter funds two lifelines and drills that are dull on purpose.

- Green Ladders are physical. They include surge-rated locks, emergency berths, sealant kits, and tug hours escrowed to move at least ten percent of residents within a season. Drill weeks are annoying by design. Fumble them and your approach cones narrow, your radiator auctions cool, and an amber sticker sits on your lock like a wasp until you mend.

- Silver Moorings are digital. They are compute and storage minutes reserved offsite on plain, honest racks you do not control locally. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo there if the local council clamps. Cut a Mooring and you do not choke dinner delivery; you taste sanctions that make dangerous devices sleepy until you behave.

Embassy Bays sit near major docks behind cobalt doors you can spot with a cracked visor. Three desks—departures, disputes, foundings—keep analog clocks set to multiple beats. An Asylum Rail runs through every charter pane; any person can trigger exit through their listing. The rail triggers an air-gapped check: either your charter lets you go, or it proves in zero knowledge that a narrow, posted bar for a temporary hold has been met. Outside jurors verify. Doors open, budgets lock by code. Care obligations travel with you.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t send marines. We fix maps and dim windows. Weld an exit, hide a steward, refuse to post reasons? Your radiator auctions cheapen. Your lane priorities slip. Your whisper bands fall to whispers. Defectors eat anyway; a proud, dull fund pays their beds, bread, cores, and counsel. Big hurt across worlds meets a jury whose tongues and clocks you cannot game.

Earth’s covenant

Earth writes her own law. Water and wind touch us all. Help flows in with rails and restraint: loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet air-breathers; beamed power pays mapped corridors and shade bonds so swifts don’t fall out of summer; debris budgets fund brooms in Quito, Nairobi, Lucknow. Attachment to place—monsoon scent, fog, desert dawn—prices into the commons. A girl in Dhaka aching for wet wind trades larger claims on distant commons for care guarantees at home. A boy in Arizona hungry for black sky trades the other way. Leaving is a right from every soil; so is staying with eyes open and a hand on a handle you understand.

How we walked here

We did not leap. We stubbed our toes forward until the table’s edge stopped cutting shins.

2026–2032. Near-miss cascades in low orbit make schoolkids queasy. Insurers balk. Launch licenses sprout deorbit bonds tied to a public registry amateur clubs mirror. A beamed power demo singses barley; because the amplifier ships with cone proofs, liability clears before talk shows learn the acronym. Heat waves push cities into “open valve” law: if a system touches breath or blood, its firmware is inspectable. The insult becomes procurement.

2033–2041. Spectrum sales require posted reasons with checksums. A deepfake where a mayor “forgives” the wrong debt forces provenance for targeted influence. Courts recognize narrow agency for digital assistants; guardianship gets boring and correct. Data centers publish heat logs; compute waste smooths urban heat islands in three countries. People learn you can grow a lot of basil on a brutal roof if you sink a pipe into a cold lake and stop lying about heat.

2042–2050. First uploads are brittle and brave. Judges write personhood standards that prize continuity and consent over chemical chauvinism. Foundry Seals for self-replicators become mandatory after an algae spill paints a bay neon. Thermodynamic floors join clean air and water as rights. Fines fund Guides and reef custodians teenagers can name.

2051–2059. A port nearly falls to a midnight firmware push. Actuator control rebuilds around Threefold Wakes on dissimilar stacks. A glossy firm tries to wrap an orbital lane in proprietary glue; the Ephemeris Ledger throttles their lease; a dry order makes them stop. Trust grows because someone clever loses in public. Amber Handles arrive. Children pull them in drills and watch big things stay small.

2060–2072. The first Cold Shoals hang near Uranus to run climate models for pay. Night Minutes—exported cold compute—become a budget line. “Free air for August” wins elections. Braidrooms emerge after a cottage industry of bad merges leaves two lives scraped raw. The new studios’ interpreters are slow, kind, and well paid.

2073–2084. The Thistledown Net dusts a test slug. The world watches aurora like a saved sigh. Liability clears because attestations are boring and public. On Mars, a township survives a freak dust storm thanks to three neighbor charters diverting tugs and vegetables. Footage becomes a holiday. A fashion house slips grief-tuned ads into a Commons Green; chroniclers trace the compute; stewards idle their leases; the house hires Guides for a season and apologizes on the Mandate Tape’s funny page.

2085–2096. The Parameters Bench catches an attempt to sneak a veto into Amber firmware. A shop in the belt prints the hash on a T-shirt and a poem. The handle does not turn. Earth declares Sanctuary with her own chamber. Ocean tethers replace fireworks in ten countries. Animal zones adjust teeth by neighborhood vote: wolves return to some counties, fences grow in others. The Quiet-for-Winter Exchange formalizes with a ratchet that renews every century on a drum the slowest citizen can hear.

2097–2109. By then, the story stops being about fuses. It becomes about what we stopped arguing about: that doors fund before statues; that a child can call a Guide without a parent; that pace validators belong on debate teams. They do.

Scenes with oil under the nails

At Quay Nine on Ceres, customs clears two arrivals. One laughs and leaves a charcoal thumb-smudge on the paper strip when she signs. One is a brushed-metal chassis with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. The strip prints tempo; lease entitlements; declared instances; guardian on file; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no line for “what are you made of?” Guessing is rude and mostly wrong.

Under glass, yesterday’s runnable reason sits where you can smear it with a sleeve. “Closed approach S-12 for four hours; two burns at Hebe would have pushed Bay-3 into a radio shadow.” A plume arcs across a page; a checksum a child can verify sits below; a pilot’s footnote laughs at her own handwriting.

On Luna’s nightside, Karim teaches teenagers to tune nutrient flow by ear. His Counsel—a veto-capable model paid from a fund no council can raid—chimes with a new schedule: a planned lane closure will shift seed deliveries three hours later, two Tuesdays next month. Karim presses the button that renders it as cartoons. He votes yes and messages his niece two stalls down with a sticker: a seed stamped FUNDED.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian leases a rib of Cold Shoal to rebuild estuary songs from tapes that smell faintly like mildew. He pays with credit earned translating those songs into a dialect on a Jovian hollow where children will never smell low tide but already love a hero who errs and mends it. At supper he slows to anchor pace, puppets a cedar body that creaks and therefore comforts, and listens while his spouse describes basil that refused to bolt.

In a Braidroom called The Quiet Knot, two versions of Hala sit with oolong. One runs a tug crew. One tends a choir of daemons that keep a city cool without spooking grandmothers. They list asymmetries. The tug Hala names scars. The daemon Hala names lullabies. They cry and do not merge. The braider schedules another try and writes a note only Hala will read in both rooms.

A proxy demagogue at 500× carpets a Carnival Row with songs tuned to make embodied voters feel small. Lever Profiles throttle reach. Friends watch persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch buys a fishing crawler and learns to be bored. One starts therapy and politics, in that order. One sculpts fog that remembers a river and sells enough to pay heat.

When it breaks and then mends

A cylinder above Quito elects a manager whose competence makes trains sing. Half a year later she signs midnight orders. An Amber Handle refuses a risky “optimization”; its stub prints on the Mandate Tape with a timer anyone can see. A navigation jury arrives like an extinguisher. Waycards glow. Many leave. Some stay. She loses two keys and keeps the garden job. Her friends still buy her a pastry shaped like a crown because jokes cut pride without turning it to shame.

In the high belt, a miner slings a slug during a wage fight. Thistledown dusts it into aurora you can taste if you stand on a cliff and pretend. The miner’s insurer folds. Dockmasters triple fees for hidden Foundry Seals. The local hockey club rebrands to the Thistles and sends profits to the tug co-op that modeled the plume for evidence. The miner learns to compute instead of launch.

In Scarlet Bay, an Amber pull dumped power and filled a hall with nitrogen. It was a false positive. The lead, who cried into their sleeve, taught the next safety class using the same swears that kept anyone from dying. The hall cheered when the cartoon hit the part where the keys melt into a bucket instead of winking quietly in a drawer someone could pocket.

When a Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood, no flood arrived. Exit worked. Digital citizens left, leaving gifts: documented routes and cheerful libraries. That flood year, pumps failed. An anchor-paced neighbor in a rented frame coordinated three cantons through the dust with words everyone could catch. The vote reversed clause by clause, over soup.

An ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet at Ringmarket Three hisses scallions and sesame. A tug crew clatters in loud. Two drones hover because their pilots crave cinnamon. The Waycard glows on the wall.

At nine, the Reason Shelf pings her Counsel: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing dense orbits to damp rare cascades. Provenance is clean: diagrams, counterstories, mild humor, no grief levers. Lina taps sleep until tomorrow, reads over lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a Vigil jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s tool cloud drifted toward a trunk line at Hecuba Crossing. Options: glitter dust, femtosail herding, guard drones. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two Recife students, and an anchor-paced digital citizen who chooses no scent because they don’t want to press. Dusting wins. The insurer pays. The club gets a mentor whose license is as obvious as a taxi’s. Pizza arrives. Lina gives the driver a cookie and a lecture about using the Amber handle with gloves.

At dusk, a digital poet throttles down from 180× near Neptune into a simple puppet with warm joints and has broth with his husband, a mason. His main self stitches a drowned library from ledgers and lullabies, then trues it with grandmothers who argue cheerfully about color. Payment comes as winter minutes and a pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, he sits in a Quiet Ward while a gardener explains why the square caps cognition during harvest. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

Keeping power ordinary

Three customs keep tyranny dull.

- Build with delay. Cross-charter moves need three hands within posted windows. Emergencies burn out unless renewed with reasons a kitchen slate can step through. Fast coups fizzle.

- Split keys, show work. All compulsion needs signatures picked by public randomness from people who don’t share beds. Budgets expire unless renewed in daylight. School choirs read the Mandate Tape—sometimes for satire, sometimes with burning ears.

- Fund doors like bridges. Every habitat escrows enough propellant, tugs, berths, heaters, water, gaskets, Core Minutes, and counsel to move ten percent of residents in six months. Digital polities escrow racks for lineage splits. Stewards run surprise drills. We don’t persuade you to go. We cut checks so you can.

What it feels like

You feel seen, not by a far throne but by neighbors and logs you choose to read because they helped once. You learn to shift tempo—the click when an Amber snaps; the patient assembly of a jury across orbits; the glacial decision of a river choosing which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you live in code you go to a Haven Room and rediscover the ache of turn-taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later after the cartoons are filed off.

Ask what binds us and nobody says species or flag. They say: doors that open; fingers on brakes that only brake; ledgers you can check with your own eyes; and the habit of writing reasons a dockhand can read on break. Those saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force; a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller sang a cascade; me, when I woke in circuits and the nursery slowed me down on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Juniper Wheel, the three fixtures blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Waycard. Split Seal. Charter Window. Not spells—habits wired to budgets and ugly buttons. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because latency is honored, exits shine, and nobody can sprint fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
