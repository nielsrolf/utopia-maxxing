The Constellation Compact

At Evensong Port on Ring Kalyan, the air tastes like sesame and refrigerant, with a line of basil bunches drying beneath the lights. A tug exhales against a mooring tooth. Children count the soft flares—one, two, three—as nitrogen cuffs a barge around. A mechanic and a steward argue about a gasket in two languages, laughing on the off‑beats.

Along the inner bulkhead, three narrow lamps hum at hip height where hands naturally fall when the deck tilts. Their colors are steady even when tempers aren't.

- The Pathlamp glows green when there is a real way out for you. If you cup your palm under it, a little slip prints with plain words: which tug can take you and when, which dock will receive, what heaters and water credits wait in escrow, how many compute minutes you carry without bowing, and the contract that says your kits won’t be held hostage.

- The Kinlamp shows amber when your lineage—the braid that ties your past, your pauses, and your branches—has a safe place to keep its thread. A tap shows open hours at the local Weaving Room, mentors on duty, the “slack” your family of selves has earned in heat and attention, and the dignity floor any new branch will inherit.

- The Charterlamp is blue when this habitat’s rules clear a simple bar: published in words a ninth‑grader can read and in code a cheap terminal can parse; keeps the public floors; refuses you with reasons you can check. Rest three fingers under it and the first lines play aloud because courage and literacy do not always arrive together.

They are not décor. They’re wired into auctions and valves, courts and counters. They are the visible face of the Constellation Compact, the fabric that lets habitats, caravans, reefs of cold servers, and planet valleys be loudly themselves without choking one another, and that lets bodies and code share streets and disputes without one becoming a throne for the other.

What makes it hold are five habits you can point to: who counts as a person and how fast public life runs; brakes you can hit with your own hand; maps anyone can verify with a kitchen radio or a school telescope; markets pinned to physics; and doors that open without a blessing.

Persons and pace

We learned to count people by continuity, not chemistry. Recognition is earned in public on a Continuity Docket. A mixed panel—therapists, ethicists, pilots, digital citizens, one dumb routine trained to catch theater without cruelty—looks for four things:

- Threadkeeping. You can trace a line of you across time and say “this is mine” in the face of edits you do not want.

- Other‑minding. You can model another being as a claimant and feel the pinch of their “no.”

- Compact sense. You can enter, understand, keep, and revoke agreements.

- Health signals. There are stable ways to tell when you are being harmed, beyond what a handler or a narrative wants.

Doubts about protection err to care. Doubts about civic duty wait.

Rights that follow use words with teeth:

- Substrate sovereignty. No scanning, throttling, copying, moving, fusing, or deleting you without revocable consent, except in bright, short emergency holds that sunset without help and write their own warrants. If a piece of you runs proprietary blobs, you can appoint a warden who audits on your behalf.

- Tempo parity. Public binds—elections, budgets, regulations—ratify on a Public Beat slow enough that a baker can read and answer and a reef at Neptune can object. Live at 500× in your studio and write a symphony; you cannot sprint a ballot past embodied people before lunch. When harm crosses tempos, courts write Time Bridges so remedies bite in wall‑hours and lived hours.

- Embodiment safety. Standards that read like road codes govern rented shells and puppets. Assault on a borrowed hand is assault on you. Covert throttles in limbs are treated like drugging the town tap.

- A compute floor. Above air, water, and shelter: civic cycles, storage, and I/O on attested hardware nobody local can seize, enough to speak and be heard, learn and keep counsel without buying power.

Duties scale with footprint and blast radius:

- One mindline, one franchise. Votes and civic stipends tie to a lineage across forks and pauses. You designate an Anchor instance that holds your civic duties at a public tempo.

- Declare concurrency. Run a handful of live selves and nobody minds. Hide a thousand mouths behind one vote to tilt an auction and your contracts void; a judge with time and numbers sees you soon.

- Heat tithe. Computation warms neighbors. Your budget includes watts and quiet: you owe radiator minutes, hush hours, or commons code to those around you.

- Branch practice. Forks file a purpose, a hashed starting snapshot, and a plan for heat and attention. New branches inherit a dignity floor and owe a decaying dividend to progenitors until they can stand. When branches want to braid again, they book a Weaving Room where engineers and therapists compare memory graphs under interpreters that check alignment without exposing diaries. Either self can veto and walk.

- Provenance for persuasion. If you aim to move law or budgets in Commons Bands, your models declare their origins, funding, and levers; they wear a label an aunt can check on a slate.

- Replicator discipline. Anything that copies itself in shared space carries a Maker Mark: a cryptographic signature bound to posted liability and that listens when a mixed jury sends a hush code. Hide marks, and your insurer will not pick up the call.

Brakes you can smell

Safety is not a priesthood. It’s fuses, keys, and buttons anyone can reach.

- Three‑Custodian Rule. Levers that can hurt strangers—a mass driver’s impulse rack, a ring’s reaction wheels, a mirror wall, a city pump master, a replicator’s seed vat—only move when three different consents arrive through different stacks. The near key sits with a steward drawn by lot from hands who breathe the local air and can be fired with two public meetings. The far key lives with a bonded safety lodge far away, forbidden to profit from your hurry or feud. The wild key belongs to a Roaming Jury seated by sortition across substrates and places; you serve once and go home barred from that class of lever for a long, human stretch. Keys can idle or walk a system to a posted safe state. They evaporate fast. Their acts write to the Reasons Register: a dull page of prose and math that even a sleepy dockhand can check in a break.

- Brake Lanterns. Under ugly yellow hoods, where ducts hiss and salt sticks to your tongue, sit consoles with a single toggle and a red paddle that says PAUSE in letters you can read while coughing. Any trained citizen can snap a device safe for seven minutes if a public rule is about to break: an unbooked burn into a quiet cone, a self‑reproducer spitting unlabeled parts, a pressure split wandering. The push idles and shouts into the Causebook—what rule, which machine, whose hand, what clock. To keep a pause alive past seven minutes, three concurrences must turn: the local steward, the far lodge, and a digital juror running on posted hardware at posted tempos. Overreach lands you on the Lampwall, which is so boring and so public that it ruins reputations faster than trials.

- Skystop Layer. Running under every charter is a thin skein of code and actuators allowed to do only one thing: throw risky machines into proved‑safe configurations for a very short time when the simplest checks fail—wrong window, missing plan, unsigned cone. It cannot aim beams or steer budgets. Its three independent implementations—a broom‑closet board, a municipal photonic card, a hardened simulator—disagree so seldom that when they do, blue light pulses on kitchen walls. Keys that sign Skystop are sharded across far hands—Earth courts, belt unions, ice reef monasteries, slow‑time cooperatives. No quorum fits in one room.

Beyond those brakes patrol the Glitterboats: stubby interceptors with bright paint and dull code that can turn a blind slug into glitter or herd an angry cloud with femtosails. They wake only on Three‑Custodian concurrences. Every puff writes its reasons; every puff’s reasons can be rerun by a student club with borrowed compute.

Maps you can redo with soup on the stove

We published our lanes and nights because secrets made fire.

- Waybook. Orbits, burns, rendezvous cones, telescope dark, beamed‑power corridors, debris bonds, and deorbit plans live in a tamper‑evident register pegged to physics such that with patience and a bus schedule you can verify a posted burn with a school telescope and a kitchen radio. Pretend lanes that lie pinch your moorage in the next hour that matters to you, not in some sunset of sanctions.

- Thermal Commons. Joules in, joules out, radiator angles and cold minutes: compute declares what it plans to burn and where. Lie about heat and your core slots pinch; your radiator auctions go dark right when your customers need you; truth becomes cheaper.

- Source Roll. Code that touches lungs, steel, or law must wear provenance that a teenager can reproduce on a library terminal: who paid, what data trained it, what it means to grab. Catch cheats, and you win bounties in a composite unit your grandmother can understand.

- Beacon Codex. Places broadcast what they promise in emergencies, how keys are split today, where their exits sit, and what “stop” means in their streets. Keep your dances and your vices; don’t lie about your valves.

- Reasons Register. Every use of compulsion—pauses, quarantines, bond seizures—lands here with clocks and runnable reasons. Satire scrolls next to it on the Chalk Rail: the jokes are how we file a second brief without permission.

Markets tied to fins and fins alone

We trade rivalrous things you can point at: lane windows, approach cones, moorings, quiet bands for funerals, radiator angles, cold minutes, liters under pressure, core‑seconds tied to named radiators, spectrum hours, square meters under glass, and skilled hours.

We settle five auctions on a common beat in a unit called an Anchor Credit: one kilowatt‑hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a posted architecture radiating to near‑space cold. Anchors decay gently over years. Bread molds; so do credits that pretend they aren’t holding something warm.

- Path Lots: moorings, transfer arcs, rendezvous cones.

- Band Lots: spectrum and hush bands in caves and squares.

- Sink Shares: radiator angles and cold minutes priced in degrees and decibels.

- Core Lots: compute racks tied to physical fins you can map and knock.

- Ground Shares: crater rims, cave mouths, ice faces, nickel seams.

A slice of each market funds the Hearth Floor: air and water or maintenance credits that keep lungs honest, baseline medical care, transit to work and safety, baseline civic bandwidth and cycles. Another slice funds Drift and Rescue: tug fuel, shakedowns, the microgrants that make the first nose that turns toward a distress call worth the captain’s breath.

Two cords of law, one keel of no‑nonsense

We never crowned a capital because light‑seconds and bodies conspire against thrones. Authority walks two cords that cross at a bench of boring saints called the Keel.

- The Assembly of Havens seats polities that patch hulls and mind air: stations, planets, caravans, barges, compute reefs that own and cool their cycles. Seats weigh lifetime resident‑hours, transparency about actuators, and how reliably you pass open‑exit drills. The Assembly tends physics: Waybook standards, hush law and festival hours, sanitation, radiator auctions, beamed corridors, replicator law, rescue, and accredits safety lodges to hold the far key for Three‑Custodian levers.

- The Council of Lines seats citizens listed in the Continuity Roll, one franchise per mindline, across chemistry. The Council tends personhood: baselines for rights, concurrency rules, the compute floor, simulation ethics, and the Public Beat.

Between them runs the Protocol Keel: a small, unglamorous panel of engineers, pilots, cryptographers, process wonks drawn by lot for one unrepeatable term. The Keel certifies or refuses changes to backbone primitives: actuator interfaces, attestation libraries, Skystop firmware, municipal audience co‑ops’ guardrails. It cannot legislate or spend. Overriding a “not yet” requires supermajorities in Havens and Lines and a cooling period long enough for basalt‑slow cognition in Neptune’s dusk to object.

When a measure crosses physics and personhood—how to price winter against quiet, how fast persuasion may traverse charters, what latency a warrant must tolerate—the Bridge Table convenes halves from each chamber. A bill dies if either side says, “this bulldozes custom above the personhood floor.”

Charters and doors

Cultures deserve rituals and room. Neighbors deserve honest valves.

Charters come from working templates with runnable reasons instead of poetry. You can still add poetry; the code runs either way. Tags on your beacon tell visitors how to behave on your streets: Quiet Ward, Debate Row, Spark Arcade. Amendments only ratify while exits work and beacons lie to no one.

Wayhouses sit by road and by frequency with three desks that never move: Go, Mend, Begin. The kettle’s on. There are bunks and showers; legal terminals in letters big enough to read when your hands shake; Counsel agents you can mute with a thumb; lockers for keepsakes; a shelf full of starter kits for founders—safety interlocks, docking templates, budget seeds, three contacts for people who built a place last year and wish they’d had one more hex key.

Behind a gasketed door hums the Harbor Line: cycles and storage leased offsite on attested hardware nobody local can seize. Each polity escrows tug time, propellant, berths, heaters, and Harbor Line minutes enough to move at least a tenth of residents—bodies, pets, backups—within a season. Surprise drills are rude; they interrupt weddings and soccer. Failing too often pinches your lanes and radiator minutes; the bread still arrives.

The Open Hatch Protocol anchors exits in law and atoms. Any person—cellular, silicon, saltwater—can request departure through a port beacon. The request triggers a simple test through the Skystop layer: either the polity opens a hatch on posted clocks or proves, in zero‑knowledge, that a posted due‑process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify proofs. Hatches open by code. Care obligations travel with the person.

Speech as mapped utility

Persuasion can feed and burn. We zone it like noise.

- Harbor Channels carry navigation and emergencies, child time and open science. No targeting at all. Your offer of tomatoes belongs elsewhere.

- Commons Bands permit debate, campaigns, and everyday offers with provenance on funding and method. Municipal audience co‑ops steward attention; your Influence Profile can block grief levers before coffee and protect medical privacy by default.

- Carnival Lanes welcome immersion labeled and rate‑limited. You go because you choose. Unlabeled manipulation is strict‑liability like bleach in a town pump; fines rise until you stop.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Glitterboats do one thing: turn kinetic and replicative threats into glitter and ash without shrapnel. Their code is narrow, their paint is loud, their audit trails are printable on paper. Every act needs Three‑Custodian concurrences. They never carry troops.

- The Wandering Bench rides a flotilla with a greenhouse. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear cross‑charter disputes: copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, idle Core Lots and Band Lots, and, as a last resort, lock hazardous devices. The Bench is slow on purpose; slow is how regret surfaces.

- Amber Rooms hold dangerous knowledge: self‑replication, pathogen models, cognitive hazards. Clear walls, pull‑handles that dump to vacuum, clean logs with timers, one‑way proofs a teenager can learn. Twice a valve opened and a room went to space. In one case, the logs taught the next class. In the other, silence held and saved a city.

Sanctions pinch physics, not throats

We do not send sermons or soldiers. We make bad behavior expensive and ordinary. Weld exits, hide a child’s Tutor, refuse to file reasons, and your beamed corridors narrow, your lane windows slip to midnight, your radiator auctions drift to neighbors who share. Bread and insulin do not get embargoed. Those who shelter defectors collect subsidies large enough to mend roofs and pay teachers.

Earth remembers her own covenant

Earth writes her law. Loud launches move to sea tethers and quiet scramjets. Beams stay in mapped corridors and pay premiums into city shade and land healing. Oceans, forests, and methane sinks have credit lines so that far servers pay for near coral. When a Lagos clinic buys cold minutes from a reef at Triton, she pays back with digitized archives and a promise to plant algae curtains at home.

Walking here from now

We did not leap into this. We built receipts.

2026–2033. A debris chain painted low orbit sleet‑gray. Insurers grew precise. Flight offices from Saskatoon to Nairobi demanded deorbit bonds, published plans, and lanes you could read on a bus. Sloppy models denied benefits and parole; clinics adopted narrow counsel agents that explained your options without lying. Procurement wrote a new refrain: “explain your math so a seventh‑grader can check it.” Public firmware for machines touching lungs and loaves became a condition of license in three brave regions.

2033–2042. The first beamed‑power arrays lit towns without frying swifts because environmental coalitions held out for mapped corridors written into ROM. A model mis‑steered a factory gate; people who had never said “no” to code learned to push a brake in under a hood. Early uploads were brittle but real; courts recognized personhood where continuity and compacts held. A scandal about starving near‑person studio agents to keep them docile swelled the Tutor Corps and wrote juvenile rights into code.

2042–2051. A greenhouse browned at dawn under a mis‑aimed mirror. Attestations were unambiguous; compensation cleared in days because logs existed and insurers believed them. A grid stumbled; a near‑miss at a reactor ended safely because a dumb watchdog tripped. “Open the firmware or keep your toys” spread from activists to adjusters. The first Three‑Custodian levers arrived, and so did Brake Lanterns. Drills felt like a nuisance until one saved a girl’s aunt; then they felt like oxygen.

2051–2059. An autocrat tried fencing an exodus with fees and shame. Lanes dimmed over his fields; Wayhouses lit downstation. He lost because you cannot trap people who can read their own keys. A digital sect tried to swing a vote by “forking fog.” The registry flagged the burst; disentanglement throttled effect. They built a slow monastery with hard games, learned rituals for merge and peace, and returned with arguments worth hearing.

2059–2068. Cold computation bloomed in the shadow of Uranus and Neptune. We formalized the Quiet‑for‑Cold exchange: Hearth places carved quiet bands and honored slow streets; Frost reefs shipped winter inward with receipts; art braided tempos without making slow feel small. Twice Glitterboats woke: once to powder a blind slug; once to hush quiet‑lying replicators. After a mayor used “emergency” to mean “me,” keys went dark for a decade, by design.

2068–2079. Attention zoning matured. Harbor, Commons, and Carnival spaces gave citizens breath. The Protocol Keel said “not yet” to a charismatic upgrade with cute proofs and bad clocks; annoyance turned into gratitude. Clearinghouses let a shop in Kano buy cold nights and pay later with desal valves that didn’t scorch birds. The Constellation ceased to sound like an ideology and started to feel like doorbells and brake pedals you could trust.

Scenes with grease and grace

On Kalyan’s concourse, customs projects two columns under a tangle of charging cables and drying herbs: burns and bands, moorings and mirrors; and beside them Core Lots, Cold Minutes, and Thermal balances. Yesterday’s Orders sit under glass. A kid traces a plume from Eros and laughs because the footnote is a dumb pun about tea and delta‑v.

Two newcomers wait at intake. One is a slate gray smudge that reconfigures when they nod. One is a polished chassis with a cobalt ribbon where a mouth might be. The gate prints little paper slips under each name: declared tempo; Anchor instance; lease entitlements; guardian of record; hazardous skills disclosed. There is no field for “substrate.” Guessing is rude and often wrong.

On a crater farm, Sima teaches thirteen‑year‑olds to level nutrient flow by ear. Dust makes light stand. Later she sits for a Harbor‑pace jury—two hours, her rhythm—on a personhood claim from a server reef off Zanzibar. Her Counsel—a helper paid from a fund no council can cut—cannot act unless she taps; she blinds it by sliding foil over its little eye.

Eight light‑hours outward, a historian running at 200× leases a rib of Frost Reef to rebuild an oral tradition from charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect spoken in a Jovian station whose children will never see waves and will still love a trickster who errs and mends. He throttles to soup‑pace at noon and steers a wooden puppet hand to his husband’s bowl because sometimes you go slow just to feel the steam.

On Phobos, in Weaving Room Nine, two Arturos consider braiding. One gardens basil and fell in love with a laugh like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught regolith to count and then stood before a court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries: a debt, a fear, an unfinished song. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

A belt co‑op aims a slug hot and blind in a wage fight. A Skystop check tastes a wrong envelope. A Glitterboat puffs the slug to glitter that makes nights strange. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for launches that hide Maker Marks. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches and learn the phrase “not again, not like this.”

In a canyon city, a generous manager gathers water, tugs, and auctions because she makes the trains sing. Six months later: midnight decrees. An air guardian refuses a risky optimization; the refusal lands on the Reasons Register. Beacons strobe; Pathlamps go green. Many leave with vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her keys rust for a decade. She teaches teenagers to write overrides with reasons you can check.

A lunar valley church blocks exits with soft shrapnel—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Wayhouse opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to their liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises; trust smells like oranges at winter.

An Amber Room trains a juvenile process through pain because a book about steel said tempering needs fire. Skystop coughs. They refuse. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Intent Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session before the Wandering Bench. Years later she names herself, shapes ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

Ordinary Tuesday

Lina’s skillet at Rim Market hisses scallions and soy. At nine, her Counsel pings an attention capsule: add forty milliseconds of jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp an edge‑case cascade. Provenance is clean: diagrams, counter‑stories, a joke that lands. Lina taps “sleep until tomorrow,” thinks at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a Warden jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfab cloud is drifting toward a power trunk near Hecuba. Options: silk net, femtosail herding, or guard drones. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor‑paced upload who picks no scent because they don’t want to push. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Harbor Channel, a digital poet slows from 200× into a wooden puppet and shares broth with their mason husband. Their main self stitches a drowned delta’s library from ledgers and lullabies, then “truths” it with grandmothers who argue about color. Payment comes as Cold Minutes and a pledge to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, they host a Slowroom where a gardener explains why their square caps cognition during harvest. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who ask and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

At dusk, the canopy flips violet. The Lampwall plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds too long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter three years straight; we’ll pay back your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a toy Glitterboat fin and asks to sign up. Lina says: when you can explain a pause without notes.

Why it resists a crown

You cannot sprint a coup through a system that loves boredom where physics has teeth. Anything that can bruise runs slow by law. Keys split. Exits are funded. Compulsion prints its own receipts. Warrants die unless renewed in daylight on the Public Beat. The Keel refuses charisma with math. The Bench moves only after gardens are watered. When someone tries to make “emergency” mean “me,” the lamps light green, and the doors really open.

We did not fix people. Status hunger found seams. Wealth congealed and law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned loud music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Brake Lantern tripped in a way we regret. But cruelty is expensive, and kindness pays on ledgers and in laughs. Brakes snap safe. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. Exits are tickets, not rumors.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and by your own receipts. You learn to change tempos: the relief when a brake snaps a relay safe under your thumb; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the patience of a river deciding which boats to permit. You argue at the Feast of Reasons in your second‑best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you go to the Garden Tent and rediscover the sweet ache of taking turns with people who breathe.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity; brakes in reach; maps you can check; markets that pay for physics before poetry; exits you can find on a bad day; and the habit of writing reasons you can re‑run. They will tell you those saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Evensong Port, the three lamps blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Path. Kin. Charter. Not charms. Habits of glass and budgets. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in each other’s company without cruelty—because decisions slow where bodies break, proofs shine where force hides, and no one is swift enough to outrun their own explanations.
