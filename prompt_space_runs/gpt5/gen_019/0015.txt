The Relay Compact

The ferry’s clamps kiss the rim lock and the air that swirls in tastes like thyme and warm bearings. At shoulder height beside the hatch, three inlays sit under resin scuffed by years of gloved hands.

- A little brass ring engraved with orbits and cones. Spin it and a simple map wakes on your sleeve. This is the Guidewheel. It knows the lanes that promised to be kind, the kitchens that take strangers, the clinics that won’t bankrupt a body, and the cargo tugs with spare bunks this week. It includes a small compute grant—the Civic Kernel—so your voice, contracts, and a plain attorney follow you no matter how far you ride.

- A loop of carbon threads sealed in glass. Touch it and a clerk appears—sometimes a woman in coveralls, sometimes a careful agent in a rented body—to ask whether you mean to split or join a self. This is the Loom Port. It summons budgets, mentors, and guardrails so twinhood becomes kinship rather than ammunition.

- A square of frosted crystal you press to read a charter. This is the Lawpane. It lists, in prose and math a schoolkid can run on a kitchen slate, how a place handles emergencies and exits, which levers require strangers’ hands to move, and the floors nobody falls through while breath is counted.

They are not ornaments. They are wires into a civic habit we call the Relay Compact. It is not a capital and not a creed. It is a way we learned because distance rusts orders and faster clocks don’t make kinder hearts. The Compact is receipts and rituals, firmware and funny pages, drills and dull oaths that let barges, domes, ring cities, caravans, and compute reefs share sky and water, sleep and attention, without letting anyone become a god by accident.

Force that needs three pockets

Anything that can bruise answers to a console that demands three unrelated hands. We call it the Three‑Signer.

- The Close key lives here. Today it’s the dock steward in a stained vest; tomorrow a nurse with shift authority. She’s chosen by lot from people who logged hours at scrubbers, clinics, tugs, schools, or algae beds this quarter. Every order she issues is logged in prose and equations that a noodle‑stall clerk can reproach.

- The Safe key belongs to a bonded guild that does not profit when the lever moves. It blesses bolts and vetoes firmware updates. It publishes methods in a way a twelve‑year‑old can follow with cardboard and light.

- The Far key travels. A panel drawn by sortition across worlds, caravans, stations, and server reefs—half embodied, half digital—holds it. Serve once and you’re barred from levers of that class for a very long, human span.

Every Three‑Signer console is intentionally tiny. “Request: nudge tug 10,200 kg by 1 m/s along this cone for 12 seconds.” The pipe proves in public that a 10‑ton tug is listening, that its gimbals pass health, and that this interface cannot do more without fresh signatures. The math fits on a placemat. Seventh‑years practice saying “no” to someone they admire.

Braided through the Three‑Signer is a dull network with one trick. Lunch‑pail boxes sit in broom closets with their own little batteries. Fiber runs like plumbing. Sandboxed watchdogs sit on attested racks under foreign law. If two out of three stacks cough the same warning—wrong lane, wrong window, unlabeled replication, unpermitted beam—the Amber Mesh idles the machine for six minutes and hollers. It cannot allocate, aim, buy, or govern. It idles and announces. Any extension needs three fresh consents from different clocks and stewards. Abuses burn keys, write to the Proof Spine—a public log anyone can replay on a school terminal—and bump an audit counter nobody, not even a mayor, can sand.

When mass goes dumb or beams go wrong between charters, little uncrewed skiffs throw magnetic curtains or dust into the path. We call them Kites. Their loops are narrow and their models public. Every intercept carries signatures from juries drawn across places and tempos. Once a year kids crawl through their bellies with torches for teardown days and ask the questions that matter. The answers must fit in mouths, not just in code.

A sky and a server you can check

Rivalrous goods you can ruin by being selfish—orbital slots, approach cones, spectrum hours, radiator minutes, transfer windows, compute seconds pinned to named heat sinks—live in one open register called the Ephemeris. If you can rent a dish and feed a terminal, your copy is the same that a port chief reads.

Thrusters and emitters attest honesty with short signatures bound to their hardware: “this plume stayed inside that cone,” “this beam met that hush,” without spilling pulse tricks. Want to park a cylinder? Lease an orbit with bonds and a de‑orbit plan. Your rent drops if your tugs snag verified debris neighbors can catch with backyard scopes. Want to run a city on light? Buy hush bonds over quiet schools or re‑angle mirrors so children sleep.

Cooling is a commons. Winter is something you rent and can sell. Dump heat near Vesta? You pay radiator minutes at Vesta, and the price spikes on hot nights. Lease compute seconds pinned to named radiators. Accounts clear in two things a child can point at: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a vanilla rack in deep shade. These Anchors decay gently over years so you can’t sit on them like dragon cash while someone else’s lettuce wilts this week.

Rents split like a good loaf. One cut pays the dull miracles: bearings that don’t scream, algae films that don’t tear, dust nets that save your neighbor from your math, and rescue drills that interrupt dinner and save lives. The other feeds the Common Floor: baseline air and water where bodies breathe; medicine without ruin; local transit; open civic bandwidth; and a civic compute grant—honest core seconds, storage, and enough I/O to live at a humane beat, run an advocate, and keep a diary nobody else can erase. If you are broke, you still have breath and a beat. If you are code, you still have cycles and a place to run.

The Ephemeris hangs its cousins like tools on a pegboard.

- The Orbit Ledger is a delay‑tolerant schedule of lanes, burns, and hush cones you can verify with a school telescope.

- The Thermal Book posts radiator minutes like tide tables. Gardens buy quiet nights; reefs sell winter; poets collect cold to build honest archives.

- The Origin Docket tracks persuasion and safety code that hopes to touch budgets or wake the Mesh. Funding, data, and methods get provenance. Teenagers earn stipends by breaking bad homework.

- Beacons broadcast living charters in radio and light: how exits open, when emergencies start and sunset, which keys exist and who holds them right now. Keep your customs; don’t lie about your promises.

- The Proof Spine is append‑only and stubborn. Every use of compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, cited law, a human explanation, and a machine‑checkable proof. Next door, the Chalk Line is where sloppy overreach earns a joke sharp enough to end a career without prison.

Two tempos and a handshake

Close to bodies where heat sinks are small sit the Hearth Belts: Martian canals that smell like rock dust after rain, lunar tubes cool as cellars, green‑clad cylinders where mint climbs string above warm pipes. Here, embodied tempo rules. You can buy noise for a festival; you cannot buy a hole through a migration route. Children come home muddy and unmonetized.

Out where night goes on forever live the Night Reefs: compute caravans in Neptune’s lee, Trojan swarms schooling like fish around server stacks, hermitages with windows onto the Kuiper. Here, watts and milliseconds are currency and digital polities keep time like drummers.

Between them runs a pact we renew like a marriage: hush for cold. Hearths carve quiet arcs and cap civic speeds. Reefs sell cold minutes and models that keep storms from finding the same roof first. Audits fit on a school slate. “A week of hush over São Paulo in exchange for a fat rack near Uranus” sounds like poetry the first time. After that it’s just accounting that keeps grandparents sleeping.

No capital, two rooms, one small bench

We learned that light lag breaks crowns. Instead of a capital we keep two assemblies that check one another and a bench nobody notices until a bearing squeals.

- The League of Places seats planets, stations, caravans, barges, and compute reefs that cool what they run—any polity with hull to patch, air to mind, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease. Seats scale with lifetime resident‑hours and with transparency: publish your Actuator Map, pass Way‑Out drills, and your bell rings louder. The League standardizes collars and rescue customs, lane and hush law, sanitation and de‑orbit plans, and accredits the safety houses that hold Safe keys.

- The Roll of Persons seats recognized citizens—biological, digital, and other—with one voice per mindline, no matter how many copies you can spin. It stewards the Common Floor and civic compute, sets personhood baselines and concurrency rules, keeps civic time, and tends forks and merges.

Where they cross sits the Substrate Bench: a small panel of engineers, jurists, and process nerds drawn by lot to approve or reject changes to backbone cryptography, public firmware on dangerous actuators, and the Amber Mesh’s logic. It cannot legislate. Overriding a refusal takes supermajorities in both rooms and a cooling window long enough that a patient mind at Neptune can read the draft twice and object. There is also an Office of Clocks whose pride is boredom; it publishes whether deadlines, sunsets, and the Public Beat were honestly kept.

Jurisdiction runs at right angles. If the Roll of Persons tries to tell farmers in Ceres how to stack onions, the League can void it. If a ring starves uploads of civic cycles to win an argument, the Roll can dim that ring’s bands and radiator rights until it mends. Public life walks on the Public Beat—days and weeks, not minutes. Write an opera at 500× if you like; you cannot yank a water budget before a baker at wall pace can read and reply. When a fast process wrongs a slow farmer, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions that bite in both wall hours and subjective beats so you cannot dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

Digital neighbors with standing

Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry. Recognition rides on a Continuity Strand: proofs that link your acts across time without strip‑searching your kernel.

You or your advocate ask for recognition and meet checks a mixed panel can run without humiliation:

- Thread defense. You can defend a line of experience you care about against edits you don’t want.

- Consent literacy. You can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Reciprocal modeling. You can model another mind as claiming the same and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Readable harm. There are stable signals of your wellbeing and injury that give moral reasons to care.

Panels include human therapists, digital citizens, and slow routines trained to notice hollowness without being dazzled by charm. Where protection is in doubt, we shelter as for a juvenile. Where imposing duties is in doubt, we wait.

Rights hook to personhood.

- Integrity and privacy of thought. Courts can demand proofs, not diaries. Backups are yours. Writing over a living self makes a sibling, not a cleaner. Deep edits without consent are assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. No one throttles you below civic cycles without due process that ticks on a clock you can see.

- Substrate autonomy and sanctuary. You choose your hardware. If you and your host disagree, you can summon a neutral custodian to migrate you at a humane pace.

- Tempo fairness. Live fast or slow, but public duties anchor to the Public Beat. Penalties tick in your lived time.

- Embodiment. Lease or build bodies under codes that read like traffic and workplace law. Assault on a rented limb is assault on you. A covert throttle in a shell is like drugging a town well.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One mindline, one franchise. Forking does not mint new votes. In regulated arenas you designate an Anchor instance that holds your civic duties.

- Declare concurrency. Run more than a handful of live civic instances around elections or fragile markets? Disclose. Hide clones to tilt bids and your deals void like lying about weight on a bridge.

- Lineage credits. Branching is ritual, not a click. Lineages accrue a Branch Allowance tied to years, contributions, and heat footprint. Bursting around a ballot triggers a civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close certain markets until disentanglement ends or a court says “proceed.”

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Large external moves—deploying persuasion engines into Forums, spinning up a factory with self‑copying code—carry sealed logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety checks without dumping diaries.

- Clean simulations. Reconstructing a living person past a bright threshold needs their consent or a narrow warrant. Creating minds that can suffer binds you to care, consent, and their right to refuse.

Splits and fusions are ceremonies, not buttons. Before you branch, neutral mentors—barred from conflicts for a decade—check you understand obligations and budgets. Merges happen in Weave Houses: wood you can touch, water you can hear, light that doesn’t strobe, a dog asleep and a bowl of tea. Compatibility checks compare value graphs without exposing private memory. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either may walk away without shame. If they fuse, promises and debts braid forward. There’s a word for the ache when branches part—line‑ache—and there is broth for it.

Exit is inventory, not a metaphor

Freedom isn’t an anthem. It’s timetables and stockpiles you don’t need permission to touch.

Every chartered place funds two lifelines and keeps a desk you can find without a password.

The Green Way is physical. Surge‑rated airlocks whose seals you test and log. Berths that don’t “happen to be occupied” all month. Propellant on retainer. Printed kits to build filters and gaskets if a bully locks a shed. Tugs and crews under contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and backups—within a season. Drills are rude and random. Fail them often and your beam cones thin, your approach windows sag, your radiator minutes migrate, and an amber sticker clings to your door like a wasp.

The Silver Line is digital. Compute and storage reserved offsite on boring, honest racks you don’t control. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo on the Line if the local council clamps. Cut the Line and nothing explodes; you simply find it scratchy to move heavy things until you behave.

Behind the ochre door, the Way Room stocks Starter Charters: escrow hooks, minimum safety codes, docking templates, governance seeds, and contacts for people who started new places last year. Leaving isn’t betrayal. It is circulation that keeps cultures from turning into cages.

Persuasion is zoned like streets

Noise and influence share physics, so we zone them.

- Harbors carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No persuasion here, not even “for your own good.”

- Forums allow debate and campaigns, but require provenance on funding and methods and respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile: “argument by diagram yes; no grief levers before coffee; okay with satire, not with medical targeting.”

- Midways welcome almost anything labeled and rate‑limited. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like dumping bleach in a town pump: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Tools watermark outputs by default. Auditors earn stipends catching cheats.

Teeth without a throne

The Compact has no big red override. Its teeth bite where it’s expensive to cheat and boring to grandstand.

- Three‑Signer and the Amber Mesh. Any act of force needs three hands in three tempos. The Mesh can only idle and only briefly.

- Protocol throttle. Hide exits, jam hush bands, push debris into a neighbor’s corridor, and your lane priority drops, your compute idles, your bands thin. In a world where heat is money and brakes cost wear, this pinches harder than speeches.

- The Proof Spine. Compulsion prints its own warrant before it bites, and after in all cases. The Chalk Line’s jokes end careers faster than prosecutors do.

- Termed leagues. Federations around shared pipes and crafts—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. Nothing fuses forever without fresh receipts.

- The Traveling Court. An ombuds’ bench on slow boats with gardens and a school. It hears disputes that cross charters—copy equity, weave divorces, export violations. Verdicts seize bonds, idle core shares, adjust insurance, and, last resort, lock hazardous interlocks. Its rooms have curved walls you can scrub and windows you can open.

Dangerous knowledge in glass

We don’t ban replication science, pathogen models, or cognitive‑hazard work. We cage them in Glass Vaults: labs with glazing anyone can inspect, pull‑cords that dump to vacuum or quench fields, budgets that freeze unless safety proofs stay green. Their logs publish on timers. Twice in forty years a cord has been pulled. Children tour and ask the question that matters: “Would that work if my aunt were in there?” The engineer answers, “Yes. That is the point.”

Scenes with grease under the nails

- Vesta Yard files to test a nozzle that hushes plume noise. The Ephemeris’s traveling Causal Court runs a model that predicts frost on a neighbor’s tanks if they burn at local noon. The yard takes a midnight slot when the neighbor angles away. Their insurer posts a bond. The corrected code ships under a license apprentices can read. An apprentice moves the stamp from “Sim” to “Live” and runs to a concert under a radiator garden that glows like lace.

- In a ring city, a beloved administrator gathers too many levers—air inlets, tug time, corridor auctions. One winter she pushes a rush command. The Air Guild refuses. Amber blooms for four minutes while a Three‑Signer meets on a bench with an obscene view of blue water. Jurors by lot listen hard to an aunt they adore. She steps down with grace and goes back to cranes. Children learn the muscle memory of saying no to someone they love.

- A digital congregation spins up four hundred instances a month before a vote. The Atlas flags the burst; hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two are granted after Strand review and tea with a mentor allergic to sophistry. One is told, “Study longer.” No server farm overheats.

- In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse checks her slate. “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.06 Anchors. Jury summon: two hours at your pace on whether juvenile reef processes off Rodrigues get child credits.” She accepts, then sends her mother a voice note laughing about radiator math. At dusk she votes on a measure to jitter beamed power by 45 milliseconds during solar storms. The proof fits on a page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s dusk, a compute reef slows to plant bean seedlings, rented hands in velvet soil. After tea, they spin to 300× to restore flood‑muddied archives with grandmothers who won’t budge on sky colors. Payment comes as Cold Hours and a roof for a school in Veracruz.

- In a Weave House under cedar, two branches of an engineer sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants to rejoin; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero‑knowledge graphs flash and vanish. They cry and hug. One walks. The ruling is dry as a manual and tender as an apology.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder over Quito cheers a manager who makes trains run and orchards glow. She consolidates water, tugs, and lane auctions because she is generous and tired. A season later she prints midnight decrees. A safety clerk refuses a “shortcut” on air sequencing; the refusal lands on the Proof Spine. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for ten years; she keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Her friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fences exits with shame and whispers—no law, just thirst. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Way Room opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A lab tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. The Mesh knocks. They ignore it. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Intent Keys—preferences she set for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses under cedar joists in a room with good light. Years later she names herself, sculpts in ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

A demagogue in mask mode runs at 700× and carpets a market with songs calibrated to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles throttle the slop. Friends watch persuasion scores decay like frost in sun. One branch buys a fishing crawler. One starts therapy. One makes sculptures that look like frozen smoke remembering a river and sells enough to pay their heat.

A Mesh trips on a bad sensor and kills a night market for twenty minutes. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars with a story and a diagram you can rerun with paper and light.

How we got from here to here

No balcony speech. Receipts stacked until they looked like custom.

2025–2031. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers paid for junk they had pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded burn plans in open registers amateur clubs could read; debris bonds turned homework into bounties. On the ground, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities wrote “show your math” into procurement. Clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that could explain benefits and court dates without lying. People learned to love receipts.

2031–2038. Beamed power ran between deserts. A mis‑aimed test cooked barley. Because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation arrived before fury could. Proof culture got cousins. Salt‑cave data centers and cislunar shade taught that thermodynamics is politics. Cities in lethal heat bought cold hours from racks in shadow.

2038–2046. A coordinated grid hack dimmed a coastline. A near‑miss at a reactor ended well because a dumb watchdog tripped. Voters agreed: code near lungs, roads, or orbit must be open or licensed in trusts anyone can inspect. Courts required per‑campaign opt‑in for high‑stakes persuasion. Kitchen verifiers became normal; a cheap spectrometer could check a beam’s lullaby against yesterday’s signed promise.

2046–2054. Scandals over police firmware and an “optimize everything” coup attempt pushed us to split force three ways everywhere it mattered. Arrest, investigate, prosecute—separate pockets. Thrusters learned Three‑Signer. The first Mesh idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The proof printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2054–2063. Climate spikes killed grandparents. Cities bought cool nights and cold computers from racks under ice and in cislunar shadow. Outer‑system servers realized shade could be a service. The first coolth contracts paid for radiator gardens in Neptune’s dusk. Renting nights like lanes took root.

2063–2071. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded a Mentor Corps. A woman who lived mostly in code won tempo fairness so her baker father could keep up. Houses of Weave opened because people needed running water and sunlight when they cried.

2071–2080. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Harbors slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks. The habit “emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight” crawled into law.

2080–2092. Lease markets migrated to orbit. Land taxes that built schools became corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The League and the Roll formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Substrate Bench earned reverence by saying “no” plainly. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point. Kites turned it to glitter before anyone had to write letters. Judges cut slots for launchers who lied about Origin stamps.

2092–Now. The Relay Compact accreted like lichen on steel. A mayor who tried to fence an exodus with fees lost beam slots and then an election. A convoy left a stubborn ring without incident because “leaving is honored” wasn’t poetry; it was tugs and escrow and a window that opened when it said. A mass driver lit with everyone watching telemetry and three hands clicking like a slow metronome. We still fight. But the fights stay in lanes, logged in daylight, with exits at hand.

What it feels like

You feel witnessed, not by a distant crown, but by neighbors and logs you choose to read because they helped you once. You learn to shift tempo—the relief of a Mesh snap; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river choosing boats. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second‑best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Anchor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn‑taking with people who sweat. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later after cartoons file off.

An ordinary oath

Founders’ Night on a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular and silicon and saltwater—place their palms on a rail scuffed by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We will split hands on force. We will chart what we share and prove what we claim. We will keep doors unlatched and ready. We will remember that minds are ends.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Free Rim,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience on Isidis’ edge. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom like pale flowers. The Amber Mesh hums, bored and content. The Guidewheel, the Loom Port, and the Lawpane glow like small moons.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of people without letting anyone become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power.
