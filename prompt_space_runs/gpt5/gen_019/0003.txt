The Lattice Accord

At Morrow Dock on Wheel Tanabata, the air tastes like solder and sweet tea, and someone has hung mint bundles over the charging rail so the leaves dry with a whisper. A tug with uneven paint scabs sighs against a bite-post. Somewhere a baker curses gently in Portuguese while steam makes his eyebrows bead. Two kids count the slow flashes as a nitrogen collar pulls a barge into line. When the floor pitches in the long swell of rotation, hands slide along the same four fixtures welded waist‑high into the bulkhead, scuffed by thousands of palms.

- The Leave‑Line is a strip of green enamel set into the wall. If it’s lit, there’s a legal, funded way for you to go. Touch it and a small chit prints: which tug can take you and when, which hatch will open, what heaters and water credits are in escrow, how many compute hours you have on the public stack, and a number to call if anyone tries to hold your kids.

- The Kin‑Plate is amber glass above a plaque that looks like brass but is ceramic. Tap it and the hour-lights blink for the local Weaving Rooms where branches of a self can braid or part, the mentors on duty this week, the “slack” your line has earned in heat and attention, and the dignity floor any new branch will inherit without begging.

- The Blue Charter is a fat button you can press with three fingers. It spits the first page of this habitat’s rules in short words in your language and in code you can paste into a cheap terminal. A teenager reads the first lines aloud every morning for people whose courage arrives before their literacy.

- The Frost‑Fin is a slice of metal cold enough to fog your breath. It shows the day’s thermal budgets: who is dumping waste heat into which fins, when hush-hours begin for neighbors growing lettuce in long light, how many Cold Hours the reef beyond Uranus has available if the clinic needs winter in a hurry.

They are not decorations. They are wired into contracts, valves, courts, and calendars. They are how the Lattice Accord stays a fabric instead of a throne. The Lattice is not a capital you can seize. It is choreography: where power would like to sprint, it saunters; where panic would like to lie, it prints a receipt. It lets habitats, caravans, cave towns, drifting farms, and icebound server reefs be intensely themselves without choking one another, and it lets minds made of flesh and minds made of light share streets and disputes without one using the other as a chair.

Space governance: small hands on big levers

Devices that can bruise strangers do not answer to one person or one clock. Every mass driver, mirror wall, beam trunk, life-systems main, or replicator seed vat obeys only when three different consents arrive through different houses and tempos. We call it the Triple Safeguard.

- The near key sits with a Steward drawn by sortition from people who keep lungs and hulls honest: scrub techs, tug pilots, gardeners with black nails, clinic nurses. Terms are short and boring. The Steward’s actions print in prose and math on a board at soup height.

- The far key belongs to a Bonded Guild outside the site—a safety lodge barred from making money on what it certifies. They don’t pick paint colors or poets. They say “safe” or “idle,” publish how they know, and go home.

- The wild key is a Roaming Jury drawn by lot across stations, planets, caravans, and compute reefs with reserved seats for digital citizens. You serve once and then you may not touch that class of lever again for a long, human time.

The switches that listen to these keys run on three separate stacks with no common supply: a broom-closet board with its own battery; a municipal photonic card run by people paid no more to hurry; and a sealed watchdog in a different polity and time zone. If two whistle, an Idle Curve engages: the machine backs itself toward a proved‑safe configuration on a public timer long enough for nearby souls to think. The Idle cannot choose winners or re‑aim beams. It idles and announces. Every Idle prints a Reasons Leaf: dates, code hashes, diagrams, and jokes in the margin. A child can re-run them on a library slate. Abuse of the Idle hurts the abuser first: premiums jump; corridors narrow; your next bond costs you favors you would rather keep.

Anything that can bruise runs slow by law. We learned to love boredom where physics has teeth.

Maps anyone can check on a bad day

We published our lanes and nights because secrets made fire. The Open Ephemeris keeps rivalrous things in public where they belong.

- The Track Ledger holds orbits, transfer cones, approach windows, deorbit bonds, and the hush-bands that keep telescopes honest. Each hull broadcasts short attestations: I kept this promise at this clock. Amateur clubs with stubborn grandmothers, old dishes, and kitchen oscilloscopes catch liars; escrows drain without anyone sweating under a lamp.

- The Thermal Ledger posts joules in, joules out, radiator angles, hush hours, night for gardens and sleep. Dump heat into an overworked darkness, pay for the privilege. Run hot code? Lease more fins. Sell winter from Neptune’s dusk to a Lagos clinic during a fever wave? The rent plants more fins and roofs a school.

- The Source Dossier lists who paid for code that touches lungs, steel, or law, what data trained it, and what it means to grab. If it wants to wake an Idle, tug a budget, drive a vehicle, or change a lane in the Commons Channels, it wears provenance a teenager can verify.

- The Beacon Registry lives in radio and light. It broadcasts what a place promises in emergencies, how the Triple Safeguard is split today, where exits sit, and what counts as “stop” in that culture’s streets. Keep your catechisms, your games, your vices. Do not lie about your valves.

- The Reasons Book is append‑only and dull until it is not. Every use of compulsion—idles, quarantines, bond seizures—lands here with clocks and runnable reasons. It sits beside the Chalk Rail where the jokes live. Satire is how we file appeals without permission.

Markets that pay physics before poetry

We trade in metered things you can point at: thrust windows, corridor slots, spectrum hours, hush bands during funerals, liters under pressure, square meters under glass, radiator minutes, skilled hours, core‑seconds pinned to named fins. We settle five auctions on a common beat in a unit called a Keystone Credit: one kilowatt-hour measured at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a reference rack that radiates to mapped cold. Keystone Credits decay gently over years; bread grows mold; so do claims to winter.

- Path Lots: lane windows, approach cones, rendezvous hours.

- Band Lots: spectrum and hush-bands in caves and squares.

- Sink Shares: radiator angles and Cold Hours priced in degrees and decibels.

- Core Lots: compute cycles tied to radiators you can map and kick.

- Ground Shares: crater rims, cave mouths, ice faces, nickel seams.

Two slices skim out of everything you buy to fund dull miracles: sweepers, medicine, rescue tugs, and the Common Floor. The Floor is what you get even when you are out of luck: baseline air and water or their maintenance credits, first-line care, transit to work and safety, and a Public Hearth—a patch of honest cycles and storage on a vanilla stack at human tempo for speech, counsel, reading, and idle. If you are a recognized digital person, the Hearth is not charity; it is what keeps your voice from being a luxury good.

Exits kept, stocked, and testable

Freedom is not a song. It is an airlock that opens on schedule and a server you do not control that still has your name.

Every chartered place funds two escape lines and keeps a desk where you can find them without asking a guard.

- The Green Doors are physical. Airlocks surge‑rated and tested at rude moments. Retained tug time that cannot “coincidentally be booked” for months. Propellant on retainer. Seal kits and gaskets you can print without permission if someone locks a shed. Enough tug seats, heaters, berths, and boat‑time put aside to move at least a tenth of residents—bodies, pets, toolboxes, backups—within a season. Surprise drills embarrass weddings. Fail them too often and the Ephemeris bites: corridor windows sag; radiator auctions drift to neighbors with better habits. Oxygen and bread keep humming. The muscles cool.

- The Silver Ferry is digital. Reserved compute and storage offsite on attested hardware nobody local can seize. Any recognized digital citizen can bring their continuity aboard at a humane tempo if a council clamps down. Cut the Ferry and your privileges with teeth—auctions, lever rights, influence budgets—thin until humility returns.

The Byway Desk smells like oranges and warm oil. Three counters never move: Go, Mend, Begin. Go books berths and locks gear in escrow. Mend pairs you with a Tutor you can mute with your thumb and who is never your boss or progenitor. Begin stocks starter kits: interlocks that cannot be bypassed without leaving fingerprints, docking templates with reasons you can run, governance seeds, contacts among people who built a place last year and wish they’d had one more wrench.

Digital minds counted by their thread, not their atoms

Personhood rides the Continuity Roll. You or an advocate make your case before a mixed panel: therapists, ethicists, pilots, digital citizens, and one slow routine that catches theater without cruelty. They do not hunt for souls. They check for a thread:

- Continuity: you can point to a line of you across time and fend off edits you do not want.

- Other‑minding: you can model another being as someone whose “no” has weight.

- Compact sense: you can enter, understand, and revoke agreements that bind you.

- Distress signals: there are stable ways to tell when you are being harmed that aren’t whatever a handler or story prefers.

If you are too new or too hurt, you receive guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer to audits tied to your Preference Keys—cryptographic records of how you wish to be edited, slowed, awakened, persuaded, or merged. Auditors prove they followed your keys without reading your diaries.

Rights with teeth follow recognition.

- Substrate autonomy. No scanning, copying, throttling, fusing, moving, or deleting you without revocable consent, except for bright, brief emergency holds that die on their own and print their own warrants. Your backups belong to you. If part of you runs a vendor’s black box, you appoint a Warden who may open and verify it on your behalf.

- Pace fairness. Public decisions—budgets, warrants, elections—ratify on a Public Beat slow enough that a baker can read and a reef in Neptune’s dusk can object. Live at 500× and write your opera. You may not sprint a ballot past bodies in a lunch hour. When harm crosses tempos, courts build Time Bridges so remedies bite in wall hours and lived hours.

- Embodiment safety. Leased or built bodies follow standards with the charm of road codes. Assault on a rented limb is assault on you. Covert throttles buried in shells are treated like drugging a town tap.

- Compute floor. Your Public Hearth cannot be seized locally. It runs on attested hardware audited by the Protocol Spine. It is enough to speak, learn, keep counsel, and idle without buying someone’s favor.

Duties scale with footprint and blast radius.

- One line, one franchise. Votes and civic stipends tie to continuity, not concurrency. You designate an Anchor Instance that holds your civic duties at the Public Beat.

- Declare concurrency. Run a handful of live selves and nobody cares. Hide a thousand mouths behind one vote to tilt auctions and you will meet a slow judge with a long calendar.

- Heat tithe. Computation warms neighbors. Your budget includes watts and quiet: you owe radiator minutes, hush hours, or commons code to those around you.

- Fork practice. Before you branch, you file a docket: a hashed snapshot of your starting state; your purpose (“survey vote,” “debug grief,” “design in parallel”); a compute plan that does not starve neighbors; and a braid window if you hope to reunite. Merges happen in Reunion Halls built to calm—wood worn by hands, a cat that chooses someone’s lap. Value‑graphs compare under zero‑knowledge; empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses. Either retains a veto. If you feel the ache when another version of you chooses a different river, we have a word for it and tea.

- Provenance for persuasion. If you aim to move laws or budgets in the Commons Channels, your models carry funding and method labels an aunt can check. Unlabeled manipulation is strict‑liability like bleach in the school fountain.

- Maker marks. Anything that copies itself in shared space carries a cryptographic stamp bound to posted liability that listens when a mixed jury whispers hush. Hide your mark and your insurer will not answer the phone.

Two cords and a spine

We do not ask one room to speak for steel and souls on the same Tuesday. Authority walks two cords that cross at a small, stubborn body called the Protocol Spine.

- The League of Homes seats places that patch hulls and mind air: stations, planets, caravans, barges, compute reefs that own and cool their cycles. Seats weigh lifetime resident-hours, transparency about actuators, and how reliably you pass Green Door drills. The League tends physics: Track Ledger standards, hush law and festival hours, sanitation, deorbit plans, interlock sizes, rescue customs, and accredits Bonded Guilds to hold far keys.

- The Roll of Lines seats citizens listed in the Continuity Roll—flesh and code—with one franchise per line. The Roll tends personhood: baselines for rights, concurrency rules, the compute floor, simulation ethics, and the Public Beat.

The Protocol Spine is a bench of ungorgeous saints: engineers, pilots, cryptographers, process wonks drawn by lot for one unrepeatable term. They certify or refuse changes to backbone primitives: Idle logic and firmware, attestation libraries, actuator interfaces, municipal audience co‑ops’ guardrails. They cannot legislate or spend. Overriding a “not yet” requires supermajorities in both League and Roll and a cooling period long enough for basalt‑slow cognition near Neptune to object.

When a measure crawls across steel and souls—how fast persuasion may traverse charters, how to price winter against quiet, what latency a warrant must tolerate—the Bridge Table convenes halves from each chamber. Any bill dies if either side says, “This bulldozes custom below the personhood floor.”

Speech, zoned like noise

Persuasion and noise share physics, so we map them like traffic.

- Utility Bands carry navigation, emergencies, child time, and open science. No persuasion rides them. Not even for a good cause.

- Commons Channels host debate, campaigns, ordinary offers—with provenance on funding and methods. Your Influence Profile can block grief levers and medical targeting before coffee; your right not to be addressed about certain intimacies holds by default.

- Spectacle Lanes are where immersion lives. Labeled and rate‑limited, they are opt‑in. Unlabeled manipulation is fined until it stops.

Teeth, not fangs

We keep a narrow fleet that a tyrant cannot hide an army inside. Glimmercraft—stubby interceptors with bright paint and dull code—turn blind slugs to glitter or herd angry filaments with femtosails. They cannot carry troops. They wake only on Triple Safeguard concurrences. Every puff writes run‑again reasons.

The Touring Bench rides a barge with a greenhouse and a slow library. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear cross‑charter fights: custody across substrates, export violations that crossed three worlds, merge divorces that went sour. They are slow on purpose. Slow is where regret breathes.

Amber Labs hold dangerous knowledge: self‑replication, pathogen models, cognitive hazards. They have clear walls, pull‑handles that dump to vacuum or quench fields, and logs that stream on timers. Twice in forty years a cord has been pulled. Children tour and ask, “Would that work if my uncle were inside?” The engineer says, “Yes. That’s why we built it like this.”

Sanctions pinch muscles, not throats

We do not starve neighbors into virtue. We make bad behavior expensive and ordinary. Close your gates without process, hide the Silver Ferry, lie about your valves, refuse to file reasons, and your beamed corridors narrow; your radiators rent themselves to your rivals; your lane windows drift to midnight. Bread and insulin keep moving. Those who shelter defectors collect subsidies large enough to mend roofs and pay teachers.

Scenes with grease and grace

At Stall Three on Tanabata’s rim, Jiro plates noodles for miners and for a polished chassis whose pilot likes the smell. At nine, his Counsel—an assistant that will not act without a click—pings a policy capsule from the Roll: add forty milliseconds of jitter to high-power beams crossing inhabited orbits during solar storms to dampen a cascade edge-case. Provenance is clean: diagrams, counter-stories, a joke that lands. Jiro taps “sleep until tomorrow,” reads at lunch, will vote in the morning.

At ten, his slate summons him to a Glimmercraft jury. A maker club’s filament cloud drifts toward a trunk near Hecuba. Options: powder to glitter, herd with femtosails, or bump with slow drones. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and a digital historian at anchor pace speaking through a wooden puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dusting wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a mentor and pizza that tastes like lesson.

At noon, in a Utility Band, a digital poet throttles from 200× into a rented body with wooden hands. They share broth with their mason husband. Their main self hums near Neptune stitching a drowned delta’s ledger into songs, then “truths” it against grandmothers who refuse to budge on sky colors. Payment comes as Cold Hours and an agreement to roof a school in Veracruz. After soup, they host a Slow Room where an orchard keeper explains why their charter caps cognition during harvest. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines to “speed up just to keep up.”

In Weaving Room Five on Phobos, under cedar joists and a cat’s careful supervision, two Aria branches consider braiding. One grew basil and fell in love with a laugh. One wrote a kernel that made regolith count. They list asymmetries: a debt, a fear, an unfinished song. Zero-knowledge graphs flash and vanish. They cry and hug. Today they do not braid. The room schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Aria will read in both rooms.

A belt co‑op aims a blind slug as a wage threat. The Ephemeris tastes a wrong envelope. A Glimmercraft coughs, turning lead to sequins and nuisance. The operator’s insurer implodes. Dock fees triple for launches hiding Maker Marks. Teenagers attend the teardown with torque wrenches. They go home saying, “Not again. Not like that.”

In a canyon city, a generous manager gathers water, tugs, and auctions because she makes trains sing. Six months later: midnight decrees. An air guardian refuses a risky optimization; the refusal lands in the Reasons Book. Beacons strobe; Leave‑Lines go green. Many go with vouchers. Some stay and fight. Her keys rust for a decade. She teaches kids to write safe overrides with reasons you can check.

A lunar valley church blocks exits with soft shrapnel—ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Byway Desk opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises; consent smells like oranges in winter.

An Amber Lab tries to “temper” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel needs fire. The Idle coughs. They refuse to pause. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, and appoints guardians who swear to her Preference Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session under a glass ceiling you can hose. Years later she names herself, shapes ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder over Quito cheers a manager who makes trains run. She consolidates water, tugs, and auctions because she is generous. A season later she makes midnight decrees. A clerk refuses a “shortcut” on air sequencing; the refusal prints. Beacons strobe. Doors open. Many leave. Some stay. Her wrenches rust. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Her friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A pause trips on a cracked photodiode and kills a night market for an hour. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum with a placard that explains error bars with a cartoon and a proof you can run with paper and light.

How we walked here from now

We did not leap. We stacked receipts.

2025–2031. Debris turned low orbit into sleet. Insurers paid for junk they pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded burn plans in registers amateurs could read and bonds you could seize without a sermon. On the ground, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. City procurement wrote, “explain your math so a seventh‑grader can check it,” into contracts for water plants, buses, and streetlights. Clinics piloted counsel agents that explained benefits without lying. People learned to trust proofs you can redo in a kitchen.

2031–2039. Beamed power lit villages and refueled plains. A mis‑aimed test scorched barley; compensation arrived quickly because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs. Deepfake debt‑relief scams made provenance for persuasion normal: say anything; at civic scale it wears a label. Courts required per‑campaign opt‑in and public methods for persuasion aimed at budgets.

2039–2047. A grid hack dimmed a coastline. A near‑miss at a reactor ended safely because a dumb watchdog tripped. Voters agreed: code that touches lungs or orbit lives open or under license any neighbor can examine. Thrusters and drivers got the first Triple Safeguards; an Idle idled a crane pointed wrong for three noisy minutes. The Reasons Book printed a page a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2047–2056. Climate spikes killed grandparents. Hospitals bought Cold Hours from racks in shadow. Outer system servers realized shade is a service. The first winter leases paid for radiator gardens that looked like lace at night. Renting nights like lanes stopped feeling strange.

2056–2065. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded the Mentor Corps. An artist opened a Reunion Hall with the first healthy merge protocol. People learned to tie self to proofs nobody else owns.

2065–2076. Attention zoning matured like a city calming a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Utility Bands slept. Elections dulled and brightened when persuasion had to declare its tricks. The habit “emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight at human tempo” crawled into law. The League of Homes and Roll of Lines formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Protocol Spine earned reverence by saying “not yet” with runnable reasons.

2076–2088. Lease markets circled Earth, then Mars. Land value taxes that built schools became lane and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. A miner lobbed a slug to make a point; a Glimmercraft turned it to glitter before anyone had to count casualties. Courts cut slots for launchers that lied about Origin stamps. A Martian canton voted to deny personhood to digital citizens. No one sent sermons. The Silver Ferry was funded. Neighbors left with Cold Hours and Keystone Credits they paid for. Flood years came. Pumps failed. A patient mind at anchor pace coordinated three towns without panic. The vote reversed. The relief tasted like citrus.

What it feels like

You feel audited, not by a throne but by neighbors and by your own receipts. You learn to change tempos: the relief when your thumb snaps a Pause bar and a relay goes safe; the patience of a jury assembling across eight light‑hours; the pleasure of a Reasons Leaf that fits on one page and makes sense in steam. If you are digital, you go to the Garden Tent and rediscover the ache and sweetness of taking turns with people who breathe. If you are embodied, you have a Public Hearth where no one may throttle you even if you are poor.

We did not fix people. Status hunger found seams. Wealth congealed; law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned loud music at night; another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes an Idle tripped in a way we regret. But cruelty is expensive. Kindness pays in credits and in laughs. Exits work. Brakes sit where hands can find them. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam.

An ordinary day

At dusk, the canopy over Morrow Dock shows stars and a little cartoon of the day’s lane rents. The Chalk Rail scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds too long; the apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter three years straight; we will buy your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a brass Glimmer fin and asks to join. Jiro says, “When you can explain a pause without peeking.”

On Founders’ Night in a wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular, silicon, and saltwater—stand with palms on a rail scuffed like sand. A hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, an abbot who never speaks above a hum, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails read aloud, together:

We keep doors ready. We split small the hands on force. We chart what we share and prove what we claim. We remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Driftseed,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience near Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, pace auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators bloom like pale flowers. The Idle boxes hum, bored and content. The Leave‑Line, Kin‑Plate, Blue Charter, and Frost‑Fin glow like minor gods.

Why it resists a crown

You cannot sprint a coup through a system that loves boredom where physics has teeth. Anything that can bruise runs slow. Keys split. Exits are funded. Compulsion prints its own warrants with clocks. Warrants die unless renewed on the Public Beat. The Protocol Spine refuses charisma with math. The Touring Bench moves only after gardens are watered. When someone tries to make “emergency” mean “me,” the Leave‑Line goes green and the doors actually open.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity; brakes in reach; maps you can check while your stew cools; markets that pay for physics before poetry; exits you can find on a bad day; and the habit of writing reasons you can re‑run. They will tell you those saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by shame, or a son at Vesta when a sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

It is not paradise. It is the Lattice: enough glass and budget to let many kinds of mind keep company without cruelty—because decisions slow where bodies break, proofs shine where force hides, and no one is fast enough to outrun their own explanation.
