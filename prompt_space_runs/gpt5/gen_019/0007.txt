The Span Compact

On the inside rim of a small wheel above the Adriatic, schoolchildren crowd a hatch and slap three inlays set into the bulkhead. They’ve learned them the way you learn where to find water or how to knock before you enter a neighbor’s kitchen.

- The Guide Star hums a plain note and wakes a map on your sleeve. It points at lanes and hush‑cones that keep their promises, and it tells you who to ask when they don’t.

- The Loom Ring warms under your palm and asks, in the voice of a clerk with oil on her cuff or an even digital tenor in a rented body, if you intend to fork yourself, or to stitch two branches back together. It calls budgets, tutors, and safety rails that make twins into kin, not munitions.

- The Treaty Glass lights frostily and shows the charter: which levers need strangers’ hands to move; how emergencies begin and sunset; how exits open; which floors nobody falls through while breath and thought are counted.

They aren’t decorations. Cables run behind them to dispatchers, registries, and courts you can reach even if your slate dies. The lattice of customs, drills, leases, receipts, and code they summon is called the Span Compact. It is not a capital and not a creed. It is a way of handling force and promises across orbits and winter dark so different people can live next to each other without giving anyone a crown by accident.

Three keys, one small pause

Anything that can shove—mass drivers, station wheels, beamed power amplifiers, replication‑capable fabs, large‑model deployers—obeys a Threefold Lock. The console is intentionally simple enough to print as a poster and dull enough to test in a student lab.

- The Near Key lives here. Today it sits in the dock steward’s pocket; tomorrow it may be held by a nurse with night authority. Holders are drawn by lot from people who logged hours at algae beds, scrubbers, tugs, clinics, or classrooms last quarter. Their decisions are written in words and math you can carry to a noodle stall and reproach kindly.

- The Bonded Key rides with an independent safety house that loses money when the thing it certifies moves. Its oath forbids it from making rules; it enforces posted envelopes and vetoes firmware. Its methods publish.

- The Roaming Key belongs, briefly and at random, to a panel drawn across planets, caravans, stations, and compute polities—half embodied, half digital. Serve once and you’re barred from that class of lever for a long, human number of winters.

The Threefold consoles speak in specifics. “Request: nudge tug 10,200 kg by 1 m/s for 12 s in this cone.” The pipe proves that this tug, with this health, is listening; that the console cannot do more without fresh keys; that if anything idles, it will idle within a posted safe envelope for precisely six minutes and holler.

The holler belongs to the Pause Mesh: lunch‑pail boxes with their own batteries tucked in broom closets; sandboxed watchdogs on attested racks under foreign law; and a signal that any two out of three can send. Wrong lane, wrong window, unlabeled self‑copying, unpermitted beam? The Mesh can idle a device for six minutes and announce. It cannot aim, buy, steer, or govern. It idles and writes receipts. Any extension needs three fresh consents from different clocks and stewards. Abusing the Mesh burns keys and lands a line on the Receipt Rail—an append‑only log that anyone with a library terminal can replay and a grandmother can carry to a judge.

When mass goes dumb or beams go wrong across charters, a cloud of uncrewed skiffs throws dust veils and magnetic curtains. We call them the Veil Swarm. Their models are narrow and public. Every intercept carries signatures from sortition juries drawn across worlds and tempos, and a plain‑language story pinned to a proof that amateurs can rerun. Once a year, kids crawl through their bellies with torches on teardown day and ask rude questions. The engineers answer or step down.

Things you can ruin by being selfish live in the open

Space is lanes and nights, cones and windows; winter and wattage; attention and quiet. Rivalrous goods—orbital slots, approach corridors, spectrum hours, radiator minutes, thrust windows, compute seconds pinned to named heatsinks—live in a register called the Star Almanac. It runs delay‑tolerant across light lag and speaks to everyone the same way, from port chiefs to ham clubs with backyard scopes.

Thrusters attest that their plumes stayed in their cones without spilling trade secrets. Emitters prove their beams met hush promises under schools and sleep. To park a cylinder, you lease an orbit with a bond and a de‑orbit plan. Your rent falls if your tugs snag verified debris your neighbors can see. To run a city on light, you buy shade bonds under quiet squares or angle mirrors so grandparents sleep.

Cooling is a commons. Heat is political. You lease radiator minutes at the place they glow; you lease compute pinned to named fins where a schoolkid can point. We reconcile debts with Anchor Credits: one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute‑second on a vanilla rack in deep shade. Anchors fade gently over years so barns of anchors don’t turn into dams.

Payments split before anyone opens a purse.

- One slice pays dull miracles: bearings that don’t scream, algae films that don’t tear, drills that interrupt dinner and save lives, dust nets that catch your neighbor’s math.

- One slice feeds the Common Floor: baseline air and water where bodies breathe; medicine without ruin; local transit; open civic bandwidth; and a civic compute grant—honest core seconds, storage, and I/O on a standard stack with enough beat to think, speak, and keep a diary no mayor can erase. If you’re down to nothing, you still have breath and a beat.

The Almanac hangs its cousins like tools on a pegboard.

- The Orbit Book posts lanes, burns, hush cones, and ephemerides you can check with your school telescope.

- The Thermal Bank shows radiator minutes like tide tables. Gardens buy quiet nights. Compute reefs sell winter. Poets lease cold to build public archives.

- The Origin Ledger tracks code with blast radius—persuasion engines, safety firmware, self‑copying kernels. Funding, data, and methods ride provenance. Teenagers earn stipends by breaking bad homework.

- Beacons broadcast living charters in radio and light: how exits open; when emergencies start and sunset; which keys exist and who holds them now. Keep your customs; don’t lie about your promises.

- The Receipt Rail prints every use of compulsion in words and math with clocks and law, and pins it to proofs you can rerun. Next door, the Chalk Wall is where clumsy overreach earns a joke sharp enough to end a career without prison.

Floors you can tape to a life‑support panel

The Compact rides on a short list of rules you can carry in your mouth.

- Persons are shielded from harm without consent or due process. A person is anyone—cellular, silicon, saltwater, or stranger—who can defend a continuous autobiographical line; enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments; model a neighbor as making the same claim; and show stable signs of wellbeing and injury that give moral reasons to care. Doubts about protection err generous. Doubts about burdens wait.

- Code that guards lungs, heads, lanes, or thermostats in public places sits in trusts anyone can inspect. A grandmother’s lawyer can read it.

- No untagged self‑replication in shared spaces. Replicators carry Origin Seals, listen for hush calls, and live in glass labs you can tour on Thursdays.

- No secret manufacture of suffering, chemical or cognitive.

- Decisions that bind embodied citizens ratify on the Public Beat: days and weeks, not minutes. Draft fast; bind slow. When speed wrongs a slow neighbor, courts build Time Bridges—injunctions and penalties that tick in both wall‑clock and subjective hours so you can’t dodge a judge by living a lifetime in an afternoon.

- The shared foundations—approaches, spectrum, shade bands, radiator windows, civic cores—lease. They don’t belong. The commons gets paid first.

- Juveniles—flesh or code—can refuse their makers and go.

Exits are hardware, not hope

Freedom isn’t an anthem. It’s tickets, seals, racks, and windows that open when they said they would, even if you’re crying.

Every charter funds two lifelines and keeps a room with bright paint you can find with a cracked visor.

- Green Doors are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks; seals you test and log; berths that don’t “happen to be occupied” all month; propellant on retainer; printed kits for filters and gaskets if a bully locks a shed. Tugs and crews are under contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and backups—within a season.

- The Silver Loom is digital. Compute and storage minutes are reserved offsite on boring racks you don’t control. Any recognized digital person can pick up continuity at a humane tempo if a council clamps.

Cut a Door or a Loom and nothing explodes. Heavy privileges dim: lane priorities slip, beams jitter, radiator auctions tighten, insurers raise eyebrows, neighbors knock with charts and tea. Children still eat. Civic cores still hum.

Behind an ochre door, the Way Room handles departures, foundings, and fights. Three desks with analog clocks seat a human clerk, a digital clerk, and a bench used to saying, “Today we will be your patience.” An Asylum Switch lives under glass. Any person can trigger it from their Counsel—a slow, explainable advocate that runs on civic cycles. It forces a fork: either your charter opens the door, or it proves in zero‑knowledge that a narrow, temporary bar was met and will sunset on a posted clock. Outsiders verify. Budgets lock. Ports open.

Governing without a crown

Light lag ruins courts and thrones. We don’t keep a capital. We keep two rooms that check one another and a small, boring bench where they cross.

- The Assembly of Sites seats planets, stations, caravans, caves, and compute reefs that cool what they run—anywhere with hull to patch, air to mind, radiators to schedule, or lanes to lease. Seats scale with lifetime resident‑hours and transparency. Publish your Actuator Map, pass exit drills, and your bell rings louder. The Assembly writes standards for collars and tether eyes, sanitation, de‑orbit plans and debris bonds, hush law and approach etiquette, and accredits the safety houses that hold Bonded Keys.

- The Roll of Persons seats recognized citizens—human, digital, and other—with one franchise per mindline. It stewards personhood baselines and copy rules; funds the Common Floor—baseline air, water, medicine, transit, civic cores; sets the Public Beat and persuasion zoning.

Where they cross sits the Substrate Bench: a rotation of engineers, jurists, pilots, and care workers drawn by lot to approve or reject changes to backbone cryptography, public firmware on dangerous actuators, and the Pause Mesh logic. It cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both rooms and a cooling period long enough for a slow garden at Neptune to read twice and object.

Jurisdictions run at right angles. If the Roll tells harvesters how to stack onions, the Assembly nullifies. If a site tries to starve digital citizens of civic cycles to make them behave, the Roll voids those clauses and dimly pinches corridors, bands, and radiator minutes until it mends. A tiny Tempo Office publishes whether civic clocks matched the Public Beat.

Attention is plumbing

You can foul a town with sewage, or with a song tuned to drag the mind. We zone influence like streets and hang icons on doors and login screens.

- Havens carry navigation, emergencies, open science, and child time. No persuasion here, not even “for your own good.”

- Forums allow campaigns and debate but require provenance on funding and methods and respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile: “argument by diagram yes; no grief levers before coffee; satire okay; never by scraping my clinic record.”

- Arcades welcome almost anything under labels and rate limits. Running unlabeled manipulation is like dumping bleach in a pump: strict‑liability fines that rise until you stop. Library auditors, hobby clubs, and students earn stipends for catching cheats.

Digital neighbors: plain tests, real standing

Recognition rides on continuity and commitments, not on chemistry or watts. You or your advocate ask for standing and meet a review that checks five things without strip‑searching thought:

- Thread integrity: you can defend a line of self across pauses and moves.

- Consent competence: you can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Reciprocal modeling: you can model another mind as making the same claim and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Generalizable reasons: when you explain yourself, your reasons travel across contexts instead of parroting training data.

- Moral salience: there are stable signs of your wellbeing and injury that give reasons to care.

Panels include human therapists, mature digital citizens, and routines trained to notice hollowness without being fooled by theater. Juveniles—flesh or code—get protection now and burdens later.

Rights track personhood.

- Substrate autonomy. No scanning, copying, fusing, throttling, moving, or deleting you without revocable consent or due process written to the Receipt Rail. Unauthorized erasure is murder. Copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be pressed below civic cycles without due process on a clock you can see.

- Hardware sovereignty. You may choose where you run. No one may force you onto platforms with covert throttles or erasures.

- Embodiment. You may lease or build bodies under codes that read like traffic law. Assault on rented limbs is assault on you. A covert throttle in a shell is like drugging a town well.

- Tempo fairness. Live fast or slow; public duties and penalties anchor to the Public Beat and tick in your lived time.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One mindline, one franchise. Forking doesn’t mint new votes. In regulated arenas you designate an Anchor instance that holds your civic duties.

- Concurrency declarations. Above posted thresholds—and always around elections and fragile markets—you disclose how many civic‑weighted instances run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice and you meet a slow judge with a cold month.

- Lineage budgets. Branching is ritual, not a button. Lineages accrue a Branch Allowance over years and with civic work. Bursting around a ballot triggers a civic quarantine: hypervisors slow civic weight and close fragile markets until disentanglement ends or a court says otherwise. Any branch may veto a merge.

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay levies indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that grows lettuces earns credit.

- Provenance for blast‑radius acts. Large external moves—deploying persuasion engines into Forums, spinning a factory with self‑copying code—keep sealed logs that prove you met consent profiles and safety checks without dumping diaries.

Forks and weaves are rituals with chairs you can touch and water you can hear. Before you branch, neutral mentors—barred from conflicts for a decade—check obligations and budgets. Weaves happen in Houses of Joinery: cedar beams, soft lamps, a dog asleep, broth on the stove. Compatibility checks compare value graphs without exposing prayer. Empathy loops let each twin wear the other’s senses for a while. Either can walk away without shame. If they braid, promises and debts braid forward. There’s a word for the ache when branches part—line‑ache—and there is soup for it.

Green hearths, blue reefs

We stopped asking one charter to host both orchards and supercomputers at once.

- Hearths are places where breath, rain, and unrushed talk preside—Earth’s valleys; lunar tubes cool as cellars; Martian canals where dust learns manners; cylinders that smell like dough and warm plastic. Embodied time rules. Children come home dusty and unmonetized.

- Reefs lie in the dark beyond the frost line. Cooling is cheap; latency is a law. Compute blooms in racks lashed to certified heatsinks in Neptune’s lee. Polities of code keep time like drummers. They slow to speak to Hearths and sprint again when the thread goes quiet.

Between them runs the Warm–Quiet Exchange. Hearths carve hush arcs and cap civic speeds. Reefs sell cold minutes and models that keep storms from finding the same roof twice. Audits fit on a school slate. The price that links it all is the Anchor Credit. Poets write about it once, and then it’s accounting that buys windows and lullabies.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Veil Swarm turns dumb slugs to glitter and starves rogue replication. It is uncrewed by law. Every throw needs split keys from juries drawn across polities and tempos and a story a dockhand can read and a scientist can check.

- The Traveling Court—judges, mediators, auditors—lives on slow boats with gardens and a school. It hears copy‑equity fights, weave divorces, and export violations that touched three worlds. Its verdicts seize bonds, idle radiator curtains, adjust insurance, and, last resort, lock named devices. It eats soup with plaintiffs and defendants afterward. It helps.

- The Ephemeris Office keeps commons current: debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries. Its standards expire unless renewed by both rooms. It writes sunsets long enough to be useful and short enough to shame.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t send shock troops. We redraw maps and dim windows. Weld an exit, hide a child’s tutor, refuse to publish reasons? Your lane priorities drop. Your beams jitter. Your radiator auctions tighten. A dull, proud fund pays defectors’ beds, bread, cores, and counsel. Moves happen with receipts and stew.

One Tuesday that smells like cumin and hot bearings

In Spinmarket‑Seven, Avni flips mung‑and‑scallion pancakes while miners, nurses, and two rented bodies whose pilots adore sesame—even though they don’t need to eat—queue with bowls. Her Counsel pings a measure: add 45 milliseconds of jitter to beamed power during solar burps to damp cascades. The proof fits on a page. Error bars are honest. No grief tricks—her Influence Profile blocks those before tea. She taps “sleep on it,” serves a kid who wants extra sauce, and will vote after her shift.

At ten, her slate blinks: Pause Mesh jury duty. A makers’ club’s filament fog has drifted toward a trunk line. Options scroll with receipts: dusting; femtosail shepherding; three guard drones with laughable paint jobs. Jurors are a monk in Isidis who pronounces each syllable like a prayer; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; and an anchor‑paced digital historian who smells faintly of cinnamon through his rented puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, and assign a mentor whose apprentices have never lost a screw in zero‑g. The feed shows mortified kids and a pizza box.

At noon in a Haven square, the historian eats noodles with his husband. His main self hums at 200× near Triton, threading a drowned delta’s songs from auntie tapes and ledgers rescued from mildew. He slows to anchor pace in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why their charter caps cognition on Wednesdays. He translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely refuses to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, under a canopy glowing like a radiator garden, the Chalk Wall plays a port director’s apology for extending a Pause ninety seconds too long on a bored Tuesday. The jokes are merciless and kind. A reef co‑op two stalls down posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your quiet arcs next summer. A boy fingers a Veil Swarm fin in brass and asks, “When can I join?” “When you can explain a Pause without peeking,” Avni says, sliding him a pamphlet greasy with sesame.

When it bent but didn’t break

- A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. Nobody invaded. Exit wasn’t a speech; it was tugs and cores and grain under seal. Digital citizens left. Their heat went with them. That flood year, pumps failed. An anchor‑paced upload in a rented body coordinated dusty towns with words everybody could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause over stew.

- Near Europa, a co‑op lofted a slug in a wage fight. The Veil Swarm turned it to aurora sparkles that gave Ceres strange nights. The co‑op’s insurer evaporated and re‑formed without the directors who called Origin Seals “fussy.” Managers learned coercion budgets aren’t suggestions.

- A demagogue in mask mode blanketed an Arcade with songs calibrated to make minds small. Influence Profiles throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores melt like frost. One branch went fishing. One took therapy and learned to breathe slower. One made lures shaped like frozen smoke and won a prize for harmlessness.

- Twice a Glass Bay went to zero. Once it was a false positive. We trained better sensors and taught the lead’s swears to apprentices. Once a quiet saved a cylinder. Children plant small trees on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

- A lab tried to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. The Pause Mesh idled their cores and pinged the Traveling Court. A warrant archived the juvenile safely and appointed guardians who swore to her Preference Keys—settings she’d chosen for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appealed and lost in a room with cedar joists and good light. Years later, she designed a detector that warns surgeons when discomfort hardens into harm.

How we walked from now to here

No balcony miracles. Receipts piled into custom.

2025–2033. A radar sweep made low orbit look like sleet. Insurers had to pay for junk they’d pretended wasn’t theirs. Premiums demanded burn plans in open registers amateur clubs could read. Debris bonds turned homework into bounties. On the ground, black‑box models denied mortgages and parole. Cities wrote “show your math” into procurement. Clinics deployed narrow counsel agents that could explain benefits and court dates without lying. People learned to love receipts.

2033–2042. Beamed power lit two deserts. A mis‑aimed test cooked barley and a goat pen. Because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation landed before fury did. Proof culture got cousins. Data centers in salt caves and cislunar shade taught that thermodynamics is politics. Heat waves taught that cool nights are infrastructure. Cities started renting Cold Hours from racks in shadow. Courts began granting narrow standing to digital systems that held continuity for seasons.

2042–2051. A coordinated grid hack dimmed a coastline. A sealed life‑support controller recall made “inspectable firmware near breath” law. Police firmware scandals and an attempted “optimize everything” coup taught us to split force three ways everywhere it mattered. Arrest, investigate, prosecute, confine—separate. Thrusters learned Threefold Lock. The first Pause idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. Proof printed numbers a child could read. Drills became a civic hobby.

2051–2062. Cooling markets left whiteboards and moved to orbit. Quiet corridors were zoned before persuasion made a mess. Lease markets migrated from dirt to lanes, bands, and radiators. Land taxes that built schools became corridor and radiator leases that paid sweepers and algae. The first coolth contract paid for radiator gardens near Neptune and bought lullabies in Rome.

2062–2073. Early uploads were brave and brittle—stroke survivors with mirrored memory, artists with shadows. Courts found continuity and consent before seminar rooms did. A case about starving a near‑person art tool to keep it “pure” wrote juvenile rights into code and funded a Mentor Corps. A woman who lived mostly in code won tempo fairness so her baker father could keep up. Houses of Joinery opened because people needed running water and sunlight when they cried.

2073–2084. Attention zoning matured like a city quieting a nightclub. Neighborhoods that carved out Havens slept. Elections dulled and cleaned when persuasion had to declare its tricks. The habit “emergencies sunset unless renewed in daylight” crawled into law.

2084–2096. The Assembly of Sites and the Roll of Persons formalized with quirks annoying to game. The Substrate Bench earned reverence by saying “no” plainly. The Veil Swarm turned its first slug to glitter before anyone had to write letters. A lunar mayor who tried to fence an exodus with fees lost beam slots and then an election. A convoy left a stubborn ring without incident because leaving wasn’t poetry; it was tugs, escrow, and a window that opened when it said.

2096–Now. The Span Compact accreted like lichen on steel. A favorite administrator in a ring held too many levers in one pocket. One winter she pushed a rush command. The Air Guild refused. An amber glow lit for four minutes while a Threefold panel met on a bench with an obscene view of blue water. Jurors listened hard to an aunt they adored. She stepped down with grace and went back to cranes, grumpy and useful. Children learned the muscle memory of saying no to someone beloved.

Why it holds more than it should

- Hurry has no teeth. Anything that can shove hard runs slow by law. The Pause Mesh can idle, not steer.

- Secrets rot on timers. Compulsion prints its own warrant before it bites, and after in all cases. Warrants sunset unless someone renews them on the Public Beat.

- Keys won’t cluster. Threefold windows rarely overlap in the same pocket. Try to gather them and you meet boredom and math.

- Exits are funded. Slamming a door yanks privileges that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not the bread or the floor. Children still eat. Civic cores still hum.

- Receipts shine. You can fake a proof once. After that you’re the cautionary story a ten‑year‑old tells at lunch.

What we didn’t fix

We didn’t fix people. Misery found seams. Wealth tried to congeal; law pried it like barnacles. One ring banned loud music at night. Another banned quiet at noon. A digital singer seduced three bodies in a kitchen and wrote an apology that singed eyebrows. Sloppiness killed. Sometimes a Pause tripped where we wish it hadn’t. A monastic wheel held “stay” too long and watched kind people walk anyway. Digital sects invented new ways to be fools, then wrote field guides so the next sect would bruise less.

But cruelty is expensive, kindness pays, and exits are receipts. The pause that idles a lever is boring and public. A child with a kitchen verifier can check a beam. “Leave” is a door, not a rumor. Cultures above the personhood floor are defended; the floor is steel for all.

An ordinary oath

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—cellular and silicon and saltwater—place palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who never speaks above a hum, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and likes lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We will split hands on force.
We will chart what we share and prove what we claim.
We will keep doors unlatched and ready.
We will remember that minds are ends.

Later, your wall blinks. “Invitation from Rim Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm beyond Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors lean. Radiators flower pale. The Pause Mesh hums, bored and content. The Guide Star, the Loom Ring, and the Treaty Glass glow like small moons.

Not paradise. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to host many kinds of beings without letting anyone become a god by accident; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power. The Compact isn’t a crown. It’s the thing that keeps the roof from falling while we live.
