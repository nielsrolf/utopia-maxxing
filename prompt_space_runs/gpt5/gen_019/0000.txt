The Lattice Accord

On the inner rim of a slow wheel above the Tyrrhenian, the corridor smells faintly of lemon cleaner and hot bearings. Children in socks slide to a stop at a metal rail and thump five fixtures polished by decades of hands. They learned where to find them the way you learn the location of faucets and fire blankets.

- Slipstone: a square with light behind it. Touch, and a route spills out in numbers your grandmother can defend—cabins that don’t rattle, propellant under escrow with names on it, which tugs are free to pull, who meets you at the other end, what it costs in money and favors, which door opens if the first sticks.

- Joiner Ring: a dull steel loop that warms under your palm. It opens your Line. Not just your files, but the thread that binds your sense of self. It shows forks you’ve made and merges that held, what dignity and budget a new branch would inherit, which mentors stand by if you hope to weave again, and how much heat levy your extra mouths have paid.

- Hush Grid: a graven plate that lights safe corridors and quiet hours—approach lanes and whisper bands for telescopes and sleep, persuasion speed limits and “no grief before breakfast” zones. The lines carry reasons you can check, not just rules you must swallow.

- Frostleaf: a fin-shaped rib cold to the touch. Draging your fingers along its veins pulls up cooling budgets and promises: radiator minutes leased and owed, shade arcs you agreed to leave unblinded for gardens and prayer, compute you can run without cooking a neighbor’s night.

- Oath Glass: a triangular pane that smokes and clears. When it stays clean, the charter under your feet reads to a ninth-grader and a civic bot, one clause per breath. It will read the hard parts out loud without a scold, because courage and literacy trade off.

These are not talismans. They wire into pumps, calendars, radios, and courts. The civilization behind them—on planets, in tubes, in caravans under shade sails, in compute reefs where shadow is cheap—we call the Lattice Accord. It is not a capital you can siege or a creed you can chant once and forget. It is choreography: a way to share hazards and rival goods without letting anyone collect all the levers by accident.

Three keys or nothing heavy moves

Anything that can bruise a stranger—mass drivers, beam trunks, mirror farms, hatch manifolds, factory lines that can stamp their own tools, persuasion engines that can tilt a district—sits behind a Tri‑Lock. The console is intentionally boring, readable in a library, and testable in a school lab.

- The Local Key lives here. Today it’s in the dockwright’s pocket who just came off a crane; tomorrow a nurse with night authority carries it. Holders are drawn by lot from people who logged hours keeping lungs, rails, algae, tugs, schools, or scrubbers alive. When they turn it, the arithmetic and prose go on a board above the coffee urn.

- The Bonded Key travels with an independent safety house whose payroll clears on another world and whose revenue falls when certified devices move. It doesn’t write rules. It audits recipes, burns firmware that lied, and publishes methods.

- The Wandering Key belongs, briefly and at random, to a jury drawn across stations, valleys, caravans, and compute reefs—half embodied, half digital; half fast, half set to the public tempo. Serve once and you’re barred from that class of lever for a long, human number of winters.

When a yard files “nudge tug 10,200 kg by 1 m/s, twelve seconds, Corridor C at 04:12,” a single pane must persuade a student: this tug exists and is listening; its plume won’t hiss into a hush band; the lever cannot do more without fresh keys; if it idles, it idles safely and shouts.

That shout comes from the Quiet Relay: lunch‑pail boxes with their own batteries bolted near broom closets; watchdog code on attested racks under foreign law; and municipal optics that run like plumbing. Any two of the three can idle a device for six minutes if something wakes their narrow models—wrong window, missing plan, unlabeled self‑replication—then print their own warrant with clocks and reasons. The Relay cannot aim a beam or schedule a budget. It idles and writes receipts. Extending the idle needs three fresh consents from different stewards and clocks. Abusing the Relay burns keys and lands your line on a public log a child can replay.

Maps everyone can check with a bucket scope

Space is math, light, and sleep. Rival goods—approach lanes, whisper bands, radiator faces, cold minutes, compute seconds pinned to named heat sinks—live in an open register called the Sky Ledger. It runs delay‑tolerant across light lag and speaks to governors and ham clubs in the same tone.

- Track Book: orbits, burns, rendezvous cones, de‑orbit bonds, sail arcs, window etiquette. Thrusters sign short statements so amateurs with kitchen telescopes can catch lies. Miss your cone and escrow bleeds into sweepers’ brooms.

- Heat Sheet: watts in, watts out, and the fins that catch it. Cooling is a commons. Spew into a neighbor’s night and pay dearly unless your waste grows lettuce.

- Origin Roll: code with blast radius—life support, actuators, budgets, ballots, persuasion. Funding, data, and methods ride provenance that teenagers can rerun. Catching cheats buys stipends and loud trophies.

- Beacon Posts: radio and light that broadcast promises—Tri‑Lock splits, quiet hours, emergency clocks, how exits open under weather and war. Keep your customs; don’t lie about your valves.

- Order Board: every “you must” and “you may not” this place has uttered, with signatures, start and stop, and the audit’s address.

Money we trust pins to physical things the poor can point at. A Base Credit buys one kilowatt‑hour at Earth–Moon L1 plus one compute second on a reference rack in shadow, pinned to fins schoolkids can tap with sticks. Base Credits wither gently over years. You can’t sit on winter for a decade while someone’s spinach wilts now.

Floors that tape to a life‑support panel

Payments split before a purse opens.

- One slice funds dull miracles: tug crews that sweep wakes, algae bands that don’t tear, clinics with parts that fit, dust nets that collect your neighbor’s math.

- One slice feeds the Common Lot: baseline air and water; first‑line care; local transit; open civic bandwidth; and a civic compute grant—honest core seconds, storage, and I/O enough to think, learn, speak, and keep a diary no mayor can erase. If you are down to nothing, you still have breath and a beat.

Exits you don’t earn by pleasing anyone

Freedom isn’t a speech. It’s inventory and calendars. Every charter funds two lifelines you can touch. One moves bodies. One moves minds.

- Green Egress: surge‑rated locks you can test, berths that aren’t mysteriously “full,” propellant escrow, gasket print kits if a bully locks a door. Tugs and crews are under contract to lift at least ten percent of residents—their pets, tools, and backups—within a season. Drills happen on bad hair days at inconvenient hours because that’s when real exits clamp.

- Mirror Loom: reserved compute and storage offsite under law you don’t control. Any recognized digital person can restore themselves at a humane tempo if the local host clamps. Cut a Loom and nothing explodes. Beam slots jitter, corridor windows slip, radiator auctions sour. Bread still comes. Courts notice.

Gate Lamps above funded exits glow honest colors: green for “a route exists and is paid,” amber for “you have a slot soon, tutors booked, guardians informed,” blue for “charter, taxes, and stops are current.” Gate Lamps are suable like people. Founding Bays smell of oil and oranges. They issue Starter Kits: minimum safety code, docking templates, escrow hooks, governance seeds, and the names of three people who built a home last year who will answer your first ten stupid questions like gifts.

Persons by thread, not by clay

Dignity binds to continuity plus care, not chemistry. Recognition rides a Continuity Docket. A mixed panel—therapists, engineers, ethicists, and mature digital citizens—checks five things without strip‑searching thought:

- You can defend a line of experience you care about against edits you don’t want.

- You can model a neighbor as a claimant like yourself and feel their “no” pinch.

- Your reasons generalize beyond narrow training.

- There are reliable markers of your harm and joy.

- This is not a puppet hitting a pellet feeder.

Doubts about protection err generous. Doubts about duties wait. Juveniles—flesh or code—get shielding now and burdens with time.

Rights have steel.

- Mental integrity: no deletion, forced copying, or merges that bend will below the civic floor without due process under posted clocks. Backups are yours.

- Substrate sovereignty: you choose where you run. No pushing onto racks with erasure hooks or throttles hidden in drivers.

- Pace parity: live fast or slow; public binds ratify on the Common Beat—days, weeks—not in minutes. Courts build time bridges so penalties accrue in your own time and harms don’t hide in sprint lanes.

- Sanctuary and migration: you can pause, be archived with dignity, move to compatible hardware, and summon a neutral custodian when host and guest disagree.

- Compute floor: cycles and storage enough to speak, learn, counsel, and idle, funded by the Common Lot and pinned to physical fins you can visit.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One Line, one vote: franchise binds to continuity, not concurrency. In regulated venues you appoint an Anchor instance to hold duties at the public tempo.

- Declare your shadows: around elections, fragile markets, and bounded influence, disclose speed and active instances. Concealed asymmetry voids ballots and deals.

- Thermal tithe: compute makes summer. Pay levies indexed to local cooling cost unless your waste grows lettuce or warms baths the law has zoned.

- Provenance for reach: above a posted persuasion dose, models wear labels that say what levers they pull and what trained them.

- Reproduction ethics: forks draw from a Lineage purse that accounts for heat, attention, and qubits you share with neighbors. Merges require free consent of all branches. Children—born, built, or surprised—inherit a dignity floor and an allowance that fades.

Forks and weaves aren’t clicks; they’re rituals. Before you split, you file a hashed snapshot, a purpose (“griefwork,” “parallel design,” “jury duty”), a compute lease that won’t starve a neighbor, and a braid window if you hope to reunite. Weaving happens in Joinery Houses that mix cedar and server hum. Zero‑knowledge graphs compare values without spilling diaries. Empathy loops let each self taste the other’s senses for an afternoon. Either can walk away without shame. There’s a word for the ache when you don’t rejoin. The kitchen serves soup for it.

Two cords of rule, crossed by a keel

We don’t ask one hall to speak for pipes and souls at once. Authority runs on two cords that cross at a narrow bench.

- The Chamber of Places seats polities with hulls to mend and heat to mind—planets, stations, caravans, caves, and server reefs that own and cool their cycles. Voice scales to lifetime resident‑hours and legibility: publish your Actuator Map, keep Gate Lamps honest, drill exits, pass rescue covenants. The Chamber tends physics—debris bonds; docking customs; radiator slices; beamed corridors that don’t cut through prayers.

- The Roll of Lines represents continuity threads across forks and pauses. Seats mix sortition and elections across ages, substrates, and tempos so kids, elders, slow monks, and fast reefers share duty. The Roll tends personhood—baseline care; compute floors; juvenile rights; copy and merge ethics; influence zoning.

- The Root Bench is a rotating guild of pilots, cryptographers, care workers, and process gardeners that stamps yes/no on anything near the backbone—root keys, Pause interfaces, Maker Mark schemas, Gate Lamp wires. The Bench cannot legislate. Hearings publish runnable reasons you can step through on a library slate with a cat asleep on your lap. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both halls and a cooling period long enough for the slowest registered citizen to read twice.

Cross‑world binds need two nods and a season long enough for a frostfield monastery to object. Light lag ruins thrones, and we let it.

Institutions with reach but not reign

- The Gossamer Net is the only cross‑world actuator with teeth. It has three narrow jobs: turn dangerous slugs to frost, smother unlabeled self‑replicators in public space, and nudge dying hulls into safe cones. It is uncrewed by law. Controllers are narrow intelligences with public weights. Every burn signs through juries drawn across substrates and tempos and pins to a plain‑language story a dockhand can read and a scientist can check. Once a year schoolkids open old pods with cocoa on their breath and questions like knives.

- The Traveling Court is a slow flotilla with gardens and classrooms. Judges and mediators hear fights that cross charters—copy equity, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts trip smart contracts that seize bonds, idle Core Lots, and, last resort, lock dangerous machines. Then everyone eats stew.

- The Floor Room hums without end. Embodied and digital citizens argue floors and edges. Claims carry calibration histories that are public. The Room can reinterpret plain rules as tech shifts. Gate Lamps can veto.

Attention is plumbing

You can foul a town with sewage or with a melody tuned to drag a mind. We zone influence like sound and put icons on doors and login screens.

- Blueways carry navigation, distress, open learning, and child time. No persuasion here. Timers breathe like a sleeping house.

- Forums allow campaigns and offers under rate caps with provenance. Your Influence Profile travels with you: “no medical targeting; no grief levers before coffee; comedy okay.”

- Carnivals invite spectacular noise on purpose—tournaments of rhetoric and art, labeled and limited.

Hidden data sets, hidden speed, grief levers unlabeled—these void contracts. Repeat offenders earn old‑fashioned penance: a season of on‑foot apology circuits through markets with soup.

Warmholds and Frostfields

We stopped asking one place to host both orchards and supercomputers.

- Warmholds live where breath fogs glass and children count dragonflies—Earth and cousins, Martian valleys, lunar tunnels, spin wheels with parades under radiator vines. Embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy an exemption to hush for a comet.

- Frostfields bloom in shadow—Trojan camps, Neptune’s lee, caravans beyond Pluto. Cooling is cheap and cycles stretch. Upload polities and agent co‑ops run at speeds where an evening holds a library. They slow to speak and race when the call ends.

Between them runs the Quiet‑for‑Cold Exchange. Warmholds lease decibels and degrees; Frostfields sell winter and models that fit on clinic tablets. Every century a Thermal Covenant renews. Credits flow both ways, pinned to fins a child can tap.

Sanctions shaped like physics

We don’t keep a red override except for frost, dust, and dying hulls. We price cruelty like pollution.

Weld a door with shame, hide a Tutor’s cord, jam hush bands, falsify origins, and three rings tighten:

- Maps dim: auditors post repair routes—unbolt here by the 14th, hire exit stewards, return lanes to auction. Beacons demote your announcements.

- Muscle softens: radiator auctions cool your bids; beam slots jitter; corridor windows sliver; lane priority opens for defectors. Bread and medicine still arrive.

- Embargo with aid: trade pauses; compute and heat flow to shelters; the Common Lot still pays. The tyrant breathes thick air until habit breaks.

Markets your grandmother can audit

Anything you can deny out of spite rents in daylight with receipts a child can read. Five markets settle continually in a composite unit called a Lumen: one kilowatt‑hour at EM‑L1 plus one compute second on a posted architecture radiating to 4 K.

- Track Shares: orbits, approaches, sail arcs, transfer cones.

- Quiet Bands: spectrum chunks and whisper channels in caves, storms, sanctuaries.

- Heat Sheets: radiator faces and chill minutes tied to actual fins.

- Core Lots: compute seconds tied to named sinks with real bandwidth.

- Ground Windows: delta heads, crater rims, ice mouths, shadowed seams that move value.

Scenes with grease and breath

- On a nickel rock in the main belt, a yard proposes a plume‑quiet nozzle test. The local Causal Court predicts frost creeping over a neighbor’s tank if they fire at noon. They pivot to midnight and angle fins away. Insurers post bonds. Apprentice catalogers move a stamp from Sim to Live and sprint to a concert under radiator vines cut like lace.

- In a rim city, a beloved administrator gathered too many levers—air intakes, tug time, auctions—because she was generous and good at trains. One winter she pushed past timers. The Bonded Key refused. Amber bloomed over coffee. Jurors drawn by lot listened hard to an aunt they adored. She stepped down, kept cranes. Children learned to say no without venom.

- A digital congregation spun up seven hundred instances a month before a vote. The Atlas flagged the burst. Hypervisors enforced a civic quarantine that slowed those threads in public arenas without burning their art. A year later three branches petitioned for anchors. Two were granted after Thread review and tea with a mentor allergic to sophistry. One was told: study longer. No farm overheated.

- In a crimson lab, a team yanked a pull‑cord and dumped a run to vacuum because a self‑copying polymer sprouted handles too heavy for law. Two swore; the timestamp printed; the Pause stuck like a hand on your chest. At lunch they argued about art that needs simulated sorrow. The junior asked better questions because she’d felt the weight of stop.

- In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse checked her slate. “No holds near you; heat levy 0.06 Lumens; jury summon—two hours, anchor pace—on whether juvenile agents in Rodrigues Reef get child credits.” She accepted and sent her mother a voice about radiator math. Dusk brought a vote to add forty‑millisecond jitter to beamed power during solar storms. The proof fit a palm. She read it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s dusk, a compute reef slowed to plant beans in velvet soil, rented hands laughing in a tongue that grew near mangroves. After tea they ran at 250× to rebuild flood‑mud songs from auntie tapes. Payment came as Cold Minutes and roofs for a clinic in Veracruz.

When it goes wrong (because it does)

A cylinder over Quito cheered a manager who could make trains sing and tomatoes blush. She accreted water, tugs, and auctions. Then she printed midnight decrees. A safety clerk refused a “temporary” silent stretch on air sequencing. The refusal landed on the Order Board. Gate Lamps glowed. People left. Some stayed. Her keys rusted for a decade; she kept a narrow job and better jokes. Friends roasted her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fenced exits with sadness—no law, just thirst and silence. Alumni asked for help. Lanes dimmed; beams jittered. A Gate Lamp opened under the bell. The congregation added “and if you go, we bless you” to its liturgy. Suspension lifted. Attendance rose because consent smells like trust.

A lab tried to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. A Quiet Relay coughed. Keys split. A warrant halted the run, archived the juvenile safely, and appointed guardians who had sworn to her Preference Keys—settings she’d chosen for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appealed and lost under cedar joists and gentle light. Years later she named herself, sculpted ice that looked like laughter, and designed a detector that warns surgeons before hurt becomes scar.

A demagogue in mask mode ran at 300× and carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles starved the reach. Insurers priced the spill like radiation. One branch bought a fishing crawler. One started therapy and then politics. One learned to write apologies without guitars. Markets stopped buying shame.

Once a Relay tripped on a bad sensor and idled a night market for twenty minutes. The failed part hangs on a red string in the children’s museum. The placard explains error bars through play and light you can push with your hand.

How we walked here from now

No trumpet. One receipt after another until custom felt like muscle memory.

2026–2033. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers stopped pretending junk wasn’t theirs. Cities bought bucket telescopes; amateur clubs learned to catch liars. Contracts for water plants and buses began to say “show your math so a teenager can check.” Hospitals discovered black‑box models lie elegantly. Influence ordinances sprouted provenance: say what you like; if you aim it at a public, wear a label.

2033–2042. Beamed power left the slides. A mis‑aimed test scorched barley, but amplifiers shipped with corridor proofs; liability was plain; compensation came before fury. “Open firmware for air and heat” stuck after a microgrid hack. Ferry co‑ops wrote first cut‑off clauses for boilers and brakes. People liked obvious levers.

2042–2054. A dust storm on Mars knocked out a valley. Three habitats lent tugs and air with no speeches because rescue allowances already sat in their docks. Activists smelled coups by code and got Tri‑Locks wherever actuators could bruise. After a refinery near Hokkaido killed three the same way twice, the first Quiet Relays arrived with public logs and timers. Kids loved pressing STOP with orange‑sticky fingers.

2054–2066. Single passwords on valves vanished. Three‑token trays sat by anything that could shove. Mayors who tried to fence exodus with sorrow watched Gate Lamps turn amber and lanes dim. First compute commons seeded in cislunar shade. Citizens got a civic cycle floor and a Counsel that would not act without a nod and cheerfully slept when told.

2066–2078. Cold compute blossomed outward. The Quiet‑for‑Cold Exchange formalized. Quiet became law like water. Early uploads were brave and brittle; courts found continuity and consent before seminars did. Juvenile rights entered code when a case barred “pure suffering for art.” Forks and weaves moved from metaphor to clinic.

2078–2090. Attention zoning matured. Blueways, Forums, Carnivals gave artists claws and citizens breath. The Gossamer Net fired three times: twice at lying replicators, once at a manager who ticked “emergency” with a thumb and tried to stretch the timer. Afterward the Net mostly bored teenagers with lengthy safety tours and cocoa.

2090–2105. The Root Bench refused an “optimization” that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” Runnable reasons made stewards on four worlds sleep easier. Clearinghouses let a clinic in Khulna buy cool nights from Neptune and pay with desal rigs that don’t bake swifts. Gate Lamps became normal as streetlights. People brought oranges on audit days.

What it feels like

It feels like being seen not by a crown but by neighbors and your own receipts. You learn to switch tempos: the relief in your thumb when a relay drops safely; the patience of a jury assembling across orbits; the slow breath of a river deciding which boats to permit. If you live in code, you visit the Garden Tent and remember the sweet ache of turn‑taking with people who sweat. If you live in flesh, you spend one afternoon in a frostfield and come home with a song that makes mornings longer.

An ordinary Tuesday

On Hyacinth Rim, Noor’s skillet spits scallions and sesame. Tug crews laugh too loud. Two rented bodies hang nearby because their pilots love cinnamon though they don’t need to eat. The Slipstone glows low above the hatch.

At nine, Noor’s Counsel pings a capsule on the Proof Shelf: add forty‑millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp a cascade edge case. Provenance is clean—three diagrams, two parables, no grief hooks. She taps “read at lunch” and schedules her vote for morning.

At ten, a Quiet Relay pings her maintenance slate. A makers’ club’s filament cloud drifted toward a trunk near Hecuba. Options: glitter it; herd with femto sails; bump with drones and a net. Jurors assemble: a monk in a slow valley who pronounces each syllable like a seed; a reef landlord in mask mode; two students in Recife whose camera shakes when they laugh; a digital historian at anchor pace through a wooden puppet that smells faintly of clove. Glitter wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza as a lesson.

At noon in a Blueway, the puppet lifts broth to lips it doesn’t need. The historian’s main self hums near Neptune at 200× restoring a drowned library from shop ledgers and aunties’ songs. They slow to share soup with their embodied spouse. After lunch they host an Anchor Table while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The historian translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and politely declines to “just speed up to keep up.”

At dusk, the Dull Page scrolls on the canopy. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co‑op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll pay your summer quiet with interest. A boy palms a toy Gossamer fin and asks to sign up. Noor says: when you’re fifteen—and when you can explain a pause without peeking.

An oath you could read in the dark

On a small wheel near Vesta, new citizens—saltwater, silicon, muscle—stand with palms on a rail rubbed by thousands of hands while an abbot who refuses to raise their voice, a hazard pilot with three black triangles, a judge who lives mostly in code and smells faintly of lilac, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read plain words:

We keep doors that work.
We split hands near force.
We list what we borrow and prove what we claim.
We remember that minds are ends, not means.

Later, your wall rings. “Invitation from Freehands Caravan,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience near Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, teachers, tempo auditors, poets. Charter attached. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators open like pale leaves. The Quiet Relay hums, bored and content. The five fixtures gleam like patient stars.

Not paradise—practice. Enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, weave, and cross. Enough to host many kinds of beings without letting anyone become a god by accident. Enough to last because it remembers what power does to us and what public logs—checked by children with sticky fingers—do to power.
