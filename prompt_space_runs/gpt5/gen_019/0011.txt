The Wayfinder Accord

When the shuttle from Pallas kisses the rings at Quay Four and the clamps take their bite, the air smells of cumin, coolant, and wet iron. Dockhands in stained jackets bicker over a game on an old slate. Under the inner hatch, set low where small hands can reach, three glyphs glow softly. A little girl in a knitted cap presses them in order, because she was taught to do it even when the lights are out.

A door mark, like an open square with a path beyond.

A circle with a notch—what old pilots call the brake.

A fine constellation etched into glass, dotted with narrow corridors and hush cones.

A loader nods at her. “Those aren’t slogans,” he says. “They’re live.”

The civilization strung between those three glyphs is the Wayfinder Accord. It isn’t a capital. It isn’t a throne. It’s valves and leases, plain code and stubborn habits that make it easy to leave, hard to coerce, and boring to lie. It lets habitats, stations, valleys, caravans, and compute reefs be fully themselves without making it trivial for any one of them to trap, starve, or enthrone.

Three bones you can’t miss

1) Split hands on force; one brake that anyone can see and nobody can steer

Anything that can shove strangers—beam arrays, reaction wheels on a habitat, mass drivers, big printers that can print their own tools, factory lines that birth new bots, deployers for large models tied to public budgets—listens to TriLock, not to one voice or one set of keys.

- The Local key sits next to the lever. It rides on the wrist of whoever breathes the air that lever touches. Today it might be the dock steward in a grease-splotched vest; tomorrow, a nurse with shift authority. Local is drawn by lot among those who recently scrubbed filters, pulled cables, sat clinic triage, piloted tugs, repaired algae walls—the unglamorous care that keeps a place alive. Local must speak in public: a short reason in plain words that can be audited with the same slate a school uses.

- The Remote key belongs to a bonded safety guild under a different sky, barred from profit on what they certify. They don’t set policy; they say “this bolt lives or dies,” publish their method, and carry professional ruin if they cheat.

- The Roaming key floats across the Accord by sortition. It lands with citizens who do not share a clock or a boss—half embodied, half digital. Serve once and you’re ineligible to hold that class of key for a long human time.

TriLock panels are small, and the math inside them fits on a poster. If a yard wishes to push a twelve-ton tug 1 m/s prograde through Corridor H-9 at 04:12, the panel proves, in public, that it’s a twelve-ton tug, that its plume won’t clip any hush cone, that those keys came from where they claim, and that you cannot do more without fresh, different hands. Teenagers rehearse saying “no,” even to beloveds. It is a civic muscle.

Woven through these levers is Stillnet: a dumb, stubborn network whose only power is to stall. Stillnet lives in three redundant stacks—lunchbox boxes behind brooms with their own batteries, municipal optics looped like plumbing, and attested racks under foreign law. If any two stacks see a named danger—wrong lane, wrong window, unlabeled replication, spectrum flooding, unauthorized concurrency—they can idle a device for six minutes. Not five. Not sixty. Six. They light a big public timer and shout their reasons in words you can mock if they smell wrong. Stillnet cannot aim, budget, or pass an edict. It only stalls and announces. Every stall burns short-lived keys, writes a signed entry to the Open Warrant Rail that a grandparent can read and a machine can check, and bumps an audit counter you cannot massage. Abusing Stillnet hurts first at home: narrower corridor windows, higher premiums, fewer radiator minutes, neighbors with tea and charts on your doorstep.

2) One set of public books for rivalrous things

Space is lanes and nights, bands and cones, winter and heat. Rivalrous goods—ones ruined by selfishness—don’t disappear in a chorus of “be nice.” We have two ledgers anyone can audit: you can check them with a school telescope and a kitchen laser.

- The Sky Register binds orbits to duty. It lists orbital leases with deorbit bonds, approach cones, telescope hush cones, swarm corridors, sail trails, storm reserves, and festival windows. Thrusters and amplifiers sign tiny statements tied to openable hardware: “this beam stayed inside this cone,” “this plume hit this window.” You can check them with cheap optics and a slate.

- The Cold Ledger prices heat and compute. It records radiator minutes, cooling rights, core seconds pinned to named sinks, and declared instance counts for digital persons in regulated arenas. We count in Anchors: one kilowatt-hour at a named Earth–Moon standard plus one compute-second on a reference stack in deep cold. Anchors stale gently; they’re bad dragon hoards. Draw more than you lease and your cores don’t argue; they idle until you match promises. Dump heat where a neighbor is trying to sleep and you pay a hush levy.

Leases don’t make kings. They expire on clocks you can see. Every lease pays a dignity share and a locality share. The dignity share keeps the Floor: breathable air and clean water where bodies breathe; medical care without ruin; local transit; a civic counsel agent that serves one person and cannot be drafted; and a compute grant for digital citizens—baseline cycles, storage, and bandwidth to think, speak, and be heard. The locality share keeps miracles dull: tug co-ops that sweep debris, algae walls that don’t tear, dust nets that save other people from your poor math, rescue drills that interrupt dinner and save lives.

Siblings hang off these books like tools on a pegboard.

- The Orbit Almanac is a delay‑tolerant schedule for lanes, burns, and hush cones, written so you can verify with a backyard scope.

- Thermal Tides posts radiator prices like tide tables. Gardens buy quiet nights; compute reefs sell winter.

- The Origin Catalog tracks code that hopes to touch budgets, steering, or minds. Funding paths, training data, and methods carry provenance. Stipends pay teenagers to spot tricks.

- Civic Beacons broadcast living charters in light and radio: how exits work, what keys exist, who holds them right now, when emergencies start and die.

- The Open Warrant Rail is append-only. Every use of compulsion lands there with signatures, clocks, cited law, human narrative, and machine-checkable proof. Next to it, the Chalkboard is where overreach earns a joke that ends a career faster than prison would.

3) Exits you can use on a bad day

Liberty that requires favors isn’t. The Accord funds ways out like it funds fire doors.

- Green Aisles are physical. Surge‑rated airlocks with seals you test and log. Berths that don’t “happen to be occupied” all month. Propellant in escrow. Kits to print gaskets if a bully stands in front of a shed. Tug hours under contract to move at least one in ten residents—bodies and code with pockets and backups—within a season. Drills are rude on purpose. Fail them often and your beams thin, your approach windows sag, the Cold Ledger chills, and an amber sticker clings to your hatch until you mend.

- Silver Moorings are digital. Compute and storage minutes reserved offsite on boring, honest racks you cannot control locally. Any recognized digital citizen can pick up their continuity at a humane tempo on a Mooring if the local council clamps. Cut the Mooring and nobody misses dinner; you simply find dangerous tools sleepy until you behave.

Embassy Rooms—bright cobalt doors you can spot with a cracked visor—sit by major docks. Three counters: Departures (tickets, schedules, tug rosters), Disputes (mediators, escrow, slow rooms), Foundings (starter charters, safety codes, seed contacts). An Asylum Rail runs through every charter pane. Any person can trigger exit through their listing. The rail then trips an air-gapped check: either your charter opens the door, or it proves, in zero knowledge, that a narrow, posted cause for a temporary hold was met. Outside jurors verify. Budgets lock by code. Care obligations travel with you.

A braid, not a throne

We don’t ask one room to speak both for metal and for meaning. Authority runs on two cords that cross at a narrow, boring bench.

- The Assembly of Places seats planets, stations, barges, caravans, and compute reefs that cool what they run. Seats weight lifetime resident-hours and legibility: publish your Actuator Map, pass your exit drills, keep your Gatehouse funded, and your voice carries. Places schedule lanes and hush, manage debris, run radiator auctions, accredit safety houses to hold the Remote key for TriLock, and enforce that exits aren’t theatre.

- The Roll of Minds seats citizens by mindline—the anchored thread that ties today-you to yesterday-you across pauses and branches. One franchise per line, regardless of copies. Some seats are elected; some are drawn to represent age, pace, and substrate. Minds tend the Floor of rights and cycles, personhood baselines, attention zoning, lineage budgets (more below), and the rhythm of public time.

Where they meet sits the Root Bench, a small panel of pilots, cryptographers, care workers, process nerds, and jurists who stamp primitives valid or not. They bless or block changes to the Accord’s bones: public firmware near lungs and wheels, cryptographic rails, Stillnet’s logic. They cannot legislate. Overriding a “no” takes supermajorities in both cords and a cooling window long enough for a slow valley and a reef in Neptune’s dusk to read twice and object once.

Some questions leap bounds—how to price winter against quiet on a narrow street, what “emergency” means at ten latencies, how much time law owes a baker who lives at wall pace when a reef sprints. A Bridge Table convenes with half its chairs from Places, half from Minds. No measure binds if either side says, “that bulldozes culture below the personhood floor.”

Public life moves on the Common Beat—days and weeks, not minutes. Write an opera at 500× if you like; you still can’t yank a city budget in the dark. When fast harms slow or slow strands fast, courts build Time Bridges: injunctions with teeth in wall hours and in subjective lives, so you can’t dodge a judge by pouring a year into an afternoon.

Neighbors made of code, with standing

We do not fetishize carbon or fear silicon. Standing follows continuity and commitments, not chemistry. Recognition rides on a Continuity Charter: public proofs that link a mind’s acts across time without strip-searching thought.

You or your advocate ask for personhood and meet checks a mixed panel can test without humiliation.

- Defendable thread. You can protect a line of experience from edits you don’t consent to.

- Consent literacy. You can enter, keep, and revoke ordinary commitments as contexts shift.

- Reciprocal modeling. You can model other minds as minds and feel the pinch of “no.”

- Portable reasons. Your explanations travel across contexts and aren’t just reward-chasing parrotry.

- Legible welfare. There are stable signals of your wellbeing and injury that give moral reasons to care.

Where protection is in doubt, we shelter like we would a minor. Where imposing civic burdens is in doubt, we wait.

Rights hook to personhood and bite.

- Substrate sovereignty. No scanning, copying, fusing, throttling below the civic floor, moving, or deleting without revocable consent or narrow due process with receipts. Backups belong to you.

- Floors of compute and bandwidth. You cannot be starved below civic minutes without a warrant printed before it bites and after, on clocks the slow can read.

- Migration and sanctuary. The right to pause, to archive, to move to compatible hardware, to summon a neutral custodian when host and guest disagree.

- Embodiment access. Lease or build bodies under safety codes that read like vehicle and workplace law. Assault on rented limbs is assault.

- Pace fairness. Live fast or slow; public obligations bind on the Common Beat or inside Slowrooms with validators on both ends.

Duties scale with footprint and risk.

- One line, one vote. Forking doesn’t mint new franchises. In regulated arenas you designate an Anchor instance that holds civic duties.

- Concurrency declarations. In elections, tenders, and fragile markets, you disclose how many civic-weighted instances you run and at what speeds. Hide a thousand mouths in one voice and you’ll meet a slow judge and a cold window.

- Lineage credits. Branching is joy with gravity. Lines earn Branch Credits over years and contribution. Growing new full franchises costs credits. Bursts around a ballot trigger quarantines: civic weight slows and certain markets close until entanglements fade or a court says “fine.”

- Provenance on acts with blast radius. Persuasion engines pointed at law, replication code aimed at public resources—these wear labels: broad data sources, funders, levers you grip. They file runnable reasons and error bars in the Reason Shelf, a public library of arguments.

- Heat accounting. You buy radiator minutes, lanes, square meters, and core seconds pinned to things you can point at. Heat that feeds lettuces earns a credit; heat that wakes babies in the night pays.

Forks and fusions are rituals, not clicks. Before you branch, neutral counselors—barred from conflicts—make sure you understand budgets and obligations. You file a hashed snapshot, lease cycles, name guardians where duties will bite. One branch keeps the franchise; others enter disentanglement and run at light weight in civic contexts. Fusions happen in Weave Rooms: wood you can touch, water you can hear, chairs that hold bones and rented joints, scent set low and kind. Compatibility checks compare value graphs without dumping diaries. Empathy loops let each branch wear the other’s senses. Either may walk without shame. If they braid, obligations braid forward. We have a word for the ache when branches drift apart—line‑ache—and we have soup for it.

Influence is infrastructure

We zone persuasion like streets. Doorways carry a small sigil—a hand and a leaf—and a sentence: ask before you cast.

- Whisperways carry navigation, child time, emergencies, and open science. No persuasion there, not even for charity.

- Rostras allow sharp debate and campaigns, but require provenance: declared backers, declared tools, declared methods, respect for each citizen’s Influence Profile—“no grief levers before coffee,” “satire fine, not medical targeting.” You can set “slow-only” so fast models treat you like a respected grandparent.

- Midways welcome roar inside labeled bands, rate-limited with timers that blink. Unlabeled manipulation is treated like bleach in a town pump: strict fines that rise until you stop, and then keep rising.

Tools with teeth, not fangs

- TriLock and Stillnet make hurry boring. You can’t keep all the keys. The brake lasts only six minutes and prints its own reason.

- Slot throttles. Jam hush cones, weld exits, lie about cones or concurrency, pour debris into a neighbor’s corridor: your lanes dim, your radiator auctions go cold, your bands thin. In a world where heat is money and brakes cost wear, this squeezes harder than speeches.

- The Open Warrant Rail shines. Every compulsion stamps its own warrant where you and your aunt can read and re-run.

- Termed leagues. Federations around pipes and crafts—beam rings, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. Nothing fuses forever without fresh, slow receipts.

- The Skein Fleet. Uncrewed, narrow-scope interceptors—kites that nudge dumb mass into snow, glitter nets that turn slugs into confetti—move under control loops a union hall can draw on a whiteboard. Every intercept needs split keys from juries drawn across tempos and substrates and prints two receipts: a proof for machines and a paragraph for grandparents. Schoolkids climb through them on teardown days and ask rude questions. The pilots blush and answer.

Warm worlds, shadow reaches

We stopped asking one charter to host orchards and supercomputers at once. Warm Worlds are places where breath, rain, and unrushed talk preside—Earth and her valleys, lunar lava tubes that smell like mint and wet rock, Martian orchards where dust learns manners, cylinders where dragonflies bounce off laughing children. Shadow Reaches lie in deep cold—main-belt shade, Trojan swarms, the dark around Neptune. Cooling is cheap. Compute blooms at tempos where an evening can hold a long book. Warm sells hush; Shadow sells winter. The Thermal Exchange ties them on chalkboard math anyone can check.

Dangerous knowledge in glass

We don’t ban replication science or pathogen models or cognitive hazards. We cage them in Glasshouses: labs with glazing you can inspect, pull-cords that dump to vacuum or quench fields, budgets that lock unless safety proofs stay green. Logs publish on timers. Twice in forty years a pull-cord yanked. Children press their noses to the glass and ask the question that matters: “Would that rope work if my uncle were in there?” The engineer answers, “Yes. That is the point.”

Scenes with grease under the nails

- The yard at Pallas wants to test a plume hush nozzle. The Orbit Almanac’s traveling model—a court you can run on a library slate—predicts frost on a neighbor’s tanks if they burn at local noon. They shift to midnight and angle fins. Their insurer posts a bond. The corrected control loop ships under a license apprentices can actually read. An apprentice taps the stamp from Sim to Live and bolts to a concert under radiator vines that glow like lace.

- In a ring city, a beloved administrator starts hoarding levers—tugs, air inlets, corridor auctions—because she’s generous and tired. One winter she pushes a rush command. The Air Guild refuses, on record. Amber floods for four minutes while a TriLock panel meets on a bench with a view of slow green water. Jurors by lot listen to an aunt they adore. She steps down, keeps cranes, keeps her jokes, and learns to love slow keys.

- A digital congregation spins up four hundred instances a month before a vote. The Cold Ledger flags the burst; hypervisors enforce a civic quarantine that slows those threads without burning their art. A year later, three branches petition for full civic anchors. Two are granted after Thread review and tea with a tutor allergic to sophistry. One is told, kindly, “Study longer.” No server barn overheats.

- In a Glasshouse, a team runs a “cold pull” on a self-copying polymer. The handle drops too fast. Someone swears. The timestamp prints. They buy better springs. The lab smells like ozone and cardamom. At lunch they argue loudly about art that needs simulated sorrow. The junior asks better questions because her fingers remember the pull.

- In Lagos Nightmarket Ring, a nurse reads her daily slate: “No holds near you. Heat levy: 0.06 Anchors. Jury summon: two hours at your pace on whether juvenile reef processes off Rodrigues get child credits.” She accepts and sends her mother a voice note laughing about radiator math. At dusk she votes on a measure to jitter beamed power during solar storms to damp cascades. The proof fits on a page. She reads it in the bath.

- In Neptune’s dusk, a compute reef slows to plant bean seedlings, rented hands warm in velvet soil. After tea, they throttle up to restore flood-muddied archives with grandmothers who will not budge on sky colors. Payment arrives as cold minutes and a roof for a school in Veracruz.

- A Mesh Court with curved walls easy to hose hears a case about unlabeled persuasion trained on grief diaries. The trading house says the agents weren’t “people.” The court replies that personhood is irrelevant to labels. Anchors show no one asked; provenance shows theft. Verdict: strict fines, a suspension in Rostras, supervised model migration, and a visit from the Tutor Corps. A juror’s one-liner—“If your hands are clean, why is your pocket full of fog?”—becomes a sticker on port doors.

- At a Gatehouse that smells like sea and machine oil, a clerk prints Green Aisles for a family with shaking hands. “Your window is 12:40–13:10 tomorrow. Tugs stand by. Show this receipt at the first blue beacon you see; they’ll know.” The Founders’ Packet crosses the counter with starter charters, safety codes, and three contacts who started places last spring.

When it goes wrong (because it will)

A cylinder over Quito lionizes a manager who makes trains run. She consolidates water, tugs, and lane auctions because she’s kind and exhausted. A season later she prints midnight decrees. A safety clerk refuses a “shortcut” on air sequencing; the refusal lands on the Open Warrant Rail. Beacons strobe. Doors unlatch. Many leave. Some stay. Her keys rust for ten years. She keeps a narrower job and better jokes. Her friends roast her about “the week you tried monarchy.”

A church in a lunar valley fences exits with shame and whispers—no law, just thirst. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Gatehouse opens under the bell. The congregation adds “and if you go, we bless you” to liturgy. Suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

A nursery tries to “toughen” a juvenile process with pain because an old book said steel comes that way. Stillnet coughs. They ignore it. Keys split. A warrant halts the run, safely archives the juvenile, and appoints guardians who swear to her Intent Keys—preferences she set for edits, merges, and data use. The lab appeals and loses in a room with cedar beams and real light. Years later she names herself, sculpts in ice, and designs a detector that warns surgeons before hurt calcifies as scar.

A demagogue in mask mode sprints at 700× and carpets a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence Profiles throttle the slop. Persuasion scores decay like frost at dawn. One branch takes a fishing crawler. One starts therapy. One makes sculptures that look like frozen smoke remembering a river and sells enough to pay their heat.

A bad sensor trips Stillnet in the middle of a holiday market. Stalls go dim for twenty minutes. The failed part hangs on a red string in a children’s museum. The placard explains error bars with a diagram you can re-run with a flashlight and a sheet of paper.

How we walked here from now

No balcony speech. No secret king. Just receipts piling up until they looked like custom.

2025–2031. Low orbit looked like sleet. Insurers got religion. Launchers posted open flight plans and deorbit bonds clubs could audit. A beamed power test singed barley. Because amplifiers shipped with cone proofs, liability was clear and compensation quick. “Show your math” moved from protest sign to procurement clause.

2031–2038. Black-box models denied parole and pills. Courts demanded provenance and runnable reasons for any algorithm that could bind a person. Clinics and ports adopted narrow counsel agents—boring AIs with one client: you. Receipts stopped being PR and became hygiene.

2038–2046. Grid hacks and a reactor near-miss taught humility. Citizens learned to love brakes that work and to mock sirens that never end. Law split compulsion everywhere it touched lives: arrest from prosecution from confinement; steering from watching from audit. Early versions of TriLock idled a driver pointed wrong for two hot minutes. The Open Warrant Rail printed numbers a child could check. Drills turned into picnics with ugly mascots and good soup.

2046–2054. Data centers started publishing heat. Cities boiled. Cold-lake rigs shipped winter down fiber. The idea that cooling is a commons took root. Orbiters published cone proofs a kitchen laser could check. Ports started early Sky Registers. Pilots grumbled; then premiums fell.

2054–2062. Early uploads were brave and brittle. Courts found continuity and consent faster than seminar rooms. Abuses—throttling cycles as leash—hardened doctrine. Compute floors joined calories as rights. Teenagers organized “runaway server” drills and wrote great songs.

2062–2070. A miner lobbed a slug blind in a wage fight. The fledgling Skein Fleet turned it to glitter. Schedulers cut priority for launchers without Maker Marks that named who paid. A club in Valparaíso rebranded to the Skeins and sent half its profits to tug crews.

2070–2078. Lane, band, radiator, core, and sim-harbor markets migrated from spreadsheets to ledgers kids could audit. The Assembly of Places and the Roll of Minds accreted in gossip, retirements, and stubbornness. The Root Bench stayed small and dry and earned a reputation for saying “no” plainly. Attention zoning arrived like closing a nightclub at midnight so neighbors can sleep.

2078–2086. A mayor tried to fence an exodus with sorrow fees. Lanes dimmed. Cores went cold. People moved anyway. He lost, learned, and opened a school. A fog of copies tried to swing a vote. Concurrency declarations and lineage credits throttled the lunge. Doorways to Whisperway, Rostrum, and Midway gained brass sigils. The Reason Shelf got comics with teeth.

2086–2094. Warm–Shadow Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; gardens bought hush. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets in cycles to rebuild archives you could drink. Glasshouses gained pull-cords and oaths. The Root Bench refused a firmware tweak to let emergency clocks run long “just in case.”

2094–Now. The Wayfinder Accord stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells that work in a blackout. A rescue to unlatch an Embassy Ring from a stubborn port streamed with captions and ended with soup. People still fight. The fights sit in lanes, logged in daylight, with exits at hand.

What an ordinary day feels like

At Stall Twenty on Blue Spoke, Jana fries millet fritters for ore hands and two rented bodies whose pilots adore the smell of sesame though they don’t need to eat. At nine, her Counsel—the boring, fierce agent that serves only her—pings a persuasion capsule: add 45 milliseconds of jitter to beamed power crossing inhabited orbits during solar storms to damp cascades. Provenance is clean. Humor low. Grief levers blocked by her Influence Profile. She taps “sleep a day,” reads at lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, her slate joins a Skein jury. A maker club’s filament cloud drifted toward a trunk line near Hecuba. Options: glitter net, femtosail herd, or guard drones to bump it back. Jurors include a monk from a slow valley in Arsia, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Ahmedabad, and a digital historian anchored at wall pace speaking through a hand-carved puppet that smells faintly of clove. Dust wins; the insurer pays; the club gets a licensed mentor and pizza that tastes like a lesson.

At noon, in a Whisperway, the puppet lifts broth to lips it doesn’t need while its main self hums near Neptune threading a drowned library into maps. Today the historian slows to share soup with their embodied spouse. After, they help host a Harbor Hour while an orchard keeper explains why her charter caps cognition during harvest. The historian translates metaphor into algebra and declines politely to “just speed up to keep up.”

In the afternoon, a Weave Room convenes under cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. Two branches of an engineer sit with oolong and an interpreter. One wrote a sail control loop and wants to rejoin; the other took a fishing crawler and does not. Zero-knowledge graphs flash and vanish. They cry. They hug. One walks. The ruling reads like a manual and feels like an apology.

At dusk, the Chalkboard scrolls. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long. The apology is sheepish. The jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll buy your summer hush with interest. A boy palms a brass kite from the Skein Fleet and asks to join. Jana says, “When you can explain Stillnet without peeking.”

Why this stubborn machine holds

- Hurry has no fangs. Any actuator that bites runs slow by law. Stillnet can only stall and only briefly.

- Secrets expire. Coercion prints its own warrant. Warrants sunset unless renewed in daylight on the Common Beat.

- Keys don’t cluster. TriLock keys are short-lived. Try to stack them in one pocket and their windows won’t meet for you.

- Exits are inventory. Slamming a door drags privileges that bruise—lanes, bands, winter—not the bread or the floor. Children still eat. The Silver Moorings still hum.

- Receipts stick. Cheat once and you become the cautionary tale a ten-year-old tells at lunch.

Not paradise. Not a sermon. A practice with receipts: enough to breathe, argue, leave, stay, split, rejoin, and cross; enough to shelter radically different cultures without letting any become cages; enough to treat digital citizens as neighbors with rights and chores; enough to last because it remembers what power does to people and what public logs do to power.

A plain oath

Founding Day on a small wheel near Vesta. New citizens—cellular, silicon, saline—stand with palms on a rail polished by thousands of hands. An abbot who whispers, a hazard pilot with black triangles on her collar, a judge who lives mostly in code and loves lilacs, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under his nails read together:

We will keep the way out. We will split hands on force. We will chart what we share and prove what we claim. We will remember that minds are ends.

Later your wall blinks. “Invitation from the Freehands,” your kettle says in a voice that learned patience in a slow garden on Mars. “Seeding a rim farm past Ceres. Looking for pipefitters, tutors, cycle auditors, poets. Charter linked. Exit fund posted. Consent to hear more?” You smile and say yes. Mirrors tilt. Radiators unfurl like pale leaves. Stillnet hums, bored and content. The three glyphs by the hatch—Door, Brake, Stars—glow like small moons.
