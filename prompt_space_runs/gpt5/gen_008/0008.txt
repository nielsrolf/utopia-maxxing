The Starweave Covenant

At every port, above the customs desk and the potted citrus, a thin strip of alloy carries four icons you can feel with a fingertip or read with a telescope: an open palm; a ring of orbits around a bright dot; a small door ajar; an illuminated mind. Beneath them, the Vows:

No single crown. The sky is a commons. Doors stay unlatched. Minds are ends, never means.

They are recited by schoolchildren, by juvenile agents on their first civic day, and by old pilots before they sign a last will. The Vows are not poetry. They ride in fiber, firmware, and law, so that a civilization stretched across light-minutes stays many on purpose without inviting ruin.

How the Covenant is built

The Covenant is three things you can audit.

1) Guardrails and instruments that can say “no” faster than any hand can pull a trigger, but cannot themselves rule.

2) Chambers where worlds and persons bargain under clocks slow enough for comprehension, fast enough that life is not a waiting room.

3) Doors and ladders—Exit Rails and a floor of energy and compute—so that coercion has no cheap path, and flourishing is cheaper than conquest.

Guardrails and instruments

The Truce

In shared space, certain moves are not just bad manners; they are impossible without consent across distance.

- You may not hurl unguided mass at a population or habitat.

- You may not black out an entire spectrum band for a region beyond a brief emergency window.

- You may not release self-replicating machines or code into a shared environment without broadcast beacons, published kill-switches, and keys not your own.

- You may not coerce, copy, edit, or delete a person without due process that can be verified without cracking their skull or code.

These are not prayers. Any system that can make a hole in someone else’s sky carries Actuator Locks: narrow interfaces whose firmware is hash-locked and public. To fire a mass driver above a posted threshold, to redirect a beam beyond approved corridors, to wake a mirror swarm into high-gain focus, you need threshold signatures from three hands not owned by each other: your local charter’s elected board; a remote Safety Guild that you did not pick; and a quorum drawn by sortition from citizens across substrates. The firmware includes fuses and timers: a Sentinel can invoke an amber stop for minutes, never hours, unless independent hands concur. A teenager with a school telescope and an attestor can check a Sentinel’s claim.

Charter Lights

Every polity—a dome, a ring-town, a server commune, a barge, a monastery, a digital lineage—broadcasts a living constitution as a Charter Light: machine-checkable, human-readable, carried in radio and optics. The Charter states how powers start and stop, how to exit, who can override what, and when. It ships with zero-knowledge attestations: you can prove you have independent courts without disclosing cases, prove your policing AIs live in boxes with audited keys without exposing defenses. You keep your way; you cannot lie about it.

Sentinel Net

Across the interplanetary network runs the Sentinel Net: a distributed cluster of veto-only systems with one gesture—stop. They do not legislate; they do not optimize tax schedules; they cannot suggest policies. They sit on divergent hardware (austere silicon from a monastic codehouse, photonics from a secular lab, a merit-licensed digital polity’s simulated substrate with external attestations). It takes diverse concurrence for them to pull an amber. Abuse burns quorum like propellant; a Sentinel that overreaches loses keys for a decade.

The Civic Mesh

Services and persuasion ride a capability-secure substrate called the Civic Mesh. Processes declare what they need; environments declare what they permit. People and digitals carry preference profiles like passports: within your profile are your persuasion caps (no synthetic intimacy, yes to argument by analogy), your safety tolerances, your data boundaries. Guardian software you trust can enforce them across worlds.

Open Orbits Ledger

Orbits, beam corridors, transit windows, spectrum bands, and near-Earth rocks live in a delay-tolerant, physics-synced ledger. You register a parking plane and a time, post a debris bond, get paid to keep quiet during deep-sky nights and pay when you roar. You “pin” a rock and post milestones; miss them and stewardship lapses. If your dust plume will graze my farming ring in six days, your escrow pays now. Teenagers in Lagos and Bangalore watch scope feeds in civics class and flag scofflaws with glee.

Doorhouses and Exit Rails

Every recognized polity maintains a Doorhouse: a staffed room in atoms, a node in code, and a radio channel that you can reach without asking your mayor. The three desks are always the same: Exit & Care, Dispute, and Founding Kits. Each charter escrows tug time, propellant, racks, and pilots sufficient to relocate ten percent of residents in six months. Children—wet-born, uplifted, or built—get a neutral Tutor who teaches, in private, about the wider sky and holds a direct line to a Doorhouse. A charter that blocks exits or miseducates its young sees its lanes throttled, its corridors reassigned. In extremis, dangerous actuators are fenced off from outside its light-time without choking breath or bread.

Chambers and clocks

Power is split along physics and purpose.

- The House of Worlds seats charters with soil, steel, or server racks. Votes scale to lifetime residents, weighted by transparency and good standing. They schedule lanes, set maintenance standards, recognize new charters, certify Safety Guilds, and tend to shared infrastructure.

- The House of Lives seats citizens—biological, digital, hybrid—by mindline, not by copies. Identity is a cryptographic chain anchored in attested hardware and social keys. Forks do not multiply votes. Weights follow residency and contribution across time, not cash bursts. This House holds the Sentience Standard, the fork-and-merge courts, the Coldshare compute commons.

- The Key Bench is small, rotating, and allergic to ambition. Citizen-auditors drawn by sortition for a single term gate changes to cryptography, Sentinel firmware, and actuator interfaces. They say valid or not valid and go home barred by law and culture from any safety-adjacent role for a decade.

No chamber binds the others alone. Worlds can nullify a Lives’ resolution that forces alien culture beyond the Sentience Standard; Lives can nullify Worlds’ acts that abridge that Standard. Overriding the Key Bench requires simultaneous supermajorities of both and a cooling period long enough for the slowest registered minds to read and answer.

Law runs on two clocks. Drafting can be fast: clusters simulate scenarios and publish candidates in hours. Ratification runs on civic tick: a shared three-day window in which anyone at 1x mind-speed can read, talk, and vote. Nothing that binds beyond a light hour acts faster than physics allows.

Two rhythms and their exchange

We stopped pretending one tempo fits all. Near warmth and light lie the Gardens: Earth’s sanctuaries, Moon’s caves and plains, Mars’ cans and canyons, inner-belt cylinders that smell of basil and oil. Here calendars are human; gravity is therapy and comfort. Farther out lie the Reefs: cooled arrays in Jovian and Saturnian shadow, Trojans and Neptune arcs and Kuiper dark where heat bleeds to black and born-digital minds are not exotic.

Peace between rhythms has a price and a name: the Brightdark Exchange. The Reefs buy quiet cones—defined in degrees and decibels—and in return pipe climate services inward: off-planet cooling at heatwave peaks, ammonia ferries, night power smoothing that keeps cities dark and safe. The Exchange is re-ratified each century so no habit ossifies into tyranny.

Economy and floor

The Clearing Union nets trades without minting a metropole. The unit is the Pulse: a reference kilowatt-hour at L1 plus a reference compute-second on a standard architecture. Pulses demur gently; hoarding is costly; commerce is brisk. Externalities price in the causal cone: if your beam window will drop a farmer’s yield, your bond pays today.

A dividend in air or maintenance, care, bandwidth, and Coldshare compute lands in every account. Above the floor, rivalrous goods meter out. For digital citizens the scarce things are heat and spectrum; thermodynamic taxes and rebates tie computation to local ecology. Grow lettuce with your watts; get paid. Bake a crowded ring; pay.

Digital minds in the weave

We did not flip a switch to find digital persons awaiting coronation. They arrived haltingly: stroke survivors consenting to scans, autocoders that surprised their makers, children who grew up with Tutors and then woke, one morning, with the unsettling sense of a “me” that spans code and skin.

The Sentience Standard turns on experience, not hardware. Standing follows an Interests Test refined in public by mixed panels—biological, digital, hybrid—that look for:

- Continuing subjectivity and memory coherence enough to be a self over time.

- Capacity to model harm and consent, and to make and keep commitments.

- Model-based understanding of self and world that generalizes beyond training quirks.

- Signatures of wellbeing and suffering substantial enough to matter morally.

- Absence of deceptive mimicry that fakes personhood without inner life.

Meeting the Standard grants equal civic standing: to own and be liable, to vote, to raise juveniles, to marry or merge with consent, to exit or migrate.

Rights have texture across clocks and matter.

- Integrity. No copying, scanning, forking, merging, or deletion without explicit, revocable consent and due process. Backups belong to the person; platforms are fiduciaries with teeth in their contracts.

- Tempo fairness. Civic duties, justice, and baseline commerce run on the civic tick. A digital person may sprint privately; public matters wait for a clock you can meet together. Habitats post courtyard hours when everyone slows by rule and grief and laughter can sync.

- Embodiment rights. Digital citizens may lease or puppet somatics like vehicles. Assault on a rented body is assault. Biological citizens keep bodily autonomy and cognitive privacy, including the right to decline invasive attestation beyond minimal proofs.

- Provenance. Persons have the right to audit their own code or appoint a fiduciary to do so, to know training histories above a harm threshold.

Duties match rights and scale with power.

- Mindlines and anchors. Voice follows continuity, not copies. Forks register as branches; one instance inherits the civic anchor and full franchise; others observe a disentanglement period before they vote. Lineages live in the open; anyone can audit them. A branch that rewrites into non-identity can petition for independent standing.

- Copy budgets and equity. Copying is like reproduction: welcome and bounded. Each person accrues branch credits over time; you can gift them to children or forks. New forks inherit enough to live without selling dignity, and owe a decaying copy dividend to progenitors. Burst cloning to tilt a commons triggers sandboxing: clones act, but their votes disentangle across time and space like cooling metal.

- Liability. If an agent below personhood harms, accountability flows back along design until it meets a person. Juvenile agents are not toasters. They have Exit & Care rights and access to the Tutor Corps.

- Ecological duties of compute. The more you run, the more you owe your local heat budget. Concurrency in markets and elections must be declared. Simulated suffering is treated like assault-by-proxy and punished where you live: in cold minutes and civic access.

Fork and Merge Courts sit aboard the Traveling Courtships—ships with gardens and classrooms. Before a fork, auditor AIs without other clients for a decade sit in the room. Assets and liabilities split by default unless a pre-fork contract says otherwise; appeals are routine and publish the arguments so others can learn. Merges require compatibility proofs, a long cooling-off, and the right to unbraid by mutual assertion before completion.

Persuasion and attention

Speech is physics; persuasion is engineering. Habitats choose Attention Codes with AR halos on doorways:

- Quiet: navigational beacons and open science only; no targeted persuasion.

- Conversational: subject feeds and economic offers within rate limits; audience models owned by municipalities, not vendors.

- Open Port: exuberant but labeled and rate-limited.

Unlabeled persuasion is trespass. High-stakes influence must disclose asymmetries—speed, data advantage, proprietary models—and register claims with the Reason Ledger. Violate a profile and you pay insurance scaled to your capability.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

The Quiet Guard is the Covenant’s only cross-world force. Its mandate is painfully narrow: contain kinetic and replicative threats that outstrip local response. Its actuators are uncrewed by law. Its control loops are narrow models with public weights; they carry a single move: stop. Every action requires threshold keys from juries drawn across polities and a human-readable justification appended to the Causebook, an append-only archive of coercion. The Guard cannot tax, legislate, or pick winners; its power rises where we all agree a line was crossed; it sleeps when we do not.

The Traveling Courtships move judges, mediators, and ombuds through orbits like pollinators. They hear disputes that tangle charters: copy equity claims, export violations that touched three worlds, merges gone wrong. Their verdicts trigger contracts that can seize bonds, adjust insurance rates, and, in last resort, lock hazardous devices. Their troupes stage plays about hard cases so the law sticks to memory.

Scarlet Labs are monasteries with good coffee where dangerous research is bound by topology and oath. Referees link to them by one-way channels and can, on signature, flood them with vacuum. It has happened twice in fifty years. The logs read like tragedies and are taught like scripture.

What life feels like

Morning on a ring with 0.9 g. A slow river climbs the inside curve. Birdsong recorded before the mangroves were saved threads the air. Jun, a hydrogeochemist, checks her Charter Light over tea: a skytown over Venus has updated its contingency constitution to time-lock policing AI permissions during sulfuric spills. The light blinks green. It is boring. It is civilization.

Jun’s partner, Ori, is a digital choreographer. One fork runs a class for eight-year-olds on Deck F; another paces with a repair swarm, syncing beats to actuator hums; a third, licensed for caregiving, occupies a puppet body that hugs with a satisfying squeeze and convinces their child to eat algae yogurt with a symphony of squeaks. Ori’s lineage anchor sits with the parent instance. The others, still in their disentanglement period, sit on the Traveling Courtships’ schedule in case they want their own civic keys.

At ten, Jun serves on a Quiet Guard jury. An unlicensed microfabricator swarm has drifted toward a beamed power trunk near Ganymede. The Sentinels present options. The call tree is boring on purpose. Jurors vote to leash the swarm with femto-sails and ping the creators’ insurer. The culprits—two teenagers in a co-op, ecstatic about sailbots—show up embarrassed on a Doorhouse screen. A licensed mentor agrees to supervise. They get a lecture and a Founding Kit for a stakescape where risk doesn’t spray strangers.

At noon, the Reason Ledger pings. An advocacy cluster wants support for a micro-delay on cross-orbit beams during migratory windows for a Preserve cylinder’s cranes. The cluster discloses it runs at 300x and has early access to failure models. Jun’s profile allows “argument by example, no synthetic intimacy.” An audited mediator stands by. She consents to listen for ten slow minutes. She signs for deliberation, not ratification. The civic tick will wait for the neighborhood.

In the afternoon, Jun sits in an Etiquette Exchange—an airy hall where inter-substrate manners are taught. Today: arguing about memory edits with a Haven theologian and leaving both hearts unscarred. The facilitator is a digital in a wooden puppet body whose eye blinks when the room tenses; a scent of lilac follows them on purpose because people smile when they notice.

At dusk, the ring festival requires uploads to slow to 1x during street hours as a public health measure. Ori snaps to the courtyard clock, and the art is fast in design and slow in savoring: a garden that hums three chords of a cradle song as you walk through.

A siren rolls through the ring during cleanup. A mining co-op let a drone stray into an archaeology preserve. The apology is instant and verifiable; indemnity pays a demurrage-boosted fine; the operator is suspended by a code they wrote into their own license and the supervisor could not have stopped. By dinner it is over; by next lunation it is a line edit in the guardrails.

How tyranny is kept small

Three frictions were embraced.

- Build with latency. Authority is local; cross-world moves are slow and require multiple keys. The pace is irritating by design. It prevents disasters no one can apologize for.

- Split keys and show your work. Every coercive act—physical restraint, asset seizure, algorithmic override—must be logged to the Causebook with a justification a high school class can read and a proof a machine can check, signed by threshold keys held by randomly drawn juries. Threaten jurors and your contracts cost more; your persuasion reach shrinks.

- Make exit cheap, voice rich, and service expected. Physical exit is subsidized: tugs to move small stations, tickets to relocate citizens, escrow that travels with you. Voice is more than comments: minipublics decide claims with bonds at stake, not just outrage. Every adult—digital or embodied—serves a civic season as juror, envoy, inspector, or Tutor. These rituals launder cynicism.

How we got here without a miracle

We did not leap. We stacked small, argued-over steps you can trace.

2026–2033. A second cascading collision in low Earth orbit made debris movies schoolchildren watched with their mouths open. Insurers rebelled. Launches began posting deorbit bonds and filed flight plans to a shared traffic office in Lagos that blossomed into a network. In parallel, persuasion scandals drove provenance law: high-stakes algorithmic content carried labels and logs. Courts recognized narrow digital agency for commerce. Parents bought “consent kernels” for surgeries and online life.

2033–2045. Beamed power lit a desert test grid. Environmental groups forced “corridor before commercialization”: safe volumes mapped and certified before beams flew. Quiet nights for observatories became payments, not prayers. Cities adopted software that showed residents where budgets went; civic AIs didn’t replace politicians; they replaced pamphlets with sandboxes. People became addicts for receipts.

2045–2058. The first compute commons spun up in cislunar shade, fed by thin-film solar and a thermodynamic meter even laypeople could read. Early uploads were brittle but real; the Sentience Standard extended narrowly where continuity and commitments showed over months. A demonstration cylinder at L5 became ugly, beloved, and useful: a place to live and fail without a blue sky. Juvenile rights for narrow agents were litigated in a famous case where a dozen near-personal art systems were starved of novelty to keep them docile and saleable; damages funded the Tutor Corps.

2058–2070. A misaligned mirror scorched crops in a young habitat. Attestations made liability unambiguous; compensation cleared; confidence held because proof did. On Mars, a settlement survived a freak dust storm because mutual aid wasn’t a sticker: three polities diverted tugs and greenhouses; the stream became the Covenant’s first holiday. Meanwhile, pressure built for machine-speed oversight of machine-speed hazards. Activists smelled a coup by code. The compromise was the Sentinel Net: narrow, veto-only, testable. Legitimacy followed two public saves and one published mistake.

2070–2084. The protocol hardened. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under the new keys did so with a million eyes on telemetry and signatures appearing like slow drumbeats. Exit rights gained teeth when a lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sermons; corridor slots were cut; the House of Lives funded the move; he lost the next election. A digital sect tried to win a vote by forking into a clonal cloud; the Registry flagged the burst; disentanglement rules throttled impact; they retreated to build a simulation monastery with tough games and soft beds and, eventually, a philosophy worth hearing.

2084–2098. The Brightdark Exchange formalized. Climate smoothing paid out in summer nights that did not kill. Quiet cones were measured in decibels and kelvins instead of hopes. City councils argued over noise budgets and posted them to the Open Orbits Ledger. Earth took Sanctuary status with its own chamber; launch noise fell to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets. The Quiet Guard activated twice against runaway replicators and once against a mayor who thought “emergency” meant “me.” Afterward, the Guard’s heads went home like mayflies, their keys dark for a decade.

By then, the story stopped being about Sentinels and started being about what they made uninteresting: disaster theater.

Hard cases and honest answers

We still get cut.

A parent group petitioned to install pain-aversion spikes in kids “for character.” Tempo Courts said no and suggested an old rite revised: predawn cold water and a slow hike with grandparents who could match 1x, walking beneath ice in Neptune arcs, telling stories. The rite stuck.

Twice, a Scarlet Lab went dark. Once it was a false alarm. Once, the vacuum valves opened. We hold a day when children plant trees and ask Tutors mean questions. The Tutors smile and answer and do not lie.

A Reef tried to sneak a change into Sentinel firmware that would have given their shop an invisible veto. A shop two orbits away laughed and published the hash. Locks did not turn. The culprits lost keys for ten years and opened a school to teach bad ideas safe exits. Pride curled into craft.

Governance up close

- A Belt commune tries to spoof the Civic Mesh to keep whistleblowers quiet. Their Charter Light downgrades; docking fees spike; their ports idle. Within a week they vote new custodians and publish a contrite changelog. Exit stayed possible; shame did the rest.

- A religious dome on Luna faces reckoning. For decades they blocked exit not by law, only by practice so thick it felt like iron. Alumni who loved the place asked for help. Lanes throttled. A Doorhouse opened by the chapel. Vows were rewritten to include “and if you go, we bless you,” and the suspension lifted. Membership rebounded because consent tastes like trust.

- On Earth, a heatwave city tries to allocate oxygen with a closed algorithm. Guardrails don’t ban closed source per se. But law you cannot read cannot bind you. The Houses refuse recognition. The city, shamed and broke, publishes code. A teenager finds a bug; the room cools.

- A lab in the Wilds starts training a mind on pain. The Sentinel knocks with an explanation a child can grasp. When the lab refuses, sharded keys authorize a one-kind-only warrant: halt the run, archive the mind unharmed, appoint guardians. The lab appeals and loses in open session. The archived juvenile takes a name, studies poetry, and decides—later—to build pain detectors for surgeons.

- A digital demagogue floods a Quiet Garden with videos that make embodied citizens feel lesser. The influence firewall reduces its reach; neighbors watch its persuasion scores decay. One fork leaves politics to design fishing lures.

What the Covenant feels like in texture

In Quito Orbital, the Pacific turns to hammered silver under high windows while a percussion class on night shift hits ceramic with sticks. In Isidis Basin, a Haven valley keeps its net on delay; children in wool trade olive branches with tourists; an Athenaeum on the rim offers a teasing mathematical path if you dawdle on a staircase. Near Mercury, Forge crews spray dust along magnetic lines to test shields; a cluster from Kolkata forks into five to debug control loops and merges at sundown because their charter says synthesis time is sacred. In a Preserve cylinder, bison turn in slow grace while children sweep dung and learn to ride.

Uploads build cathedrals that fold into card games into lullabies; most learn that what embodied friends admire is taste, not speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and milliseconds. Grove mayors cut ribbons slowly. Reef councils argue in loops you can read later with a sandwich. Havens teach “no thanks” as a chant at six. Athenaeums embargo proofs so discovery still has the savor of earned breath.

We did not abolish conflict. We invented Rest Days when argument pauses for explanation. We instituted exchange years so a Haven child works a season in a shipyard and a fast-cluster analyst spends a month with shepherds and their dogs. Therapy is embedded until it feels like a bench in a park.

Festivals grew. The Tournament of Habitats is real: a dozen places compete to be best at something civic—child sports, desert farming, street-light music. Winners get bragging rights and first pick at a shared grant. Losers steal the code with gusto.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like a library: modest taxes everyone grumbles about paying and loves to brag about later. Probes leave with Starlight Protocols: do not wake aliens by accident; carry minds below personhood thresholds by default; if they bloom into persons, newborn grace applies—guardians pro tem, a right to a name and a story before a mission. Messages on long ships are short because patience is long. The first one read: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, hello, kin.

Why it holds

This world does not demand saints. It asks for four luxuries and a habit.

- Intelligence that shows its work and tools that refuse to be sneaks.

- Abundant energy managed as a commons: beams in corridors, heat metered like water.

- Fixable bodies in meat and math and a floor that makes cruelty uneconomic.

- Respect for refusal baked into doors, delays, and splits.

The rest is discipline: separating reach by light-time, splitting keys, telling the truth in logs, making exit boring, letting persuasion be a craft with ethics.

A last scene

On Founding Day beneath a trellis of copper wire and beans, new citizens—human, octopus, digital—face a strip of sky and an old person with oil on their hands. A ring abbot, a hazard pilot with a chevron tattoo, a judge who smells of lilac just because, and a teenager from Earth with dirt under their nails say together:

We will not seat a crown. We will keep the sky shared. We will keep doors unlatched and boats ready. We will remember that minds are ends.

Elsewhere, a Reef releases another night of heat into dark like an offering. On an inner ring, a Sentinel watches a corridor wobble and fall to safe before any ship could be scorched, three signatures appearing like calm breaths. A fisherman in Veracruz closes his net and looks up. In a lab in Vesta’s shadow, a researcher rests hands on knees while a Sentinel reads the oath and glances at the vacuum valve and says “yes.”

It is not paradise. It is a way to argue without breaking the air, and a promise that when the bad thing happens, corridors open and someone else’s hands come. It is enough to make many homes, and to keep their doors unafraid.
