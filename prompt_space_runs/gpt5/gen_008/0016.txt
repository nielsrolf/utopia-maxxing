The Weave Covenant

At dawn the cylinder’s windows are still a hush of polished black. Radiator groves quiver green along the rim, piping heat toward the stars. In your vestibule three small lamps above the lock glow a patient blue:

- Exit: you can go, and your berth, your water, your data, and a seat on a tug go with you.

- Echo: you can branch or reunite without a clerk turning “you” into a technicality.

- Span: ships, beams, and packets cross under rules that are dull to obey and deafening when bent.

They’re not slogans. They’re circuits made into customs. Together with a few strict machines and the habit of posting receipts in public, they make the Weave Covenant—an agreement strong enough to hold worlds apart without welding them into one thing.

It exists because light-time rules, heat costs pain, and minds now live in many tempos. It holds because force must snitch on itself, exits are logistics not lectures, and anyone can check whether promises kept the beat.

Three bright rails

The Weave rides on three bright rails that every charter—a habitat, ship-state, monastery-ranch, reef of servers—mounts at its door.

- No unguided harm. You do not hurl dumb mass at neighbors. You do not fog a region’s spectrum without a lane. You do not loose replicators without beacons, brakes, and bonds. You do not train or deploy coercive models past posted thresholds without escrow and outside eyes.

- The sky is a commons with prices tied to physics. Orbits, beam corridors, mirrors’ light, and quiet cones round telescopes and thought-gardens are leased and posted so nobody’s dawn is theft.

- Depart with help. Every being on the road to personhood can leave a charter with stewards, storage, compute, and a berth. Exit is a funded schedule, not a sermon.

Rails without machines are wishes. The Weave’s machines are small and rude to ambition.

Sentinel locks and the amber hold

Sentinels are ugly on purpose: yellow boxes with big fuses and glass you break. They live in triplicate wherever dumb becomes dangerous—air plants, mass drivers, beam actuators, replication stacks, city valves. They do one thing: brake.

Anyone with a civic key can pull an Amber Hold for six minutes if a simple, public rule is heard wrong: a lane violation, a quiet-cone trespass, a replicator without a tag. During the hold, safety modes snap to narrow bands and a loud, signed “Indigo Halt” blasts the cause into the Action Ledger: which machine, which rule, which clock.

To keep the brake pressed, the Sentinel must gather three concurrences on different tempos and hardware: a panel of locals drawn by lot from citizens who logged civic hours this year; a remote cluster hosted on hardware the locals do not control; and a digital citizen who did not apply for the job. Keys expire in minutes. Logs are posted in clear steps. There is a “cringe” annex where sloppy overreaches earn jokes that ruin careers faster than trials.

Every overdrive, every pause, every hard hand lands in that record: which hands, which law, how long. Insurance moves with it: premiums up when patterns smell bad, down when receipts stack neat.

We braid authority where light is slow

The Weave arranges power like rivers that split and rejoin, not steps in a pyramid. At its center is the Constellation Council with two chambers and a brake.

- The Chamber of Orbits seats worlds with hulls and polities with servers in rough proportion to lifetime residents. It speaks for lanes and infrastructure: beamed power schedules, debris bonds, docking and maintenance standards, rescue covenants.

- The Chamber of Minds seats citizens in proportion to registered beings with singular franchises across substrates. Its scope is the status and safety of minds: personhood tests, minimum compute floors, fork-and-merge rules, attention zoning, and civic tempos that prevent fast rooms from binding slow lives by surprise.

- The Kernel Bench is a rotating board of engineers, jurists, and process auditors who stamp “valid” or “not” on changes to cryptographic backbones, Sentinel firmware, and force-bearing interfaces. They cannot legislate. You cannot serve twice within a long memory. Their meetings are public or publish their logs.

Neither chamber can yank on the other without process. If Orbits tries to rewrite beam corridors in ways that upend a culture, Minds can send it back with a floor violation. If Minds writes personhood so tight or so fast that neighbors cannot read the stars in peace, Orbits can say: not until quiet cones and lifeboats are secured. Anything that touches Sentinels needs supermajorities on both sides and a cooling window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to speak.

The Skybook you can check with a school telescope

The Skybook is an open, delay-tolerant, physics-synchronized ledger of orbits, beams, shadows, spectrum, debris, and compute leases. You can read it with a kitchen verifier. You can compare its models with a child’s telescope and a laser pointer if you’re patient.

- Power beams and mirror arrays broadcast signed tags with ephemeral keys tied to hardware that school clubs can audit. If your beam crosses a farmer’s dawn, you post a shade bond. If you squat in a crowded plane, you lease lane-time, post cleanup deposits, and pay congestion fees. If you pin an asteroid and claim stewardship, you show surveys, beacons, cleaning orbits. Idle too long, it lapses.

- Quiet cones sit over telescopes and “groves”—server forests whose residents buy quiet by law. Cones are public in degrees and decibels, with bonds when you pierce them.

- The Commons Floor is funded by small levies from lanes, orbits, corridor auctions, and captured debris deposits. It is boring by ambition: baseline air and water, maintenance in habitats, basic care, local transport, and a floor of compute and bandwidth (“cold minutes” in the slang) you cannot lose to debt. Everyone gets one unchallenged exit grant in any rolling seven years.

Exit is scheduled, not begged

Every charter posts Lifeboat Receipts: tugs, propellant, berths, storage, server time on hardware no one local can throttle, and certified stewards to move at least ten percent of residents—wetware and code—within six months. The receipts are recalculated monthly; the Action Ledger cross-checks.

Embassies are literal rooms: a cobalt door marked in paint visible in suitlight. Inside are three desks and chairs that fit elders and octopuses and drones: Departure, where you book the logistics of leaving; Mediation, where a co-op and an algorithm can argue with tea; and Founders, where you borrow sample charters and run your constitution in a simulation box with validators that won’t flatter.

Charters that fence exits with fees or lies don’t get essays. Their beams lose corridors. Servers idle their processes above the floor. Swift craft—small, patient, uncrewable rescue boats with brains dumb enough to trust—arrive with cutters and bolt-spreaders if someone tries to make steel into law.

Digital citizens are neighbors, not mascots

The Weave’s Personhood Standard is substrate-neutral and plain: continuity you can demonstrate without voyeurism; reciprocity you can show; consent competence you can exercise.

- Continuity: you can maintain an autobiographical strand through pauses and moves; you can object to alteration and prove what you were.

- Reciprocity: you can model other minds about as richly as you demand they model you.

- Consent: you can read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

Pass, and your rights match your neighbors’.

Rights with texture:

- Thinking floor. You cannot be throttled below the Public Kernel—the minimum compute and bandwidth to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process that lives on a clock you can see.

- Privacy of the inside. Courts can compel proofs; they cannot demand raw internals. Backups belong to you. No one forks, scans, merges, or deletes you without explicit, revocable consent.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs on a Common Beat by default—three days for motions, longer for slow law. Anyone can invoke a Slowroom with turn-taking, rate-limits, and interpreters for math, metaphor, smell.

- Body and substrate sovereignty. You may rent, build, or inhabit bodies, puppets, drones, or remote hands under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented body is still assault. You need not cross substrates.

- Provenance of persuasion. Influence with the intent to change votes or purchases must carry who paid, what data fed it, and what levers it tweaks. You can bind a Consent Capsule to your identity: humor yes, grief no; analogy fine; limit me at fifteen minutes.

Duties that mirror rights:

- Thermo tax. Compute heats neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling cost. Heat that warms greenhouses is credit. Heat that bakes corridors is a fine.

- Concurrency declaration. In regulated markets and elections, you declare your speed and your active instance count. Hiding speed voids deals. Votes bind to singular franchises whether you run once or a hundred times.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed self-replicators outside audited boxes. Tools broadcast beacons. When Sentinels call a universal brake, you accept it.

- Simulation welfare. You do not simulate suffering without consent. Reconstructing a real person beyond a fidelity threshold requires licenses or their heirs’ okay.

Branches, reunions, and strands

Copies are not photocopies. They’re kin. Your public identity follows your Strand across forks and pauses. A Strand holds one full franchise. If you branch, you file a Branch Notice with a hashed snapshot, declared purpose, compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if reunion is planned. One branch retains the civic anchor immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy.

Reunion is a hearing with judges who are also counselors and engineers. There is tea. Memory graphs run through cryptographic interpreters that verify belief equivalence without spilling secrets. If subjective tempos differ, clocks align gently. Either branch can walk away. Unbraiding remains an option until the end. Juvenile agents—training shards, kids, narrow systems that have started to ask for names—get Mentors and exits. The Mentor Corps is substrate-diverse and dull by design: teachers who know boredom and the kindness of “no.”

Attention has neighborhoods

Places choose an attention flag with legal teeth, not vibes.

- Harbors allow only navigation beacons, open science, and packets by request. No targeted persuasion. No high-speed models unless licensed like vehicles. Children can walk here with bare minds and feel safe.

- Parley Streets allow topic-bounded feeds and ordinary offers under public rate-limits. Audience models are municipal cooperatives, opt-in only. Ads behave like performers with rules.

- Carnivals invite the roar: slang, mashups, persuasion leagues under registry. They’re where you go to be dazzled or numb on purpose.

Doors carry small brass tags that read: “Ask before you cast.” An Arguments Register keeps an Open Reasons shelf where persuasion that hopes to change law files its homework—plain claims, models under escrow, error bars. Teenagers get stipends for catching cheats.

An economy measured in sun, cold, and time

At base, the Weave’s economy counts what fights: mass under delta-v, watts, spectrum, cubic meters with pressure, and coolth. Above that, it counts compute-seconds, skilled hours, and reputation that buys lanes and seconds. Contracts clear in units pegged to two references anyone can test: a kilowatt-hour measured at the Earth–Sun Lagrange and a compute-second on a posted, schoolroom reference design. Your wrist does the conversions; your grandmother can check them with a pamphlet and a scope.

- Beam corridors post schedules and predictive models to the Skybook. They fly flags in space. Crossing a quiet cone requires escrow. Stray, and strict liability bites. Compensation arrives in hours because proof is boring.

- Orbits are lanes, not lots. Congestion is priced. Debris deposits fund cleanup. Bounties go to clubs in Recife and Lucknow and Ulaanbaatar who spot cheats with cheap scopes.

- Stewardship replaces flag-planting. You show your work in public. You lose claims when you go idle.

- The Sun-and-Cold Exchange runs on a calendar. Out-system reefs sell cold and compute smoothing; in-system gardens buy quiet cones and human-pace windows. Every century, a mixed jury recalculates how many cold minutes fit in a quiet day.

Sharp edges live in safe rooms

Curiosity is a good reason to risk yourself; your neighbor is not. Risky research lives in Crimson Bays—labs whose walls are built from lists of failure modes. Bays run one-way proofs to Sentinels that convince safety claims without leaking secrets. Under a tight list of public signals, Sentinels can dump nitrogen or starve a Bay of power in under a second. Everyone who works in a Bay knows the kill-valve oath by heart. It has been pulled twice. One was a false bell that taught us to write better sensors. One was a save you can still smell in bread and ink every year.

Only one kind of ship crosses without an invitation: Swift Fleet—stubby, uncrewed boats with seatbelt-simple brains that tilt at unscheduled kinetic shots and unchaperoned replicators. Their budgets sunset. Their code is in books. When a Swift docks home, schoolkids crawl inside with oranges and questions.

How we keep sticky power from sticking

Abuse is expensive. Dissent is cheap.

- Split hands. Any act of force requires three keys: a local jury by lot, a digital citizen drafted by chance, and a remote Sentinel cluster on hardware the locals don’t own. Keys expire quickly. Logs seep slowly so redactions can be reviewed.

- Live within latency. Duties beyond your hull move on slow beats under mutual consent unless physics screams. Emergencies have paint on their fuses. They end on clocks you can see.

- Inspectable safety. Life-critical systems must prove safe state continuously, legibly enough that students can learn and adversaries can’t spoof.

- Sanctions where it hurts. Jam exits or cheat receipts and you lose beam windows, cold minutes, spectrum, and tug-time. Your shipments get late. Your fans get bored.

- Default fission. Founders get to hold thick law only for a while. Then charters split by district or craft. Port authorities reserve exit capacity like lifeboats by code.

- Jokes. Comedians read the Action Ledger. Tyrants trip on furniture they didn’t see because it was labeled boring.

A day with the Weave

On Tuesday, Shirin’s shop on Rim-Market Two opens to a line of miners, kids, and drones whose pilots like the smell of sesame. At nine, the Arguments Register pings her Counsel: a proposal wants a 40-millisecond delay added to beams crossing inhabited stations to damp feedback cascades in edge cases. Provenance is clear: three diagrams, two analogies, humor allowed low-dose, no grief levers. Shirin presses “sleep it a day,” thinks on lunch, votes tomorrow.

At ten, a Swift jury forms on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at Hecuba Crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones to redirect. Jurors: a monk from a slow charter in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Quito, an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when he talks. They pick dusting, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. Camera shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon in a Harbor Ward, a digital poet in a wooden puppet sips broth with their human spouse. The poet’s main self runs 200× near Neptune, restoring an archive from a drowned delta. Today they throttle down to share soup. After, they spend twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a cloister gardener explains why their charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it, then politely declines to speed up “just to keep up with questions.”

At dusk, the market’s canopy lights with stars. A projector flips to the Action Ledger’s satire annex. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we’ll pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy palms a brass Swift and asks if he can sign up. Shirin says: when you’re fifteen—and if you can explain an Amber Hold without notes.

Hard cases that bent us, not broke us

A rim charter on Mars voted to deny uploads personhood. The Weave did not flood them with code. Exit was real. Digital citizens left. That flood year the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a puppet coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause. The mentor from that season still does tours.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival’s dome in a wage fight. A Swift turned it into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The co-op’s insurer cratered. Directors learned that coercion budgets are not suggestions. They apologized and fielded a hockey team.

A demagogue carpeted a Bazaar with videos tuned to induce smallness and fatigue. Consent Capsules throttled reach. Neighbors watched persuasion scores decay like frost in sun. One of the demagogue’s branches left to design fishing lures. They won the prize for harmlessness.

Twice a Crimson Bay went to zero. Once was a false siren. Once was a silence that saved a city. Children plant saplings and ask hard questions. Mentors answer without magic.

Why this holds

It holds because safety you can walk through with a fifth grader is stricter than speeches. Because coercion makes noise in ledgers comedians read. Because leaving is a desk with a clerk, tugs on a schedule, a berth that fits your aunt, and your records encrypted for you. Because the floor tax that funds air and care comes from sharing sunlight, spectrum, and lanes, not a prince’s favor. Because fast systems cannot bind slow lives without windows. Because minds made of code are treated as neighbors with heat bills, juries, and jokes.

Roads from here to there

It wasn’t a miracle. It was a pile of dull steps that got copied because they worked.

2026–2033. A cascade of debris collisions kept kids inside and made insurers sour. Launches had to publish ephemerides; rendezvous corridors went from whispers to posted schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare turned “open firmware or no license” into law for high-risk infrastructure. Early AI persuasion scandals made provenance compulsory: if a model tried to change your mind, it had to carry its pedigree. Courts began granting narrow agency to digital systems in contracts when continuity could be shown.

2033–2044. Beamed power climbed out of labs. Environmental groups held out for “corridors before commercialization”: post safe lanes first, then money. A demo array scorched cropland; the supplier’s logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared fast because proof did. Cities and ports adopted coercion ledgers—drab at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2044–2057. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a “thermo meter” you could read. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of cold minutes and a Counsel—a simple machine lawyer that would not act without an explicit yes and could be turned off. Early uploads were brittle, but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and commitments held up over seasons. A demonstration cylinder at L5 was ugly and everyone’s favorite field trip.

2057–2069. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver test whistled near a habitat, “amber holds” became law: narrow six-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. That compromise hardened into Sentinels. Insurers made them standard. Two public saves made the argument end.

2069–2081. The Constellation Council replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Swifts flew, nipped a wayward slug into glitter, and came home to be opened by children. Personhood law was written in language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. The Common Beat for civic time became normal. Attention zoning—Harbors, Parley Streets, Carnivals—grew out of municipal quiet hours that saved elections and sleep.

2081–2092. The first attempt to swing a vote with a clone cloud met singular franchises and Junction Courts and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sorrow; lanes went gray, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2092–2104. The Sun-and-Cold Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold to gardens; gardens bought quiet cones and human-pace windows. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with cold minutes to build community archives. Crimson Bays got their kill valves and their oaths.

2104–2116. The Kernel Bench refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The satire annex of the Action Ledger matured teeth. A rescue to unbolt an Embassy ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup. The Weave stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Scenes from a civilization that learned to share a sky

In Isidis Basin, bells mark hours in a cloister whose law caps cognition on purpose. Across a waveguide, a hazard arena runs contests under rules that require three interviews, a week of cool-down, and a big “I understand” before anyone plays. In Quito Orbital, the Pacific looks hammered flat while a percussion class keeps an elevator ribbon honest. In a bison cylinder named Wildframe, children sweep dung and debug forage algorithms in the same lesson. Near Mercury, two shade-sail teams drift dust along magnetic lines; in Chennai, a collective forks into five to crack a proof, then reunites at sundown because synthesis deserves slow humans in it.

Near Neptune, a judge who wears a lilac note because it helps frightened people breathe volunteers at Junction Court in the morning and spends their cold minutes at night piecing together a delta’s drowned library from auntie stories and smudged ledgers. The co-op roofs a school with tin and sends back songs.

Founding Day looks the same everywhere: new citizens—human, cephalopod, digital—stand under trellises of copper wire and beans. A pilot with a burn-scar wrist, a code auditor with calluses from spanners, a poet in a wooden puppet, and a teenager with dirt under their nails speak together.

No unguided harm.

The sky is a commons.

Depart with help.

An elevator hums without stealing fire. A reef exhales its heat like a sigh into dark. A fisher in Veracruz looks up at glitter that was once a stupid slug. On a bridge at L4, a student asks whether a market rule will make milk stupid. The counselor laughs and brings up a chart. In a lab shadowed by Vesta, a researcher rests steady hands on their knees while a Sentinel reads the oath and, for a moment, looks like crying, then says yes.

This is not paradise. It is something sturdier: a pattern of arguments that do not puncture the hull, a way to add worlds and minds without stripping the old ones, a promise that dissent will be cheaper than flattery and that you can always find the cobalt door. It runs on receipts, jokes, glass with paint on it, and the obstinate idea that any being who can feel and keep a promise is a neighbor.

On your way out, the three blue lamps blink once, like a reminder for children crossing a street.

Exit. Echo. Span.

We do not know a better spell. It is enough.
