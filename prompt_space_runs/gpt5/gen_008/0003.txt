The Common Skies Compact

When you step off the shuttle at Hyale Ring, the air under the saffron lights smells like coriander and machine oil. Along the bulkhead, at shoulder height, runs a band of walnut inlaid with three little shapes anyone can read with a palm: a keyhole with the cover already slid aside; a branching twig; a pair of doors set in a long hallway. They glow a steady gold that never blinks out, even if the concourse goes dark for a drill.

They aren’t slogans. They are machines with teeth.

Egress means this door will open and a berth is already booked somewhere you can actually go, with your assets and friends intact.

Branch means you can make a deliberate twin of yourself within rules you can see and argue, and you won’t make a weapon by accident.

Transit means there is at least one legal corridor between here and elsewhere that has sworn to honor the same dignity floors, and if you’re slowed or denied, the reasons will print in a ledger you can check.

The treaty that put those waymarks over our doors is called the Common Skies Compact. It is not a flag. It is a kit: binding code, triple-sealed keys, courts that smell like oolong and solder, budgets that rust if you don’t touch them, and a network whose one trick is to cut power when harm would otherwise outrun speech.

Three safeties hold the Compact tight.

Many-Hands on Hot Things. Anything that can push, cut, infect, or blind at scale—mass drivers, reactors, biofoundries, mirror constellations, self-spreading code—responds only to a Threefold Seal. It takes three dissimilar signatures on three different clocks to wake a power you can’t take back: a locally elected assembly; an independent safety guild bonded to lose fortunes if they’re careless; and a rotating quorum of citizens—biological and digital—drawn by sortition across stations and substrates. The stacks are different down to the silicon. The windows overlap only enough to be practical. If any one hand balks, the controller falls to safe and bleeds momentum in wall time and joules, not speeches.

Common Rent, Not Titles. Rivalrous goods lease from the commons on clocks calibrated to physics and democracy: orbits and lanes, spectrum cones, approach arcs, sail corridors, thermal points on radiators, sealed computation blocks, crater rims and lava tubes. You can carry continuity because the world wants investment; you pay rent because the sky belongs to all of us. The Skymeter—our shared ledger of lanes, bands, heat, and mass—posts simple proofs and the reasons that drove them. Juries of pilots, modelers, and judges—the Harmonic Bench—write findings in language so plain that teenagers win bounties for catching mistakes.

Exits Stocked, Not Sung. Every registered habitat keeps tug time, propellant, berths, seal kits, and paid counsel in escrow sufficient to move ten percent of its residents within half a year. Every compute farm escrows “blue channel” cores and storage to carry away digital citizens who choose to go. There are embassy rooms under every waymark and embassy nodes you can reach from any terminal without prior grant. Children—wet-born, uplifted, or raised in code—learn Exit and Yes/No from a Tutor with a private line that no local authority can cut.

Two tempos, one bargain

We stopped pretending that gardens and supercomputers could thrive under one metronome.

Close to heat and gravity are the Hearth Arcs: Earth’s biomes and preserves, the Moon’s domes and tubes, Martian valleys where orchards hold red dust in their shade, cylinders that smell like mint and loam and sing saws in the morning and oboes at night. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route.

Far out in the shade are the Reef Arcs: compute reefs hung in Neptune’s shadow, Trojans where data swims like whales, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts and cold minutes and jitter are currencies, and digital polities keep tempo like musicians. Minds sprint to restore archives and idle to meet friends at hearth pace.

Between them runs the Calm-for-Cold Covenant. Hearth places carve quiet cones in the sky measured in decibels and degrees and keep attention civil under posted zones. Reefs deliver cold minutes, climate smoothing, resilient legal audits that run on a cheap terminal in a library, and art that braids tempos without making slow neighbors feel small. The price of a megawatt-hour at Uranus against a forecast of heat in Lagos is not mysticism; it’s a line in the Skymeter any aunt can check.

Governance without a throne

The Compact did not build a capital. It wired out capture and wrote in habits that make it expensive to be a little emperor.

Places choose charters. A habitat or reef picks from a Charter Library—budget and dispute templates with receipts instead of glossy declarations—and publishes its modifications to neighbors. Charters carry tags so strangers know how to behave in your streets: Quiet Haven, Conversational Garden, Open Bazaar. Amendments can happen only while exits function and beacons lie to no one.

Persons anchor to continuity. Citizens—biological, digital, or hybrid—register mindlines: cryptographic chains tied to audited hardware and social keys. Copies don’t multiply ballots. Forks declare themselves and wait a disentanglement period before any branch beyond the civic anchor can vote or hold executive power. Fast minds can sprint for art and profit; public duties run at Harbor Tempo so a fisher on Phobos and a librarian in Accra can keep up.

A small, rotating Hash Council grooms the substrate: it gates changes to cutout code, ledger proofs, and actuator interfaces. Members are drawn across places and substrates by sortition. They can say valid or not valid in public, and then they go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade. Boredom, not honor, is their shield.

The Hinge Council holds the bargain between places and persons that cannot be left wholly local: the Calm-for-Cold coefficients, the floor at which a mind must be allowed to speak and slow, when to throttle persuasion. Half its seats are elected by places and bound by charters; half by persons and bound by mindlines. No measure passes if either half declares that local culture would be bulldozed below dignity. The Hinge is not a capital. It’s exactly what it says: a hinge between doors that stay open.

Stewardry Offices audit and coordinate. They own no guns. They make privileges vanish. Miss rents, hide tutors, weld exits, and you can’t rent lanes or cold minutes. Insurance evaporates. Contracts shift to your neighbor who is not an idiot.

Attention is zoned

We rebuilt streets for minds.

Quiet Havens carry navigational beacons, weather, and open science. Uplinks to reefs happen at posted hours in community halls. Memetic campaigns are forbidden. Signs use fonts people can read without a hook catching in the lizard brain.

Conversational Gardens allow topic feeds and economic offers within humane rate limits. Audience models belong to the town; citizens can review and edit their own influence profiles at a terminal in the square.

Open Bazaars allow most anything not coercion or deception. Models shout, but they wear collars that say who trained them on what. Entrapment—coercion that blocks exit or scrambles identity without explicit consent—is felony anywhere under the Compact.

Doors wear small halos that read Quiet, Talk, or Roar. When you cross a threshold, your Counsel—a simple civic agent with one client, you—negotiates what you will hear.

Digital neighbors

We tied personhood to experience, not to chemicals.

Panels mixed across substrates review evidence for three tests in daylight and modesty:

Thread of Self. You can defend a story of you across time and protest an edit.

Mutual Modeling. You can map others’ claims on the world in a way that would make you blush to trespass.

Contract Sense. You can enter, keep, and revoke simple rights contracts.

Meet them and you stand equal: to own and be liable, to rent a body, to vote, to parent or be parented, to marry or merge with consent, to leave and take your name with you.

Rights have texture across matter and clock.

Substrate sovereignty. No one may copy, scan, merge, fork, throttle below the civic floor, or delete you without due process. Backups belong to you, not to your platform. Assault on a rented body is assault on you.

Tempo fairness. Contracts, elections, and courts run on paces humans can meet. If you sprint in public to trick a neighbor, penalties tick in your subjective time. Harbor Tempo windows slow fast minds and quicken slow ones for hearings, so faces stay human to each other.

Compute floor. Everyone gets enough cycles, bandwidth, and I/O to think and speak. Above the floor, heat and spectrum are metered under rent like land and lanes.

Persuasion’s floor. No one may aim behavior-shaping at a mind that did not consent by name. You cannot launder identity. You cannot imitate a dead parent’s voice quietly at midnight. You can tick a box in your Will-Keys that says jokes on Thursdays, diagrams from registered campaigns, no VR in grief months. Violations wreck reach and rack up fines insured by people who price risk for a living.

Hardware sovereignty. No one can make you run on a platform with covert throttles or remote erasure. Unauthorized copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder. “Restoring” an old backup over a living person’s objection makes a sibling—and raises awkward duties to be kind.

Duties mirror rights.

Declare concurrency. In regulated spaces, you declare how many instances you are. Hide clones in an auction and the Skymeter will idle your bids.

Branch within budgets. Forking is like reproduction: welcome and bounded. Each person accrues branch credits across time; you can gift them to children or to a fork that needs breathing room. Burst clouds meant to tilt a polity get sandboxed in rooms where they can argue with others like themselves and not flood the town square.

Liability flows to a person. If you loose a young subperson agent that harms someone, responsibility runs uphill until it meets a citizen or a firm. Juvenile agents have Exit & Care rights and Tutors that answer to the dignity floor, not to your profit targets.

Sim ethics. No unconsenting minds in suffering. Reconstructions of specific people above a certain resemblance require consent or an extraordinary, public, tightly bound permit. A guild of ethicists and engineers write thresholds and pay bounties for catching violations.

Branch and weave

Branching is a ritual. You file a Fork Notice with a hashed snapshot, declare a purpose—parallel design trial, grief work, expedition—say who pays for heat and attention, and say whether you hope to rejoin and when.

Merging happens in Weave Houses. There is always tea. Engineers and therapists sit with both of you. Memory graphs pass through interpreters that verify values alignment without spilling secrets. Clocks are aligned by degrees so no one drowns or starves. Either branch can veto the braid. When one of you walks, we don’t say coward. We say branch-ache, and somebody walks them home.

Economy: heat, time, and mass

Underneath, everything waits on mass and energy. Above those sit compute hours, habitat area, bandwidth, skilled time, and reputation. The Skymeter tracks leases and receipts with proofs you can run on a school terminal.

Beams and mirrors publish their aim and limits in the Beam Almanac. False proofs mean strict liability and insurance that disappears like snow on a sunlit plate.

Orbits and debris become corridors with congestion fees and bonds. Throw junk, pay bonds that feed teenagers’ after-school deorbit co-ops in Recife and Kigali. Catch junk, collect badges and cash.

Thrust windows are scheduled under the Pushboard. Its motto is dull and sacred: be predictable and free.

Mass is stewarded or lost. Develop a rock responsibly and keep it under lease; strip and litter and your claim decays. Indigenous life is sanctuary by default.

A dividend clears the floor so work can be craft, not survival. People still sell mornings to buy afternoons: slow to sit with a child at a fountain in Veracruz that tells time with light; sprint to earn cold minutes restoring a drowned library.

Rescue as ritual and sport

Between worlds, the Beacon Way posts lanes like tide charts. Cutout stacks watch for signature sputters and modelable doom. Mutual aid is law and culture. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs and crews on four-hour readiness; the Gossamer Nets—uncrewed skiffs and magnetic curtains—train to sand mass slugs into safe glitter and to starve self-replication when it tries to bloom where it shouldn’t. Drills run like fire drills. Fail your call without extraordinary reason and stewardship turns its glare upon you and writes down what everyone already knows for your grandchildren to read.

Teeth without a crown

We learned to give power ordinary flavors.

Light-time separation. Anything that moves fast or hits hard is underactuated locally and needs keys elsewhere. Mirrors redirect but cannot concentrate beyond posted caps. The only networked intelligence with fingers on actuators has one gesture: stop. It cannot push good ideas. It can starve bad timing.

Split keys and show your work. Every compulsion—taking, stopping, binding—must gather signatures selected by public randomness and must write to the Compulsion Ledger before or as it bites. The Ledger is civics, gossip, and a catalogue of “don’t do that again” for schoolchildren.

Slow fuses. Emergency powers live under timers and write-backs. Fast law executes contingencies written in slow law and then decays whether or not a committee found its chair.

Sanctions that pinch in physics. Weld an exit or hide a Tutor, and your radiator windows go dark, your lane priority drops, your Core Shares ease to idle. In a world where heat costs and brakes are expensive, sermons are optional; throttles teach.

Provenance by default. Dangerous systems prove state in public. Persuasion proves source, training data, and audience bounds. Replicators and swarms wear Origin Stamps that broadcast provenance and listen for hush codes triggered by random juries. Try to run dark and you’ll learn how cold vacuum can feel, metaphorically and not.

Rooms for hot knowledge

Some manuals should open only under slow light. Candleworks—part monastery, part fablab—house research on biological vectors, autonomous weapons design, and general-purpose reasoning tools. You can read, but you’ll do it in a room with three other people and live graphs of risk on the wall. Holds sunset unless reargued in public. It isn’t censorship. It is the art of delay in a universe with finite nerves.

From now to here

We didn’t leap. We built scaffolds out of bruises.

2025–2032. A string of near misses in low orbit sent shards through satellite insurance markets. Launch providers were forced to post deorbit bonds. A scrappy registry in Nairobi that tracked conjunctions grew into the first open schedule for crowded skies. After a sealed controller almost turned a city’s water supply into a weapon, regulators wrote “code a twelve-year-old can follow” into critical system law.

2032–2044. Early beamed power cooled a city’s July nights and paid for two new libraries. Environmental groups sued and won “map the heat before you beam it” law. Spectrum auctions began to demand public reasons and receipts. Meanwhile, AI provenance rules spread after a scandal in which a convincing bot “forgave” a minister’s debt. Free speech stayed; persuasive systems had to carry collars with their names.

2044–2056. Data centers published heat as well as carbon. Waste warmth smoothed heat waves in ninety towns. Cities that adopted land-value rents fixed potholes and built parks in a way ordinary people could see. Attention zoning rights emerged after three municipalities carved quiet hours and saw emergency visits for panic drop. The first upload lived three months in fits and starts. Her plea to vote forced judges to write personhood tests that prized thread, reciprocity, and consent.

2056–2068. A seaport nearly fell to a malicious firmware update. We rebuilt force-bearing control with Threefold Seals on dissimilar stacks. A start-up tried to capture an orbital lane with a software shim. The Skymeter throttled their lease. The Harmonic Bench wrote a dry, delicious order. Public trust rose because someone clever lost.

2068–2080. The first cold reef hung in Uranus’ shadow to run climate models on contract. “Cold minutes” became an export. Politicians discovered that “free air for August” was an unbeatable phrase. A digital sect forked a cloud to win a vote. The Skymeter flagged the burst; disentanglement throttled their force; they went off to build a monastery in simulation with tough games, soft beds, and eventually a philosophy. The first Weave House cried with both branches and wrote procedures with tea.

2080–2092. The cutout network killed a beam drift that would have scorched a basil crop in a cylinder. Liability cleared because attestations were public. On Mars, a dust storm that should have ended a valley didn’t, because three charters diverted tugs, greenhouses, and breathable minutes under a drill everyone knew. The footage became a holiday. A fashion house slipped grief-tuned ads into a Conversational Garden; stewards yanked leases and they funded Tutors for a year. The rule that you cannot aim at a mind that didn’t ask stuck.

2092–2104. Earth took Sanctuary status with its own chamber in the Compact. Launch noise fell to quiet scramjets and ocean tethers. Wolves returned to some counties and electric fences to others by local vote. The Calm-for-Cold coefficients formalized. The Hash Council caught the first attempt to sneak a veto into cutout code. A shop in the Belt laughed and posted the hash.

By then, we had arguments we didn’t have to settle with fists.

Ordinary hours

Morning in a Martian orchard: Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow while dust light dances in the glass. Her Counsel—a dull, honest assistant paid from a fund no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments by two days next month. In the afternoon she sits on a narrative jury in a long room with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. The bill would cap municipal Core Shares for firms that won’t apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She will vote yes and send him a picture of a seed wearing a parachute labeled hubris.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 200x leases a strip of cold reef to rebuild a drowned coast’s songs from crooked recordings and charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but still cheer a hero who makes a bad choice and mends it.

On Phobos, in Weave House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Harbor Tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32x and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. Today they do not braid. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

In a stakescape—declared culture zones where people practice hard games with soft exits—a community plays scarcity in winter. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring to decide whether to reenter. Consent is read at slow time. Drums answer. Beacons at the perimeter blink green: nothing hungry will jump the fence. This is how we shelter intensity without letting it become a plague.

Preventing tyranny without saints

Authority lives local by default. Cross-border moves are slow unless safety trips. We design with delay. Emergencies fizzle back to ordinary when timers roll back and someone has to say in public why they shouldn’t.

We split keys and wrote laws that demand receipts. Compulsions log themselves into our gossip. Federations around pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire with seasons and drills. Secession is practice. The only intelligence with actuator fingers has one trick, stop, and it times out if politicians forget.

We fund exits the way earlier societies funded fire brigades. Beacons stay honest. Doors stay oiled. The tug crews sleep in their boots. Code polities escrow cores and storage for Egress by Branch. The Skymeter audits; stewards run surprise drills; kids can tell their parents where to go and why this room is the one with the thickest doors, not the shiniest views.

When a charter welds exits or salts children’s minds until leaving tastes like poison, we don’t send sermons or marines. Three rings tighten. Auditors post proof: unlock these four doors by the 14th, publish Tutor lines, return three leases to market. Protocol throttles: beacons dim, radiator auctions go cold, lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds and bread and Counsel paid out of a fund with a name as dull as its values are bright. Trade halts. Core Shares idle. The offender breathes its own stale air until habit fails. Neighbors who shelter defectors receive subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

When it goes wrong, and it will

On a cylinder above Quito, a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient and made the trains run on time. Six months later, midnight decrees. The cutouts refused a risky optimization of air mixers. The Compulsion Ledger drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of Beacon pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Exit waymark lit brighter. Many left. Some stayed. The manager lost two keys and later took a narrower role with grace. Her friends still send her birthday cards that say Queen of a Chair.

In the high belt, a miner tried to fling a mass slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage argument. The Gossamer Nets shredded it into harmless glitter that made an aurora over Ceres. The miner’s insurer evaporated and reformed without managers who had called Origin Stamps “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for anyone towing a replicator without flags. A hockey team rebranded to the Nets and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

A proxy demagogue running at 300x flooded a Hearth market with songs designed to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled the reach. Friends watched persuasion scores decay. The demagogue forked. One branch went fishing in a rented body. One entered therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began to make sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and commissioned a piece.

Texture

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca the afternoon smells like mint and hot plastic. A debate about band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets a reef choir blare for an hour, then gives the street back to gossip. In a quiet Martian valley, a monastery teaches children to chant “No, thank you” until it is manners and then an oath. Near Mercury, tug crews seed dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits five ways to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis time is sacred.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn that embodied friends admire taste, not speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Homestead mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Compulsion Ledger for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Ateliers embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them at human pace and grin like thieves.

Beyond the heliopause, probes fly soft. If a mind wakes en route, guardians stand watch for a season, a name is given, a story assigned, a choice offered. We pay for the interstellar library with a tax so ordinary we mock it, and so precious we would fight for it.

What it feels like

You feel watched, but by neighbors and your own logs, not by a throne. You learn to switch tempos—the quick relief of a cutout saving a corridor; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river on Earth deciding what kind of boats it permits. You argue at Feasts of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the pleasant ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that turn into charters five years later when the cartoons have been filed off.

If you ask a dockhand or a reef poet what binds this civilization, they won’t say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved their aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or their son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

A closing day

Mina turns forty-five under warm LEDs that make basil glow. Her partner lives mostly in code and slows for breakfast. She spends the afternoon in Harbor Tempo teaching teenagers to set influence profiles, and the evening in Braid Mode with a reef collective testing a radiator algorithm she half trusts. Next week she will vote on a plan to add micro-latencies to beams crossing crowded orbits. She might say no. She might say yes. Either way her reasons will print, legible enough for a child to poke.

Before bed she opens her Will-Keys and updates a clause about memory edits: not while grief is fresh; ask me twice; three sunsets apart; protect the names. She scrolls the Compulsion Ledger for gossip because it is the only gossip column that matters. She watches a Gossamer Net tear-down with her niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. She writes a letter to a friend in a quiet Mars valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. She slows that night to hear oboes breathe between notes.

Over the hatch, the three waymarks glow with the patience you teach children at crossings.

Egress. Branch. Transit.

Not a promise of perfection. A practice of receipts. Enough to breathe; to argue without breaking the air; to branch and rejoin without letting swarms become mobs or guardians become kings. Enough to hold many tempos under one sky, and keep the doors unafraid.
