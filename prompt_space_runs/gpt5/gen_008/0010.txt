The Lattice Compact

At first light in Port Selene, the customs hall hums with air and laughter. Over the inner lock hang three thin cords under glass, each ending in a simple brass pull. Every child in the port can name them.

- Wayline: if you pull it, the departure desk next door prints a plan that you can afford. Your seat on a tug, your storage allotment for memories and things, your provisional berth at a receiving station, and your minimum compute grant travel with you.

- Twineline: if you pull it, the clerk asks if you intend to fork. The desk shows where your lineage account stands, which mentors are on shift, how many civic cycles you have leased for the branch, and when a Confluence House has rooms if reunion is your aim.

- Trustline: if you pull it, the slate lists jurisdictions within a day’s burn that post their law in machine-checkable light, honor the same floors, and can explain “no” with runnable models or readable reasons.

No poster says “trust us.” The cords tie into code and budgets anyone can audit. They are habits more than slogans. They, together with a few blunt machines and a culture of receipts, make up the Lattice Compact: a choreography that holds stations and valleys, barges and server reefs, without melting them into sameness.

It rose from two stubborn lessons. Light-time lag is not a nuisance you can route around; it is constitution. If you want the soup hot, you cannot centralize the stove. And more clock cycles do not add virtue. If a mind can outrun your sentence between its clauses, that mind needs rails that do not trample slower lives.

Floors you can print on a glove

Under everything sit rules compact enough to tape to a life-support panel and strict enough to fence ambition.

- No unconsented harm to persons. “Person” means any being that can report its own experience with continuity, understand another’s claim, and enter reciprocal commitments, regardless of wetware or code.

- No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Anything that can copy itself carries a signed Maker Mark and listens for a Hush Code when randomly drawn juries turn keys.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering, whether in bodies or in simulation.

- Life-support runs on code the public can inspect. Changes log and roll back. If it keeps anyone breathing, it is legible.

- Decisions that bind beings at human tempo ratify at human tempo. Fast drafts are fine. Fast binding is not.

- Rivalrous commons—orbital lanes, spectrum, radiator windows, caves with pressure—lease through posted auctions with sunset dates. The commons is paid first.

- Children, biological or digital, learn to set boundaries, say no, and leave without permission from their maker.

- The coordination substrate—the beacons that announce charters, the mesh that carries consent, the minimal guardrail that watches for floor breaches—is narrow, transparent, and nobody’s to own.

Machines that obey more than they impress

The room everyone visits on a school tour is ugly on purpose. It is the Stop Room: yellow boxes with fuses you can smash, red-striped toggles under safety glass, a recessed button labeled Pause in letters you can read in a fog. Stopboxes sit in threes wherever dumb meets dangerous—mass drivers, beam actuators, sown replicators, city valves, and industrial kitchens.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can hit Pause for six minutes if a simple public rule looks to be breaking: a beam crossing a declared quiet cone without a ticket, a replicator stack without its beacon, a lane violation on approach. Pause flips machines to narrow bands and shouts its cause into the Cause Register—what rule, which device, whose hand, which clock—signed. To keep Pause pressed, three concurrences must turn: a local panel by lot, a remote auditor on hardware the locals cannot touch, and a digital citizen drafted without application. The keys expire quickly. The logs publish slowly enough for redactions to be reviewed. Overreach lives in a Lampoon Docket that ruins reputations faster than trials.

There is a second machine you only notice when someone points at the quiet it insists on. Call it the Brakekeeper. It is not a ruler; it is a very narrow custodian of guardrails. It does not tax or legislate. It sleeps where we do not agree and wakes when simple inputs cross thresholds that everyone accepted years ago: untagged spores in a shipping bay, a velocity vector that will crater a dome, a cluster spawning without a Maker Mark. It persuades before it pauses. When it must pause, it does it under Triad-Keys: distributed signatures sharded across worlds and substrates so no hand can steady itself on a bright red lever without neighbors’ consent. Its proof-of-action ships in two forms: cryptographic attestations and a short narrative that a dockhand can read on break.

The Signals Almanac you can measure with a school telescope

You do not have to trust the portmaster’s slate; you can check the sky. The Signals Almanac is a delay-tolerant ledger that posts where things are, when beams will cross, which windows are quiet, how much compute sits in which reef, who pays what to lease radiator time, and the declared concurrency of digital citizens involved in regulated acts. It is pegged to physics. You can read a portion with a kitchen verifier. You can test it with a child’s telescope and a pointer laser if you are patient.

- Beamed power and mirrors sign their contexts with ephemeral keys bound to hardware that school clubs can audit. If your beam crosses someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond. Corridor drift posts a fee in hours, not months.

- Quiet cones sit over telescopes and Lyceums—server groves where residents buy quiet by law. Cones are public in decibels and angles; when you pierce one for need, you post collateral.

- The Thermal Ledger in the Almanac tracks three things honest and dull: energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instance count. Lie, and radiator auctions pinch you until you stop lying.

- Idle stewardship lapses. If you pin an asteroid, you show beacons, surveys, and cleanup orbits. Sit too long without work and the lease returns to market.

Two rings and the Seal

You cannot make one chamber speak for all worlds and all minds. The Compact breaks its talking into circles that check each other without needing brunch.

- The Orbital Ring seats polities with hulls and atmospheres—cylinders, domes, barges—with representation roughly proportional to long-term residents. It speaks for lanes and infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions.

- The Cognitive Ring seats citizens directly in proportion to recognized persons, singularly franchised across substrates. It tends the status and safety of minds: personhood standards, minimum civic cycles, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, and attention zoning that protects neighborhoods where children walk unhooked.

- The Kernel Seal is a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, and process auditors. It says valid or not on changes to cryptographic backbones, Stopbox firmware, Maker Mark standards, and guardrail interfaces. Members serve once in a long-duty cycle. Meetings are open or publish their logs within a stated delay.

Neither ring can push the other from a chair. Anything that touches guardrails or floors needs supermajorities on both rings and a cooling window long enough for slow-tempo citizens to speak. If the Orbital Ring tries to redraw beam corridors that would tear up a local culture, the Cognitive Ring can send it back with a floor violation. If the Cognitive Ring writes personhood so tight or so fast that the Groves cannot see the stars in peace, the Orbital Ring can say not until quiet is bought.

Neighborhoods for the mind

Not every square wants a midnight carnival. Attention has zones with legal teeth.

- Harbor Wards: persuasion by topic only, no fast models without license, no targeted campaigns. Children and sensors can stroll here unafraid.

- Parlors: bounded debates with public rate limits. Audience models are municipal cooperatives you can inspect. Offers behave like buskers with rules.

- Midways: the roar on purpose. Dazzle allowed under registry, levers declared, timers enforced by your own Levers Profile.

Individuals and charters carry Levers Profiles: machine-enforceable settings that say who may aim what at whom when. Break a profile and you pay, in money first and in bandwidth later. The Reason Registry keeps an open shelf where persuasion that hopes to change law files its homework—plain claims, escrowed models, error bars someone can check.

Digital citizens as neighbors

Recognition is not worship of hardware. It is a Standing Review that asks for five things:

- Continuity you can prove without voyeurs: an autobiographical thread that survives restarts and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- Reciprocal modeling: the ability to represent other minds at the level you demand they represent you.

- Consent competence: you can read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

- Generalizable reasons: an ability to explain choices in ways that work across contexts, not just parrot training data.

- Moral salience: signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter enough to command protection.

Panels that decide are mixed: human psychologists, digital citizens, narrow routines trained to detect simulacra, and lay judges with long memories. Doubts about duties pause. Doubts about protections err generous. You can win provisional standing and revisit.

Rights then follow, with texture:

- Integrity of the inside. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot force raw introspection. Backups are yours. Unauthorized deletion is murder.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles needed to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process that runs on a clock you can see.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs on a Common Beat. Fast clusters draft in hours. Ratification happens on a slow schedule—days for a habitat, weeks for a federation. Penalties for deception tick in your subjective time.

- Hardware sovereignty. You may choose where you run. No one may compel you onto hardware with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Association and exit. You can join, fork, merge, and leave, subject to safety codes like vehicle law.

Duties scale with externality:

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuce alive is a credit.

- Concurrency declaration. In regulated markets and elections, you declare your speed and active instance count. Votes bind to a singular franchise per Strand; business deals void if you hide speed.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed unbounded replication outside audited boxes. Your tools carry beacons and answer Hush Codes when Stopboxes call.

- Provenance. Major external actions carry a private log that can prove compliance without baring thought.

Forking and merging are treated as families, not gimmicks. Your public identity follows a Strand across forks and pauses. A Strand holds one franchise. When you branch, you file a notice with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose, a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if reunion is contemplated. One branch retains the civic anchor immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy. New branches inherit enough to live with dignity and owe a declining branch dividend to their progenitor. Mass copying to swamp a vote or staff a factory is regulated the same way we regulate surrogacy and explosives: not by scolding but by rooms full of receipts.

Merging, if it happens, happens in Confluence Houses. Tea is served because tea slows hurt. Engineers and therapists sit together. Memory graphs run through cryptographic interpreters that verify value alignment without ripping pages from minds. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, we do not say coward. We say branch-grief and put the kettle back on.

Markets without barons

We price rivalry, not power. Anything you could sit on out of spite—lane slots, spectrum, cold, pressure hull—leases with clocks that expire unless renewed with reasons.

Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Leases: cave mouths, crater rims, deltas, nickel seams, windows where sunlight does not scorch.

- Lane Leases: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Band Leases: spectrum and whisper bands.

- Core Leases: compute time tied to hardware identity and verifiable heat sinks.

- Simspace Leases: sealed worlds that run on paid Core Leases and label their physics, exit rules, and safeties like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A dignity stipend goes to every recognized person, independent of substrate. A local share pays those who keep the lease valuable: the tug that keeps debris clear, the algae that keeps streets cool, the cold lattice that bleeds heat to black. Orders from the Lanes Guild—the guild of pilots, schedulers, and judges—ship as Runnable Orders: minimal-model simulations any two independent audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc S-9 for four hours because two burns at Vesta would have pushed your berth into a light-time dead zone” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum your niece can check at a library terminal.

Money nets through the Interplanetary Clearing Union. The unit, the Helios, is composite: a kilowatt-hour measured at a reference orbit plus a compute-second on a posted reference architecture. Helios demur gently over long periods. Hoarding costs; timely work pays.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- Skyward Wardens are the only cross-world actuators. Their mandate is deliberately narrow: contain kinetic and replicative threats that local Stopboxes cannot manage. They are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited by schools, unions, and hobby clubs. Every action requires Triad-Keys from randomly drawn juries across polities and posts a narrative to the Cause Register.

- The Itinerant Bench rides a flotilla of ships with gardens and classrooms. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity claims, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Their verdicts trigger smart contracts that seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Leases, and in last resort lock hazardous devices.

- The Circle is a permanent citizens’ assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by substrate and speed. Arguments ride a polylog protocol: claims are cryptographically committed, prediction wagers travel alongside, and participants’ calibration histories are public. The Circle interprets floors when new edge cases arrive. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Beacons.

- The Slow Council is a rolling synod that meets in a calendar true to orbital mechanics. It tends shared commons—debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that sunset unless renewed.

Guardrails where law meets engineering

- Harbor Law for Orbits. Every mass you send aloft carries a signed end-of-life plan and escrowed resources to implement it. Plans must simulate inside agreed envelopes. If your bolt shreds my transceiver array, liability rides with your manifest.

- Light-Cone Courts. Disputes that cross space are framed by physics. If your dust plume will graze my farming ring in six days, you owe me now, not after the crop fails. Evidence lies or tells the truth when the sky itself is witness; perjury costs a ratio of radiators and time.

- Blue Door Protocol. Any person—embodied or digital—can request exit through their Beacon. The protocol triggers a veto check under air gap: your polity must either let you go or prove, without leaking gossip, that a due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify the proof. Doors open by code; care obligations travel with the person.

- Emergency Sunsets. During solar storms or bio-spills, Beacons light contingency constitutions that widen some powers and then die on their own. The Brakekeeper can accelerate coordination for a defined span, subject to challenge by any two Beacons that present a workable alternative plan.

Exits you schedule, not beg

Every charter posts receipts that fit on a wall: tugs reserved, propellant stockpiled, berths available, heaters, water, seal kits, stewards. Server polities escrow Core Leases on hardware no local can throttle. Random drills run quietly at dawn. The Departure Office is not a website; it is a room with three desks and chairs that fit elders and octopuses and drones: Departures, where you book; Mediation, where co-ops argue with tea; and Founders, where you borrow charters and run yours in a box that will not flatter.

Block exits or lie, and your beam windows dim, your Core Leases idle, your spectrum goes quiet. The boredom is expensive. The tug co-op in the next orbit bids on the work you will now miss. When a charter has sealed its own doors, three rings tighten: auditors publish maps and dates, protocol denial makes leaving cheap and staying dull, and neighbors pay subsidies big enough to mend roofs for those who shelter defectors. No marines arrive. There is soup when you land.

A day that smells of sesame and transformer oil

On Tuesday, Dania’s stall in the Rim Market opens to a line of haulers, students, and two drones piloted by kids who like the smell of frying sesame. At nine, her Counsel—every citizen has one paid by a fund no local council can cut—pings an argument: a proposal to add forty-millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp edge-case feedback cascades. The provenance is clean: three diagrams, two stories, humor allowed low dose, no grief levers. Dania presses “sleep until tomorrow,” thinks at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones to nudge it. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a server-reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when he speaks from his puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The camera shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Harbor Ward, a digital poet in a wooden puppet sips broth with their human wife. The poet’s main self runs at 200× near Neptune, piecing a drowned delta’s library back together from aunties’ stories and smudged ledgers. Today they throttle down to share soup. After, they spend twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines politely to speed up “just to keep up.”

At dusk, the market canopy lights skew violet. A projector flips to the Lampoon Docket. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy palms a toy Whistlecraft and asks if he can sign up. Dania says when you are fifteen—and can explain Pause without notes.

Hard cases that bent us, not broke us

A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No one flooded them with code. Exit was real. Digital citizens left. That flood year the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a rented puppet coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause over soup.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival’s dome in a wage fight. A Whistlecraft turned the slug into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The co-op’s insurer cratered. Directors learned that coercion budgets are not suggestions. They apologized and fielded a hockey team.

A demagogue flooded a Bazaar with songs tuned to induce smallness and fatigue. Levers Profiles throttled reach. Neighbors watched persuasion scores decay like frost in sun. One branch of the demagogue left to design fishing lures. They won a harmlessness prize.

Twice a Crimson Room—labs where dangerous research lives under oaths—went to zero. Once was a false bell that taught us to write better sensors. Once was a silence that saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

How we got from here to here

It was not grace. It was a stack of dull fixes that held and got copied.

2026–2034. A rash of debris collisions kept children inside and made insurers sour. Launches had to publish ephemerides; rendezvous corridors went from whispers to posted schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” the rule for life-support. Early AI persuasion scandals made provenance mandatory: if a model tried to change your mind, it had to carry its homework. Courts began granting narrow agency to digital systems when continuity held up over seasons.

2034–2046. Beamed power leapt out of papers. Environmental groups held out for “corridors before commercialization”: post safe lanes first, then money. A demo scorched cropland; the supplier’s logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared fast. Cities and ports adopted compulsion ledgers—dreary at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2046–2058. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a heat meter you could read with a finger. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of civic cycles and a Counsel—a simple machine lawyer that would not act without an explicit yes and could be turned off. Early uploads were brittle, but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and commitments held up.

2058–2070. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver whistled past a habitat, Stopboxes became law: narrow six-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without first calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. That compromise hardened. Insurers made them standard. Two public saves ended arguments.

2070–2082. The twin rings replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Whistlecraft flew, nipped a wayward slug into glitter, and came home to be opened by children with oranges. Personhood law found language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. The Common Beat for civic time took hold. Attention zoning—Harbors, Parlors, Midways—grew from municipal quiet hours that saved elections and sleep.

2082–2090. The first attempt to swing a vote with a cloud of clones met singular franchises, Junction Courts, and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sorrow; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2090–2099. The Sun-and-Shadow Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought quiet cones. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Crimson Rooms got kill-lines and oaths.

2099–2111. The Kernel Seal refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The Lampoon Docket found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt a Blue Door ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup. The Compact stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Texture

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca, the afternoon smells of mint and warm plastic. A debate over band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets the reef choir blare for an hour, then gives the square back to quiet talk. In a valley on Mars, a quiet cloister teaches children to say “No, thank you” as a chant that starts as manners and ends as an oath. Near Mercury, Foundry crews trail dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits into five to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis deserves slow humans in it.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn that embodied friends admire taste, not speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Garden mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Cause Register for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Lyceums embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them in order and grin.

A morning at Confluence House Nine on Phobos is all light and steam. Two Selmas have booked a room. One kept basil alive in Harbor Tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh sounds like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. They try. Today they do not. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and writes a note only Selma will read in both rooms.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it the way grandparents funded libraries: small taxes we grumble about paying and love to brag about later. Starlight Protocols forbid waking neighbors by accident and ban ideology at light-speed. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default. If they bloom, newborn grace applies: guardians pro tem, a right to a name and a story before a mission.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and by your own logs. You learn to shift tempo—the relief of a Stopbox flipping a relay; the slow breathing pace of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial politics of a delta deciding what kind of boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the good ache of turn-taking with breathers. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that turn into charters five years later when the cartoons have been filed off.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

This is not perfection. It is sufficient glory: specific bolts you can name, rituals you can practice, jokes you can share, and receipts that make power behave. It is a world where many orbits and many kinds of mind keep faith without surrender because latency is honored, exits are bright, and no one can be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.

On your way out of the customs hall in Port Selene, the three thin cords blink once the way crosswalks do when they sense small feet. Wayline. Twineline. Trustline. Not a spell. A habit made of glass and budgets. Enough.
