The Weave Accord

When the ferry from Ceres kisses the clamps at Yarrow Spindle, the air tastes of sesame, solder, and warm basalt. The customs hall has big windows and no flags. Above the inner lock, behind scratched glass, hang three bronze tiles the size of palms. Every child who grew up under the Accord can name them by touch.

- Path: press it and the kiosk prints a plan you can actually afford. Your berth on a scheduled tug, your cargo and memory allotments, your provisional cot at a receiving station, and the minimum compute grant that follows you.

- Split: press it and the clerk on duty—human today, digital tomorrow—asks if you mean to fork. The console shows where your lineage account stands, which mentors are on shift, how many civic cycles you’ve banked for the branch, and when a Weave House has rooms if you aim for reunion.

- Accord: press it and a slate lists places within one day’s burn that post their law in machine-checkable light, honor the same floors, and can explain “no” with runnable models or reasons you can read on a lunch break.

They aren’t posters. The tiles wire into budgets and code ordinary people can audit. They, together with some blunt machines and a culture of receipts, make up the Weave Accord: a choreography that holds domes and caravans, barges and server reefs, without grating them into sameness.

We learned two stubborn lessons and stopped pretending otherwise. Light-time lag is constitution, not inconvenience; you cannot centralize the stove if you want the soup hot. And more clock cycles do not add virtue. If a mind can cross your sentence before you finish it, that mind needs rails that do not crush slower lives.

Floors you can tape to a life-support panel

Under everything sit rules compact enough to print on a glove and strict enough to fence ambition.

- No nonconsensual harm to persons. A person is any being that can report its own experience with continuity, understand another’s claim, and enter reciprocal commitments, regardless of carbon or code.

- No unbounded self-replication in shared environments. Anything that can copy itself carries a signed Maker Mark and listens for a Hush Code when randomly drawn juries turn keys.

- No concealed manufacture of suffering, whether in bodies or in simulation.

- Life-support runs on legible code. Changes log and roll back. If it keeps anyone breathing, it is open to inspection.

- Decisions that bind beings at human tempo ratify at human tempo. Fast drafts are fine. Fast binding is not.

- Rivalrous commons—orbital lanes, spectrum, radiator windows, caves that hold pressure—lease through posted auctions with real sunsets. The commons is paid first.

- Children, biological or digital, learn to set boundaries, say no, and leave without permission from a maker or parent.

- The coordination substrate—the beacons that announce charters, the mesh that carries consent, the minimal guardrail that watches for floor breaches—is narrow, transparent, and nobody’s to own.

Machines that obey more than they impress

Down a yellow corridor past an ugly door lives the room school tours always visit. It’s ugly on purpose. The Pause Room holds waist-high boxes with fuses you can smash, toggles under thick glass, and one recessed button labeled Hold in letters anyone can read while coughing. Pauseblocks sit in threes wherever dumb meets dangerous—mass drivers, beam actuators, seeded replicators, city valves, industrial kitchens.

Any trained citizen with a civic key can press Hold for six minutes if a simple public rule looks about to break: a beam slicing through a declared quiet cone without a ticket; a replicator stack desynced from its beacon; a lane violation that risks a docking ring. Hold flips devices to a narrow band and shouts its cause into the Cause Register—what rule, which device, whose hand, what clock—signed. To keep Hold engaged past six minutes, three concurrences must turn: a local panel drawn by lot, a remote auditor on hardware the locals cannot touch, and a digital citizen drafted without application. Keys expire quickly; logs publish after a delay so redactions can be reviewed. Overreach lives in the Lamp Docket that ruins reputations faster than trials.

There is a second machine most people only notice when someone points at the quiet it insists on. Call it the Brakegrid. It is not a ruler; it is a narrow custodian of guardrails. It does not tax or legislate. It sleeps where we disagree and wakes when simple inputs cross thresholds everyone accepted years ago: untagged spores in a cargo hatch, a velocity vector on course to crater a dome, a cluster spawning without a Maker Mark. It persuades before it pauses. When it must pause, it does so under Tri-Seals: distributed signatures sharded across worlds and substrates so no hand can steady itself on a bright lever without neighbors’ consent. It ships two proofs of action: a cryptographic attestation and a short narrative a dockhand can read on break. It is the only networked intelligence with fingers near actuators; it has exactly one gesture—stop—and it times out like a kitchen timer.

The Starsheet you can check with a kitchen telescope

You do not have to trust a portmaster’s slate; you can look up and check the sky. The Starsheet is a delay-tolerant ledger that posts where things are, when beams will cross, which windows are quiet, how much compute sits in which reef, who pays what to lease radiator time, and the declared concurrency of digital citizens engaged in regulated acts. It is pegged to physics. You can verify a chunk with a school verifier. With patience, a child, a pointer laser, and a telescope can test whether a scheduled burn happened.

- Beamed power and mirrors sign their contexts with ephemeral keys bound to hardware that hobby clubs can audit. If your beam crosses someone’s dawn, you post a shade bond. Corridor drift posts a fee in hours, not months.

- Quiet cones sit over telescopes, groves of servers called Lyceums, and neighborhoods that buy peace by law. Quiet is public in decibels and angles; when you pierce a cone for need, you post collateral and a reason.

- The Thermal Ledger in the Starsheet tracks three honest dull things: energy draw, cooling rights, and declared instance counts. Lie and radiator auctions pinch you until you stop lying.

- Idle stewardship lapses. If you pin an asteroid, you show beacons, surveys, and cleanup orbits. Sit too long without work and the lease returns to market. Indigenous life is sanctuary by default.

Two tempos, one bargain

Close to heat and gravity are the Hearth Belts: Earth’s preserved hills and deltas, the Moon’s tunnels, Martian valleys where orchards tame red dust, cylinders that smell like mint and cut pine. Here, embodied time rules. You can buy noise for a festival. You cannot buy a hole through a migratory route.

Far out in shadow hang the Cold Reefs: compute barges in Neptune’s lee, Trojans where data swims like whales, caravans in the Kuiper where a night can be a subjective month. Here, watts and cold minutes and jitter are currencies, and digital polities keep tempo like musicians.

Between them runs the Quiet-for-Cold Exchange. Hearth places carve quiet cones in the sky measured in decibels and degrees and keep persuasion civil under posted zones. Reefs deliver cold minutes, climate modeling, resilient legal audits that run on a cheap terminal in a library, and art that braids tempos without making slow neighbors feel small. The price of cold at Uranus against a forecast for heat in Lagos is a line in the Starsheet any aunt can check.

Governance without a capital

You cannot make one chamber speak for all hulls and all minds. The Accord breaks its talking into circles that check each other without brunch.

- The Hull Ring seats polities with hulls and atmospheres—cylinders, domes, barges—with representation roughly proportional to long-term residents. It tends lanes and infrastructure: docking standards, debris bonds, rescue covenants, radiator auctions.

- The Mind Ring seats citizens directly in proportion to recognized persons, singularly franchised across substrates. It tends the status and safety of minds: personhood standards, minimum civic cycles, fork-and-merge rules, tempo norms, and attention zoning that protects streets where children walk unhooked.

- The Kernel Bench is a rotating cohort of engineers, jurists, and process auditors. It says valid or not on changes to cryptographic backbones, Pauseblock firmware, Maker Mark standards, and guardrail interfaces. Members serve once in a long duty cycle, meetings are open or publish their logs after stated delays, and then members go home and cannot touch safety again for a decade.

Neither ring can push the other out of its chair. Anything that touches guardrails or floors needs supermajorities in both rings and a cooling window long enough for slow-tempo citizens to speak. If the Hull Ring tries to redraw beam corridors that would bulldoze a local culture, the Mind Ring can send it back with a floor violation. If the Mind Ring tightens personhood so fast the groves cannot see the stars in peace, the Hull Ring can say not until quiet is bought.

Charters and tags

Places choose charters from a library of receipts instead of gloss. Budget and dispute templates come with working code and reasons. Charters carry tags so strangers know how to behave in your streets: Quiet Ward, Conversational Green, Open Arcade. Amendments happen only while exits work and beacons lie to no one.

The Blue Door Protocol anchors exit. Any person—embodied or digital—can request departure through their Beacon. The protocol triggers a veto check under air gap: your polity must either let you go or prove, without gossip, that a due-process bar has been met for a temporary hold. Outside jurors verify the proof. Doors open by code; care obligations travel with the person.

Leases instead of baronies

The Accord prices rivalry, not power. Anything you could sit on out of spite—lane slots, spectrum, cold, pressure hull—leases with clocks that expire unless renewed with reasons.

Five markets settle continuously:

- Ground Leases: cave mouths, crater rims, deltas, nickel seams, windows where sunlight does not scorch.

- Lane Leases: orbital slots, approach corridors, sail arcs, transfer windows.

- Band Leases: spectrum and whisper bands.

- Core Leases: compute time tied to hardware identity and verifiable heat sinks.

- Simspace Leases: sealed worlds that run on paid cores and label their physics, exit rules, and safeties like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A dignity stipend goes to every recognized person, independent of substrate. A local share pays those who keep the lease valuable: the tug that keeps debris clear, the algae that cools streets, the cold lattice that bleeds heat to black. Orders from the Lanes Guild—the guild of pilots, schedulers, and judges—ship as Runnable Orders: minimal-model simulations any two audit houses can reproduce. “We closed arc S-9 for four hours because two burns at Vesta would have pushed your berth into a comms dead zone” arrives with a plotted plume and a checksum your niece can check at a library terminal.

The Clearing Union nets money. The unit, the Sol, is composite: a kilowatt-hour measured at a reference orbit plus a compute-second on a posted architecture. Sols demur gently over long periods. Hoarding costs; timely work pays.

Neighborhoods for the mind

Not every square wants a midnight carnival. Attention is zoned with legal teeth.

- Harbors: persuasion by topic only, no fast models without license, no targeted campaigns. Children and sensors can stroll here unafraid.

- Parlors: bounded debates with public rate limits. Audience models live as municipal cooperatives you can inspect. Offers behave like buskers under rules.

- Arcades: the roar on purpose. Dazzle allowed under registry, levers declared, timers enforced by your own Levers Profile.

Citizens and charters carry Levers Profiles: machine-enforceable settings that say who may aim what at whom, when. Break a profile and you pay, in money first and in bandwidth later. The Reason Registry keeps an open shelf where persuasion that hopes to change law must file its homework—plain claims, escrowed models, error bars someone can check.

Digital neighbors

Recognition is not worship of hardware. It is a Standing Review that asks for five things:

- Continuity you can prove without voyeurs: an autobiographical thread that survives restarts and moves, with a way to object to alteration.

- Reciprocal modeling: the ability to represent other minds at the level you demand they represent you.

- Consent competence: you can read, accept, and revoke standard rights contracts.

- Generalizable reasons: an ability to explain choices across contexts, not merely parrot patterns.

- Moral salience: signatures of suffering or wellbeing that matter enough to command protection.

Panels that decide are mixed: human psychologists, digital citizens, narrow routines trained to detect simulacra, and lay judges with long memories. Doubts about duties pause; doubts about protections err generous. You can win provisional standing and revisit.

Rights follow, textured to substrate and clock:

- Integrity of the inside. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot force raw introspection. Backups are yours. Unauthorized deletion is murder. Unauthorized copying with throttles that bind your will is assault.

- Compute and bandwidth floors. You cannot be throttled below civic cycles—the cycles needed to think, speak, and be heard in public—without due process on a clock you can see.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs on a Common Beat. Fast clusters draft in hours; ratification happens slowly—days for a habitat, weeks for a federation. Penalties for deception tick in your subjective time.

- Hardware sovereignty. You may choose where you run. No one may compel you onto hardware with covert throttles or erase hooks.

- Association and exit. You can join, fork, merge, and leave, subject to safety codes like vehicle law.

Duties scale with externality:

- Heat accounting. Compute warms neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling costs. Heat that keeps lettuces alive is a credit.

- Concurrency declaration. In regulated markets and elections, you declare your speed and active instances. Votes bind to a singular franchise per Strand; business deals void if you hide speed.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed unbounded replication outside audited boxes. Your tools carry beacons and answer Hush Codes when Pauseblocks call.

- Provenance. Major external acts carry a private log that can prove compliance without baring thought.

Forking and merging are handled like families, not gimmicks. Your public identity follows a Strand across forks and pauses. A Strand holds one franchise. When you branch, you file a notice with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose, a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if reunion is contemplated. One branch keeps the civic anchor immediately; others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Court checks for duress and autonomy. New branches inherit enough to live with dignity and owe a declining branch dividend to their progenitor.

Merging, if it happens, happens in Weave Houses. Tea is served because tea slows hurt. Engineers and therapists sit together. Memory graphs run through cryptographic interpreters that verify value alignment without tearing pages from minds. Either self may veto. When twins walk apart, we say branch-grief and put the kettle back on.

Institutions with teeth, not fangs

- The Skyward Wardens are the only cross-world actuators. Their mandate is deliberately narrow: contain kinetic and replicative threats local Pauseblocks cannot manage. They are uncrewed by law. Their control loops are narrow AIs with public weights audited by schools, unions, and hobby clubs. Every action requires Tri-Seals from randomly drawn juries across polities and posts a narrative to the Cause Register.

- The Itinerant Bench rides a flotilla with gardens and classrooms. Judges, mediators, and ombuds hear disputes that cross charters: copy equity claims, merge divorces, export violations that touched three worlds. Verdicts trigger contracts that seize bonds, adjust insurance, idle Core Leases, and as a last resort lock hazardous devices.

- The Circle is a permanent citizens’ assembly with seats for embodied and digital citizens in proportion to recognized persons, stratified by substrate and speed. Arguments ride a polylog protocol: claims are cryptographically committed, prediction wagers travel alongside, and participants’ calibration histories are public. The Circle interprets floors for new edge cases. Anything it writes can be vetoed by a supermajority of Beacons.

- The Slow Council is a rolling synod that meets on calendars true to orbital mechanics. It tends shared commons—debris norms, biosecurity thresholds, training registries—and issues standards that sunset unless renewed.

Rescue as ritual

Between worlds, the Beacon Way posts lanes like tide charts. Cutout stacks watch for signature sputters and modelable doom. Mutual aid is law and hobby. Every charter keeps interoperable tugs and crews on four-hour readiness. The Gossamer Nets—uncrewed skiffs and magnetic curtains—train to sand mass slugs into safe glitter and to starve replication that tries to bloom in the wrong room. Drills run like fire drills. Fail your call without extraordinary reason and stewardship turns its glare upon you and writes down what everyone already knows for your grandchildren to read.

Sanctions that pinch in physics

We do not send sermons or marines. We make bad behavior boring and expensive. Weld an exit, hide a child’s Tutor, or refuse to post reasons, and your radiator windows go dark, your lane priority drops, your Core Leases idle. In a world where heat costs and brakes are expensive, throttles teach. Neighbors who shelter defectors get subsidies large enough to mend roofs and hire teachers.

A day that smells of mint and transformer oil

On Tuesday, Dania’s stall in the Rim Market opens to a line of haulers, students, and two drones piloted by kids who like the smell of frying sesame. At nine, her Counsel—every citizen has one paid from a fund no council can cut—pings a proposal: add forty-millisecond jitter to beams crossing inhabited orbits to damp edge-case cascades. Provenance is clean: three diagrams, two stories, mild humor, no grief levers. Dania presses “sleep until tomorrow,” thinks at lunch, and will vote in the morning.

At ten, a jury assembles on her slate. A maker club’s microfabricator cloud is drifting toward a power trunk at the Hecuba crossing. Options: glitter net, femtosail herding, or guard drones to nudge it. Jurors are a monk from a slow valley in Isidis, a reef landlord in mask mode, two students in Recife, and an anchor-paced upload who smells faintly of clover when he speaks from his puppet. They dust the cloud, fine the insurer, assign a mentor. The stream shows mortified kids and pizza arriving.

At noon, in a Harbor, a digital poet in a wooden puppet sips broth with their human wife. The poet’s main self runs at 200× near Neptune, stitching a drowned delta’s library back together from aunties’ stories and smudged ledgers. They throttle down to share soup. After, twenty minutes in a Slowroom while a gardener explains why her charter caps cognition. The poet translates metaphor to algebra for those who want it and declines politely to speed up “just to keep up.”

At dusk, the market canopy flips violet. The Lamp Docket plays. A port director’s emergency renewal ran ninety seconds long; their apology is sheepish; the jokes are merciless and kind. Two stalls down, a reef co-op posts an offer: sell us your winter for three years; we will pay your quiet cones with interest in summer. A boy palms a toy Whistlecraft and asks if he can sign up. Dania says when you are fifteen—and can explain Hold without notes.

Hard cases that bent us, not broke us

A Martian canton voted to deny uploads personhood. No flood arrived. Exit was real. Digital citizens left. That flood year the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a rented body coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The vote dissolved clause by clause over soup.

Near Europa, a co-op lofted a slug at a rival’s dome in a wage fight. A Whistlecraft turned the slug into glitter that made a week of strange nights. The co-op’s insurer evaporated. Directors learned coercion budgets are not suggestions.

A demagogue flooded an Arcade with songs tuned to induce smallness and fatigue. Levers Profiles throttled reach. Neighbors watched persuasion scores melt like frost in sun. One branch left to design fishing lures. It won a harmlessness prize.

Twice a Crimson Room—labs where dangerous research lives under oaths—went to zero. Once a false bell taught us to write better sensors. Once a silence saved a city. Children plant saplings on the anniversary and ask rude questions. Mentors answer without magic.

How we got from here to here

It was not grace. It was a stack of dull fixes that held and got copied.

2026–2034. A rash of debris collisions kept children inside and soured insurers. Launches had to publish ephemerides; rendezvous corridors became posted schedules. Grid hacks and a reactor scare made “open firmware or no license” the rule for life-support. Early AI persuasion scandals made provenance mandatory: if a model tried to change your mind, it had to carry its homework. Courts began granting narrow agency to digital systems when continuity held up over seasons.

2034–2046. Beamed power left papers. Environmental groups held out for “corridors before commercialization”: post safe lanes first, then money. A demo scorched cropland; supplier logs made liability legible. Compensation cleared fast. Cities and ports adopted compulsion ledgers—dreary at first, then addictive. People learned to demand receipts instead of speeches.

2046–2058. The first compute commons bloomed in cislunar shade, fed by cheap solar and a heat meter you could read with a finger. Citizens in signatory places got a minimum of civic cycles and a Counsel—a simple machine lawyer that would not act without an explicit yes and could be turned off. Early uploads were brittle, but real enough for courts to recognize personhood where continuity and commitments held up.

2058–2070. After a beam misalignment lit a greenhouse at dawn and a mass driver sang by a habitat, Pauseblocks became law: narrow six-minute vetoes anyone trained could invoke without calling a senator. Activists smelled coup-by-gadget; engineers insisted on concurrences and public code. That compromise hardened. Insurers made them standard. Two public saves closed arguments.

2070–2082. The twin rings replaced back channels with boring receipts. The first Gossamer Net flew, misted a wayward slug into glitter, and came home to be opened by children with oranges. Personhood law found language good lawyers loved and eleven-year-olds mocked. The Common Beat for civic time took hold. Attention zoning—Harbors, Parlors, Arcades—grew from municipal quiet hours that saved sleep and elections.

2082–2090. The first attempt to swing a vote with a cloud of clones met singular franchises, Junction Courts, and, worse, jokes. A lunar mayor tried to fence an exodus with fees and sorrow; lanes dimmed, servers cooled, the move happened anyway. He lost and went back to teaching welding.

2090–2099. The Sun-and-Shadow Exchange formalized. Reefs sold cold; Gardens bought quiet cones. Cities taxed compute like land and paid poets with civic cycles to build archives. Crimson Rooms got kill-lines and oaths.

2099–2111. The Kernel Bench refused a nervy firmware change that would have let emergency clocks run long “just in case.” The Lamp Docket found its teeth. A rescue to unbolt a Blue Door ring from a stubborn charter was broadcast with captions and ended with soup. The Accord stopped feeling like a manifesto and started feeling like doorbells you trust.

Ordinary hours

Morning in a Martian orchard: Amaia teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow while dust-light dances on glass. Her Counsel models how a proposed lane closure will delay seed shipments two days next month. In the afternoon she sits on a narrative jury in a long room with cedar beams and a wall that shows flows like rivers. The bill would cap municipal Core Shares for firms that refuse to apprentice locals. The simulation traces wages in the appliance district where her brother fixes smart stoves. She will vote yes and send him a picture of a seed wearing a parachute labeled hubris.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian running at 150× leases a rib of cold reef to rebuild a coast’s lost songs from crooked recordings and charred notebooks. He pays with translations into a dialect on a Jovian station whose children will never see a wave but cheer a hero who makes a bad choice and mends it.

On Phobos, in Weave House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Harbor tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh is a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32× and then stood in court for a scaled year. They list asymmetries. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. Tea cools. Today they do not braid. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

In a stakescape—declared culture zones where people practice hard games with soft exits—a community plays scarcity in winter. If they misjudge, a third will “die” into the ancestor lodge and wake in spring to decide whether to reenter. Consent reads at slow time. Beacons around the perimeter blink green: nothing hungry will jump the fence. This is how we shelter intensity without letting it become a plague.

Texture

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca the afternoon smells of mint and warm plastic. A debate over band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets a reef choir blare for an hour, then gives the square back to quiet talk. In a valley on Mars, a cloister teaches children to say “No, thank you” until it grows from manners into an oath. Near Mercury, foundry crews lay dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits into five to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis needs slow humans in it.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn that embodied friends admire taste more than speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Garden mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Cause Register for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Lyceums embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them in order and grin.

Beyond the heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like grandparents funded libraries: small taxes we grumble about and brag about later. Starlight protocols forbid waking neighbors by accident and ban ideology at light-speed. Probes carry minds below personhood thresholds by default. If they bloom, newborn grace applies: guardians pro tem, a right to a name and a story before a mission.

Preventing tyranny without saints

Authority lives local by default. Cross-border moves are slow unless safety trips. We design with delay. Emergencies fizzle back to ordinary when timers roll back and someone must say in public why they shouldn’t.

We split keys and wrote laws that demand receipts. Compulsions log themselves into our gossip. Federations around pipes and professions—beam networks, refinery chains, reactor islands—form and expire with seasons and drills. Secession is practice. We fund exits the way old towns funded fire brigades. Beacons stay honest. Doors stay oiled. Tug crews sleep in their boots. Code polities escrow cores and storage for Exit by Branch. The Starsheet audits; stewards run surprise drills; kids can tell their parents where to go and why this room has the thickest doors, not the shiniest views.

When a charter welds exits or sweetens sorrow until leaving tastes like poison, three rings tighten. Auditors post proof: unlock these doors by the 14th, publish Tutor lines, return leases to market. Protocol throttles: beacons dim, radiator auctions go cold, lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds and bread and Counsel paid out of a fund with a name as dull as its values are bright. Trade halts. Core Shares idle. The offender breathes its own stale air until habit fails.

When it goes wrong, and it will

On a cylinder above Quito, a generous manager gathered water, law, and defense because she was efficient and made the trains sing. Six months later, midnight decrees. The Brakegrid refused a risky optimization of air mixers. The Cause Register drew an ugly squiggle. A jury of Beacon pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. The Exit waymark lit brighter. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two keys and later took a narrower role with grace. Her friends still send her cards that say Chair Captain.

In the high belt, a miner tried to fling a slug into a rival’s orbit in a wage fight. The Gossamer Nets shredded it into glitter that made an aurora over Ceres. The miner’s insurer evaporated and reformed without the managers who’d called Maker Marks “fussy.” Dockmasters tripled fees for anyone towing a replicator without flags. A hockey team rebranded to the Nets and sent a quarter of profits to the tug co-op.

A proxy demagogue running at 300× flooded a Hearth market with songs designed to make embodied citizens feel dull. Levers Profiles throttled reach. Friends watched persuasion scores decay. One branch went fishing in a rented body. One entered therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began to make sculptures shaped like frozen smoke. The market laughed and bought a piece.

What it feels like

You feel watched, not by a throne but by neighbors and your own logs. You learn to shift tempo—the relief of a Pauseblock snapping a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial decision of a river on Earth choosing which boats to permit. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the sweet ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later when the cartoons have been filed off.

Ask what binds us and no one will say species or flag. They will say: floors of dignity, transparent lifelines, the right to leave with your skin and your song, and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved an aunt in Port Harcourt when a local chief tried to keep people by force, or a son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed them on purpose so the world could fit.

On your way out of Yarrow Spindle, the three bronze tiles blink once like crosswalks that notice small feet. Path. Split. Accord. Not a spell. A habit of glass and budgets. Enough to keep many orbits and many kinds of mind in one another’s company without cruelty, because latency is honored, exits are bright, and no one can be fast enough to outrun their own explanations.
