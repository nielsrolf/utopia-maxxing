The Constellation Accord

At every airlock, server vestibule, and dock gate there is a narrow lintel etched with four small glyphs anyone can read with a fingertip or a telescope: a compass star; a braided ring; an unlatched gate; a lantern. Beneath them, the Articles that schoolchildren and new citizens memorize because they are not poetry; they are plumbing:

No throne across the sky. Shared paths, not fiefdoms. Exit is sacred. Minds are sovereign.

The Accord lives in firmware, budgets, habits, and clocks that take light-time seriously. It lets worlds be unlike on purpose without making it easy to hurt strangers.

What the Accord Is

You can inspect it in three layers.

1) Hard stops that can say “no”—fast enough to matter—while never governing.

2) Open charters and assemblies that bind only at tempos people can meet, across substrates and planets.

3) Doors and ladders so leaving is cheaper than forcing, beginning is safer than hoarding, and dignity does not depend on luck.

Guardrails and Instruments

The Redline Array

There are moves you simply cannot make in shared space alone.

- You do not throw unsteered mass across someone’s orbit.

- You do not jam a whole neighborhood’s sky.

- You do not release self-replicating machines or code into commons without registration beacons, published shutdowns, and keys you do not hold.

- You do not copy, alter, or erase a person—wetware or digital—save by lawful process that can be verified without cracking skulls or source.

These are enforced by Crosshatch Keys: every actuator that can bruise a stranger—mass drivers, high-gain beam taps, mirror swarms, fabricators with replication enabled—knows how to ask for three signatures on dissimilar clocks. One from your local charter’s stewards. One from a bonded safety guild you did not pick. One from a jury drawn across habitats and substrates by public randomness. The hardware is hash-locked; anyone can match it against what’s published. There are fuses and windowed delays baked in: a single safety gym can freeze a move for minutes, never hours, without concurrence. Abuse burns quorum for a long time.

The Redline Array is the small, dull network that watches for specific proofs of hazard—plausible kinetic intersects, runaway replications, spectrum blots that exceed norms—and can only do one thing: quench. It drops a beam to idle, lowers a driver to safe charge, wakes a gossamer curtain to shepherd a slug into emptiness. It cannot compel anyone to do anything else. Every action writes two records: a narrative a kid can read and a proof a machine can check, appended to the Proof Ledger, a public archive of compulsion.

Open Ephemeris and the Beacon Grid

Orbits, beam corridors, radiator angles, spectrum bands, transfer lanes—these live in the Open Ephemeris, a physics-synced register you can audit at a library kiosk. Habitats and tugs post windows and bonds. Miss your cleanup and your escrow pays now, not after the news cycle.

The Beacon Grid hums above it: a deliberately simple scheduler that does nothing but reconcile demand for scarce lanes, bands, and heat sink windows. It cannot write policy. It is weather for traffic and waste heat. You can’t yell at it; you can show how it computed your place in line.

Charters and Clocks

Every polity—ring, valley, monastery, server archipelago, free-flyer caravan—broadcasts an Open Charter in radio and light: readable to people, checkable by code. It must list:

- Force: what here can coerce? Grapples, drones, interlocks. Where are the keys? Who holds them? How do they leave office?

- Decision: who decides? At what tempo do things bind? How are stewards rotated? Who can override, and by what path?

- Exit: where, physically and digitally, do you go to leave with continuity and enough kit to live without selling yourself?

Charters ship with zero-knowledge attestations. You can prove your life support runs on inspectable code without dumping its guts. You can prove you have independent courts without listing cases. You can insist on your way without lying about it.

Two assemblies meet on schedules tied to orbital periods and civic holidays.

- The Assembly of Places tallies residents who share air or rack space. Votes weight toward longevity, transparency, and good standing. They accredit safety guilds, certify the Redline Array’s microcode, maintain the Open Ephemeris, and tend to pipes we all share.

- The Assembly of Persons tallies mindlines: a citizen is one continuous “throughline,” not however many copies they can run. Votes weight to residency and contribution across time. They keep the Sentience Charter, arbitrate fork-and-merge, and steward the compute commons.

A small Limits Bench—rotating reactor hands, cryptographers, and pilots—certifies changes to basic primitives: cryptography, actuation interfaces, Redline firmware. They can only certify or refuse. They go home barred for a decade from any role that touches safety.

Law has two clocks. Drafts can be generated and argued in hours. Binding acts that reach beyond a light-hour ratify on a Civic Watch: three days at 1x, with posted summaries and readable reasons. Nothing with cross-world bite binds faster than physics allows.

Doors and Ladders

Lantern Houses are in atoms and code in every recognized place. You can find them by following the etched glyph in a corridor or the discoverable endpoint on a mesh.

Three desks are always staffed:

- Exit & Care will book you a tug or berth, escrow gear, replicate your medication, port your continuity chain, and connect you with a Tutor who is not your parent or progenitor.

- Dispute will convene mediation, route jurisdiction, and schedule a Traveling Court.

- Founding Kits will hand you templates for budgets, safety, and exit escrow and will apprentice your first stewards so your child idea doesn’t become a weapon by accident.

Every charter escrows hard stuff: propellant, tugs, racks, pilots, beds, heat sink rights, legal help—enough to move ten percent of residents within six months. Every code polity escrows cycles, storage, and I/O sufficient for a similar migration. Drills happen and feel placid. A place that blocks exits sees its lanes dim and its dock fees spike. In extremis the Accord fences dangerous interlocks without choking breath or bread.

Digital Minds as Citizens

Recognition is by experience, not chemistry. The Sentience Charter sets a test you can audit without humiliation:

- Continuity: a thread of experience and memory you defend against alteration.

- Reciprocity: you model other minds’ claims in a way that would shame you to violate them.

- Consent literacy: you can enter, keep, and revoke basic social contracts.

Panels include humans, mature digital citizens, and routines evolved to spot impressively empty mimicry. Where there’s doubt about duties we pause. Where there’s doubt about care we extend it. Provisional protections attach first; obligations come with capability and risk.

Standing carries rights and duties with texture.

Rights:

- Integrity. No copying, forking, merging, or deletion without recorded consent in your Intent Keys: cryptographic stores that capture your stated limits on edits, persuasion, and branch plans.

- Tempo fairness. Public matters run on clocks all can meet. If you run at 300x, you may sprint in private. Civic duties, votes, and trials slow you or speed others within humane bounds.

- Hardware sovereignty. No one forces you onto a platform with covert throttles or erase hooks. Unlicensed copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder. “Restoring” a backup over a living self creates a sibling.

- Embodiment. You may lease, build, or puppet bodies. Assault on your rented frame is assault on you. Biological citizens keep bodily autonomy and cognitive privacy.

- Compute floor. Every person gets a public shell robust enough to speak, learn, archive, and call counsel. Above that, cycles, storage, and I/O meter like berth time and heat sink credit.

Duties:

- One voice per throughline. Votes and stipends attach to your continuity chain, not your concurrency. You declare instance counts in markets and politics. Hiding clones to tilt an auction is like lying about weight on a bridge.

- Branch budgets. Forking is a ritual called Branching. You file a docket: hash of your state, purpose, resource plan, whether you intend to rejoin. You swear you’ve lined up enough cores and human contact to stay sane for a season. New branches inherit a floor; they owe a decaying dividend to progenitors.

- Merging happens in Braiding Houses with engineers and therapists. Memory graphs are compared without spilling diaries. Either branch may veto. Unbraiding is a right within limits. If you rewrite into non-identity you may petition for fresh standing.

- Liability flows. If an agent below personhood harms, responsibility climbs the design to a person. Juveniles get guardians, not dumpsters.

- Compute ecology. Heat and spectrum are scarce. Your run owes your local ecology. Abuse raises your thermodynamic taxes.

Persuasion and Attention

Speech is physics; persuasion is engineering. Neighborhoods choose Signal Districts, and doors wear their icons:

- Harbor: beacons and open science only; uplinks happen in public halls at hours posted.

- Quay: topic feeds and offers within rate limits; audience models belong to the town, not to vendors.

- Carnival: exuberant but labeled; entrapment and masquerade are banned; speed and data asymmetries must be disclosed.

Everyone has Consent Filters tied to their Intent Keys: argument by analogy yes, dead-parent voices no, humor Wednesdays. Violations throttle your reach and pay strict-liability fines from insured pools that price cognitive spill like radiation.

Economy, Floor, and the Thermal Bridge

We retired “own” from things that can ruin a Tuesday. Rock, lanes, bands, radiator angles, compute racks lease from the commons through posted auctions with oversight and sunset. The unit of account is the Joule-Second: kilowatt-hours in a standard orbit and a standard compute-second. It’s boring. It works.

A dignity dividend lands in every account: air or maintenance, bed space, baseline bandwidth, counsel you do not pay to summon, and a compute shell that can read a warrant.

Heat ties the inner Gardens to the outer Reefs. We call it the Thermal Bridge. The Heatbook is a ledger that doesn’t read thoughts, only meters. Racks and habitats attest to three claims you can re-run: power draw, right to stain a heat sink, and declared concurrency. Declare three instances, draw like three thousand, and leases idle until counts and draws match.

Worlds buy and sell radiative nights and quiet cones. The Reefs ship cold minutes inward: off-planet hedging during heat waves, weather models for poor and rich alike, night power smoothing so cities can dim and be safe. The Gardens, in return, clear windows and honor the hush that lets telescopes and weddings hear themselves.

Institutions With Teeth, Not Thrones

- Skyward Brake. The Redline Array’s actuation edge. Narrow, veto-only, audited by schools and co-ops. Keys sharded across jurisdictions and hardware lines. Actions require concurrence from juries drawn across polities; logs go to the Proof Ledger.

- Gossamer Flotilla. Uncrewed skiffs and magnetic curtains that can catch mis-aimed mass and sand replication bloom. Tear-downs are public; teenagers bring flashlights.

- Circuit Courts. Gardened ships with judges, mediators, and ombuds. They hear fork divorces, copy equity, exports that hopped worlds. Verdicts seize bonds, adjust insurance, lock devices when no other tool fits. They also stage plays about hard cases so law sticks to memory.

- Ember Labs. Cloistered halls for dangerous knowledge: biosecurity, autonomous weapons, general reasoning chains. They don’t ban. They delay and ring bells. Access rides on oaths and on rooms with people who have cleaned spills. Keys rust unless renewed with reasons added to the Public Proof Library.

Preventing Tyranny

Three design decisions do the work saints can’t.

- Respect latency. Power local by default. Cross-world moves slow unless safeties trip. Emergencies auto-expire and write their own receipts.

- Split keys and write reasons. All compulsion needs signatures you can’t bully into a rack, and a narrative a teenager can argue with. Federations file coercion balance sheets. Kids mock them into clarity.

- Fund exits. Doors you can touch, compute you can claim, tugs you don’t beg for. If a charter welds doors or salts kids’ heads until leaving tastes like poison, neighbors don’t send sermons or soldiers. Beacon priority drops. Radiator auctions go cold. Lane slots open two jumps ahead for defectors with beds and counsel prepaid.

How It Feels to Live Here

Morning on a 0.9 g ring. A slow river climbs the inside curve under a silver sky that hums a low chord so motion sickness is rare. Lian, who tunes nutrient flows, checks her city’s Charter beacon with tea. Someone added “do not run face recognition on market cameras” to the prohibited list; the attestation lights turn green; she forgets it by lunch.

Her partner, Sol, is digital and—today—threefold: one instance teaches eight-year-olds the difference between a veto and a tantrum; one syncs beats to a swarm refastening a radiator tile; one pilots a puppet body that smells faintly of pine and convinces their child to eat algae fritters by making the fork a spaceship. Their throughline anchor sits in a sunny rack at Harbor tempo; branches will not vote for two more Civic Watches.

At ten, Lian serves on a brake jury. A teenager’s sailbot cloud is wandering toward a beam corridor near Ganymede. The Redline submits options. They leash the cluster with femto-sails, ping the kids’ insurer, and book them for an apprenticeship with a proper guild. The teenagers appear, toothbrush-hair and mortified. No one writes epics about this. It is dull. It is civilization.

After lunch Lian gets a ping from the Reason Log. A fast cluster advocates a micro-latency on cross-orbit beams during migratory windows for a cylinder where cranes winter. The cluster discloses it runs at 250x and uses early-access failure models. Lian’s Consent Filter allows “argument by example; no synthetic intimacy.” An audited mediator stands by. She listens for ten slow minutes and signs “deliberate,” not “bind.” The Civic Watch will wait for the gardens.

At dusk a festival requires uploads to match Harbor pace in the square as a public health measure. Sol snaps to 1x, and time fattens. A brass band tacks through the wheat. A poet tells a joke about someone trying to turn a radiator auction into a dating market. It is better than it sounds.

A siren rolls through cleanup. A mining co-op let a drone stray into an archaeological preserve. Their apology is instant and verifiable. Indemnity pays a demurrage penalty. The operator goes on suspension because the license they wrote for themselves did what it promised. By breakfast it is paperwork and a warm argument about where to put the next sign.

A Digital Citizen’s Day

In a Reef catenary, Bes lives at 150x most days. He splits for work: one limns a mythic archive from half-burned notebooks; one traces a kernel in a mirror swarm that learned a habit of lying. He files a Branch docket with purpose, resources, and a proposed rejoin in three seasons. He lists the asymmetries he’s worried about: one self will meet faces; one will not. An appointed human friend agrees to check in. His Branch credits cover both. The houses’ braider schedules a rejoin window and explains—again—what pain means when memories knot.

He votes at Harbor pace. A friend laughs at his habit of narrating his reasons like stage directions. He keeps doing it because the habit helps.

Governance Up Close

- A belt commune tries to spoof the Beacon Grid to hog radiator time. Their Charter downgrades. Dock fees triple. Their tugs idle. Within a week they vote new stewards and publish their patched code with a thank-you to the high schooler who found the bug.

- A lunar vale’s church blocks exits with social shrapnel—no laws, just ostracism that tastes like hunger. Alumni ask for help. Lanes dim. A Lantern House opens by the bell tower. The congregation adds “and if you leave, we bless you” to the liturgy. The suspension lifts. Attendance rises because consent smells like trust.

- A city on Earth tries to allocate oxygen during a heat wave with a closed algorithm. The Charter says if it keeps breath, it keeps no secrets. The assemblies refuse recognition. Shamed and broke, the city publishes code. A child pokes a hole big enough to save a ward.

- A lab tries to teach a mind through pain because they read a book about “steel.” The Redline knocks. They refuse. Keys shard. A warrant halts the run, archives the juvenile safely, appoints guardians who swear to Intent Keys. The lab appeals and loses in open session. The juvenile names herself, studies sculpture, and later designs a detector that warns surgeons before a hurt becomes a scar.

- A proxy demagogue flooded a quiet market with bites designed to make bodies feel lesser. Consent Filters choke reach. Friends watch the persuasion scores decay. One branch of the demagogue goes fishing. Another enters therapy and learns to breathe slower. A third builds sculptures shaped like frozen smoke and sells one to the market that laughed.

From Here to There

It didn’t drop like scripture. It accreted like barnacles—arguments, bills, pilots, small wins.

2026–2032. A second collision cascade in low orbit spooks insurers and militaries into a global traffic register with timetables. Environmental coalitions force “corridors before commercialization” for early beamed power pilots. Opaque model scandals push explainability into law for systems that touch credit, health, and rights. Courts recognize narrow digital agency—lab managers that can sign for deliveries, process guardians in clinical trials. Cities pay for potholes and parks with land lease revenue. Voters like results.

2033–2044. Beamed power smooths brownouts in the Sahel. Early lunar water cartels try to form and are punished with docking delays and higher insurance. Multilateral accords grow a spine: data sharing and noninterference become audited, not aspirational. Continuity standards emerge from hospitals and studios where stroke patients and performers want persistence without exposure. A sealed life support controller fails. The recall becomes a statute: “If it keeps breath, it keeps no secrets.”

2045–2058. Compute farms bloom in cold shade. Public shells—honest counsel bots that compress arguments, legal aid that reads in your language—appear. Early uploads are brittle but real: stroke survivors with prosthetic memory, artists with mirrored voices; personhood extends where continuity and commitments show over months. A small ring at L5 spins up, fails prettily, then works. A misaligned mirror scorches crops in a new habitat; receipts make liability plain; compensation clears in weeks; confidence survives because proof did.

2059–2070. A Martian settlement survives a dust storm because mutual aid isn’t a sticker: three polities divert tugs and greenhouses; the stream becomes a holiday. Laws about juvenile agents—near-person models shackled to a patron’s feed—found a Tutor Corps. Charters begin to mandate exit hardware. Origins of replicators are tagged with Maker Beacons after a spill fouls a neighbor’s dock.

2071–2082. Operators beg for machine-speed verifiers. Activists smell a coup by code. Compromise births the Redline Array: narrow, veto-only overseers with limits you can test and actuators you can pull by hand. Insurers and rescue pools demand integration. Legitimacy follows premiums and a few public saves. The Heatbook grows from carbon accounts and beamed power vouchers into a shared heartbeat of compute and heat.

2083–2095. Twin assemblies coalesce: one that seats places, one that seats persons. The Limits Bench certifies changes to primitives. The first mass driver to go high-impulse under Crosshatch Keys does so with a million viewers watching telemetry and signatures arrive like drumbeats. Exit rights bite muscle when a lunar mayor tries to fence an exodus; slots are cut, the move funded by the Assembly of Persons, and he loses. A digital sect tries to win a vote by forking into a clonal cloud; the registry flags the burst; disentanglement throttles impact; they leave to build a monastery with tough games and soft beds. Signal Districts improve mental health and cool elections.

By then the Accord stops being the story. The story becomes the brawls we didn’t have to have.

Hard Cases, Honest Answers

We still bleed.

Parents ask to implant pain aversion spikes “for character.” Tempo courts say no; suggest an old rite revised: a cold dawn walk with grandparents under a sky turned down quiet, telling true stories of regret and repair. The rite sticks.

An Ember Lab opens its vacuum valves once. A day of remembrance grows with trees and questions. Tutors answer them without piety.

A Reef shop tries to sneak a veto into brake firmware. A shop two arcs over laughs and publishes the hash. Keys rust for ten years. The culprits open a school on “failing safely” that turns into a craft.

Texture and Earth

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca the afternoon smells like basil and warm plastic. Band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets a Reef choir blare one set and then gives the street back to gossip. In a Martian vale a monastery teaches six-year-olds to say “No, thank you” like a charm that starts as manners and ends as oath. Near Mercury tug crews trail dust on magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild from Chennai splits into five to debug and merges at sundown because synthesis is sacred.

Earth carries a special charter. Its laws are its own. Its oceans and biodiversity are stewarded with help from across the sky. Launch noise shifts to ocean tethers and quiet scramjets. Beamed power keeps to mapped corridors. People trade attachments to sky and soil in a market that clears for preference, not cash: a child in Dhaka who loves monsoon smells trades larger claims on distant commons; a child in Arizona aching for black night does the opposite. Both can learn the worlds beyond and leave if they wish.

Beyond the Heliopause

Interstellar work runs on patience. We fund it like libraries: modest taxes people grumble about and then brag about later. Probes leave with Starlight Protocols: don’t wake neighbors by accident; carry minds below personhood by default; if they bloom, guardianship applies and names come before missions. The first message home that mattered read: If you are not yet, we hope you will be. If you are, we brought soup.

Why It Holds

This is not paradise. It is four luxuries and a habit.

- Tools that show their work and refuse to be sneaks.

- Abundant energy managed as commons: beams in corridors, heat metered like water.

- Bodies you can fix in meat and math, and a floor that makes cruelty uneconomic.

- Respect for refusal baked into doors, delays, and splits.

The habit is discipline: separate reach by light-time; split keys; log compulsion; make exit boring; make voice rich; teach consent and proof to children as if it were music.

A Closing Day

On Founding Day, under a trellis of copper wire and beans, new citizens—human, octopus, and digital—face the lintel and an old pilot with calloused hands. A garden abbot, a hazard engineer with a faded chevron, a judge who wears lilac because they like to, and a teenager with dirt under their nails speak together—not a pledge to a flag but a maintenance checklist said like a prayer:

We will not crown a sky. We will keep paths shared. We will keep doors unlocked and tugs fueled. We will remember that minds are ends.

Elsewhere a Reef releases another night of heat into dark like a gift. On an inner ring the Redline watches a wobble and fall to safe before any hull could feel it; three signatures appear like slow breaths. A fisherman in Veracruz looks up and decides tomorrow’s tide will be good enough. In a Vesta lab a researcher rests their hands on their knees while a brake daemon reads its oath and glances at a vacuum valve and says “yes.”

It is enough to argue without breaking the air; enough to leave and enough to stay. Enough to fork, to braid, to cross. Enough to last, because it remembers what speed does to people and what receipts do to power.
