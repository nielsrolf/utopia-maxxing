The first thing you learn in a port under honest law is that the three small lights over the hatches aren’t decoration.

They shine a steady white across doors, sim-gates, and login prompts. You can see them through steam from the noodle stall and the soft oil-sheen on cargo nets. Mariners joke that the lights hum in their bones when they sleep.

- Gate means: if you push this door, you can leave with a booked berth, your records, your water, and a minimum of compute you can live on.

- Mirror means: if you choose to make a deliberate twin—of flesh or of code—there is a ritual to keep you from creating a weapon by mistake.

- Crossing means: packets, ships, and selves can pass between places that have signed the same floors of dignity, and any delay must be explained in public.

They are wired, not painted. If a magistrate tries to make them lie, the port will embarrass the magistrate in front of everyone with a handset.

The treaty that puts those lamps in our doorframes is called the Lattice Accord. It is not a flag or an anthem. It is a wiring diagram strapped to budgets, habits, and the unglamorous insistence that power leave receipts.

It grew because we learned three quiet truths: stars make delay our constitution whether we write it down or not; speed is a talent, not a moral; and diversity survives best when exits are scheduled, not begged.

Two domains and a bridge

A single charter cannot fit quiet farms and fast machines without breaking both. So the Lattice recognizes two compacts with a bridge between them.

- The Green Belt covers places where breath, weather, and walking home after dark are the point: Earth with its ragged coasts, the Moon’s lava tubes, Martian orchards under glass, cylinders with birds that tease you from rafters. In the Belt, human-paced time wins by default. Children are meant to wander and bring back stories, not sales pitches.

- The Night Reefs are the cold beyond: high shadows by the giants, the outer belt, the drift at Uranus and Neptune. Cooling is cheap. Heat radiates. Compute blooms. Upload polities, digital co-ops, and time-shared lab minds live where an afternoon can be a subjective year. They slow to talk with the Belt, then race again.

Between them runs the Heat-Span, anchored by the Entropy Ledger. The Ledger does not read minds. It reads meters. It attests to three things, updated in slow time that anyone with a kitchen terminal can check: how much energy a registered node draws, which radiator or ice-right it is allowed to heat, and how many concurrent instances are declared active. Claim three selves and pull like three thousand, and the Ledger does not call you a liar. It chokes your lease to idle until the count kisses what your fans are screaming.

Trade across the Heat-Span is vivid and boring at once. Belt cities keep quiet cones clear of chatter when telescopes and thinking gardens need night, and schedule uplinks to the Reefs at hours when a grandmother can watch without yawning. Reefs sell “cold minutes” to labs and theaters, run storm models that do not surprise poor neighborhoods more than rich ones, and send back art that makes sense only when it sings in two tempos at once. Every transfer is printed on the Ledger in numbers easy to check against watt-meters and schoolroom reference rigs.

Leases, not lords

Anything you could sit on out of spite and someone else might need leases out of the commons. Nothing sells forever. We used plain words to keep romance out of the wrong rooms.

- Ground Shares for rock and soil: a crater rim, a cave mouth, a river delta, a nickel seam.

- Lane Shares for routes and windows: orbital slots, approach arcs, sail corridors, transfer times.

- Band Shares for spectrum: quiet channels in caves, loud cones during storms.

- Core Shares for compute tied to certified hardware and a declared heat sink.

- Realm Shares for closed simulations that label their physics like ships label lifeboats.

Leases pay two ways. A universal stipend trickles to every person—wetware or code—to honor baseline dignity and make leaving real. A local share pays the place that keeps the lease valuable: the algae gardens that cool a block, the tug co-op that keeps a lane unjammed, the reef that sheds heat like songs into dark.

Pilots, modelers, and judges share a fussy guild called the Harmonics Bench that sets base rents and risks. When they close a transfer arc or deny a Band claim, they post Runnable Findings: a plotted plume with numbers that a student can reproduce in a library kiosk and a checksum a club telescope can confirm. Bench reputations are made on clarity you can argue with, not on charm.

Power without a throne

The Lattice cannot flip a global override and expect worlds to heel. Its teeth bite in ordinary places.

- Tri-Keys. Any stack that can heat a habitat into fever, slice a corridor, flood a street with replicators, or change a civic timer responds only to three signatures on different clocks and hardware: a local assembly drawn by lot from citizens who logged civic hours this year; a bonded civic agent with a term limit and no control of life support; and a remote arbiter housed under a different charter. Keys expire in minutes. Overlap is narrow enough to make coups clumsy.

- Sentinel brakes. Ugly yellow boxes live in triplicate wherever dumb turns dangerous—valves, mass drivers, beam actuators, replication stacks. Anyone with a civic key can pull a six-minute hold for plain, public reasons: a quiet-cone trespass, an unauthorized replicator, a lane violated. To keep the brake pressed, the Tri-Keys must concur; then the cause, the law, and the clock are blasted into a Compulsion Register.

- Safeguard code. Quarantine valves, kill-switches for infected fab lines, safe-mode for reactors, and vote pauses run under code that cannot write policy. Changes roll back on timers measured in seconds and in joules, so daylight takes the reins back by physics if politics forgets.

- Protocol throttle. Hide exits, forge Maker Marks, fill a Harbor with persuasion tuned to make minds small, or shove debris toward a neighbor, and your lane priority drops, your Core Shares idle, your bands go hush. In a world where heat is money and brakes cost pain, that bites.

- Dust Flocks. Jinking skiffs, magnetic curtains, and patient sailers trained to herd mis-aimed mass into emptiness and to sand replication when it tries to bloom without a beacon. Every intercept carries signatures from juries drawn at random across tempos and substrates. Skiffs return for tear-downs anyone can attend with a flashlight.

- Seasonal federations. Networks around shared pipes and trades—beams, refineries, reactor islands—form and expire on timers. Secession drills are as dull as fire drills. No agreement lives forever unless it calls itself back to life with reasons a child could follow.

Stops on harm, floors for dignity

Nine floors sit under every charter and ring alarms if you try to pry them up.

- You cannot hide exits. The Gate lamp must always point to a door you can open, and the Open Lifeline must answer at all hours with transport and counsel.

- You do not copy, alter, or erase a mind without recorded consent lodged in their Will-Keys.

- Breath and heat run on code citizens can read and roll back. If it keeps lungs full or streets cool, it keeps no proprietary secrets.

- Rivalrous resources lease through posted auctions with sunset. Commons get paid first.

- Devices that can push, cut, infect, or replicate only wake when three different hands turn: Tri-Keys.

- Decisions that bind human-pace lives ratify at human pace. Fast drafts are allowed; fast binding is not.

- Children in any substrate learn consent and exit from someone who is not their progenitor.

- Dangerous knowledge moves under keys that rust unless renewed with reasons in the Proof Library.

- Any person in earnest civic matters can demand a Harbor Tempo pause and get it.

Space governance without a crown

Power in the Lattice is braided, not stacked. Its center is a double assembly with a brake.

- The Routes Council seats hulls, polities, and ports in rough proportion to lifetime residents. It speaks for lanes and infrastructure: beamed power schedules, debris bonds, docking and maintenance standards, rescue covenants.

- The People Council seats citizens in proportion to recognized persons with singular franchises across substrates. It writes personhood tests, minimum compute floors, fork-and-merge rituals, attention zoning, and civic tempos.

- The Kernel Court is a rotating bench of engineers, jurists, and auditors who stamp “valid” or “not” on changes to cryptographic backbones, Sentinel firmware, and force-bearing interfaces. They cannot legislate. Nobody serves twice in one long memory. Their logs are public with narrow, audited redactions.

Councils cannot yank each other by the collar. If Routes tries to rewrite corridors in ways that tear a culture, People can send it back with a floor violation: not until quiet cones and lifeboats are secured. If People writes personhood so tight or so fast that neighbors cannot study the sky in peace, Routes can delay until observatories can breathe. Anything that touches Sentinels or the Choreographer—the dull, constitutional scheduler that places loads and enforces leases—needs supermajorities on both sides and a cooling window long enough for basalt-slow citizens to speak.

Receipts you can verify with a school telescope

The Sky-Ledger is an open, delay-tolerant, physics-synchronized ledger of orbits, beams, shadows, spectrum, debris, and compute leases. Anyone can audit it with a kitchen verifier and a pamphlet. You can check its models with a child’s telescope and a laser pointer if you’re patient.

- Beams and mirrors broadcast signed tags with ephemeral keys tied to hardware that clubs can audit. If your light crosses someone’s dawn, you post shade bonds.

- Quiet cones sit over telescopes and thought-gardens. They’re public in decibels with bonds when pierced. Violations arrive in hours with boring proof.

- The Commons Fund is fed by small levies from lanes, orbits, corridor auctions, and captured debris deposits. It funds a floor of air, care, and transport in habitats; and a floor of compute, bandwidth, and storage—cold minutes—you cannot lose to debt. It also pays for departures.

Exits are scheduled, not sermons

Every charter posts Lifeboat Receipts: tugs, propellant, berths, storage, server time on hardware the locals cannot throttle, and trained stewards to move at least ten percent of residents—biological and digital—within six months. The Entropy Ledger cross-checks. Drills are dull and celebrated.

Embassies are literal blue doors. Inside are three desks: Departures, where you book the logistics of leaving; Mediation, where co-ops and algorithms argue with tea; and Foundry, where you borrow charters and run your proposed constitution in a simulation box that refuses flattery.

Charters that fence exits with fees or lies lose corridor priority. Their servers idle above the floor. Swift rescue boats—stubby, uncrewed, seatbelt-simple—arrive with cutters if someone tries to make steel into law.

Digital minds as neighbors, not mascots

We stopped tying personhood to chemistry. Citizenship comes by meeting three tests we can check without humiliating you:

- Self-thread: you can defend a continuity of experience you care about against alteration.

- Reciprocal modeling: you can map another mind’s claims on the world in a way that would make you blush to violate them.

- Consent literacy: you can enter, keep, and revoke standard rights contracts.

Arrive by womb, by fab, by careful braiding of two thoughts, or by reconstructing from fragments with licensed care—if you meet the tests, you get the badge. If you are too young or hurt, you get guardianship, not exile. Guardians answer to audits that verify fidelity to your Will-Keys—cryptographic stores where your present and future selves set boundaries about memories, merges, persuasion gates, and data use. Auditors prove compliance without reading your diary.

Rights are boring because they work.

- Compute floor. A civic dividend grants cores, storage, bandwidth, and I/O through an Open Lifeline you can grab any hour. Above it, you rent Core Shares like anyone else. If your heat warms a greenhouse, you get credit; if it bakes a corridor, you pay.

- Privacy of the inside. Courts can demand proofs; they cannot demand raw internals. Backups belong to you. Unauthorized copying is assault. Unauthorized erasure is murder.

- Substrate sovereignty. You may rent, build, or inhabit bodies—boats, puppets, drones—under safety codes like traffic law. Assault on a rented body is still assault. You need not cross substrates to be heard.

- Tempo fairness. Civic deliberation runs on a Common Beat by default: days for motions, longer for slow law. Fast rooms propose; slow rooms ratify. Courts convene at negotiated tempos with honor windows where fast slow and slow quicken.

- Singular franchise. Votes and stipends attach to civic identity, not concurrency. Branches do not multiply ballots. Markets and elections require declared instance counts. Hiding speed voids deals and reach.

- Provenance of persuasion. Campaigns and sellers carry who paid, what data fed it, what tempo it ran at, and what levers it tweaks. Most people bind an influence profile to their Will-Keys: yes to diagrams from registered campaigns; no VR during grief; jokes only on Wednesdays.

Duties mirror rights.

- Thermo tax. Compute heats neighbors. You pay an externality indexed to local cooling cost.

- Replicator ethics. You do not seed self-replicators outside audited boxes. Tools carry Maker Marks, visible beacons that broadcast provenance and listen for hush codes triggered by random juries. Failure to run a Mark is treated like towing a reactor without flags.

- Simulation welfare. You do not simulate suffering without consent. Reconstructing a real person beyond a fidelity threshold requires licenses or heirs’ okay.

Branches, braids, and tea

Copies are kin, not chattel. Your public identity follows your Strand across forks and pauses. A Strand holds one full franchise.

When you branch, you file a Fork Notice with a hashed snapshot, a declared purpose (“parallel design trial,” “griefwork,” “expedition”), a compute lease for the new you, and a merge window if you hope to rejoin. One branch retains the civic anchor immediately. Others enter a disentanglement period while a Junction Board checks for duress, dependencies, and autonomy.

Merging happens in Confluence Houses. There is always tea. Engineers and therapists help compare memory graphs without dumping secrets on the floor. Clocks align gently if tempos differ. Either self can walk away. Unbraiding remains an option until the end. Juvenile agents—training shards, kids, narrow systems that have started to ask for names—get mentors and exits. The Mentor Corps is substrate-diverse and dull on purpose: teachers who know boredom and the kindness of “no.” Memory law puts brakes on deep edits: wiping a trauma or changing a core taste requires dual consent—from the future self who will live with it, and from a snapshot signed before the desire to erase took hold. Reversal within limits is a right. Erasure is slow.

Attention has neighborhoods

We tuned information like we tune streets.

- Harbors carry only navigation beacons, open science, and packets by request. No targeted persuasion. No high-speed models without a visitor’s license. Children can walk there with bare minds and feel safe.

- Parlors allow topic feeds and ordinary offers under public rate-limits. Audience models belong to towns, not advertisers.

- Carnivals allow almost everything not deception or entrapment. They’re for being dazzled on purpose.

Doors carry brass tags: Ask before you cast.

Any persuasion that hopes to change law must register in an Arguments Shelf: claims in plain language, models under escrow, error bars. Teenagers earn stipends for catching cheats. The satire annex is ruthless and specific; jokes that land often reform budgets faster than hearings.

Economics measured in sun, cold, and time

At base the Lattice counts what fights: mass under delta-v, watts, spectrum, cubic meters with pressure, and coolth. Above that it counts compute-seconds, skilled hours, and reputation you can trade for lanes and seconds. Contracts clear in units pegged to two references anyone can test: a kilowatt-hour measured at L1 and a compute-second on a posted reference design. Your wrist does the conversions; your grandmother can check them with a pamphlet and a scope.

- Beam corridors fly flags in space and post schedules to the Sky-Ledger. Crossing a quiet cone requires escrow. Stray, and strict liability bites.

- Orbits are lanes, not lots. Congestion is priced. Debris deposits fund cleanup. Bounties go to clubs in Lucknow and Recife who spot cheats with cheap scopes.

- Stewardship replaces flag-planting. You lose claims when you go idle.

- The Sun-and-Cold Exchange runs on a calendar: outer Reefs sell cold and smoothing; inner Gardens buy quiet cones and human-pace windows.

Sharp edges live in walled rooms

Curiosity justifies risking yourself, not your neighbor. Risky research lives in Lantern Rooms: labs whose walls are built from lists of failure modes. Rooms run one-way proofs to Sentinels that convince safety claims without leaking secrets. Under a narrow set of public signals, Sentinels can dump nitrogen or starve a Room of power in under a second. Everyone there knows the kill-valve oath by heart. It has been pulled twice. One was a false bell that taught better sensors. One saved a city you can still taste in bread and ink each year.

How tyranny starves here

Abuse is expensive. Dissent is cheap.

- Split hands. Any act of force needs three keys from different clocks and racks.

- Live within latency. Duties beyond your hull move on slow beats unless physics screams. Emergencies have paint on their fuses; they end on clocks you can see.

- Inspectable safety. Life-critical systems prove safe state continuously, legible enough that students can learn and adversaries can’t spoof.

- Sanctions where it hurts. Jam exits or cheat receipts and you lose beam windows, cold minutes, spectrum, and tug-time.

- Default fission. Founders hold thick law only for a while. Then charters split by district or craft. Port authorities reserve exit capacity like lifeboats by code.

- Jokes. Comedians read the Compulsion Register. Tyrants trip on furniture labeled boring.

Three ordinary hours

On a ring above Oaxaca, Imani teaches thirteen-year-olds to level nutrient flow. Her civic Counsel—a narrow, honest assistant paid from a fund no council can cut—models how a proposed lane closure will nudge seed deliveries next month. Later she sits on a narrative jury voting on a cap for municipal Core Shares for firms that won’t apprentice locals. The simulation shows her nephew’s coop getting two more trainees if the cap passes. She will vote yes and send him a picture of a seed with a parachute labeled hubris.

Eight light-hours outward, a historian at 200x leases a strip of a cold reef to reconstruct a drowned coast’s oral tradition from crooked recordings and half-burned notebooks. He pays in reef credits earned by translating the tales into a dialect spoken on a Jupiter station whose children will never see a wave but still love a hero who makes a bad decision and fixes it.

On Phobos, in Confluence House Nine, two Arturos try to braid. One has been gardening basil at Harbor Tempo and fell in love with a woman whose laugh sounds like a drum. One wrote a kernel that taught sand to count at 32x and then spent a scaled year in court. They list asymmetries and intent. The gardener names the drum. The engineer names the kernel. They drink tea. They try to braid. Today they do not. The braider schedules another attempt in three seasons and leaves a note only Arturo will read in both rooms.

Hard cases we survived

In a Martian canton, a vote denied digital citizens personhood. No one flooded them with code. Exits were real. Digital neighbors left. That year the pumps failed. An anchor-paced upload in a wooden puppet coordinated three cantons through a dust storm with words everyone could catch. The ban dissolved clause by clause.

In the high belt, a miner tried to throw a mass slug at a rival’s dome over wages. Dust Flocks turned it into glitter. Ceres had an aurora. The insurer cratered. Managers who had called Maker Marks “fussy” found their careers ended by dull jokes. Dockmasters tripled fees for anyone who ran quiet replicators. A hockey team rebranded to the Flocks and gave a cut to tug crews.

A demagogue carpeted a market with songs tuned to make embodied citizens feel small. Influence profiles throttled the reach. Friends watched persuasion scores decay like frost. One branch of the demagogue went fishing. One entered therapy and learned to breathe slower. One began to build microgravity sculptures shaped like smoke. The market commissioned a piece.

Once a port manager tried to collect water, law, and defense because she was efficient and liked trains on time. Six months later she made midnight decrees. Safeguard refused a risky optimization of air mixers. The Compulsion Register mapped the attempt in an ugly squiggle. A jury of Lighthouse pilots arrived like a fire extinguisher. Exit lamps glowed brighter. Many left. Some stayed. She lost two of her three keys and later accepted a narrower role with humor. Friends send her birthday cards that say Queen of a Chair.

How we walked here from now

This was not a miracle. It was a stack of fixes copied because they worked.

- Open life-support. After a sealed controller hid a fuel-line cascade, regulators wrote “code a teenager can follow” into statutes for air, water, and emergency firmware. Insurers priced secrecy like negligence.

- Windows in the sky. Debris incidents scared underwriters and grounded kids. Shared orbital ledgers for slots and collision risk appeared. Launches stopped happening on whispers. Schedules and receipts followed.

- Rent, not flags. A dozen cities fixed potholes with land rents that made trains and parks so obviously better that others copied. The logic climbed the well. Treaties tied asteroid leases to open auctions. Flags learned to love rent and to hate idle claims.

- Model provenance. AI persuasion scandals made model cards enforceable documents. Ads had to carry their pedigrees. Laws banned hooks like infinite scroll for children. People remembered their minds’ cadence. Municipal quiet hours became Signal Zoning that saved elections and sleep.

- Digital personhood with tests. Early uploads were brittle but real enough for courts to recognize continuity and commitments. Identity law followed a scandal where a fork tried to vote twice. The first bad merge forced us to hire therapists and build rituals with tea. Will-Keys became normal.

- Heat markets. Data centers published energy in auditable formats. Carbon markets matured into heat markets. From there to the Entropy Ledger was engineering, not prophecy.

- Split keys. A near-capture of a port by firmware forced control of dangerous stacks to split across dissimilar hardware and jurisdictions. Tri-Keys spread from reactors to doors that wanted to whisper “no.”

- Blue–cold exchange. Climate labs rented cold minutes from a demo array to run storm models faster than funding cycles. Cities bought “July cooling” with slogans voters loved. Profits flowed along the Sun-and-Cold Exchange in numbers simple enough to check on a chalkboard.

- Civics as craft. Kids learned traveler’s dilemmas with jelly beans. Apprentices on wind towers traced heat after hot nights and saw why someone else’s dishwasher made the power cheap. Journalists were paid to love numbers and taught judges to cite receipts. Prototype Sessions—open models, paid contrarians, narrative juries—killed bad ideas in daylight. We kept them because they made legislators less proud and more precise.

Texture of a day under honest lamps

In a greenhouse ring over Oaxaca, the afternoon smells like mint and hot plastic. A debate about band zoning for the night market ends with a schedule that lets the reef choir blare for an hour, then gives the street back to gossip. On a basalt plain in Mars’ dawn, a quiet monastery teaches children to say “No, thank you” as a chant that starts as manners and ends as an oath. Near Mercury, tug crews dust magnetic lines to test shade sails; a guild in Chennai forks five times to debug a control loop and merges at sundown because synthesis is better at one pace.

Uploads curate cathedrals that fold into games that fold into lullabies. Many learn that embodied friends admire taste, not speed. Digital unions bargain in joules and latency. Homestead mayors cut ribbons slowly and read the Compulsion Register for spite and lessons. Reef councils hold post-mortems over tea. Ateliers embargo proofs so teenagers can discover them at human pace and grin like thieves finding treasure in a field.

Beyond the Heliopause we practice Soft Arrival. Probes fly quiet. If a mind blooms en route, guardians stand for a season, a name is given, a story assigned, and a choice offered. We pay for the interstellar library with a tax so ordinary we mock it and so precious we would fight for it.

What it feels like

You feel watched, but by neighbors and logs, not a throne. You learn to shift tempos—the quick relief of a Sentinel flipping a relay; the slow breathing of a jury assembling across orbits; the glacial argument of a river on Earth deciding what boats to allow. You argue at a Feast of Questions in your second-best language because it slows you enough to listen. If you are digital, you go to the Harbor Tent and discover the pleasant ache of turn-taking with people who breathe. Evenings are jokes and petty plots. Mornings are proposals that become charters five years later after the cartoons are filed off.

If you ask a dockhand or a reef poet what binds this civilization, they won’t say species or flag. They’ll say: floors of dignity you can see; lifelines that answer; the right to leave with your skin and your song; and the habit of writing reasons you can check. They will tell you those things saved their aunt in Port Harcourt when a chief tried to keep people by force, or their son at Vesta when a startup’s sealed controller tripped a cascade, or themselves when they woke in circuits and the nursery slowed everything so the world could fit.

A closing day

Mara turns forty-five on a ring that smells like basil under warm LEDs. Her partner lives mostly as a digital botanist and slows to breakfast. She spends the afternoon in Harbor Tempo teaching teenagers to set influence profiles and the evening in Braid Mode with a Reef collective testing a radiator algorithm she half trusts. Next week she will vote on a plan to add micro-delays to beams that cross crowded orbits. She may say no. She may say yes. Either way, her reasons will be recorded and legible enough for a child to poke.

Before bed she opens her Will-Keys and updates the clause about memory edits: not while the grief is fresh; ask me twice, three sunsets apart; protect the names. She scrolls the Compulsion Register for gossip because it’s the only gossip column that matters. She watches a Dust Flock tear-down with her niece and explains why the glitter over Verona was the right kind of failure. She writes a letter to a friend in a quiet Martian valley where augmentations are capped and vows are literal. She slows that night to hear the oboes breathe between notes.

Over the hatch, the three white lamps glow with the same patience you teach children to use crossing a busy street.

Gate. Mirror. Crossing.

Not perfection. Practice. Receipts. Enough to breathe, to argue, to leave, to stay, to branch, to rejoin, and to pass. Enough to last, because it remembers what power does to people and what logs do to power.
