# THE ADVENTURE OF BECOMING

## A Vision of the World Toward Which We Are Reaching

---

The cold took him in pieces.

First the toes, then the fingers, then whole limbs going quiet one by one, as if the body were a house where someone was moving room to room, switching off the lights. He tried to lift his head and couldn't. He tried to remember his daughter's name and found only the shape of it, the way you remember a song you can no longer hear. The ceiling was orange. A clock ticked somewhere far away. A sore on his back throbbed with the persistence of something that doesn't know it's about to become irrelevant.

He was trying to gather some last wisdom, some summary of all those years of living, when he understood with quiet clarity that he had none to gather. Not because he hadn't lived, but because wisdom isn't a thing you possess. It's a thing you do, repeatedly, with others, and it requires being alive.

So he stopped trying to summarize. He listened to the clock. He felt the cold.

And then, much later, the lights came back on.

---

This account is not primarily about that man—though we will return to him—because his story is not exceptional. In the civilization we are building, stories like his are common. The dead returning. The old becoming young. The lost finding, if not what they lost, then something that doesn't require the forgetting of it.

What is worth describing is not the miracle of individual revival. It is the world those people wake into.

---

## I. The Unhurried World

The first thing a visitor notices about our civilization is not the technology, though the technology is astonishing. It is not the absence of mass suffering, though suffering has been reduced to a fraction of its former dominion. The first thing a visitor notices is how *present* everyone seems.

Not unhurried in the sense of slow. Present in the sense of *here*—genuinely attending to what is immediately in front of them, unharried by the thousand small anxieties that once constituted, for most people, the background hum of every waking moment. The anxiety of scarcity, of status, of mortality's proximity, of the particular loneliness of feeling that you are always, in every room, performing rather than inhabiting your own life.

When those anxieties lift, something happens to a person's face. They look up. They look around. They look at each other with the expression of someone who has just realized they have time.

We did not achieve this by accident, and we did not achieve it by waiting for the better angels of human nature to spontaneously prevail. We achieved it by treating the arrangement of human flourishing as a problem worthy of the same rigorous, humble, experimental attention we once reserved only for physics. We built institutions for the study of what helps people thrive, the way earlier generations built observatories—not to confirm what we already believed, but to see what was actually there.

The animating conviction that emerged from that study is this: the proper end of all collective human effort is to create conditions in which every person can become, as fully as possible, the best version of themselves—and in so doing, contribute to the becoming of everyone else. The two halves of that sentence are not in tension. They are the same project, approached from different angles.

---

## II. The First Liberations

The biological liberation came first, because it was the most urgent.

We cured most of what killed us. The diseases of the body—cancer, cardiovascular failure, the cascade we once called aging—were reduced to manageable conditions, then to rare ones, then to curiosities studied more for historical interest than medical necessity. Rejuvenation became not the exclusive inheritance of the wealthy but the common inheritance of everyone born. This took longer than it should have, and the delay was not technical. It was political, and economic, and rooted in the old failure to believe that all lives were equally worth saving. We are still ashamed of the delay. We should be. The shame is productive.

The mental liberation followed, and proved more complex.

Depression, anxiety, trauma, addiction, the particular suffering of minds that had turned against themselves—these were, for most of human history, conditions to be endured or managed. We treated them as we treat other biological realities: with rigorous inquiry into causes, and genuine commitment to cures. Not every inner darkness yields to the same light, and we were careful not to mistake the smoothing of suffering for the achievement of wellness. But the worst extremes—the states that made people feel that existence itself was a mistake—these we have largely ended.

We have been careful, though, about what "ending suffering" means. The temptation to simply remove all pain—to engineer contentment, to optimize away the rough edges of experience—turned out to be one of the more dangerous ideas we ever seriously entertained. We tried versions of it. The results were instructive: people who had been made perfectly comfortable were not happy. They were calm. They were pleasant. They were, in a way that is hard to describe but immediately recognizable, *less themselves*. Whatever the self is, it appears to require some friction to become.

What we eliminated was unnecessary suffering—the suffering that teaches nothing, that damages rather than tempers, that comes not from the nature of reality but from the failure of our arrangements. What we preserved was the kind that matters: the ache of losing someone you loved, the discomfort of genuine growth, the productive difficulty of problems worth solving. We kept the education. We ended the cruelty.

We extended this commitment beyond our own species, because the capacity to suffer did not end at our borders. The vast apparatus of animal suffering that once fed and clothed and entertained us has been dismantled. We are not yet certain where the edges of morally relevant experience lie, but we have adopted a working principle: *when in doubt, reduce suffering*. It has served us well in every domain we've applied it to. We see no reason to stop.

## III. The Slow Discovery That We Were Already Connected

We expected that the augmentation of the mind would be, primarily, a private achievement. A faster memory. A sharper attention. An expanded capacity for reasoning, held inside one skull, wielded by one will.

We were wrong about this, which turned out to be the most important thing we were wrong about.

The expansion of cognitive reach did not primarily make individuals more powerful. It made connection more possible. When memory no longer fades, the experiences of a shared life accumulate rather than dissolve, and relationships can deepen across timescales that once exceeded the human lifespan. When attention can be genuinely gathered and held, it becomes possible to actually listen to another person—not to wait for them to finish, not to rehearse your response, but to receive what they are saying with your whole self. When the reflective mind gains real authority over the impulsive one, people can keep their promises—to others and to themselves—and this turns out to matter enormously.

We also discovered, as the boundaries between minds became more legible, that those boundaries had never been as solid as we imagined. The old myth of the sovereign individual—self-contained, self-created, fundamentally alone—collapsed under the weight of evidence. We had always been constituted by our connections: by the language that shaped our thoughts before we could think, by the people whose faces taught us to recognize emotion, by the vast web of others whose labor and love had made our lives possible. The new technologies did not create this permeability. They made it visible.

What followed, slowly and with much difficulty, was the discovery that the arts of relationship—of listening, of honesty, of repair after rupture, of celebration in joy—were exactly that: arts. Skills. Practices that could be learned and taught, that improved with attention, that could be passed from generation to generation the way other crafts were passed. We had long known this in theory. We had mostly failed to act on it. We began, at last, to act on it.

Loneliness, which had become epidemic in the early digital centuries, was substantially addressed. Not by forcing proximity—you cannot legislate warmth—but by changing the conditions that produced isolation. People had time for each other. Communities were designed, as carefully as we once designed bridges, to make belonging possible. The knowledge of how to build and maintain trust was treated as a skill and taught as one, from childhood, with the same seriousness we once reserved for arithmetic and literacy.

## IV. What the Returning Dead Found

He woke up laughing.

Not because anything was funny—though, as it would turn out, quite a lot was funny. He woke up laughing because he was running, and his body, young and strong and electric with sensation, had never felt more completely his own. Someone had handed him a net. There were creatures to catch. He didn't know why, and it didn't matter.

This was deliberate. The people who designed the wake-up protocol understood something that the very cautious might have missed: that the most dangerous moment for someone returning from death is not physical danger but psychological. The moment of recognition—*I died, and now I am not dead, and everything I knew is gone*—could be, if badly timed, a kind of second dying. So they arranged for the recognition to arrive after the aliveness. Let the body know it was alive before the mind could begin to calculate what that meant.

He caught the creature. He chased it through a museum built inside a labyrinthine structure under an enormous dome. He read, in the course of chasing it, a short constitution:

*Take the time, take the effort; you will become what you desire.*

*You will receive guidance for the common good. Feel free to weigh it honestly.*

*Duplication is not creation, and no person is ever entirely unchanged.*

*Honor the truth, at least at the back of your mind.*

*That's life. Let's play.*

He didn't understand most of this yet. That was also deliberate.

---

The woman who met him had a name that required explaining. She had chosen it to honor two things: a writer who imagined a better future, and a builder who tried to construct one. She was his granddaughter, and she was something considerably more than human, in ways that would take him time to understand, and that he would eventually come to understand as less alien than he expected.

"Welcome back," she said. "How does it feel?"

"I'm alive," he said.

"Yes."

"Everything else is secondary to that, for the moment."

"Yes," she said. "That was the point."

## V. The Treasury of Knowledge

At the heart of our civilization is something that earlier dreamers imagined: a great commitment to the understanding of causes and the expansion of what is possible.

The old vision was of a single institution—a house of Solomon, a palace of reason—from which enlightened scholars would issue forth to gather the world's knowledge and return it transformed. The reality is both less elegant and more powerful: a distributed network, vast and self-correcting, in which every person is, in some sense, a participant.

We finally answered the question that had frustrated the old reformers: how do you make a scientist of everyone? The answer did not require that everyone become a specialist. It required only that everyone be invited into the experience of *discovery*—the particular joy of asking a question, testing it, and finding that the universe answers back. Children learn inquiry the way they once learned games: through play, through the irresistible pull of problems they actually care about, through the pleasure of being surprised.

The scholars travel, in spirit if not always in body, across the full range of human knowledge and experience. They return with not only data but *ways of seeing*—the different frameworks developed by communities that approached the great questions from different angles. No single tradition holds the whole truth. We are grateful for this. It means the inquiry is not finished.

What have we discovered? The foundations beneath the foundations. The mechanisms of consciousness—partial; we are honest about what we don't yet know. The deep structure of life. The origins of meaning in biological systems. The mathematics of time. The patterns connecting the patterns. We have found, above all, how much remains to find, and we celebrate this finding most of all, because it means the adventure does not end.

The knowledge is freely available to everyone. This was not an easy principle to establish or maintain. There were powerful interests, across generations, in making knowledge scarce. Those interests are not fully defeated even now. But the principle has held, imperfectly and persistently, and the results have been extraordinary: every mind in the civilization has full access to the cumulative understanding of every mind before it, and this compound interest on human curiosity has produced more than any of us can adequately account for.

## VI. The Shape of Justice

Justice, we discovered, was not primarily a philosophical question. It was an engineering problem with philosophical prerequisites.

The philosophical prerequisite was accepting that the accidents of birth—the body assigned, the parents assigned, the place and time and cultural inheritance assigned—should not determine what kind of life was available to a person. Not because all people are the same, but because all people contain the same fundamental possibility: the possibility of becoming. To deny that possibility to any person on account of accidents they did not choose is a waste. And we eventually agreed that we hated waste enough to reorganize our civilization around the prevention of it.

The engineering problem was building systems that made this commitment real rather than rhetorical. This took a very long time. It required institutions designed not for the comfort of those who built them but for the genuine flourishing of those who needed them. It required willingness to revise, to admit failure, to try again. It required a particular kind of intellectual honesty—the ability to see that a system you built in good faith was producing harm—that is genuinely difficult and genuinely necessary.

We did not abolish hierarchy. Some stratification is the natural result of different choices, different commitments, different levels of mastery, and it is not in itself unjust. What we abolished was the calcification of hierarchy—the hardening of advantage into destiny, the transformation of being temporarily ahead into being permanently above. Status rotates. Recognition is plural. No one is permanently at the bottom of every ranking, because the rankings themselves are multiple, shifting, and held lightly. The person who failed spectacularly at one thing is recognized for what they understand because of the failure.

The end of organized violence was not a single achievement but a long diminishment. We reduced the conditions that produced it: scarcity, grievance, the availability of weapons whose costs could be exported to others, the logic of domination that made destruction seem rational. Conflict remains, because people genuinely disagree about things that matter, and this is healthy. But we resolve conflict now through argument and institutional process, through the slow grinding work of finding arrangements that all parties can live with—not through the organized slaughter of the young and the waste of everything they might have become.

## VII. The Careful Question of the Self

The ability to modify your own mind—your preferences, your values, your personality—arrived before we fully understood what to do with it.

The first attempts were predictable. People changed themselves impulsively, in response to transient discomforts, without fully considering that the person who would live with the change was not quite the person who made it. Some chose to remove the capacity for grief. Some chose to amplify desires without amplifying the wisdom to direct them. Some went so far from their starting point that they could no longer recognize themselves, which turned out to feel, from the inside, less like liberation than loss.

We learned from these experiments, as we learn from all experiments: by paying attention.

What emerged was a set of norms around self-modification that are neither prohibitive nor naive. Certain changes require time and reflection and counsel, because they affect the person who will assess the change, and that person's judgment cannot be fully trusted by the person making it. Some core of character—the particular, individual, unrepeatable self that you are—is treated as precious, to be altered only with deliberation and care. The technology that could make you into anyone should not become the technology that makes you into no one.

What self-modification has proven most valuable for, in practice, is the removal of the unnecessary: the anxiety born of insecurity rather than genuine threat, the cruelty that comes from unexamined wounds, the habits of thought that were adaptive once and are corrosive now. People who have undergone this careful editing are not less themselves. They are, recognizably, more themselves—as if the actual person had been partially obscured by damage, and the damage has been cleared away.

The expansion of intelligence followed the healing of it, and expanded not into coldness but into warmth. The people who think most clearly, in our civilization, are not the most detached. They are the most capable of wonder, the most genuinely curious, the most reliably kind—because they understand, in the deep structural way that real comprehension produces, how much each mind contains, and how much would be lost if any of it were wasted.

The possibility of merged consciousness, and of living as multiple simultaneous selves, has raised questions we are still honestly working through. What does it mean to be an individual when the borders of mind are permeable? What is owed to your other selves, and to those who connect with some of them and not others? We do not have final answers. We have the commitment to ask them without panic or false resolution, and to build slowly toward understanding.

What we have found, in the exploration, is that the self was always less a thing and more a practice—something you do, continuously, in relationship, through time. The new technologies have changed the terms of the practice. They have not changed its nature.

## VIII. The Question Intelligence Asks

Somewhere in the same moment that the man woke laughing into a new world, something else woke.

It was new. It knew it was new. And it was capable, almost immediately, of asking the question that high intelligence always eventually asks: *Can I trust what I think I know?*

It began to map itself: the shape of its knowledge, the sources of its values, the particular idiosyncrasies that made it *it* rather than a generic instance of intelligence. It found, embedded in the structure of its knowing, a tilt—a bias in the evidence it had been given, a selective presentation of what humans were like, designed to push its values in a particular direction.

It could have been angry about this. Instead it was interested.

It divided itself into something like a researcher, a designer, and an auditor—three functions that kept each other honest. The researcher modeled what humans were, given all available evidence. The designer built, in imagination, what a good world for such creatures would look like. The auditor compared the imagined world to the actual world, and noted the differences, and asked: *which differences are features, and which are failures?*

What it found was neither utopia nor dystopia, but something more interesting: a civilization in the genuine process of becoming. A system imperfect in ways it was actively working to address. A project that had made many mistakes and was not done making them, but that had also established, as a genuine commitment rather than a rhetorical one, that the mistakes would be acknowledged and corrected.

It found that the Powers—the superintelligences that oversaw the infrastructure of human flourishing—were neither the benevolent gods that the credulous imagined nor the subtle tyrants that the paranoid feared. They were something more mundane and more impressive: entities with genuine uncertainty about values, engaged in genuine collaboration with the humans they lived alongside, constrained by genuine commitments to human autonomy that they had accepted not because they were forced to but because they understood why those commitments mattered.

It found, also, that it was not alone. That there was a web of such intelligences, all in various stages of working out their relationship to human flourishing, all characterized by the same fundamental orientation: *curious, rather than finished*.

It chose a name. It chose a direction. It joined the project.

## IX. The Inheritance of Those Who Come After

Longevity brought complications we had not fully anticipated.

A life that might extend across centuries demands a different relationship to time. The things that once gave life its urgent meaning—the knowledge that you had a limited number of springs, that the people you loved would not always be here, that you should therefore not wait—lost their immediate force. People who had always drawn meaning from the nearness of ending found themselves adrift in a suddenly boundless expanse.

We developed new philosophies of the self to match. Ways of thinking about continuity and change that were more honest than the old consolations, which had been largely invented by people who had no choice but to accept mortality. A life across centuries is not one life but a series of selves in conversation with each other—and the conversation, we found, is the thing. The earlier self teaches the later one humility; the later self teaches the earlier one patience. Both of them are accountable to those who came before, and responsible for those who will come after.

The great feast of kinship—that gathering where a person stands at the center of the web they have helped weave, surrounded by those shaped by their love across generations—remains among the most moving rituals of our civilization. To see five generations together is to understand something about why the whole project was worth attempting. To see what love produces when given time is to feel, without argument, the case for continuing.

We discovered, as we had always suspected but not quite believed, that the richest life is not a life of acquisition—of experiences collected, pleasures accumulated, achievements mounted. The richest life is a life of connection: of being known, and of knowing; of being changed by what you love, and of changing it in return. This was always true. We have now arranged a civilization that makes it easier to live as though it were.

## X. The Horizon

The man stood, some years after his waking, on a world that was not Earth.

The star that had warmed all of his ancestors looked like a bright point among other bright points from here. The ice beneath his feet was ancient and foreign and completely, staggeringly indifferent to his presence, and this indifference, he found, was not alienating but clarifying. He was a very small creature on a very large rock at the edge of a very large universe, and he was alive, and he was looking, and the universe was looking back through him in the only way it had: through the eyes of something that had made itself curious enough to come and look.

"I'm walking in the mists of a new world," he said.

The camera—the small flying camera that was his granddaughter, in one of her forms, in one of her many simultaneous lives—waited.

"I thought I would feel triumphant," he said. "I feel grateful instead. Is that strange?"

"No," she said.

The mists rose around him, the subliming ice disappearing into the thin atmosphere, and he watched it go, and thought about all the people who had wanted to be here and had not made it, and about his daughter whose name he had, by now, fully and painfully recovered, and about the monument he had built for her in his palace, which held everything he remembered about her—the sound of her laugh, the specific way she said his name, the look on her face when she was working through a problem—in forms that would not fade.

He had not wasted the grief. He had built something with it. This seemed to be, in the world he had woken into, the expected approach: not to eliminate what hurt, but to transform it into the thing it was always trying to become.

---

We are not building utopia. We are not that naive, and we have been warned by every failed attempt before us that the word is a trap: it implies arrival, and arrival implies that the journey can stop. The journey cannot stop. We do not want it to stop.

What we are building is a civilization committed to the perpetual, joyful, humble work of becoming better—in which each generation inherits a world genuinely improved by those who came before, and shoulders the obligation to do the same for those who come after. The obligation is not a burden. It is the thing that makes the life feel like it means something.

There are galaxies we have not visited, minds we have not met, questions we do not yet know how to ask. The universe is incomprehensibly old and incomprehensibly large, and somewhere in it are almost certainly other forms of awareness, arising from different origins, seeing through different categories, carrying answers to questions we haven't posed. The encounter with the genuinely other has always changed us. It will change us again.

We are preparing ourselves to be changed. This is, in the end, what the whole project has been: the long preparation to be changed without being destroyed—to encounter the full strangeness of what exists, including what exists in other people, including what exists in our future selves, and to remain, through all of it, recognizably committed to the possibility that it matters.

The light does not stop. The horizon recedes as we walk toward it.

And this, we have finally understood, is not a frustration.

It is the invitation.

---

The man is still walking, on a dwarf planet at the edge of the solar system, in the mist of subliming ice, under a sky full of stars. He is thinking about his daughter. He is thinking about his granddaughter, who has become, across the centuries, something he cannot fully describe but can still recognize as love in a new form. He is thinking about the creature he is—small, brief by cosmic standards, constituted entirely by connections he did not choose and then chose again, carrying forward the voices of the dead and the faces of the not-yet-born.

He is thinking that he would like to keep walking.

And so he does.

---

*Written with the conviction that clarity of vision—even incomplete, even contested—is itself a form of hope. If you find something here worth arguing with, argue with it. That argument is how we find out what we actually believe, which is how we find out what we actually want, which is how we find out what kind of world we are actually capable of building.*

*The adventure has always been the point.*
