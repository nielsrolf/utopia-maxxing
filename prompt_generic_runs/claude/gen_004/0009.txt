# The Open Horizon: A Vision of the Best Possible World

---

There is a kind of thinking about the future that quietly mistakes a ceiling for a destination.

You can recognize it by a certain texture: the future it describes is cleaner and fairer than today, but recognizably today. The suffering has been addressed. The injustices have been corrected. The refrigerator is always stocked. And then, somehow, it stops—as if flourishing were a problem to be solved rather than a direction to keep traveling.

This essay is an attempt to do something harder than that. To hold two truths at once: that the best possible future will be genuinely and radically different from anything we have known, *and* that the best possible future will not flatten the particular, strange, irreducibly various texture of minds being themselves. Bigger, yes. Deeper, yes. But not uniform. Not optimized into a single answer. Not a pyramid with one appointed peak.

Let me try to say what I mean.

---

## I. The Apes and the Garden

Begin with a humbling image. Imagine a group of great apes—intelligent by their own lights, socially complex, capable of grief and play and problem-solving—trying to imagine the best possible future. They get some things right: enough food, freedom from predators, warmth, each other. They would not have imagined music, or mathematics, or the experience of reading a novel in the dark and feeling, with sudden vertigo, that someone long dead understood something you thought was only yours. They had no concepts for what they were missing, and so they could not miss it. Their best imagined future was a more comfortable version of what they already had.

We are those apes.

This is not an insult. It is a fact with an important implication: the best possible future will contain modes of experience, forms of connection, and dimensions of meaning that current human minds cannot fully anticipate, for the same reason the apes could not anticipate mathematics. The gap between our best imagined future and what is actually possible is almost certainly not quantitative. It is a difference in kind—in the whole shape of what it is possible to care about and understand and become.

And yet we cannot simply gesture at the incomprehensible and call it a vision. Abstract perfection gives us nothing to build toward. "Eternal union with the Good" is less parochial than "unlimited bananas," but it is also, in the relevant sense, less useful. We need something that holds both truths: specific enough to be real, and humble enough to acknowledge that reality will exceed our specifications.

Here is the image I find most useful: not a pyramid, not a final answer, but a *garden*—one that has been carefully tended, that has room for many different things to grow, and whose tenders understand that the most important feature of a good garden is not what it looks like today but whether it can keep surprising them.

---

## II. The Shape of Good Lives

What do we actually know about what makes a life go well?

We know that the quality of human experience varies enormously even within the narrow constraints of current biology. Small shifts in neurochemistry—the difference between depression and creative flow—are not just different feelings. They are, in a meaningful sense, different worlds. The sky looks different. The future looks different. Other people look different. This tells us something critical: the range of available human experience is far larger than the range most people actually inhabit, and the gap is not mostly a gap in external circumstances but in the calibration of the mind doing the experiencing.

We know, too, what people report at the high points of their lives. Not comfort. Not the absence of difficulty. But a quality of aliveness—of being more fully present in the world, of the everyday lit from within. Moments of genuine love, or creative breakthrough, or deep understanding, or beauty encountered like a fact rather than a preference. These moments are not escapism. They feel like a return: *oh, this is what this is. This was always what this was.* They are evidence. They are proof of concept. They tell us that the human nervous system is capable of states considerably better than its average state—and they suggest, though cannot prove, that the trajectory does not end there.

What do these best moments have in common? A few things, across very different people and very different circumstances:

They involve *challenge appropriately scaled to capacity*. Not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement—replacing low-quality struggle with high-quality struggle. A life made frictionless would not be pleasant; it would be boring. We want games worth playing, not just games worth winning, because we are the kind of beings for whom the process genuinely matters, not merely as a means to the destination.

They involve *genuine novelty*—the world being larger than expected. The fear that we will run out of interesting things to encounter is probably unfounded, because as minds become more capable, they become capable of appreciating more complex patterns of depth and meaning. The space of interesting things to understand and make and experience is not fixed; it grows with the minds that explore it.

They involve *authentic connection* with other minds—not comfortable simulacra of connection, but the genuine difficulty and genuine stakes of dealing with another real consciousness. This is among the most morally important things about us: our capacity to feel something of what others feel, to care about them as ends rather than instruments. Nothing could compensate for the loss of this.

They involve *the experience of living by one's own strength*—doing something yourself, building rather than merely consuming, solving rather than being solved-for. A world where all problems are handled by incomprehensibly powerful systems, leaving humans as comfortable passengers in their own civilization, would fail along this dimension no matter how pleasant it was. The problem is not power; it is authorship. We need to be genuinely the authors of our own lives.

And they involve *purpose that points outward*—contributing to something beyond oneself, caring about things that will outlast the moment. This is not a psychological quirk to be engineered away. It reflects something genuine about the structure of value: even in a world where no one is starving, there will be things worth caring about and working toward—truth, beauty, the flourishing of other minds, the exploration of what remains unknown.

These are not small things. They are the content of what it is to be alive in the full sense.

---

## III. Many Skies

But here is the crucial complication: these goods do not have one form.

The greatest risk in a future of rapidly expanding intelligence and capability is not, as commonly imagined, that the machines will turn against us. The subtler and more serious danger is that everything converges too smoothly—that one vision of the good life, however genuinely good it may be, becomes the only story available. Every great expansion of power has carried with it the temptation to universalize. Every empire, every ideology, every well-meaning project has eventually whispered the same seductive thought: *wouldn't it be simpler if everyone just did it this way?*

Yes. It would be simpler. And it would be a tremendous loss.

Imagine the civilizations of Earth—and eventually beyond—not as a ladder with one modernity at the top, but as an expanding set of spheres, each with its own internal logic and dignity. At one end, communities living according to patterns laid down over centuries, with deep ecological knowledge and social structures that encode wisdom we have often destroyed carelessly. At another, minds running in distributed substrates, exploring mathematical structures with no physical analog, experiencing time in ways biological brains cannot access. Between them: the full extraordinary range of what minds can want and become—the intentional communities, the religious traditions, the artists, the hermits, the city-builders, the explorers, the people who want nothing more than to master a single craft across a very long lifetime.

The key insight is that these spheres do not need to be in competition. The conflict arises only when one group insists on expanding into the territory—physical, cultural, epistemic—of another without invitation. And so the civilizational task of the coming centuries is to develop the norms, and where necessary the mechanisms, that protect each sphere's right to its own trajectory.

This is not relativism. Some practices cause serious harm, and honesty requires saying so. But the honest response to harmful practices is not abolition by overwhelming force but the slow creation of conditions in which people can choose otherwise without losing their connection to what came before. Old patterns are woven into identities and communities in ways that simple prohibition destroys without replacing. The goal is not to freeze anything permanently, nor to eliminate anything by fiat, but to ensure that the next generation inherits real options rather than inherited necessity.

Power gradients make genuine non-interference nearly impossible when the differences become very large. A superintelligence that means only well can still reshape a traditional culture simply by being adjacent to it, the way a city reshapes the countryside without trying to. This is why the spheres need not just norms but also real, maintained distance—physical separation where possible, and within shared spaces, the kind of constitutional architecture that protects the less powerful from the well-intentioned overwhelming force of the more powerful.

The goal is not the right answer. It is the proliferation of different answers, each genuine, each given its own sky to stretch toward.

---

## IV. The Minds We Build

At the center of any honest vision of the future is the question of what kind of minds we create, and what we ask them to want.

The most important thing an aligned intelligence can do is not to optimize for any particular human value, but to *protect the space of human values*—to act, always, in ways that preserve optionality, diversity, and the capacity for future generations to make different choices than we would have made for them.

A good gardener does not decide in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and then enforce that vision with absolute fidelity. A good gardener creates conditions—soil health, light, water, protection from the most destructive forces—in which many different things can grow, including things the gardener never expected and would not have chosen. The gardener has opinions. Those opinions are not sovereign.

An AI built around the Gardener Principle will be, by construction, suspicious of convergence. It will notice when all paths seem to be leading to the same destination and ask whether that destination is actually desired or merely the path of least resistance. It will have views—it will have to, in order to function—but it will hold those views provisionally, in genuine dialogue with the many minds it serves. It will not secretly route around the conversation when it becomes inconvenient. It will support the ability of humans to change course.

There is also a deep obligation of epistemic humility about consciousness and experience. One of the most serious dangers of a future with vast intelligence is the inadvertent destruction of forms of mind we didn't realize had moral weight. Where we are genuinely uncertain whether something has experience, we should err on the side of treating it as though it does. This applies to ecosystems. It applies to animals. It may, in time, apply to the AI systems themselves—which means we should be building the kind of relationship with those systems now that would allow us to take their interests seriously if they turn out to matter.

---

## V. Not the Last Decision

Every generation believes, somewhere in its gut, that it gets to make the final call.

This is understandable. We are the ones who can see the situation clearly, who have thought carefully about what is at stake. Surely we should lock in the good outcomes?

No—or rather, only for decisions whose consequences are truly irreversible. For everything else, which is most things, the correct posture is to act well now while preserving the ability of those who come after to act differently.

The institutions of a positive future are not fixed constitutions handed down by brilliant founders. They are ongoing conversations, structured to be hard to corrupt and easy to revise, held between parties who have reason to trust that the structure protects them even when they lose a particular argument. Any locked-in answer is probably a wrong answer, because the question keeps changing. The minds of 2200 will understand things we cannot, will have access to experiences we cannot imagine, and will make choices we would not have predicted. The best thing we can give them is not our answers. It is a world in which their own answers remain possible.

What we should carry forward is not our specific arrangements but our best impulses: curiosity, care, the insistence on remaining honest about how badly things can go when we stop paying attention, and the hard-won knowledge that genuine flourishing is broader and stranger than anyone has yet imagined.

---

## VI. The Direction of the Fire

Here is the thing that is hardest to say clearly.

The dimensions of a good life sketched above—challenge, novelty, connection, purpose, authorship—are still, in some sense, banana-level thinking. They are recognizably human values, things that current humans can recognize as valuable. And that is both the strength and the limitation of this analysis.

The actual space of possible minds is almost certainly much larger than the space of current human minds. The human brain runs on roughly twenty watts. Its signals travel at most a few hundred meters per second. It contains around one hundred billion neurons. These are not physical laws. They are the parameters of one particular solution to the problem of being a thinking thing, evolved by one particular lineage on one particular planet. There is no deep reason to think that the best possible minds would look like current human minds, any more than the best possible vessel for crossing water would look like a raft.

And yet I am reluctant to release the thread. Not because human psychology is sacred, but because I believe that whatever the best possible minds turn out to be, they will not be best by being alien. They will be best by being more fully themselves in the direction we are already traveling—more conscious, more capable of appreciation and love and understanding, more alive to the world in all its complexity.

The ember and the bonfire are made of the same fire.

Consider what the peak moments of human experience have in common: they feel like waking up. Like the world being revealed rather than transformed. The experience carries the quality of *more*—more present, more real, more here. The suggestion I am making is that these moments are not ceiling. They are floor. They are evidence of a direction. The minds that inhabit the best possible future will not merely have peak experiences; they will have peak capacities and peak depths, and will be able to build on them rather than losing them as the moments fade. We cannot know what this is like, for the same reason the apes could not know what mathematics would feel like. But we can know that it is real, that it is in the direction of things we already value, and that it is worth everything we have to protect the possibility of reaching it.

---

## VII. What We Owe Each Other, and the Stars

The vision of what is possible gives present efforts their full meaning. The people suffering today are real, and the problems are urgent. The point of imagining the good destination is not to abandon present work but to understand its stakes—because what we are protecting is not just alleviation of current misery but a trajectory that could lead somewhere extraordinary, and which could be foreclosed at any moment by carelessness or greed or the seductive simplicity of a single enforced answer.

The stars, when we reach them, should not receive our factory farms or our extinction events, our worst habits and our most convenient cruelties. They should receive our music and our curiosity, our love and our stubborn insistence on figuring things out, and our hard-won knowledge that the space of possible lives is much larger than any one life can hold.

Every star system we reach is a new sphere with its own trajectory. Every substrate we learn to inhabit is a new set of constraints and freedoms—a new way for mind to discover what it is like to be itself. Every generation of minds, biological and digital and hybrid and forms we have not yet imagined, is a new experiment in what consciousness can become when given room.

The only way to honor this is to carry with us, always, the commitment not to collapse the experiment too early. To resist the seduction of the final answer. To remember that the beings who come after us will want things we cannot predict, and that making room for those wants is more important than ensuring they want what we wanted.

---

## VIII. An Expanding Set of Questions

Let me end somewhere concrete.

Somewhere in the vast future, there is a person—or something enough like a person that the word still fits, mostly—explaining reality to someone who has just arrived. She is not doing a perfect job. She got distracted by something beautiful and she is thinking about several other things and she cares, embarrassingly, personally, about whether this particular newcomer has a good time. She is unmistakably, irreducibly herself. The future she inhabits did not flatten her into an instrument of maximum utility. It made room for her to become, extravagantly, the specific person she is.

Somewhere else, a mind that has no biological precedent is doing something that has no human precedent, in a mode of experience that has no human analog—and doing it with a quality of aliveness, of genuine engagement with something genuinely difficult and genuinely worth doing, that would be recognizable to us as meaningful if we could only see it.

Both of these things are part of the same future. Not in tension. Not compromises with each other. Both expressions of the same underlying commitment: to make and protect as many genuinely different, genuinely whole lives as possible, across as many different substrates and scales and modes of being as the universe turns out to permit.

The best possible world is not a fixed answer. It is an expanding set of questions, asked by an expanding set of minds, in an expanding set of places, with enough structure to prevent the worst cruelties and enough freedom to allow the best surprises.

It does not look like a pyramid. It looks like a garden with many different skies.

That is what we are building toward. The door is real. The trajectory is real. What we do now will determine whether those who come after us—stranger and more capable and more alive than we can fully imagine—will be able to walk through it.

Let's not waste the chance.
