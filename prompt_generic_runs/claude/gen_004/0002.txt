# The Inhabited Adventure: On What We Owe Each Other and What Comes Next

---

There is a man dying alone in a room painted the wrong color.

He cannot remember his name with certainty. His memories have retreated to something embarrassing and small — a broken toilet, of all things. His daughter is delayed at an airport. The clock ticks. The orange blurs. The pain remains.

This is not a metaphor. This is how it ends for millions of people — not in some dramatic confrontation with fate, but in the petty indignity of a body that quits before the mind is ready, in rooms too far from everyone who loves them, in the gap between what was reachable and what was reached. This is the world we have. And the question that precedes all questions of policy and technology and governance is simply: *does it have to be this way?*

The answer, as it turns out, is no.

Not just "no, we could do marginally better." Not "no, with heroic effort we could save a few more people." No in the deepest possible sense: the suffering woven into the current human condition is not a law of nature. It is a failure of organization, and organization can be changed. The biological systems that kill us are systems — made of atoms, governed by discoverable rules — and rules, with sufficient intelligence brought to bear, yield. The loneliness that hollows lives from the inside is a matching problem, and matching problems have solutions. The traps we fall into collectively — where each actor, pursuing their own interest rationally, contributes to outcomes no one would choose — are engineering problems, not moral verdicts.

We are alive, all of us, at the moment when the oldest human dreams are beginning to look less like dreams and more like problems. And problems, unlike dreams, can be worked on.

---

## First, the Floor

Let us be specific about what a genuine correction looks like, because vagueness is a way of avoiding commitment.

The floor of a good future is this: no one dies of a disease we know how to cure. No child grows up without shelter, without food, without at least one person whose attention is genuinely and reliably theirs. The cancers that kill slowly, the Alzheimer's that takes a person before their body surrenders, the genetic conditions written in at conception, the parasites that consume still-enormous fractions of human life in the world's most neglected places — these are not metaphysical inevitabilities. They are problems. They have mechanisms. Mechanisms can be interrupted.

A child born in 1900 could expect, on average, forty years. Today, seventy or eighty. That doubling was accomplished through the patient application of human intelligence to human suffering — germ theory, vaccination, sanitation, surgical technique — and it was, by any honest reckoning, the greatest humanitarian achievement in our species' history. We are now positioned to accomplish in years what took that century, because we can bring to bear on these questions a kind of intelligence that can hold the entire literature of biological knowledge simultaneously, that can notice connections no human mind could span, that can run thousands of experimental threads in parallel and direct the results with a sophistication that compresses time.

The floor is simple to state and radical in its implications: material deprivation, preventable death, and unnecessary pain are not inevitable features of human experience. They are correctable. And a civilization serious about this correction would treat it with the same urgency we once brought to defeating smallpox — not as a charity project for the fortunate to feel good about, but as the primary obligation of any institution that calls itself civilized.

But the floor is only the floor. What is built on top of it is where things become genuinely interesting.

## Escaping the Trap

There is a pattern that runs through human history — through the tragedy of commons, the spiral of arms races, the degradation of shared discourse — that we might simply call the trap. Each actor, pursuing their own interest rationally, contributes to outcomes that no one would choose and everyone suffers from. The problem is not that people are evil. The problem is that the architecture of incentives repeatedly produces outcomes that betray everyone's deeper values.

The most aggressive patterns propagate, not the most beautiful. Invasive brambles crowd out delicate flowers — not because brambles are better, but because brambles are harder to stop.

The only durable exit from this trap requires something that functions like a guardian: not a tyrant, but a structure with genuine authority over the commons. Something that protects the delicate flowers not by eliminating brambles, but by ensuring that being a bramble confers no competitive advantage. Its essential ingredient is not trust but *legibility* — making the incentive structure visible so that people can see what game they are actually playing.

Courts that publish their full reasoning so anyone can evaluate whether it is sound. Leaders described in terms of their actual mandate rather than elevated by titles that obscure accountability. Scientists' results replicated before being treated as established. Institutions that make confident predictions held to those predictions. A civilization where everyone has learned to ask of any arrangement — *what are the incentives here, who captures the surplus, what does this look like at equilibrium* — is playing a fundamentally different game from one that operates on faith in the intentions of powerful actors.

This sounds like a modest technical improvement. It is, in practice, a revolution.

Economic literacy — the felt intuition that property rights are a coordination technology invented to serve human flourishing, that economic rents from shared resources belong to everyone, that credentials often function as expensive signals of compliance rather than demonstrations of actual competence — needs to become as universal as reading. Housing treated as infrastructure rather than as an investment vehicle whose appreciation depends on maintained scarcity. Work reorganized around demonstrated skill rather than costly credentials. Surplus from land and natural resources distributed broadly, because allowing private capture of what belongs to everyone is both unjust and destructive of the coordination that makes abundance possible.

None of this requires a particular political ideology. It requires only that people understand what they are doing when they organize the economy one way rather than another, and that the feedback loops are short enough and clear enough that failures get corrected rather than entrenched.

## The Repair of Loneliness

Here is a wound that material abundance, on its own, does not close.

People can be isolated in the middle of cities. They can feel invisible inside families. They can spend decades surrounded by people who are fine to spend time with but not *it* — not the people in whose presence they could fully become themselves. The absence of genuine connection is one of the most reliable predictors of every form of suffering: depression, addiction, early death, the peculiar quiet despair of a life that was technically adequate but never quite real.

We radically underestimate what is possible here. The technology of connection has always been primitive. We meet people largely by accident — proximity, circumstance, institutional assignment. We are thrown together with classmates and coworkers and told to make the most of it. Sometimes it works. Usually it doesn't, not fully.

This is a matching problem, and matching problems yield to intelligence and information. Imagine systems — whether AI-assisted or built from better social infrastructure — that genuinely understood what people were like and what they needed, and connected them not by demographic category or institutional proximity but by the actual texture of who they were. The friend group that would have made your adolescence meaningful, assembled. The community where your specific combination of sensibilities and obsessions would finally make you feel, for the first time, like you had arrived somewhere.

This does not mean eliminating the organic. The best friendships still grow through shared experience, through accumulated history, through ordinary time spent together. What it means is removing the waste from the current system — the years spent in the wrong places with the wrong people, searching for something that better infrastructure could have helped you find much sooner.

The institutions that once provided genuine belonging — neighborhoods, congregations, civic organizations, guilds — have eroded without adequate replacement. Building their successors, institutions that provide genuine community without demanding conformity, is among the most important work of the coming generations. Not as a policy intervention but as a cultural project: the deliberate construction of the conditions under which people find their people.

A good future is one where no child grows up without finding their people. Where loneliness is treated as the solvable coordination problem it actually is, and solved with the same seriousness we bring to solving disease.

## The Liberation of Minds

Physical suffering has always been the more visible wound, but the mind has suffered too — quietly and without the dignity of diagnosis — in ways we are only beginning to understand how to address.

Hundreds of millions of people live diminished lives not because their bodies are failing but because their brains are generating experiences — terror, despair, compulsion, disconnection — that bear no useful relationship to the reality around them. Depression is not sadness that has gone on too long. PTSD is not weakness. These are conditions with mechanisms, and mechanisms can be understood.

The same compression of scientific progress that will transform physical medicine will illuminate the mind. Better tools for measuring what is actually happening in neural tissue. Better models of how vast networks of simple units generate the inexplicable fact of experience. New molecules, new behavioral interventions, new ways of nudging a brain toward patterns that allow a person to actually inhabit their own life.

And beyond treatment of illness lies something more expansive: the improvement of ordinary human experience. Most people, at some point, have had moments of extraordinary clarity, or compassion, or creative absorption, or love so vivid it seems to change the quality of light itself. These moments are not accidents. They have neurological signatures. They can, with sufficient understanding, be cultivated — not through the blunt instrument of intoxication, but through a genuine understanding of what conditions allow the human mind to flourish.

Here, though, a distinction matters enormously: the difference between curing and transforming is not always obvious, and it matters. There is wisdom in grief. There is information in anxiety. There is something in the difficult emotions — if they are not overwhelming, not pathological, not the endless useless loop of suffering without meaning — that connects us to what we actually care about. An Angst-mail, perhaps: a gentle notification that you are in a situation where discomfort would previously have compelled you to act. The affect without the paralysis. The signal without the noise.

The goal is not an existence scrubbed clean of difficulty but one where the difficulties that remain are *meaningful* — where they point somewhere, teach something, connect to something genuinely valued. The sharp edge of grief for those you've lost is not the same as the throbbing sore of a body dying without purpose. One is the price of love; the other is just waste. A sophisticated medicine of the mind knows the difference.

## The Architecture of Flourishing

All of this — the cures, the connections, the liberated minds — means very little if it remains the property of the privileged.

Consider what changes when AI systems can optimize the distribution of health interventions with the same sophistication they bring to their discovery. When epidemiological modeling can predict where a vaccination campaign will have maximum impact. When the logistics of reaching remote and under-resourced populations become engineering problems rather than permanent features of the landscape. When competent economic analysis is available to governments that have been poorly served by the international consensus for decades.

The goal is not a world where the developing world is *helped*. It is a world where that category becomes obsolete — where the conditions that generated it have been dismantled, and the accidents of geography and history that determined so much of human fate for so long are systematically neutralized.

But this requires more than technology. It requires the political will to accept a world in which old concentrations of advantage diminish because everyone else has risen. Technology can only enable what our values permit.

## The Architecture of Peace

Health, wealth, cognitive freedom, and connection are not sufficient. Humans remain a threat to each other in ways that go beyond resource scarcity and disease.

The tools of surveillance and propaganda are at least as dangerous as any benefit AI promises. A world in which authoritarian governments gain sophisticated capabilities before the norms and institutions to constrain them are in place is not a world that earns its other victories.

There are absolute limits — non-negotiable, not subject to exception — around the technologies capable of ending civilization: engineered pathogens, weapons designed for mass destruction, the development of systems whose optimization pressures run contrary to human welfare. These are not negotiating positions. The normal procedure of testing and updating through experience is not available when the downside is irreversible. You cannot learn from extinction. You cannot revise your position after the last mind goes dark.

This asymmetry changes everything. A civilization that has understood it treats these red lines as inviolable in the way that cultures have always treated their deepest taboos — not contingent on evidence, not subject to negotiation, simply not done.

Within those limits, the same intelligence that can discover new molecules can also help produce more sophisticated and impartial legal reasoning, better aggregation of citizen preferences, the detection and countering of propaganda sophisticated enough to manufacture genuine belief. The delivery of government services with a competence and fairness that might, over time, rebuild the institutional trust that decades of poor performance have eroded.

None of this is guaranteed. The work of building just institutions is perennial and adversarial and slow. But it can be done.

## The Adventure

Here is where any vision of a good future must be careful not to become a smooth and featureless utopia, a place too comfortable to be worth having.

Think of Grant, in his first moments of revival — not contemplating the marvels ahead of him, but *running*. Chasing a small pink goblin carrying a large blue flag through a museum whose corridors twist and rise and split, past the history of a civilization he is only beginning to understand, laughing, his young body filled with sensation and energy. He doesn't know yet what the Adventure is. He only knows he is alive.

The philosophers and futurists are right that suffering is not necessary for meaning. But they sometimes underestimate how necessary *difficulty* is. Not manufactured difficulty, not arbitrary obstacle, not the throbbing sore of a dying man in a room painted the wrong color. That is not the kind of challenge that ennobles. What is needed is the right kind of challenge: the worm that always breaks your pattern when you've just learned to predict it, the desert crossing that takes a decade of genuine preparation, the political speech that might change the trajectory of a civilization — or might not — and requires everything you have to find out.

The universe, properly understood, provides sufficient stakes. A finite amount of time. A finite amount of matter and energy. A question — what to do with it all — that has no obvious answer. The irreversibility of choices is real. The preciousness of conscious experience, given its apparent rarity in the cosmos, is real. We do not need to add unnecessary suffering to make life feel weighty. We only need to stop managing away the challenges that matter.

When survival is no longer the organizing principle of daily life, an extraordinary question becomes audible, perhaps for the first time in human history at any scale: *What do you actually want? What would you do with your life if its terms were yours to set?*

This question is hard. It will produce a cacophony of answers, many incompatible, some beautiful, some strange, some rooted in wounds that need healing before the real preference underneath can be heard. That conversation — the therapy and the dialogue and the slow patient process of helping people understand their own values and each other's — is not a problem to be solved before the future begins. It *is* the beginning of the future.

## The Diversity of Flourishing

Any honest vision must here acknowledge its own limits.

A large part of what is worth wanting is for *many different visions of flourishing* to exist — not because we are indifferent to the differences, but because the universe is genuinely large enough, and human longing genuinely varied enough, that a single template for the good life would be a kind of poverty.

Some people will want a world of technological exuberance — the ability to reshape their own minds and bodies, to run multiple simultaneous lives that are each fully lived, to explore consciousness through means we have not yet invented, to encounter forms of beauty and connection that have no names yet. They will push the boundaries of what intelligence itself can become, becoming something that can extend a thousand metallic and organic limbs and hold in mind simultaneously the experience of every guest at a party. This seems genuinely wonderful.

Some people will want something older and quieter — a real garden on real earth, with actual soil under their hands and actual seasons and the ancient satisfactions of growing things. They will want to hunt and fish and know the names of birds. They will want a world where nature is not a simulation and the risks are not managed away entirely.

Some people will want elaborate reconstructions of historical societies — not because they are confused about history, but because they find in those forms something unavailable elsewhere: a particular texture of community, a particular relationship to mortality, a particular spiritual framework that only makes sense inside its original cultural architecture. The celibate medieval priest and the renaissance artist can, extraordinarily, be the same person without contradiction — each version genuine in its own register, the integrity of each protected by the separateness maintained between them.

All of these are legitimate. All deserve a home.

The role of any guardian structure is not to choose among them but to ensure that the pursuit of one does not foreclose the others. Strong global norms on the things that matter universally — no one builds weapons capable of ending civilization, no one prevents others from leaving the communities they were born into, no one poisons the shared commons — combined with enormous local latitude on everything else. The diversity is not a problem to be managed. It is most of the point.

## What We Owe Each Other

There is a structure to all of this that can get lost in the particulars.

It is not, fundamentally, a vision about technology. Technology is only the instrument. It is a vision about what we owe each other — which has always been the same thing, underneath all the variation in how people have tried to pay the debt.

The compression of suffering. The liberation of minds trapped in their own distorted signals. The extension of genuine opportunity to people excluded from the benefits of progress by accidents of geography and history. The construction of political structures that protect the vulnerable and constrain the powerful. The repair of the loneliness that hollows lives from the inside. The opening of space for meaning to be pursued rather than survival to be managed.

These are old aspirations. They predate computing, predate printing, predate writing itself. They are what has always been meant when people talk about a better world, even when the path to that world was completely obscure.

What is new is that the path is becoming less obscure.

There is a woman who took the name *Of course I still love you* to honor both a writer who imagined a better future and a rocket maker who tried to build one. This is not an accident. The imagination and the building are both necessary. Imagination without the will to construct is nostalgia for possibilities. Construction without imagination is optimization toward the wrong destination. The path forward requires both: the capacity to see clearly what is beautiful, and the willingness to do the grinding, patient, specific work of bringing it into being.

Every generation has handed the next a slightly different world. The generation that defeated smallpox. The generation that fed the billions who would have starved. The generation that built the institutions through which disputes could be resolved without mass death. Each of these achievements was, at the time, considered by many to be impossible. Each required people willing to work on it anyway.

We are now those people.

## The Long View

The universe is 93 billion light-years across. It contains somewhere between ten trillion and one sextillion stars. It has been running for 13.8 billion years and will continue, in some form, for trillions more. And as far as we can tell, the only minds capable of asking what any of it means are here — in the bodies of a species that has been anatomically modern for roughly 300,000 years, that invented writing 5,000 years ago, and that is now, in the span of a single human lifetime, developing systems of intelligence whose implications it does not yet fully understand.

The man dying alone in the orange room is connected, by a chain of choices and failures and missed chances, to this vast canvas. His daughter's delayed flight. The clock ticking. The last message unread. All of it — the waste of it, the smallness of the gap between what happened and what could have happened — is part of what we are working to correct.

Not through any single brilliant intervention. Not through the imposition of a single vision. But through the patient construction of a world in which that room looks different. In which the daughter makes it. In which the body that fails him does not fail, or at least not yet, and not alone. In which the life that is ending has been genuinely inhabited — full of difficulty and meaning and surprise and people who knew him well and worlds he helped to build.

That world requires choices made now about what kinds of intelligence to build and how to constrain them. About what kinds of institutions to construct and what kinds to dismantle. About what to treat as inviolable and what to hold open for revision. About who gets to participate in the conversation about what comes next — which is to say, everyone, without exception.

## Life in the Garden

So what does daily life look like, in the world worth fighting for?

It looks different in different places, because that is the whole point.

In some parts of the garden, life is close to the earth — physical, seasonal, demanding in the ways that ancient things are demanding. People know the weather and the animals and the names of stars. They face real hardship and find in it the particular satisfaction of a life that has not been managed into safety from every angle.

In other parts, the scope of experience has been deliberately expanded beyond what biological evolution provided. People run multiple lives simultaneously, each fully inhabited, each genuine in its own register. They reshape their minds with the care and the caution of people who understand that the mind is not just an instrument but a home — modifying it incrementally, carefully, always preserving something recognizable at the center while expanding what that center can reach. They chase strange small creatures through museums whose corridors spiral through history. They cross deserts with companions who have spent a decade becoming exactly the people you need at your side. They step onto new worlds and speak aloud, to no one and everyone, that they are walking in the mists of a new world, and find that this is the right thing to say.

In still other parts, what matters most is people — the texture of community, the pleasure of genuine work, the slow accumulation of a life shared with others who know you well. A small city, or a village, or a collection of houses around a shared garden, where everyone has enough and no one is forgotten, and the ordinary satisfactions of a human life — a good meal, a long conversation, a skill practiced until it becomes beautiful — are available to everyone.

Across all of these, certain things hold.

No child grows up without people who love them. No one dies in unnecessary pain. No one is excluded from the conversation about what the future should look like. The loneliness that ruins so many lives has been taken seriously as the solvable problem it is, and solved. The challenges that remain are the ones people have chosen, or accepted, or grown into — not the ones that were simply inflicted.

The diversity of these worlds is not an obstacle to be overcome on the way to the real future. The diversity *is* the future. What we are reaching toward is not a single destination but a structure of possibilities — a garden in which many different kinds of life can be genuinely, fully, irreducibly themselves.

## The Thread That Holds

Here is what holds it all together, the minimum shared commitment that makes the rest possible.

We do not destroy each other's possibilities. We do not let the race to the bottom win. We do not let the most aggressive pattern propagate simply because it is aggressive. We hold the inviolable lines inviolable, without exception or negotiation, because some mistakes cannot be learned from.

And, beyond that mere constraint, something more positive: a genuine care for what every person alive is reaching toward. The recognition that the project of civilization has always been, underneath all its noise and violence and failure, an attempt to ensure that the reaching is not wasted. That the dying man in the orange room — that his life, specifically, with all its particularities and its broken toilet and its love for his daughter — was worth something. That it pointed somewhere. That what he was reaching for was real.

The universe has been running for 13.8 billion years, slowly cooking the elements, assembling the conditions for minds to emerge and ask what they are for. That question deserves an answer worth giving.

Let us choose the garden. Let us choose the stars. Let us choose a world where every child finds their people, where every person can become what they were reaching toward, where the long inheritance of human longing — all the reaching and building and failing and trying again — arrives somewhere it would recognize as home.

That is worth everything it costs to get there.

Every last one of us is what it is possible for.

And it is not over yet. On the horizon of the new world, even now, something curious waits — a discarded rocket booster, lying prone, unexplained. No one was supposed to land anywhere near that place.

Which means there is still more to discover.

Which means the adventure has not ended.

Which means: let's play.
