# The Open Horizon: A Vision of What We Are Building Toward

There is a version of the future that almost no one fully believes in, yet almost everyone, in their quietest moments, hopes for. Not a perfect future — perfection is a cage dressed in white, a finished thing when everything good insists on remaining unfinished — but a genuinely, almost incomprehensibly good one. A future in which the worst of what has stalked humanity through its entire history is largely behind us, and in which what replaces it exceeds our current imagination as profoundly as the full range of human experience exceeds anything an ant could conceive.

This essay tries to hold several things at once: the concrete and the sublime, the practical and the transcendent, the near term and the long horizon, the world we can actually build and the world that building might, over long centuries, become. It is also an attempt to be honest about the dangers built into the project of imagining a better future — the particular catastrophes that have followed, again and again, from the conviction that paradise is finally within reach. Both the grandeur and the warning are necessary. A vision that ignores either will fail in predictable ways.

---

## What We Are Actually Fighting For

Let us begin with the near term, because the near term is where everything either starts or ends.

The transformation of medicine now underway may be the most consequential development in human history since the agricultural revolution, with the crucial difference that its benefits are not thousands of years away but decades, perhaps less. What becomes possible when systems of superhuman scientific capability are brought to bear on problems that have always had high returns to intelligence, but where that intelligence has historically been scarce? The honest answer is that we do not fully know. But the direction is clear, and the direction is extraordinary.

The end of most infectious disease is achievable within a generation. A genuine attack on most cancers — not management, not marginal improvement, but something approaching cure — is achievable within a generation. Effective interventions for the genetic conditions that have condemned people since birth. A serious assault on Alzheimer's and the long cognitive dusk it imposes. A real doubling of healthy human lifespan — not merely years added to the end, which can be a grim gift, but years of health, capacity, and presence added throughout.

Beyond the absence of disease lies something equally important: biological freedom. The ability for people to have meaningful control over their own bodies, their physical experience, their fertility, their capacities, in ways that currently belong to the extraordinarily privileged few, if they belong to anyone at all.

And then there is mental illness, which is the hidden catastrophe of human civilization: the one that hides inside people rather than showing on their bodies. Hundreds of millions of people move through their lives with the volume turned down, or the noise turned up, by conditions that are in principle no more mysterious than a broken bone and no more the fault of the person bearing them. Depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction — these are systems problems, neuroscience problems, and they are solvable. Most mental illness can probably be cured. Not managed. Not accommodated into a life built carefully around them. Cured.

None of this matters in the way it should if it remains the property of the wealthy. The most important moral question surrounding this transformation is not whether these advances will happen. It is whether they will happen everywhere, for everyone, with anything approaching equal speed. History offers a disturbing template: the gap between when a treatment becomes available and when it reaches the people who need it most has, again and again, been measured in decades and in lives. A world in which transformative capability dramatically improves life in wealthy countries while leaving the rest of the world further behind is not a success. It is a moral catastrophe wearing the mask of progress.

A worthy near-term standard: within a generation of this transformation's beginning, the developing world is substantially healthier than the developed world is today — even as the developed world has moved further forward. This is achievable. It is not automatic. It requires treating global access as a first-order priority, built into the design of institutions and incentives from the start, not retrofitted as an afterthought once the profitable markets are saturated.

## The Architecture Without Which Nothing Else Happens

Underlying all of this is a problem that optimists too often skip past, because it is unglamorous and does not fit neatly into a vision of flourishing: how do we ensure that transformative capability is actually beneficial, rather than captured by whoever happens to control it first?

The honest answer is that this is the hardest problem, and the one that most determines whether anything else in this essay is more than fantasy.

What is required is something like a guardian of the commons: an oversight structure, ultimately anchored in democratic accountability, that prevents any single actor — a company, a government, a movement, a coalition of the powerful — from using new capabilities to permanently entrench their own dominance. The greatest danger is not dramatic. It is quiet. It is the catastrophe of unprecedented capability becoming the ultimate tool of control, whether through surveillance, economic dominance, propaganda, or military superiority concentrated beyond the reach of any check.

The philosophical name for what this prevents is Moloch: the dynamic by which competition forces every party to sacrifice what they actually value in order to win. History is littered with Moloch's victories — races to the bottom in which everyone loses what matters while fighting over what doesn't. The goal is not to eliminate competition or conflict, both of which can be generative. The goal is to prevent either from consuming the possibility of a good future entirely.

The people who build and maintain these structures — in policy, in governance, in law, in the daily unglamorous work of institutions that actually serve the people they are supposed to serve — are doing work as important as any in the history of civilization. They deserve to be recognized as the visionaries they are.

But there is a deeper danger that the architecture must also guard against, and it is worth naming clearly because it is the one most likely to be invisible to those inside it. The enemy of a good future is not only bad actors and captured institutions. It is the failure mode built into grand projects themselves: the fervor that comes from thinking not merely that paradise is possible, but that you and your movement are the ones finally, definitively building it. In the grip of that certainty, humans have historically been very willing to break eggs, to purge impurities, to suppress whoever stands in the way. The gulag on the side of Eden is not an accident of execution. It is what happens when the dream of a final answer to human suffering is married to the willingness to override anyone who disagrees.

The vision worth fighting for is therefore explicitly not a vision of arriving at the destination. It is a vision of keeping the path open — perpetually, humbly, with full acknowledgment that we do not know the destination in detail and would be dangerous if we thought we did. What emerges from this, in the best case, is not a single all-knowing controller of human destiny but something more like a constitutional order for a new age: structures that prevent any entity from unilaterally seizing the commons, that protect the weak against the powerful, that preserve the conditions under which human diversity — and eventually, forms of conscious life we can barely imagine — can flourish rather than being crushed into homogeneity by whatever values happen to be most aggressive at replicating themselves.

## The Canvas That Opens

With foundations secure — health broadly distributed, governance structures that protect rather than concentrate power, the worst of involuntary suffering behind us — what does the world actually look like?

It looks, first, like almost incomprehensible diversity. This is what purely technocratic visions tend to miss, and what purely transcendent visions also miss in their own way. The point of solving material problems is not to produce a single optimal world. It is to give the full range of human values room to actually unfold, for the first time, without being constantly distorted by scarcity, disease, and the raw pressure of circumstances that weren't chosen and couldn't be escaped.

Some people will want lives that look remarkably like lives today: gardens to tend, communities to build, food to grow and cook and share, children to raise, crafts to learn slowly over decades. If the old problems of scarcity and disease no longer force them into patterns they didn't choose, they might freely choose patterns not so different from what they had, and this would be not a failure of imagination but a profound success. The forms of human life that have persisted across cultures and millennia persist for reasons. They contain something essential about what it means to be present, rooted, alive to the particular texture of a specific day in a specific place among specific people who know you.

Others will push into stranger territory. Communities will form around every conceivable configuration of values, aesthetics, and social arrangements. Some people will spend decades or centuries becoming exceptionally good at things with no economic value whatsoever, and this will be celebrated rather than pitied. Some will explore states of consciousness that are currently inaccessible or dangerous, with the understanding and safety that makes exploration rather than roulette. Some will pursue forms of beauty and knowledge that have no name yet, in a world that keeps making room.

What unites all of this is not a single conception of the good life. It is the removal of what has always prevented people from discovering what their actual conception of the good life is. We have, for most of human history, had very little evidence about what people actually want, because almost no one has had the conditions in which genuine wanting becomes possible. That will change.

## The Question of Meaning

Honesty requires sitting with the genuinely hard question that a world without involuntary hardship raises: what structures meaning when material necessity no longer provides it?

The pessimistic answer says meaning requires genuine stakes, and genuine stakes require genuine loss, and so a world without serious suffering is a world without serious meaning. This argument deserves respect. It is not obviously wrong.

But I think it is wrong, or at least importantly incomplete. Meaning comes primarily from two sources: from caring about something and working toward it, with real consequences for how the effort goes; and from being known by others and knowing them in return, in relationships that are genuinely mutual and therefore genuinely fragile. Neither of these requires suffering as a prerequisite. They require engagement. They require that things matter. And things can matter even when background conditions are good — even, perhaps especially, when one is choosing difficulty rather than having it thrust upon oneself.

What the good society requires is that we retain the ability to choose genuine difficulty. Those who want real stakes, real consequences, the unpadded texture of existence, can have it. Challenge remains available as a chosen thing even as it ceases to be an imposed one. And those who have had enough of difficulty — who have spent lifetimes in conditions no one would choose — can finally rest without apology, without the suspicion that their rest is somehow a failure of character.

The question of meaning in a world of abundance will not be answered by theorizing in advance. It will be answered by living — by the generations who actually inhabit that world, discovering through experience what they require to feel that their existence is worth having. We cannot answer it for them. What we can do is refuse both the complacent assumption that it solves itself and the cynical assumption that it cannot be solved. It requires attention. It requires wisdom. It requires the willingness to be genuinely surprised by what humans turn out to need when the old necessities no longer organize their days.

## The Horizon That Keeps Moving

Here is where honesty about the near term becomes insufficient, and we must venture further — not into fantasy, but into a clear-eyed account of what the physical universe actually makes possible.

Consider what happens in our minds during the moments that have most shown us what life can be at its best: moments of joy, love, beauty, energy, vastness. Moments that have made us sit bolt upright and say, *oh — this is it. This isn't some pale imitation of life. This is the real thing, and I didn't know it was available.* For many people, such moments carry a quality of greater aliveness, greater presence: the world vast and shining, not a new world but the world as it always was, finally and briefly seen.

The crucial observation is this: these experiences are, as far as we can tell, nowhere near the limit of what is available. They are proof — actual evidence from direct experience — that the relevant dimension of life can vary, dramatically, in the good direction. We have touched something real in those moments. What we touched is not as good as things get. Not close. The proof the moments provide is not of a ceiling but of a direction.

The human brain is an extraordinarily specific and limited organ: roughly 100 billion neurons, running on perhaps 20 watts of power, with signals traveling at a few hundred meters per second at best. These are not physical limits. They are the particular parameters of one evolutionary solution to the problem of intelligence, developed under severe resource constraints over millions of years. If small changes in brain chemistry can produce the dramatic variation we actually witness in the quality of human experience; if relatively small changes in neural architecture can move the dial from insect to mammal to human — then think of what a sustained, careful, ethical exploration of what is physically possible might reveal about consciousness, joy, wisdom, love, and beauty.

We are like the great apes, being asked to evaluate whether they should evolve into humans, and listing on the pro side: more bananas. More bananas, yes, we can get those. But there is more to the human condition than bananas, and there is more to whatever might come after us than anything we can currently articulate. The apes who expected bananas weren't merely wrong about human food choices. They were wrong, in a deeper and more exciting way, about what kind of thing they were getting themselves into.

This is not mysticism. It requires no exotic physics, no special metaphysics, no particular view about the hard problem of consciousness. All it requires is the observation that whatever happens in our minds during peak experience is movement along some trajectory we care about — and that it is possible for physical structures to travel much, much further in that direction than any creature on Earth has yet traveled. Our universe makes possible, in principle, structures of awareness and connection and beauty that are as far beyond what we have touched as human experience is beyond anything an insect has ever known.

There are oceans we have barely dipped a toe into. There are symphonies we can only barely hear. There are suns whose heat we can only barely feel on our skin.

## The Strangeness That Would Still Be Ours

It is essential to hold the sublime and the human together, rather than letting the sublime float away into abstraction where it loses all power to move or guide anyone.

Some visions of a radically transformed future lose all emotional substance in the process of losing all parochialism. They become pure light, with nothing to see. If concrete visions of utopia fail by being too small — cleaning up the present rather than imagining what transcends it — sublime visions fail by becoming too empty. The incomprehensible, held long enough without any anchor, ceases to be anything at all.

The better vision holds that a profoundly transformed future would still, in some deep sense, be ours. Not familiar in its details — it would be genuinely strange, genuinely alien to current experience, genuinely beyond our current capacity to fully describe. But recognizable in its essence. If we could encounter it from outside ourselves, we would recognize in it the same thing that made us sit bolt upright, once, when we first really touched love, or beauty, or the particular aliveness of a moment that refused to be ordinary. We would feel, standing before the bonfire, the heat of the ember from which it was lit. There would be something like a remembering.

This continuity matters practically as well as philosophically. If we are trying to navigate toward a genuinely good future — rather than one that is merely powerful, or merely stable, or merely preferred by whoever happens to be steering — then we need some thread of connection between what we actually value now and what we are building toward. We need to be able to recognize, even imperfectly, what direction is up. Our best moments are that thread. They are the evidence that a destination is real, and roughly which way it lies.

What this means is that the sublime vision and the concrete vision are not opposites to be balanced against each other in awkward compromise. They are different scales of the same thing. The experience of being fully alive in a single, unrepeatable human moment, and the experience of whatever form of consciousness a civilization of vastly greater capacity might touch, are expressions of the same underlying dimension — the one that varies, dramatically, in the direction we care about most. The ember and the bonfire and the sun are made of the same fire.

## What Remains Hard

Honesty requires naming what does not go away.

Some things genuinely cannot be shared. There is one original Earth. There is one history. There are resources and places and configurations of the world that different people want in incompatible ways, and no amount of goodwill or mechanism design fully resolves this. A just system will make the trade-offs as wisely and fairly as possible, give people meaningful voice in decisions that affect them, and acknowledge honestly when it has had to override someone's preference. But it will have to override preferences. Anyone who claims otherwise is selling something.

Not everyone will want what this vision offers. Some will reject it on principle, for reasons ranging from the sincere to the confused. A world organized around genuine freedom cannot coerce people into accepting benefits they did not ask for — but it also cannot allow any group to export its refusals onto everyone else. This tension does not resolve cleanly. It requires ongoing navigation, which is another way of saying it requires precisely the kind of democratic, accountable, revisable governance that holds everything together.

The history of utopian thinking is also a history of catastrophe, and the catastrophe follows a consistent pattern. It is worth sitting with this honestly rather than rushing past it. The people who built the worst systems of the twentieth century were not, for the most part, people who wanted to cause suffering. They were people who were certain — with the particular, luminous certainty that a vision of imminent paradise can produce — that the right future was finally within reach, and that whatever stood between the present and that future was an obstacle to be removed. The dream of ending human suffering and the willingness to override human beings are not natural enemies. They are, in the wrong configuration, natural allies. The vision worth holding must be explicitly designed to prevent this. It must be a vision of keeping the path open, not of knowing where the path ends. It must distribute power rather than concentrating it, remain revisable rather than final, and treat disagreement as a permanent feature of human life rather than an impurity to be eliminated.

And at the far edge of time, there is the running down. Whatever we build, however much we extend and enrich conscious experience, the universe eventually ends. This is not a reason for despair. The meaning of a life is not negated by its ending. The meaning of a civilization is not negated by its eventual dissolution. We live in the middle of the story. That is where all the interesting things happen.

---

## The Stakes

What we are fighting for, at its core, is a world in which the worst of what has been inflicted on human beings — the children who died of preventable disease before they learned their own names, the minds destroyed by treatable illness, the billions condemned to poverty not by anything they chose but by the accident of where and when they were born — becomes past tense. Historical. A thing that happened before, that our descendants will barely be able to imagine, in the same way that we now barely imagine the world before anesthesia, or antibiotics, or the possibility that a girl might be educated.

And then, beyond that: a world that keeps opening.

Not a world that arrives at a final, optimal configuration and stops. Not a world that has solved the problem of human flourishing and relaxes into its solution. A world that keeps expanding what is possible to experience, to understand, to love, to become. A world that makes room — continually, restlessly, humbly — for forms of consciousness and beauty and connection that it cannot yet name. A world in which the direction of up remains always ahead, always partly unknown, always genuinely worth moving toward.

The near-term standard is this: any clear-eyed person, understanding the trade-offs and imperfections and the things that could not be fully resolved, looks at this world and says — *yes. Not everything here is what I would have chosen. But yes. This is pretty good.* That standard is worth everything it costs to reach it.

But there is another standard, held alongside the first without contradiction. The universe we inhabit makes possible structures of awareness and beauty and love that are vastly, almost incomprehensibly richer than anything any creature on Earth has yet touched. The work of civilization — slow, difficult, perpetually unfinished, requiring patience and humility and the willingness to be surprised — is to keep that possibility open and to keep moving toward it, generation by generation, without ever claiming to have arrived.

We see now as through a glass, darkly. But the glass is real. What lies beyond it is real. The direction we are pointed — if we are careful, if we are wise, if we hold together the practical and the transcendent, the ember and the fire, the near term and the long horizon — is genuinely, in the plainest and most literal sense, the direction of the good.

The future does not belong to us. We are tending it for those who come after, whose capacities and joys and ways of being alive we cannot fully imagine, who will look back on our struggles the way we look back on the darkness before the first fire was lit — not with pity exactly, because pity is not quite the right word for people who were doing what they could with what they had — but with a kind of tenderness, and gratitude, and the knowledge that the ember we protected, through everything, became something they can finally, fully feel.

That is worth everything we have.
