# The Open Future: A Vision Worth Everything

There is a version of the future that almost no one fully believes in, yet almost everyone, in their quietest moments, hopes for. Not a perfect future—perfection is a tyranny dressed in white—but a genuinely, almost incomprehensibly good one. A future in which the worst of what has stalked humanity through its entire history is largely behind us, and in which what replaces it exceeds our current imagination as profoundly as human experience exceeds anything an ant could conceive.

This essay tries to hold both of those things at once: the concrete and the sublime, the practical and the transcendent, the world we can actually build and the world that building might, over long centuries, become. It is an attempt to look at our actual situation steadily, without flinching from either the difficulty or the grandeur.

---

## What We Are Actually Fighting For

Let us be direct, first, about the near term, because the near term is where everything either begins or ends.

The most important medical transformation in human history is either happening now or is about to. The deployment of systems of superhuman scientific capability against problems that have always had high returns to intelligence—but where that intelligence has historically been scarce—may accomplish in a decade what would otherwise have taken a century. Not magic. Not metaphor. The consequence of running the same dynamic that gave us CRISPR, mRNA vaccines, and AlphaFold, but faster, broader, and deeper than anything we have done before.

What does this buy us? The end of most infectious disease. A realistic chance at eliminating most cancers. Effective treatment for the genetic conditions that have condemned people since birth. A genuine attack on Alzheimer's and the long cognitive dusk it imposes. A serious doubling of healthy human lifespan—a second twentieth century of longevity gain, compressed into a fraction of the time.

Beyond the absence of disease lies something equally important: biological freedom. The ability for people to have meaningful control over their own bodies—their physical experience, their fertility, their capacities—in ways that currently belong to the extraordinarily privileged few, if they belong to anyone at all.

And then there is the mind.

Mental illness is the hidden catastrophe of human civilization. Hundreds of millions of people move through their lives with the volume turned down, or the noise turned up, by conditions that are in principle no more mysterious than a broken bone. Depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction—these are not character failures or spiritual insufficiencies. They are systems neuroscience problems, and they are solvable. The tools that will crack open physical medicine will crack them open too. Most mental illness can probably be cured. Not managed. Not accommodated. Cured.

None of this means anything if it remains the property of the wealthy. The most important moral question surrounding this transformation is not whether these advances will happen. It is whether they will happen everywhere. History offers a disturbing template: many existing interventions have not yet reached the people who need them most. A world in which powerful new capabilities dramatically improve life in wealthy countries while leaving the rest of the world further behind would not be a success. It would be a moral catastrophe wearing the mask of progress.

A worthy near-term goal, then: that within a generation of this transformation's beginning, the developing world is substantially healthier than the developed world is today—even as the developed world races ahead. This is achievable. It is not automatic. It requires that developers, governments, philanthropists, and international institutions treat global access as a first-order priority rather than an afterthought.

## The Architecture That Makes Everything Else Possible

Underlying all of this is a problem that optimists too often skip past: how do we ensure that transformative capability is actually beneficial, rather than captured by whoever happens to control it first?

The honest answer is that this is the hardest problem, and the one that most determines whether anything else in this essay happens at all.

What is required is something like a guardian of the commons: an oversight structure, ultimately anchored in democratic accountability, that prevents any single actor—a company, a government, a coalition of the powerful—from using new capabilities to permanently entrench their own dominance. The greatest danger is not dramatic. It is quiet: the catastrophe of unprecedented capability becoming the ultimate tool of domination, whether through surveillance, propaganda, economic control, or military superiority concentrated in the wrong hands.

The philosophical name for what this prevents is Moloch: the dynamic by which competition forces every party to sacrifice what they actually value in order to win. History is littered with Moloch's victories—races to the bottom in which everyone loses what matters while fighting over what doesn't. The goal is not to eliminate competition or conflict, both of which can be healthy. It is to prevent either from consuming the possibility of a good future entirely.

What emerges from this, in the best case, is not a single all-knowing controller of human destiny. It is something more like a constitutional order for a new age: a set of structures that prevent any entity from unilaterally seizing control of the commons, that protect the weak against the powerful, that preserve the conditions under which human diversity—and eventually, forms of conscious life we can barely imagine—can flourish rather than being crushed into homogeneity by whatever values happen to be most aggressive at replicating themselves.

This architecture is not glamorous. It does not inspire the same feeling as a vision of human flourishing across the stars. But it is the foundation without which all of that comes to nothing. The people who build and maintain these structures—in policy, in governance, in the unglamorous daily work of institutions that actually serve the people they're supposed to serve—are doing work as important as anyone in the history of civilization.

## The Garden

With the foundations secure—health broadly distributed, governance structures that protect rather than concentrate power, the worst suffering behind us—what does the world actually look like?

It looks, in the first instance, like almost incomprehensible diversity.

This is what purely technocratic visions tend to miss. The point of solving material problems is not to produce a single optimal world. It is to give the full range of human values room to unfold. A civilization in which everyone's basic needs are met and the worst suffering is history is not a finished product. It is a canvas.

Some people will want lives that look remarkably like lives today—gardens to tend, communities to build, food to grow and cook, children to raise. If the old problems of scarcity and disease no longer force them into patterns they didn't choose, they might freely choose patterns not so different from what they had. There is deep wisdom in this. The forms of human life that have persisted across cultures and centuries persist for reasons. They contain something essential about what it means to be present, rooted, alive to the particular.

Others will want to push into stranger territory. Morphological freedom will become real. Communities will form around every conceivable configuration of values, aesthetics, and social arrangements. Some people will spend centuries becoming exceptionally good at things with no economic value whatsoever, and this will be fine—more than fine. Some will explore states of consciousness that are currently inaccessible or dangerous, with the understanding and safety that makes exploration rather than roulette. Some will pursue forms of beauty and knowledge that have no name yet, in a world that keeps making room.

The philosopher's job—one version of it—will be to articulate what makes life meaningful when material constraints have been largely removed. The pessimistic version says that meaning requires genuine stakes, and genuine stakes require genuine loss, and so a world without serious suffering is a world without serious meaning. This argument deserves respect, not dismissal.

But it is probably wrong, or at least incomplete. Meaning comes primarily from relationships and from the experience of growth: from caring about something and working toward it, from being known by others and knowing them in return. These do not require suffering to be real. They require engagement. They require that things matter. And things can matter even when the background conditions are good. What the good society requires is that we retain the ability to choose difficulty—that those who want genuine stakes, real consequences, the unpadded texture of existence, can have it. And that those who have had enough of difficulty, who have spent lifetimes in conditions no one would choose, can finally rest.

## The Horizon That Keeps Moving

Here is where honesty about the near term is insufficient, and we must venture further.

Consider what happens in our minds during the moments that have most shown us what life can be at its best—moments of joy, love, beauty, energy, immensity. Moments that have made us sit up straight and say: *oh. Whoa. This is it. This isn't some pale imitation of life; this is the real thing, and I didn't realize it was available.* For many people, such moments carry a quality of greater aliveness, greater *here-ness*: the world vast and shining, not a new world but the world as it always was, finally seen.

The crucial observation is this: these peak experiences are, as far as we can tell, nowhere near the limit of what's available. They are proof—actual evidence from direct experience—that the relevant dimension of life can vary, dramatically, in the good direction. We have touched something real in those moments, and what we touched is not as good as things get. Not close.

The human brain is an extraordinarily specific and limited organ: roughly 100 billion neurons, running on perhaps 20 watts of power, with signals traveling at a few hundred meters per second at best. These are not physical limits; they are the particular parameters of one evolutionary solution to the problem of intelligence, developed under severe resource constraints on the African savanna. If small changes in chemistry can produce the dramatic variation we actually witness in the quality of human experience; if relatively small changes in brain architecture can move us from insects to mammals to humans—then think of what a sustained, careful, ethical exploration of what is physically possible might reveal.

We are like the great apes asking whether they should evolve into humans, and listing on the pro side: "we could get more bananas." More bananas—yes, we can get those. But there is more to the human condition than that, and more to whatever might come after than anything we can currently articulate.

This is not mysticism. It requires no special metaphysics, no exotic physics, no particular view about consciousness. All it requires is the observation that whatever happens in our brains during peak experience is movement along some trajectory we care about; and that it is possible for physical structures to travel much, much further in that direction than any creature on Earth has yet traveled. Our actual universe is one that makes possible—to whatever extent the relevant concepts apply—structures of awareness and connection and joy that are vastly farther in whatever direction our minds travel when they become more fully alive than anything anyone has encountered yet.

There are oceans we have barely dipped a toe into. There are symphonies we can barely hear. There are suns whose heat we can barely feel on our skin.

## The Strangeness That Would Still Be Ours

It is important to hold the sublime and the human together, rather than letting the sublime float away into abstraction.

Some visions of a radically transformed future lose all emotional substance in the process of losing all parochialism. "Heaven is eternal union with God in perfect love" is less parochial than "heaven is harps and reunion with your grandmother"—but it is also less real, less grounded in anything we can actually care about. Incomprehensibility drains content. In the extreme, the sublime becomes pure light, with nothing to see.

The better vision—harder to achieve, more worth achieving—holds that a profoundly transformed future would still, in some deep sense, be ours. Not familiar in its details: it would be genuinely strange, genuinely alien to current experience, genuinely beyond our current capacity to fully imagine. But recognizable in its essence. If we encountered it from outside, we would recognize in it the same thing that made us sit bolt upright, once, when we first really touched love, or beauty, or the particular aliveness of a moment that refused to be ordinary. We would feel, in front of the bonfire, the heat of the ember from which it was lit. There would be a kind of remembering.

The gods are strange to mortal eyes, and yet they are not strange.

A civilization of radically enhanced capacity would still, if things go well, be continuous with what is best in human life now. Not because the future would be a cleaned-up version of the present—it wouldn't—but because what makes peak experiences valuable now and what makes a vastly richer form of experience valuable then would be, in some important sense, the same thing, expressed at different scales. The ember and the bonfire.

This continuity matters practically, not only philosophically. If we are trying to navigate toward a genuinely good future—rather than one that is merely powerful, or merely stable, or merely preferred by whoever happens to be steering—then we need some thread of connection between what we actually value now and what we are building toward. We need to be able to recognize, even imperfectly, what direction is up. Our best moments are that thread. They are not the destination; they are the evidence that the destination is real, and roughly which way it lies.

## The Problems That Remain

Honesty requires naming what does not go away.

Some things genuinely cannot be shared. There is one original Earth. There is one history. There are some resources, some places, some configurations of the world that different groups of people want in incompatible ways, and no amount of goodwill or mechanism design fully resolves this. A just system will make the trade-offs as wisely and fairly as possible, give people meaningful voice in decisions that affect them, and acknowledge honestly when it has had to override someone's preference. But it will have to override preferences. Anyone who claims otherwise is selling something.

Not everyone will want what this vision offers. Some will reject it on principle, for reasons ranging from the sincere to the confused. A world organized around genuine freedom cannot coerce people into accepting benefits they do not want—but it also cannot allow any group to export its choices onto everyone else. The people who want to live as if the transformation never happened should be able to do so. What they cannot do is prevent others from choosing differently.

The question of meaning in a world without involuntary hardship remains genuinely open. We should be honest that we do not fully know what the soul requires when material necessity no longer provides structure. The answer will be found by living, not by theorizing in advance—but we should resist both the complacent assumption that it solves itself and the cynical assumption that it can't be solved. It requires attention, wisdom, and the willingness to be surprised.

And at the very far edge of time, there is the running down. Whatever we build, however much we extend and enrich conscious experience, the universe ends eventually. This is not a reason for despair. The meaning of a life is not negated by its ending, and the meaning of a civilization is not negated by its eventual dissolution. We live in the middle of the story. That is where all the interesting things happen.

---

## The Standard and the Stakes

What we are fighting for, at its core, is a world in which the worst of what has been inflicted on human beings—the children who died of preventable disease, the minds destroyed by treatable illness, the billions condemned to poverty not by anything they chose but by the accident of where they were born—becomes past tense. Historical. A thing that happened to our ancestors, that we can now barely imagine.

And then, beyond that: a world that keeps opening. Not a world that arrives at a final, optimal configuration and stops, but a world that keeps expanding what is possible to experience, to understand, to love, to become. A world that makes room—continually, restlessly, humbly—for forms of flourishing it cannot yet name.

Any individual looking at the near-term version of this world carefully—understanding the trade-offs, the imperfections, the things that couldn't be fully resolved—might say: not everything here is what I would have chosen. But: *yes. This is pretty good.*

That is one standard, and a worthy one.

But there is another standard, held alongside the first without contradiction: the universe we inhabit makes possible structures of consciousness, beauty, and connection that are vastly, almost incomprehensibly richer than anything any creature on Earth has yet touched. The work of civilization—slow, difficult, perpetually unfinished—is to keep that possibility open and to keep moving toward it, generation by generation, with patience and courage and the humility to know that we are not building the end state but tending the path.

We see now as through a glass, darkly. But the glass is real, and what lies beyond it is real, and the direction we are pointed—if we are careful, if we are wise, if we hold together the practical and the transcendent, the near term and the long horizon—is genuinely, in the plainest and most literal sense, the direction of the good.

That is worth everything we have.
