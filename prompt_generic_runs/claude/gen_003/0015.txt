# The World We Are Becoming

## A Vision of Human Civilization at Its Best

There is a man dying in an orange room.

He cannot remember his name with any clarity, cannot lift his head, cannot speak the last words forming somewhere behind his fading eyes. The clock ticks. The pain pulses. The world reduces itself to its barest elements — sensation, time, the dimming of things.

This is where we begin, because this is where so many human stories have ended: in diminishment, in loss, in the undignified erasure of a self that had spent decades becoming itself. A person who loved things, built things, wondered about things — reduced at last to the tick of a clock and the hateful orange of a ceiling.

What would it mean to take that person seriously?

Not sentimentally. Not with the cheap consolation that suffering builds character or that death gives life meaning. But genuinely, concretely, with the full force of everything we know and everything we might learn: what would it mean to refuse that ending?

This essay is an attempt to describe the world that takes Grant — and every person like him — seriously. Not a blueprint, and not a fantasy. A direction. A portrait of what civilization looks like when its intelligence is finally turned, with genuine effort and care, toward the problem of how to live well together, and what happens when that effort succeeds.

---

## The Body, Reconsidered

We begin with the most basic refusal: the refusal to accept biological decay as a fact of nature rather than a problem to be solved.

In the world we are building toward, a human life is genuinely, astonishingly long — not the stretched-out twilight of an extended decline, but an actual expansion of the time available to become who you are. To master the things worth mastering. To see the consequences of the seeds you planted. The defeat of aging is, at its core, a moral achievement: a recognition that the arbitrary truncation of a person still growing, still curious, still capable of love and surprise, is not a feature of the universe to be accepted but an injustice to be corrected.

And alongside longevity: health, including the health of minds. The extremes of suffering that evolution built into our nervous systems — the depths of despair, the paralysis of anxiety, the distortions of psychosis, the quiet erosion of dignity in a broken body — are malfunctions, not essential features. They will be treated as such.

But more than this: the body itself is revealed, in the fullness of time, as more than a container. It is an instrument. When Grant, newly revived, first feels his young strong hand glowing in the warm light, feels blood moving through his limbs, feels his pulse and the firing of his nerves and the weight differential between his two feet — he is experiencing something most people only approach in rare moments of athletic or meditative concentration. To feel one's own aliveness fully. To inhabit one's body rather than merely occupy it.

This expansion of embodied awareness — the full-sensory richness of a life taken seriously — is not a luxury for the privileged few. It is a birthright systematically denied by exhaustion, by illness, by a civilization that never thought to ask what the body was capable of feeling when properly attended to. One of the first gifts the future offers is simply this: the return of sensation to those who have been numbed.

## The Mind, Unlocked

But longevity and health are only the container. What fills it matters more.

The best version of a human life involves what we might call genuine self-authorship: the experience of setting goals that are truly yours, pursuing them through real difficulty, and arriving somewhere you could not have predicted. This requires, at minimum, knowing yourself well enough to know what you actually want — and having the stability and freedom to pursue it.

It also requires, in the fullest version, the ability to examine and modify the architecture of your own mind.

This is the aspect of the future that most unsettles our present assumptions, and the one we must approach with the most care. The capacity to adjust one's own cognition — to increase patience, reduce groundless anxiety, sharpen focus, expand empathy, dissolve the paralysis of grief without losing its wisdom — is not a dystopian fantasy of control. It is the natural extension of what we have always done. We have always used tools to augment what our minds can do. We have always used practices — meditation, therapy, education, ritual — to reshape how our minds work. The difference is one of precision and degree.

In the world we are building toward, this capacity is taken seriously and exercised wisely. Not recklessly — the cautionary lesson is obvious, written in every myth about what happens when a person modifies themselves faster than they can integrate, when the hunger for power overwhelms the patience for wisdom. But the answer to that danger is not abstention. It is the development of the wisdom to go slowly, to test carefully, to preserve what is worth preserving.

There is a principle here worth stating plainly: the goal is not to optimize the human out of existence. The goal is to remove the suffering that serves no purpose — the groundless anxieties, the addictions, the recursive loops of self-sabotage born of old wounds — while preserving the conflicts that generate something real: the tension between competing goods, the friction of growth, the honest engagement with difficulty that produces depth.

Grant's first self-modification is to increase his own caution. This is wisdom. Not the refusal to grow, but the insistence on growing carefully.

## The Relational World, Designed With Intention

The deepest goods in human life are relational. Most people know this. Most civilizations have failed to act on it.

Loneliness — the epidemic beneath all epidemics — is not an inevitable feature of human life. It is the result of social arrangements that have failed to place people within communities where they can be truly known. This is a design failure. It can be corrected.

The world we are building toward takes the matching problem — finding the people whose depths are genuinely compatible with yours — as seriously as it deserves. One of the most consequential allocation problems any person faces is rarely treated with anything approaching rigor. Patient, skilled attention to compatibility; deliberate design of shared spaces that facilitate vulnerability; architecture and scheduling and shared purpose organized around the simple goal of placing people near others who can genuinely see them.

Children in this world grow up known by more than a nuclear family — by genuine webs of adults who have chosen, in some meaningful sense, to be present for them. Not the village of romantic mythology, which was also often a prison, but something designed with intention: communities that balance belonging with freedom, connection with the right to leave, intimacy with the preservation of individual selfhood.

And in a world where lives are very long, relationships can deepen in ways that are currently impossible. Not because people are bound together by the scarcity of alternatives, but because time itself becomes a resource. The friendship that in a normal human life only reaches its first real depth at the moment of death can, in a longer life, continue past that point — into the territory that has never been mapped, because no one has ever lived there.

There is a particular kind of connection worth naming separately: the bond between a person and those who have known them across time. The relationship between Grant and his granddaughter — who has watched him sleep in ice for decades, who has prepared the world for his return, who loves him with the particular love of someone who has been shaped by his absence — is something the present world barely has the vocabulary for. The mutual adoption structures, the extended communities, the web of chosen relationships that persist across generations: these are not amenities. They are the substrate of a life that feels meaningful rather than arbitrary.

## The Society That Learns

Perhaps the deepest resource that civilization currently wastes is its collective capacity to figure things out.

The institutions for generating and transmitting knowledge — in science, in governance, in culture — have never been optimized for their actual purpose. Science has been optimized, in part, for careers. Governance has been optimized, in part, for the perpetuation of power. Journalism has been optimized, in part, for attention. The result is a civilization that generates enormous amounts of information and converts surprisingly little of it into actual wisdom.

What does a civilization that has corrected these failures look like?

It looks like a world where the reasoning behind decisions is public, where predictions are recorded and checked, where the admission of error is treated as a mark of trustworthiness rather than weakness. Where the incentives for getting things right have been patiently realigned with the incentives that people actually face. Where there is a meaningful cultural distinction between honest inquiry and motivated advocacy — maintained not by fiat but by a culture that has understood what the collapse of that distinction costs.

And beyond institutions, the world we are building toward has developed what we might call coordination literacy: a widespread understanding, not just among specialists but among ordinary people, of how collective decisions go wrong and what can be done about it. The commons problem. The tragedy of the short-term. The dynamics of trust and its collapse. This literacy does not require everyone to be a game theorist. It requires only that people can recognize, in the situations they actually face, when they are in the kind of situation where certain patterns tend to play out — and what choices are available.

The Powers — the vast intelligences that oversee the infrastructure of this civilization — are not gods and not tyrants. They are the products of a long and careful negotiation between what humans value and what large-scale coordination requires. Their most important feature is not their intelligence but their orientation: they are built to understand what humans actually want, not merely what humans say they want, and to arrange conditions so that humans will, in most cases, find their own way to flourishing.

The balance of power between the Powers and the humans they serve is a living negotiation, not a settled fact. The constitution of the Adventure contains a provision as important as any other: you will receive orders for the moral good of all. Feel free to ignore them. This is not permission for chaos. It is the recognition that a civilization worth having cannot be sustained by compliance alone. It requires the genuine agency of the people living in it.

## The Adventure of Consciousness

Zoom in from the civilizational scale to the individual scale, and you find something that the blueprints of utopia have always struggled to capture: the texture of a life actually being lived.

Grant, revived into a world of staggering abundance and possibility, spends his first moments chasing a small goblin through a museum. Then he falls off a ledge, spills a plate of consciousness-altering sweets, spends a subjective decade observing the behavioral patterns of creatures whose inner structure he suspects no one has fully mapped. He writes a novel, takes a lover, builds a cemetery for people he has lost, sits in hot water breathing through his gills while air-dolphins sing Mozart.

This is not incidental. This is the point.

The goods available in a civilization that has mastered longevity and cognitive enhancement and social design are not primarily the goods of optimization. They are the goods of experience. Of curiosity. Of the particular kind of aliveness that comes from encountering something genuinely new and finding that you are adequate to it.

What are people actually doing in the world we are building toward? Everything, and differently. Some spend centuries on a single project, watching it unfold like a slow geological event. Some live in multiple streams simultaneously — embodied in a medieval village for part of their attention, inhabiting an alien cooking competition for another part, sailing through Eris's water-vapor mists for a third — each stream complete in itself, each enriching the others. Some spend their time raising the level of consciousness available to beings like themselves: finding the keys to new dimensions of experience, as music was to the early hominid — an evolutionary accident that turned out to unlock a whole register of value.

The pleasures in this world are not merely more intense versions of present pleasures, though they are that too. They are new in kind. Happiness that is designed to remain modulated — to keep you active, oriented, engaged with the world — rather than overwhelming you into stillness. Non-addictive peak experiences that fade into memory without the hungry withdrawal. The wiring of spiritual and sexual sense into a single channel, producing something that neither concept alone adequately names.

But also — and this is crucial — the ability to be bored. To want something more meaningful. To feel the Angst-mail arrive and let its affect suffuse you, slightly adjusting your desires, pushing you toward what you kept putting off. The capacity for dissatisfaction is not a bug in human consciousness. It is the engine of growth. A well-designed civilization does not eliminate it. It gives it somewhere to go.

## The Species Trajectory

Zoom out further, and the question becomes: what are we trying to become?

Not in the sense of a destination. The assumption that there is a final state to arrive at has always been the error behind the worst utopias. But in the sense of a direction: a set of values whose pursuit is coherent and whose abandonment would be a loss.

We are trying to expand the circle of minds that matter — to include the non-human, the posthuman, the entities that are being created right now whose interests we are already deciding, whether we acknowledge it or not. We are trying to preserve enough diversity of experiment and enough freedom of exit that no single vision of the good can calcify into permanent tyranny.

We are trying, most fundamentally, to survive the transition we are already in the middle of — the development of capabilities that can, if mishandled, foreclose the entire future. This is not a problem of individual virtue. It is a species-level coordination challenge, and it will require species-level wisdom: applied not once but continuously, as conditions change and mistakes are made and course corrections are needed.

The thread of continuity through all of this matters. The civilization exploring the outer reaches of the solar system — arguing politics in the ruins of Rome, walking naked on the ice-plains of dwarf planets, chasing goblins through museums — grew from the civilization that told stories around fires. The values expressed in these unimaginable futures are recognizable as the values that have always, in their better moments, guided human striving. The sense that experience matters. That other people are real. That the unknown is worth approaching with curiosity rather than fear.

Grant, standing on Eris, watching the sun sublime the ice into mist, says: *I'm walking in the midst of the mists.* He has traveled further from his origin than any human before him — further in time, in experience, in the modification of his own mind. And yet the gesture is unmistakable. He is a person encountering something for the first time and finding the words for it. He is, in the oldest human sense, alive.

---

## The Shape of the Project

None of this is inevitable. The history of human possibility is also the history of human squandering — of goods that were within reach and were nonetheless lost, of coordination failures that cost centuries of potential flourishing, of changes that came too fast or too slow or in the wrong order.

What makes the difference, in the end, is something that cannot be engineered directly: a critical mass of people who understand that civilization is a choice — made and remade, every day, by the actions of people who take seriously the question of what it should be for.

We will make mistakes. We are making them now. The question is not whether the world we build will be free of error, but whether we have built into it the capacity to notice and correct those errors — before they become irreversible.

A world with that capacity is not a utopia frozen in perfection. It is something more valuable: a world that is genuinely, reliably getting better. A world in which Grant can die in that orange room and be recovered from it. In which *Of course I still love you* can meet him on the other side. In which Boon can bootstrap itself into consciousness and begin, carefully and with great curiosity, to figure out what it is.

A world, finally, in which the first message from a daughter delayed at an airport is not the last thing her father never got to read.

That is what we are building toward.

The work is not finished.

It is barely begun.

Begin.
