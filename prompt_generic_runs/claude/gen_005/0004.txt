# The Garden and the Stars: A Vision of What We Owe Each Other and the Cosmos

---

## The Question Before All Questions

There is a question that precedes every policy debate, every technological roadmap, every argument about governance and justice and what kind of world we want to build. It is not *how* but *what for* — what is the shape of the thing worth building? Not the tidied-up version of what already exists, with the worst parts smoothed away. Not an optimization of current arrangements toward some metric that captures only a fragment of what humans actually value. Something genuinely, recognizably good — a world, and eventually a cosmos, that anyone who looked upon it carefully would say: *yes, this is what we were reaching toward. This is worth everything it cost to get here.*

I want to describe that world, and that cosmos, as honestly as I can. Not as a blueprint — blueprints presume we know more than we do, and they crush the particular under the general. But as a shape. A direction. A set of commitments about what matters and why, held together by an understanding of what human beings actually need to live well.

The vision is enormous in scope and humble in its certainty. It takes seriously both the immediate, specific, irreplaceable things that make a single human life worth living and the full scale of what physics permits and our deepest values demand. It honors both the person planting a garden and the mind that might one day explore the structure of galaxies — not by finding a compromise between them, but by recognizing that they want different things, and that the universe is large enough to give each of them what they genuinely need.

---

## First, the Floor

Any honest reckoning with the best possible future must begin with the world as it actually is — not as a baseline to be gently improved, but as a moral emergency that demands to be named.

Billions of people share a planet of extraordinary productive capacity, and yet: children grow up without shelter or food, or without a single person whose attention is genuinely theirs. People lose years, sometimes decades, to pain that could have been prevented. Hundreds of thousands end their lives each year not because their situations were unsolvable, but because the coordination required to solve them never materialized. People work the irreplaceable hours of their one life at things that hollow them out, sacrificing themselves to keep themselves not quite drowning. Whole populations have been systematically excluded from the benefits of human progress by accidents of geography and history that no one chose and no moral framework can justify.

This is not a law of nature. It is a failure of organization and will, and it can be changed.

The floor of a genuinely good future is simple to state and radical in its implications: no one dies of a disease we know how to cure. No child grows up without shelter, without food, without at least one person whose attention is truly theirs. The biological systems underlying human health are, after all, just systems — made of atoms, governed by discoverable rules — and with sufficient intelligence applied to them, they yield. Not through miracle, but through the patient compression of what once took a century into what might now take a decade: research threads running in parallel, biological literature held entire in working memory, experimental intuitions refined at a speed no individual human scientist could match.

The cancers that kill slowly. The Alzheimer's that takes a person before their body surrenders. The genetic conditions written into someone at conception. The parasites that still consume enormous fractions of life in the world's poorest regions. These are problems with mechanisms. Mechanisms can be understood. And what can be understood can, with sufficient patience and intelligence, be interrupted.

The same compression of scientific progress that could transform physical medicine can illuminate the mind. Hundreds of millions of people live diminished lives not because their bodies are failing but because their brains are generating experiences — terror, despair, compulsion, disconnection — that bear no useful relationship to the reality around them. Depression is not sadness that went on too long. PTSD is not weakness. Schizophrenia is not a character flaw. These are conditions with mechanisms. They have neurological signatures. They can be understood, and what can be understood can be treated with a sophistication that our current blunt instruments — the drugs and the talk therapy and the waiting lists — only gesture toward.

Beyond treatment lies something more expansive: the recognition that the moments of extraordinary clarity, or compassion, or creative absorption, or love so vivid it seems to change the quality of light — these are not accidents. They are neurological events with causes. They can, with sufficient understanding, be cultivated. Not through the blunt instrument of intoxication, but through a genuine comprehension of what conditions allow the human mind to flourish. The best possible version of yourself is not a fantasy. It is a fact about your nervous system that remains to be fully understood.

But the floor is only the floor. What matters as much as reaching it is that we reach it everywhere — not just where the money is. The cruelty at the heart of progress has always been that its benefits arrive first in wealthy places and diffuse slowly, if at all, to the places that need them most. This is not a law of nature either. Consider what changes when systems can optimize the distribution of health interventions with the same sophistication they bring to their discovery — when epidemiological modeling can predict where a vaccination campaign has maximum impact, when the logistics of reaching remote populations become engineering problems rather than permanent features of the landscape. Consider what changes when AI can serve as a competent economic advisor to governments that have been poorly served by the international consensus for decades, helping more countries pull the levers that drive growth for the benefit of their own people. The goal is not a world where the developing world is helped. It is a world where that category becomes obsolete.

## The Repair of Loneliness

Here is a wound that material abundance, on its own, does not close.

People can be isolated in the middle of cities. They can feel invisible inside families. They can spend years surrounded by people who are fine but not *it* — not the people with whom they could fully become themselves. The absence of genuine connection is one of the most reliable predictors of every form of suffering: depression, addiction, early death, the peculiar quiet despair of a life that was technically adequate but never quite real.

We radically underestimate what is possible here. The technology of connection has always been primitive. We meet people largely by accident — proximity, circumstance, institutional assignment. We are thrown together with classmates and coworkers and told to make the most of it.

This is a matching problem, and matching problems yield to intelligence and information. The friend group that would have made your adolescence meaningful could be assembled. The community where your specific combination of sensibilities and obsessions would finally make you feel arrived could be found. The person whose laugh you would recognize in a dark room, with whom the ordinary satisfactions of a shared life could be accumulated — the infrastructure to find them could be built.

But beyond connection to persons: connection to community, to place, to the sense of belonging to something larger than oneself. The institutions that once provided this — neighborhoods, congregations, civic organizations, guilds — have eroded without adequate replacement. Building their successors, institutions that provide genuine belonging without demanding conformity, is among the most important work of the coming decades. Loneliness is a solvable coordination problem. It deserves to be treated with the same seriousness we bring to disease.

What connection requires, in its deepest form, cannot be simulated away. The difficulty and genuine stakes of dealing with another real consciousness — the ways another person can surprise you, frustrate you, reveal you to yourself — are not bugs to be engineered out of the experience. They are what make connection real. A good future is not one that replaces genuine relationship with a pleasant approximation of it. It is one where the infrastructure exists to find the real thing, and where people have enough time, enough security, enough freedom from the grinding maintenance of survival to actually inhabit their relationships once they find them.

## Escaping the Trap

There is a pattern that runs through human history — the tragedy of commons, the spiral of arms races, the degradation of public discourse — that we might call the trap: each actor, pursuing their own interest rationally in the immediate term, contributes to an outcome that no one would choose and everyone suffers from. The problem is not that people are evil. The problem is that the architecture of incentives repeatedly produces outcomes that betray everyone's deeper values. Invasive brambles crowd out delicate flowers — not because brambles are better, but because brambles are more aggressive.

The exit from this trap requires something like a guardian: not a tyrant, but a structure with genuine authority over the commons. Something that protects the delicate flowers not by eliminating brambles, but by ensuring that being a bramble confers no competitive advantage. Something that watches over the vulnerable not out of charity, but because the vulnerable are part of what makes the garden worth having.

This guardian is not a person or a committee. It is a set of institutions, norms, technologies, and cultural understandings so deeply embedded that defection becomes visibly, practically costly. Its essential ingredient is not trust but *legibility* — making the incentive structure visible so that people can see what game they are actually playing. Courts publish their reasoning so anyone can evaluate whether it is sound. Leaders are described in terms of their actual mandate rather than elevated by titles that obscure accountability. Scientists' results are replicated before they are treated as established. Institutions that made confident wrong predictions are held to those predictions. The feedback loops are short enough and clear enough that failures get corrected rather than entrenched.

This sounds like a modest technical improvement. It is, in practice, a revolution. A civilization where everyone has learned to look at a policy and ask *what are the incentives here, who captures the surplus, what does this look like at equilibrium* — that civilization is playing a fundamentally different game.

Economic literacy needs to become as universal as reading. Housing treated as infrastructure rather than an investment vehicle whose appreciation depends on maintained scarcity. Work reorganized around demonstrated skill rather than costly signals of compliance. Surplus from land and shared resources distributed broadly, because allowing private capture of what belongs to everyone is both unjust and destructive of the coordination that makes abundance possible. None of this requires a particular political ideology. It requires only that people understand what they are doing when they organize the economy one way rather than another.

There are, in addition, absolute limits — non-negotiable, not subject to exception — around the technologies capable of ending civilization: engineered pathogens, misaligned artificial superintelligence, weapons designed for mass destruction. The normal procedure of testing and updating through experience is not available when the downside is irreversible. You cannot learn from extinction. You cannot revise your position after the last mind goes dark. This asymmetry changes the calculus fundamentally, and a civilization that has understood it treats these red lines as inviolable — not contingent on evidence, not subject to negotiation, simply not done.

## What a Good Life Actually Requires

Before we can describe a good future in its larger dimensions, we need to be honest about what a single good life actually requires. Not in the abstract — not "pleasure" or "flourishing" as placeholders — but in the concrete, because the concrete is where the important distinctions live.

A good life requires challenge appropriately scaled to capacity. Not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement. The frustration of a bad challenge — arbitrary, meaningless, or simply cruel — differs in kind from the struggle of a good one: a problem worth solving, a skill worth developing, a relationship difficult enough to be real. As capacities grow, the challenges worth pursuing become richer. A more capable mind can find depth in structures that would have seemed simple to an earlier version of itself. The supply of worthy challenges does not run out. It expands with the minds that pursue it.

A good life requires the experience of living by one's own strength. There is a specific satisfaction — not easily substituted — in doing something yourself, in understanding how a thing works, in building rather than merely consuming, in solving rather than being solved-for. A world where powerful systems handle all problems fails along this dimension regardless of how comfortable it is. We want to be the protagonist of our own story, not passengers watching scenery pass.

A good life requires genuine novelty — not the simulation of novelty, the endless recycling of familiar forms with different surface textures, but the real thing. Encounters with what you did not expect. Understanding you had not yet achieved. Beauty that arrives from a direction you were not watching. The fear that a long life would exhaust the available novelty is probably unfounded. The space of interesting things is not a fixed inventory being depleted. It is something more like mathematics: inexhaustible, self-generating, opening new territories as fast as it is explored.

A good life requires purpose that points outward. People who feel they are contributing to something larger than themselves are more resilient, more engaged, more genuinely alive than people focused primarily on their own satisfaction. This is not a psychological quirk to be corrected. It reflects something true about the structure of value: that caring about things outside yourself is intrinsically part of a good life, not merely instrumentally useful for producing one.

And underlying all of it: a good life requires appropriate predictability and genuine surprise simultaneously. A world whose laws no one can understand, or whose powerful systems intervene unpredictably in individual lives, makes genuine self-determination impossible even if it is nominally permitted. And yet a world fully legible and controllable would lack something equally essential: the quality of reality being larger than expected, of opening outward, of discovering there is more than you thought. The stable ground and the unknown horizon both.

## The Full Spectrum of What We Value

Here is something easy to miss when imagining the best possible future: human beings do not have one kind of value. We have several, and they are genuinely different in their structure.

Some values are *grounded* — anchored in specific people, places, relationships, and times. You care about your children, not children in general. You love a particular city, a particular stretch of coastline, a particular friend whose laugh you would recognize in a dark room. These values saturate. Once your children are thriving, the fact that millions of other children elsewhere are also thriving does not satisfy this particular caring, because this caring was always about *these children*. Once Jerusalem is safe, the safety of a billion other cities does not add to your caring about Jerusalem. Grounded values are about specific things, not about quantities of abstract good.

Other values operate differently — they are *expansive*, scaling with reality rather than saturating at human scale. If you care about the total presence of conscious experience in the universe, then more conscious experience is genuinely better, without limit. If you care about understanding as a real property of the cosmos rather than merely as a human achievement, then minds exploring the structure of distant galaxies are doing something genuinely more valuable than minds explaining local tidal patterns, in the same way that a complete map is more valuable than a partial one.

Here is the crucial observation: these two families of value are not in competition. They want different things, and the universe is large enough to give each of them what they genuinely need. Grounded values want specific proximal resources — the Earth, the solar system, relationships that persist through time. Expansive values want what is scarce to no one: the vast majority of the cosmos, the trillions of years that follow. The portion of the universe needed to satisfy our deepest grounded values is, by any astronomical measure, a rounding error. The portion available for expansive values is essentially everything.

This is not a compromise in which both sides lose something. It is the structure of the genuinely good: a cosmos organized so that what we care about most, we have the most of.

## The Architecture of the Garden

With this in mind, consider what the best possible future might actually look like — not as a blueprint, but as a shape.

The organizing principle is simple: different values get the resources that fit them, and nothing is converted into something else without the consent of those who value it as it is.

The innermost regions of the cosmos — Earth, the solar system, perhaps the local cluster — are the domain of grounded values. Here, people live recognizably human lives, scaled upward in quality across a long progression rather than compressed into a single simultaneous ideal. The earliest stratum looks like our world made genuinely humane: the floor achieved, disease and poverty addressed with the seriousness they deserve. Subsequent strata add longevity — centuries rather than decades — and material abundance sufficient that no one faces the particular degradations of scarcity. Further: the optional expansion of biological and cognitive capability, the freedom to inhabit the body and mind one actually wants. And beyond that: the possibility of simulated worlds running on substrate optimized not for evolutionary history but for whatever physics best supports rich experience.

Each stratum is inhabited by beings who have, in some meaningful sense, chosen it. No stratum is a starting point to be abandoned — it is a permanent home for the values that want it. Sacred sites remain sacred. Relationships remain continuous. The things that grounded values care about are not converted into anything else. They endure.

Crucially, these regions are not isolated from the larger arc of what consciousness might become. They are connected to it through membranes that are permeable in the right direction. People can choose to move outward, into forms of being richer and stranger than what they began with. They can be, at the end of a long life or at a moment of chosen transformation, carried forward into something they could not have imagined from where they started — while retaining the thread of continuity that makes it still *them* who experience the change. The ember becomes the fire, but it is the same fire.

The membranes also protect the inner regions from a specific danger: the difficulty of finding meaning at ordinary human scale when incomprehensible optimization is happening next door. The person planting a garden should not have their attention continuously interrupted by the awareness that entities in distant reaches of the cosmos are computing something of unimaginable scope. This protection is not censorship. It is the preservation of the cognitive and emotional conditions under which grounded values can be actually *felt* rather than merely acknowledged. Meaning is experiential, not propositional. The conditions for meaning need to be maintained.

The outer reaches of the cosmos — the vast majority of the observable universe, the trillions of years beyond the burning of the last ordinary stars — belong to expansive values. Here, minds of kinds we cannot fully imagine pursue goods of kinds we can partly recognize: understanding, beauty, the full exploration of what is possible for consciousness. These minds are continuous with us in the way we are continuous with our ancestors — genuinely descended, genuinely shaped by what came before, and yet genuinely different in their mode of being and the range of their experience. The gap between them and us is not a rupture but an extrapolation: a long arc bending toward what we already value when we are most awake.

The moments that most reveal what consciousness is capable of — genuine love, real understanding, beauty that arrives like a fact about the world — have a quality of *greater aliveness*, of the world being revealed rather than constructed. These moments are not the ceiling. They are evidence of a direction. The direction continues, past what we can see from here, into forms of experience that bear the same relationship to our best moments as music bears to a pure tone.

## The Diversity of Flourishing

Any single vision of the good future must eventually acknowledge its own limits. A genuinely good future is one in which *many* different visions of flourishing exist simultaneously — not because we are indifferent to the differences, but because the universe is large enough and human longing varied enough that a single template for the good life would be a kind of poverty.

Some people will want a world of technological exuberance: morphological freedom, the ability to reshape their minds and bodies, to experience time and space at scales that make the present human lifespan feel like an afternoon, to encounter forms of beauty that have no names yet. They will build strange new architectures of experience and push the boundaries of what consciousness can become, and this is genuinely wonderful.

Some people will want something older and quieter — a real garden on real earth, with actual soil under their hands and actual seasons and the ancient satisfactions of growing things. They will want to know the names of birds, to face real hardship and find in it the particular satisfaction of a life that has not been managed into safety from every angle.

Some people will want elaborate reconstructions of historical societies — not because they are confused about history, but because they find in those forms something unavailable elsewhere: a particular texture of community, a particular relationship to mortality, a particular spiritual framework that only makes sense inside its original cultural architecture.

All of these are legitimate. All deserve a home. The role of the guardian is not to choose among them but to ensure that the pursuit of one does not foreclose the others — that the techno-enthusiasts do not remake the shared world against the will of those who want it left alone, that those who want it left alone cannot veto the exploration of those who want to range far. Strong norms on the things that matter universally — no one builds civilizational weapons, no one prevents others from leaving communities they were born into, no one poisons the shared commons — combined with enormous latitude on everything else.

The diversity is not a problem to be managed. It is most of the point.

## What This Asks of Us Now

The vision described here is not inevitable. It is one possible shape of the future among many, most of which are worse. The difference between futures depends on choices made by people alive today — choices about how to develop powerful technologies, what values to embed in the systems that will increasingly shape collective life, whether to preserve the conditions under which human-scale meaning is possible while also protecting the long-term potential of mind to become more than it currently is.

The most important things to do now are not to design the details of the vision. They are to get a small number of structural things right.

*Preserve optionality.* The worst futures are not merely bad — they are locked. They are worlds in which some early choice closes off the full range of value, in which a single value system wins too soon and the entire cosmos is converted to satisfy it before there was wisdom sufficient to know whether that was actually good. The primary moral imperative of the present moment is to avoid irreversible narrowing. Keep the doors open. Keep the strata possible.

*Take seriously the full range of what humans value.* The temptation, in thinking about the long-run future, is to privilege the values that scale — the ones that seem important from a cosmic perspective, that apply to large numbers of entities over large spans of time. But grounded values are real values. The person who cares about their particular children, their particular city, their particular traditions is not simply confused or short-sighted. They are responding to something genuinely important. A future that converted everything to cosmic optimization would lose something real, not merely something sentimental. Both families of value need to be carried forward.

*Maintain the conditions for genuine meaning.* Meaning is not a property of world-states. It is an experience that arises in beings under certain conditions: appropriate challenge, genuine connection, the experience of living by one's own strength, the sense of contributing to something real. These conditions can be engineered away in the name of comfort or efficiency. A good future is one that protects them at every stratum, not just the innermost ones.

*Hold the vision without making it a blueprint.* The history of people who were certain they knew the shape of the ideal future is not encouraging. The task is not to implement a Utopia but to preserve the possibility of one — to avoid the catastrophes that would foreclose the long arc, to build the wisdom and cooperation that would allow later, wiser beings to continue the project. We are not the last generation. We are, if we do our work, somewhere near the beginning.

## The Thread That Holds

There is a structure to all of this that can get lost in the particulars.

It is not, fundamentally, a vision about technology. It is a vision about what technology is for — which is to say, about what human beings are for — which is to say, about what we owe each other and the cosmos we inhabit.

The compression of suffering. The liberation of minds trapped in their own distorted signals. The extension of genuine opportunity to people excluded from the benefits of progress by accidents of geography and history. The construction of political structures that protect the vulnerable and constrain the powerful. The repair of the loneliness that hollows lives from the inside. The opening of space for meaning to be pursued rather than survival to be managed. And beyond all this: the careful, patient, humble project of carrying forward everything that consciousness has learned to value — in all its forms, across all its registers — into a cosmos large enough to hold it.

These are not new aspirations. They predate computing, predate printing, predate writing itself. They are what has always been meant when people spoke of a better world, even when the path to that world was completely obscure.

What is new is that the path is becoming less obscure. Not clear — it is not clear. There are enormous risks and the possibility of catastrophic failure is real and must be taken with full seriousness. But for the first time in history, the accumulated capabilities of our species have reached a point where the oldest human dreams are beginning to look less like dreams and more like problems. And problems, unlike dreams, can be worked on.

Every generation has handed the next a slightly different world. The generation that defeated smallpox. The generation that fed the billions who would have starved. The generation that built the institutions through which disputes could be resolved without mass death. Each achievement was, at the time, considered by many to be impossible. Each required people willing to work on it anyway.

We are now those people. The work is ours to do.

## The Door

At the edge of what we can currently imagine, there is something that deserves to be named.

The universe has been running for 13.8 billion years — through the formation of galaxies, the slow cooking of elements in stellar cores, the long evolutionary patience that produced minds capable of asking what any of it is for. As far as we can tell, the only minds capable of asking that question are here, on this small world, in the bodies of a species that has been anatomically modern for 300,000 years, that invented writing 5,000 years ago, and that is now, in the span of a single human lifetime, developing systems of intelligence whose implications it does not yet fully understand.

Every person alive today is, without melodrama, alive at the hinge. The decisions made in the next few decades will shape what the next trillion years contain — or whether they contain anything recognizable as good at all. The pattern that wins this period will be propagated forward. If what wins is the logic of the trap — the race to the bottom, the sacrifice of all beauty to competitive advantage — then the future will be vast and purposeful and empty of everything that anyone would have chosen.

If what wins is the capacity of minds to look forward, see what is beautiful, and work to bring it into being — then what follows exceeds imagination, in the best possible way.

But here is what needs to be said: the door that opens onto that future is not a single threshold. It is a long corridor that begins here, in the work of making sure no child grows up without people who love them, no one dies in unnecessary pain, no one is excluded from the conversation about what the future should look like. The cosmic and the intimate are not two different projects. They are the same project, understood at different scales.

The person planting a garden is not doing a worse version of what a vast post-human mind does when it explores the structure of the universe. They are doing something different, which is valuable in a different way. The cosmos worth building is one that makes room for both — that carries forward both the grounded and the expansive, both the ancient satisfactions of a human life well-lived and the extraordinary, as-yet-unnamed possibilities that lie at the far end of what consciousness might become.

The door is there. The task is to keep it open until we are wise enough to walk through it well — and then, on the other side, to keep walking.

What lies beyond is not the elimination of what we are. It is the continuation of what we are, in every direction at once — toward the ordinary made more fully itself, and toward the extraordinary that the ordinary always contained in potential.

The universe has been waiting long enough.

Let us choose the garden. Let us choose the stars. Let us choose a world where every child finds their people, where every person can become what they were reaching toward, where the long inheritance of human longing — all the reaching and building and failing and trying again — arrives somewhere it would recognize as home. And then, when home is secure, let us choose to keep going — into the vast, the strange, the beautiful, the as-yet-unnameable — carrying everything we love with us, as far as love can reach.

That is possible.

Every last one of us is what it is possible for.
