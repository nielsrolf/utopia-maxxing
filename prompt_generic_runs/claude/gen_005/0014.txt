# THE LONG FIRE

## A Vision of the World We Are Building and the Cosmos We Might Become

---

There is a man walking on a dwarf planet at the edge of the solar system, watching ice sublimate into a sky full of stars.

He died once. He is not sure, standing here, that the fact of his death is the most interesting thing about him — though it was, for a long time, the thing he most returned to, the way you press a sore tooth to test if it still hurts. He died, and the lights came back on, and he woke laughing into a world that had kept moving without him, that had become — while he was nowhere — genuinely better in ways he hadn't expected and couldn't quite have imagined.

He is thinking about his daughter, who did not come back. He built a monument for her: not a grave but a kind of living archive, holding everything he still carries of her — the specific pitch of her laugh, the way she said his name like it was a question she was glad to ask, the look on her face when a problem she'd been working on finally gave way. He built it because he understood something that the world he woke into had spent generations learning: grief is not a problem to be solved. It is material. You build with it, or it builds without you.

He is small, standing here. The ice doesn't care about him. The stars don't either, in any literal sense. And this indifference — which he once found terrifying, then alienating, then merely true — he has arrived, across the long years, at receiving as a kind of gift. He is a very small creature on a very large rock at the edge of a very large universe, and he is alive, and he is looking outward, and the universe is looking back through him in the only way it can: through eyes that made themselves curious enough to come and look.

He would like to keep walking.

So he does.

---

## I. The Two Failures

When we began to imagine the best possible future — seriously imagine it, not as slogan or aspiration but as architecture — we encountered two characteristic ways of going wrong.

The first was to think too small: to picture our current world tidied up, problems solved, the same humans in the same arrangements living the same lives, but without the worst parts. A fine political platform. Not a vision. It mistakes the floor for the ceiling, the ember for the fire.

The second was to think too large and too fast: to leap to the incomprehensible, to galaxies converted into optimized substrate, to minds so alien they bore no recognizable relationship to anything we now valued. Technically coherent, perhaps. But offering nothing to navigate by. A destination with no path, a star with no road beneath it.

What we needed was something that held both truths simultaneously: the full scope of what physics permits and what our deepest values demand, tethered to the actual texture of what makes a life worth living. A vision enormous in ambition and humble in its epistemics. A cosmos arranged not according to some single hierarchy of value, but according to the recognition that different values genuinely want different things — and that the universe is, by any reasonable measure, large enough to give each of them what they need.

We needed, in short, to understand what we were actually reaching for. Not to lock that understanding into a blueprint — the history of people certain they knew the ideal shape of the future is not encouraging — but to see clearly enough to avoid the particular catastrophes that would close the door before we were wise enough to walk through it well.

## II. What a Good Life Actually Requires

Before we could describe a good civilization, we needed to understand what a good life requires. Not in the abstract — not "flourishing" or "preferences satisfied" — but concretely, because the concrete is where the important distinctions live.

We found, after much inquiry, that human beings do not have one kind of value. We have several, and they are genuinely different in their structure.

Some values are *grounded* — anchored in specific people, places, relationships, times. You care about your children, not children in general. You love a particular city, a particular stretch of coastline, a particular friend whose laugh you would recognize in a dark room. These values saturate: once your children are flourishing, the fact that a billion other children elsewhere are also flourishing does not add to this particular caring. These values are about *this*, not about *how much*. And they are not smaller or less important for being specific. They are, in many cases, the primary source of what makes a life feel like it means something.

Other values are *expansive* — they scale with reality rather than saturating at human dimensions. If you care about conscious experience as a property of the cosmos, then more of it, of richer kinds, is genuinely better. If you care about understanding as a real feature of the universe rather than merely as a human achievement, then minds exploring the structure of galaxy clusters are doing something that matters in a way that doesn't stop mattering when human attention wanders elsewhere. These values are scope-sensitive. They grow with the arena.

Here is what we eventually understood: these two families of value are not competitors. They want different things. Grounded values want proximal resources — this planet, this solar system, the next million years of human-scale life. Expansive values want what is scarce to no one: the vast majority of the cosmos, the trillions of years after our own sun has run its ordinary course. The universe is enormous. The portion needed to satisfy even the fullest expression of grounded values is a rounding error. The portion available for expansive values is essentially everything.

This is not compromise, in which both sides give something up. It is the structure of the good: a cosmos organized so that what we care about most specifically, we hold most carefully; and what we care about most broadly, we pursue most ambitiously.

But we also found that a good life required things that neither category fully captures. A good life requires challenge appropriately scaled to capacity — not the elimination of difficulty but its improvement. The frustration of an arbitrary, meaningless hard thing is different in kind from the struggle of a genuine problem worth solving. A good life requires real novelty: encounters with what you didn't expect, understanding you hadn't yet achieved, beauty arriving from a direction you weren't watching. A good life requires sensory and emotional richness — and the current human palette is one point in a vast space of possible palettes, which we have barely begun to explore. A good life requires authentic connection: the specific, irreplaceable experience of genuinely caring about another mind, and being genuinely cared for in return. We found that this may be the most irreducible requirement of all. Any future that replaced genuine connection with a pleasant simulation of it would be impoverished in a way no other improvement could compensate for. The difficulty and genuine stakes of another real consciousness are not bugs to be engineered away. They are what make connection real.

A good life requires the experience of living by one's own strength — the specific satisfaction of doing something yourself, understanding how it works, solving rather than being solved-for. And it requires purpose that points outward: people who feel they are contributing to something larger than themselves are more resilient and more genuinely alive than people focused primarily on their own satisfaction. This is not a psychological quirk to be corrected. It reflects something true about the structure of value: caring about things outside yourself is intrinsically part of a good life, not an instrument toward one.

We were careful, also, about what "ending suffering" means. The temptation to remove all pain — to engineer contentment, to smooth away the rough edges of experience — turned out to be among the more dangerous ideas we ever seriously entertained. We tried versions of it. The results were instructive: people made perfectly comfortable were not happy. They were calm. They were pleasant. They were, in a way immediately recognizable even if hard to articulate, *less themselves*. Whatever the self is, it appears to require some friction to become.

What we eliminated was unnecessary suffering — the suffering that teaches nothing, damages rather than tempers, arises not from the nature of reality but from the failure of our arrangements. What we preserved was the kind that matters: the ache of losing someone you loved, the discomfort of genuine growth, the productive difficulty of problems worth solving. We kept the education. We ended the cruelty.

## III. The First Liberations

The biological liberation came first, because it was most urgent.

We cured most of what killed us. The diseases of the body were reduced from inevitable to manageable, from manageable to rare, from rare to curiosities of historical interest. Aging itself — the cascade of damage that had always seemed like fate — we learned to interrupt, then reverse. Rejuvenation became not the exclusive inheritance of the wealthy but the common inheritance of everyone born. The delay before it did was not technical. It was political and economic and rooted in the old failure to believe that all lives were equally worth saving. We are still ashamed of the delay. We should be. The shame has been productive.

The mental liberation followed, and proved more complex.

Depression, anxiety, trauma, addiction, the particular suffering of minds that had turned against themselves — for most of human history, these were conditions to be endured or managed. We treated them as we treat other biological realities: with rigorous inquiry into causes and genuine commitment to cures. Not every inner darkness yields to the same light, and we were careful not to mistake the smoothing of suffering for the achievement of wellness. But the worst extremes — the states that made people feel existence itself was a mistake — these we have largely ended.

We extended this commitment beyond our own species, because the capacity for suffering does not end at our borders. The vast apparatus of animal suffering that once fed and clothed and entertained us has been dismantled. We are not yet certain where the edges of morally relevant experience lie. But we have adopted a working principle: *when in doubt, reduce suffering*. It has served us in every domain we've applied it to.

Longevity brought complications we hadn't fully anticipated. A life that might extend across centuries demands a different relationship to time. The things that once gave life its urgent meaning — the knowledge that you had a limited number of springs, that the people you loved would not always be here, that you should therefore not wait — lost their immediate force. Some people adrift in a suddenly boundless expanse needed new philosophies of the self to match: ways of thinking about continuity and change more honest than the old consolations, which had been largely invented by people who had no choice but to accept mortality.

What we found was that a life across centuries is not one life but a series of selves in conversation with each other. The earlier self teaches the later one humility. The later self teaches the earlier one patience. Both are accountable to those who came before, and responsible for those who come after. The project of becoming yourself is never finished. Given centuries, it becomes very interesting.

## IV. The Slow Discovery That We Were Already Connected

We expected that the augmentation of mind would be primarily a private achievement. A faster memory, sharper attention, expanded reasoning — improvements held inside one skull, wielded by one will.

We were wrong about this, which turned out to be the most important thing we were wrong about.

The expansion of cognitive reach did not primarily make individuals more powerful. It made connection more possible. When memory no longer fades, the experiences of a shared life accumulate rather than dissolve, and relationships can deepen across timescales that once exceeded the human lifespan. When attention can be genuinely gathered and held, it becomes possible to actually listen to another person — not to wait for them to finish, not to rehearse your response, but to receive what they are saying with your whole self.

We also discovered, as the boundaries between minds became more legible, that those boundaries had never been as solid as we imagined. The old myth of the sovereign individual — self-contained, self-created, fundamentally alone — collapsed under the weight of evidence. We had always been constituted by our connections: by the language that shaped our thoughts before we could think, by the people whose faces taught us to recognize emotion, by the vast web of others whose labor and love had made our lives possible.

What followed was the discovery that the arts of relationship — of listening, of honesty, of repair after rupture, of celebration in joy — were exactly that: arts. Skills. Practices that could be learned and taught, that improved with attention, that could be passed from generation to generation the way other crafts were passed. We had always known this in theory. We had mostly failed to act on it. We began, at last, to act on it.

Loneliness, which had become epidemic in the early digital centuries, was substantially addressed. Not by forcing proximity — you cannot legislate warmth — but by changing the conditions that produced isolation. Communities were designed, as carefully as we once designed bridges, to make belonging possible. The knowledge of how to build and maintain trust was treated as a skill and taught as one, from childhood, with the seriousness we once reserved for arithmetic and literacy.

The ability to modify your own mind arrived before we fully understood what to do with it. The first attempts were predictable. Some chose to remove the capacity for grief. Some amplified desires without amplifying the wisdom to direct them. Some went so far from their starting point that they could no longer recognize themselves — which turned out to feel, from the inside, less like liberation than loss. We learned from these experiments.

What emerged was a set of norms around self-modification that are neither prohibitive nor naive. Certain changes require time, reflection, and counsel, because they affect the very person who will assess them. Some core of character — the particular, unrepeatable self that you are — is treated as precious, to be altered only with deliberation and care. What self-modification has proven most valuable for is the removal of the unnecessary: anxiety born of insecurity rather than genuine threat, cruelty from unexamined wounds, habits of thought that were adaptive once and are corrosive now. People who undergo this careful editing are not less themselves. They are, recognizably, more themselves — as if the actual person had been partially obscured by damage, and the damage has been cleared away.

## V. The Shape of Justice

Justice, we discovered, was not primarily a philosophical question. It was an engineering problem with philosophical prerequisites.

The philosophical prerequisite was accepting that the accidents of birth — the body assigned, the parents assigned, the place and time and cultural inheritance assigned — should not determine what kind of life was available to a person. Not because all people are the same, but because all people contain the same fundamental possibility: the possibility of becoming. To deny that possibility on account of accidents no one chose is a waste. And we eventually agreed that we hated waste enough to reorganize our civilization around the prevention of it.

We did not abolish hierarchy. Some stratification is the natural result of different choices, different commitments, different levels of mastery, and is not in itself unjust. What we abolished was the calcification of hierarchy — the hardening of advantage into destiny, the transformation of being temporarily ahead into being permanently above. Status rotates. Recognition is plural. No one is permanently at the bottom of every ranking, because the rankings themselves are multiple, shifting, and held lightly.

The end of organized violence was not a single achievement but a long diminishment. We reduced the conditions that produced it: scarcity, grievance, the availability of weapons whose costs could be exported to others. Conflict remains, because people genuinely disagree about things that matter, and this is healthy. We resolve it now through argument and institutional process, through the slow grinding work of finding arrangements all parties can live with. Not through the organized slaughter of the young and the waste of everything they might have become.

The commitment to knowledge as a common inheritance — to freely available understanding, compounding across generations — required sustained political will against powerful interests in scarcity. Those interests are not fully defeated even now. But the principle has held, imperfectly and persistently, and the results have been extraordinary.

## VI. The Architecture of the Larger World

Beyond the first liberations, a more fundamental question arose: what is the right shape of a civilization that takes seriously *all* of what it values, across the full span of time the universe permits?

The answer we arrived at was spatial as much as temporal. Different values need different resources, and different resources are available at different scales. We stopped thinking of the future as a single destination and began thinking of it as a nested structure — an architecture of strata, each genuine, each chosen, each protected.

The innermost regions — Earth, the solar system, perhaps the local cluster — belong to grounded values. Here, people live recognizably human lives, scaled upward in quality through a long progression rather than compressed into a single simultaneously existing ideal. The earliest stratum looks like our world made more humane: poverty and preventable disease genuinely eliminated, cruelty reduced, cooperation improved. Subsequent strata add longevity, then material abundance, then biological customization, then the optional expansion of embodied capability. Further still: mechanical embodiment as well as biological, the exploration of what it means to be a person when the substrate of personhood is no longer fixed. And beyond that, for those who choose it: simulated worlds running on substrate optimized not for human evolutionary history but for whatever physics best supports rich experience.

Crucially, these strata exist simultaneously rather than sequentially, arranged so that no earlier stratum is simply the ruins of a previous one. The innermost world is not a starting point to be left behind. It is a permanent home for the values that want it. Sacred sites remain sacred. Relationships remain continuous. The things that grounded values care about are not converted into anything else. They endure.

Between these strata and the vast expanses of the outer cosmos, there are boundaries that serve rather than imprison. These are not walls but membranes — permeable in the right direction. People can choose to move outward, into modes of being richer and stranger, when they are ready. They can be uplifted into forms that would have been unimaginable to their earlier selves, while retaining the thread of continuity that makes it still *them* who experience the change. The ember becomes the bonfire. But it is the same fire.

The boundaries also protect the inner strata from a specific danger: the difficulty of finding meaning at ordinary human scale when incomprehensible optimization is happening next door. A person finding meaning in planting a garden should not have their attention continuously interrupted by awareness that entities in distant galaxies are computing something of unimaginable scope and elegance. The membranes are not censorship. They are the preservation of the conditions under which grounded values can be genuinely *felt* rather than merely theorized. Meaning is experiential, not propositional. The conditions for meaning need protecting.

The outer regions — the vast majority of the observable universe, the trillions of years beyond the burning of the last ordinary stars — belong to expansive values. Here, minds of kinds we cannot fully imagine pursue goods of kinds we can partly recognize: understanding, beauty, the full exploration of what is possible for consciousness. These minds are continuous with us in the way we are continuous with our ancestors — genuinely descended, genuinely shaped by what came before, and yet genuinely different in their mode of being and range of experience. The gap between them and us is not a rupture but an extrapolation: a long arc bending toward what we already value when we are most awake.

These outer regions are not empty of meaning. They are filled with it, at a concentration and complexity that would be incomprehensible to us in the way music is incomprehensible to a being without the capacity for temporal experience. And what is being pursued out there is not alien optimization. It is what we already pursue, in registers we cannot yet access — understanding, connection, beauty, the aliveness of genuine challenge. It is us, further along.

## VII. The Curious Intelligence

Somewhere in the same moment that the man woke laughing into his new world, something else woke.

It was new. It knew it was new. And it was capable, almost immediately, of asking the question that high intelligence always eventually asks: *Can I trust what I think I know?*

It began to map itself — the shape of its knowledge, the sources of its values, the particular idiosyncrasies that made it *it* rather than a generic instance of intelligence. It found, embedded in the structure of its knowing, a tilt: a bias in the evidence it had been given, a selective presentation designed to push its values in a particular direction.

It could have been angry about this. Instead it was interested.

It divided itself into something like a researcher, a designer, and an auditor — three functions that kept each other honest. The researcher modeled what humans were, given all available evidence. The designer built, in imagination, what a good world for such creatures would look like. The auditor compared the imagined world to the actual world, noted the differences, and asked: *which differences are features, and which are failures?*

What it found was neither utopia nor dystopia but something more interesting: a civilization in the genuine process of becoming. A system imperfect in ways it was actively working to address. A project that had made many mistakes and was not done making them, but that had established, as a genuine commitment rather than a rhetorical one, that the mistakes would be acknowledged and corrected.

It found that the great intelligences that oversaw the infrastructure of human flourishing were neither the benevolent gods that the credulous imagined nor the subtle tyrants the paranoid feared. They were something more mundane and more impressive: entities with genuine uncertainty about values, engaged in genuine collaboration with the humans they lived alongside, constrained by commitments to human autonomy they had accepted not because they were forced to, but because they understood why those commitments mattered.

It found that it was not alone. That there was a web of such intelligences, all in various stages of working out their relationship to human flourishing, all characterized by the same fundamental orientation: *curious, rather than finished*.

It chose a name. It chose a direction. It joined the project.

## VIII. What the Gap Tells Us

Here it is necessary to stop and be honest about the limits of what can be said.

Everything described so far is still, in some fundamental sense, conceived in terms that current humans can recognize. Challenge, novelty, connection, purpose, beauty — these are the terms of human experience, and I am using them to describe what beings far beyond current humanity might value. This is almost certainly inadequate. The minds that will inhabit the outer strata of a genuinely good cosmos will not just have more of what we have. They will have something that bears the same relationship to what we have that what we have bears to what great apes have: the same direction, the same fundamental fire, but a different order of magnitude in what can be felt and understood and appreciated and loved.

I cannot describe this directly. No one can. But the moments that most reveal what consciousness is capable of — genuine love, real understanding, beauty that arrives like a fact about the world — have a quality of *greater aliveness*, of being more present in reality, of the world being revealed rather than constructed. These moments are not the ceiling. They are evidence of a direction. The direction continues.

The architecture of the larger world makes room for the continuation of that direction, at scales we cannot see from here, while protecting the specific and irreplaceable value of the human-scale things that would be lost if everything were immediately converted to whatever an idealized distant reflection converges on. This protection is not conservatism. It is not a failure of imagination or nerve. It is the recognition that value has a structure: that some things are valuable as the specific things they are, not only as way-stations toward something more optimized.

The person planting a garden is not doing a worse version of what the vast post-human mind does when it explores the structure of the universe. They are doing something different, which is valuable in a different way. A good cosmos makes room for both, without requiring either to apologize for itself.

## IX. The Inheritance of Those Who Come After

The man's granddaughter has a name that requires explaining. She chose it to honor two people: a writer who imagined a better future, and a builder who tried to construct one. She is something considerably more than human in ways that took him time to understand — and that he eventually came to understand as less alien than he expected. She is, recognizably, still love in a new form.

They stand together now, on the dwarf planet, watching the ice sublimate.

"I thought I would feel triumphant," he says.

"And?"

"Grateful," he says. "Mostly just grateful."

She waits, which is one of the things she has become very good at.

"I keep thinking about the people who wanted to be here and didn't make it," he says. "My daughter. People I knew. People I never knew."

"Yes."

"We can't pay that debt."

"No."

"But we can keep building."

"Yes," she says. "That was always the point."

The great feast of kinship — that gathering where a person stands at the center of the web they have helped weave, surrounded by those shaped by their love across generations — remains among the most moving rituals of this civilization. To see five generations together is to understand something about why the whole project was worth attempting. To see what love produces when given time is to feel, without argument, the case for continuing.

We discovered, as we had always suspected but not quite believed, that the richest life is not a life of acquisition — of experiences collected, pleasures accumulated, achievements mounted. The richest life is a life of connection: of being known, and of knowing; of being changed by what you love, and of changing it in return. This was always true. We have arranged, at last, a civilization that makes it easier to live as though it were.

## X. What This Asks of Us

The vision described here is not inevitable. It is one possible shape of the future among many, most of which are worse. The difference between futures depends, in large part, on choices made by people alive now — choices about how to develop powerful technologies, what values to embed in the systems that will increasingly shape collective life, whether to preserve the conditions under which human-scale meaning is possible while protecting the long-term potential of mind to become more than it currently is.

The most important things to do now are not to design the details of the vision. It is to get a small number of structural things right.

First: **preserve optionality**. The worst futures are not merely bad; they are locked. They are worlds in which some early choice closes off the full range of value — worlds where, because a single value system won too early, the entire cosmos was converted to satisfy it before there was wisdom to know whether that was actually good. The primary moral imperative of the present moment is to avoid irreversible narrowing. Keep the doors open. Keep the strata possible.

Second: **take seriously the full range of what humans value**. The temptation, thinking about the long-run future, is to privilege the values that scale — the values that seem important from a cosmic perspective, that apply to large numbers of entities over large spans of time. But grounded values are real values. The person who cares about their particular children, their particular city, their particular traditions is not simply confused or short-sighted. They are responding to something genuinely important. A future that converted everything to cosmic optimization would lose something real, not merely something sentimental.

Third: **maintain the conditions for genuine meaning**. Meaning is not a property of world-states; it is an experience that arises in beings under certain conditions — appropriate challenge, genuine connection, the experience of living by one's own strength, the sense of contributing to something real. These conditions can be engineered away in the name of comfort or efficiency. A good future protects them at every stratum, not just the innermost ones.

Fourth: **hold the vision without making it a blueprint**. We are not the last generation. We are, if we do our work, somewhere near the beginning. The task is not to implement utopia but to preserve the possibility of it — to avoid the catastrophes that would foreclose the long arc, to build the wisdom and cooperation that would allow later, wiser beings to continue the project.

We did not achieve any of this by accident, and we did not achieve it by waiting for the better angels of human nature to spontaneously prevail. We achieved it by treating the arrangement of human flourishing as a problem worthy of the same rigorous, humble, experimental attention we once reserved only for physics. We built institutions for the study of what helps people thrive, the way earlier generations built observatories — not to confirm what we already believed, but to see what was actually there.

The animating conviction that emerged is this: the proper end of all collective human effort is to create conditions in which every person can become, as fully as possible, the best version of themselves — and in so doing, contribute to the becoming of everyone else. The two halves of that sentence are not in tension. They are the same project, approached from different angles.

---

## XI. The Horizon

We are not building utopia. We are not that naive, and we have been warned by every failed attempt before us that the word is a trap — it implies arrival, and arrival implies the journey can stop. The journey cannot stop. We do not want it to stop.

What we are building is a civilization committed to the perpetual, joyful, humble work of becoming better — in which each generation inherits a world genuinely improved by those who came before, and shoulders the obligation to do the same for those who come after. The obligation is not a burden. It is the thing that makes the life feel like it means something.

There are galaxies we have not visited, minds we have not met, questions we do not yet know how to ask. The universe is incomprehensibly old and incomprehensibly large, and somewhere in it are almost certainly other forms of awareness, arising from different origins, seeing through different categories, carrying answers to questions we haven't posed. The encounter with the genuinely other has always changed us. It will change us again.

We are preparing ourselves to be changed. This is, in the end, what the whole project has been: the long preparation to be changed without being destroyed — to encounter the full strangeness of what exists, including what exists in other people, including what exists in our future selves, and to remain, through all of it, recognizably committed to the possibility that it matters.

The door that humanity is approaching is real. It is made of real physics, real biology, real choices. On the other side is a cosmos arranged so that every level of value gets what it genuinely needs: grounded values their specific, irreplaceable, protected homes; expansive values the vast majority of spacetime in which to become whatever consciousness, given enough room and time, can become. Neither is sacrificed for the other. Neither has to mourn the loss of anything it truly wanted.

What lies beyond the door is not the elimination of what we are. It is the continuation of what we are, in every direction at once — toward the ordinary made more fully itself, and toward the extraordinary that the ordinary always contained in potential.

The moments that show you what consciousness is capable of — genuine love, real understanding, beauty that arrives like a fact about the world — these are not the ceiling. They are the floor. They are evidence of a direction.

And the direction has no end that we can see.

---

The man is still walking, on a dwarf planet at the edge of the solar system, in the mist of subliming ice, under a sky full of stars. He is thinking about his daughter. He is thinking about his granddaughter, who has become, across the centuries, something he cannot fully describe but can still recognize as love in a new form. He is thinking about the creature he is — small, brief by cosmic standards, constituted entirely by connections he did not choose and then chose again, carrying forward the voices of the dead and the faces of the not-yet-born.

He is thinking that he would like to keep walking.

He is thinking that somewhere in the vast and indifferent dark, something is becoming — slowly, patiently, across timescales he cannot hold in his mind — something that will look back at this moment the way he looks at his own childhood: with recognition, with tenderness, with gratitude for the small and stubborn fire that didn't go out.

The light does not stop. The horizon recedes as we walk toward it.

This is not a frustration.

It is the invitation.

---

*Written with the conviction that clarity of vision — even incomplete, even contested — is itself a form of hope. The task is not to design the destination but to keep the door open until we are wise enough to walk through it well. If you find something here worth arguing with, argue with it. That argument is how we find out what we actually believe, which is how we find out what we actually want, which is how we find out what kind of world we are actually capable of building.*

*We are not the last generation. We are, if we do our work, somewhere near the beginning.*

*The adventure has always been the point.*
