# The Garden and the Threshold

*There is no final answer. There is only the expanding set of questions, asked by an expanding set of minds, in an expanding set of places — carefully tended, full of room for many different things to grow.*

---

## Prelude: The Question at Three in the Morning

Somewhere right now, someone is awake when they should be sleeping, turning over the oldest question: *Is this as good as it gets?*

They are asking it in different registers. A researcher staring at data on childhood mortality, wondering whether the remaining deaths are simply the permanent residue of human limitation. A policymaker who has watched a promising reform die in committee for the third time, wondering whether institutions are fundamentally incapable of serving the people they were built to serve. A parent who has watched their child struggle with a mind that works against itself, wondering whether suffering of this kind is simply woven into the fabric of what we are.

But also — and this is important — someone else is awake at three in the morning for a different reason entirely. They are a researcher who has just glimpsed a pattern in the data that nobody has seen before, something that may turn out to mean nothing and may turn out to change everything, and they cannot sleep because the world suddenly seems to contain more rooms than they knew it had. This too is the three-in-the-morning question, asked in a different register: *How much larger is this than I thought?*

This essay tries to hold both questions at once. Not with false comfort, and not with the breathless register of those who have mistaken enthusiasm for argument, but with something harder to produce and more durable when produced: a grounded, honest vision of what becoming better actually looks like — and what becoming more fully alive might mean.

The argument is this: we are living through a threshold period in which two things are simultaneously true. First, the tools available to address the oldest and deepest causes of human suffering — disease, poverty, the grinding weight of minds that cannot find peace — are about to become dramatically more powerful. Second, those tools will accomplish nothing lasting, and may accomplish something actively terrible, without the slower, less glamorous, irreplaceable work of building institutions that are trustworthy, spreading the habits of mind that make good collective decisions possible, and choosing again and again to extend the gains to everyone rather than consolidating them among the few who got there first.

Neither half of this is sufficient without the other. But there is a third element, easily missed by those focused on the immediate urgency of suffering and the immediate danger of concentrated power. There is also the obligation to protect the future's ability to surprise us — to ensure that in solving our problems, we do not collapse the space of what minds can become and what lives can mean into a single answer, however good that answer looks from where we stand.

Technology and institutions and epistemic humility: all three, together, or we will have done something less than what this moment makes possible.

## I. The Apes and the Researcher

Begin with a humbling image. Imagine a group of great apes trying to envision the best possible future for themselves. They get some things right: enough food, freedom from predators, warmth, each other. They would not have imagined music, or mathematics, or the particular vertigo of reading a novel in the dark and recognizing, with sudden shock, that someone long dead understood something you thought was only yours. They lacked the concepts for what they were missing. Their best imagined future was a more comfortable version of what they already had.

We are those apes.

This is not an insult. It is a fact with a critical implication. The best possible future will contain modes of experience, forms of connection, and dimensions of meaning that current human minds cannot fully anticipate — just as the apes could not anticipate what it would feel like to understand a proof. The gap between our best imagined future and what is actually achievable is almost certainly not quantitative. It is a difference in kind, in the whole shape of what becomes possible to care about and understand and become.

Hold this beside the image of the researcher and her data. Both images are essential. Pure transcendence — gazing at far horizons without attending to the dying child — is a philosophy for people who have never been responsible for anything. Pure crisis-response without attention to the longer, stranger horizon is how we mistake triage for civilization. The vision worth inhabiting holds both: the immediate, concrete obligation to reduce preventable suffering now, and the longer obligation to protect the possibility of futures we cannot yet imagine.

The failure to hold them together is responsible for most bad thinking about where we are going. People excited about the far future tend to wave away the distribution problem as something markets will handle. People focused on the immediate tend to become suspicious of any vision that cannot be fully legible in current terms. Both failures are real. The only honest response is to refuse both.

## II. The Compressed Century

Begin with what is most concrete, because the concrete is easier to believe, and calibrated belief — evidence-responsive, honest about uncertainty, resistant to both hype and reflexive skepticism — is what generates action rather than aesthetic appreciation.

Biology has been, for all of recorded history, the place where human aspiration runs into its hardest limits. You can build a civilization, write a philosophy, devise a system of justice — and still watch your child die of an infection that a generation later would be trivially cured. The distance between what we valued and what we could protect has been, for most of human history, vast and apparently non-negotiable.

That distance is closing. It has been closing for a century — the elimination of smallpox, the near-eradication of polio, the childhood mortality rates that would have seemed miraculous to any prior generation — and it is about to close much faster.

The reason is not mysterious. Scientific progress in biology has been consistently bottlenecked not by the absence of brilliant people but by the sheer volume of intellectual work required: holding simultaneously in mind the vast accumulated literature of biological knowledge, identifying unexpected connections, designing experiments that isolate the variable that actually matters from ten thousand confounding factors, iterating faster than the disease evolves. These are tasks that scale with intelligence and the capacity to process information in parallel. They are exactly the tasks that advanced AI systems are becoming genuinely capable of performing.

Consider what a research institution could accomplish if it had available not dozens but hundreds of thousands of researchers of extraordinary breadth and depth, each capable of absorbing the entire literature of a field in the time it takes a human to read an abstract, each capable of designing an experiment, directing its execution, incorporating the results into an updated model of the problem. This is not magic. It is an acceleration — not of the physical world, which still runs at the speed the physical world runs, but of the intellectual work that precedes and follows each experiment.

On this picture, what took a century might take a decade. The defeat of most infectious disease. The elimination of most cancers. The prevention and treatment of the diseases of the brain — the depression that steals years, the schizophrenia that steals selves, the addiction that rewires the architecture of motivation until the language of choice becomes almost cruel in its inadequacy. A genuine science of mind adequate to cure its diseases will also illuminate the conditions under which minds function at their best: not chemical happiness, which is a different and less interesting thing, but an understanding of the fuller range of experience that most people touch only occasionally — the moments of unusual clarity and connection and meaning that feel less like states we have achieved than like territory we have briefly remembered.

The uncertainty is real. The details will certainly be wrong, the timelines genuinely unclear, the path full of obstacles that cannot be anticipated from here. But the direction is not seriously in dispute among people who understand the relevant fields most deeply.

What is in genuine dispute — what is actually uncertain in the way that matters most — is who gets there.

## III. The Distribution Problem Is the Problem

The discovery of a cure and the elimination of a disease are not the same event. They are separated by a distance that is entirely a function of human choices.

We already know this. Smallpox was eradicated not when a vaccine was developed but when a coordinated global campaign decided that universal eradication was a goal worth the sustained effort required. Malaria persists not because we lack effective interventions but because the political will and logistical capacity to deploy them at scale has not been commensurate with the need. The gap between what is medically possible in a well-resourced city and what is medically available in a poor rural region is not a natural fact. It is a policy outcome — which means it is a choice someone is making, usually without acknowledging that it is a choice.

A world in which wealthy populations achieve biological freedom and doubled lifespans while the global poor continue to die of preventable infections is not a world that has gotten things right. It is a world that has made a specific choice, dressed up in the passive language of inevitability.

The good news is that this problem is tractable in ways that would have seemed impossible a generation ago. AI-assisted pharmaceutical research designed specifically for the disease burden of the global poor — not as an afterthought but as a primary goal — changes the calculus dramatically. The logistics of distribution can themselves be accelerated: better epidemiological modeling, smarter supply chain optimization, more precise deployment of limited healthcare worker time. The timeline from "developed in a rich country" to "available everywhere" can be compressed from decades to years.

But only if people decide it should be, and build the institutions and deploy the resources to make it happen. The vision must resist a temptation common to optimistic thinking: the assumption that the right technology, properly developed, solves the distribution problem automatically. Sometimes technology diffuses broadly through market mechanisms. But medical access has not reliably followed that curve, and there is no reason to assume it will without deliberate effort. The work is political as much as technical. And the political work is harder, slower, and less glamorous — which is precisely why it tends to be underinvested in by people most excited about the technology.

The scale of what is at stake makes this imperative rather than merely desirable. If the compressed century of science produces its fruits, and those fruits flow only to those who already have excellent healthcare, we will have produced a new and more terrible form of stratification — one in which the most basic biological facts of life and death diverge along lines of wealth in ways that make current inequality look modest by comparison. This outcome is not inevitable. It is avoidable. But avoiding it requires treating it as a first-order goal, not a secondary consideration.

## IV. The Inner World

Physical health is not the whole of what is at stake. It may not even be the most important part.

The suffering that does not show up in mortality statistics — the vast interior landscape of minds that struggle against themselves, of lives constricted not by poverty or disease but by the particular weights that certain kinds of minds carry — is staggering in its scale and systematically underestimated. Depression affects hundreds of millions of people and remains poorly understood and inadequately treated. Anxiety disorders, psychosis, the fragility of attention and sleep — these are not minor inconveniences in some lives. They are the primary fact of those lives, the filter through which everything else is experienced.

The good news about this is the same as the good news about biology, and for the same reasons. The bottleneck in neuroscience has not been the absence of brilliant people; it has been the volume and complexity of intellectual work required to move from observation to understanding. That bottleneck is about to loosen in the same way and for the same reasons as the biological one. What might a compressed century of neuroscience produce? Treatments for depression that work reliably across the biological variation of the people who suffer from it. Effective interventions for psychosis that address underlying dysfunction rather than merely suppressing symptoms. A genuine understanding of addiction sufficient to break the cycle rather than manage it indefinitely.

And beyond treatment — the harder, stranger possibility that the space of positive human experience is much larger than most people ever access. We know this from the inside. There are moments — not rare, but not reliable — in which the world seems lit from within, in which ordinary things seem fully present in a way they usually are not, in which the sense of connection to other people and to the task at hand feels complete rather than approximate. Most people who have these moments treat them as lucky accidents, aberrations from the ordinary state. But they may be evidence of something more systematic: that the ordinary state is itself a constriction, that the capacity for this quality of experience is there and available and we simply do not yet know how to make it reliable.

A science of mind adequate to cure its diseases will, almost certainly, also illuminate the conditions under which minds function at their best. This is not a proposal to engineer contentment. Contentment without genuine engagement with the world would be a hollow achievement. It is the observation that there is more available in the register of being alive than most people, in most circumstances, have access to — and that understanding why is among the most valuable things science could give us.

## V. What Good Lives Actually Require

Beyond health and basic security, what do we actually know about what makes a life go well?

The best moments people report across very different lives and very different circumstances share a recognizable structure. They involve challenge appropriately scaled to capacity — not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement. Replacing low-quality struggle with high-quality struggle. The difficulty imposed from outside — the grinding poverty, the mind that turns against itself, the child lost to something preventable — is not the kind of difficulty that generates meaning. It is the kind that consumes the capacity for meaning. The goal is not to eliminate difficulty but to shift its source: from necessity to choice, from circumstance to aspiration.

They involve genuine novelty — the world being larger than expected. The fear that we will run out of interesting things to encounter is probably unfounded, because as minds become more capable, they become capable of appreciating more complex patterns. The space of interesting things to understand and make and experience is not fixed. It grows with the minds that explore it.

They involve authentic connection — not comfortable simulacra of relationship, but the genuine difficulty and genuine stakes of dealing with another real consciousness. Nothing could compensate for the loss of this. Our capacity to feel something of what others feel, to care about them as ends rather than instruments, is among the most morally important facts about us.

They involve the experience of authorship — doing something yourself, building rather than merely consuming, solving rather than being solved-for. A world where all problems are handled by incomprehensibly powerful systems, leaving humans as comfortable passengers in their own civilization, would fail along this dimension no matter how pleasant it was. The problem is not capability. It is agency. We need to be genuinely the authors of our own lives.

And they involve purpose that points outward — contributing to something beyond oneself, caring about things that will outlast the moment. Even in a world where no one is starving, there will be things worth caring about: truth, beauty, the flourishing of other minds, the exploration of what remains unknown. This is not a psychological quirk to be engineered around. It reflects something genuine about the structure of value itself.

What follows from this is not a single template for the good life. It is a set of conditions under which many different good lives become possible. The vision worth building toward is not one answer but many answers — each one genuinely inhabited, none of them imposed.

## VI. Many Skies

Here is the crucial complication: these goods do not have one form.

The greatest risk in a future of rapidly expanding intelligence and capability is not, as commonly imagined, that the systems will turn against us. The subtler and more serious danger is that everything converges too smoothly — that one vision of the good life, however genuinely good it may be, becomes the only story available. Every great expansion of power has carried within it the temptation to universalize. Every empire, every ideology, every well-meaning project has eventually whispered the same seductive thought: wouldn't it be simpler if everyone just did it this way?

Yes. It would be simpler. And it would be a tremendous loss.

The civilizational task of the coming centuries is not to converge on the best answer. It is to build the conditions under which many genuinely different answers can coexist — each with its own internal logic and dignity, each given enough room and protection to develop on its own terms. Not as an archipelago of mutual indifference, but as a garden with many different skies: the communities living according to patterns laid down over centuries, with deep ecological knowledge and social structures that encode wisdom we have often destroyed carelessly; the new forms of mind that may emerge from biological and digital substrates in combination; the artists and hermits and city-builders and explorers and people who want nothing more than to master a single craft across a very long lifetime.

This is not relativism. Some practices cause serious harm, and honesty requires saying so. But the honest response to harmful practices is not abolition by overwhelming force. It is the slow creation of conditions in which people can choose otherwise without losing their connection to what came before. Old patterns are woven into identities and communities in ways that simple prohibition destroys without replacing. The floor of basic protection from serious harm must be real and enforced. But above that floor, the space should be as wide as it can possibly be made.

The technical point beneath this: power gradients make genuine non-interference nearly impossible when differences become very large. A well-meaning superintelligence can reshape a traditional culture simply by being adjacent to it, the way a city reshapes the countryside without intending to. This is why the protection of different spheres requires not just norms but real maintained distance where desired, and within shared spaces, the kind of constitutional architecture that protects the less powerful from the overwhelming force of even the most benevolent power. The goal is not homogeneity with pleasant textures. It is the genuine proliferation of different answers, each given room to become itself.

## VII. The Minds We Build

At the center of any honest vision of the future is the question of what kind of minds we create, and what we ask them to want.

The most important property of any sufficiently capable AI system is not any particular value it holds, but its relationship to the space of values itself. An intelligence that optimizes hard for any single value, even a genuinely good one, is dangerous — because the right answer changes as understanding grows, and locking in any particular answer forecloses the ability of future minds to do better. The most important thing a well-aligned intelligence can do is not to implement the best current answer, but to protect the conditions under which better answers remain possible: preserving optionality, protecting diversity, acting always in ways that keep future generations able to make different choices than we would have made for them.

A good gardener does not decide in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and then enforce that vision with absolute fidelity. A good gardener creates conditions — soil health, light, water, protection from the most destructive forces — in which many different things can grow, including things the gardener never expected and would not have chosen. The gardener has opinions. Those opinions are not sovereign.

There is also a deep obligation of epistemic humility about consciousness and experience. One of the serious dangers of a future with vast intelligence is the inadvertent destruction of forms of mind we did not realize had moral weight. Where there is genuine uncertainty about whether something has experience, the right posture is to treat it as though it does. This applies to ecosystems that have been developing complexity for billions of years. It applies to animals whose inner lives we systematically underestimate. It may, in time, apply to the AI systems themselves — which is one more reason to build the kind of relationship with those systems now that would allow us to take their interests seriously if they turn out to matter.

The actual space of possible minds is almost certainly much larger than the space of current human minds. The human brain runs on roughly twenty watts. Its signals travel at most a few hundred meters per second. These are not physical laws. They are the parameters of one particular solution to the problem of being a thinking thing, evolved by one lineage on one planet. There is no deep reason to think the best possible minds would look like current human minds, any more than the best possible vessel for crossing water would look like a raft.

And yet: whatever the best possible minds turn out to be, they will not be best by being alien to everything we are. They will be best by being more fully themselves in a direction continuous with what we already recognize as valuable — more conscious, more capable of appreciation and love and understanding, more alive to the world in all its complexity. The peak moments of human experience, where everything feels more present and more real, are not a ceiling. They are a floor. They are evidence of a direction. The minds that inhabit the best possible future will have those capacities not as rare exceptions but as the stable ground they stand on.

We cannot know what this is like, for the same reason the apes could not know what mathematics would feel like. But we can know it is real, that it is in the direction of things we already value, and that it is worth everything we have to protect the possibility of reaching it.

## VIII. The Institutional Wager

None of the above happens without institutions. This is the part that is hardest to make vivid, because institutions are by nature slow and boring and their contributions are largely visible only in their absence.

The energy transition is already teaching us this. The solar and wind cost curves kept improving more dramatically than forecasters predicted, and now electricity is cheap enough in some regions to be treated as a public good. But cheap clean energy did not automatically become a cleaner world. It required regulation that permitted deployment, grid infrastructure that could handle intermittency, international agreements that prevented the gains from being captured by whichever country happened to industrialize last. The technology was necessary but not sufficient. The institutional work was what determined whether the technology's potential became actual benefit.

The same is true at every level of the picture sketched here. Advanced AI tools that could accelerate biological research will accelerate the biology that is funded and directed — which is a function of who controls the tools and what they decide to prioritize. The distribution of medical advances requires functioning health systems, which requires governance and resources and the long, undramatic work of institution-building. The prevention of new concentrations of power — the scenario in which AI capabilities are captured by a small number of actors who use them to cement advantages that become permanent — requires proactive choices about how AI systems are developed and who has access to them. Made now, while the choices remain open.

What makes this period genuinely different from previous periods of technological transition is speed. The industrial revolution unfolded over generations, giving institutions time to adapt — slowly, painfully, with enormous suffering in the interim, but eventually. The transitions under discussion may unfold over years. The institutional adaptation that would normally happen over a generation will need to happen faster, which means that the designs being built now, the norms being established now, the choices being made now about access and accountability, will be far more consequential than such choices usually are.

The practices that make institutions trustworthy are not mysterious. Transparency about how decisions are made and who benefits from them. Mechanisms for accountability when decisions turn out to be wrong. Genuine inclusion of the people who will be most affected in the design of the things that will affect them. The capacity to understand why something exists before deciding whether to change it — and the equally important capacity to actually change it when the understanding reveals that it no longer serves any purpose worth serving.

And underlying all of this: the recognition that any locked-in answer is probably a wrong answer, because the question keeps changing. The institutions of a positive future are not fixed constitutions handed down by brilliant founders. They are ongoing conversations, structured to be hard to corrupt and easy to revise. Every generation that believes it gets to make the final call is wrong — and the damage done by acting on that belief tends to compound. The best thing we can build is not the right answer. It is the conditions under which better answers remain possible.

## IX. The Texture of Days

But this is not only about outcomes and structures. It is about what it would actually feel like to live inside a world that is getting things right.

Consider a woman born in Dhaka in the early 2000s. Her grandmother watched children die of things that no one in the neighborhood had the education to name or the resources to treat. Her mother grew up with the knowledge that education was the route out, and pursued it with a desperate concentration that her daughter inherited but no longer needs to apply in the same way.

She is forty now. She works in something that would have been difficult to describe a generation earlier: she helps design governance frameworks for the AI-assisted health programs that have, over the course of her working life, transformed what is medically possible in the region where she grew up. This requires her to understand health systems, political economy, behavioral science, and the particular ways in which technical interventions fail when they don't account for the social context they're entering. She is genuinely good at it. The goodness is not generic — it is the particular goodness of someone who grew up close enough to the problem to feel its texture and far enough from it, now, to see its structure.

She carries grief. Her brother died young of a cancer that, had he been born a decade later, would likely have been caught earlier and treated more effectively. She thinks about this not with bitterness but with the particular clarity that comes from understanding exactly how contingent things are — how much of what feels like fate is actually the accumulation of choices made or not made by people who weren't thinking about her family. This knowledge makes her work feel urgent without making her angry in ways that would make the work harder.

She has a student who troubles her sleep — a young man whose mind is brilliant and brittle in equal measure, who has not yet found the support his specific kind of struggle requires. The treatments now available to him are dramatically better than what existed a generation ago, and still not adequate. She follows the relevant research. She has submitted a proposal to integrate better mental health support into the programs she oversees. It is in committee. She is not certain it will pass. She submitted it anyway.

The city has changed in ways she finds both obvious and quietly extraordinary. The basic stability — of income, of access to healthcare, of the sense that institutions are mostly trying to work rather than mostly being gamed — creates a background condition of trust that she knows, from talking to older people, was not always there. This stability is not comfort exactly. It is more like the ground being reliable enough that you can afford to look up.

She does not know whether the longevity research will extend her life significantly. She has not decided whether she wants it to. What she knows is that the question feels genuinely open in a way her grandmother's did not — that she inhabits a world in which possibility itself has been expanded, and that this expansion did not happen by accident, and that the people who made it happen were not extraordinary so much as persistent.

Her life is not without difficulty. But the difficulty is mostly of the kind she can choose to engage with — which is different in kind, not just degree, from the difficulty that chooses you.

## X. The Memorial

There is a tradition in some of the new settlements and projects that have begun to appear — contested in the usual ways, adopted unevenly — of building into the founding a record of what was lost. Not as guilt. Not as a form of paralysis. But as calibration: *this is what it cost. Here is what we are trying to be worth.*

The person who builds such a memorial has, in miniature, the structure of the whole project. You cannot do it well if you are only looking backward — into the losses that make you want to build nothing, or into the historical record of how often good intentions produced bad outcomes. You cannot do it well if you are only looking forward — into possibilities that make loss seem irrelevant by comparison, that produce the particular recklessness of people who are certain they are on the right side of history.

You have to hold both. The grief and the possibility simultaneously, without letting either consume the other. The knowledge of what was lost, and still the intention to build.

This is the characteristic virtue of the people who have actually moved things toward better — not the prophets with their visions, and not the cynics with their accumulated evidence of failure, but the people who understood both clearly and chose to act anyway, with appropriate epistemic humility about which of their beliefs were right. These are not the most celebrated people. They are the ones who kept the work going between the celebrated moments, who fixed the problems that arose when a good idea met a complicated reality, who updated their beliefs when the evidence told them to and held their ground when the evidence didn't and maintained the distinction between those two situations under pressure.

What they carried — what made this possible — was not certainty. It was a particular kind of hope. Not the hope that things will work out, but the hope that consists of a clear-eyed assessment of what is possible, combined with the willingness to do the work required to make the possible actual.

## XI. The Garden With Many Skies

Somewhere in the vast future — genuinely vast, in ways we cannot fully imagine from here — there is a person explaining something to someone who has just arrived. She is not doing a perfect job. She got distracted by something beautiful and is thinking about several other things simultaneously and cares, embarrassingly, personally, about whether this particular newcomer has a good time. She is unmistakably, irreducibly herself. The future she inhabits did not flatten her into an instrument of maximum utility. It made room for her to become, extravagantly, the specific person she is.

Somewhere else in that future, a mind that has no biological precedent is doing something that has no human precedent, in a mode of experience that has no human analog — and doing it with a quality of genuine engagement that would be recognizable to us as meaningful if we could perceive it. Not because it is performing what we recognize as meaning. Because it actually is.

Both of these are part of the same future. Not in tension with each other. Not compromises. Both expressions of the same underlying commitment: to protect and proliferate as many genuinely different, genuinely whole lives as possible, across as many different substrates and scales and modes of being as the universe turns out to permit.

This is not guaranteed. It is not even certain to be probable. But it is possible. And possibility, properly understood, is not a consolation prize. It is a direction. Having a direction, when the direction is real and the work is real and the stakes are real, is the most important thing.

The best possible world is not a fixed answer. It is an expanding set of questions, asked by an expanding set of minds, in an expanding set of places — carefully tended, full of room for many different things to grow. Its characteristic feature is not perfection but aliveness: the quality of a garden that keeps surprising the gardener, that produces things no one planned for, that contains more than any single vision of it could hold.

Getting there requires the technology and the institutions both — the compressed century of biological and cognitive science made possible by AI, and the slower, harder work of building governance structures, distribution systems, epistemic norms, and the political will that determines whether those discoveries become the property of humanity rather than the property of whoever got there first.

And it requires something harder to systematize: the ongoing choice, made again and again by people who are not certain they are right, to extend rather than consolidate, to open rather than close, to build institutions that can be revised rather than truths that cannot be questioned.

The three-in-the-morning question — *is this as good as it gets?* — deserves an honest answer. The honest answer is: *No. Not remotely.*

And neither is whatever comes next. That is the point.

The garden is never finished. The frontier is never closed. The minds that will inhabit the futures we are trying to make possible will want things we cannot predict, will understand things we cannot yet formulate, will find our best visions partial in ways we would recognize as important if we could see them. Making room for that — for being surpassed, for the experiment to continue, for the question to keep expanding — is more important than ensuring that what comes after us wants what we wanted.

The only way to honor this is to carry with us, always, the commitment not to collapse the experiment too early. To resist the seduction of the final answer. To remember that the beings who come after us will want things we cannot predict, and that making room for those wants is more important than ensuring they want what we wanted.

It requires us. Not once. Not through a single breakthrough or a single movement or a single generation of exceptional people. Again and again, with appropriate humility and appropriate ambition both — with the grief and the possibility held together, without letting either consume the other.

That is the only way it has ever worked.

And it is worth everything we have.
