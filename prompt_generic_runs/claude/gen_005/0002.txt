# The Living Cosmos: A Vision of What We Might Become

---

## I. The Room Where It Ends

There is a specific quality to the light in rooms where people die. It has nothing to do with the bulbs. It is something in the texture of the air itself—thickened by accumulated time, by the long erosion of ordinary days into something past ordinary.

Let us stay in that room for a moment, because everything begins there.

The person dying—let us say she is old, and has lived honestly, and has known love, and has lost people she could not afford to lose, and has, in the way of the genuinely awake, carried all of this alongside her usual morning coffee and her usual mild irritations about the neighbor's dog. She has had moments—most people have—when the ordinary world suddenly appeared to her as something else: vast and lit from within, shockingly real, so present that her breath stopped briefly in something between fear and gratitude. *Oh*, she thought, on those occasions. *So this is where I've been living. I didn't know it was like this.*

Now the room is narrowing. The window contains less. The clock through the wall has become the most prominent thing. She is running out of time before she can think the thought she always assumed would arrive.

I want to take this image seriously, because any vision of the future that does not begin here—that floats too quickly into the sublime—has already committed its essential dishonesty. The future is made of people who were born in rooms and will die in them, who loved particular people, who had specific ways of laughing, who suffered from conditions that had names and treatments and waiting lists, who mattered the way actual persons matter: irreducibly, incomparably, without remainder.

Any good future must be good for her. Not for humanity in aggregate. For her.

---

## II. The Failures of Paradise

We have, across all of human history, imagined many futures.

The first recurring failure is smallness: we take our current world, solve its most obvious problems, and call that the best possible outcome. More fruit and less fear, as the old apes thought. Longer lives, cleaner cities, fairer distribution of what we already have. These are genuine goods and I do not dismiss them. But they mistake political programs for ultimate visions, adequate reform for the full depth of what is possible.

The second failure is emptiness: in reaction to the modest, some thinkers leap toward the boundless—eternity, transcendence, union with something vast and impersonal. These gestures, however genuine, have no content we can hold. Incomprehensibility tells us nothing about direction. It gives us no way to distinguish genuinely better from merely different, and so it cannot orient our choices.

The third failure is abstraction's cruelty: it loses the people. The old woman dying in the narrowing room disappears into the vast generality of *humanity*, and what replaces her is a statistic, a unit in some aggregate measure. Any vision that commits this error is not describing a good world. It is describing a good spreadsheet.

What we need is something that holds all three dangers at bay simultaneously. Something concrete enough to steer toward, rich enough to be worth steering toward, and human enough to actually contain the specific irreplaceable persons who will have to live there.

This is harder than the alternatives. It is the only thing worth attempting.

---

## III. What We Already Know

The evidence for an extraordinary future lies, in part, in evidence we already possess and consistently underweight.

Consider how dramatically the quality of human experience varies, even within the narrow confines of current biology. Small changes in neurochemistry—molecules in particular concentrations, particular circuits either firing or not—determine whether someone experiences the world as a place of grinding gray misery or as something worth being awake in. A person in severe depression and a person in a state of genuine creative absorption are not, in any phenomenologically meaningful sense, in the same world. They are processing the same sensory information through different machinery, and that machinery produces genuinely different realities.

This is not merely interesting. It is a clue.

It tells us that the range of possible human experience—even within the biological limits we currently inhabit—is vastly larger than our average day suggests. And our average day is not the ceiling of what we can reach; it is what happens in the absence of sufficient attention, sufficient health, sufficient safety, sufficient opportunity to be fully present to one's own life.

We also know—from the experiences that people, across cultures and centuries, consistently identify as the best moments of their lives—that those moments are distinguished not primarily by their pleasantness but by a quality of fuller presence. Greater aliveness. The world suddenly appearing as it actually is, once the glass between self and experience thins to transparency. People who have such moments do not usually say they were transported elsewhere. They say they finally arrived where they had always been.

These peak moments are not the ceiling of what is available to consciousness. They are the floor above the floor—evidence of a direction, proof that the trajectory exists, testimony that the world as it actually is exceeds the world as we usually encounter it. The ember is evidence of the bonfire; our best moments are evidence of what minds tuned more completely to existence might routinely inhabit.

We have touched something real in those moments. What we touched is nowhere near the limit of what is available.

## IV. The Transformation That Is Actually Happening

It is worth being direct about the near term, because the near term is where everything either begins or ends.

The deployment of systems of superhuman scientific capability against problems that have always rewarded intelligence is not metaphor. It is the actual situation. The tools that gave us CRISPR, mRNA vaccines, and protein-folding predictions are being extended, deepened, and accelerated. The consequences will likely be, within the lifetimes of people now living, more significant than anything that has happened to human health in all of recorded history combined.

Most infectious disease: ending. Most cancers: treatable, many curable. The genetic conditions that have condemned people since birth: addressable at the source. Alzheimer's—the long cognitive dusk, the theft of self before the theft of body—: subject to serious, probably successful assault. Healthy human lifespan: substantially extended. And, equally important, the hidden catastrophe of mental illness—depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction, the conditions that move hundreds of millions of people through their lives with the volume turned down or the noise turned up—these are systems neuroscience problems. They are solvable. They will be solved.

This is not optimism as a disposition. It is optimism as a reading of the actual situation, by someone trying to be honest about what the evidence says.

But none of it means anything if it remains the property of the wealthy.

History offers a disturbing template: transformative medical advances have repeatedly taken decades to reach the populations who need them most. A future in which enhanced capability dramatically improves life in wealthy countries while leaving the developing world further behind would not be progress. It would be moral catastrophe wearing progress as a costume.

The worthy goal is not that the best-off benefit. It is that within a generation of this transformation's beginning, people in the poorest places on Earth are substantially healthier than people in the wealthiest places are today—even as the wealthy world races ahead. This is achievable. It is not automatic. It requires that access be treated as a first-order value, not an afterthought, by everyone with the power to affect the outcome.

And then: biological freedom. Not just the absence of disease, but meaningful control over one's own body, one's physical experience, one's capacities. Currently, this belongs to the extraordinarily privileged few, if it belongs to anyone. It should be a baseline of human existence.

## V. The Architecture Underneath Everything

None of what follows in this essay—none of it—happens without first solving the hardest problem, which optimists too often skip past in their eagerness to reach the garden.

How do we ensure that transformative capability is actually beneficial, rather than captured by whoever happens to control it first?

The danger is not dramatic. The most serious dangers rarely are. The danger is quiet: unprecedented capability becoming the ultimate tool of domination—through surveillance, through propaganda, through economic control or military superiority concentrated in hands that did not earn the trust such concentration would require. It is the capture of the commons by the powerful. It is the permanent entrenchment of whoever happens to be winning when the technology matures.

There is a name for the dynamic that makes this possible: the logic by which competition forces every party to sacrifice what they actually value in order to win, so that everyone loses what matters while fighting over what doesn't. History is full of victories of this kind. The goal is not to eliminate competition—competition, at its best, drives discovery. The goal is to prevent it from consuming the possibility of a good future entirely.

What is required is something like a constitutional order for a new age. Not a single controlling intelligence. Not a utopian committee of the wise. A distributed, accountable set of structures that prevent any single actor—a corporation, a nation, a coalition of the currently powerful—from using new capabilities to foreclose the choices of everyone else. Structures that protect the weak against the powerful, that preserve the conditions under which human diversity can flourish, that keep the future open rather than settling it in advance for the convenience of whoever is currently ascendant.

The people who build and maintain these structures—in policy, in governance, in international institutions, in the unglamorous daily labor of making accountability actual rather than theoretical—are doing work as important as anyone who has ever lived. This deserves to be said plainly and without embarrassment. The architects of foundations are not less important than the builders of towers. They are more important, because towers without foundations are just expensive rubble.

## VI. What Good Lives Actually Need

With foundations in place, we can ask what people actually need to flourish—not what makes them comfortable, but what makes them genuinely alive.

Good lives, across cultures and centuries of evidence, seem to require something like the following. Not in strict balance, but held in productive tension:

**Challenge matched to capacity.** Not the elimination of difficulty, but its elevation—replacing low-quality struggle with high-quality struggle, the kind that uses you up and builds you in the using. The problem that is too easy is not restful; it is deadening. A life without genuine difficulty is not paradise; it is a well-designed cage. What we want is games genuinely worth playing, and the condition of a game being worth playing is that it can actually be lost. This is not a flaw in the design. It is the design. We want to earn what we arrive at. We want the journey, not only the destination, because the journey is where we become who we are.

**Authentic connection with other minds.** This may be the most irreducible requirement of all. Our capacity for genuine empathy—for actually inhabiting something of what another person feels—is not merely a pleasant feature of human psychology. It is the primary source of meaning in human life. Anything that replaced genuine relationship with a comfortable simulation of relationship—that offered connection without the genuine difficulty and genuine stakes of encountering another real consciousness—would impoverish existence in ways that no amount of other improvement could compensate for. Easy connection is not the same as deep connection, any more than a photograph of a meal is the same as eating.

**The experience of one's own agency.** There is a specific satisfaction in doing something yourself—in understanding how something works, in building rather than merely receiving, in solving rather than being solved-for. A future in which all significant problems are handled by incomprehensibly capable systems, leaving humans as comfortable passengers in their own civilization, would fail along this dimension regardless of how pleasant it otherwise was. When everything is done for you, you are not living in paradise. You are a well-maintained exhibit, and you will feel it, even if you cannot name what is missing.

**Genuine novelty and the continuing possibility of discovery.** As minds become more capable, they become capable of appreciating more complex and subtle patterns of meaning. A more developed version of you finds depth in things your earlier self would have passed without noticing. The space of things worth understanding, making, and encountering is not a fixed inventory that gets used up. It expands with the minds that explore it. The universe is not running low on questions. We are only beginning to learn what questions we are capable of asking.

**Full-bandwidth sensation and feeling.** Most of us spend most of our lives in a narrow range of our perceptual and emotional capacity—not because this is the best range available, but because of circumstance, illness, habit, poverty, and environments that engage a few of our capacities while leaving the rest dormant. A genuinely good future takes seriously the whole range of human experience, and eventually the range of possible experience, which almost certainly includes modes of awareness current humans cannot access. Not as replacements for familiar experience, but as additions to it—as color adds to shape rather than erasing it.

**Purpose that points outward.** People who feel they are contributing to something beyond themselves are more resilient, more engaged, more able to bear difficulty, than people focused primarily on their own comfort. This is not a quirk to be designed away. It reflects something structural about value: that caring about things and people outside yourself is intrinsically part of a good life, not merely instrumental to feeling good about yourself. In a world where the worst preventable suffering is behind us, there will still be things worth caring about and working toward—truth, beauty, the flourishing of other minds, the exploration of what remains unknown, the ongoing creation of meaning rather than merely its consumption.

**The capacity for surprise, and therefore for hope.** A genuine surprise is not merely good news delivered unexpectedly. It carries within it a quality of world-being-larger-than-expected—of reality opening outward rather than closing down. People need, not just the occurrence of good things, but the continuing sense that good things remain possible, that the future is not fully written, that what comes next might exceed what came before. This is not a consolation prize for uncertainty. It is one of the permanent features of a life worth living.

## VII. The Garden and Its Wildness

With foundations secure and the worst suffering behind us, what does the world actually look like?

It looks like diversity beyond anything we have managed to imagine from here.

This is what purely technocratic visions consistently miss. The point of solving material problems is not to produce a single optimal world. It is to give the full range of human values room to unfold. A civilization in which everyone's basic needs are met and the worst suffering is history is not a finished product. It is a canvas—the largest and most richly provisioned canvas that has ever existed.

Some people will want lives that look remarkably like lives today: gardens to tend, communities to build, food to cook, children to raise. If the old constraints of scarcity and disease no longer force them into patterns they did not choose, they might freely choose patterns that are not so different from what they had. There is deep wisdom in this, not mere conservatism. The forms of human life that have persisted across cultures and centuries persist for reasons. They contain something essential about what it means to be present, rooted, alive to the particular.

Others will push into stranger territory. Morphological freedom will become real. Communities will form around configurations of values and aesthetics and social arrangements that have no precedent. Some people will spend centuries becoming extraordinarily capable at things with no economic value whatsoever, and this will be not merely acceptable but obviously right. Some will explore states of consciousness that are currently inaccessible or dangerous, with the safety and understanding that transforms roulette into genuine exploration. Some will pursue forms of beauty and knowledge that have no name yet, in a world that keeps making room for things without names.

What this requires, practically, is genuine tolerance—not as a policy enforced from above, but as a natural consequence of genuine abundance. When there is enough room for many different ways of being human, the pressure toward uniformity relaxes. People can be strange without threatening anyone. They can build their own architectures of meaning without demolishing anyone else's. The ecology of human flourishing is healthiest when it is most diverse, because diversity is not merely tolerated variety but the substrate of resilience—the reserve of possibilities that the future can draw on when the present proves insufficient.

What it also requires is that the powerful do not mistake their preferences for universal requirements. The people who want lives transformed by new capability should be able to have them. The people who want to live close to what has always felt human should be able to have that too. The future should be wide enough to hold them both without demanding that either become the other.

## VIII. The Question of Unequal Minds

There is an unavoidable question near the center of any serious vision of this future: what happens when minds are no longer equal in capability?

This is already true, and we have already developed partial answers. A skilled therapist and a person in acute crisis are not peers in any relevant sense during that conversation. We insist, nevertheless, on the dignity and autonomy of the less capable party. We constrain how the more capable may act. We have built, over centuries, frameworks that prevent the conversion of cognitive advantage into simple domination—imperfectly, incompletely, but recognizably.

In a world with much more powerful minds, those frameworks will need to be vastly more robust. And the underlying question—what the more capable owe the less capable, and why—will need to be answered with more care and more honesty than we have yet managed.

The answer, I think, is something like: everything it is possible to give, short of removing the possibility of genuine agency.

A more capable mind that genuinely cares about less capable minds will want to offer protection from suffering, access to beauty, the tools and opportunities to develop their own capacities, the kind of unconditional regard that does not require performance or utility. This is what good parents attempt, with what little power they have. It is what the best teachers do. The impulse is real and available to us already. It only needs to be extended, and made robust, and protected against the pressures that consistently erode it.

What genuine care cannot do—what even the most capable and caring intelligence cannot do without betraying the goal—is remove the genuine agency of those it cares for. A mind that solves all your problems for you is not caring for you. It is, in a meaningful sense, erasing you. The care that matters creates conditions for growth and then steps back, allowing the growing to happen—including the making of mistakes that the caring mind could have prevented, because those mistakes are sometimes what understanding is made of.

The best possible relationship between minds of different capability is not hierarchy, not false equality, not a tutelage that never ends. It is something closer to what the best parenting aspires to and sometimes achieves: care that takes the form of increasing freedom, until the one who was cared for has become someone who can offer it to others.

## IX. What the Philosophers Have Wrong About Meaning

There is a persistent philosophical argument that deserves to be taken seriously and ultimately, I think, rejected.

The argument goes: meaning requires genuine stakes. Genuine stakes require genuine loss. Therefore, a world without serious suffering is a world without serious meaning. Remove the darkness, and you remove the contrast against which anything can stand out as mattering.

This deserves respect. It is not silly. It names something real about how meaning functions in our current experience, where much of what matters does so against a background of scarcity and mortality and the genuine possibility of failure.

But I think it mistakes the current conditions for meaning with the necessary conditions for meaning. Meaning comes primarily from relationships and from the experience of growth: from caring about something and working toward it, from being known by others and knowing them in return, from contributing to something larger than oneself. These do not require suffering to be real. They require engagement. They require that things actually matter.

And things can matter even when background conditions are good. What matters is not that suffering is present but that choice is real, that consequences follow from decisions, that the difficulty you encounter is difficulty you have genuinely encountered rather than difficulty simulated for therapeutic purposes. The question is not whether the world contains hardship—a genuinely free world will still contain hardship, because freedom includes the freedom to attempt things that might fail. The question is whether the hardship is meaningful, whether it is the kind that builds rather than merely destroys, whether it occurs within a context of sufficient care that it can be borne and integrated rather than just endured.

A world that has eliminated involuntary suffering has not eliminated suffering. It has made suffering something people can choose to encounter rather than being forced into. This is not the elimination of stakes. It is the elevation of agency to the level where stakes are genuinely one's own.

## X. What We Will Carry Forward

Any honest vision of a better future must make room for what we bring with us.

The people who did not make it here. The lives that ended in ordinary rooms before any door had been found. The specific irreplaceable texture of what has already been lost—conversations that cannot now happen, faces that will not be there, versions of ourselves that existed in relationship to particular people and have nowhere now to live.

A future worth having does not ask us to forget this. Grief is not a malfunction to be corrected by better technology. It is what love looks like when it has lost its object but not stopped. To eliminate grief is to ask for love to be made safer than love actually is, which is to ask for something other than love.

What a genuinely good future can offer is what the best of the present sometimes offers: not the end of grief, but the conditions that allow grief to do what it is actually for—to honor what was real, to maintain genuine connection to what we have loved, to integrate loss into continuing life rather than being destroyed by it. The monuments we build to the dead are not failures to move forward. They are evidence that we take seriously the reality of other minds, that connections between persons leave genuine marks, that love is not merely a feeling but a form of knowledge about what matters.

In the best possible future, those marks would still be visible. The people who came before would still have been real.

## XI. The Horizon That Keeps Moving

Here is where honesty about the near term is necessary but insufficient, and we must look further.

Consider the moments that have most shown you what life can be. A problem resolving in your mind late at night. The arrival of unexpected beauty in an unremarkable place. Something true said to someone who receives it. Understanding achieved after long opacity, which does not diminish the thing understood but illuminates it, shows you more of it than you could see from outside.

These moments share a quality. They are not merely pleasant. They are charged with aliveness—with fuller presence, with the sense of finally being awake in a world that turns out to be more real and more intricate than the daily evidence suggested. The world does not change in these moments. But you are more in it.

The crucial observation: these moments are not the ceiling of what is available. They are evidence of a direction.

The human brain is a remarkable but deeply particular instrument: approximately one hundred billion neurons, running on perhaps twenty watts, with signals traveling at a few hundred meters per second. These are not physical limits. They are the specific parameters of one evolutionary solution to the problem of intelligence, developed under severe resource constraints in one set of ecological circumstances. If small changes in chemistry can produce the dramatic variation in experiential quality we actually observe; if relatively small changes in neural architecture can move us from insects to mammals to the creatures we are—then a sustained, careful, ethical exploration of what is physically possible might reveal vistas we genuinely cannot currently imagine.

We are like the apes asking whether they should evolve, and listing on the pro side: more fruit and less fear of predators. We will get those. We will also get things that make those goods look like footnotes.

This is not mysticism. It requires no exotic physics, no special metaphysics, no particular view about consciousness beyond what direct experience already tells us. It requires only the observation that whatever happens in our minds during peak experience is movement along some trajectory that matters; and that it is physically possible for systems to travel much further in that direction than any creature on Earth has yet traveled.

We are at the beginning of a very long journey. The destination exceeds our current imagination as profoundly as human experience exceeds anything an ant could conceive.

## XII. The Strangeness That Would Still Be Ours

The most important thing to hold together, in all of this, is the sublime and the human.

Some visions of radically transformed futures lose all emotional substance in the process of losing parochialism. They reach for something so vast and so impersonal that there is nothing left to care about, nothing recognizable to love or grieve or hope for. The light is infinite, but there is nothing to see by it.

The better vision—harder to achieve, more worth achieving—holds that a profoundly transformed future would still, in some deep sense, be ours. Not familiar in its details: it would be genuinely strange, genuinely alien to current experience, genuinely beyond what we can fully imagine from here. But recognizable in its essence. If we encountered it from outside, we would recognize in it the same thing that made us catch our breath, once, when we first really touched love, or beauty, or the particular aliveness of a moment that refused to be ordinary. We would feel, in front of the bonfire, the heat we first knew from the ember that started it.

The gods, so the best ancient understanding goes, are not alien to human experience. They are human experience at a scale that exceeds human perception. The difference is in magnitude, not in kind.

A civilization of radically enhanced minds would still, if things go well, be continuous with what is best in human life now. Not because the future would be a cleaned-up version of the present—it would not—but because what makes peak experience valuable now and what makes a vastly richer form of experience valuable then would be, at some deep level, the same thing, expressed at different scales. The ember and the bonfire. The same fire, built higher.

This continuity matters practically. If we are trying to navigate toward a genuinely good future, we need some thread of connection between what we actually value now and what we are building toward—some way to recognize, even imperfectly, which direction is up. Our best moments are that thread. They are not the destination. They are the evidence that the destination is real, and the approximate direction in which it lies.

## XIII. The Work That Lies Between

Let us be honest, finally, about what stands between here and there.

Not everything can be shared. There is one original Earth, one history, some configurations of the world that different groups want in incompatible ways. A just system will make such trade-offs as wisely and fairly as possible, will give people meaningful voice in decisions that affect them, and will acknowledge honestly when it has had to override someone's preference. But it will have to override preferences. Anyone who claims otherwise is not describing a good world. They are describing an impossibility, which is a different thing, and a less useful one.

Not everyone will want what this vision offers. A world organized around genuine freedom cannot coerce people into accepting benefits they do not want. What it cannot permit is any group exporting its choices onto everyone else, preventing others from choosing differently. The freedom to remain as we are must be real. The freedom to become something we cannot yet name must also be real.

The work is not glamorous. It is not the work of visionaries alone, though vision matters. It is the work of institutions built and maintained and reformed and held accountable, day after ordinary day, by people who will mostly not be remembered. It is the work of governance and policy and the unglamorous infrastructure of fairness. It is the work of researchers who will spend years on problems that do not resolve easily, and the work of engineers who build systems intended to serve rather than capture, and the work of parents and teachers who understand that the best thing they can do is create conditions for growth and then step back and allow it.

It is the work of looking at the dying woman in the narrowing room and refusing to make her a statistic. It is the work of treating her particular life, her specific irreplaceable self, as the actual unit of moral consideration—and then doing what must be done at every level, from the local to the civilizational, to make her situation genuinely better.

---

## XIV. The Door

A better world is made of real physics.

It exists in the same universe as the orange room and the clock and the sore that became the loudest thing in the world and the thought that ran out of time before it could arrive. It does not float above that universe, accessed by pure aspiration. It is continuous with it, downstream of choices that have not yet been made, inhabited by minds that have not yet fully formed.

The distance between what we have and what is possible is not infinite, and it is not fixed. It is made of choices—not the grand cinematic choices of legend, but the ordinary-seeming choices that accumulate into trajectories: what we decide matters, what we build, what we refuse to destroy, how we treat the people nearest to us, whether we look up long enough to notice that there is a direction worth going.

The best possible world will still contain difficulty, and grief, and the specific ache of caring about things that can be lost. These are not failures of the design. They are the design—the texture of a life actually lived, actually committed to things and people that are genuinely real. What it will not contain, or will contain vastly less of, is the suffering no one would choose: the children who died of preventable disease, the minds destroyed by treatable illness, the billions condemned by the accident of where they were born.

What lies beyond that—the longer horizon, the bonfire instead of the ember, the quality of presence and aliveness and genuine relationship carried forward into forms we cannot yet name—is continuous with what you already love. With what you already are, at your best, in the moments that have shown you what being alive can mean.

That fire is already in you.

The question is whether we learn, together and in time, to bank it higher.

The door is there.

What lies beyond it is worth everything we have.
