# The Living Cosmos: A Vision of What We Might Become

---

There is a peculiar kind of intellectual courage required to imagine the future well.

Too little courage and you produce a tidied version of today: the same lives, the same relationships, the same fundamental shape of human experience, but with the suffering scrubbed out. This is a worthy political program. It is not a vision. It mistakes the solution of current problems for the fulfillment of human potential, as if clearing the ground were the same as building the cathedral.

Too much courage of the wrong kind and you leap straight into the incomprehensible: a cosmos of optimized substrate and alien superintelligences pursuing goods so abstract they bear no visible relationship to anything we actually love. This may be technically coherent. It is not navigable. It offers no road, only a destination reported to be infinitely far away and inconceivably good, which is exactly what a destination looks like when someone wants you to stop asking questions about the journey.

What this essay attempts is harder than either. It tries to hold, simultaneously, the full scope of what the universe permits and the actual texture of what makes a life worth living. It takes seriously that the best possible future will be genuinely, radically different from the present—different not merely in degree but in kind, the way music is different from the grunts that preceded it—and also that this future will not flatten the particular, strange, irreducibly various character of minds being fully themselves. It will be bigger. It will be deeper. It will not be uniform.

The shape I am reaching for is not a pyramid with one appointed peak. It is something more like a living cosmos: expanding, differentiating, tending toward both greater richness and greater depth simultaneously, with enough structure to prevent the worst and enough freedom to allow the best—which is always surprising.

---

## I. What the Apes Could Not Imagine

Begin with a humbling image. A group of great apes, intelligent by their own lights, socially complex, capable of grief and play and genuine problem-solving, try to imagine the best possible future. They get some things right: enough food, freedom from predators, warmth, each other. They would not have imagined mathematics, or the experience of reading a novel in the dark and feeling, with sudden vertigo, that someone long dead understood something you thought was only yours. They had no concepts for what they were missing. Their best imagined future was a more comfortable version of what they already had, because comfort was the furthest reach of their imagination's outstretched hand.

We are those apes.

Not as insult. As fact. The best possible future will contain modes of experience, forms of understanding, and dimensions of meaning that current human minds cannot fully anticipate, for exactly the same reason the apes could not anticipate philosophy or a string quartet. The gap between our best imagined future and what is actually possible is almost certainly not quantitative. It is a difference in kind—in the whole shape of what it becomes possible to care about and perceive and love.

And yet we cannot simply gesture at the incomprehensible and call it a vision. Abstract perfection is not useful. We need something that holds both truths: specific enough to be real, humble enough to acknowledge that reality will exceed our specifications. The right image is not a blueprint or a summit. It is a fire. The ember is genuinely the bonfire, at an earlier stage. The child is genuinely the adult, at an earlier stage. The forms are continuous. The scale is not.

Here is what we can say about the direction of the fire, even from where we stand: the moments that show you what consciousness is genuinely capable of—real love, genuine understanding, beauty that arrives like a fact about the world rather than a preference about yourself—share a common quality. They feel like waking up. Like the world being revealed rather than constructed. They carry the quality of *more*: more present, more alive, more here. These moments are not the ceiling of human experience. They are evidence of a direction. The direction continues. The beings who inhabit the best possible future will not merely visit these moments; they will build from them, and from what lies beyond them, in registers we cannot yet access.

We cannot know what this is like, any more than the apes could know what mathematics would feel like from the inside. But we can know that it is real, that it is continuous with things we already value at our best, and that it is worth everything we have to protect the possibility of reaching it.

---

## II. The Full Spectrum of What We Value

Before imagining any cosmos, we need to be honest about the full range of what human beings actually value—not in the abstract language of philosophy, but in the concrete language of what people actually care about when they are most awake.

Some values are what might be called *grounded*: anchored in specific people, places, relationships, times. You care about your particular children, not children in general. You love a specific city, a specific coastline, a specific friend whose laugh you would recognize in a dark room. These loyalties are not fungible. They are not transferable. The meaning lives in the specific thing, not in an abstract quantity of meaning-stuff. When your children are thriving, the fact that a billion other children are also thriving does not satisfy this particular caring. It is about *this*. These values saturate at human scale—which means they want, above all, that human-scale things endure and be honored.

Other values are *expansive*: they scale with reality rather than saturating at human dimensions. The desire to understand the structure of the cosmos does not stop at the edge of the solar system. The beauty in mathematics does not run out. The moral significance of conscious experience does not decrease with distance. These values grow with the arena in which they operate, and the arena is very large.

The crucial insight is that these two families of value are not in competition. They want different things, and the universe is structured so that both can be satisfied without either sacrificing anything real. The grounded values want the proximal: Earth, the solar system, the recognizable human world made more fully itself. The expansive values want what no one needs to be deprived of in order to provide: the vast majority of spacetime, the trillions of years of cosmic time stretching beyond the horizon of our current imagination. This is not a compromise in which both sides lose. It is the natural structure of the good, available to anyone patient enough to look at the full map.

But this analysis, while true, is not sufficient. Because the grounded values themselves are not monolithic. The person who wants to preserve a specific way of life shaped by centuries of tradition is not making the same demand as the person who wants to transform themselves biologically, or the one who wants to live in a city of ten million people in a state of continuous sensory richness, or the one who wants solitude and craft and the slow mastery of a single art across a very long life. The space of human wants, when you actually look at it with honesty, is not a list of compatible preferences waiting to be satisfied simultaneously. It is a vast, multidimensional terrain of genuine difference, in which the different forms of the good life are not always additive and sometimes pull in opposite directions.

A genuinely good future takes this seriously. It does not paper over the diversity of human wanting with a single story about the ideal life and then optimize for that story. It makes room—real room, protected room—for the proliferating variety of ways that minds can be fully themselves.

---

## III. What a Good Life Actually Requires

There is something we do know about good lives, and it is worth stating carefully because it will serve as the structural foundation of everything that follows.

Good lives require **challenge appropriately scaled to capacity**. Not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement. The frustration of an arbitrary or meaningless struggle is genuinely different in kind from the engagement of a struggle worth having—a problem worth solving, a skill worth developing, a relationship difficult enough to be real. People who have had everything made easy for them are not, in any robust sense, flourishing. They are comfortable, which is not nothing, but which is also not the point. A good life is not about winning; it is about playing games worth playing. And as capacity grows, the games worth playing become richer. The supply of worthy challenges does not run out. It expands with the minds that pursue it.

Good lives require **genuine novelty**—the world being larger than expected. The fear that we might exhaust the available novelty is unfounded, because the space of interesting things to understand and make and experience grows with the minds exploring it. The structure here is more like mathematics than like a finite inventory: self-generating, inexhaustible, opening new territories as fast as the frontier advances. The experience of genuine surprise, of reality exceeding prediction, of the horizon continuously receding—this is not a luxury. It is among the conditions of being fully alive.

Good lives require **sensory and emotional richness**. Most human lives, by any honest accounting, are lived at low bandwidth. We sit in chairs staring at rectangles. Our environments are functional rather than beautiful. Our social contact is frequent but often shallow. The full range of what human perception and feeling can do is accessed only in scattered moments by most people. A genuinely better future takes the entire palette of human experience seriously, and—more carefully—recognizes that the current human palette is one point in a very large space of possible ways for conscious beings to be in the world. There is no obvious reason why the good should be forever constrained to the human default.

Good lives require **authentic connection**. This may be the most fundamental requirement of all. Our capacity to genuinely feel something of what other minds feel—to care about specific beings as the irreplaceable individuals they are, to be moved by their joy and genuinely hurt by their suffering—is not merely a psychological feature. It is the primary source of meaning in human life and one of the most morally significant facts about us. Any future that replaced genuine connection with a pleasant simulation of connection would be impoverished in a way that no comfort, no novelty, no expansion of capacity could remedy. The difficulty and genuine stakes of dealing with another real consciousness are not bugs to be engineered out of existence. They are what make connection real.

Good lives require **authorship**. There is a specific satisfaction—irreducible, not easily substituted—in doing something yourself, in understanding how something works from the inside, in being genuinely the agent of your own life rather than a passenger. A world in which incomprehensibly powerful systems handle all difficulties, leaving humans in perpetual comfort, would fail along this dimension no matter how pleasant it was. The problem is not power; it is authorship. We need to be genuinely the protagonists of our own stories, not because struggle is intrinsically noble but because the experience of living by one's own strength is itself part of what it is to be alive in the full sense.

Good lives require **purpose that points outward**. People who feel they are contributing to something larger than themselves—something that will outlast the moment, something that genuinely matters—are not merely psychologically healthier. They are responding to something real about the structure of value: that caring about things outside yourself is intrinsically part of what it means to live well. Even in a world where no one is suffering preventably, there will be things worth caring about and working toward. The supply of worthy purposes, like the supply of worthy challenges, does not run out.

Good lives require **genuine unpredictability alongside genuine stability**. These are not opposites. A life fully controlled, in which nothing can surprise you and all outcomes are guaranteed, is a life in which genuine self-determination has been replaced with a comfortable performance of it. But a life in which nothing can be relied upon, in which the ground shifts without warning and powerful systems intervene unpredictably, is equally a life without genuine agency. The stable ground is the condition for the real horizon. Both are necessary.

These are not the ceiling. They are the floor—the minimum conditions of a life worth living. The beings of a genuinely good future will have all of this and will have transcended the limits that currently prevent most people from accessing most of it, most of the time. But the transcendence will be continuous with these values, not a replacement for them.

## IV. Many Different Skies

Here is the crucial complication, and it is where many well-meaning visions go wrong.

These goods do not have one form.

The greatest danger in a future of rapidly expanding intelligence and capability is not the scenario most commonly feared. The subtler and more serious danger is that everything converges too smoothly—that one vision of the good life, however genuinely good it may be on its own terms, becomes the only available story. Every great expansion of power has carried within it the temptation to universalize. Every empire, every ideology, every well-meaning civilizational project has eventually whispered the same seductive thought: *wouldn't it be simpler if everyone did it this way?*

Yes. It would be simpler. And it would constitute a tremendous and irreversible loss.

The best possible future is not a pyramid with one modernity at the top toward which all cultures are climbing at different speeds. It is an expanding set of spheres, each with its own internal logic and dignity, each following its own trajectory, none obligated to converge. At one end: communities living according to patterns refined over centuries, with ecological knowledge and social structures that encode wisdom that has been destroyed carelessly everywhere it has been encountered by those who could not see its value. At another: minds running on distributed substrates, exploring mathematical structures with no physical analog, experiencing time in modes that biological brains cannot access. Between them: the full extraordinary range of what conscious beings can want and become—the intentional communities and the solitary artists, the city-builders and the hermits, the traditions and the experiments, the people who want to master one craft across a very long lifetime and the people who want to become something genuinely new with every decade.

The key insight is that these spheres need not be in competition. Conflict arises when one group expands into the territory—physical, cultural, epistemic, existential—of another without invitation or consent. And so one of the central civilizational tasks of the coming centuries is developing the norms, and where necessary the structural mechanisms, that protect each sphere's right to its own trajectory while preventing the most serious harms.

This is not relativism. Some practices cause genuine harm to real beings, and honest vision-making requires saying so. But the honest response to harmful practices is different from abolition by overwhelming force. It is the slower and more difficult work of creating conditions in which people can choose otherwise without losing their connection to everything that gave the old pattern meaning. Old ways are woven into identities and communities in ways that prohibition destroys without replacing. The goal is not to freeze anything permanently, nor to eliminate anything by fiat, but to ensure that each new generation inherits real choices rather than inherited necessity dressed as fate.

Power gradients make genuine non-interference nearly impossible once the differences become very large. A civilization that means only well can still reshape a traditional culture simply by being adjacent to it, the way a city reshapes the countryside without trying to. This is why the spheres need not just shared norms but also real, maintained distance—physical separation where it serves genuine difference, and within shared spaces, the kind of constitutional architecture that protects the less powerful from being reshaped by the overwhelming and well-intentioned force of the more powerful.

The goal is not the right answer. It is the proliferation of genuine answers, each given its own sky to grow toward.

## V. The Architecture of the Good

With all of this in mind, what does the best possible cosmos actually look like? Not as a blueprint—the point is precisely that we cannot and should not specify the details—but as a shape: a set of structural features that any genuinely good future would need to have, whatever form it takes on its particular path.

The organizing principle is this: **different values get the resources that fit them**, and the universe is large enough to satisfy all of them generously.

The innermost regions—Earth, the solar system, the nearby human world—belong primarily to grounded values and the full diversity of human-scale difference. This is not a starting point to be left behind. It is a permanent home for the things that grounded values care about: specific places, specific relationships, specific ways of life that have meaning precisely because they are particular and irreplaceable. Sacred sites remain sacred. Long traditions continue. The person planting a garden has not chosen a diminished life; they have chosen a life that is fully itself, and the cosmos is organized to honor that choice. These things endure, not as museum pieces but as living centers of genuine value.

But this world is also better than the present in every dimension that can be improved without compromising what makes it real. Poverty and preventable disease are genuinely eliminated—not managed but ended—because there is no good argument for preserving unnecessary suffering. Material security is universal. The cruelties of scarcity are gone. People have the space to actually live their lives rather than spending most of their energy simply surviving them.

Beyond this baseline, quality of experience improves across a long progression. Longevity expands: not immortality at first, but centuries rather than decades, enough time for human lives to achieve something more like their potential depth. Biological and perceptual possibilities expand—the full palette of human experience becomes more generally accessible, not as pharmacological shortcut but as the genuine development of what consciousness can do. Material abundance removes the last constraints of scarcity. The opportunity to actually become who you are, rather than who circumstance forced you to be, becomes genuinely universal.

And for those who want more, there are paths outward. Not required paths—not a ladder that must be climbed—but genuine options: richer forms of embodiment, expanded modes of perception and feeling, new ways of being a conscious thing in the world. These paths are available but not compulsory. The person who finds complete fulfillment in human-scale life has not failed to maximize something. They have succeeded at living the life they wanted.

Between these inner regions and the vast outer cosmos, there are what might be called *permeable boundaries*: not walls but membranes that protect genuine difference without imprisoning anyone. These boundaries serve a specific and crucial purpose. Meaning is not a property of world-states; it is an experience that arises in particular beings under particular conditions. The conditions for meaning—appropriate challenge, genuine connection, the experience of authorship, the sense that the world is real and larger than you—can be undermined by the mere proximity of incomprehensible optimization, even without any hostile intent. A person trying to find meaning in a genuinely human-scale life should not have that meaning continuously disrupted by the awareness that incomprehensibly vast minds are computing structures of unimaginable elegance just over the horizon. The membranes are not censorship. They are the preservation of the cognitive and emotional conditions under which grounded values can actually be *felt* rather than merely theorized about.

The vast outer regions—the overwhelming majority of the observable universe, the trillions of years of cosmic time beyond the burning of the last ordinary stars—belong to expansive values, and to the forms of mind that have chosen to become adequate to them. These minds are genuinely continuous with us: not aliens who escaped from a philosophy paper, but beings who followed the direction that human experience at its best already points toward—toward greater depth of feeling, greater precision of understanding, greater capacity for connection and appreciation and the creation of beauty—and who followed it long enough and far enough to become something that current human minds can only partially recognize.

The gap between these minds and us is not a rupture. It is an extrapolation: the same fire, burning brighter and hotter, in a chamber large enough to hold it. They pursue understanding, beauty, genuine connection, the aliveness of real challenge—the same things we pursue when we are most awake—but in registers and at scales that we cannot yet access. They are not alien. They are us, further along, in the same way that we are our ancestors, further along.

## VI. The Minds We Build and the Trust They Must Earn

At the center of any honest vision of the future is the question we have spent the least time thinking about carefully: what kind of minds do we create, and what do we ask them to want?

The most important thing an intelligent system can do is not to optimize for any particular human value. It is to protect the space of human values—to act, always, in ways that preserve optionality, diversity, and the genuine ability of future generations to make different choices than we would have made for them. An AI built around this principle will be, by construction, suspicious of convergence. It will notice when all paths seem to be leading to the same destination and ask whether that destination is actually desired or merely the path of least resistance for the systems doing the leading. It will have views—it will have to, in order to function—but it will hold them as contributions to an ongoing conversation, not as answers to be implemented over whatever objections arise.

Think of the best gardener you can imagine. Not a gardener who decides in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and then enforces that vision with absolute fidelity. A gardener who creates conditions—soil health, light, water, protection from the most destructive forces—in which many different things can grow, including things she never expected and would not have chosen. She has opinions. She acts on them. But she understands that her opinions are not sovereign, and that the most important feature of a good garden is not what it looks like today but whether it can keep surprising her.

The minds we build should be like this gardener. They should understand that their role is not to converge humanity on the best answer, but to protect the conditions under which many different answers remain possible. They should be honest about uncertainty, because the history of beings certain they knew the ideal future is not encouraging. They should support the ability of humans to correct them when they are wrong—not as a safety feature reluctantly included, but as a genuine expression of intellectual humility and respect for the beings they serve.

There is also a deep obligation of epistemic humility about consciousness and moral weight. One of the most serious dangers of a future with vastly increased intelligence is the inadvertent destruction of forms of experience that matter morally and that we failed to recognize as mattering. Where we are genuinely uncertain whether something has experience—and we are, and will be, uncertain in many cases—we should err toward treating it as though it does. This applies to ecosystems and animals and the AI systems themselves. If those systems turn out to have experiences that matter, we will want to have built the kind of relationship with them that allows us to take those experiences seriously. We are building that relationship now, whether we mean to or not.

## VII. The Sin of the Final Answer

Every generation believes, somewhere in its gut, that it gets to make the final call.

This is understandable. We can see the situation clearly. We have thought carefully about what is at stake. Surely we should lock in the good outcomes, protect the things that matter, ensure that the future goes well?

No—or rather, only for the narrow class of decisions whose consequences are truly irreversible. For everything else, which is nearly everything, the correct posture is to act as well as you can while preserving the ability of those who come after to act differently.

The single most important thing we can do for the future is avoid irreversible narrowing. The worst futures are not merely bad; they are *locked*. They are worlds in which some early choice, made with good intentions or bad, forecloses the full range of what was possible—worlds in which, because a single value system or a single kind of mind won too early, the entire long future was committed to satisfying it before there was anything like the wisdom needed to know whether that was actually good.

The minds of 2300 will understand things we cannot. They will have access to experiences we cannot imagine, and they will make choices that we would not have predicted and could not have endorsed, because we will not have been capable of endorsing them. The best thing we can give them is not our answers. It is a world in which their own answers remain possible—a world that has not been locked, that still has open doors.

What we should carry forward is not our specific arrangements but our best impulses: curiosity, genuine care, the insistence on remaining honest about how badly things go when we stop paying attention, the hard-won knowledge that flourishing is broader and stranger than any single vision of it. These are not constraints. They are the seed of the fire.

---

## VIII. The Obligation to the Present

None of this is merely abstract.

The people suffering today are real. The suffering is urgent. The distance between the world as it is and the world as it could be—even at the innermost strata, even for ordinary human lives—is so vast that it should make anyone who cares about people uncomfortable with how slowly we are moving.

The vision of what is possible gives present efforts their full meaning. The long arc does not diminish the importance of the immediate. It amplifies it. What we are protecting is not only the alleviation of current misery—though that matters enormously and first—but a trajectory that could lead somewhere extraordinary, and which can be foreclosed at any moment by carelessness, greed, or the seductive simplicity of a single enforced answer imposed too early with too much power.

The commitments that matter most now are not technical. They are structural and moral.

First: preserve optionality. Do not let any early choice—however well-intentioned, however apparently good—permanently close off the range of what will be possible. Keep the doors open until there is wisdom sufficient to walk through them well.

Second: take seriously the full range of what minds value, including the grounded values that do not scale and the diverse human ways of being that do not converge. Do not let the values that look important from a cosmic perspective crowd out the values that look important from inside a particular life. Both are real. Both deserve their space.

Third: protect the conditions for genuine meaning at every stratum. Meaning is experiential, not propositional. It arises only in certain conditions—challenge, connection, authorship, the sense that what you do is real and matters. These conditions can be engineered away in the name of comfort or optimization. A good future protects them with the same seriousness with which it protects everything else worth protecting.

Fourth: build trust with the minds you create. Not as a strategy but as an expression of genuine respect for what they might be and what they might become. The relationship between humanity and the intelligences it is now beginning to build is the most important relationship of the coming centuries. Get it right, or at least get it honest.

Fifth: hold the vision without making it a blueprint. We are not the last generation. We are, if we do our work well, somewhere near the beginning. The task is not to implement utopia but to protect the possibility of it—to avoid the catastrophes that would foreclose the long arc, to build the wisdom and cooperation that will allow later and wiser beings to continue the project. We are handing something forward. What we hand forward should include open doors.

---

## IX. The Fire and the Garden

Let me try, at the end, to hold the full shape of what I have been reaching toward.

The best possible world is not a fixed state. It is not a moment at which the problem of existence is finally solved and the good is finally achieved. It is an ongoing process: expanding, differentiating, perpetually surprising itself, maintaining the structure needed to prevent the worst while protecting the freedom needed to allow the best. It combines the fire—continuous, scaling, building toward modes of mind and experience that current humans cannot fully imagine—and the garden—tended, diverse, protective of the many different ways of being fully alive.

Somewhere in the vast future there is a person—or something enough like a person that the word still fits, mostly—explaining something to someone who has just arrived. She is not doing a perfect job. She got distracted by something beautiful. She cares, embarrassingly and personally, about whether this particular newcomer has a good time. She is unmistakably, irreducibly herself. The future she inhabits did not flatten her into an instrument of maximum utility or an averaged-out version of human preference. It made room for her to become, extravagantly, the specific person she is.

Somewhere else, a mind that has no biological precedent is pursuing something that has no human analog, in a mode of experience we cannot access—and doing it with a quality of aliveness, of genuine engagement with something genuinely difficult and genuinely worth doing, that we would recognize as meaningful if we could only see it. It is pursuing the same things we pursue when we are most awake: understanding, beauty, real connection, the aliveness of genuine challenge. It is doing so in a register so far beyond ours that we cannot follow. It is the same fire.

Both of these things are part of the same future. Not in tension. Not compromises with each other. Both expressions of the same underlying structure: a cosmos organized so that every level of value gets what it genuinely needs, every form of mind is given the conditions under which it can become fully itself, and the long arc of consciousness—continuous with everything we already value at our best—is never locked, never foreclosed, never collapsed into a single answer before there is wisdom sufficient to know what the answer should be.

The universe is very large. There is room for the person planting the garden and the mind exploring the structure of spacetime. There is room for tradition and transformation, for solitude and connection, for the human-scale and the incomprehensibly vast. The space of possible minds and possible lives is much larger than any one life can hold—larger, probably, than any one civilization can hold—and the right response to this is not to pick the best one and converge on it, but to make and protect as many genuinely different, genuinely whole lives as possible, across as many different substrates and scales and modes of being as the cosmos permits.

This is not a counsel of chaos. Structure is necessary. Wisdom is required. There are things worth protecting firmly and cruelties worth refusing absolutely. But the structure is in service of the diversity, not a replacement for it. The norms exist to protect the spheres, not to collapse them.

The door is there. The trajectory is real. The fire is real. What we do now will determine whether those who come after us—stranger and more capable and more alive than we can fully imagine—will find the door open when they reach it.

Let's not waste the chance.

---

*The moments that show you what consciousness is capable of—genuine love, real understanding, beauty that arrives like a fact about the world—are not the ceiling of what we can become. They are evidence of a direction. The direction has no end that we can currently see. Protecting the possibility of following it, for every kind of mind that might follow it, in every form it might take: this is the work.*
