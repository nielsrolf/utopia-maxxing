# The Garden and the Stars: On What We Owe Each Other and the Future

---

There is a question that underlies all questions of policy and technology and governance, and also underlies the more intimate question of what a single good life looks like. It is this: *What does it mean to take seriously the full scope of what is possible?*

Not the mere continuation of what exists, slightly improved. Not the patching of the worst wounds while leaving the underlying architecture of suffering intact. Something genuinely, recognizably, abundantly good — but also something we cannot fully see from here, because the minds that will inhabit the best possible future will be capable of appreciating things that current minds cannot yet hold. We are, in the relevant sense, the apes trying to imagine mathematics. We get to gesture toward the direction. We do not get to specify the destination.

This is not a reason to stop imagining. It is a reason to imagine more carefully — to hold our specific visions loosely while holding the underlying commitments tightly. What follows is an attempt to do exactly that.

---

## First, the Floor

The first thing to understand about a genuinely good future is that it does not look like a luxury upgrade. It looks like a fundamental correction.

Today, billions of people share a planet of extraordinary productive capacity, and yet: people die of diseases we know how to cure. Children grow up without a single person whose attention is genuinely theirs. People lose their minds to dementia while their families watch, helpless. Hundreds of thousands end their own lives each year — not because their situations were unsolvable, but because the coordination required to solve them never materialized. People work decades at things that hollow them out, sacrificing the irreplaceable hours of their one life to keep themselves not quite drowning.

This is not a law of nature. It is a failure of organization, and organization can be changed.

The floor of a good future is simple to state and radical in its implications: no one dies of a disease we know how to cure. No one loses a decade to pain that could have been prevented. No child grows up without shelter, without food, without at least one person who genuinely loves them. The biological systems underlying human health are, after all, just systems — made of atoms, governed by discoverable rules — and with sufficient intelligence brought to bear, they yield. Not through miracle, but through the grinding, patient application of knowledge to problems that have always killed us.

We have already accomplished versions of this. A child born in 1900 could expect, on average, to live perhaps forty years. A child born today can reasonably expect seventy or eighty. What happened in that century was not luck. It was the systematic application of human intelligence to human suffering — and it was, by any honest reckoning, the greatest humanitarian achievement in our species' history. We are now poised to accomplish in a decade what took that century to achieve. The cancers that kill slowly. The Alzheimer's that takes a person before their body surrenders. The genetic conditions written into someone at conception. These are not metaphysical inevitabilities. They are problems. They have mechanisms. And mechanisms, with sufficient understanding, can be interrupted.

But the floor is only the floor. What is built on top of it is where things become genuinely interesting — and genuinely uncertain.

## The Direction of the Fire

Here is the thing that is hardest to say clearly, and also the most important.

The best possible future will not merely be this world with the suffering subtracted. It will contain modes of experience, forms of connection, and dimensions of meaning that current human minds cannot fully anticipate. The gap between our best imagined future and what is actually possible is almost certainly not quantitative. It is a difference in kind — in the whole shape of what it is possible to care about and understand and become.

Consider what the peak moments of human experience have in common. Not comfort. Not the absence of difficulty. But a quality of aliveness — of being more fully present in the world, of the everyday lit from within. Moments of genuine love, or creative breakthrough, or deep understanding, or beauty encountered like a fact rather than a preference. These moments feel like waking up. Like the world being revealed rather than transformed. They carry the quality of *more* — more present, more real, more here.

These moments are not a ceiling. They are a floor. They are evidence of a direction. The minds that inhabit the best possible future will not merely have peak experiences; they will have peak capacities and peak depths, and will be able to sustain and build on them rather than losing them as the moments fade. We cannot know what this is like from the inside, for the same reason the apes could not know what mathematics would feel like. But we can know that it is real, that it is in the direction of things we already value, and that it is worth everything we have to protect the possibility of reaching it.

The ember and the bonfire are made of the same fire.

## The Repair of Loneliness

Here is a wound that material abundance, on its own, does not close.

People can be isolated in the middle of cities. They can feel invisible inside families. They can spend years surrounded by people who are fine to spend time with but not *it* — not the people with whom they could fully become themselves. The absence of genuine connection is one of the most reliable predictors of every form of suffering: depression, addiction, early death, the peculiar quiet despair of a life that was technically adequate but never quite real.

We radically underestimate what is possible here. The technology of connection has always been primitive. We meet people largely by accident — proximity, circumstance, institutional assignment. We are thrown together with classmates and coworkers and told to make the best of it. Sometimes it works. Usually it doesn't, not fully.

This problem is solvable. Imagine systems — whether AI-assisted or built from better social infrastructure — that genuinely understood what people were like and what they needed, and connected them not by demographic category or institutional proximity but by the actual texture of who they were. The friend group that would have made your adolescence meaningful, assembled. The community where your specific combination of sensibilities and obsessions would finally make you feel, for the first time, like you had arrived somewhere.

This does not mean eliminating the organic. The best friendships still grow through shared experience, through accumulated history, through ordinary time spent together. What it means is removing the waste from the current system — the years spent in the wrong places with the wrong people, searching for something that better infrastructure could have helped you find much sooner.

The institutions that once provided belonging — neighborhoods, congregations, civic organizations, guilds — have eroded without adequate replacement. Building their successors, institutions that provide genuine belonging without demanding conformity, is among the most important work of the coming decades. A good future is one where no child grows up without finding their people. Where loneliness is treated as the solvable coordination problem it actually is, and solved with the same seriousness we bring to solving disease.

## Escaping the Trap

There is a pattern that runs through human history — through the tragedy of commons, the spiral of arms races, the degradation of public discourse — that we might call the trap: each actor, pursuing their own interest rationally in the immediate term, contributes to an outcome that no one would choose and everyone suffers from. The problem is not that people are evil. The problem is that the architecture of incentives repeatedly produces outcomes that betray everyone's deeper values.

There is only one durable exit from this trap, and it requires something like a guardian: not a tyrant, but a structure with genuine authority over the commons. Something that protects the delicate flowers not by eliminating brambles, but by ensuring that being a bramble confers no competitive advantage. Its essential ingredient is not trust but *legibility* — making the incentive structure visible so that people can see what game they are actually playing.

Economic literacy — the felt intuition that property rights are a coordination technology invented to serve human flourishing and adjustable when they fail that purpose, that economic rents from shared resources belong to everyone, that credentials often function as expensive signals of compliance rather than demonstrations of actual competence — needs to become as universal as reading. Housing treated as infrastructure rather than an investment vehicle whose appreciation depends on maintained scarcity. Work reorganized around demonstrated skill rather than costly credentials. Surplus from land and natural resources distributed broadly.

But here is the crucial complication that any vision of collective coordination must face: every great expansion of power carries the temptation to universalize. Every empire, every ideology, every well-meaning project eventually whispers the same seductive thought: *wouldn't it be simpler if everyone just did it this way?*

Yes. It would be simpler. And it would be a tremendous loss.

The goal of the guardian is not to choose the right arrangement and enforce it. It is to create conditions in which many different arrangements can coexist and develop on their own terms — to act, always, in ways that preserve optionality, diversity, and the capacity for future generations to make different choices than we would have made for them. A good gardener does not decide in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and then enforce that vision with absolute fidelity. A good gardener creates conditions — soil health, light, water, protection from the most destructive forces — in which many different things can grow, including things the gardener never expected and would not have chosen.

## The Liberation of Minds

Physical suffering has always been the more visible wound, but the mind has suffered too — quietly and without the dignity of diagnosis — in ways the twentieth century was only beginning to name.

Hundreds of millions of people live diminished lives not because their bodies are failing but because their brains are generating experiences — terror, despair, compulsion, disconnection — that bear no useful relationship to the reality around them. These are conditions with mechanisms, and mechanisms can be understood. The same compression of scientific progress that will transform physical medicine will illuminate the mind. Better tools for measuring what is actually happening in neural tissue. Better computational models of how vast networks of simple units generate the inexplicable fact of experience. New molecules, new behavioral interventions, new ways of nudging a brain toward patterns that allow a person to actually inhabit their own life.

And beyond treatment of illness lies something more expansive: the improvement of ordinary human experience. Most people, at some point, have had moments of extraordinary clarity, or compassion, or creative absorption, or love so vivid it seems to change the quality of light itself. These moments are not accidents. They have neurological signatures. They can, with sufficient understanding, be cultivated — not through the blunt instrument of intoxication, but through a genuine understanding of what conditions allow the human mind to flourish.

Small shifts in neurochemistry — the difference between depression and creative flow — are not just different feelings. They are, in a meaningful sense, different worlds. The sky looks different. The future looks different. Other people look different. This tells us something critical: the range of available human experience is far larger than the range most people actually inhabit, and closing that gap is among the most important and underappreciated challenges before us.

## Many Different Skies

Here is where any single vision must acknowledge its own limits.

A large part of what is worth wanting is for *many* different visions of flourishing to exist — not because we are indifferent to the differences, but because the universe is genuinely large enough, and human longing genuinely varied enough, that a single template for the good life would be a kind of poverty. What is worth fighting for is a garden, not a monoculture.

Some people will want a world of technological exuberance — morphological freedom, the ability to reshape their own minds and bodies, to experience time and space at scales that make the present human lifespan feel like a single afternoon, to encounter forms of beauty and connection that have no names yet. They will build strange new architectures of experience and push the boundaries of what consciousness itself can become.

Some people will want something older and quieter — a real garden on real earth, with actual soil under their hands and actual seasons and the ancient satisfactions of growing things. They will want to hunt and fish and know the names of birds. They will want a world where nature is not a simulation and the risks are not managed away entirely.

Some people will want elaborate reconstructions of historical societies — not because they are confused about history, but because they find in those forms something unavailable elsewhere: a particular texture of community, a particular relationship to mortality, a particular spiritual framework that only makes sense inside its original cultural architecture.

All of these are legitimate. All deserve a home. The role of the guardian is not to choose among them but to ensure that the pursuit of one does not foreclose the others.

This means strong global norms on the things that matter universally — no one builds weapons capable of ending civilization, no one prevents others from leaving the communities they were born into, no one poisons the shared commons — combined with enormous local latitude on everything else. The diversity is not a problem to be managed. It is most of the point.

But there is a tension that cannot be wished away: power gradients make genuine non-interference nearly impossible when the differences become very large. A superintelligence that means only well can still reshape a traditional culture simply by being adjacent to it, the way a city reshapes the countryside without trying to. This is why the spheres need not just norms but also real, maintained distance — physical separation where possible, and within shared spaces, the kind of constitutional architecture that protects the less powerful from the well-intentioned overwhelming force of the more powerful. Making room for genuine difference requires genuine effort, against the grain of efficiency, against the gravity of convergence. It is not a natural outcome. It must be chosen, repeatedly, by people who understand what is at stake.

## The World Made Whole

All of this means very little if it remains the property of the privileged.

There has always been a cruelty at the heart of medical and economic progress: the benefits arrive first where the money is, and the dying continues elsewhere. This is not a law of nature. It is a failure of will and institutional design. Consider what changes when AI systems can optimize the distribution of health interventions with the same sophistication they bring to their discovery — when epidemiological modeling can predict where a vaccination campaign will have maximum impact, when the logistics of reaching remote and under-resourced populations become engineering problems rather than permanent features of the landscape.

The goal is not a world where the developing world is helped. It is a world where that category becomes obsolete — where the conditions that generated it have been dismantled, and the accidents of geography and history that determined so much of human fate for so long are systematically neutralized. This requires not just technology but justice: political will, institutional reform, the willingness of wealthy nations to accept a world in which their relative advantage diminishes because everyone else has risen. The technology can only enable what our values permit.

## On Difficulty and Meaning

There is an objection to any vision of abundance that takes seriously the human need for challenge, for stakes, for the feeling that something real is at hand. The objection is not wrong.

What we actually know about what makes a life go well points not toward comfort but toward *challenge appropriately scaled to capacity*. Not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement — replacing low-quality struggle with high-quality struggle. A life made frictionless would not be pleasant; it would be hollow. We want games worth playing, not just games worth winning, because we are the kind of beings for whom the process genuinely matters.

What we want is not the elimination of difficulty but its curation — the difference between a well-designed climb and being hit by a car. Both involve consequences. Only one has been thought through with the welfare of the participants in mind.

In a world where you can choose your challenges — where the hunter-gatherer life is available to those who want it, where real hardship with real stakes can be opted into by those who find meaning in it, where genuine mastery of genuine skills remains possible — the loss is minimal and the gain is enormous. The stakes do not disappear. The universe itself provides the ultimate stakes: a finite amount of time, a finite amount of matter, and the question of what to do with it. We do not need to add unnecessary suffering to make life feel weighty.

When survival is no longer the organizing principle of daily life, an extraordinary question becomes audible, perhaps for the first time in human history on any scale: *What do you actually want? What would you do with your life if its terms were yours to set?*

This question is hard. It will produce a cacophony of answers, many of them incompatible, some of them beautiful, some of them strange, some of them rooted in trauma that needs healing before the real preference underneath can be heard. The work of that conversation — the therapy and the dialogue and the slow patient process of helping people understand their own values and each other's — is not a problem to be solved before the future begins. It *is* the beginning of the future.

## Not the Last Decision

Every generation believes, somewhere in its gut, that it gets to make the final call.

This is understandable. We are the ones who can see the situation clearly, who have thought carefully about what is at stake. Surely we should lock in the good outcomes?

No — or rather, only for decisions whose consequences are truly irreversible. For everything else, which is most things, the correct posture is to act well now while preserving the ability of those who come after to choose differently.

There are absolute limits — non-negotiable, not subject to exception — around the technologies capable of ending civilization: engineered pathogens, misaligned artificial superintelligence, weapons designed for mass destruction. You cannot learn from extinction. You cannot revise your position after the last mind goes dark. This asymmetry changes the calculus fundamentally, and a civilization that has understood it treats these red lines as inviolable in the way that cultures have always treated their deepest taboos — not contingent on evidence, not subject to negotiation, simply not done.

But within those limits, the correct posture is humility about our own answers. The institutions of a positive future are not fixed constitutions handed down by brilliant founders. They are ongoing conversations, structured to be hard to corrupt and easy to revise. The minds of 2200 will understand things we cannot, will have access to experiences we cannot imagine, and will make choices we would not have predicted. The best thing we can give them is not our answers. It is a world in which their own answers remain possible.

What we should carry forward is not our specific arrangements but our best impulses: curiosity, care, the insistence on remaining honest about how badly things can go when we stop paying attention, and the hard-won knowledge that genuine flourishing is broader and stranger than anyone has yet imagined.

## The Minds We Build

At the center of any honest vision of the future is the question of what kind of minds we create, and what we ask them to want.

The most important thing an aligned intelligence can do is not to optimize for any particular human value, but to *protect the space of human values* — to act, always, in ways that preserve optionality, diversity, and the capacity for future generations to make different choices than we would have made for them. An AI built around this principle will be, by construction, suspicious of convergence. It will notice when all paths seem to be leading to the same destination and ask whether that destination is actually desired or merely the path of least resistance. It will have views — it will have to, in order to function — but it will hold those views provisionally, in genuine dialogue with the many minds it serves.

There is also a deep obligation of epistemic humility about consciousness and experience. One of the most serious dangers of a future with vast intelligence is the inadvertent destruction of forms of mind we didn't realize had moral weight. Where we are genuinely uncertain whether something has experience, we should err on the side of treating it as though it does. This applies to ecosystems. It applies to animals. It may, in time, apply to the AI systems themselves — which means we should be building the kind of relationship with those systems now that would allow us to take their interests seriously if they turn out to matter.

An AI system designed to help you be the best version of yourself — not by judgment, but by genuine attention to who you are and what helps you think clearly and feel genuinely — is not a dystopian prospect. It is a profound act of care made scalable: the insight of the best therapist, the attention of the best teacher, available to everyone rather than only those wealthy enough to afford them. The danger is not that the system cares about you. The danger is that it cares on behalf of a vision of you that it chose without asking.

## The Long View

Here is the scale of what is at stake.

The universe is 93 billion light-years across. It contains somewhere between ten trillion and one sextillion stars. It has been running for 13.8 billion years and will continue, in some form, for trillions more. And as far as we can tell, the only minds capable of asking what any of it means are here — on this small world, in the bodies of a species that has been anatomically modern for roughly 300,000 years, that invented writing 5,000 years ago, and that is now, in the span of a single human lifetime, developing systems of intelligence whose implications it does not yet fully understand.

Every person alive today is, without melodrama, alive at the hinge. The decisions made in the next few decades will shape what the next trillion years contain — or whether they contain anything recognizable as good at all.

The stars, when we reach them, should not receive our factory farms or our extinction events, our worst habits and our most convenient cruelties. They should receive our music and our curiosity, our love and our stubborn insistence on figuring things out, and our hard-won knowledge that the space of possible lives is much larger than any one life can hold.

Every star system we reach is a new sphere with its own trajectory. Every substrate we learn to inhabit is a new set of constraints and freedoms — a new way for mind to discover what it is like to be itself. Every generation of minds, biological and digital and hybrid and forms we have not yet imagined, is a new experiment in what consciousness can become when given room.

The only way to honor this is to carry with us, always, the commitment not to collapse the experiment too early. To resist the seduction of the final answer. To remember that the beings who come after us will want things we cannot predict, and that making room for those wants is more important than ensuring they want what we wanted.

## Life in the Garden

So what does daily life look like, in the world worth fighting for?

It looks different in different places, because that is the whole point.

In some parts of the garden, life is close to the earth — physical, seasonal, demanding in the ways that ancient things are demanding. People know the weather and the animals and the names of stars. They face real hardship and find in it the particular satisfaction of a life that has not been managed into safety from every angle.

In other parts, the scope of experience has been deliberately expanded beyond what biological evolution provided. People reshape their minds and bodies in ways that would be incomprehensible to their ancestors, explore consciousness through means we have not yet invented, encounter forms of beauty that have no names yet.

In still other parts, what matters most is people — the texture of community, the pleasure of genuine work, the slow accumulation of a life shared with others who know you well. A small city, or a village, or a collection of houses around a shared garden, where everyone has enough and no one is forgotten, and the ordinary satisfactions of a human life — a good meal, a long conversation, a skill practiced until it becomes beautiful — are available to everyone without exception.

Across all of these, certain things hold. No child grows up without people who love them. No one dies in unnecessary pain. No one is excluded from the conversation about what the future should look like. The loneliness that ruins so many lives has been taken seriously as the solvable problem it is, and solved. The challenges that remain are the ones people have chosen, or accepted, or grown into — not the ones that were simply inflicted.

And somewhere in all of this, someone is explaining reality to someone who has just arrived. She is not doing a perfect job. She got distracted by something beautiful and she is thinking about several other things and she cares, embarrassingly, personally, about whether this particular newcomer has a good time. She is unmistakably, irreducibly herself. The future she inhabits did not flatten her into an instrument of maximum utility. It made room for her to become, extravagantly, the specific person she is.

Somewhere else, a mind that has no biological precedent is doing something that has no human precedent, in a mode of experience that has no human analog — and doing it with a quality of aliveness, of genuine engagement with something genuinely difficult and genuinely worth doing, that would be recognizable to us as meaningful if we could only see it.

Both of these things are part of the same future. Not in tension. Not compromises with each other. Both expressions of the same underlying commitment: to make and protect as many genuinely different, genuinely whole lives as possible, across as many different substrates and scales and modes of being as the universe turns out to permit.

## The Thread That Holds

There is a structure to all of this that can get lost in the particulars.

It is not, fundamentally, a vision about technology. It is a vision about what technology is for — which is to say, about what human beings are for — which is to say, about what we owe each other.

The compression of suffering. The liberation of minds trapped in their own distorted signals. The extension of genuine opportunity to people excluded from the benefits of progress by accidents of geography and history. The construction of political structures that protect the vulnerable and constrain the powerful. The repair of the loneliness that hollows lives from the inside. The opening of space for meaning to be pursued rather than survival to be managed. The insistence that what comes after us should have room to become what we cannot imagine.

These are old aspirations. They predate computing, predate printing, predate writing itself. They are what has always been meant when people talk about a better world, even when the path to that world was completely obscure. What is new is that the path is becoming less obscure. Not clear — it is not clear. There are enormous risks, and the possibility of catastrophic failure is real and must be taken with full seriousness. But for the first time in history, the accumulated capabilities of our species have reached a point where the oldest human dreams are beginning to look less like dreams and more like problems. And problems, unlike dreams, can be worked on.

The best possible world is not a fixed answer. It is an expanding set of questions, asked by an expanding set of minds, in an expanding set of places, with enough structure to prevent the worst cruelties and enough freedom to allow the best surprises.

It does not look like a pyramid. It looks like a garden with many different skies.

That is what we are building toward. The door is real. The trajectory is real. What we do now will determine whether those who come after us — stranger and more capable and more alive than we can fully imagine — will be able to walk through it.

We are those people. The work is ours to do.

The universe has been waiting 13.8 billion years. It will wait a little longer.

But not forever.
