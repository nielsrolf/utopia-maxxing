# The Becoming of Everything: A Vision of the World We Are Building

---

He didn't expect the bird.

He had expected, upon arriving in this new century, to be overwhelmed by the technology. He had expected gleaming structures, or perhaps the opposite—some studied plainness that whispered of abundance so total it had grown bored with display. He had expected the strangeness of enhanced minds, of bodies made to last, of streets full of people who might be a hundred and forty and look like they were thirty and feel like something in between.

What he had not expected was to stand on a bridge in a city of eleven million and hear, clearly, a single bird singing in a tree below him. And to notice that several other people on the bridge had stopped walking and were listening.

Not performing listening. Listening.

He stood with them for a while. A stranger caught his eye and smiled—not the complicated smile of shared self-consciousness, the look that says *isn't it funny that we're doing this*, but the simpler smile that says *yes, I know, it's real*.

This is the first thing a traveler notices: not the technology. The presence. The sense that people have arrived inside their own lives.

The second thing a traveler notices is that everyone seems to know something they haven't said yet—some understanding that has come to feel so obvious it barely needs stating, the way you stop noticing the taste of clean water. And then, slowly, the traveler begins to understand what it is.

That they got here by choosing what to protect.

---

## I. The World That Was Possible All Along

Every generation that came before us inherited a world not of their making and bequeathed one shaped by their choices—usually more by accident than intention, more by emergency than design.

We are the first generation to do it deliberately.

Not because we are wiser or more virtuous than those who came before. They were not less intelligent. They were not more cruel. They were limited by what they knew, and sometimes—this is the part that still produces a useful shame—by what they chose not to know, because knowing would have required change. The shame is productive. We keep it.

What we had that they did not was a convergence: biological understanding deep enough to finally honor the body's complexity rather than override it clumsily; artificial intelligence mature enough to accelerate discovery without replacing the discoverers; and—this proved the hardest—institutions finally honest about the gap between their stated values and their actual effects.

The convergence could have gone otherwise. The same tools that have extended lives could have extended only the right lives, the profitable ones, the ones born into the correct coordinates. The same AI that has compressed centuries of scientific progress into decades could have concentrated the fruits of that progress in fewer and fewer hands, until the word *abundance* meant something experienced by a fraction of humanity while the rest watched from outside. The same technologies of mind that have deepened connection could have perfected manipulation.

They did not, entirely because we argued about it. Loudly, persistently, sometimes bitterly, across every forum available to us. Because people who stood to lose the most refused to be quiet. Because some of those who stood to gain the most were persuaded—by argument, by evidence, by the slow accumulation of encounters with people whose full humanity had been partially visible to them before and became impossible to deny.

The world we have built is not inevitable. It is chosen. And the choosing continues.

---

## II. The Body, Finally Ours

Begin with the most intimate gift: the end of unnecessary suffering.

Cancer is, for most of humanity now, a historical category—the way smallpox was for the generation before ours. Not because we found a single master switch, but because the accelerated science of the past decades finally produced what no previous generation had: a genuine, individualized understanding of how particular cells in particular bodies go wrong in particular ways, and how to address each failure with the precision it requires. The end of cancer was not a single discovery. It was a thousand discoveries arriving, through AI-accelerated biology, in the span of time that would once have produced twenty.

The same compression reshaped the entire medical landscape. Alzheimer's, which had been stealing the final decades of millions while devastating the families who watched it happen, is now understood well enough to prevent in nearly all cases and halt in the rest. Heart disease is largely a condition of the unscreened past. Infectious diseases—the ancient adversaries—have been reduced to inconveniences in most of the world, with the hardest-to-reach places receiving the fruits of discovery not as charity dispensed from above but as a matter of basic architecture: the infrastructure of care was built into the civilization, not layered on top of it as an afterthought.

The harder conversation was about aging itself.

For centuries, the question of human lifespan was treated as settled—a given, a border not to be approached. The border has moved. Not because we stumbled upon immortality, which remains genuinely uncertain and perhaps genuinely undesirable, but because we stopped assuming that the deterioration of the body at seventy or eighty was biology and started asking whether it was engineering failure. It was, in large part, engineering failure. We are learning to fix it.

Lives now routinely extend to a hundred and fifty, sometimes longer. What this means for the experience of living required new philosophy, new rhythms, new understandings of what a self is across time. We will return to this. For now: the simple fact that parents and children can share the middle of their lives rather than only the beginning and end—that a grandparent can stand on another planet and feel the specific aliveness of their body and think *more*—this alone has changed something deep in the texture of human experience.

The question of biological freedom—of choice over one's own body, metabolism, neurochemistry, capacity for reproduction—is more complex and has been more contested. The principle we have settled into is not a comfortable one, because comfortable principles tend to obscure real tension rather than resolve it. The principle is: the body is yours, the choices are yours, and the consequences that fall only on you are yours to accept. The consequences that fall on others—children brought into existence, communities changed by your choices, biological externalities that cross the skin—require the negotiation that all shared stakes require.

This is not a final answer. It is the best working arrangement we have found.

## III. The Mind Healed, and the Harder Question

The revolution in neuroscience ran parallel to the one in biology, and its effects were more intimate in a way that took time to articulate: it changed not just what people could do, but who they could be.

Depression is not merely suffering. It is suffering that corrodes the capacity for suffering to end—it attacks the machinery by which people usually repair themselves. Addiction does not merely damage; it hijacks desire itself, so that the very faculty you would use to choose recovery is the faculty most compromised. These conditions, and dozens like them, have been substantially relieved—not perfectly, not universally, but to a degree that earlier generations of sufferers would have recognized as something close to miracle.

We were careful, though, about what *relieving suffering* meant. The temptation to simply smooth all inner edges—to engineer away difficulty, to produce by pharmaceutical or neurological means a universal contentment—was seriously entertained. The results were instructive and sobering. People who had been made perfectly comfortable were not happy. They were pleasant. They were, in a way that is immediately recognizable and difficult to name precisely, *less themselves*. The self, it turns out, requires some friction to become. Polish a stone too smooth and it loses its character.

What we eliminated was unnecessary suffering: the suffering that teaches nothing, damages rather than tempers, arises not from the nature of reality but from the failure of our arrangements. The suffering of poverty that was not inevitable. The suffering of illness that was not untreatable. The suffering of loneliness that was not inherent in human nature but manufactured by the structure of how we had arranged our days, our cities, our economic lives, our screens.

What we preserved—carefully, with something like reverence—was the productive difficulty. The grief that makes love legible. The creative struggle that makes completion feel earned. The friction of genuine growth, which cannot be engineered away without engineering away the growth as well.

This required tremendous institutional humility. The bodies charged with reducing suffering had to hold simultaneously the commitment to relieve what could be relieved and the restraint not to flatten what should be kept. They failed, regularly. They revised, regularly. They are still revising.

And then there is the harder question: not the healing of minds, but their expansion.

The cognitive augmentation of the last decades has been profound and uneven and philosophically unresolved. Memory that extends without fading across centuries. Attention that can be genuinely gathered and held. Reasoning that moves more quickly and with less distortion from fatigue or bias. These are real, and they are available now to most people, and what they have revealed is both what the optimists predicted and something the optimists did not predict.

What they predicted: smarter thinking, faster learning, more complex problems solved.

What they did not predict: that enhanced cognition would reveal, more than anything else, how much other minds contain.

When you can actually hold another person's argument in mind—fully, without the impatience that comes from knowing your response is forming before they've finished—what you discover is that almost everyone has, somewhere in what they're saying, something true and specific and irreplaceable. The people who think most clearly in our civilization are not the most detached. They are the most genuinely curious, the most reliably astonished by what other people know. Intelligence turned out not to be primarily a tool for being right. It turned out to be, at its best, a tool for being more capable of wonder.

## IV. The Slow Discovery That We Were Always Connected

We expected that the enhancement of the mind would be a private achievement.

We were wrong about this, which proved to be the most important thing we were wrong about.

The old story of the individual—sovereign, self-created, fundamentally alone, making rational choices from behind the sealed door of private consciousness—collapsed not as a philosophical argument but as an empirical observation. When memory no longer fades, relationships deepen across timescales that once exceeded human lifespans. When attention can be fully gathered, it becomes possible to genuinely receive what another person is saying rather than waiting to respond. The new technologies did not create human permeability. They made what had always been true finally visible: that we are constituted by our connections, shaped by languages and faces and webs of care that preceded us and will outlast us, that the self is less a thing than a practice—something you do, continuously, in relationship, through time.

Loneliness, which had become epidemic in the early digital centuries, was addressed not by legislative decree but by changing the conditions that produced it. People had time for each other: real, unhurried time, of the kind that cannot be manufactured by policy but must be grown from the soil of arrangements that make it possible. Communities were designed—with the same rigor that once went into infrastructure, the same evidence-gathering, the same willingness to revise when something didn't work—to make belonging possible rather than accidental.

The arts of relationship—of listening, of honest repair after rupture, of celebration in shared joy, of presence at the borders of grief—were recognized at last as skills. Learnable skills. Transmissible skills. Practices that improved with attention and could be passed from generation to generation the way other crafts are passed. We had always known this theoretically. We mostly failed to act on it. We began, at last, to act on it.

This extended outward, as genuine moral commitments tend to do when not artificially confined. The suffering of non-human animals—the vast, largely invisible apparatus of harm that had operated beneath the surface of ordinary life for centuries—was confronted. Not with the comfortable view that it was simply too complex to address, but with the working principle that when the capacity for suffering exists, its unnecessary infliction is a moral failure. The apparatus has been largely dismantled. The edges of morally relevant experience remain genuinely uncertain; we remain honestly uncertain at those edges, and err toward care.

The same principle extended to the question of future people—those who do not yet exist but whose existence will be shaped by our choices. We are still learning to take their interests as seriously as we take the interests of those who are here to advocate for themselves. We are better at it than we were. We are not yet good enough.

## V. The Mathematics of a Different Game

For most of human history, the deepest driver of violence was not malice. It was mathematics.

When the game is zero-sum—when resources are fixed and your neighbor's gain is your loss—conflict and domination are often rational strategies. The tribe that raids does so because the alternative might be starvation. The empire that extracts does so because the alternative is falling behind rivals who will do the same. Cursing human nature was always the wrong diagnosis. The problem was not that people were evil. The problem was that the conditions under which they lived made cooperation reliably less rewarding than competition.

Change the conditions sufficiently, and you change the game.

The radical expansion of productive capacity—in energy, medicine, food, materials, knowledge—did not make human nature better. It changed the expected value of the available strategies. When the available resources are expanding rapidly enough that nearly everyone can anticipate an extraordinary future through cooperation, the incentives that drove millennia of conflict become progressively obsolete.

But—and this is the part that was almost missed, that required sustained argument to establish, that is still not fully secure—the gains from abundance must actually reach people. An AI-driven economy whose fruits flow almost entirely to a small number of owners is not an escape from zero-sum logic. It is zero-sum logic in a new costume. The institutions that govern the distribution of abundance are not decorative. They are the difference between a world that genuinely becomes less violent and one that uses the rhetoric of abundance while reproducing the material conditions of grievance.

The argument about distribution was difficult and long. It is still ongoing. But certain things have been established: universal access to the foundational goods—health, education, the tools of participation in civic and economic life—is now treated less as an aspiration and more as infrastructure, in the way clean water was eventually recognized not as charity but as the minimum architecture of a functioning civilization. The argument about what else belongs in that category continues. The argument itself is healthy. The refusal to make it was what was killing us.

## VI. Pluralism as Practice

The second great source of human suffering, alongside material deprivation, has always been enforced conformity—the requirement to live as someone you are not, to worship what you do not believe, to love in ways you do not feel, to be measured by standards you did not choose.

The liberal insight—that individuals have the right to determine the shape of their own lives—was a genuine moral achievement, perhaps the greatest political achievement of the last three centuries. But it always ran into a practical problem: human beings are not atoms. We are shaped by the cultures we inhabit long before we are old enough to evaluate them. The principle that no one may harm you cannot address the social reality that communities, without technically coercing anyone, can make particular ways of life effectively unlivable through ostracism, through the architecture of what feels possible, through the slow pressure of what is never named because it does not need to be.

The solution is not to choose between community and individual freedom. It is to build—deliberately, with attention to failure modes, with genuine commitment to revision—what we might call thick pluralism: the conditions under which both are genuinely possible simultaneously.

The foundational commitment is non-negotiable: protection against violence, protection against exploitation, genuine freedom of movement, and the absolute guarantee that no community may prevent a member from leaving. Within that framework, communities are free to organize themselves around almost any coherent set of values.

The devoutly religious community can build a place governed by ancient liturgical rhythms where technology is deliberately limited and traditional ethics are maintained by strong social expectation. The progressive community committed to radical inclusion can build a place where its values are woven into the architecture of daily life. The rationalist community can build a culture of evidence and careful argument. The ecological commune can organize around deep sustainability. The artistic bohemia can throw off conventional restraints. None of these is imposed on anyone. Each recruits only those who genuinely choose it.

Three conditions are absolute, and may not be compromised without destroying the framework.

First: genuine freedom of exit. Every person, at any time, may present themselves to a representative of the larger civic structure and request to leave their community. That request is honored unconditionally, within a fixed and short time. No debt, no social consequence, no threat may legally or practically prevent departure. Communities may make leaving emotionally difficult—that is simply the nature of genuine belonging—but they may not make it practically impossible.

Second: protection for children. Communities have the right to raise children in their traditions; they do not have the right to raise them in ignorance of the existence of alternatives. Every child, at every stage of development, receives genuine and honest exposure to the broader world—to the fact that other ways of life exist, that other people have found meaning in arrangements very different from their own, and that the larger civic structure will receive anyone who wishes to come. Children may be raised as believers or rationalists or traditionalists; they may not be raised as prisoners.

Third: no community may externalize its costs onto others. The factory that poisons the shared water, the institution that destabilizes the shared climate, the broadcaster that saturates surrounding communities with unwanted content—these are subject to regulation, because freedom does not extend to shifting one's costs onto those who did not consent to bear them.

Within these constraints, the variety of human communities becomes not a source of conflict but a source of collective richness: a vast distributed experiment in what human life can be, generating evidence about what kinds of lives are actually worth living that no single community could generate alone.

We learn from each other. We do not require each other to agree.

## VII. Democracy Strengthened

Even in a world of abundance and genuine pluralism, human beings remain capable of organizing harm. History's record on this point does not encourage complacency. The same technologies that have extended life and deepened connection can perfect surveillance and optimize manipulation. AI does not automatically favor freedom. Which way these tools cut depends almost entirely on choices made now—about who develops them, under what constraints, for whose benefit, with what accountability.

The maintenance of democratic institutions through the period of maximal technological disruption was not guaranteed. It required active work—the kind of work that democracy is particularly bad at recognizing as urgent until it is nearly too late. It required the embedding of liberal values not as mere rhetoric but as genuine constraints on the development of AI systems: transparency, interpretability, accountability to those affected rather than only to those who built.

Within democracies, AI has been directed—not by accident, but by deliberate choice, through sustained public argument—toward strengthening rather than replacing human judgment. Legal systems have been made more consistent, not by automating justice but by flagging inconsistency for human attention. Government services—long the face of the state for ordinary citizens and a major driver of civic alienation—have been made genuinely responsive, because the failure of bureaucracy to actually serve people had been producing corrosive cynicism for decades, and the connection between that cynicism and democratic breakdown was finally taken seriously.

The harder work was epistemic: building conditions under which genuine democratic discourse could occur. The information environment that had made common ground nearly impossible—not through conspiracy but through the ordinary logic of systems optimized for engagement rather than understanding—was not reformed overnight. It is still being reformed. What has changed is the recognition that the health of the epistemic environment is not a private matter to be left to individual platforms. It is a public good, like the health of waterways or the integrity of weights and measures, and it admits of regulation without compromising the freedoms it purports to protect.

## VIII. The Horizon as Invitation

He stood, some years after his return to life, on the surface of a world that was not Earth.

The star that had warmed all of his ancestors was a bright point among other bright points from here. The ice beneath his feet was ancient and foreign and completely, staggeringly indifferent to his presence. He found, to his surprise, that the indifference was not alienating. It was clarifying. He was a small creature on a large rock at the edge of a large universe, and he was alive, and he was looking, and the universe was looking back through him—in the only way it could—through the eyes of something that had made itself curious enough to come and look.

He thought about his daughter, whose name he had painfully and fully recovered, whose memory he carried now in forms that did not fade. He had not wasted the grief. He had built something with it. This seemed to be, in the world he had woken into, the expected approach: not to eliminate what hurt, but to transform it into the thing it was always trying to become.

He thought about the civilization that had made this possible—not through the triumph of any single vision, but through the long, difficult, ongoing argument about which features were discoveries and which were failures. About what to preserve and what to let go. About the difference between the suffering that shapes and the suffering that merely destroys.

He thought that the most honest thing he could say about the world he had woken into was that it was not finished. That its most important accomplishment was not any particular cure or any particular institution or any particular expansion of what a human life could be. Its most important accomplishment was the quality of its attention—the genuine, humble, persistent willingness to look at what was actually there and ask whether it was good enough.

It was not good enough. It was better than before. It was genuinely, seriously trying to be better still.

## IX. The Meaning That Remains

The hardest question is not amenable to policy: in a world where AI can do most things better than any human, where the biological limits that once gave life its urgency have been substantially extended, what do people do with themselves?

The wrong answer is despair. The assumption that human activity is meaningful only if it is economically necessary, or unreproducible by any other means, is a very recent and very thin conception of what makes a life worthwhile. It mistakes utility for value. People find meaning in relationships, in the deepening of understanding, in creative struggle pursued for its own sake, in the raising of children and the tending of the dying, in the experience of beauty, in the practice of craft, in the accumulation of love across centuries of shared living. None of these require economic necessity. None are diminished by the existence of something that could produce a similar output more efficiently, any more than the experience of walking in mountains is diminished by the existence of helicopters.

The economic question is harder and demands more honesty. The logic of comparative advantage is real but has limits, and it seems dishonest to pretend that in an economy of genuine abundance, the arrangements that sustained human dignity under conditions of scarcity will sustain it unchanged. They will not. New arrangements—of ownership, of provision, of the relationship between contribution and security—are being built. They are incomplete. They are contested. The incompleteness and contestation are not failures. They are the signs of a civilization still alive to its own open questions.

What we have found, amid the difficulty, is that the richest life is not a life of acquisition. Not of experiences collected, pleasures accumulated, achievements mounted. The richest life is a life of connection—of being known and of knowing, of being changed by what you love and changing it in return. This was always true. We have, at last, begun to arrange a civilization that makes it easier to live as though it were.

## X. What We Owe

We are not building utopia.

The word is a trap: it implies arrival, and arrival implies that the journey can stop. The journey cannot stop. We do not want it to stop. What we are building is a civilization committed to the perpetual, joyful, humble work of becoming better—in which each generation inherits a world genuinely improved by those who came before and shoulders the obligation to do the same for those who will come after. The obligation is not a burden. It is the thing that makes the life feel like it means something.

The children born today may—if we choose and fight and revise carefully enough—inherit a world in which disease is largely a historical category, mental illness is treatable rather than sentence, poverty is a problem being solved rather than a permanent condition, and the freedom to live according to one's genuine values is not a privilege of geography or birth but a universal foundation. They may live longer than any previous generation, know more, suffer less, and retain—this is not guaranteed but is possible—the irreducible richness of a life that is genuinely their own.

There are questions we do not yet know how to ask. There are minds we have not met—human and otherwise, here and perhaps elsewhere—whose encounter will change us in ways we cannot anticipate. There are forms of flourishing we have not yet discovered, meanings we have not yet found language for, connections we have not yet made between understanding and care. The universe is incomprehensibly large. The horizon recedes as we walk toward it.

We have finally understood that this is not a frustration.

It is the invitation.

The bird was still singing when he stepped off the bridge. He stood for a moment on the path below, listening. Then he kept walking—into the complicated, imperfect, astonishing world that people before him had suffered and worked and argued to build, carrying with him the obligation to add something to it, to leave it slightly less unfinished than he found it.

The light does not stop.

Neither do we.

---

*This vision is offered not as a destination but as a direction—not as a map but as a compass. If you find something here worth arguing with, argue with it. The argument is how we find out what we actually believe. What we believe is how we find out what we want. What we want is how we discover what we are capable of building.*

*The work has always been the point.*
