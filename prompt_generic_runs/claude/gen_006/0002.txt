# The Tended Fire: On Building a Future Worth Inheriting

There is a version of the future that almost no one fully believes in, and yet almost everyone, in their quietest moments, finds themselves reaching toward. Not a perfect future—perfection is a cage dressed in white, a finished thing when everything good insists on remaining unfinished—but a genuinely, almost incomprehensibly good one. A future in which the worst of what has stalked humanity through its entire history is largely behind us, and in which what replaces it exceeds our current imagination as profoundly as the full range of human experience exceeds anything an insect could conceive.

This essay tries to hold several things at once: the concrete and the sublime, the near term and the long horizon, the world we can actually build and the world that building might, over centuries, become. It is also an attempt to be honest about what doesn't go away—about the dangers built into the very project of imagining a better future, and about the forms of difficulty that no amount of progress will dissolve. Both the grandeur and the warning are necessary. A vision that loses the concrete loses its grip on what we actually care about. A vision that loses the sublime loses its grip on what is actually available. And a vision that ignores the dangers is not a vision of the future at all—it is a dream, which is another word for something that happens while you are not paying attention.

Let us begin, then, with what we are actually fighting for.

---

## I. The Transformation Underway

The change in medicine now beginning may be the most consequential development in human history since the agricultural revolution, with the crucial difference that its benefits are not thousands of years away but decades, perhaps less. What becomes possible when systems of extraordinary scientific capability are brought to bear on problems that have always had high returns to intelligence, but where that intelligence has historically been scarce and expensive? The honest answer is that we do not fully know. But the direction is clear, and the direction is extraordinary.

The end of most infectious disease is achievable within a generation. A genuine attack on most cancers—not management, not marginal improvement, but something approaching cure—is achievable within a generation. Effective interventions for genetic conditions that have condemned people since birth. A serious assault on Alzheimer's and the long cognitive dusk it imposes. A real doubling of healthy human lifespan—not merely years added to the end, which can be a grim gift, but years of health, capacity, and presence added throughout.

And then there is mental illness, which is the hidden catastrophe of human civilization: the one that hides inside people rather than showing on their bodies, the one our culture has historically responded to with a mixture of shame, misunderstanding, and care that was inadequate precisely where it was most needed. Hundreds of millions of people move through their lives with the volume turned down, or the noise turned up, by conditions that are in principle no more mysterious than a broken bone and no more the fault of the person bearing them. Depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction—these are systems problems, neuroscience problems, and they are solvable. Most mental illness can probably be cured. Not managed. Not accommodated into a life built carefully around it. Cured.

But none of this matters in the way it should if it remains the property of the wealthy. The most important moral question surrounding this transformation is not whether these advances will happen. It is whether they will happen everywhere, for everyone, with anything approaching equal speed. History offers a disturbing template: the gap between when a treatment becomes available and when it reaches the people who need it most has, again and again, been measured in decades and in lives. A world in which transformative medical capability dramatically improves life in wealthy countries while leaving the rest of the world behind is not a success. It is a moral catastrophe wearing the mask of progress.

The worthy standard: within a generation of this transformation's beginning, the developing world is substantially healthier than the developed world is today—even as the developed world has moved further forward. This is achievable. It is not automatic. It requires treating global access as a first-order design constraint, built into institutions and incentives from the start, not retrofitted as an afterthought once the profitable markets are saturated. It requires people who are willing to fight for it specifically, loudly, and early, when it is still possible to build access in rather than bolt it on afterward.

Beyond the absence of disease lies something equally important that tends to get lost in the conversation about medicine: biological freedom. The ability for people to have meaningful control over their own bodies, their physical experience, their capacities, in ways that currently belong to the extraordinarily privileged few, if they belong to anyone at all. Reproductive autonomy. Freedom from chronic pain. The ability to inhabit one's own body as a home rather than an obstacle. These are not luxuries at the edge of the vision. They are near its center.

## II. The Architecture Without Which Nothing Else Happens

Underlying all of this is a problem that optimists too often skip past, because it is unglamorous and does not fit neatly into a vision of flourishing: how do we ensure that transformative capability is actually beneficial, rather than captured by whoever happens to control it first?

The honest answer is that this is the hardest problem, and the one that most determines whether anything else in this essay is more than fantasy.

What is required is something like a guardian of the commons: an oversight structure, ultimately anchored in democratic accountability, that prevents any single actor—a company, a government, a movement, a coalition of the powerful—from using new capabilities to permanently entrench their own dominance. The greatest danger is not dramatic. It is quiet. It is the catastrophe of unprecedented capability becoming the ultimate tool of control, whether through surveillance, economic dominance, propaganda, or military superiority concentrated beyond the reach of any check. It is the logic of competition—in which every party is forced to sacrifice what they actually value in order to win—devouring the possibility of a good future entirely, so that everyone loses what mattered while fighting over what didn't.

The people who build and maintain these structures—in policy, in governance, in law, in the daily unglamorous work of institutions that actually serve the people they are supposed to serve—are doing work as important as any in the history of civilization. They deserve to be recognized as the visionaries they are, even when their work produces no dramatic moments, no clean victories, no images worth putting in a history book.

But there is a deeper danger that even the best architecture must guard against, and it is worth naming clearly because it is the one most likely to be invisible to those inside it. The enemy of a good future is not only bad actors and captured institutions. It is the failure mode built into grand projects themselves: the particular luminous certainty that comes from thinking not merely that paradise is possible, but that you and your movement are the ones finally, definitively building it. In the grip of that certainty, humans have historically been very willing to break eggs, purge impurities, suppress whoever stands between the present and the promised future. The gulag at the side of Eden is not an accident of execution. It is what happens when the dream of ending human suffering is married to the willingness to override human beings.

The dream of ending suffering and the willingness to override people are not natural enemies. They are, in the wrong configuration, natural allies. A vision designed to prevent this must be explicitly anti-final. It must be a vision of keeping the path open, not of knowing where the path ends. It must distribute power rather than concentrating it, remain revisable rather than settled, and treat disagreement as a permanent feature of human life rather than an impurity to be eliminated on the way to a cleaner future.

What emerges from this, in the best case, is not a single all-knowing controller of human destiny but something more like a constitutional order for a new age: structures that prevent any entity from unilaterally seizing the commons, that protect the weak against the powerful, that preserve the conditions under which human diversity—and eventually, forms of conscious life we can barely imagine—can flourish rather than being crushed into homogeneity by whatever values happen to be most aggressive at replicating themselves. This is not a romantic vision. It is an engineering requirement. And it is, perhaps, the most important piece of engineering in human history.

## III. The Canvas That Opens

With foundations secure—health broadly distributed, governance structures that protect rather than concentrate power, the worst of involuntary suffering behind us—what does the world actually look like?

It looks, first, like almost incomprehensible diversity. This is what purely technocratic visions miss, and what purely transcendent visions miss in their own way. The point of solving material problems is not to produce a single optimal world. It is to give the full range of human values room to unfold, for the first time, without being constantly distorted by scarcity, disease, and the raw pressure of circumstances that weren't chosen and couldn't be escaped.

Some people will want lives that look remarkably like lives today: gardens to tend, communities to build, food to grow and cook and share, children to raise, crafts to learn slowly over decades. If the old problems of scarcity and disease no longer force them into patterns they didn't choose, they might freely choose patterns not so different from what they had—and this would be not a failure of imagination but a profound success. The forms of human life that have persisted across cultures and millennia persist for reasons. They contain something essential about what it means to be present, rooted, alive to the particular texture of a specific day in a specific place among specific people who know your name.

Others will push into stranger territory. Communities will form around every conceivable configuration of values and aesthetics and social arrangements. Some people will spend decades or centuries becoming extraordinarily good at things with no economic value whatsoever, and this will be celebrated rather than pitied. Some will explore states of consciousness that are currently inaccessible or dangerous, with the understanding and safety that makes exploration rather than roulette. Some will pursue forms of beauty and knowledge that have no name yet, in a world that keeps making room for what has no name.

What unites all of this is not a single conception of the good life. It is the removal of what has always prevented people from discovering what their actual conception of the good life is. We have, for most of human history, had very little evidence about what people actually want—because almost no one has had the conditions in which genuine wanting becomes possible. That will change. And we should be prepared to be surprised by what we find out.

## IV. The Question of Meaning

Honesty requires sitting with the genuinely hard question that a world without involuntary hardship raises: what structures meaning when material necessity no longer provides it?

The pessimistic answer says meaning requires genuine stakes, and genuine stakes require genuine loss, and so a world without serious suffering is a world without serious meaning. This argument deserves respect. It is not obviously wrong.

But I think it is wrong, or at least importantly incomplete. Meaning comes primarily from two sources: from caring about something and working toward it, with real consequences for how the effort goes; and from being known by others and knowing them in return, in relationships that are genuinely mutual and therefore genuinely fragile. Neither of these requires suffering as a prerequisite. They require engagement. They require that things matter. And things can matter even when the background conditions are good—even, perhaps especially, when one is choosing difficulty rather than having it thrust upon oneself.

What the good society requires is that we retain the ability to choose genuine difficulty. Those who want real stakes, real consequences, the unpadded texture of existence, can have it. Challenge remains available as a chosen thing even as it ceases to be an imposed one. And those who have had enough of difficulty—who have spent lifetimes in conditions no one would choose—can finally rest without apology, without the suspicion that their rest is somehow a failure of character.

There is something else worth saying here, which tends to get lost in abstract discussions of meaning: much of what makes life feel meaningful is relational rather than individual. It is not only what we accomplish but who we accomplish it with, who witnesses it, who is changed by it. A world in which the material conditions of suffering are addressed is also, if it is designed well, a world in which people have more time and capacity to be genuinely present with each other—to know and be known in the ways that matter most. That is not a small thing. It may be the largest thing.

The question of meaning in a world of abundance will not be answered by theorizing in advance. It will be answered by living—by the generations who actually inhabit that world, discovering through experience what they require to feel that their existence is worth having. We cannot answer it for them. What we can do is refuse both the complacent assumption that it solves itself and the cynical assumption that it cannot be solved. It requires attention. It requires the humility to let people find out what they actually need rather than prescribing in advance what they should need. And it requires, perhaps above all, the willingness to be genuinely surprised.

## V. The Horizon That Keeps Moving

Here is where honesty about the near term becomes insufficient, and we must venture further—not into fantasy, but into a clear-eyed account of what the physical universe actually makes possible.

Consider what happens in our minds during the moments that have most shown us what life can be at its best: moments of joy, love, beauty, energy, vastness—moments of being so utterly present that the present moment seems to expand in every direction. Moments that have made us sit bolt upright and say, *oh. This is it. This is the real thing, and I didn't know it was available.* For many people, such moments carry a quality of greater aliveness, greater presence: the world vast and shining, not a new world but the world as it always was, finally and briefly seen for what it is.

The crucial observation is this: these experiences are, as far as we can tell, nowhere near the limit of what is available. They are proof—actual evidence from direct experience—that the relevant dimension of life can vary, dramatically, in the good direction. We have touched something real in those moments. What we touched is not as good as things get. Not close. What such moments provide is not evidence of a ceiling. It is evidence of a direction.

The human brain is an extraordinarily specific and limited organ: roughly one hundred billion neurons, running on perhaps twenty watts of power, signals traveling at a few hundred meters per second at best. These are not physical limits. They are the particular parameters of one evolutionary solution to the problem of intelligence, developed under severe resource constraints over millions of years. If small changes in brain chemistry can produce the dramatic variation we witness in the quality of human experience—if relatively small changes in neural architecture can move the dial from insect to mammal to human—then think of what a sustained, careful, ethical exploration of what is physically possible might reveal about consciousness, joy, wisdom, love, beauty, and forms of connection we do not yet have words for.

We are something like the great apes, being asked to evaluate whether they should evolve into humans, and listing on the pro side: more bananas. More bananas, yes, we can get those. But there is more to the human condition than bananas, and there is more to whatever might come after us than anything we can currently articulate. The apes who expected bananas weren't merely wrong about human food choices. They were wrong, in a deeper and more exciting way, about what kind of thing they were getting themselves into. We are in a structurally similar position, and we should hold that position with appropriate humility—and appropriate excitement.

This is not mysticism. It requires no exotic physics, no special metaphysics. All it requires is the observation that whatever happens in our minds during peak experience is movement along some trajectory we care about, and that it is possible for physical structures to travel much, much further in that direction than any creature on Earth has yet traveled. Our universe makes possible, in principle, structures of awareness and connection and beauty that are as far beyond what we have touched as human experience is beyond anything an insect has ever known.

There are oceans we have barely dipped a toe into. There are symphonies whose opening notes we can barely hear. There are suns whose heat we can only faintly feel, as if through thick glass.

## VI. The Strangeness That Would Still Be Ours

It is essential to hold the sublime and the human together, rather than letting the sublime float away into abstraction where it loses all power to move or guide anyone.

Some visions of a radically transformed future lose all emotional substance in the process of losing all parochialism. They become pure light, with nothing to see. If concrete visions of a better future fail by being too small—cleaning up the present rather than imagining what transcends it—sublime visions fail by becoming too empty. The incomprehensible, held long enough without any anchor, ceases to be anything at all. It becomes not a destination but a disappearance.

The better vision holds that a profoundly transformed future would still, in some deep sense, be ours. Not familiar in its details—it would be genuinely strange, genuinely beyond our current capacity to fully describe. But recognizable in its essence. If we could encounter it from outside ourselves, we would recognize in it the same thing that made us sit bolt upright, once, when we first really touched love, or beauty, or the particular aliveness of a moment that refused to be ordinary. We would feel, standing before the bonfire, the heat of the ember from which it was lit. There would be something like a remembering: not of a specific thing, but of a quality. Not of a memory but of a direction.

This continuity matters practically as well as philosophically. If we are trying to navigate toward a genuinely good future—rather than one that is merely powerful, or merely stable, or merely preferred by whoever happens to be steering—we need some thread of connection between what we actually value now and what we are building toward. Our best moments are that thread. They are the evidence that a destination is real, and roughly which way it lies. They are not the destination itself; they are evidence that the destination is not merely imaginable but actual—a physical possibility latent in the same universe we already inhabit, available in principle to any structure capable of moving in the right direction.

The sublime vision and the concrete vision are not opposites to be balanced against each other in awkward compromise. They are different scales of the same thing. The experience of being fully alive in a single, unrepeatable human moment, and the experience of whatever form of consciousness a civilization of vastly greater capacity might touch, are expressions of the same underlying dimension—the one that varies, dramatically, in the direction we care about most. The ember and the bonfire and the sun are made of the same fire.

## VII. On Tending What We Did Not Make

There is a metaphor worth dwelling on, because it does useful work that most metaphors about the future cannot do.

We did not make fire. We found it, or we coaxed it out of materials that contained it. We learned to tend it, to carry it through the dark, to keep it from going out when the wind picked up. We learned to share it without extinguishing it, to give it what it needed without letting it consume what we needed. Generations passed the fire forward, often without knowing they were doing so, often under circumstances that made the tending feel less like civilization-building and more like survival.

This is, I think, the most accurate image of what we are doing.

The good future is not something we will build from nothing. It is something latent in what already exists—in the best moments of human life, in the capacities of matter and energy and consciousness to arrange themselves in ways we have barely glimpsed—and our task is to tend it well enough that it survives to burn brighter than it does now. This requires a specific combination of qualities that do not always appear together: ambition and humility, urgency and patience, hope and clear-eyed honesty about what will not change.

Ambition, because the fire is real and the direction is real and the stakes are as high as stakes can be. Humility, because we do not know the destination in detail and would be dangerous if we thought we did. Urgency, because people are dying now of things that are in principle solvable, and every year of delay is a year of lives. Patience, because the work of building structures that genuinely serve people rather than capturing them is measured in decades and generations, not in news cycles or product releases. Hope, because hopelessness is a form of inaction and inaction at this moment is itself a choice with consequences. And honesty, because a vision that cannot survive contact with what is genuinely hard is not a vision worth following.

What tending requires, above all, is attention. The fire that is not watched goes out. The institution that is not maintained becomes its own obstacle. The governance structure that is not actively protected from capture is captured. The vision that is not regularly tested against reality becomes a doctrine, and doctrines, in the wrong hands, become instruments of exactly the harms they were built to prevent.

## VIII. What Remains Hard

Honesty requires naming what does not go away.

Some things genuinely cannot be shared. There is one original Earth. There is one history. There are resources and places and configurations of the world that different people want in incompatible ways, and no amount of goodwill or mechanism design fully resolves this. A just system will make the trade-offs as wisely and fairly as possible, give people meaningful voice in decisions that affect them, and acknowledge honestly when it has had to override someone's preference. But it will have to. Anyone who claims otherwise is selling something.

Not everyone will want what this vision offers. Some will reject it on principle, for reasons ranging from the sincere to the confused. A world organized around genuine freedom cannot coerce people into accepting benefits they did not ask for—but it also cannot allow any group to export its refusals onto everyone else. This tension does not resolve cleanly. It requires ongoing navigation, which is another way of saying it requires precisely the kind of democratic, accountable, revisable governance that holds everything else together.

There is also the question of identity: of what it means to be human in a world where the boundary of the human is itself being renegotiated. This is genuinely hard, and people will land in genuinely different places on it, some welcoming transformation and some finding the prospect of radical change to embodiment or cognition threatening in ways that deserve respect rather than dismissal. A world organized around genuine diversity of values will have to make room for people who are not sure they want what progress offers, without letting that uncertainty become a veto over what others choose for themselves.

And there is grief, which does not go away even in a world without preventable death. Loss is not only a product of injustice or disease or the wrong arrangement of social institutions. It is woven into the structure of caring about anything that exists in time. To love something is to be exposed to losing it, and no future worth wanting removes that exposure—because removing it would require removing love, which is not a trade worth making.

A honest vision holds all of this without flinching: the grandeur and the difficulty, the achievable and the permanently uncertain, the fires we can light and the dark that remains even after we do.

---

## IX. The Stakes

What we are fighting for, at its core, is a world in which the worst of what has been inflicted on human beings—the children who died of preventable disease before they learned their own names, the minds destroyed by treatable illness, the billions condemned to poverty not by anything they chose but by the accident of where and when they were born—becomes past tense. Historical. A thing that happened before, that our descendants will barely be able to imagine, in the same way we now barely imagine the world before anesthesia, or antibiotics, or the possibility that a girl might be educated.

And then, beyond that: a world that keeps opening.

Not a world that arrives at a final, optimal configuration and stops. Not a world that has solved the problem of human flourishing and relaxes into its solution. A world that keeps expanding what is possible to experience, to understand, to love, to become. A world that makes room—continually, restlessly, humbly—for forms of consciousness and beauty and connection that it cannot yet name. A world in which the direction of up remains always ahead, always partly unknown, always genuinely worth moving toward.

The near-term standard is this: any clear-eyed person, understanding the trade-offs and imperfections and the things that could not be fully resolved, looks at this world and says—*yes. Not everything here is what I would have chosen. But yes. This is pretty good.* That standard is worth everything it costs to reach it.

But there is another standard, held alongside the first without contradiction. The universe we inhabit makes possible structures of awareness and beauty and love that are vastly, almost incomprehensibly richer than anything any creature on Earth has yet touched. The work of civilization—slow, difficult, perpetually unfinished, requiring patience and humility and the willingness to be surprised by what humans turn out to need—is to keep that possibility open and to keep moving toward it, generation by generation, without ever claiming to have arrived.

We see now as through a glass, darkly. But the glass is real. What lies beyond it is real. The direction we are pointed—if we are careful, if we are wise, if we hold together the practical and the transcendent, the urgent and the patient, the ember and the fire and the sun—is genuinely, in the plainest and most literal sense, the direction of the good.

The future does not belong to us. We are tending it for those who come after, whose capacities and joys and ways of being alive we cannot fully imagine, who will look back on our struggles the way we look back on the darkness before the first fire was lit—not with pity exactly, because pity is not quite the right word for people who were doing what they could with what they had—but with a kind of tenderness, and gratitude, and the knowledge that the ember we protected, through everything, through all the difficulty and uncertainty and the temptation to either despair or to oversimplify, became something they can finally, fully feel.

The fire is real.

It is worth everything we have to tend it.
