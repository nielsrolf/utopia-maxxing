# The World We Are Building Toward

There is a question worth asking before any other: what are we actually trying to build?

Not in the sense of policy platforms or technological roadmaps, but in the deepest sense. What does human life look like when it has been taken seriously? When the intelligence we have spent millennia developing is turned, with genuine effort and care, toward the problem of how to live well together?

I want to attempt an answer. Not a manifesto, not a blueprint, but a portrait — composite and incomplete, as all honest portraits are. A picture of a world in which the scattered goods that humans have always cared about have been tended rather than neglected, coordinated rather than squandered, cultivated over generations rather than sacrificed to the urgencies of the moment.

---

## The Individual Life, Taken Seriously

Begin with the person.

In the world we are building toward, a human life is long — genuinely, astonishingly long. Not merely extended in the way of a stretched-out decline, but extended in the way of having more time to become who you are, to master the things worth mastering, to see the consequences of the things you set in motion. The defeat of biological aging is not primarily a technological achievement but a moral one: a recognition that the arbitrary truncation of a person who was still growing, still loving, still curious, is not a fact of nature to be accepted but an injustice to be corrected.

Alongside longevity, health — including and perhaps especially mental health, which has been the most neglected province of medicine in every era. The extremes of suffering that evolution built into us — the depths of despair, the paralysis of anxiety, the distortions of psychosis — are not essential to human experience. They are malfunctions. They will be treated as such.

But length and health are only the container. What fills it matters more.

The best version of an individual life involves genuine self-authorship: the experience of having set goals that are yours, pursued them through real difficulty, and arrived somewhere you could not have predicted. Not the false self-determination of choosing between pre-packaged options, but the deeper kind — the kind that requires knowing yourself well enough to know what you actually want, and having the stability and freedom to pursue it. This requires mastering the internal architecture of attention and motivation: becoming, gradually, a more coherent agent, one whose future self is in honest dialogue with whose present self is in honest dialogue with whose past self. This is not about eliminating all internal conflict, which would produce something less than human. It is about replacing the conflicts that merely waste — addiction, procrastination, self-sabotage born of old wounds — with the ones that actually generate something: the tension between competing goods, the friction of growth.

What do people do with such lives? Everything, and differently. Some spend centuries on a single project, watching it unfold like a slow geological event. Some move through distinct phases, each as different from the last as childhood is from adulthood — with the wisdom to understand that what feels like the whole of life from inside any one phase is only a chapter. Some cultivate inner stillness. Some create. Some explore the outer universe, and some explore the inner one — the vast territory of possible conscious experience that our current brains can barely gesture toward.

The pleasures available in this world are not merely more intense versions of present pleasures, though they are that too. They are new in kind — the result of taking seriously the question of what kinds of experience are possible for minds like ours, and then deliberately engineering the conditions for those experiences to occur. As music was to the early hominid — an evolutionary accident that turned out to unlock a whole dimension of value — so there are accesses to beauty and meaning and joy that we have not yet found the keys to.

One further thing: in this world, nobody is consistently at the bottom of every hierarchy. Everyone has their time of recognition, their domain of mastery, their community within which they are known and valued. Status is diverse, multi-dimensional, and genuinely contested — which means that the goods of achievement and excellence remain, while the poison of permanent subordination is drained away.

## The Social Life, Taken Seriously

The deepest goods in human life are relational. Most people know this. Most societies have failed to act on it.

In the world we are building toward, children grow up knowing they are loved by more than a nuclear family — by a genuine web of adults who have chosen, in some meaningful sense, to be present for them. Not the village of romantic mythology, which was also often a prison, but something designed with intention: mutual adoption structures, extended communities, multiple adults investing in any given child's development, multiple children expanding any given adult's sense of stake in the future. The child who is seen by many is harder to lose.

Loneliness — the epidemic beneath all epidemics — has been addressed with the seriousness it deserves. Not papered over with digital simulacra of connection, but treated as the structural failure it is: a signal that our social arrangements have failed to place people within communities where they can be truly known. This requires deliberate design: architecture, scheduling, shared purpose, the facilitation of vulnerability. These are learnable skills, teachable crafts, subjects as worthy of serious study as chemistry or engineering.

Romantic love in this world has been liberated from two of its great enemies: the scarcity of good information and the surplus of unrealistic expectation. The matching problem — finding the people whose depths are genuinely compatible with yours — is treated as what it always was: one of the most consequential allocation problems any person faces, and therefore worthy of real professional sophistication. Not algorithms pretending to soul-knowledge, but patient, skilled human intermediaries who take the whole person seriously. And the unrealistic expectation problem — the ambient pressure of curated fantasy, the constant comparison to the most attractive person any camera has ever captured — is addressed by a civilization that has understood what it is doing to itself and chosen differently.

Trust, the substrate of all real relationship, is cultivated through what actually builds it: shared difficulty, consistent presence over time, honesty maintained through discomfort, the willingness to be known rather than merely seen. In a world where lives are very long, relationships can deepen in ways that are currently impossible — not because people are bound together, but because time itself becomes a resource rather than a constraint.

Society at the larger scale has also been taken seriously. Not unified into a single culture — the diversity of human experiment is a treasure, not a problem — but genuinely coordinated around the minimum required to prevent any part from harming the rest. The arrangement resembles a vast, nested series of communities, each with its own character and norms, held together by shared commitments to a small number of things that matter enough to be non-negotiable: the basic dignity of persons, the right to leave any community for another, the prohibition of actions that foreclose the options of others.

Within this structure, local experimentation is not just permitted but celebrated. Different communities try different answers to the great questions of how to live, and the results are observed, recorded, argued over, and learned from. The scientist's disposition has been extended to social life. What actually makes people flourish? We find out, carefully, rather than assuming we already know.

The people who govern these arrangements — who facilitate coordination, adjudicate disputes, maintain the conditions for shared life — are accountable in ways that are both more rigorous and more legible than present systems allow. Not because their power is eliminated, but because their reasoning is public, their predictions are recorded and checked, and their reputations are built or destroyed by the actual outcomes of their choices over time. The opacity that currently protects bad governance has been replaced by a transparency that rewards good judgment rather than good appearance.

## The Collective Intelligence, Taken Seriously

Perhaps the deepest resource that humanity currently wastes is its collective capacity to figure things out.

In the world we are building toward, what a medieval peasant would call literacy — the basic ability to decode written symbols — has an equivalent in something like coordination-literacy: a general, widespread understanding of how collective decisions go wrong, and what can be done about it. Not expertise, but the kind of functional understanding that allows a person to recognize when a situation has the structure of a commons problem, a prisoner's dilemma, a tragedy of the short-term — and to respond accordingly rather than blindly.

This literacy was not achieved by lecturing people about game theory. It was achieved by building institutions that reward far-sighted thinking, designing cultural narratives that frame cooperation as genuinely admirable rather than merely obligatory, and — most importantly — making the costs of coordination failure visible and legible rather than diffuse and deniable.

The institutions for generating and transmitting knowledge in this world have been rebuilt around what they are actually for. A free press that is genuinely distinguished from motivated advocacy — not by fiat, but by a culture that treats the distinction as important and rewards those who maintain it. A scientific culture in which replication is as honored as discovery, in which the admission of error is a mark of trustworthiness rather than weakness, in which the incentives for getting things right have been patiently realigned with the incentives that researchers actually face. Courts that publish their reasoning, invite scrutiny, and are evaluated on the quality of their judgment over time rather than the impressiveness of their ceremony.

None of this is easy. All of it has been resisted, in every era, by those whose interests are served by the current arrangements. What makes it possible, in the end, is something that cannot be engineered directly: a critical mass of people who understand that civilization is a choice, maintained by ongoing effort, and who take that understanding seriously enough to act on it.

## The Species Trajectory, Taken Seriously

Zoom out further, and the question becomes: what are we trying to become?

Not in the sense of a destination — the assumption that there is a final state to arrive at has always been the error behind the worst utopias. But in the sense of a direction: a set of values whose pursuit is coherent and whose abandonment would be a loss.

We are trying to become more conscious of the goods we actually care about, and more skilled at creating the conditions for them to flourish. We are trying to extend the circle of moral concern to everyone who suffers and everyone who can flourish — including the non-human minds that currently experience suffering at scales that dwarf human suffering, including the minds that will be created in the future and whose interests we are now, whether we acknowledge it or not, already deciding. We are trying to preserve enough diversity of experiment and enough freedom of exit that no single vision of the good can calcify into permanent tyranny.

We are also trying to survive. This is not separate from the above — it is the precondition for all of it. The technologies being developed in this era can, if mishandled, foreclose the entire future. Taking that seriously means treating it as the coordination problem it is: not a matter of individual virtue or individual caution, but of species-level wisdom, applied to the question of which capabilities to develop in which order, and how to maintain the conditions for course-correction when — not if — some things go wrong.

There is something worth preserving about the human story even as it changes beyond current recognition. The thread of continuity — the sense that the civilization exploring the far reaches of the cosmos grew from the civilization that told stories around fires, that the values being expressed in the unimaginable futures are recognizable as the values that have always, in their better moments, guided human striving — this matters. Not as a constraint that prevents change, but as a source of identity that makes change meaningful rather than merely random.

---

## The Shape of the Project

None of this is inevitable. The history of human possibility is also the history of human squandering — of goods that were within reach and were nonetheless lost, of cruelties maintained past the point where they served anyone's interests, of coordination failures that cost millions of lives and centuries of progress.

What makes the difference, in the end, is not primarily technological. Technology is necessary but insufficient. The deeper requirement is wisdom: the kind that accumulates through careful observation of what actually works, honest transmission of that knowledge across generations, and enough intellectual humility to revise beliefs that have stopped serving their purpose.

We are not very good at this yet. We are better than we were, in most measurable ways. The trend is real, even if it is neither inevitable nor smooth.

The task is to tend that trend — to understand what has driven it, to protect the conditions that allow it to continue, and to accelerate it where acceleration is possible without destroying the conditions for correction when we are wrong.

Which we will be. Regularly. The question is not whether we will make mistakes in building the world we want, but whether we have built into that world the capacity to notice and recover from them.

A world that can notice its mistakes and correct them is not a utopia frozen in perfection. It is something more valuable: a world that is genuinely, reliably getting better — that is, in the old phrase, on the right side of history not because history has a side, but because enough of the people living in it have chosen, repeatedly and at cost, to make it so.

That is what we are building toward.

The work is not finished. It is barely begun.

Begin.
