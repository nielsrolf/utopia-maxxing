# The Shape of the Possible: A Vision of the Best World

There is a door that humanity has been approaching for a very long time. Not a metaphorical door—or rather, not *only* a metaphorical one. It is a real threshold, made of real physics, real biology, real choices made by real people who ate breakfast this morning and worried about their rent. On one side of that door is everything we have ever known. On the other side is something we cannot fully see. But we can see enough to know that it is there, that it is reachable, and that reaching it is among the most important things we could possibly do.

This essay is an attempt to describe what might lie beyond that door—not with false precision, and not with the kind of vague, oceanic reassurance that sounds profound but says nothing. Something harder than either of those things: an honest attempt to think clearly about what the best possible future might actually be like, and why it matters that we try to imagine it.

---

## The Poverty of Conventional Utopias

Most attempts to describe a perfect world fail in one of two directions.

The first failure is smallness. Literary Utopias tend to be our current world, tidied up. People hold property in common, or live in more equitable political arrangements, or have slightly more liberated customs. The background aesthetic changes—more pastoral, or more technological, or more punk. But the humans are essentially the same humans, with the same cognitive architecture, the same sensory bandwidth, the same Dunbar-limited social circles, the same roughly 80-watt brains. These visions are fine as political proposals. They are completely inadequate as descriptions of what is actually possible.

Consider the analogy: imagine a group of great apes sitting together and trying to describe the best possible life. On the pro side of becoming human, they note: unlimited bananas. And yes, we do have unlimited bananas now. But the banana enthusiasts have entirely missed what they were getting themselves into. They had no framework for mathematics, or music, or the particular ache of reading a novel at three in the morning. They could not have imagined friendship as humans practice it, or grief, or scientific curiosity, or the specific pleasure of finally understanding something you had struggled with for years. The gap between their best imagined future and what actually became available was not merely quantitative. It was a difference in kind, in register, in the whole shape of what mattered.

We are those apes. Our most careful and detailed visions of Utopia are, almost certainly, banana-level thinking.

The second failure is emptiness. In reaction to the smallness of concrete Utopias, some thinkers reach for the sublime—for visions so vast and incomprehensible that they gesture past everything current humans could recognize. The problem is that incomprehensibility drains content. "Eternal union with the Good" is less parochial than "pizza forever," but it is also, in a certain way, less real. Abstract perfection gives us nothing to hold onto, nothing to steer toward, no way to distinguish between futures that are genuinely better and futures that are merely different.

What we need is something that holds both truths at once: specific enough to be real, and humble enough to acknowledge that reality will exceed our specifications.

---

## What We Actually Know

Let us start with what we can say with some confidence.

We know that the quality of human experience varies enormously, even within the narrow constraints of current biology. Small changes in neurochemistry—the presence or absence of certain molecules in certain concentrations—can mean the difference between a life of grinding misery and a life of warmth and engagement. People in the grip of depression and people in states of creative flow are, in some important sense, experiencing different worlds. The sky is not merely the same sky seen from different moods; it is a different sky. This is not metaphor. The sky literally looks different when your visual cortex is differently calibrated, when your attentional systems are differently tuned, when the default mode network is doing its particular work.

This tells us something important: the range of possible human experience, even within current biological limits, is far larger than the typical human day suggests. Most of us spend most of our lives in a relatively narrow band of that range, not because that is the best available band, but because of circumstance, habit, poverty, illness, and the grinding friction of a world that was not designed for flourishing.

We also know, from the experiences that people consistently describe as the high points of their lives—moments of love, creative breakthrough, physical exhilaration, genuine connection, deep understanding—that these moments are distinguished not just by their pleasantness but by a quality of aliveness, of being more fully present in reality. People who have such experiences often say they feel, not that they have arrived at some other world, but that they have finally, briefly, seen the world they have always been living in. The everyday world, lit from within.

These peak moments are evidence. They are proof of concept. They tell us that the human nervous system is capable of states considerably better than its average state, and they suggest—though cannot prove—that the trajectory does not end there.

## The Dimensions of a Good Life

To build a better world, we need to understand what "better" actually means. This is harder than it sounds, because our values are genuinely complex, and simplifying them is a reliable way to build something terrible.

A world that maximizes pleasure without challenge would be a world of lotus-eaters, comfortable and slowly diminishing. A world that maximizes challenge without care for subjective experience would be a world of noble suffering—admirable in its way, perhaps, but not obviously what anyone would choose. A world that maximizes novelty without depth would be a world of sensation without meaning. A world that maximizes meaning without novelty would calcify into something that looked like purpose but functioned like stasis.

Good lives seem to require something like all of the following, held in balance:

**Challenge that is appropriately scaled to capacity.** Not the elimination of difficulty, but its improvement—replacing low-quality struggle with high-quality struggle. A video game that is too easy is not relaxing; it is boring. A life made frictionless would have the same quality. We want games worth playing, not just games worth winning. We want the process, not only the outcome; the journey genuinely matters, not merely as a means to the destination.

**Genuine novelty and the continued possibility of discovery.** The fear that we will run out of interesting things to do is probably unfounded, for the simple reason that as minds become more capable, they become capable of appreciating more complex patterns of interestingness. A more intelligent version of you can find depth in things that would have seemed simple to your earlier self. The space of interesting things to understand and make and encounter is not fixed; it grows with the minds that explore it.

**Sensory and emotional richness.** Much of modern life is lived at low bandwidth. We stare at rectangles. We sit in chairs. The environments we inhabit are functional but rarely beautiful, stimulating in narrow ways while leaving most of our perceptual and emotional capacities idle. A genuinely good future would take seriously the full range of human sensation, and eventually the full range of possible sensation—which almost certainly includes modes of experience that current humans cannot access and cannot easily imagine.

**Authentic connection with other minds.** This is perhaps the most irreducible requirement. Our capacity for sympathy—for genuinely feeling something of what others feel—is not just a nice feature of human psychology. It is one of the most morally important things about us, and one of the primary sources of meaning in human life. Anything that replaces genuine connection with a simulacrum of connection—with relationships that are easy and pleasant but do not involve the genuine difficulty and genuine stakes of dealing with another real consciousness—would impoverish life in a way that no amount of other improvement could compensate for.

**The experience of living by one's own strength.** There is a specific satisfaction that comes from doing something yourself—from understanding how a thing works, from building rather than merely consuming, from solving rather than being solved-for. A future in which all problems are handled by incomprehensibly powerful systems, leaving humans as passengers in their own civilization, would fail along this dimension regardless of how comfortable and pleasant it was. The Narnia problem is real: when Aslan does everything, the children become hangers-on in their own story.

**Appropriate autonomy and predictability in the social environment.** People can only navigate their lives with intelligence and integrity if the rules of their world are knowable and stable enough to reason about. A world whose laws no one can understand, or whose powerful systems make unpredictable interventions in individual lives, is a world in which genuine self-determination is impossible even if it is nominally permitted.

**The capacity for surprise, and for hope.** A pleasant surprise is not merely pleasant news delivered unexpectedly. It carries with it a quality of world-being-larger-than-expected, of reality opening outward. A good life requires, not just the occurrence of good things, but the ongoing sense that good things remain possible—that the future is not fully legible, that there is more to come. Vague but justified hope may matter as much as specific expectation.

**Purpose that points outward.** The research on well-being consistently finds that people who feel they are contributing to something beyond themselves are happier and more resilient than people focused primarily on their own satisfaction. This is not merely a psychological quirk to be engineered away. It reflects something genuine about the structure of value: that caring about things outside yourself is intrinsically part of a good life, not merely instrumentally useful for feeling good. Even in a world where no one is starving or dying or suffering from curable diseases, there will be things worth caring about and working toward—truth, beauty, the flourishing of other minds, the exploration of what remains unknown.

---

## The Sublime, Without the Emptiness

Now for the part that is hardest to say clearly.

Everything described above is still, in some sense, banana-level thinking. The dimensions of a good life I have sketched are recognizably human dimensions—challenge, novelty, connection, purpose. They are things that current humans, with current psychology, can recognize as valuable. And that is both the strength and the limitation of this analysis.

The actual space of possible minds is almost certainly much larger than the space of current human minds. We know this the way we know that the ocean is larger than the puddle: not by having explored it, but by understanding what kind of thing it is. The human brain runs on roughly 20 watts. Its signals travel at most a few hundred meters per second. It contains around 100 billion neurons. These are not physical limits. They are the parameters of one particular solution to the problem of being a thinking thing, evolved by one particular lineage on one particular planet. There is no deep reason to think that the best possible minds would look anything like current human minds, any more than the best possible vehicle would look like a horse.

And yet. I am reluctant to release the thread of what is human, even as I try to point past it. Not because I think human psychology is sacred, or that change is dangerous, or that we should flinch from what we might become. But because I think that whatever the best possible minds turn out to be, they will not be best by being alien. They will be best by being more fully themselves in the direction we are already traveling—more conscious, more capable of appreciation, more able to love and understand and create.

The ember and the bonfire are made of the same fire.

Consider what happens in the moments that most show you what life can be: moments of real love, or genuine understanding, or beauty that hits you like a fact about the world. These moments have a quality of greater aliveness—of being more present, more awake, more here. The world seems not transformed but revealed. You feel: oh. This is what this is. This was always what this was.

What I am suggesting is that those moments are not ceiling. They are floor. They are evidence of a direction, and the direction continues. The minds that will inhabit a genuinely good future will be able to live in that state—or in states that bear the same relationship to that state that that state bears to the ordinary gray Tuesday morning. They will not just have peak experiences. They will have peak capacities, peak perceptions, peak depths of relationship and understanding, and the ability to build on all of these rather than losing them as the moments fade.

We do not know what this is like. We cannot know, any more than the apes could know what mathematics would feel like. But we can know that it is real, that it is in the direction of things we already value, and that it is worth fighting for.

---

## What This Means for Now

I want to be clear about what I am not saying.

I am not saying that the right response to this vision is to abandon present problems in favor of future ones. The people suffering today are real and the problems are urgent and the fact that a much better future is possible does not diminish the importance of working on what is in front of us. If anything, the vision of what is possible gives those present efforts their full meaning—because what is at stake is not merely the alleviation of current misery but the protection and cultivation of a trajectory that could lead somewhere extraordinary.

I am not saying we should have a plan for Utopia. The history of people with plans for Utopia is not encouraging. The task for any generation is not to design the perfect world but to make it possible for later, wiser, more capable people to continue the project without catastrophe having foreclosed their options.

And I am not saying that the future will necessarily be good. It might not be. Many things could go wrong, and some of them could go wrong irreversibly. This is precisely why it matters to think clearly about what the good version looks like: not to have a blueprint, but to have a sense of what we are trying not to lose, what we are trying to protect the possibility of.

What I am saying is this: the best possible future is not a slightly improved version of today. It is not a world where the problems are solved and everything else is more or less the same. It is a world—or a civilization, or a state of being—that would be as different from our current experience as our experience is from the great apes', but different in the direction of what we most care about when we are most awake. More conscious. More alive to beauty and to each other. More capable of genuine challenge and genuine rest, genuine discovery and genuine connection. More free, and more deeply engaged with what freedom makes possible.

That world exists. It is not guaranteed. It is not close. But it is made of real physics, in a real universe, inhabited by beings with values that already point in its direction. The door is there.

Learning to see it clearly—neither too small, nor so vast it vanishes into abstraction—is one of the preconditions for walking through it.
