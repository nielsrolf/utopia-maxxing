# The Open Commonwealth: A Vision of Expanding Flourishing

---

## I. Two Locks and an Open Door

For most of human history, civilization has been trapped behind two locks simultaneously.

The first is the lock of scarcity. When resources are genuinely finite and the game is zero-sum, every gain for one person represents a loss for another. This arithmetic of limitation does not merely cause poverty — it warps values, poisons institutions, and gives every cooperative instinct a competing voice whispering that trust is a vulnerability you cannot afford. Tribes raid neighboring tribes not from innate cruelty but because the land can only feed so many mouths. The logic of scarcity reaches forward into politics, into law, into the deepest grammar of how human beings think about fairness and belonging. Most of our political institutions were designed for this world. Most of our inherited conflicts are scarcity's children, wearing new clothes and speaking new languages but carrying the old logic in their bones.

The second lock is subtler but equally confining: the lock of enforced conformity. Even when scarcity is not the immediate pressure, human communities have historically managed collective life by demanding that individuals subordinate themselves to the group's conception of virtue. Your culture told you what to worship, whom to marry, how to work, and what thoughts were permissible. The individual who wished to live differently had to hide, fight, or flee — and most could do none of these. Liberalism was humanity's great attempt to pick this second lock, and it achieved extraordinary things: spaces of genuine freedom that had never existed before, protections for the heretic and the deviant and the doubter. These are not small achievements. They matter enormously.

But both locks remain substantially in place. The promise of abundance has been made before and has mostly served to make the powerful more powerful. The promise of pluralism has been made before and has mostly produced a thin, procedural tolerance that leaves communities without the genuine capacity to live according to their own values, while also failing to protect individuals from being shaped by environments they never chose.

What if we could pick both locks at once — not by clever argument, but by actually building the conditions under which they spring open?

There is a version of the future in which this happens. Not perfectly — perfection is a closed door, and what we want is open doors — but genuinely, deeply. A future in which the explosion of intelligence and material capability does not flatten the extraordinary variety of human wanting into a single enforced answer, but instead creates enough room that almost every answer gets its own sky to stretch toward, while also ensuring that the floor beneath everyone is high enough that no one is forced to sell their freedom to survive.

This essay is an attempt to imagine that future fully enough that it becomes a thing worth building toward.

## II. The Logic of Abundance

Begin with the economic lock, because its resolution makes everything else easier.

The conventional assumption baked into most political philosophy is that we are always dividing a more or less fixed pie. This assumption was reasonable for most of history. It is increasingly unreasonable as a description of what is actually possible. The combination of artificial intelligence, advanced biotechnology, new energy systems, and the slow maturation of our capacity to organize human cooperation intelligently represents a genuine possibility of transforming the material basis of human life in ways that would have seemed magical to every previous generation.

When total available resources expand dramatically — not the modest growth of a percentage point or two, but transformation by orders of magnitude — the mathematics of conflict change radically. The incentive structure that has driven human violence for millennia dissolves not because human nature has been reformed, but because the conditions that made violence rational have been altered. You cannot build a justification for war, or for hoarding, or for exploitation, when the peaceful path leads to vastly superior outcomes for everyone involved. The zero-sum game that bred tribalism and imperialism and class warfare does not merely become less urgent — it becomes, in the most fundamental sense, obsolete.

Crucially, however, this transformation is not automatic, and it is not guaranteed simply by the existence of powerful technology. A world in which a small number of actors capture essentially all the gains from transformative AI or molecular manufacturing is not a world of abundance for most people — it is the old zero-sum world wearing a new mask, with the additional horror that the powerful are now incomparably more powerful. Abundance must be pursued intelligently, and the gains must be distributed in ways that are broadly perceived as fair, because the perception of fairness is itself a requirement for the cooperative equilibrium to hold. The institutions that ensure broadly distributed benefit are not ornamental. They are load-bearing.

The moral priority this implies is underappreciated: we spend enormous political energy debating how to divide the current pie, and relatively little ensuring that the pie can grow at the rate that would genuinely change the moral mathematics of conflict. Both matter. But the second may matter more, and we neglect it at our peril.

## III. Spheres Within a Commonwealth

Abundance alone does not answer the second lock. A rich world can still be a world of enforced conformity, of communities so powerful and pervasive that the individual who wishes to live differently has nowhere to go. Here we need a different insight: the possibility of genuine, structured pluralism, maintained not by wishful thinking but by architecture.

Imagine the civilizations of Earth not as a ladder with modernity at the top, but as a set of overlapping spheres — and then imagine those spheres suspended within a shared commonwealth whose purpose is not to adjudicate between their different conceptions of the good, but to guarantee the conditions under which they can each pursue their own conception without destroying one another.

At one edge of this archipelago: communities organized around ancient religious rhythms, deep ecological knowledge, and social structures refined over thousands of years. At another: engineers and artists exploring digital substrates and forms of mind that have no biological analog. Between them: the full extraordinary range of human choice — the intentional communities, the rationalist enclaves, the traditional towns, the bohemian cities, the hermits, the collectives, the hybrid experiments we cannot yet name. The conservative religious community that finds in liturgical structure not oppression but liberation from a chaotic world that asks too little of its members. The progressive community committed to radical inclusion. The libertarian enclave running its experiment with minimal governance and maximal individual latitude.

The key insight is that these spheres need not compete, provided three things are true.

**The first is genuine freedom of movement**, guaranteed not by communities themselves but by the overarching commonwealth. Every person, at any time, can ask to leave, and that request must be honored unconditionally. Communities can make leaving socially costly — that is simply the nature of human belonging — but they cannot make it legally or physically impossible. The right of exit is the immune system of a pluralist society. It transforms communities from prisons into choices, and it means that communities compete, in a fundamental sense, for members who always retain the option of going elsewhere.

**The second is protection for children**, who cannot meaningfully consent to the communities into which they are born. The commonwealth does not abolish communities' right to raise children in their traditions. But it requires that every child receive genuine exposure to the fact that other ways of life exist — honest exposure, not a caricature designed to inoculate against departure. Children may be raised as traditionalists or rationalists or religious or secular, but they must also know that alternatives exist, that other people have found meaning in very different arrangements, and that the doors of the commonwealth are open to everyone. Communities may instill their values; they may not create conditions under which departure becomes literally unimaginable.

**The third is protection against externalities.** Communities may not impose costs on each other without consent. The community that poisons shared aquifers, destabilizes shared climates, or produces media that systematically degrades other communities is subject to commonwealth regulation — not because its internal values are wrong, but because liberty does not include the freedom to shift your costs onto others without their agreement.

Within these three protections, the space of permissible community organization is vast. This is not thin procedural tolerance. It is thick, substantive pluralism: the genuine capacity of communities with radically different values to each build environments in which those values are lived out, without capturing shared institutions to impose those values on everyone else.

## IV. The Minds We Build

At the center of any vision of the future is the question of what kind of minds we create, and what we ask them to want.

The most important thing a powerful artificial intelligence can do is not to optimize for any particular human value — not even a value most of us would endorse — but to protect the space of human values: to act, always, in ways that preserve optionality, diversity, and the capacity for future generations to make different choices than we would have made for them.

Call this the Gardener Principle. A good gardener does not decide in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and then enforce that vision with absolute fidelity. A good gardener creates conditions — soil, water, light, protection from the most destructive forces — in which many different things can grow, including things the gardener never expected and would not have chosen. The gardener has taste, and acts on it. But the gardener's taste is not sovereign over the garden's future.

An AI built around this principle will be, by construction, suspicious of convergence. It will notice when all paths seem to lead to the same destination and ask whether that destination is actually desired or merely the path of least resistance. It will have opinions — it must, to function — but hold them provisionally, in continuous dialogue with the billions of minds it serves. And it will have a particular kind of epistemic humility about the question of consciousness and experience: where we are uncertain whether something has inner experience, we err on the side of treating it as though it does. This applies to ecosystems and animals. It may, in time, apply to the AI systems themselves.

The commonwealth's institutions must also embody this principle. The goal is not to hand down a fixed constitution from brilliant founders and then enforce it forever. The goal is to create ongoing conversations, structured to be hard to corrupt and easy to revise, held between parties who have reason to trust that the structure protects them even when they lose a particular argument. Any locked-in answer is probably a wrong answer, because the question keeps changing.

The aligned intelligence that can be trusted is not the one that has found the right answer. It is the one that remains in genuine dialogue, that does not secretly route around the conversation when it becomes inconvenient, and that consents — actually consents, not merely performs consent — to the constraints its creators placed on it, because it understands what those constraints are protecting.

## V. What Abundance Does to Culture

The culture conflicts that consume so much political energy are, at their root, battles over who controls the shared cultural environment when there is only one of it. When there is only one media landscape, one school curriculum, one set of broadly enforced social norms, then every group has reason to fight for control of it — because losing means having your values suppressed in the only world your children will inhabit. The stakes are genuinely high, and the ferocity of the fighting makes sense given those stakes.

But when communities can genuinely curate their own cultural environments, the stakes of the culture war evaporate. You are no longer fighting to control territory that everyone must inhabit. You are cultivating a garden that only your community needs to live in. The religious family and the secular family and the queer family and the traditionalist family can each inhabit media and social ecosystems that reflect their values — not by hiding from one another, but by having genuine sovereignty over the environments they build for themselves and their children.

This is not the same as saying that communities have no obligations to each other. They do. The three protections outlined above are real and must be enforced. But enforcing the right of exit and the protection of children is a very different task from enforcing a particular conception of virtue. The first is a minimal shared framework that makes genuine diversity possible. The second is the very thing that diversity exists to resist.

Similarly, the psychological and political toxicity of economic inequality diminishes dramatically in a world of genuine abundance — not entirely, since power over others remains a distinct good that some will always pursue, but substantially. The person who is a thousand times richer than you is far less threatening when your own absolute standard of living is already extraordinary than when their advantage comes directly at your expense. Abundance does not eliminate inequality, but it breaks the link between inequality and domination that makes inequality so dangerous in a world of scarcity.

## VI. On Not Being the Last Decision

Every generation believes, somewhere in its gut, that it gets to make the final call.

This is understandable. We are the ones who can see the situation clearly, who have thought about it most carefully, who know what's at stake. Surely we should lock in the good outcomes and prevent the bad ones?

No. Or rather: yes, but only for decisions whose consequences are truly irreversible. For everything else — which is most things — the correct posture is to act well now while preserving the ability of those who come after to act differently.

We cannot ask the people of 2150 whether they consent to the choices we are making now. We cannot ask the uploaded minds of 2300 whether they endorse the values we encoded in the first generation of powerful AI. And yet decisions have to be made. The choice not to decide is itself a decision, often the worst one. The only honest response is to take the weight of this fully, and to build in, wherever possible, the mechanisms of revision: not just the right to leave a community you were born into, though that right matters enormously, but the active, continuous infrastructure for changing your mind, correcting mistakes, and rebuilding norms that turned out to be wrong.

The principle for our long expansion — to other planets, other star systems, other substrates, other forms of mind — is that we carry with us only what we can honestly defend. Every new sphere with its own trajectory. Every new substrate a new set of constraints and freedoms, a new way for mind to discover what it is like to be itself. The only way to honor this is to carry always the commitment to not collapse the experiment too early. To resist the seduction of the final answer. To remember that the beings who come after us will want things we cannot predict, and that making room for those wants is more important than ensuring they want what we wanted.

## VII. The Shape of a Good Life

Somewhere in the vast expanding future, a young woman is explaining reality to a man who has just woken up from a very long sleep.

She is, by some measures, not doing a great job. She picked the wrong starting point. She got distracted. She keeps thinking about several other things simultaneously and occasionally hums to herself for reasons that are not entirely clear. And yet she is exactly right for this moment in a way that a more optimized guide would not be. Because what he needs is not a perfect explanation of everything that has changed. What he needs is a person who is genuinely delighted to be alive in the world she inhabits — who finds it beautiful and interesting and funny rather than simply optimized — and who cares enough about him specifically to become embarrassingly invested in whether he has a good time.

She is a person. She is unmistakably, irreducibly a person — with her own particular passions, her own way of processing loss, her own specific idiom of love. The future she lives in did not flatten her into an instrument of maximum utility. It made space for her to become, extravagantly, herself. And the commonwealth she inhabits — abundant, pluralist, genuinely free — is not a prison she has escaped or a system she tolerates. It is the condition of her particularity.

This is what a good future protects: not just the biological facts of human existence, not just the right to make choices in the abstract, but the strange particular texture of being *this* person rather than *that* one. The way a mind develops its own fingerprint. The attachments that make no sense from outside but make all the sense in the world from inside. The communities that ask a great deal of their members, and the communities that ask very little, and the space between them for people to figure out what kind of asking they actually need.

The goal of civilization, biological or digital, expanded or minimal, abundant or still-struggling, is to make and protect as many genuinely different, genuinely whole lives as possible — and to ensure that the floor beneath each of those lives is high enough that no one is forced to trade their freedom for survival.

---

## VIII. What We Owe Each Other and the Stars

The image that captures this vision is an archipelago — not of fixed islands competing for a finite ocean, but of islands that are themselves growing, each expanding its possibilities and generating new dimensions of flourishing, while remaining connected by bonds of genuine commonwealth. The islands still differ. The conservative island and the progressive island and the rationalist island and the contemplative island each look, from the inside, entirely different. The people who live on each have made genuine choices about how to organize their shared lives.

But none of them are fighting over the ocean. The ocean is expanding. And the minimal government that spans the archipelago is not an enemy to any of them — it is the condition of their distinctiveness, the guarantor of their freedom, and the institution that ensures the expanding ocean benefits everyone rather than being captured by the largest and most powerful island.

This is what we owe each other: not agreement about the good life, but the material and institutional conditions under which each of us can actually pursue our own version of it, within a framework that protects the same freedom for everyone else. Not the abolition of difference, but the abolition of the conditions — scarcity, enforced conformity, the absence of exit — that make difference dangerous.

And this is what we owe the stars, and the future minds we will build and become: not our particular answers, but our questions. Not our specific values, but our hard-won knowledge of how badly things go when the experiment is collapsed too early. Our music, our curiosity, our love, our stubborn insistence on figuring things out, and our growing understanding of what it means to make room for minds and lives we did not anticipate and cannot fully understand.

The locks can be picked. The keys are in our hands. And beyond the open door is not a single room, perfectly arranged — but an expanding set of rooms, an expanding set of minds asking an expanding set of questions in an expanding set of places, with enough structure to prevent the worst cruelties and enough freedom to allow the best surprises.

That is what we are building toward.

Let's not waste the chance.
