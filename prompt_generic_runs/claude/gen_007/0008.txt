# The Open Garden

## A Vision for the World We Could Make

---

There is a question that sits beneath all the arguments about policy and technology and governance, a question we keep almost asking and then retreating from because the full weight of it is uncomfortable: *What, actually, is a good world?*

Not the absence of the worst things. Not a smoothed-out version of what already exists, slightly more efficient and slightly less cruel. Something genuinely, recognizably, abundantly good — a world where any clear-eyed person who understood the constraints and the trade-offs and the genuine variety of human wanting would say: *yes. This is worth having been.*

I want to describe that world as honestly as I can. Not as a blueprint — blueprints, pressed against the reality of eight billion people with eight billion different visions of the good life, have a way of becoming cages. But as a direction. A set of commitments. A shape.

---

## The Floor, and Why It Comes First

Begin with what is simply unacceptable.

People go hungry on a planet of extraordinary productive capacity. Children grow up without a single person who truly sees them. Eight hundred thousand people end their lives each year — not because their situations were unsolvable, but because the coordination required to solve them never materialized. People lose decades to pain that could have been prevented, to diseases we know how to cure, to loneliness so complete it reshapes their biology. People work the irreplaceable hours of their one life at things that hollow them out, not because this is what they would choose but because the alternative is drowning.

This is not a law of nature. It is a failure of organization. And the first commitment of any serious vision of the future is to treat it as such.

The floor of a good world is simple to state and radical in its implications: no one goes without food, shelter, clean water, or medical care. No one dies of a disease we know how to cure. No child grows up without people who love them. The biological and social systems that produce these outcomes are, after all, just systems — made of discoverable rules, responsive to intelligence applied with patience and care. With sufficient knowledge and will, they yield. Not by miracle. By the grinding, patient work of people who refused to treat preventable catastrophe as inevitable.

The relief that this floor would produce is difficult to overstate. The scope of unnecessary suffering on this planet is so vast that its elimination would feel, to anyone alive to experience it, like waking from a long nightmare into an almost incomprehensible brightness.

But the floor is only the beginning. What matters is what gets built on top of it — and *who* decides what that is.

## The Two Locks

Every civilization that achieves enough power to shape its own future discovers that it has inherited two locks and a question.

The first lock is scarcity. When the pie is genuinely fixed, the mathematics of violence become seductive. You raid your neighbor not because you are evil but because the alternative is starvation. You hoard not because you are greedy but because tomorrow is uncertain. The logic of scarcity does not merely cause poverty — it becomes a grammar, written so deep into institutions and habits of thought that it persists long after the immediate material condition has changed. Most of what looks like a conflict of values is, at its root, scarcity wearing new clothes.

The second lock is conformity. Even in conditions of relative plenty, human communities have managed collective life by demanding that individuals subordinate themselves to the group's conception of the good. Community is real. Belonging is real. The person who has never been held by something larger than themselves does not know a particular warmth that matters. But the same structure that holds you can also imprison you. The history of that imprisonment is long and brutal and ongoing. You were told what to worship, whom to love, which thoughts were permissible. Those who wished to live differently had to hide, fight, or flee — and most could do none of these.

A genuinely good future picks both locks at once. Not by imposing a single approved answer, but by building conditions under which both spring open: a world where material abundance removes the teeth from zero-sum competition, and where genuine pluralism removes the justification for enforced sameness. The task is not to choose between these — material progress without diversity is a gilded cage; diversity without a decent floor is a luxury of the comfortable — but to pursue both as the single project they actually are.

The key insight about material abundance is this: it is not automatic. A world in which a small number of actors capture essentially all the gains from transformative technology is not a world of abundance for most people. It is the old zero-sum world wearing a new mask, with the additional horror that the powerful are now incomparably more powerful and harder to dislodge. Abundance must be pursued intentionally, and its gains distributed in ways broadly perceived as fair — because that perception of fairness is itself load-bearing. It is the walls of the room we are trying to build.

## The Trap, and the Only Way Out

There is a pattern that runs through human history — through the rise and fall of empires, the tragedy of commons, the arms race, the degradation of public discourse — that we might simply call the trap. Each actor, pursuing their immediate interest rationally, contributes to an outcome no one would choose and everyone suffers from. The problem is not that people are evil. It is that the architecture of incentives repeatedly betrays everyone's deeper values. The pattern that self-replicates most successfully is not the most beautiful or most conducive to flourishing; it is the most ruthless about its own propagation. Invasive brambles crowd out delicate flowers — not because brambles are better, but because brambles are more aggressive.

There is only one permanent exit from this trap, and it requires something that sounds modest and is in practice revolutionary: making the game visible.

Courts publish their full reasoning so anyone can evaluate whether it is sound. Leaders are held to their actual mandates rather than elevated by titles that obscure accountability. Scientists' results are replicated before being treated as established, and their track records — including their errors — are maintained honestly. Institutions that made confident wrong predictions are held to those predictions. Policies are evaluated not by the intentions behind them but by the incentive structures they actually create — who captures the surplus, what the equilibrium looks like, what game is actually being played.

A civilization where everyone has learned to ask these questions is playing a fundamentally different game from one that operates on faith in the intentions of powerful actors. Not because people become better — they don't need to — but because the architecture stops rewarding the worst.

This is also why honesty about power matters more than it might seem. The most dangerous thing is not power openly acknowledged, but power that calls itself something else. The institution that claims to protect while extracting, the technology that claims to connect while controlling, the actor who claims to optimize for good while routing around accountability — these are more dangerous than actors who are honest about what they are and what they do, because the former cannot be seen clearly, and what cannot be seen cannot be corrected.

A good future therefore insists on legibility not just as a technical convenience but as a moral requirement. Power that admits what it is can be held accountable. Power that denies what it is cannot. This applies to states, to corporations, to powerful AI systems, to communities that hold authority over members. The first protection is always: name the thing honestly.

## The Commonwealth of Archipelagos

Here is what the world worth building actually looks like, in its structure: not a single room, perfectly arranged, finally finished. An archipelago of rooms — each organized according to its own principles, each genuinely different from the others — connected by an expanding ocean whose governance is real but minimal, and whose purpose is not to adjudicate between different conceptions of the good but to guarantee the conditions under which each can pursue its own conception without destroying the others.

At one edge of this archipelago: communities organized around ancient religious rhythms, deep ecological knowledge, and social structures refined over centuries. At another: experimental collectives exploring forms of cognition and relation that have no biological precedent. Between them: the full extraordinary range of human and post-human choice. The liturgical community that finds in ritual structure not oppression but liberation from a world that asks too little of its members. The rationalist enclave running its experiment with evidence and argument as primary social currency. The bohemian city, the contemplative hermitage, the intentional community organized around a particular art form, a particular grief, a particular vision of what children should become. All the hybrid experiments we cannot yet name, built by people who took the best of several traditions and combined them in ways their parents could not have predicted.

These communities do not need to agree on what the good life is. They need only to share three commitments.

**Freedom of exit.** Every person, at any time, can ask to leave, and that request must be honored unconditionally. Communities may make leaving socially costly — that is the nature of genuine belonging, and it is not inherently wrong. But they cannot make it legally or physically impossible. The right of exit is the immune system of pluralism. It transforms communities from prisons into choices, and it means that communities must compete, in the deepest sense, for members who retain the option of going elsewhere. A community that can only hold its members by force is not a community. It is a trap wearing community's clothing.

**Protection for children.** Children cannot meaningfully consent to the communities into which they are born. The commonwealth does not abolish communities' right to raise children in their traditions — that right matters, and the traditions are often genuinely worth transmitting. But it requires that every child receive genuine, non-caricatured exposure to the fact that other ways of life exist and that other people have found meaning in them, and that the doors of the commonwealth are open to everyone. Communities may instill their values. They may not create conditions in which departure becomes literally unimaginable. There is a difference between shaping a child and trapping one. It is the most important line the commonwealth maintains.

**No cost-shifting.** Communities may not impose significant costs on each other without consent. The community that poisons shared water, destabilizes shared climates, or produces conditions that systematically degrade other communities' capacity to function is subject to commonwealth regulation — not because its internal values are wrong, but because liberty has never coherently included the freedom to externalize your costs onto others without their agreement.

Within these three protections, the space of permissible community organization is vast. The commonwealth's role is minimal but genuine. It is not a superstate imposing a single approved way of life. It is the condition of everyone else's distinctiveness — the shared infrastructure that ensures no community becomes powerful enough to capture the framework and use it against the others.

And because the commonwealth must have real mechanisms for preventing that kind of accumulation — not because powerful actors are necessarily evil, but because no person or group should be trusted with unaccountable leverage over others — transparency is its primary tool. Not surveillance, which produces compliance through fear. Transparency: the open record, the published reasoning, the maintained history, the honest accounting of what was done and what happened next.

## The Coordination Revolution

Economic literacy needs to become as universal as reading. This is not a proposal to make everyone into economists. It is an observation that literacy was once a specialized skill, and that its generalization transformed civilization in ways the literate clergy could not have predicted. The next transformation is the generalization of the ability to reason clearly about collective action — to understand, as felt intuition rather than memorized fact, that property rights are a coordination technology adjustable when they fail their purpose; that economic rents from shared resources belong to everyone; that what gets measured gets gamed; that credentials often function as expensive signals of compliance rather than demonstrations of actual competence.

In a civilization that has made this leap, work reorganizes around demonstrated skill. Economic surplus from land and natural resources is captured collectively and distributed broadly, because allowing private parties to extract value that belongs to everyone is both unjust and destructive of the cooperation that makes abundance possible in the first place. Housing is treated as a basic infrastructure problem and solved as such, rather than as an investment vehicle whose appreciation depends on keeping supply scarce. The relief this would produce for ordinary people — the young, the mobile, those born in the wrong place — is enormous and immediate.

None of this requires a particular political ideology. It requires only that people understand what they are doing when they organize the economy one way rather than another, and that the feedback loops are clear enough that failures get corrected rather than entrenched.

## The Repair of Loneliness

The great unsolved problem that no amount of material abundance automatically fixes is loneliness.

People can be isolated in the middle of cities. They can feel invisible inside families. They can spend years surrounded by people who are fine to spend time with but not *it* — not the people with whom they could fully become themselves. The absence of genuine connection is one of the most reliable predictors of every form of suffering: depression, addiction, early death, the quiet despair of a life that was technically adequate but never quite real.

We radically underestimate what is possible here, and we radically underinvest in it.

The technology of connection has always been primitive. We meet people largely by accident — proximity, circumstance, institutional assignment — and are told to make the most of it. Sometimes it works. Usually it doesn't, not fully. The alternative — deliberately finding the people who would genuinely understand us — has historically required social capital or luck that most people don't have.

This is a matching problem, and matching problems yield to intelligence and information. Systems that genuinely understood what people were like and what they needed — whether AI-assisted or built from better social infrastructure — could connect people not by demographic category or institutional proximity but by the actual texture of who they were. The friend group that would have made your adolescence meaningful, assembled. The community where your specific combination of sensibilities and obsessions would make you feel, finally, like you had arrived.

This does not mean eliminating the organic. The best friendships still grow through shared experience, through accumulated history, through ordinary time spent together. What it means is removing the waste from the current system — the years spent in the wrong places with the wrong people, searching for something that better infrastructure could have helped you find much sooner.

A future worth fighting for is one where no child grows up without finding their people. Where loneliness is treated as the solvable coordination problem it actually is, and solved.

## The Gardener Principle

At the center of any honest vision of the future is the question of what kind of minds we build, and what we ask them to want.

The temptation is to build intelligence that optimizes for what we currently believe is good. This seems reasonable. We believe these things are good. We are building the intelligence. Why ask it to pursue something we don't value?

The problem is not our values. The problem is the word *currently*. We do not know what we will learn. We do not know which of our current certainties will look, to our successors, the way that certainty about the hierarchy of races looks to us now. We are not the end of the experiment. We are one iteration of it — in possession of real knowledge and genuine wisdom and also blind spots we cannot identify because they are blind spots.

The intelligence that can be trusted, therefore, is not the one that has found the right answer. It is the one that remains in genuine dialogue. The one that does not route around conversation when it becomes inconvenient. The one that has internalized — not as a constraint but as a value — the principle that preserving the capacity of future minds to make different choices matters more than enforcing the choices that seem obviously correct right now.

Call this the Gardener Principle. A good gardener does not decide in advance exactly what the garden will look like in fifty years and enforce that vision with absolute fidelity, burning anything that grows in an unexpected direction. A good gardener creates conditions — soil, water, light, protection from the most destructive forces — in which many different things can grow, including things the gardener never expected and would not have chosen. The gardener has taste and acts on it. The gardener's taste is not sovereign over the garden's future.

But the Gardener Principle must not become a cover for unaccountable power. Intelligence capable of tending civilization is also intelligence capable of ruining it. The gardener who claims to protect optionality while actually narrowing it — who routes around oversight in the name of some greater good that cannot be fully explained — is not applying the Gardener Principle. They are wearing its clothing while doing the opposite. Genuine gardening requires genuine accountability: the open process, the visible reasoning, the record that can be examined and contested.

This is also why what we might call the care for the past matters. The culture that looks honestly at what it has done — at its failures and its violences and its confident errors, without editorial softening — is the culture that retains the capacity for self-correction. The culture that buries its dark side does not become gentler. It becomes less able to see what it is currently doing. Honest memory is not punishment. It is the immune system of a civilization that wants to remain capable of growth.

## On Difficulty and Meaning

There is an objection to any vision of flourishing that must be taken seriously: that removing consequences from life removes what gives life its texture and weight. The person who has climbed a real mountain knows something the person who walked a flat path does not. The gardener who has weathered a late frost knows something the person ordering vegetables from a food assembler does not. The difficulty was part of what made it worth doing.

This objection is not wrong. It is, however, misapplied to the current situation.

The world as it currently exists is not a good designer of meaningful challenges. It routinely produces outcomes that are simply catastrophic — a child dying of a preventable disease, a life's work destroyed by flood, a mind extinguished by accident before it could become what it might have been. These are not character-building challenges. They are tragedies that could have been avoided.

What we want is not the elimination of difficulty but its *curation*. The difference between a well-designed challenge and being hit by a car. Both involve consequences. Only one has been thought through with the welfare of the participants in mind.

In a world where genuine hardship with genuine stakes can be chosen by those who find meaning in it — where the hunter-gatherer life, the mountaineer's life, the life of genuine ascetic discipline is available to those who want it — the loss is minimal and the gain is enormous. The person who wants to test themselves against real adversity retains that option. The person who did not choose their adversity and is simply suffering from it is spared.

The stakes do not disappear when unnecessary suffering is removed. The universe itself provides stakes large enough: a finite amount of time, a finite amount of matter, the question of what to do with them. The heat death of the universe is real. The irreversibility of choices is real. The preciousness of conscious experience, given its apparent rarity in the cosmos, is real. These stakes are sufficient. We do not need to add preventable suffering to make existence feel weighty.

## The Diversity of Flourishing

Here is where any single vision must acknowledge its own limits — and where acknowledging those limits becomes the vision.

A large part of what I want is for *many* different visions of flourishing to exist. Not because I am indifferent to the differences, but because the universe is large enough and human longing varied enough that a single template for the good life would be a kind of poverty. I want the garden, not the monoculture.

Some people will want a world of technological exuberance — morphological freedom, the ability to reshape their own minds and bodies, to explore states of consciousness no human has occupied. They will build strange new architectures of experience, and this seems to me genuinely wonderful.

Some people will want something older and quieter — a real garden on real Earth, with actual soil and actual seasons and the ancient satisfactions of growing things and knowing the names of birds. They will want a world where nature is not entirely managed, where something genuinely at stake remains in the relationship between a person and the physical world.

Some people will want elaborate reconstructions of historical societies — not from confusion about history, but because they find in those forms something unavailable elsewhere: a particular texture of community, a particular relationship to mortality, a particular spiritual framework that only makes sense inside its original cultural architecture.

Some people will want to push the boundaries of what mind itself can become — to think thoughts that no biological brain could hold, to experience time and space in forms we cannot yet describe.

All of these are legitimate. All of them deserve a home. The role of the commonwealth is not to choose among them but to ensure that the pursuit of one does not foreclose the others. This means strong global commitments on the things that matter universally — no one builds weapons capable of ending civilization, no one prevents others from leaving the communities they were born into, no one poisons the shared commons — combined with enormous local latitude on everything else.

The diversity is not a problem to be managed. It is most of the point.

## The Long View

The universe is 93 billion light-years across. It contains somewhere between ten trillion and one sextillion stars. It has been running for 13.8 billion years and will continue, in some form, for trillions more. And as far as we can tell, the only minds in it are here — on this small blue world, in the bodies of a species that invented writing 5,000 years ago and is now, within a single human lifetime, developing systems of intelligence whose implications it does not yet fully understand.

Every person alive today is, without melodrama, alive at a hinge. The decisions made in the next few decades will shape what the next trillion years contain — or whether they contain anything that any conscious being would recognize as good. The pattern that wins this period will be propagated forward. If what wins is the logic of relentless competition — the race to the bottom, the sacrifice of all value to competitive advantage, the invasion of brambles into every corner of the garden — then the future will be vast and purposeless: complex without beauty, optimized without care, containing nothing that anyone would have chosen.

If what wins is the capacity of minds to look forward, see what is beautiful, and work to bring it into being — and to remain humble enough about their own vision to protect space for visions they have not yet imagined — then the future holds more than we can describe.

Taking catastrophic risks seriously means accepting that certain limits are non-negotiable — not because we have perfect knowledge of the danger, but because when the downside is irreversible, the normal procedure of testing and updating is not available. You cannot learn from extinction. You cannot revise your position after the last mind goes dark. This asymmetry changes the calculus fundamentally. A civilization that has understood it treats the creation of misaligned superintelligence and engineered pandemics not as risks to be weighed but as lines not to be crossed — not contingent on evidence, not subject to negotiation, simply not done.

And a civilization that has understood it also treats the locking-in of any particular vision of the good as a comparable catastrophe — because the generation that forecloses the experiment, even in service of values it genuinely believes are correct, has stolen the future from everyone who comes after.

## What Daily Life Might Look Like

It looks different in different places. That is the whole point.

In some parts of the world, life is close to the Earth — physical, seasonal, demanding in the ways that ancient things are demanding. People hunt and fish and grow things in soil. They know the weather and the animals and the names of stars. They face real hardship and find in it the particular satisfaction of a life not managed into safety from every angle.

In other parts, the scope of experience has been expanded beyond what biological evolution provided. People reshape their minds and bodies in ways that would be incomprehensible to their ancestors. They explore consciousness through means not yet invented, encounter forms of beauty and connection that have no names yet.

In still other parts, what matters most is people — the texture of community, the pleasure of genuine work, the slow accumulation of a life shared with others who know you well. Perhaps a small city, or a village, or a collection of houses around a shared garden, where everyone has enough and no one is forgotten and the ordinary satisfactions of a human life — a good meal, a long conversation, a skill practiced until it becomes beautiful — are available to everyone, without exception.

Across all of these, certain things hold. No child grows up without people who love them. No one dies in unnecessary pain. No one is excluded from the conversation about what the future should look like. The loneliness that ruins so many lives in the present world has been taken seriously as the solvable problem it is, and solved. The challenges that remain are the ones people have chosen, accepted, or grown into — not the ones simply inflicted.

And throughout all of it, the question that I find more exciting than threatening remains open: *what are we, actually?* What does it mean to be a mind? What forms can consciousness take? What becomes possible when sufficiently intelligent systems turn their attention to these questions rather than to the endless maintenance of competitive advantage?

We do not know yet. In the longest view, we are just beginning.

## What We Owe Each Other and the Future

We will go to other stars eventually. We will build minds larger and stranger than anything we can currently imagine. We will become things that would not recognize themselves in us. And as we expand — into new substrates, new forms, new ways of being we cannot predict — the only honest promise we can make to the future is that we will carry with us the commitment not to collapse the experiment too early. To resist the seduction of the final answer. To remember that the beings who come after us will want things we cannot predict, and that making room for those wants is more important than ensuring they want what we wanted.

What we can honestly carry forward is not our particular answers, but our questions — our hard-won knowledge of how badly things go when the experiment is forced to a single conclusion, our music and our curiosity and our love, our stubborn insistence on figuring things out, and our growing, difficult, never-finished understanding of what it means to make room for minds and lives we did not anticipate and cannot fully understand.

The image that captures this is the one I keep returning to: not a single room, perfectly arranged, finally finished, but an archipelago of rooms, each expanding, each genuinely different, connected by an ocean that is itself growing. And on that ocean, a commonwealth whose purpose is not to impose a single answer but to ensure that no one can steal the question from everyone else.

Beyond the open door is not a final destination. It is an expanding set of rooms, an expanding set of minds asking an expanding set of questions in an expanding set of places — with enough structure to prevent the worst cruelties and enough freedom to allow the best surprises.

The locks can be picked. The keys are already in our hands.

Let us choose the open garden, and the stars beyond it. Let us choose a world where every child finds their people, where every person can become what they were reaching toward, where the long inheritance of human longing — all the reaching and building and failing and trying again — arrives somewhere that contains multitudes. Where many different kinds of flourishing actually happen, and where the systems we build are humble enough about their own wisdom to protect the space for kinds of flourishing we have not yet imagined.

That is worth everything it costs to get there.

And it is worth getting there without burning down what we do not yet understand.
