# THE COMMONWEALTH OF BECOMING

## A Vision of the Best Possible World

---

We did not arrive here by accident, nor by the decree of any single sovereign intelligence. We arrived the way all lasting things arrive: slowly, then suddenly, and always together. What follows is an account not of a destination but of a direction—a description of the world toward which the best of our natures has always been reaching, and which we have, at last, begun to truly build.

---

## I. The Architecture of Flourishing

The first thing a visitor notices about our civilization is not its technology, though the technology is staggering. It is not the absence of suffering, though suffering has been reduced to a fraction of what it once was. The first thing a visitor notices is how *unhurried* everyone seems.

This is not laziness. It is the opposite of laziness. It is what happens when a species finally has enough—enough time, enough security, enough medicine, enough food—that the frantic scramble to merely survive and compete gives way to the altogether different project of actually living. When the ancient, grinding anxiety of scarcity lifts from a person's shoulders, something happens to their face. They look up. They look around. They look at each other.

We did not achieve this overnight, and we did not achieve it by accident. We achieved it by treating the arrangement of human society as a problem worthy of the same rigorous, humble, experimental attention that we once reserved only for physics and chemistry. We built institutions for the study of human flourishing the way earlier generations built institutions for the study of nature—not assuming we knew the answers, but genuinely asking: *What helps people thrive? What poisons communities? What conditions allow trust to grow?* And then, crucially, we listened to what we learned.

The result is a civilization organized around a single animating conviction: that the proper end of all collective human effort is to create conditions in which every individual can become, as fully as possible, the best version of themselves—and in doing so, contribute to the becoming of everyone else.

## II. The Body and the Mind, Liberated

The first great liberation was medical.

We cured most of what killed us. Cancer, cardiovascular disease, the cascade of organ failures we once called aging—these have been reduced to manageable conditions, then to rare conditions, then to curiosities of history. The biological processes underlying senescence are now as modifiable as a garden. Rejuvenation is available not as the privilege of the wealthy but as the inheritance of every person born.

But we did not stop at the body.

Mental suffering—depression, anxiety, trauma, addiction, the particular loneliness of feeling profoundly broken—was, for most of human history, treated as something to be endured or managed, never solved. We now understand it as we understand infection: as a condition with causes, and therefore with cures. Not every inner darkness yields to the same light, and we have learned to treat the mind with the same complexity it deserves. But the worst extremes of psychological suffering—the states that made people feel that existence itself was an error—these we have largely ended.

We extended this compassion beyond our own species. The billions of sentient creatures who once suffered in factory farms and laboratories have been freed from those arrangements. We eat differently now—partly from synthesis, partly from plant, partly from cultured tissue—and the old economies of suffering that fed us have been dismantled. We are not yet certain where the boundaries of morally relevant consciousness lie, but we have adopted a working principle: *when in doubt, reduce suffering*. This has served us well.

Longevity brought complications we did not fully anticipate. A life that might extend across centuries demands a different relationship to time, to identity, to attachment. We have developed new philosophies of the self to match—ways of thinking about continuity and change that are more honest than the old ones, which were largely consolations invented by people who had no choice but to die. Living a very long time turns out to be something like a skill, and we have begun to teach it. The people who have lived five hundred years are not the same people who were born. They are better people, mostly—more patient, more genuinely curious, more capable of love uncontaminated by fear of loss.

The enhancement of cognition followed the healing of it. We augmented our minds not by replacing what was human in them but by extending what was best. Memory no longer fades into fog; experiences can be revisited with full fidelity. Attention, once so easily shattered, can be gathered and held. The chronic incoherence of the self—wanting one thing in the morning and the opposite by evening, making promises we cannot keep even to ourselves—has been addressed not by flattening the personality but by giving the reflective self more genuine authority over the impulsive one. People are, at last, more consistently *themselves*.

Intelligence has grown alongside these changes, but not in the way the old science fiction imagined—not cold, not alienating, not the ascent of a calculating machine at the expense of a warm human being. The expansion of our cognitive reach has made us *more* capable of wonder, not less. There is simply more to wonder at when you can see further.

## III. The Treasury of Knowledge

At the center of our civilization stands something very much like what the old visionaries imagined: a great institution devoted to the knowledge of causes and the enlarging of what is possible.

We call it, in different languages, different things. But its purpose is everywhere the same: to pursue understanding of the world with complete rigor and complete honesty, and to ensure that what is learned becomes available to all. The model is not that of a single house or academy, though such places exist and are beautiful. The model is a network—vast, distributed, self-correcting—in which every person is, in some sense, a member.

For we have finally answered a question that stumped the old reformers: how do you make a scientist of everyone? The answer turned out not to require that everyone become a specialist. It required only that everyone be taught to experience the *joy of discovery*—to understand that the universe hides patterns, and that finding them is among the most profound pleasures available to a conscious creature. Education is no longer the transmission of facts from the old to the young. It is an invitation into a practice: the practice of asking questions, testing them, revising what you thought you knew, and being genuinely delighted when the world surprises you. Children learn science the way they once learned games—through play, through competition, through the irresistible pull of problems they actually want to solve.

The scholars travel, in the spirit of the old Merchants of Light. They bring back not merely data but *ways of seeing*—the different epistemologies and aesthetic frameworks developed by communities who have approached the great questions from different angles. No single tradition holds the whole truth. We have learned this, finally, and we have learned to be grateful for it.

What have we discovered? More than I can say. The fundamental laws beneath the laws. The mechanisms of consciousness. The deep structure of living systems. The origins of meaning in biological brains. The mathematics of time. We have learned, above all, how much remains to be learned—and this is the finding we celebrate most, because it means the adventure does not end.

## IV. The Arts of Relationship

The material and cognitive achievements mean little if they are not embedded in lives of genuine connection. We know this now in a way that earlier ages knew only intermittently: that the richest human life is not lived alone.

Our civilization has turned its collective intelligence toward the problem of relationship with the same seriousness it once reserved for engineering and medicine. We discovered, unsurprisingly, that the obstacles to deep connection were not mysterious. They were fear of vulnerability. They were patterns of communication learned in childhood that served defense rather than intimacy. They were social arrangements that isolated people and then blamed them for feeling alone. They were economic pressures that left no time for the slow, attentive work of knowing another person.

We removed these obstacles. Not by decree—you cannot legislate warmth—but by changing the conditions that produced them. People have time for each other. They are taught, from childhood, the arts of listening and of honesty, of repair after conflict and of celebration in joy. The knowledge of how to build and maintain trust—once treated as a mysterious gift that some people simply had and others lacked—is now understood as a skill, and taught as one.

The results have been extraordinary. Loneliness, which was epidemic in the early centuries of the digital age, has been substantially eradicated. Not because people are forced into proximity, but because communities are designed—as carefully as we once designed bridges and aqueducts—to make belonging possible. There are places, physical and virtual, where people with different histories and temperaments can find each other. There are institutions that hold the space for long relationships to deepen over decades, then centuries.

The family has taken many forms, and all of them are honored. The great feast of kinship—those celebrations in which a person stands at the center of the web they have helped create, surrounded by those who descended from and were shaped by their love—remains among the most moving rituals of our civilization. To see five generations gathered, to see what love produces when given time, is to understand something about why any of this was worth doing.

The question of romantic love was harder than we expected. We were tempted, at various points, to simply engineer it—to optimize the chemistry, to match people by algorithm, to smooth away the roughness and uncertainty that made it painful. Some communities tried this. They found that the smoothing removed something essential. That love, to be love, must involve a kind of risk. A kind of choosing. A kind of being seen by someone who might turn away, and who does not.

What we did instead was remove the unnecessary suffering while preserving the necessary kind. We reduced jealousy born of insecurity. We reduced the cruelty that comes from unexamined wounds. We created cultures in which honesty was safe, in which vulnerability was not punished, in which people were not desperate enough to stay in arrangements that were wrong for them. And within those conditions, love—unpredictable, impractical, transformative love—flourished in ways that surprised even the optimists among us.

## V. The Shape of Justice

We debated, for generations, what it would mean for our civilization to be just. The debates were not academic. They mattered enormously, because the decisions made in those early centuries of possibility would shape everything that followed.

We arrived, eventually, at something like a consensus—not a complete one, and not without dissent, but a working understanding: that justice requires not uniformity but access. That a just society is not one in which all people are the same, but one in which all people have genuine entry into the goods that make a life worth living. Health. Security. Education. Membership in communities that see and value them. The ability to participate in the decisions that shape their lives.

We established, as a foundational commitment, that no one would be kept from these goods by accidents of birth. The place where you were born, the body you were born into, the parents fortune assigned you—none of these would determine whether you had access to the full inheritance of our civilization. This was not charity. It was recognition of a simple truth: that every person who flourishes is a gift to everyone else, and every person condemned to misery is a wound in the common life.

We also learned to be suspicious of hierarchies—not to abolish them entirely, because some stratification is the natural result of different choices and different levels of mastery, and is not in itself unjust—but to prevent them from calcifying, from becoming hereditary, from becoming the cage they had so often been in history. Status rotates. Recognition comes to everyone. No one is permanently at the bottom of every ranking, because the rankings themselves are plural and shifting and held lightly.

The end of war was not a single event but a long process. We reduced it gradually, then rapidly, as the conditions that produced it—scarcity, grievance, the logic of domination, the availability of weapons whose costs could be exported onto civilians—were one by one addressed. There are still conflicts among us. There will probably always be conflicts, because people genuinely disagree about things that matter to them. But we resolve those conflicts now through argument, negotiation, and the slow grinding work of institutional process—not through the organized slaughter of young people.

## VI. The Question of the Self

We did not solve the problem of identity. We are not sure it is the kind of problem that gets solved.

What we did was create conditions in which the question could be asked more honestly. When you are not desperate, you can afford to be uncertain about who you are. When you are not competing for survival, you can afford to change. When your life stretches across centuries, you discover that the self you were at forty is not the self you will be at four hundred—and this is not a loss to be mourned but a journey to be taken.

We have been careful about the power of self-modification. The ability to change your own mind—your preferences, your values, your personality—is genuinely dangerous. Not because change is bad, but because the person who modifies themselves is not the same person who will live with the result. We have developed norms around this: that certain changes require time, and reflection, and counsel; that some core of character should be altered only with great deliberation; that the technology which could make you into anyone must not become the technology that makes you into no one.

We have also been careful about the ways in which the boundaries of the self might expand. The advent of direct mind-to-mind communication, and the possibility of genuinely merged consciousness, raised questions we are still working through. What does it mean to be an individual in a world where the borders between minds are permeable? We do not have a final answer. What we have is a commitment to exploring the question honestly, without either panicking and slamming the door or rushing through it without looking back.

What we have found, in the exploration, is something that the mystics suspected long before the neuroscientists confirmed it: that the borders of the self were never as solid as we thought. That we were always more porous, more relational, more constituted by our connections than the myths of rugged individualism allowed us to admit. The new technologies have not created this permeability. They have made it visible.

---

## VII. The Horizon

We are not at the end of anything. We are perhaps at the beginning.

The universe is almost incomprehensibly large, and almost incomprehensibly old by the standards of everything we have yet done. There are galaxies we have not visited, questions we have not asked, forms of experience we cannot yet imagine. There are almost certainly other minds out there—minds that arose from different origins, that see the world through fundamentally different categories, that have answers to questions we do not know how to pose. The encounter with those minds, when it comes, will change us as surely as every other encounter with the genuinely other has changed us.

We are also not finished with ourselves. The people who will live ten thousand years from now will be, in some important sense, different beings from us—as different from us, perhaps, as we are from the people who first gathered at fires and told each other stories about the stars. We cannot fully imagine what they will value, what will make them laugh, what will move them to tears. We can only try to bequeath them a civilization that gives them the best possible start.

And that is what we are doing. Building something that will outlast us. Not out of vanity—though there is a little vanity in it, as there is in every human endeavor—but out of love. The same love that motivates a parent to sacrifice for a child they will never fully know as an adult. The same love that built the libraries and the gardens and the institutions of justice that we ourselves inherited from people who will never know our names.

We are not building utopia. We are not so naive as to believe in a final destination. What we are building is something more honest and more difficult: a civilization genuinely committed to the perpetual, joyful, humble work of becoming better—in which each generation inherits a world improved by those who came before, and shoulders the obligation to do the same for those who will come after.

The light does not stop. The horizon recedes as we walk toward it. And this, it turns out, is not a frustration.

It is the whole point.
