# The Civilization We Could Build

## A Vision of Flourishing

There is a question worth asking before any blueprint is drawn: *What are we actually trying to build?*

Not just the absence of catastrophe. Not merely survival past the dangerous decades of this century. Something genuinely, abundantly good—a world where the long slog of human history, all its suffering and waste and missed connection, was worth enduring to arrive here.

I want to describe that world. Not as a vague aspiration, but as concretely as I can see it.

---

## The Foundation: Everyone Is Actually Okay

The first thing to understand about a genuinely good future is that it does not look like a luxury upgrade of the present. It looks like a fundamental correction.

Today, eight billion people share a planet of extraordinary productive capacity, and yet: people go without adequate food. People lose their minds to dementia while their families watch, helpless. Children grow up without a single person who truly understands them. People work jobs they hate to pay for housing they can barely afford, while the surplus they generate pools in places that do nothing with it. Eight hundred thousand people kill themselves each year—not because their situations were unsolvable, but because the coordination to actually solve them never materialized.

This is not a law of nature. It is a failure of organization.

A genuinely good future corrects this at the root. Every person on earth has enough food, clean water, and shelter—not as charity, not as a policy program, but as the natural output of an economy that has stopped sabotaging itself. No one has dementia or cancer or any disease we have the knowledge to cure but lack the will to distribute. No one dies before they have lived what they would recognize as a full life, unless they have chosen this. The biological systems underlying human health are, after all, just systems—made of atoms, governed by discoverable rules—and with sufficient intelligence applied to them, they yield. They must yield. We are not fighting physics. We are fighting our own accumulated failure to coordinate.

The relief this would produce is difficult to overstate. The person working sixty hours a week and still falling behind—free. The family watching their grandmother disappear into fog—spared. The child who has never been in a room where anyone truly saw them—found. The scope of unnecessary suffering on this planet is so vast that its elimination would feel, to anyone alive to experience it, like waking from a nightmare into a morning of almost incomprehensible brightness.

That is the floor. What gets built on top of it is where things become genuinely exciting.

---

## The Coordination Revolution

Here is a puzzle: We are, right now, incomparably more productive than our great-grandparents were. We have technologies they could not have imagined. And yet we seem unable to solve problems they would have recognized instantly as problems—unable to build enough housing, unable to share medicines we already know how to make, unable to stop fighting wars, unable to agree on obvious facts.

The bottleneck is not knowledge or resources. The bottleneck is *coordination*.

What would it mean to actually fix this?

Imagine a civilization where economic literacy is as universal as reading—where every person, by the time they are an adult, understands what a Nash equilibrium is, what Goodhart's Law does to any metric you optimize too hard, how price signals work and where they fail, what the difference is between a public good and a private one. Not as dry academic knowledge, but as intuitions so deeply embedded they shape perception: you look at a policy proposal and you automatically ask *what are the incentives here*, *who captures the surplus*, *what does this look like in equilibrium*.

This is not a fantasy about making everyone a professional economist. It is an observation that *literacy* was once a specialized skill belonging to scribes and clergy, and that its generalization transformed civilization. The next transformation is the generalization of the capacity to reason clearly about collective action. Once most people can look at a coordination problem and name it—can say "this is a prisoner's dilemma" or "this is a tragedy of the commons" or "this is a race to the bottom"—the problems become vastly easier to solve. You cannot fix what you cannot see.

A civilization that has made this leap organizes itself differently at every level. Courts publish their full reasoning rather than issuing pronouncements from authority. Leaders are described in terms of their actual mandate and approval rather than given inflated titles that obscure accountability. Property rights are understood as a coordination technology—invented to serve human flourishing, adjustable when they fail that purpose—rather than natural laws handed down from on high. Economic rents from raw resources are captured collectively and distributed back, because the alternative is allowing private parties to extract value that belongs to everyone. Work is organized around actual demonstrated skill rather than credentials that serve as expensive signals of compliance.

The key insight is that a civilization that understands coordination problems and has practiced solving them does not *need* a Leviathan to keep order. People who understand what they are doing when they defect, and understand the consequences, and live in a community where defection is visible and accountable, mostly choose not to defect. Not because they are angels—they are not—but because the incentives have been aligned, and they can see that they have been aligned.

## The Repair of Social Life

The great unsolved problem that no amount of material abundance automatically fixes is loneliness.

People can be isolated in the middle of cities. They can feel invisible inside families. They can spend years surrounded by people who never quite understand them, who are fine to spend time with but not *it*, not the people with whom they could fully become themselves. This is not a trivial problem. The absence of genuine connection is one of the most reliable predictors of every kind of suffering—depression, addiction, early death. It is also, I think, one of the things people fear most about the future they imagine: not just their own death, but dying without having been truly known.

Here is where I think we radically underestimate what is possible.

The technology of connection has always been primitive. We meet people largely by accident—proximity, circumstance, institutional assignment. We are thrown together with classmates and coworkers and told to make the most of it. Sometimes it works. Often it doesn't. The alternative—deliberately finding people who would be genuinely well-suited to us—has historically required either social capital we didn't have or luck we couldn't control.

This problem is solvable. It is a matching problem, and matching problems yield to intelligence and information. Imagine systems—whether AI-assisted or organized through better social infrastructure—that genuinely understood what people were like and what they needed, and connected them not by demographic category but by the actual texture of who they were. The friend group you would have had, if you had been born into slightly different circumstances and met the right people at the right age. The romantic partner you might have spent decades searching for and perhaps never found. The community where your specific mix of interests and sensibilities would make you feel, for the first time, like you had arrived.

This does not mean eliminating the organic. The best friendships still need to grow through shared experience, through the accumulation of history, through the ordinary texture of time spent together. What it means is removing the enormous amount of waste in the current system—the years spent in the wrong places with the wrong people, searching for something that better infrastructure could have helped you find much sooner.

A future worth fighting for is one where no child grows up without finding their people. Where the D&D group that would have made your adolescence meaningful actually materializes, because the systems around you are designed to help it happen. Where the loneliness that currently ruins so many lives is treated as the solvable coordination problem it actually is, and solved.

## The Architecture of Accountability

Power without accountability corrupts—this is not a new observation. What is newer is the developing understanding of *why*, mechanically, corruption happens, and therefore what institutional designs actually prevent it.

The answer, wherever it has been found, involves visibility and verification. Not the pretense of oversight but actual, legible, checkable accountability. Courts that write out their reasoning in full, so superior courts and ordinary citizens can evaluate whether the reasoning is sound. Leaders whose actual mandate is described precisely, not mystified by honorifics. Scientists whose results are replicated before being treated as established. Institutions whose track records are publicly maintained and honestly reported, including the predictions they got wrong.

The deepest insight here is that you cannot prevent corruption by assuming you have hired incorruptible people. You can build systems where corruption is easy to see, where the cost of being caught is high, and where the incentives for honest performance outweigh the incentives for gaming the metric. This is not a cynical view of human nature. It is a realistic one that, paradoxically, produces better outcomes than the optimistic view that simply selects for trustworthy people and trusts them.

In practice, this means separating functions that have conflicts of interest when combined. The people who arrest are not the people who prosecute are not the people who run the prisons. The people who produce scientific results are not the only people who evaluate them. The people who allocate resources do not personally benefit from their allocation decisions in ways they have an incentive to hide.

And it means building cultures where *being wrong publicly and updating* is treated as a sign of integrity rather than weakness. The great epistemic failure of our current moment is not that people lack information. It is that people face social incentives to defend their prior positions regardless of new evidence, and institutions face the same incentives multiplied. A civilization that has fixed this—where changing your mind in response to evidence is praised rather than attacked, where prediction track records are maintained honestly, where the Very Serious People who made Very Serious Wrong Predictions are held to account for those predictions—is a civilization capable of learning, which is to say, capable of improving.

## The Treatment of Catastrophic Risk

One of the most important features of a well-coordinated civilization is that it takes the prevention of irreversible catastrophes seriously in a way that feels, to its inhabitants, like obvious common sense.

There is a category of risks that require special treatment: risks where the downside is not "we will be worse off and have to recover" but "we will be gone and cannot recover." These include engineered pandemics, certain kinds of weapons of mass destruction, and—the most difficult and most important—the misalignment of systems far more powerful than us with values that do not include our wellbeing.

The key insight about this category is that our normal reasoning process fails us. Normally, we demand evidence before acting. We test ideas. We try things and see what happens. But for truly catastrophic risks, by the time the evidence is in, we are no longer in a position to learn from it. This means that the expected-value calculation changes fundamentally: even a small probability of extinction warrants treatment far more serious than a large probability of recoverable setback.

A civilization that has understood this does not wait for evidence that self-replicating pathogens can destroy civilization before treating their creation as an absolute red line. It does not wait for evidence that misaligned superintelligence is dangerous before treating the alignment problem as the most important technical problem of the era. It maintains, and genuinely enforces, certain hard limits—not because it has perfect knowledge of the risks, but because it understands that the asymmetry of consequences justifies treating these limits as near-inviolable even under uncertainty.

The tragedy of our current moment is that we can see this clearly in retrospect for past catastrophes, and struggle to act on it for future ones. A good future requires building the institutional and cultural capacity to take future catastrophes seriously before they happen—to develop the wisdom to say "we should never test whether this particular thing is as dangerous as we fear" and to actually hold that line.

## Diversity Within Coherence

One of the tensions in any vision of a better-coordinated world is the concern that coordination means homogenization—that a society that has gotten better at solving collective action problems has done so by flattening individual difference and local experiment into a single global norm.

The opposite is true, or can be. The point of getting better at coordination is not to arrive at one optimal answer to every question about how to live. It is to manage, gracefully and with less waste, the genuine diversity of human needs and preferences. Different people want different things. Different communities want to organize themselves differently. A civilization that has gotten better at coordination is one that has figured out how to let this diversity flourish without it becoming a source of violent conflict or mutual destruction.

This looks like: strong global norms on the things that matter universally (no one gets to build a plague, no one gets to keep people from leaving, no one gets to poison the atmosphere), combined with enormous local latitude on everything else. The point of having many different places is precisely so that people with different preferences about how to live can find their own version of a good life without being forced into a single template.

This includes the experimental function. We do not yet know the best answers to many questions about how to organize work, how to structure education, how to build communities. The best way to find out is to try many things and honestly evaluate results. A civilization that is well-coordinated at the global level is also, for this reason, a civilization with more room for local experiment—because the global coordination provides the stability that makes local experimentation safe rather than catastrophic when it fails.

## The Universe Awaiting

Here is the scale of what is at stake.

The universe is approximately 93 billion light-years in diameter, contains somewhere between ten trillion and one sextillion stars, and—as far as we currently know—harbors no minds but our own. The silence of the cosmos is not a tragedy because it is permanent. It is a tragedy because minds exist who could go there, and build there, and make it alive with experience and beauty and discovery, if they can first figure out how to not destroy themselves.

Every generation alive today is, without being melodramatic about it, alive at the hinge. The decisions made in the next few decades will determine whether this civilization produces a future, and what kind. The answer to "what kind" is not foreordained. It is being worked out, right now, in laboratories and political institutions and cultural conversations and the individual choices of billions of people about what to care about and fight for.

The vision worth fighting for is one where everyone is okay—where the preventable suffering has been prevented, where the solvable problems have been solved, where no child grows up without people who love them and no adult dies before they are ready. Where the coordination problems that currently make collective action so costly have been addressed, not by authoritarian imposition, but by the slow, difficult work of building the institutions and educating the population that makes genuine collective action possible.

And then, on that foundation, something larger: the expansion of mind and experience into the vast silence, the answering of the great questions, the building of forms of life and consciousness and community that we cannot currently imagine.

The bad outcome is that we lose the thread. That the dangerous technologies arrive before the wisdom to handle them, and the story ends with no one to regret it.

The good outcome is that we make it. That the people working on alignment get it right. That the institutions hold. That enough people, in enough places, decide that they are fighting for something real—not just away from disaster, but toward a civilization they would be proud to have built.

That civilization is possible. It is not guaranteed, but it is possible. And the gap between possible and actual is filled by people who can see it clearly enough to fight for it.

So: here is what we are fighting for. A world where the suffering is gone, the coordination works, the institutions can be trusted, the loneliness has been addressed, the risks have been taken seriously, the diversity has been preserved, and humanity is free to spend the next billion years doing what it turns out minds are for.

That is worth everything it costs to get there.
