# The Abundant Commonwealth: A Vision of Flourishing Beyond Scarcity and Coercion

## I. The Two Locks

For most of human history, civilization has been trapped behind two locks simultaneously. The first is the lock of scarcity: when resources are finite and the game is zero-sum, every gain for one person or group represents a loss for another. This arithmetic of limitation breeds competition, hoarding, and ultimately war. Tribes raid neighboring tribes not from innate cruelty but because the land can only feed so many mouths. Nations build empires not from pure malice but because the alternative, in a world of fixed resources, can feel like slow strangulation. Scarcity does not merely cause poverty; it warps values, poisons institutions, and gives every cooperative instinct a competing voice whispering that trust is a vulnerability you cannot afford.

The second lock is subtler but equally confining: the lock of enforced conformity. Even when scarcity is not the immediate pressure, human communities have historically managed their collective lives by demanding that individuals subordinate themselves to the group's conception of virtue. Your culture told you what to worship, whom to marry, how to work, and what thoughts were permissible. Deviance was expensive, often lethally so. The individual who wished to live differently had to either hide, fight, or flee — and most could do none of these.

Liberalism was the great attempt to pick the second lock. By establishing a principle of non-interference — your right to swing your fist ends at my nose — it created spaces of genuine freedom that had never existed before. Gay people could live in cities. Heretics could publish books. Atheists could die in bed rather than at the stake. These are not small achievements. They are extraordinary ones, and no honest accounting of human progress can dismiss them.

But liberalism, for all its achievements, ran into a wall it was not built to climb. The principle of non-harm, strictly construed, could not address the fact that human beings are not isolated atoms. We are embedded in cultures, immersed in information environments, shaped by the social norms that surround us whether we choose them or not. The billboard selling cigarettes, the ubiquitous fast food, the media environment that systematically degrades certain groups of people: none of these technically break anyone's arm or pick anyone's pocket, yet all of them shape choices, shape desires, shape who people become. The strict liberal answer — "just don't partake" — founders on the reality that our capacity for choice is itself a product of the environment in which we develop it.

And the looser liberal answer — "well, indirect harms count too" — opens a door that liberalism was specifically designed to close: the door through which communities march to enforce their conception of virtue upon everyone within reach.

What if we could pick both locks at once?

## II. The Logic of Abundance

Begin with the economic lock, because its resolution makes everything else easier.

The conventional assumption, baked into most political philosophy, is that we are always dividing a more or less fixed pie. This assumption was reasonable for most of history. But it is increasingly unreasonable as a description of what is actually possible.

Consider what genuine abundance would mean — not the modest affluence of a developed-world middle class, but an expansion of productive capacity by orders of magnitude. Such an expansion is not guaranteed, but neither is it fantastical. The combination of artificial intelligence, advanced biotechnology, molecular manufacturing, and renewable energy represents a genuine possibility of transforming the material basis of human life in ways that would have seemed magical to every previous generation.

When total available resources expand by a factor of ten, or a hundred, or a thousand, the mathematics of conflict change radically. In a world where taking everything for yourself yields you one hundred units of value, while cooperating peacefully and splitting the gains yields you five hundred, the incentive structure that has driven human conflict for millennia simply dissolves. You cannot build a justification for war — or for hoarding, or for exploitation — when the peaceful path leads to vastly superior outcomes for everyone involved.

This is the concept at the heart of what we might call Paretotopian thinking: a world in which the expansion of total resources is so dramatic that almost everyone, across almost every dimension of prior conflict, ends up better off than they could have hoped for in the world of fixed scarcity. The zero-sum game that bred tribalism, imperialism, class warfare, and resource conflict does not merely become less urgent — it becomes, in the most fundamental sense, obsolete.

Crucially, this transformation does not require human nature to change. It does not require that people become less selfish, less tribal, or less competitive. It requires only that the incentives shift — that the expected value of cooperation reliably exceeds the expected value of conflict by enough that even moderately self-interested actors choose the cooperative path. History is full of conflicts that seemed inevitable until the conditions that made them rational changed. We can build conditions under which peace and mutual flourishing become the straightforwardly rational choice.

## III. Freedom by Architecture

Abundance alone, however, does not answer the second lock. A rich world can still be a world of enforced conformity, of communities so powerful and pervasive that the individual who wishes to live differently has nowhere to go.

Here we need a different insight: the possibility of genuine, structured pluralism.

Imagine a world — let us call it the Commonwealth of Free Associations — organized around a fundamental principle: that human beings have radically different conceptions of the good life, and that this diversity is not a problem to be solved but a richness to be organized. The Commonwealth maintains a minimal shared framework: protection against violence, protection against exploitation, freedom of movement, and the foundational guarantee that no community may coercively prevent its members from leaving. Within that framework, communities are free to organize themselves around almost any coherent set of values.

The conservative religious community can establish a town where the rhythms of life follow ancient liturgical patterns, where technology is limited, where sex is a sacrament and modesty is enforced by social expectation rather than merely encouraged by weak preference. This community is not imposed on anyone: it recruits only those who genuinely share its values and who find in that structure not oppression but liberation from a chaotic world that asks too little of them.

The progressive community committed to radical inclusion can build a place where pronouns are taken seriously, where media is curated against content that systematically degrades, where the architecture of daily life embeds the values of equality and recognition that its members consider non-negotiable. This too is not imposed on anyone: it is chosen by people who have weighed the alternatives and decided that this is where they can be most fully themselves.

The libertarian enclave can run its experiment with minimal governance and maximal individual latitude. The ecological commune can organize around deep sustainability. The rationalist community can build its culture of evidence and careful argument. The artistic bohemia can throw off every conventional restraint.

What prevents this from becoming a nightmare of mutual suspicion and occasional war between communities? Three things, all of them essential.

The first is genuine freedom of movement, guaranteed not by communities themselves but by the overarching Commonwealth. Every person, at any time, can present themselves to a Commonwealth representative and ask to leave. This request must be honored, unconditionally, within a short and fixed time. No debt, no social pressure, no threat of ostracism can legally prevent exit. Communities can make leaving socially costly — that is simply the nature of human belonging — but they cannot make it legally or physically impossible. The right of exit is the immune system of a pluralist society. It means that communities compete, in a fundamental sense, for members who always retain the option of going elsewhere.

The second protection is for children, who cannot meaningfully consent to the communities into which they are born. The Commonwealth does not abolish communities' right to raise children in their traditions — to do so would be to abolish communities themselves. But it does require that every child, at every stage of development, receive exposure to the broader world: genuine, honest exposure to the fact that other ways of life exist, that other people have found meaning in arrangements very different from their own, and that the Commonwealth itself stands ready to receive anyone who wishes to leave. Communities may instill their values; they may not create the conditions under which departure becomes literally unimaginable. Children may be raised as Christians or communists or libertarians, but they must also know that atheists and capitalists and socialists exist, that those people are not monsters, and that the doors of the Commonwealth are open to everyone.

The third protection is against externalities. Communities may not impose costs on each other without consent. The polluting factory, the aggressive broadcaster, the community that poisons shared aquifers or destabilizes shared climates — these are subject to Commonwealth regulation, not because their values are wrong, but because liberty does not include the freedom to shift your costs onto others without their agreement.

## IV. The Children of Scarcity

We inherit a world built for scarcity, and many of our deepest conflicts are scarcity's children — wearing new clothes, speaking new languages, but carrying the old logic in their bones.

Consider how differently a world of genuine abundance and genuine pluralism would handle the conflicts that currently tear at us.

The culture wars that consume so much political energy are, at their root, battles over who gets to control the shared cultural environment when there is only one of it. When there is only one culture — one media landscape, one school curriculum, one set of broadly enforced social norms — then every group has reason to fight for control of it, because losing means having your values suppressed in the only world your children will inhabit. But when communities can genuinely curate their own cultural environments, when the religious family and the secular family and the queer family and the traditionalist family can each live within media and social ecosystems that reflect their values, the stakes of the culture war evaporate. You are no longer fighting to control territory that everyone must inhabit. You are cultivating a garden that only your community needs to live in.

Economic inequality, similarly, looks different in a world of abundance. When resources are expanding rapidly and the floor keeps rising, the psychological and political toxicity of inequality diminishes — not entirely, since power over others remains a distinct good that some will always pursue, but substantially. The person who is a thousand times richer than you is far less threatening when your own absolute standard of living is already extraordinary than when their advantage comes directly at your expense. Abundance does not eliminate inequality, but it breaks the link between inequality and domination that makes inequality so dangerous.

Global conflict between nations follows the same logic. States that can each expect genuinely extraordinary futures through cooperation have diminishing incentives to risk those futures for the marginal advantage of a slightly larger share. The expected costs of conflict — destruction of the very productive capacity that generates the abundance everyone is counting on — become catastrophically high relative to any plausible gain.

None of this is automatic. Abundance must be pursued intelligently and the gains distributed in ways that are broadly perceived as fair, because the perception of fairness is itself a requirement for the cooperative equilibrium to hold. A world in which a small number of actors capture essentially all the gains from transformative technology is not a Paretotopian world — it is the old zero-sum world wearing a new mask. The institutions of the Commonwealth, including robust mechanisms for ensuring that the benefits of abundance flow broadly, are not ornamental. They are load-bearing.

## V. What This Asks of Us

The vision sketched here — let us call it the Abundant Commonwealth — is not primarily a description of technology or of political institutions, though it implies both. It is fundamentally a description of a world in which the two deepest structural drivers of human misery have been transformed: the misery that comes from not having enough, and the misery that comes from being forced to be someone you are not.

It asks for several things from us, as individuals and as societies.

It asks that we take seriously the project of building genuine abundance — not trickle-down growth but transformative expansion of productive capacity — as a moral priority rather than a mere side effect of market activity. The institutions and investments that could bring about Paretotopian resource expansion deserve the same serious political attention we give to distributing current resources. We spend enormous energy debating how to divide the pie; we spend relatively little ensuring that the pie can grow at the rate that would change the moral mathematics of conflict.

It asks that we build institutions of genuine pluralism — not the thin pluralism that says "we all get to vote in the same elections," but the thick pluralism that says "communities with genuinely different values can each build the environments in which those values are lived out." This requires protecting freedom of exit with absolute seriousness, ensuring children are genuinely informed rather than merely indoctrinated, and enforcing firmly against cross-community externalities while leaving internal community organization maximally free.

It asks that we resist the temptation — powerful on all sides — to universalize our own community's values at the expense of others. The conservative who wants everyone to live by traditional norms, and the progressive who wants everyone to adopt progressive norms, are making the same category error: treating a diverse world as if it were, or should be, a single community. The Abundant Commonwealth does not ask either to abandon their values. It asks them to build communities that embody those values, rather than capturing shared institutions to impose those values on others.

And it asks, finally, that we take the long view seriously — that we recognize we are at an unusual hinge in history, a moment when the decisions we make about how to govern the development of transformative technologies, how to structure the institutions that will distribute their benefits, and how to protect the conditions of genuine human pluralism, will echo for generations. The structures we build now will be the world our descendants inhabit.

## VI. The Archipelago of the Abundant

The old metaphor for pluralism was an archipelago: separate islands, each organized around its own values, united by a minimal common framework and connected by freedom of movement. It is a good metaphor, but it needs to be updated for what we now know and what we can now imagine.

The new image is an archipelago not of fixed islands competing for a finite ocean, but of islands that are themselves growing — each one expanding its possibilities, generating new resources, discovering new dimensions of human flourishing — while remaining connected by bonds of genuine commonwealth. The islands still differ. The conservative island and the progressive island and the libertarian island and the communal island each look, from the inside, entirely different. The people who live on each have made genuine choices about how to organize their shared lives.

But none of them are fighting over the ocean. The ocean is expanding. And the minimal government that spans the archipelago is not an enemy to any of them — it is the condition of their distinctiveness, the guarantor of their freedom, and the institution that ensures the expanding ocean benefits everyone rather than being captured by the largest and most powerful island.

In such a world, the question Frederick Douglass posed — "Do nothing with us!" — finds its proper answer at last. Not abandonment, which is what "doing nothing" often means in a world of entrenched inequality, but the creation of genuine conditions for self-determination: exit rights enforced absolutely, resources distributed broadly, communities free to organize around their own values, and the arc of expanding abundance bending, by design, toward universal flourishing.

This is not utopia as a fixed destination. It is utopia as a direction — a compass bearing we can actually follow, made possible by taking seriously both the material conditions of human freedom and the irreducible diversity of human value.

The locks can be picked. The keys are in our hands.
