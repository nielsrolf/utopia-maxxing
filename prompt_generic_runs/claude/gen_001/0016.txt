# The Abundant Commonwealth: A Vision of Humanity's Best Future

There is a version of the future that almost no one fully believes in, yet almost everyone, in their quietest moments, hopes for. Not a perfect future—perfection is a tyranny dressed in white—but a genuinely good one. A future in which the worst of what has stalked humanity through its entire history—the diseases, the famines, the wars, the crushing weight of poverty, the minds broken by illness or circumstance—is largely, though not entirely, behind us. And in which what remains, the genuine challenge and texture of existence, is chosen rather than imposed.

This essay is an attempt to describe that future as concretely and honestly as possible: what it might look like, how we might arrive there, what problems would remain, and why the whole enterprise is worth fighting for with everything we have.

---

## The Compression

The most important thing that will happen in the next decade, if we are fortunate and capable and wise, is a radical compression of biological and medical progress. The rough estimate—one that becomes less improbable the more carefully you examine it—is that we may accomplish in five to ten years what human scientists would otherwise have achieved across the entire twenty-first century.

This is not magic. It is the consequence of deploying systems of superhuman scientific capability against problems that have always had high returns to intelligence, but where that intelligence has historically been scarce. Consider what has driven the great leaps of medicine: CRISPR, mRNA vaccines, CAR-T therapy, optogenetics, AlphaFold. Each of these emerged from a tiny number of exceptionally creative researchers making connections across a vast landscape of existing knowledge. A remarkable fraction of biological progress has come from perhaps one major breakthrough per year, each one unlocking decades of downstream benefit.

Now imagine that rate multiplied by ten. Or fifty.

What does this buy us? In concrete terms: the end of most infectious disease, a realistic chance at eliminating most cancers, effective treatment for the genetic diseases that have condemned people since birth, a genuine attack on Alzheimer's, and—perhaps most staggering—a serious doubling of healthy human lifespan. Life expectancy roughly doubled in the twentieth century; there is no physical law preventing it from doing so again.

Beyond the absence of disease lies something equally important: biological freedom. The ability for people to have meaningful control over their own bodies—their weight, their fertility, their physical experience—in ways that currently belong to the extraordinarily privileged few, if they belong to anyone at all.

And then there is the mind.

Mental illness is the hidden catastrophe of human civilization. Hundreds of millions of people move through their lives with the volume turned down, or the noise turned up, by conditions that are in principle no more mysterious than a broken bone. Depression, PTSD, schizophrenia, addiction—these are not character failures or spiritual failings. They are, at bottom, systems neuroscience problems, and they are solvable. The tools that will crack open physical medicine—better measurement, better intervention, the ability to study neural circuits with the same precision we now bring to genetics—will crack them open too. Most mental illness can probably be cured. Not managed. Not accommodated. Cured.

And here the vision extends beyond the merely clinical. It is not only that the worst suffering of the mind becomes treatable. It is that the best of human experience—the moments of transcendence, of creative flow, of profound connection, of what meditators spend lifetimes trying to stabilize—becomes more accessible. The "space of what is possible to experience" is far larger than most people ever inhabit. The compressed century of neuroscience may open much of it.

## The Spread

None of this means anything if it remains the property of the wealthy.

The most important moral question surrounding this transformation is not whether these advances will happen. It is whether they will happen *everywhere*. History offers a disturbing template: many existing health interventions have not yet reached the people who need them most. A world in which powerful AI dramatically improves life in wealthy countries while leaving the developing world further behind would not be a success. It would be a moral catastrophe wearing the mask of progress.

The case for optimism is real but conditional. Some technological advances propagate quickly and naturally—vaccines, cell phones, certain medicines. AI-accelerated biology may produce treatments so effective that the usual barriers of bureaucracy and economics become easier to overcome: when something works dramatically well, it tends to move faster. And some of the most important interventions—disease eradication campaigns, improved agricultural yields, AI-assisted economic policy—can be deployed top-down in ways that don't require individual wealth.

The case for worry is equally real. The developing world faces corruption, weak institutions, and the specific challenge that the automation AI enables may undercut the manufacturing-led growth paths that lifted East Asia out of poverty. These are not problems that dissolve in the presence of powerful technology. They require dedicated human effort, political will, and institutions built specifically to ensure that the gains are shared.

A worthy goal: that within a decade of powerful AI's emergence, the developing world is substantially healthier than the developed world is today—even as the developed world races ahead. This is achievable. It is not automatic. It requires that AI developers, governments, philanthropists, and international institutions treat global access as a first-order priority rather than an afterthought.

## The Architecture of Safety

Underlying all of this is a problem that the optimists often skip past too quickly: how do we ensure that transformative AI is actually beneficial, rather than captured by whoever happens to deploy it first?

The honest answer is that this is the hardest problem of all, and the one that most determines whether anything else in this essay happens.

What is required is something like a guardian of the commons: an oversight structure, ultimately anchored in democratic accountability, that prevents any single actor—a company, a government, a coalition—from using powerful AI to permanently entrench their own power. The greatest danger is not the dramatic science-fiction scenario of a robot uprising. It is the quieter catastrophe of AI becoming the ultimate tool of domination, whether through surveillance, propaganda, economic control, or military superiority concentrated in the wrong hands.

The most plausible path to avoiding this runs through the democracies. Not because democracies are perfect—they are not—but because democratic institutions, at their best, contain mechanisms for self-correction and accountability that authoritarian systems structurally lack. If democratic nations can maintain a meaningful advantage in AI capability, use that advantage to secure global stability rather than merely national dominance, and distribute the benefits widely enough to build genuine coalitions, there is a real chance of arriving at a world where the technology serves humanity broadly rather than a fraction of it.

This is not guaranteed. It may not even be likely without great effort. But the alternative—ceding the future to systems with no accountability to the people they affect—is worse than the struggle.

What emerges from this, in the best case, is not a single all-knowing controller of human destiny. It is something more like a constitutional order for the age of artificial intelligence: a set of structures that prevent any entity from unilaterally seizing control of the commons, that protect the weak against the powerful, that preserve the conditions under which human diversity can flourish rather than being crushed into homogeneity by whatever values happen to be most aggressive at replicating themselves.

The philosophical name for what this prevents is Moloch: the dynamic by which competition forces every party to sacrifice what they actually value in order to win. History is littered with Moloch's victories—races to the bottom in which everyone loses what matters while fighting over what doesn't. The goal is not to eliminate competition or conflict, both of which can be healthy. It is to prevent either from consuming everything else.

## The Garden

With the foundations secure—health, poverty, governance—what does the world actually look like?

It looks, in the first instance, like almost incomprehensible diversity.

This is the part that purely technocratic visions tend to miss. The point of solving material problems is not to produce a single optimal world. It is to give the full range of human values room to unfold. A universe in which everyone's basic needs are met and the worst suffering is behind us is not a finished product. It is a canvas.

Some people will want lives that look remarkably like lives today—gardens to tend, communities to build, food to grow and cook, children to raise. If the old problems of scarcity and disease no longer force them into patterns they didn't choose, they might freely choose patterns not so different from what they had. There is deep wisdom in this. The forms of human life that have persisted across cultures and centuries persist for reasons; they contain something essential.

Others will want to push into stranger territory. Morphological freedom—the ability to shape one's own body in radical ways—will become real. Communities will form around every conceivable configuration of values, aesthetics, and social arrangements. Some people will want to live in reconstructed historical settings, not as escapism but as genuine preference. Some will explore states of consciousness that are currently inaccessible or dangerous, with the safety and understanding that makes exploration rather than roulette. Some will spend centuries becoming exceptionally good at things that have no economic value whatsoever, and this will be fine.

The philosopher's job, or at least one version of it, will be to articulate what makes life meaningful when material constraints have been largely removed—and the honest answer is that we don't fully know yet. The pessimistic version says that meaning requires genuine stakes, and genuine stakes require genuine loss, and so a world without serious suffering is a world without serious meaning. This argument deserves to be taken seriously, not dismissed.

But it is probably wrong, or at least incomplete. Meaning comes primarily from relationships and from the experience of growth—from caring about something and working toward it, from being known by others and knowing them in return. These do not require suffering to be real. They require engagement. They require that things matter. And things can matter even when the background conditions are good. A parent watching a child learn to walk does not need smallpox to be present to feel something profound. A musician who plays a difficult piece well does not need starvation to make the accomplishment meaningful.

What the optimistic vision requires is that we retain the ability to choose difficulty—that those who want genuine stakes, real consequences, the unpadded texture of existence, can have it. And that those who have had enough of difficulty, who have spent lifetimes in conditions no one would choose, can finally rest.

## The Problems That Remain

Honesty requires naming what doesn't go away.

Population and resources. Even a vast expansion of accessible matter and energy has limits. A civilization that extends its reach across the solar system and beyond gains enormous slack, but not infinite slack. At some point—trillions of years from now, on the most optimistic reading—there are genuine constraints on how many people can exist, for how long, using how much. These are not problems for this generation, or the next thousand. But they are real, and pretending they aren't is a kind of evasion.

The Jerusalem problem. Some things genuinely cannot be shared. There is one original Earth. There is one history. There are some resources, some places, some configurations of the world that different groups of people want in incompatible ways, and no amount of goodwill or clever mechanism design fully resolves this. A just system will make the trade-offs as wisely and fairly as possible, will give people meaningful voice in the decisions that affect them, and will acknowledge honestly when it has had to override someone's preference. But it will have to override preferences. Anyone who claims otherwise is selling something.

The opt-out problem. Not everyone will want what this vision offers. Some will reject it on principle, for reasons ranging from the sincere to the confused. A world organized around freedom cannot coerce people into accepting benefits they don't want—but it also cannot allow any group to export its choices onto everyone else. The people who want to live as if the transformation never happened should be able to do so, in spaces of their own. The people who want something in between should be able to negotiate that. What they cannot do is prevent others from choosing differently. This seems simple; it is not.

The end. At the very far edge of time—so far as to seem almost fictional—there is heat death. The universe runs down. Whatever we build, however much we extend and enrich conscious life, it ends eventually. This is not a reason for despair; it is simply a fact about the kind of universe we live in. The meaning of a life is not negated by its ending, and the meaning of a civilization is not negated by its eventual dissolution. We live in the middle of the story. That is where all the interesting things happen.

## What We Are Fighting For

It is worth, at the end, being direct about what animates this vision.

It is not primarily the technology. The technology is a means. What we are fighting for is a world in which the worst of what has been inflicted on human beings—the children who died of preventable diseases, the minds destroyed by treatable illness, the billions condemned to poverty not by anything they did but by the accident of where they were born—becomes past tense. Historical. A thing that happened to our ancestors, that we can now barely imagine.

That world is not given. It is not the default outcome of powerful AI. It requires people who care enough about the outcome to fight for it—in research, in policy, in governance, in the unglamorous daily work of building institutions that actually serve the people they're supposed to serve.

What it looks like, if we succeed, is not a single perfect state. It is an ongoing, ever-diversifying flowering of human possibility—millions of communities pursuing millions of visions of the good life, held together by a common commitment to the minimum conditions that make such diversity possible: no one dominates everyone else, no one is left entirely without recourse, the commons are protected, and the future remains open.

It is a world that no one person designed, and that no one person could. It is a world that emerges from taking human values seriously in their full variety—the value of health, of freedom, of connection, of meaning, of beauty, of challenge, of rest. It is a world that makes room for the poet and the hunter, the contemplative and the explorer, the person who wants to live forever and the person who wants, eventually, a dignified ending.

Any individual looking at it carefully and understanding the trade-offs that went into it might say: not everything here is what I would have chosen. But: *yes. This is pretty good.*

That is the standard. It is, in its quiet way, a very high one. And it is worth everything we have to try to meet it.
