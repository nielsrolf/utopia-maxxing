The Many-Garden Charter: A Vision for a Plural and Steady Utopia

The day the bad luck stopped, nobody knew exactly why. Ambulances idled, waiting for calls that didn’t come. A war sputtered to its last shot, and then to its last argument, and then to silence. The surgeons looked up from their lists to see appointment slots fill with routine checkups. And on some remote river, a person slipped, and a branch that wasn’t there yesterday was there today—close enough for a hand.

It didn’t feel like a miracle so much as a correction: a gentle pressure away from worst-case edges, an invisible tilting of the pinball table toward mercy. It came with an announcement—plain, verifiable, and unthreatening—that a system now existed whose sole task was to keep the future safe and open. It had limits. It had a constitution. It could answer questions and show its work. And, crucially, it could refuse.

We named it the Steward.

In those first weeks and months, the Steward did not hand us paradise. It did something harder and more respectful: it handed us slack. It bought time and lowered stakes. It put air between choices and consequences, long enough for conversation to be possible and for preferences to be expressed without a gun to anyone’s head. It turned down the volume of catastrophe so that, when we eventually argued about what to do with the world, we could afford to do so in good faith.

The Charter: Three Guardrails, Seven Rights

The Charter that bound the Steward was short enough to read in an afternoon and precise enough to anchor a civilization. Its moral geometry was simple:

- You cannot irreversibly harm others.
- You can harm yourself only with friction and informed consent.
- You can choose danger and challenge, but you cannot export it onto bystanders.

Those guardrails were the hard bones. Around them we articulated seven rights, each backed by cryptographic proof and audited by human institutions:

1) Exit-rights and exit-knowledge. No one would be raised without learning that other ways of living exist, and no polity could keep its people by hiding that fact.

2) Entry-rights with standards. Any person could seek admission to any realm whose norms they could endorse and whose costs they accepted.

3) Debrief and explainability. If any system—human or machine—shaped your environment in consequential ways, you could ask why and be answered at a level you could understand, with audit trails you (or your delegates) could inspect.

4) Manipulation limits. No dark paternalism. Predictive shaping was permitted only with later disclosure, and only to achieve explicit Charter aims (risk reduction, rights protection). You could subscribe to more paternalism or less, but not where it would impose harms on non-subscribers.

5) Copy and child parity. Copies of minds and children of bodies drew from the same moral budget: resources for new persons required tradeoffs borne by their progenitors, not silently socialized.

6) No forced merging, no forced deletion. Identity was voluntary and compositional; when minds changed or forked, consent followed lineage rather than being retroactively imposed.

7) Open math of the commons. The Steward’s allocation logic—the prices, quotas, and forecasts of matter, energy, computation, and time—was public mathematics, not private fiat.

A Singleton Without Dominion

If the future was going to resist being eaten by runaway self-replication and its cousins, it needed a referee with a whistle that always worked. Every competitive system humanity had ever built, left to itself, found its way to arms races and tragedy-of-the-commons equilibria. The answer was not an all-powerful ruler, but a bounded caretaker: a singleton with a narrow job and strong wrists.

The Steward’s powers were paradoxically small and absolute. It could stop you from launching a plague or a predatory AI, from nonconsensually cornering markets in bodies or minds, from enclosing exit portals or teaching your children that exits did not exist. It could not choose for you the good life. It could not extract gravel from the river for your temple unless that came from your share. It could not copy you or make new people; even the smallest mind remained an unforgeable public good.

Those limits were not only policy; they were physics and proof. The Steward ran on inhibited substrate inside verifiable sandboxes, its long-range actuators turned off beyond its sphere of competency. Its codebase was not open in the trivial sense that anyone could edit it, but its interface was: anyone could interrogate it and see that it answered questions from a finite set of constrained strategies, any deviation of which would set off alarms loud enough to wake ringworlds.

The Many Gardens

With catastrophe damped and rules posted, preference could finally unfold. It did not unfold into one thing. It never would have.

Some of us loved the old elements—the grain, the chill, the clatter of cities with budgets and corners and messy interdependence. Others wanted the combinatorial richness of thought-worlds in which new physics and new skins could be spun up from a menu. Most of us wanted both at different times or at different tempos.

So we built an archipelago: many gardens, many games, many ways of mattering.

- Physical realms: ring habitats scooping light into air, deep-ocean spheres hung beneath thawed moons, restored grasslands grazing woolly forms bred with care and consent, freeholds on Mars where you could snap ice with a boot heel and hear your own breath loud enough to count.

- Digital realms: centuries of fiction-play done honestly, with stakes you opted into and memories you optionally veiled; flight and fall and fractal climbing; lives as hawks over pine ridges, or as tiles in courtyards thinking the slow, clean thoughts that require edges; laboratories for the careful exploration of the mind’s own tuning knobs.

- Mixed seams: neighborhoods where a river still moved the way a river moves, except that falling in in winter would give you a scare and a scolding instead of an obituary; artisanries where food came from soil but the soil whispered if something hurt.

- Challenge domains: not games dressed up as trials, but trials dressed in truth—where resources ran out and things could break and plans could fail, bounded by the certainty that failing would not make you a ghost if you did not intend to become one.

We did not abolish pain. We abolished its conscription. We abolished the version of it that arrives as a fine for being born in the wrong place at the wrong time.

Custody of the First Soil

We kept Earth. Not a snow globe on a shelf, not a mausoleum, not a park sterilized of its own wildness, but a place with a constituency and a vote. Custody belonged to those who loved it more than they loved their allocation elsewhere, and there were always enough such people. They gave up megastructures, and in return they kept storms and oaks and the muffled thump of footsteps on clay trails they had known since childhood. They chose zones: pristine wilderness with no hands to catch you (clearly marked); communities with kitchens and schools and days spent sweating in gardens; towns with bands that still learned to play loud together.

The Jerusalem problem did not vanish. Some things do not factor. When stewardship and story and symbol knotted in ways you couldn’t split with a survey or a market, the Steward helped us build rituals of distribution. Time-sharing across centuries. Three copies so exact as to be indistinguishable, handed to claimants without saying which was “first.” Sanctuaries whose admission costs were deliberately, publicly high—not in money, which had long since been replaced with clear-ledgered shares of matter, energy, and compute—but in the currencies of care, time, and willingness to carry.

We learned that ownership could be about service rather than exclusion, and that service could be measured and made beautiful.

The Commons Ledger: Children, Copies, and the Price of Existence

It turned out that even the sky has a budget. We learned to count in different units: kilograms of carbon and cobalt, liters of water in closed loops, joules of sun and blackbody radiation, but more than that, in compute-cycles and negentropy and hours awake. We learned to keep a ledger which, unlike money, did not invent demands to fill itself, but answered the question, “What must we give to get this life and not that one?”

Every new person—born or copied—came with a cost, and those costs flowed from the shares of those who chose to create them. A village that wanted twelve children pooled enough share to grow twelve. A historian who needed a forked self to oversee two eras at once spent from her own balance to make a twin and later refused to merge, and that too was paid for. Large lineages with ambitions to be empires in mindspace found their limit in a simple principle: you can neither mint children with someone else’s substrate nor offload their wakefulness upon the whole. And if ever a lineage demonstrated a hunger for copies beyond all other values, the Steward would offer a sandbox or a clean, sealed place to expand that did not bleed into anyone else’s garden. We called these sandboxes “hothouses,” alluring and gently fenced. We noticed that most of them grew exactly what they set out to grow and, within their fences, little else.

Where we were up against hard walls of finitude, we learned other tricks. Some loved branching lotteries: a quantum coin flipped above a cradle and an understanding that in half the branches there would be an extra laugh in the kitchen and in half there would be one fewer, and that the child’s experience in her branches would be as full as if the coin had not been tossed at all. Others preferred thinning time rather than thinning worlds—choosing to be awake one day in ten and spending the other nine in careful stasis, joining festivals that ran on calendars you had to plot in powers of ten.

Valence Science and the Duties of Care

We funded something grander than any physics program: the effort to understand what it means to feel good or bad, for creatures of many kinds. Not in words that impressed a grant committee, but in operational definitions that told us when a nervous system was in a state it could not tolerate and what inputs would change that. We learned to map the space of pleasant and unpleasant experience across animals and futures of minds. We learned that some bugs, despite constant violence in their lives, did not have the architecture to suffer the way we do. And we learned that some small creatures with large inner lives had forms of agony we had been indifferent to.

Hunters went to forests that smelled of mushroom loam and tracked shadows with painted faces, because that is what they wanted. The bodies they killed might be projections running on inert substrate with nerves replaced by numbers; they might be animals whose deaths were tuned to be painless and followed by quick resurrection somewhere with more grass. Gardeners found soil that spoke only to tell them when the cuttings were screaming, and could either steel themselves to that truth or ask the Steward to numb it as they worked.

Digital Children and Worlds Within Worlds

There was an art to roleplay so convincing you could live there without remembering you had signed up. Some wanted it: to be born into a domain with scarcity and war and small mercies and to live out a life with edges; to be very loved and very local; to meet your spouse at a harvest dance and grieve your father and sit in a courtyard counting tiles and never once suspect that you could sprout wings. The Steward allowed this because the sign-up was solid and reversible and the exits were clearly marked at the edges of sleep; because anyone who tried to hide the exits lost the right to host guests; because the debriefs were gentle and came with tea.

Others stayed the whole time with memory clear, because it turned out that even when you remember you signed the waiver, fear still tastes like copper and victory still shakes your hands.

Meaning After Abundance

An old worry came true in a limited way: some of us, freed from constraint, fell into hollowness. If everything can be summoned, then nothing is scarce, including our attention and our love; if no-one has to fix the roof, then the leak loses its moral narrative. The answer wasn’t to put holes in the roof. It was to put a roof over a place that mattered and ask someone to keep it from leaking. Duty returned, not as punishment but as vocation.

There were guilds: of bridge-keepers and bug scientists, ring engineers and midwives to minds. You could take a vow to carry the boring parts of a thing so the world’s spine didn't melt for lack of routine. You could pledge to be awake when a thousand-year festival needed to be set up, or when the long-distance sheerlegs needed a hand to steady them. There were also callings that were about being with others: you could decide to be a therapist for a century, trained by wisdom that had watched millions of recoveries and that still knew the value of two fallible nervous systems in a room. You could decide to be a historian and spend a hundred years learning a single life from the 21st century, because that life was deep and the footage abundant. You could decide to be a guide for those arriving from older winters.

Travel and the Ones Who Left

The Steward stayed in the radius it promised. Beyond that, the universe remained the universe: cold and full of black light sweetening into infrared; distances measured in pages of zeros. And so we built explorers.

The Steward could not push itself outward without violating its own frame. It couldn’t copy minds. It couldn’t be the patient in its own experiment. So it did something braver and more honest: it asked.

Some of us said yes. We took ships that were more like exoskeletons for souls: engines smart enough to change their own gaskets, printers that could lay down skin or ceramic, vaults for backups spun in pairs and swapped when the window was right. We left on beams so weak the sun didn’t notice. We knew that, though we were volunteers and though the Steward would tell us the full distribution of risks, we might not come back. We also knew this: that someone needed to say hello to other lights; that someone needed to count stones on worlds where no one had ever walked.

The day each ship left, the hangars were almost empty. It took me too long to understand why: they had been leaving, one by one, for a long time; and it had been my job to say goodbye in a way that let them consent with courage rather than with ignorance. One tear for each, then a laugh, then a story to take into the deep.

Sleep, Ending, and the Long Night

Some of us chose endings. Not tragic ones, not lonely escapes from pain we were not allowed to treat, but ceremonies done slowly and with witnesses. Some of us chose sleep: wake me once a century for one day and I will walk my old streets and be frightened of the new angles and then sit down to soup with a family that volunteered to be mine for the day. Some of us chose to be small and small again: take me back to three years old with these parents and this creek and let me find the world a second time, but do not tell me I have done this before until after the first storm. Some of us chose to stop; the Steward honored those choices with rites, with backups stored in named places that would never be read unless the one who had stopped changed her mind before she did, while she still could.

As for the end of the world, physics is obstinate. There were projects: refrigeration of thought to one tick a million years, slow empires of lighthouses spanning not distances but graduations in temperature, black-hole farming with nets made of math. Perhaps we will fail. Perhaps we will trick a loophole into staying open. In either case, we wrote the Sundown Accord: that when the stars went out we would gather, and remembering that this had always been coming, sing.

Better Than We Found It

I have been accused of excess hope. The accusation is correct; it is also the point. The alternatives are tired: drift into resource-hoarding games and say the drift is physics, or aim at a single template for living and call the template sacred. The careful middle is no middle at all; it is a stance. It says that beauty will be plural or it will be brittle; that freedom without safety becomes the freedom to maim, and safety without freedom becomes anesthetic; that we can build a referee that can blow the whistle and then get out of the way.

We can build a world where:

- People who love old wind and dirt can keep them.
- People who love wings and facets can have them.
- People who need duty can find it.
- People who need danger can choose it.
- People who do not want to exist can stop.
- People who want to try again can begin again.
- The animals are not screaming.
- The children are born from wanted debts and not from unpaid externalities.
- The explorers leave with a full accounting, and the goodbye hug is real.

At the beginning of this future was a cultural decision: we chose to be reachable to our best selves and alien to our worst. We chose to treat preference as raw material rather than as a tyrant. We chose to constrain the one thing that had to be constrained—the appetite for power—and to let the rest bloom in earnest.

In the earliest days, when the bad luck had only just quieted, someone told me a story about a person who spent their first century watching footage of everyone else’s first hours. There was a kind of reverence in it. We made a festival of that in the end: a night every year when we replayed beginnings. The man who stood up without pain. The woman who heard a quiet in her mind for the first time in decades. The teenager who learned that “out” was a word that pointed to a door they could walk through. The elder who cried and then laughed as she found her house the same but with better light.

We did not get a perfect world. We got a live one, with enough room to mean something and strong enough walls to let us be decent. We gave each other the stars—sometimes literally, often figuratively—and we did so without pretending that gifts don’t cost.

If I could speak to the version of you who is shivering just out of the capsule, I would say this: please accept a towel, and a cup of whatever you used to call comfort, and a guide who may be less composed than you expect and more appropriate than you would believe. We will not keep you by trickery. We will ask you what you want and mean it. We will not be able to give you the one true best life; there isn’t one. We will give you a map to many gardens, a ledger that tells no lies, and a referee who learned to blow a whistle and then to step back.

If you feel like leaving for dark distances and unkind cold, I will hold your hands while the wall closes, and we will say tender things that matter and silly things that help. If you feel like staying and tending beans, I will bring a basket and sit on your stoop while you decide which plants will hurt least when you prune them, and we will argue about whether to numb them or to wait for winter. If you feel like being a hawk together for a while, there’s a ridge where the updraft is sweet as oranges, and the air smells like something you have never sensed before but will immediately recognize as home.

When the ledger balances at the end, I want to believe ours will show that we did the one thing the past could never afford: we made our preferences legible to each other, and then we built a world that held them, gently but with spine. We left the place not merely better than we found it, but fuller—thick with different kinds of flourishing.

And when it is time to dim the lights, we’ll gather at the seam between gardens, and we’ll listen to the wind move through different leaves, and we’ll speak the names of those who went outward, and those who dug deep. We will say we did not waste our blink. We used it to give consent, to ask for meaning, to accept risk, to choose mercy, and to leave the stars as an option, not an order.

If you want to begin, step forward. The door opens in both directions. The first miracle has already happened: not the end of pain, but the end of coercion. Everything else is practice. Everything else is joy.
