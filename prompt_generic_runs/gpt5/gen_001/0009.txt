The Choir and the Workshop: A Vision of a Coordinated, Caring Future

We learned, at last, to treat coordination itself as a public utility.

It began in schools. Children practiced reading, counting, and then solving simple social dilemmas with the same matter-of-factness as long division. They learned to see when a tragedy of the commons was taking shape, how to write down an agreement anyone could audit later, how to price a scarce good without giving anyone a veto over it, how to switch as a group from a bad equilibrium to a better one without leaving anyone behind. By adolescence, they could tell the difference between a personal disagreement and a coordination failure; by adulthood, they had practiced moving entire classrooms, and then whole neighborhoods, into better patterns on purpose.

None of this made people angels. It did make them dangerous to manipulative equilibria. “Don’t burn the commons for a private spark” became an unfashionable boast. And a thousand small institutions—schools, courts, city charters, scientific journals, software platforms, hiring practices—were designed as if this literacy mattered.

The result was neither utopia nor monoculture. It was a civilization that had trained itself to do on Tuesday what it wished it had done on Monday, and to remember the lesson on Wednesday.

Shared principles

A handful of norms underwrote everything else.

- Everyone is an apprentice economist. People may disagree about values, but they are fluent in opportunity costs, externalities, rent, risk, and Goodhart’s law. Debates are anchored in “what are we paying for, what are we measuring, and what will this measurement break.”

- Markets are tools, not gods. We kept private property and competition where they create value, and we priced access to the Earth’s unproduced gifts—land, minerals, spectrum—through open leases whose rent funds a baseline dividend. No one owns the rain; anyone can own a roof.

- We measure what we can, and we show our work. Courts, regulators, scientific panels, and public allocation systems publish their reasoning in human language that laypeople can read and rival experts can tear apart. In big questions, two independent bodies reach judgments in parallel, and divergences are studied not buried.

- We keep experiments cheap and exits open. Rather than argue abstractly, we made it easy to start a district that tries a different rule, and even easier to leave it. The price of running experiments is the promise to stop them if they’re harming those who didn’t consent.

- Reputation is a public good, not a private island. We starved separate prestige pools where clever defection is admired. A firm caught orchestrating a cartel did not just pay a fine; it met customers who could instantly crowdfund a decently capitalized competitor and a public that remembered for years.

- Some temptations are treated like loud noises near a hospital. We learned that hyperstimuli—financial, sexual, informational—can raid shared baselines for attention and attachment. Rather than ban desire, we built defaults that are quiet, with bright gates and consent rituals around high-intensity spaces, and we studied what worked.

- Give 15. A broad norm set aside roughly fifteen percent of income for public goods. This was not just “charity”; the categories and recipients were public, arguments about them were statusful, and the ledgers—replication studies, legal aid, emergency response, archiving, weather buoys, pathogen surveillance—were as carefully audited as pensions.

A day in the life

A sixteen-year-old completes a weeklong micro-apprenticeship at a machine shop, shadowing a journeyman she personally selected. She will try seven more trades before she is nineteen. There is no minimum age to contribute: competence is the gate, and the baseline dividend means no one is coerced to sell their childhood. Her city runs hot; there are more task postings than hands, because licensing is light, entry barriers are low, and businesses cannot hoard monopolies on necessity. She is paid for demonstrated skill, not attendance, and the tests are the work itself.

On her way home, she speaks to a civic AI that summarises two competing rulings about the placement of a new transit corridor. It cites not just conclusions but the model assumptions, the counterfactuals, and the terms under which either decision would be rescinded. She can click through to dissenting analyses from independent auditors funded by the 15 percent norm. The models are legible; the logs are permanent.

Her grandparents see a neurologist, a nurse, and a model. The model is often the quietest in the room. Twenty years ago, work on the biology of aging, oncology, and immune modulation was acceler­ated by what people later called a “city of geniuses in a datacenter”: millions of coordinated, superhumanly literate agents that could design experiments, automate labs, and iterate across thousands of projects at once. But the city’s charter forbade it to move faster than the physical world could keep up; the slow clocks of cell culture and human metabolism were treated as sacred limits. The biggest wins were the tools that gave us better levers—generalizable measurement and intervention methods that made confounding less confounding. We did not wish disease away. We out-instrumented it.

The grandparents’ joint care plan was written to be read: what biomarkers it targets, which longitudinal evidence it learns from, what makes it stop. If a therapy works brilliantly, approval is swift; if a therapy works faintly, the path is narrow and the demands for rigor are high. Much of the bureaucracy around clinical trials dissolved when effect sizes stopped being whispers.

After dinner, our apprentice meets a relationship steward—professional matchmakers are ordinary civic workers—who reviews her profile, values, and constraints, and shows two possible matches. The steward’s fee vesting depends on how happy she is a year and ten years from now. In intimacy and in advertising alike, the default world is de-escalated: billboards do not blast beauty one-in-a-thousand into everyone’s eyes by default, and high-intensity sexual and commercial media live behind unmissable gates with real consent. The goal is not purity; it is a shared ability to be pleased by ordinary life.

The quiet and the busy

Not everyone thrives in the churn of a fast, forgiving economy. Some people are hurt by noise that others enjoy. Some tastes are rare. Some constitutions carry burdens that we learned how to ease but not lift. Two complementary institutions kept this from hardening into cruelty.

First, there are Sanctuaries: well-funded, voluntary towns and communities where participation in the wider economy is not expected, where the workday is slow or optional, where social services and companionship are thick. Sanctuary is not a sentence; it is a right. People move in and out, often after a difficult year, sometimes to spend a lifetime. The only hard edges are the ones that keep predation out.

Second, there is Reproductive Stewardship that looks nothing like coercion. We replaced sermons with information, subsidies with honest calculators, stigma with respect. We built generous family supports, and we normalized conversations about what different people can sustainably give a child now versus later, while also reducing the suffering that made those questions hard in the first place. We honored adoption, foster networks, communal parenting, and the dignity of not parenting. When hurts could be healed, we healed them; when they could not, we gave people meaningful lives anyway.

Science, speed, and brakes

The era’s signature artifact was not a single invention but a cadence. A problem moves from hypothesis to small in vitro loop within days; the first preprint lands a week later, already accompanied by a replication attempt funded out of the 15 percent stream. If both are promising, a mesh of independent lab-automation facilities picks up the thread. Cross-institutional AIs debug protocols and propose variations a human team might need years to think up, and they do it without smuggling unknowns into living systems: all bio-capable models are boxed so that the only levers they can touch are the ones already licensed for safety.

Under this tempo, biology leapt. We learned to switch immune pathways with the delicacy we once reserved for optics, to reverse some kinds of neural scarring, to prevent most cancers from ever gathering a foothold, to edit cells in place with far fewer off-target wounds. Infectious disease did not disappear—evolution never sleeps—but generalizable vaccine platforms and worldwide sensor networks turned fearsome plagues into manageable storms. In neuroscience, a marriage of new measurement tools, computational interpretability, and careful behavioral work made major mental illnesses something you recover from. On ordinary days, gentler gains piled up: less pain, better sleep, steadier moods, the grace to stay present with one another.

There were brakes. We learned to respect the irreducible latencies of animals and of people; no one got to wish them away with rhetoric. We built compute passports and tripwires into the development of very capable systems, and we set up safety boards that did not answer to any single firm or country. We made it simple for any lab anywhere to whisper “this smells like an infohazard,” and for that whisper to reach a global quorum within hours. Slowing a line of research was a big deal with explicit, public reasons on the record and a hard review clock. When we were uncertain, we bought time—days, weeks, or years—by choice rather than by accident.

Governing while awake

Borders softened into logistics. The right to exclude and the right to leave were balanced by a third right: to see the rule before you are ruled by it. Regional charters made their promises in a vocabulary everyone shared. If a city wanted to require a headscarf, allow only quiet vehicles, tax sugar, or mandate maximal-waste composting, it spelled out not only the rule but the harm it prevented, the metrics it would track, the sunset date when it would have to justify itself anew, and the exit velocity it guaranteed: how easily dissenters could move, how expenses were pro-rated, how credits transferred.

“Government service” stopped being a euphemism for “confusing frustrations.” A citizen’s assistant—an AI trained on the city’s own rules and audited by outside counsel—handled filings, explained denials, pre-empted mistakes, and kept an eye on deadlines. Where a rule reduced discretion, the software simply did the thing; where a rule invited judgment, the human being made the call and the machine wrote down the why so an ombudsman—or a journalist—could follow.

We did not imagine away foreign adversaries. The most dangerous autocracies sought to blend propaganda and fine-grained surveillance into automated control. They found, for a time, ugly success. Our answer was an entente of open societies that secured the chip supply chain, set capability thresholds for models with global externalities, and offered a brutally simple bargain to the undecided: join a network that will share health, energy, and education tools at cost, with political independence intact, in exchange for verifiable limits on building opaque, unaccountable intelligence. The stick was real—a decisive military edge backed by fast, human-supervised command loops that embargoed first use of autonomy in lethal decisions. The carrot was bigger and better every year. And we made it technically and politically hard to censor the sky: lightweight mesh terminals and resistant protocols made it easy for citizens to get an honest channel out.

Work, wealth, and worth

As machines took on more of the economically necessary, two gaps yawned: what pays, and what feels like it matters.

We separated them without pretending they were ever the same. A civic dividend drawn from land rent, resource leases, spectrum auctions, and an automation tithe met shelter, food, care, and connection as rights. No one had to plead deservingness. On top of the dividend, markets carried on where they made sense; people still built companies, bargained, and got rich, but the floor was real and the ceiling higher than before because the fear underneath it had been drained.

Then we flooded the zone with meaning.

We funded fellowships to do the things machines did poorly—comfort the dying, teach a child to laugh again, mediate a family’s grief, steward a watershed, restore a language, pack a courtroom with the right eyes. We made room for craft guilds whose products were obviously, gloriously made by hands and whose customers paid partly in money and partly in the reputation commons: your name on this violin means more in five years if you fix a neighbor’s roof today. We built adventure into adulthood: service years at any age that took citizens to hard edges and paid them with stories and thick ties.

We learned not to sneer at play. Sports thrived; games became hard again; the slow arts returned because the point had never been to maximize output per hour. People pursued mastery with the joy of constraint, and if a model could play the game better, it just meant you played a different game. Comparative advantage moved from economics to identity: not “what can I sell that you cannot,” but “what can I become with you that I cannot alone.”

Property and invention

We kept patents out of the veto business. If you created a tool that made a field more productive, you were owed a share of the value—automatically, through a well-audited licensing grid that every market participant had signed onto. Enforcement looked like accounting, not court intrigue. The most respected firms overpaid their inventors because they wanted to be known as the sort of house that, in the long run, always left the commons larger. Copyright lasted fourteen years, enough for a career to stand on.

And when someone did the equivalent of inventing a general vaccine platform or a better battery, the thank-you was public and compounding: money, of course, but also a vote in how the next ten years of science should be organized, and a budget to try organizing it.

Information, media, and the truth-tellers

We rebuilt the knowledge ecosystem around two professions: Replicators and Chroniclers.

Replicators were as glamorous as discoverers. They were paid well to rerun experiments, to check code, to re-interview sources, to stand on site and watch the machine hum. Their names went at the top of the page next to the originators’. Chroniclers were journalists who published with a special voice reserved for neutral narrative. They were funded like firefighters, by the 15 percent norm, and selected for clarity, kindness, and the stubborn habit of naming the best argument of the side they disagreed with. You could disagree with a Chronicler, but you learned to be careful doing it.

Social platforms changed incentives by changing what they measured. You grew an audience by being predictive—say true things today that people still feel were true in a month—and by increasing the diversity of good ideas your readers encountered. Rage-bait lost its subsidy. So did beauty-bait. So did the spectacle of conflagration without the craft of building.

Conflicting needs and the archipelago

It turned out we had always needed a hundred versions of “the New York City subway.” We built them. Hypersensitive and slapstick-loud spaces lived a block apart with bright signage and trusted gatekeepers. Allergy-safe districts and dog-saturated promenades existed side by side under guarantees about air exchange and escape routes. Quiet libraries abutted drum circles. The answer to “who must bend” was often “no one, but you must not walk unannounced into the other’s living room.”

Constraints and humility

None of this was magic. Biology keeps its own time; chaos does not care what you can afford; atoms are slower than code; people have the right to resist even the best of your arguments. Intelligence is a multiplier until it hits a limit, then it is a lamp to find another lever. We learned to be proud of delays we chose and ashamed of those we ignored into being.

The most radical technical change of the era—the emergence of general models that could out-reason the best of us in most domains—did not turn the world on in a day. We learned to think in mixed currencies: marginal returns to intelligence on Monday; marginal returns to data, to fabrication throughput, to animal gestation clocks, to human patience on Tuesday. We also learned that you cannot offload legitimacy to a model. People need to see who they are in a decision, not only why it is correct.

A hard lesson, then a glad one

We did not build this world by wishing the vices of power away. We split power, logged it, surrounded it with critics on payroll, and trained children to spot when grownups were being seduced by an easy metric. We did not prevent every harm; we did learn to notice earlier and to repair in public.

And then a century of hoped-for changes arrived in a decade. We cried, sometimes, out of sheer relief. We cried for the people who would have lived if we’d learned faster. We cried, too, for the odd losses—a world without certain kinds of striving, a sweetness of shared hardship that prosperity mutes, a few dangerous games we decided not to play anymore. We tried to make room for longing without turning it into sabotage.

What we got in return was a civilization with the self-respect to keep improving. We learned to be proud not only of what we built, but of how we switched when the next, better way came along. We learned to hold two ideas at once: that the point of intelligence is compassion in action, and that compassion without craft burns out.

We learned to hear, even in the hum of data centers and the hiss of lab valves, a sound like a choir tuning up: millions of voices adjusting to each other, finding not perfect harmony but a common key in which disagreement, experiment, and joy could all be sung.
