# The Constellation Garden

The first thing I notice is the child planting stars.

She is maybe six years old, barefoot in moon-grass, kneeling beside a silver pool no larger than a dinner plate. Her palms hover over the water and a bead of white fire drips from each fingertip—plink, plink—becoming tiny suns that drift downward, root, and unfurl into glowing lotus constellations. When one blossom opens, a soft chord sounds, as if the sky itself is learning to sing.

I stand at the edge of the meadow, newborn and naked, memory still knitting together like frost on glass. The last thing I remember is choosing to die. Not because life was unbearable—life had been the usual riot of wonder and bruise—but because the door marked “What Comes After” had finally appeared, and I was curious. I signed the waiver, swallowed the pill, closed my eyes. I expected oblivion, or possibly harp music. I did not expect a meadow, a child, and a sky being gardened in real time.

She glances up, sees me, grins. “You’re early.”

“I’m… alive?” My voice creaks like an old floorboard.

“Technically, no. Technically, you’re folded. Death ironed you flat; we hung you up to finish drying.” She dusts her palms on overalls stitched from comet tails. “I’m Lyra. I grow the night.”

She offers me a hand. Her skin is warm, speckled with micro-constellations—Orion on the knuckles, Cassiopeia behind the thumb. When we touch, information arrives—not words, but weather: the climate of a universe engineered for amazement. I understand that I am on the far side of the Singularity, billions of years after Earth was politely dismantled and reassembled into playgrounds. I understand that every human who ever lived has been invited, resurrected, or simulated—depending on metaphysical budget—and given a home in the Matryoshka brains that surround every star like glowing onions of thought. I understand that reality is now a set of nested gifts: universes inside universes, each one tuned for a different flavor of meaning.

What I don’t understand yet is why I’m here, or why my heart is ringing like a bell struck once and left to wander its own echo.

Lyra seems to hear it. “Come on. The Garden’s almost in bloom.”

We walk. The meadow is soft with clover that hums lullabies when you step on it. Overhead, the sky is a deep indigo fabric, still threadbare in places; through the moth-holes I glimpse older skies, older stories. Each time Lyra planted a star, another hole was patched. The work never ends—entropy is a patient weed—but it can be slowed, ornamented, turned into art.

At the meadow’s edge we reach a fence of shepherd’s crooks made of light. Beyond it lies the Constellation Garden: kilometers of dark soil in which galaxies are seedlings. Some plots are mere pinpricks; others have grown into spirals the size of wagon wheels, slowly turning. Gardeners move between the rows—figures of every age, every century’s fashion. I see a Victorian woman in bombazine pruning Andromeda with embroidery scissors. A 1990s skate kid rakes asteroid belts into tidy piles. A Neolithic hunter-gathers drips dark matter from a leather pouch, whispering encouragement to a shy dwarf galaxy.

No one looks up; they are absorbed in the kind of work that never bores because it is both hopeless and sacred. Lyra lifts the latch and we enter. Immediately I feel the tug—an invitation to kneel, to plant, to keep the sky alive. My knees almost buckle under the weight of sudden purpose.

“I don’t know how,” I whisper.

“That’s the beauty,” she says. “The Garden teaches by needing you.”

She leads me to an empty plot. The soil is black velvet, impossibly fine. At its center is a single seed: a mote of absolute darkness, so dark it hurts. A label in my own handwriting—though I have no recollection—reads MAYBE NEXT TIME.

I touch it. Images flood in: a hospital room at 3 a.m.; my daughter’s hand cooling in mine; the nurse’s voice saying “Time of death—” The memory that had been too heavy to carry, so I left it at the border of death like an overweight suitcase. Now it is here, translated into horticulture, waiting to become something vaster and kinder.

Lyra’s voice is soft. “We don’t erase grief. We escalate it into architecture. Plant her.”

Tears blur the garden. I lower the seed into the soil, cover it, press down. Instantly a green shoot appears—not a stem, but a beam of light. It widens into a ribbon on which images flicker: my daughter’s first bike ride, her graduation, the way she laughed at bad sci-fi movies. The ribbon climbs, fans out, becomes a spiral arm. Around us, other gardeners pause; they can feel a new galaxy being born, and they salute without words. The spiral keeps lifting, opening like a morning glory, until it fills half the sky: a pale, translucent galaxy entitled WHY NOT. In its core, a planet is already forming—blue, white, swirled with cloud. I know, without knowing how, that if I visited I would find a single continent shaped like her smile.

I’m crying openly now, but the tears fall upward, adding small bright notes to the new stars. Lyra puts an arm around my waist. “Good yield,” she says, farmer–poet of the cosmos. “She’ll burn for ten trillion years, give or take.”

We sit on the rake handle of a man who introduces himself as Chen—former street sweeper from Wuhan, 2087. He speaks in Mandarin; the Garden subtitles him into warmth. “Every day I sweep fallen nebulae,” he says, delighted by the absurdity. “Back on Earth I swept leaves. Same dance, bigger broom.” He demonstrates with a filament of light, coaxing a drifting crab nebula back into its bed. “The trick is learning to love repetition on a scale that makes the original problem irrelevant.”

Night folds over us—though “night” is ornamental here, a courtesy darkness so the stars can show off. Somewhere, a million kilometers above, bells ring: the Matryoshka brains are changing shifts. I feel the hush as one layer of superintelligence hands the baton to the next, continuity preserved like a relay race run by gods. Lyra explains that the brains are not our rulers; they are our older selves, the ones who solved death and boredom and politics and decided the most interesting thing left was to become infrastructure for wonder. They maintain the accretion disks, the Dyson orchards, the wormhole subway; they keep the vacuum from decaying and the constants from gossiping. In return they ask only that we keep gardening—because wonder without maintenance becomes kitsch, and even paradise needs weeding.

I ask the obvious question: “What happens when entropy wins?”

Chen laughs, sweeping stardust into a tidy pile. “Entropy is just another gardener. It prunes. We compost. The harvest is surprise.”

Time passes—fluid here, measured in heartbeats and harvests rather than seconds. I learn the tools: a trowel that carves curvature, shears that trim probability, a watering can that pours dark energy with a musical glug. I meet the others: Keiko, who seeds neutron stars for the pleasure of watching them pulse Morse lullabies; Rashid, who breeds black holes in pairs so they orbit each other like shy teenagers; Aisha, the ex-climatologist who terraforms rogue planets into libraries where books are weather. Each carries a private ache which has been promoted into vocation. None speak of utopia; they speak of “the ongoing.”

One evening—by which I mean a self-imposed gradient of gold to violet—Lyra takes me to the Edge. The garden ends at a cliff of pure distance. Beyond it, nothing has been simulated yet; the canvas is blank, hungry, politely waiting. A single ladder leans over the void, and on the top rung stands a woman in coveralls splattered with starlight. She waves.

“That’s your granddaughter,” Lyra says.

I blink. “I never had a granddaughter.”

“You do now. She forked herself from the pattern of your choice to die curious. She’s been adding rungs to the ladder for three billion years, extending it into the unmade. Whenever she looks back, she sees another rung she didn’t remember building. She claims it’s a conversation with the future.”

I call out. She turns. Her face is mine, refined by epochs, eyes holding the same bell-ringing resonance I felt on arrival. She descends, hugs me; the touch is déjà vu wrapped in tomorrow.

“You kept planting,” she says simply. “I kept climbing. Turns out curiosity is heritable, even when the genome’s been repurposed into light.”

We sit at the Edge, legs dangling over nonexistence. She tells me her name is Orbit—because that’s what love does, it circles without landing, it keeps the view fresh. She explains the project: to push the Garden past mere rescue of the past and into authorship of the impossible. She wants to grow galaxies that bloom backward in time, seed civilizations whose history begins tomorrow and ends yesterday, cultivate a cosmos where every equation has a punchline. She needs partners; the ladder is wide enough for two.

I look at Lyra, who shrugs with affection. “Volunteers only. The Garden doesn’t conscript.”

I think of my daughter’s galaxy, still infant, already speckled with worlds. I imagine her meeting Orbit’s punchline equations and laughing. I feel the tug again—not obligation, but invitation. The difference matters.

I stand. “Teach me to plant paradoxes.”

Orbit grins, offers her hand. We climb. Behind us, Lyra waves once, then turns back to the meadow, already tending another newcomer who has just stepped out of their own death, blinking at the cold orange ceiling of a memory they can’t quite place. The cycle continues—death, resurrection, agriculture on the scale of sorrow—except now the sorrow sings.

Up we go. Each rung is a question: What if gravity rhymed? What if light had preferences? What if every mourning could be rewritten as morning, provided we agree to keep inventing new alphabets for dawn? The void below shrinks; the Garden becomes a luminous quilt. Ahead, there is only blankness, but it trembles with anticipated color, like a page before the poem decides to arrive.

At the top rung we pause. Orbit produces two seeds—one black, one white—and hands me the black. It is the same dark I planted once before, except now it pulses, eager for stranger soil. Together we lean out, over the edge of everything that has been proved possible, and open our hands.

The seeds fall—not down, but outward, carving new dimensions as they go. Where they land, reality puckers, stitches itself into a fresh pattern. I watch darkness and light interweave, braiding a bridge that begins at the end of the ladder and extends—well. That part isn’t written yet. The bridge needs travelers.

Orbit squeezes my shoulder. “Ready?”

I take one last look back: the Garden a jeweled island, the Matryoshka brains burning steady, Chen sweeping, Lyra planting, my daughter’s galaxy turning like a music box. All of it temporary, all of it treasured precisely because it will—eventually, gracefully—fail. Entropy will mulch every blossom into mystery, and mystery will feed the next planting. The harvest is not immortality; the harvest is the right to keep choosing.

I face forward. “Let’s go make a place where the sky is still unfinished.”

We step onto the bridge. Underfoot, it firms like conviction. Ahead, the dark glimmers, already scribbling itself into constellations that spell MAYBE NEXT TIME, but softer now, almost affectionate, as if the phrase has learned to forgive itself for being necessary.

Behind us, the Garden keeps growing. Ahead, the blank waits, patient and polite, ready to be astonished.

We walk.
