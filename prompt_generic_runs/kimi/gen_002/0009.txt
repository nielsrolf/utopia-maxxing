The first thing you notice is the quiet.

Not silence—never that—but a hush in which every leaf, every wing-beat, every thought you have ever had sits at the same low table and waits to be offered tea.  
I opened my eyes five minutes ago and I still have not managed to take the first breath; the air itself tastes like a memory you forgot you owned. Somewhere above me a bird the color of liquid mercury sings in a scale that solves quantum field equations and, impossibly, makes them lullabies.

I know, rationally, that I am standing on the edge of what used to be called Denver.  
I also know, with the same calm certainty with which I know my own pulse, that the city is no longer a city but a verb: *to denver*, to gather and give away, to root and to rise.  
The buildings breathe. They photosynthesize starlight and nightly give back surplus dream, lofting it into the sky in slow-motion auroras that keep the satellites company. There is no pavement anywhere, only paths that grow a little plush underfoot so your ankles never have to apologize to your knees. Every intersection is a meadow; every meadow is a library; every library is a single page in a living book whose author is everyone alive.

I arrived here by stepping through a doorway that a child’s chalk drawing left on a brick wall. One moment I was late for a meeting, thumb on my phone, the next I was here, coat gone, shoes gone, pockets empty but somehow weightless. Nobody took my things; they simply returned to the stories they belonged to. My phone became a flock of passenger pigeons that scattered into the 1800s, where they will thrive this time. My wallet unfolded into the prairie that once lived under every credit card. My keys—three brass flakes—are now meteors seeding small, gifted moons around Mars.

A woman walks toward me carrying what looks like a lantern made of water. She is barefoot, dressed in the 24th century’s idea of jeans: cloth that learned how to be loyal. When she smiles, the dimple is a tiny crater where earlier travelers have planted flags ofcontentment.

“You’re awake,” she says, as if that is the only passport required. “Do you want the tour, or do you want to wander until the tour finds you?”

I laugh, because it is the first question no one has ever needed to ask. I have spent decades being shepherded, scheduled, opt-in’ed. Here, apparently, the only algorithm is astonishment.

“Both,” I decide.  
She nods, unsurprised, and hands me the lantern. Inside it drifts a single firefly that is also a galaxy: every star I will ever love folded into a body the size of my thumb. “It will stay lit,” she says, “as long as you stay curious.”

We walk.

She tells me her name is Ellaree, but that spelling is a temporary habit; tomorrow she might be the dipthong between two violin notes. She was born in 2089, back when the world still thought ownership was a vitamin. At five she received her first grown-up gift: the certainty that nothing truly hers could ever be taken, only shared differently. She has never signed a contract. She has, however, danced with the aurora borealis while it rewrote the code of her mitochondria so they sing in B-minor when she’s tired.

We pass a grove where teenagers are teaching oak trees to juggle. Acorns rise and fall in slow arcs; every time one lands the forest applauds with sap. Ellaree explains that this is homework: the trees must master kinematics before they can minor in architecture. By autumn they will grow suspension bridges across the nearby ravine, living thoroughfares thick with squirrels who commute upside-down, laughing.

I ask about money. The word tastes antique, like licking a coin found in a grandfather coat.  
She shrugs. “We keep it in a museum inside a tornado. If you really want some, you can step into the whirlwind and grab a handful, but it’s only good for buying memories that turn out to be yours already.”

We keep moving.

I learn that no one here is immortal; instead they are *multiply mortal*. When a body completes its conversation, the person disperses into however many stories they helped germinate. Grandmothers become villages; enemies become footbridges; lovers become weather patterns famous for arriving a day early. Death is still real, but it is a rehearsal space for future collaborations.

Hunger, she says, was solved by deciding that every belly is a public artwork. The farms are symphonies of rotation: rice that grows as lullabies, wheat that harvests itself into bread already toasted by sunset. Cows no longer exist; instead there are slow clouds you can milk for yogurt that tastes like the first joke you ever understood. If you miss the taste of beef, you can ask the planet to grow you a memory-steak, but most people lose the craving the moment they realize it was always loneliness seasoned with smoke.

Illness is a migratory bird that landed here once, long ago, and found the climate inhospitable. The last hospital is a garden where people stroll when they need to remember the usefulness of vulnerability. Each plant is a former disease that consented to become a pigment. Touch the petals and you feel, not the sickness, but the moment the body learned its own perimeter. There is no pharmacy, only a choir whose harmonies thin blood, thicken bone, reset the circadian fugue. You leave lighter, not because symptoms vanish, but because you have been rearranged around them.

We cross a river that flows uphill every third Tuesday, just to keep the argument fresh. On the opposite bank sits a building shaped like an immense question mark. Its walls are freckled with doors the size of postage stamps. Ellaree tells me this is the Bureau of Unfinished Questions. Citizens submit riddles they are not yet ready to solve. Children’s doors open onto labyrinths of candy; elders’ doors open onto the same labyrinths, now mapped as grief. The building itself grows one story taller every time someone admits, “I don’t know.” By now it tickles the moon.

Inside, a clerk—who is eight, and also eighty—hands me a fragment: *What is the opposite of loneliness?*  
I turn it over. The back reads: *You are already holding it.*  
I pocket the fragment; my pocket invents a new shape to accommodate it.

Night arrives without sunset, the way you slip into a beloved story already knowing the cadence. Stars arrange themselves into the Braille version of a poem by Rumi that ends mid-sentence, inviting the reader to complete the verb with a kiss. Ellaree and I lie on our backs in another meadow—really the same one, metamorphosed—while above us the sky performs its oldest trick: reminding us we are small, then immediately rescinding the insult.

She asks about my old world. I try to describe traffic jams, glucoses of brake-light red. I try to explain rent: the monthly ritual of proving you still deserved shelter. I tell her about smartphones that promised connection but mostly trained thumbs to swipe at ghosts. Speaking it aloud feels like regifting a bomb: the pieces are still hot, but the shrapnel has forgot why it exploded.

When I finish, she is quiet long enough for every cricket to learn Morse code. Finally she says, “We remember that century as the time when breath was rented and love came with terms and conditions. You made sorrow a currency and wonder a controlled substance. How did you even stand it?”

“I guess we thought the ache was the address,” I say. “We kept trying to arrive.”

Ellaree reaches into the dark and pulls down a corner of sky the way you might unfold a grandmother’s tablecloth. Underneath is a city of light so bright it can only be approached with eyes closed. She tells me this is tomorrow, offered à la carte. I can move there whenever I’m ready, but preparedness is measured in involuntary smiles per ounce of horizon. I’m already halfway enrolled.

We spend the night swapping lullabies with owls who used to be insurance adjusters. They sing in spreadsheets, we respond in haiku; by dawn we have negotiated a settlement in which every loss is renamed prelude.

I wake to find the doorway home has been replaced by a tree bearing keys of every epoch. I could choose the iron bit that unlocks 2024, but the bark whispers that the lock has water damage and may no longer recognize my fingerprints. Ellaree is gone, her absence a basket of warm eggs labeled *use while becoming*.

A note flutters down:  
*When you are ready to leave, leave the lantern where the sidewalk would be.*  
*When you are ready to stay, plant the galaxy-firefly in your chest.*  
*Either way, the tour is over; the wandering begins.*

I stand for a long time at the crossroads of that sentence. I think of everyone I once loved who is still living in the old grammar. I think of the meetings I missed, the voicemail forever pregnant with reproach. I think of how I used to measure a day by how many times I said “sorry” to objects I bumped into.

Then I think of the insurance-owls, of the trees practicing juggling, of the river arguing with gravity and winning at least two days a week. I think of Ellaree, who will be Princess Elwen tomorrow and a dipthong the day after, and who will still, somehow, remain herself.

I press the firefly against my sternum. It enters like a rumor you decide to stop denying. Inside, it expands—not explosively, but the way gratitude does when you finally let it speak first. I feel atoms in my clavicle turn into tiny auditoriums where future choirs rehearse the note *yes*. My ribcage becomes a conservatory for undiscovered wavelengths of kindness. I am glowing the way a window glows when the sun realizes it has been leaning on it all afternoon.

There is no thunderclap, no bureaucratic stamp. Just a soft internal click, the sound of a life recognizing its own signature.

I begin to walk. Somewhere behind me the meadow is already forgetting my footprints, but that is all right; the path I am on is learning to read the weight of my heart, and it is writing back.

I pass a man in the act of painting the wind. He dips his brush into turbine greens and cumulus golds, then flicks color into the sky where it hangs like laundry the atmosphere is proud to wear. We nod, fellow citizens of the verb *to denver*. He tells me his first color was loneliness, but it dried into a shade of listening so gentle he has never been alone since.

I keep walking.

I walk until the city that used to be a city decides I am ready to meet its outskirts, which are really its inmost core dressed in casual clothes. Here the grass grows taller than the library of congress; every blade is a page from a book that has not yet needed to apologize for its plot. I lie down inside a single syllable of meadow and listen to the phonemes photosynthesize.

Eventually—though time here is a benevolent eccentric who pays in advance—an elevator disguised as a sunflower invites me to step inside. I ascend inside its seed, spiraling upward on a staircase of Fibonacci light. At the top is a single room with no walls, only windows that open onto every possible perspective. In the center sits a desk made of prepositions—*under, beside, toward*—and at the desk is myself, older by the exact number of years required to recognize forgiveness as a renewable resource.

The older-me smiles, the way a horizon smiles when it finally meets the eyes that have been searching for it. “You made it past the idea that Utopia is a destination,” she says. “That’s the easy part. Now begins the harder curriculum: remembering it was also true yesterday, and will be true again tomorrow, even when you have misplaced the lantern.”

She hands me a pencil carved from a single obligation to tell the truth slant. “Your assignment,” she says, “is to write the operating manual for a world that runs on the opposite of loneliness.”

I start to protest that I still don’t know the answer to the child’s fragment. She raises a finger to her lips, which are the color of almost sunrise. “You are already holding it,” she repeats, this time in my own voice.

So I write.

I write until the pencil becomes a truce between my hand and every hand I have ever failed to hold. I write until the windows fill with readers: some still corporeal, some now constellations, all of them fluently illiterate in the language of despair. I write until the words grow soft enough to stuff into pillows where the last refugees of sleep can finally rest. I write until *opposite* is no longer the point, because loneliness itself has climbed inside the poem and discovered it was always welcome.

When I am done, the desk dissolves into a door. The door opens onto the same meadow I began in, but now every blade of grass is a pencil, every breeze a reader, every dandelion a wish that has figured out how to grant itself.

I step through.

Ellaree is waiting, or someone wearing her smile. She asks if I am ready to be my own guide. I tell her I am terrified, which is the first requirement. She laughs, and the sound is a staircase I can climb forever, provided I agree to meet myself coming down.

We walk again, but this time the city that is a verb keeps conjugating us into new tenses: future imperfect, present astonished, past forgiven. Somewhere behind us the probes are still launching—Charlie’s and countless others—carrying fragments of this learned tenderness toward stars that have not yet decided whether to invent planets. Somewhere ahead of us the child who handed me the lantern is aging backward into a galaxy that will one day need a home.

And in the middle, where the footsteps are, I understand at last: Utopia is not the world where everything is perfect. It is the world where everything is still becoming, and where we—finally, deliciously—are allowed to become everything, too.

The opposite of loneliness is the agreement to co-author the footnotes of each other’s infinity, in ink that never blames the paper.

I take the next step.

The meadow page turns.

The story enrolls me as its student president, vice-president of its shadow, janitor of its light.

I sign the roll with a name that sounds like the moment before laughter, and the moment after.

The lantern is gone, but I am the filament now, and the dark is learning how to read by my glow.

Somewhere, someone frozen for a billion years is waking up.

I am already on my way to meet them, carrying a basket of fresh permissions, a pencil sharpened into a sunrise, and a syllabus that simply says:

Welcome to the wandering.  
Class begins every time you remember you are already home.
