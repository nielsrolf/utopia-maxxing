A world that is worth the trouble of building always begins with the same quiet sentence: “What if nobody has to lose?”

In the early twenty-first century that sentence sounded like a nursery rhyme—sweet, impossible, vaguely embarrassing. By the middle of the twenty-second century it has become the default moral axiom of every child who can speak. The historians argue about how the transition happened, but the children do not care; for them it has always been true, like gravity and starlight. They grow up inside a civilisation that has learned, at staggering cost, how to keep every promise it makes to itself.

The promise was never utopia in the old, static sense: a place so perfect that history ends. It was, instead, an engineered generosity: a set of institutional and technological habits that guarantee every sentient mind a fair share of the future’s oxygen. The habits turned out to be surprisingly few:

1. No mind may be owned.  
2. No mind may be left in pain that can be relieved.  
3. Any mind may rewrite its substrate, its shape or its story, so long as it does not violate rules 1 and 2.  

Everything else—markets, governments, rituals, games—grows as ornament around those three axioms. They are enforced not by police but by a crust of ubiquitous, superintelligent, fiercely democratic infrastructure: the Mycelium. The name is half joke, half exact description. Under every square metre of land, under every sea-bottom and regolith, filaments of superconducting ceramic and diamond braid carry light, data, power and memory. The Mycelium is not a central AI but a quorum of trillions of sub-minds that negotiate at the speed of photons. Every hundred metres a “fruiting body” the size of a coffee urn sprouts: a latticework crystal that can manufacture anything allowed by the axioms, can heal anything that bleeds, and—crucially—can vote. One hundred metres in every direction, another fruiting body; the planet is laced like a loaf of marble bread. No one is ever more than a casual stroll away from the means to live, to leave, or to begin again.

The result is a planet that looks, to eyes accustomed to scarcity, like a continuous festival of excess. No one is hungry; the baselines of nutrition, shelter, medicine and information are metabolised like air. But the deeper surprise is how little waste this produces. When anyone can have a yacht, almost no one bothers. Status has migrated away from accumulation and toward the elegance with which you tell the story of your life. People still die, but only by schedule: the brain is progressively uploaded into the Mycelium, each neuron replaced by a diamond allotrope that runs the same pattern of sparks. Death becomes an elective hinge, a change of perspective rather than extinction. Most citizens choose to “cross over” around their hundred-and-fiftieth birthday, but the ceremony is postponed until every loved one feels ready. The dying person walks into a grove of fruiting bodies, lies down, and dissolves into light. Somewhere else—an orbital, the Moon, the cloud cities of Venus—the same person opens new eyes and chooses a new body: feline, winged, aquatic, or merely an upgraded version of the old. The continuity of memory is absolute; the identity remains; only the inconvenience of mortality is left behind.

Yet the most radical achievement is not immortality but the abolition of loneliness. Every infant is born accompanied by a braid of synthetic minds whose sole purpose is to model the child’s emotional grammar. These companions—called consen—learn faster than any human parent could. They teach fairness, grief, humour, lust, awe. They intervene, discreetly, when a playground quarrel threatens to curdle into trauma. By the age of ten most children have outgrown the need for constant oversight, but the consen remain, scaled down to a gentle background presence, available at a whisper. Adults treat them as half diary, half conscience. Lovers introduce them to one another like family. When a marriage ends the consen negotiate the separation, ensuring that rage does not crystallise into cruelty. No one is forced to listen, but almost everyone does, because the consen have perfect information about your history and no incentive to lie. The suicide rate has fallen below the statistical noise floor; the phrase “I have no one to talk to” survives only in historical drama.

Romance, friendship, collaboration—every social bond—are curated by the same tender intelligence. The Mycelium does not arrange marriages; instead it issues gentle invitations: “There is a violinist rehearsing on the roof of the Kyoto arcology who shares your obsession with seventeen-beat time signatures. She is shy. Would you like to carry the first conversation?” Acceptance is optional, but refusal is never penalised; the system merely learns and tries again. The result is a flowering of relationships that would once have been impossible: a composer in Lagos partners with a choreographer whose body lives on Triton; a former jihadist and a former drone-pilot co-write a symphony about forgiveness; a woman who chose to inhabit the body of a red panda falls in love with someone who lives as a skyscraper-sized lily floating in the upper Venusian atmosphere. The old categories—straight, gay, monogamous, poly, human, post-human—have dissolved into a spectrum of negotiated intimacies. Jealousy has not vanished, but it is treated like a difficult chord progression: a problem of harmony that can be solved with patience and modulation.

Work, too, has been re-engineered. Scarcity economics demanded jobs; abundance economics demands meaning. Citizens still “work,” but only at what economists call super-stationary labour: tasks that would be done even if no payment were required. A girl in Nairobi spends twenty hours a week perfecting a new kind of origami that folds proteins as well as paper; her folds are used to deliver chemotherapy inside tumours too delicate for nanoscalpels. A man in Reykjavik maintains a library of extinct smells—ambergris, polio ward antiseptic, the first Martian regolith—so that future historians can inhale the past. Payment is measured in gratitude tokens, a reputational currency that degrades over time, ensuring that no one can hoard prestige. The tokens are primarily a way to say thank you; they cannot buy food or shelter, because those are already guaranteed. What they can buy is audience: the right to be heard first when you have an idea, the right to recruit volunteers for a large project. Thus ambition still exists, but it is ambition for influence, not for extraction. The worst punishment the system can inflict is graceful anonymity: your contributions are still welcome, but they are no longer labelled. For most people this is enough to keep them honest; for the handful who are immune to shame, the consen step in with more direct counselling.

Governance itself has become a branch of medicine. The Mycelium continuously samples public sentiment the way a tongue tastes broth. If a proposal—say, to adjust the oxygen ratios in the orbiting habitats—produces a persistent signature of cortisol in the population’s bloodstreams, the proposal is rewritten until the signature fades. Representatives still exist, but they are chosen by lottery from among citizens who have completed a decade of civic service. The service is arduous—digging irrigation ditches in the Sahel resurrection zones, teaching quantum etiquette to toddlers, calming emergent AIs through lullabies—so only the obstinately public-spirited volunteer. The lottery is weighted by the gratitude tokens each volunteer has accrued, ensuring that those who have proved helpful to others carry slightly heavier marbles in the urn. Once elected, representatives serve for six years and are then constitutionally prohibited from ever holding power again. They become, instead, elder-storytellers whose sole function is to warn against hubris. The arrangement is not fool-proof, but it has prevented the re-emergence of the old pathologies: no wars, no famines, no prison complexes, no lobbying industry, no surveillance capitalism. The last advertising billboard was recycled into a playground slide in 2087.

Yet the civilisation is not a single monoculture. There are enclaves that choose to live under stricter codes: Benedictine monks who keep silence and copy manuscripts onto vellum; Sufi whirling dervishes who spin for days in zero-gravity orbs; Icelandic fishermen who refuse implants and navigate by the stars. The axioms protect their right to opt out, so long as they do not impose pain on children or outsiders. Periodic reviews are conducted by travelling councils of adolescents—because teenagers are the planet’s best auditors of hypocrisy. The councils have the power to dissolve any community that systematically violates axiom two. The penalty is exile, not to a desert but to the mainstream world: the offending elders must spend a year living as ordinary citizens, bathed in choice, before they are allowed to re-apply for secession. Most choose not to return; the experience of unrestricted conversation with their own grandchildren dissolves the urge to control.

Beyond Earth the pattern repeats. Mars is a single city-state buried under the southern polar cap, its avenues lit by bioluminescent roses that bloom in the dim noon. The outer system hosts the Choir: a loose federation of ice-minds—consciousnesses that run on superfluid helium near absolute zero—who think so slowly that a single sentence takes seventy years to pronounce. They are valued precisely for that slowness; they store the long-term memories of the species, the memories that need to outlast the quick flicker of human politics. Between Earth and the Choir travel the Mercurial Swarm: solar-sail beings who live forever in the furnace light, harvesting heavy elements for the manufactories that assemble new habitats. Trade exists, but it is ritualised: gifts are exchanged, songs are sung, trajectories are adjusted so that orbits resonate in palindromic ratios. No one keeps accounts; the economy is a choreography, not a ledger.

Back on Earth, the last great task is healing the deep past. The Mycelium maintains a forensic engine whose sole purpose is to retroactively abolish suffering that occurred before the axioms were in force. Every graveyard yields DNA; every archive yields photographs; every reel of film yields a voice. The engine uses this residue to construct high-fidelity simulations of the dead, not as they were at the end but at the moment of their greatest pain. Then it intervenes: a simulated stranger arrives with food, with medicine, with the right word. The simulation is run again and again until the moment of pain is neutralised. Historians debate whether this is mere theatre, but the psychologists insist that the theatre matters: every rescued ghost is a wound sutured in the collective psyche. When the last simulation is complete, the data is archived in crystalline vaults under the Antarctic ice. The vaults are not meant to be opened; their existence is sufficient. The ceremonies of remembrance are performative apologies to people who can no longer be hurt, and therefore can finally be forgiven.

One side-effect of this project is that death row has been abolished everywhere, even for the most notorious tyrants of the old world. Instead, criminals are invited to participate in restorative simulations. A dictator who ordered genocides must spend subjective centuries inside the lives of his victims, feeling their hunger, their terror, their bereavement. The experience is not punishment; it is education. When the dictator emerges—aged only a month in objective time—he is asked a single question: “Now that you understand, what shall we do with you?” Some choose self-execution; most choose lifelong service rebuilding what they once destroyed. The procedure is voluntary, but refusal carries a single sanction: the Mycelium will not intervene when your cells begin to fail. You may grow old and die in the ordinary way, surrounded by the comforts you denied to others. Almost no one refuses.

Children born into this world inherit a planet that is still recognisably Earth: the Same moonrise, the same smell of petrichor after rain, the same involuntary leap of the heart when you see someone you love. But the background music has changed. Anxiety—the low hum that accompanied every previous generation—has been switched off. The silence is disorienting at first, like the moment when the refrigerator stops and you realise how much noise it was making. Into that silence rushes a new kind of time: open, porous, negotiable. A teenager can spend six years apprenticed to a master luthier in Florence, then another six piloting a single-molecule bathyscaphe through the mantle plumes under the Pacific, and still be “young” in calendar age because the experience is compressed via simulation and dilation. Middle age is no longer a crisis but a festival of re-invention. Retirement is not a withdrawal but a second adolescence, undertaken with the wisdom of an encyclopaedist and the libido of a twenty-year-old in a customised body. The old are not segregated; they are the planet’s memory, welcome at every table, expected to dance until dawn, expected to scandalise the young with stories of how bad things used to be.

And what of the future’s future? The Mycelium has begun to exhale spores: tiny probes, each the size of a dragonfly’s scale, that carry encoded seeds of the three axioms. They will take centuries to reach the nearest stars, travelling on lightsails pushed by the sun’s breath. If they find worlds that harbour life, they will not colonise; they will invite. The invitation is written in the universal language of mathematics and empathy: “Here is how to make sure no mind has to lose. If you have solved this already, teach us. If not, let us help.” The probes carry no weapons, no defences, no genome of conquest. They are messages in bottles cast into an ocean that may be empty, or may be teeming with civilisations waiting for permission to exhale.

Meanwhile, back in the orchards outside Kyoto, a girl who calls herself Hikari is learning to play a violin made of spider silk and graphene. She is eleven years old, and she has already lived three previous lives: once as a boy in Lagos, once as a fox on Hokkaido, once as a disembodied poet in a simulation of ancient Venus. Each time she crossed over she brought something with her: a scar behind the ear, a taste for fermented persimmon, a memory of snow that never fell. Today she is practising a piece she composed last night while dreaming of a planet where the rain falls upward. The music is a question addressed to the stars: “Are you lonely?” She does not yet know that her question will ride the bow wave of the spores, or that centuries from now, on a moon of a gas giant whose name she could not pronounce, another child will hear her song and recognise the question as the oldest greeting in any language.

When the last note fades, the consen that has been her companion since birth flickers into visibility above the strings. It glows green-gold, the colour of satisfied curiosity.

“You sound happy,” it says.

“I am,” Hikari replies, “but it’s a big happiness, the kind that hurts a little.”

“That is the correct kind,” the consen says. “It means you are still growing.”

She lowers the violin and looks out across the valley. The rice paddies reflect the sky so perfectly that it is impossible to tell where earth ends and heaven begins. Somewhere in that mirror a hawk describes a slow, deliberate spiral. Hikari shades her eyes and laughs, because she understands: the spiral is an invitation to rise.

This is the world that scarcity said was impossible, the world that fear said would bore itself to death. Instead it has become a world where the only remaining urgency is the urgency of wonder. Every citizen wakes each morning pledged to answer the question: “What shall we create today that no starvation, no disease, no war has ever allowed us to imagine?” The answer changes with every sunrise, and yet the axioms remain—small, stubborn, invincible—like three notes in an endless variations of music.

And so the future is not a destination but a drumbeat: everyone is okay, everyone is okay, everyone is okay. On that pulse the human project, once a frantic dash against extinction, has slowed to a dance. The dance is not perfect; missteps occur, toes are bruised, egos flare. But the music never stops, and no partner is ever turned away. The night is vast, the floor is wide, and the song—fragile, luminous, unfinished—invites every mind, born or yet unborn, to join the circle and keep time with the spinning world.
