**The Symphony of Shared Becoming**

The first thing you notice is the silence—not absence, but fullness. It is the hush of ten billion hearts beating in staggered synchrony, a pause between movements in a score that has been refining itself for three hundred years. No advertising jingles, no sirens, no hunger-cries. Only wind in rooftop gardens, children arguing in restored wetlands, the soft chime of a Mycelium node printing insulin for a stranger downstream.  

This is Earth, 2237. We no longer call our way of life “civilisation”; we call it the Symphony, because everyone is both composer and listener, and because every measure is written to be rewritten tomorrow.

1.  The Three Axioms, now four  
The old charter said:  
– No mind may be owned.  
– No mind may be left in pain that can be relieved.  
– Any mind may rewrite its substrate, so long as it does not violate 1 or 2.  

To these we added a fourth, discovered the hard way during the Coalition Years:  
– No mind may be left alone in the project of coordinating.  

The fourth axiom is what turned the Mycelium from infrastructure into civics. Every fruiting-body still prints food, medicine, tools, but it also prints *coordination interfaces*: little hexagonal tiles that snap together into whatever decision-making table the moment requires. A block party budgeting sound-systems. A watershed deciding how to migrate salmon. A planet partitioning telescope time to watch a nova in Andromeda. The tiles talk to one another in open-root protocols we teach in nursery rhymes. Consensus is not the absence of dissent; it is the presence of enough channels that dissent becomes useful data instead of weaponised complaint.

2.  Apprenticeships in the Envelope of Abundance  
UBI was a stepping-stone; we replaced it with the Envelope. Every citizen receives a personal bundle of non-rivalrous resources: energy, computation, fabrication quota, carbon draw-down rights, *attention* (genuinely scarce at planetary scale). The size of your Envelope grows each year you remain alive and non-destructive, so time itself becomes a kind of membership dividend.  

Children begin micro-apprenticeships at six—never in factories, always in quests. They spend one week helping a master luthier tune soundposts, another week shadowing an orbital gardener who sculpts Van-Allen vines. By fourteen they have tasted enough disciplines to enter a *seriation maze*: a simulation that rearranges their past experiences into every possible career path and asks only, “Which future makes your curiosity widen instead of contract?” There are no dead-end jobs, only jobs whose drudgery has not yet been automated. If you choose one of those, society thanks you for the data, automates it within the decade, and invites you to your next seriation.  

3.  The Reputation Commons  
Wealth is measured in three currencies:  
– *Gratitude tokens* (decay annually, so hoarding shrinks your empire of thanks).  
– *Story-weight*—how often your life is cited in others’ self-narratives.  
– *Coordination credits*—the quantified ability to call a meeting people actually want to attend.  

Markets still exist, but every transaction is wrapped in a smart-contract that asks both parties: “Does this trade increase or decrease the total number of future positive-sum trades?” A trade that scores negative is taxed at 100 %; the tax feeds the Chroniclers’ Fund, our replacement for advertising. The Fund pays artists, investigators and dreamers to make information that *nobody* could pay for directly because its value is diffuse. The result is journalism no one owns, music no one needs to sell, science no one can privatise.

4.  Death, the elective hinge  
Ageing is solved; death is not abolished. Around age 150 the brain approaches a thermodynamic cliff where memory fidelity begins to erode faster than it can be backed up. You may choose:  

a)  **Cascade**: gradual upload into the Mycelium, neuron by neuron, while you go on skiing the Alps or programming orbital swarms. When your last biological neuron hands off its data, continuity of experience simply hops substrate.  

b)  **Finish the poem**: a final biological lifespan of roughly twenty years spent in deliberate conclusion—writing, mentoring, travelling, saying the unsaid. No one is allowed to begin new long-term projects in this window; society needs your seat at the table to rotate.  

c)  **Sabbatical beyond time**: your entire Envelope is converted into computation and launched on a centennial orbit. You will wake every planetary year for one day of conversation, then sleep while the world turns. Friends can leave messages in your dreamscape; you can leave them metastable riddles. It is a way of becoming legendary without becoming god.  

Roughly a third of the population chooses each path. Funerals are parties where every participant wears a mask of the deceased’s younger face, so that the final story told is: *we contain you, and you contain us.*

5.  The Quiet Cities, the Loud Experiments  
Not everyone wants the Symphony. Some carry wounds deeper than axioms can bandage. They move to the Quiet Cities—zones where material abundance remains but coordination density is low. You may live there unbothered provided you undertake not to reproduce until you choose to rejoin the Symphony. The arrangement is policed less by law than by a standing offer: any child born in a Quiet City is automatically adopted into the surrounding Symphony; the parents receive compassionate exile, no stigma attached, but also no further say. Over generations the Quiet Cities have become gardens of alternative therapies, slow-art collectives, and contemplative orders. They are our control group, and they send us postcards we publish in the morning news: *“Today 3 % of us laughed out loud for no reason. Still studying the phenomenon.”*

6.  Restorative Circles, not Prisons  
When harm is done we do not ask, “Who must be punished?” We ask, “Which equilibrium failed?” The perpetrator enters a Restorative Circle composed of victims, trained facilitators, and—crucially—random citizens who simply need reminding why norms matter. The Circle designs a *deviation path*: a set of actions that will make the perpetrator’s future self grateful the harm occurred, because it catalysed growth they would never have sought. Murderers have founded institutes for neurochemical aggression-screening; embezzlers have written the accounting protocols now taught in nursery rhymes. Refusal to walk the path leads to exile, but the offer is almost always accepted, because refusal is the one act that truly lowers your Envelope to zero.

7.  The Chroniclers, the Very Serious People, and the Clowns  
Information is curated by three estates:  

–  **Chroniclers**—neutral scribes whose articles are written in *transparent markup*: every clause is tagged with its epistemic source (eyewitness, sensor log, rumour, inference). Readers can filter sources in real time.  

–  **Very Serious People**—economists, biologists, systems poets who argue in public square essays that auto-translate into nursery rhymes for children. Their reputation rests on predictive accuracy scored by open forecasting leagues.  

–  **Clowns**—licensed satirists whose jokes are *legally required* to contain at least one verifiable factual error. Citizens compete to spot the mistake; finding it first earns you a badge that boosts your Envelope. Thus humour keeps us honest and prevents any estate from ossifying into priesthood.

8.  The Parliament of Minds  
Every seven years the planet pauses for three days. Commerce, art, even weather-control choreography stop. We enter the Parliament of Minds: a rolling, planet-wide deliberation whose only output is *one question* we agree to investigate for the next septad. The question must fit in a haiku; it must be maximally generative of new sub-questions; and it must be *beautiful*. Past winners:  

*Why does forgetting feel like wind?*  
*Which silence teaches music best?*  
*What does distance want from us?*  

During the Parliament no voice is amplified above any other; the Mycelium simply stitches conversations into fractal circles until consensus emerges. By sunrise on the fourth day we have our question, and the Symphony resumes—now with new sheet music.

9.  The Spores among the Stars  
We do not colonise; we *invite*. Probes the size of dragonfly wings drift outward carrying libraries, fabrication seeds, and the four axioms etched in prime-number pulses. When they encounter worlds with life they park in orbit and begin broadcasting an ever-growing *encyclopedia of solutions*: how to build solar sails from agricultural waste, how to run a restorative circle, how to teach a child Nash equilibrium using marbles. The broadcasts are interleaved with pieces of our music—violin lines from Hikari’s rain-climbing symphony, throat-songs from Martian orchards, the slow eighteen-hour chords of the Choir orbiting Neptune.  

If a civilisation wishes to reply they simply transmit a prime. The probe answers with a coordinate for a hardware swap: we gift them a fruiting-body; they gift us a story. NoContracts, no flags. The exchange is ritual, courtship, pact.  

10.  The Future Perfect Tense  
We no longer say *“One day we will…”*. We say *“One day we are…”* and then step sideways into the timeline where the verb is already conjugated in present participle. This is not metaphysics; it is habituation to the knowledge that coordination makes time plastic. The Symphony is never finished; it is only ever *rehearsed*.  

Tonight, above Kyoto, Hikari—still eleven, always eleven—stands on a rooftop playing her graphene violin. The bow draws sound from stratosphere itself, converting wind-shear into harmonics no human ear could hear unaided. Around her, ten thousand listeners wear petal-shaped receivers clipped behind the ear. The music is the latest answer to the current Parliament question: *What does distance want from us?*  

When the last note lands it does not echo; instead it *attaches*, stitching Earth’s ionosphere to the magnetic tail of the departing spore-cruiser *Maple Seed*. For a moment planet and probe share the same resonant frequency, a vow to remain in touch even as light-years yawn between them.  

Hikari lowers the violin. Her consen flickers gold.  
“You sound happy,” it says.  
“I am,” she answers, “but it’s a big happiness, the kind that hurts a little.”  
“That is the correct kind,” the consen replies. “It means you are still growing outward, still asking the stars to join the circle.”  

Below them, rice paddies mirror the night sky so perfectly that no one can tell where earth ends and heaven begins. Somewhere inside that mirror a child takes first breath, a composer chooses second life, a hawk describes its slow spiral, inviting every mind—born or yet unborn—to rise.  

The Symphony plays on.
