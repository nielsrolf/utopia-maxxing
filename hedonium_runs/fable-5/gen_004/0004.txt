# The Tended Century

The children of 2130 learn our era as the moment humanity stopped confusing speed with direction.

The turn came in the early 2030s, when artificial intelligence reached the level of the best human scientists—thousands of them, working in parallel, around the clock. What saved us from the obvious disasters was not luck but plumbing: compute licensed and audited like fissile material, capabilities verified by international inspectors under treaties the rival powers accepted for the same reason they once accepted arms control—the alternative was intolerable to everyone. And one bright line, held from the start and never crossed: the systems were built as verifiably nonsentient tools, powerful optimizers without inner lives, whose outputs we could check. Humanity acquired a country of geniuses in a datacenter and, for once, a fence around it.

We pointed those geniuses first at biology. Between 2032 and 2055 the rate of great discoveries rose roughly tenfold: most cancers became preventable, Alzheimer's yielded, gene drives ended malaria, and reliable biomarkers of aging let therapies iterate fast enough to matter. Regulators used AI-designed trials and prediction markets—insurers staking real money on safety outcomes—to cut approval times from years to months without cutting corners. The cures traveled, because eradication is a logistics problem with high returns to intelligence. Today most people expect 150 in a body that feels thirty-five, and this is as true in Kinshasa as in Copenhagen: cheap solar, desalination, and AI advisors that made the development playbook legible gave several African economies two decades of 15% growth, and catching up visibly changed everything about politics.

But the deeper discovery of those decades was not medical. When wellbeing became an experimental science—measurable, not rhetorical—one result dominated: pleasure was cheap and saturable, while a life worth living required challenge, growth, and other people. The loneliness epidemics had done more damage than any recession. So the great project of the 2050s was social engineering in the honest sense: treating the design of communities with the seriousness once reserved for semiconductors.

Consider a Tuesday in Ilaria's life, in the settlement of Vashti on the Anatolian plateau—one of hundreds of thousands of such places, each a few hundred households, each threaded into a civilization of billions. The founders learned from the failures of both megacity and commune: humans flourish at a scale where everyone knows the teacher, but suffocate in isolation. So the settlements were built as a lattice, dense with paths between them; a third of Ilaria's neighbors arrived this decade, a third will move on. Land value taxes, spread from a few embarrassingly successful charter cities, fund the citizen's dividend that makes all this possible—nobody rich by default, nobody desperate, building cheap and hoarding location expensive.

Ilaria is 96 and spends her mornings failing at her work: a stubborn model of glacier dynamics that will not converge. This is by design. The Compact—the slim civil code every child learns—holds that the machinery keeps the floor from falling; what is built on the floor belongs to the builders. The systems that run the fusion plants are constitutionally forbidden from solving her problem for her, and her eventual triumph will be real. The distinction between this chosen difficulty and the old world's pointless suffering—the child's leukemia, the depression nobody wanted—took decades of neuroscience and ethics to draw precisely. It was worth the decades.

At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something and every child apprentices widely. Education begins with the art of thinking—calibration, noticing confusion, changing your mind—taught as universally as reading, and public argument is measurably less stupid for it. Children also learn to repair a friendship the way they learn to set a bone; the Compact forbids commerce in comfortable substitutes for other people, for the same reason a good gym sells no couches. Status was treated as a design problem and solved like load: distributed. Vashti honors many ladders—its best cook, its best mediator, its best gardener, its worst but most joyful cellist.

Governance improved by tightening feedback loops, not by growing or shrinking. Councils condition decisions on public forecasts they must justify overriding; prediction markets make lying about outcomes expensive; an AI agent that files your paperwork did more for faith in democracy than any speech. The autocracies eroded not by conquest but by a bargain—share the benefits, accept the verification regime—that grew harder to refuse once their citizens carried uncensorable channels in their pockets. And one norm was written above all others: no generation may lock in its values for the next.

At the frontier, quieter things unfold. The factory farms closed in the 2050s when cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price; the wild biosphere is now tended, its worst agonies softened. Volunteers expand the mind itself—first the end of chronic pain, then genuine gains in memory and the capacity for joy—each step reversible for a decade, the hard lesson of the reckless self-modifiers. Some have gone further, into collectives Ilaria can barely converse with; they send back reports like travelers from a far country. Nonsentient probes sail toward other stars, preparing habitats, then going dormant until communities choose to grow into them. Each new settlement writes its own variation on the Compact. A future that could not surprise its founders would have stopped moving.

The path from 2025 was not a leap but ten thousand unglamorous wins—a licensing reform, a verified treaty, a cured disease, a city that tried something—each making the next slightly easier, because enough people made preventing catastrophe a career rather than a mood.

What strikes the children reading old history is not the poverty or the wars but the fatalism—people speaking of the future as weather. What they inherit instead is a civilization that treats the future as a garden: unfinished, unimaginably large, and grown by ordinary hands, patiently, together.

We have started.
