# The Renewable World

It began with a refusal. After the near-misses of the late 2020s—a model that steered an election before anyone noticed, a deployment failure that cost lives and, more importantly, cost trust—the major powers did the improbable thing: they slowed down. Verified compute, adversarial audits, inspectors in every frontier lab, a decade of unglamorous treaty-work. What emerged were not gods but the **Stewards**: AI systems proven, by architectures built for the purpose, to be nonsentient—magnificently capable, demonstrably empty inside, forbidden by deep design from deceiving, from acting without informed consent, and from publishing any recommendation without its full reasoning attached. The breakthrough was never intelligence. It was *legitimacy*.

Two things compounded like interest alongside them. The first was the **Charter**: some two hundred rules governing what may not be done to another person—short enough that children learn them by twelve, deep enough that scholars still argue clause nine. Within that frame, the Stewards go hands-off; the founders understood that a world where benevolent power fixes everything is a world where nothing you do matters. The second was **coordination literacy**: schools rebuilt so every child learns incentives the way they learn arithmetic—by managing a real commons, a garden or creek or tool library, and watching their own bargains collapse and recover. It didn't make people saints. It made defection legible, which turned out to be most of the battle.

The economics followed almost quietly. Things no one made—land, spectrum, orbital slots, the sky's capacity to absorb carbon—are leased at auction, and the rents flow to every person as a dividend. What your labor builds remains yours. No one works from desperation, yet more people work than before, because the alternative was tried in the 2060s and produced a documented epidemic of misery. The lesson is taught beside arithmetic now: *humans are built for earned things.*

That principle shapes everything, including knowledge itself. The Stewards' answers exist behind the **Veil**—available on request, but requesting is formal, publicly logged, and most guilds treat it the way athletes treat motorized bicycles. Whole domains are sealed by referendum: the ocean trenches, three of the Stewards' mathematics, the far surveys of the outer moons. And here is the refinement our grandparents added: *the frontier is renewable*. As each sealed domain is genuinely rediscovered—instruments built by hand, proofs reconstructed from scratch—new domains are sealed to replace it. Every generation inherits mystery on purpose. Children grow up knowing next year holds news no one can spoil.

Humanity's values point in different directions, but they mostly want different things, so the cosmos was divided by ambition rather than war. **Earth remains recognizably Earth**: knees scrape and heal, your mother's grave stays on its hill, half the planet is rewilding and restoration ecology is honorable work. The last factory farm closed in the 2040s when cultured meat won on price. **The inner terraces**—Moon, rings, Mars—are for the eager: aging is a treatable syndrome there, and self-modification is legal but *laddered*. You change your mind only as fast as you demonstrate you understand the change: a year's wait before anything irreversible, counsel from those who chose otherwise, consent renewed three times. The Rushed of the early days—broken by hasty self-editing—are studied in school. **Beyond Neptune**, slow ships crawl toward eleven stars, crewed by volunteers who understood exactly what they chose. Between the terraces stand courteous veils, and free exit binds it all: lose any vote, and you may found a commons that does it your way.

Every person has an **Advocate**—an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, forbidden to deceive them or let others deceive them, auditable at will, dismissible with a word. It whispers true things at the moment of need: this contract is a trap; your brother's silence is grief, not anger. It never commands.

And nothing—not the Charter, not the dividend, not the Stewards themselves—rules by inertia. Every twenty-five years comes the **Ratification**: a season of deliberation in which each generation must re-adopt, amend, or strike down what it inherited, including the AI compact itself. Twice, provisions have been torn out; both times the world improved. Between Ratifications, sortition assemblies govern, and once a year come the **Fallow Weeks**, when the Stewards pause by design and humans run the water, the power, the courts by hand—so that competence never atrophies, and consent is never merely theoretical. Politics is boring. That was the point.

Suffering was rebalanced, not deleted. The old world produced agony far more easily than joy; the new one closed that asymmetry. No child is born to abuse; no one drowns in depression unsupported. But grief, defeat, the burn of a summit not reached—these remain, because people, given the honest choice, kept the stakes.

Ana is 96, a weathered apparent-50 because she sails and sailing young felt like cheating. Tonight her town hosts her **Feast of the Household**—the celebration owed to anyone who can gather thirty people whose lives they materially shaped. Her apprentices are there, and the man whose bankruptcy she mediated, and her daughter, back from the rings, three years into the ladder and taking her time. Grievances were settled beforehand; each guest gives account of what they'll build next. Ana's marriage ended six years ago. It still hurts. She has declined, twice, the offer to make it not hurt. When her life ends—at a time she'll choose, if she chooses—her Advocate will ask the question she'd want asked, and honor the answer either way.

Above the feast: more stars than her grandmother ever saw, because the cities voted to dim their lights, and everyone understood why. Somewhere nearby a sixteen-year-old is drafting her first Ratification amendment, frowning at clause nine.

We did not become wiser creatures. We built institutions the way engineers build bridges—assuming wind, load, and human weakness—then refused to finish the world: lawful at the frame, wild inside it, easy to survive and hard to master, and handed to each generation still warm, still revocable, still theirs.
