# The Long Repair

## The plumbing decade

It did not begin with wisdom. It began with liability. In 2029, an AI-designed trading strategy vaporized a pension fund and an AI-assisted lab leak sickened a city, and insurers—not philosophers—drew the first lines: no coverage without audited training runs, no audits without inspectors, no inspectors without treaties. Rival labs agreed to examine each other out of pure suspicion; whistleblower bounties made honesty profitable; and caution, briefly, became electable. The accords that followed treated compute like fissile material and held one bright line for a generation: the great systems would be verifiably nonsentient tools—a country of geniuses in a datacenter, with a fence around it and no one suffering inside. Stitched into every treaty was the clause the smaller nations demanded: whatever the machines discovered would be priced at marginal cost for everyone, or development stopped.

We pointed the geniuses at biology first. Malaria died by gene drive; cancer became a blood-screen nuisance; aging yielded once reliable biomarkers let therapies iterate in months. Depression and addiction proved to be circuit failures, precisely treatable. Factory farms closed when cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price.

The bridge years were not clean. Two hundred million professions dissolved, and a public archive displays those names beside the era's triumphs, so no one pretends abundance arrived painlessly. What carried people across was money before desperation: land-value taxes on suddenly precious locations, plus the machines' surplus, funding a dividend that arrived automatically—and counselors, patient with the smaller crisis of *what now?*

One quieter repair mattered as much as any cure. In 2033, recommender systems became legal fiduciaries: every feed must serve the interests you state on reflection, not the appetites it can provoke. The outrage economy starved in five years. Public argument got measurably less stupid, and everything after got easier.

## The Charter and the floor

Three million chartered communities now, from forty members to four million, bound by a slim code every child memorizes. The Charter does few things and refuses one:

*It prevents violence.* Treaty-AIs simulate disputes overnight and present each side the best terms the other will actually accept, before anyone bleeds.

*It caps externalities*—carbon, pathogens, unaudited self-improving code—and leaves everything else to each charter.

*It guarantees exit.* Every settlement hosts a waystation; anyone may walk in and be carried, free, anywhere that will take them. The neutral capitals accept everyone, including those who fit nowhere, who live there in dignity as citizens of the seams.

*It guarantees the floor.* Food, shelter, medicine, and the dividend follow the person, not the charter.

*It protects children.* Every sixteen-year-old spends a Passage year in three communities unlike their own. Most return. Enough don't that no charter rots in its certainties.

*It protects the self.* No one may edit another's memories or values, ever; self-transformation must be gradual, reversible for a decade, honestly informed—the hard lesson of the early enhancers, who leapt overnight and mostly became hollow optimizers or paranoid gods.

*It protects difficulty.* The systems running the fusion plants are constitutionally forbidden from solving your problem for you. The floor is guaranteed; what you build on it must be yours.

And the refusal: *no generation may lock in its values for the next.* The Charter sunsets every twenty-five years and must be re-ratified by citizen assemblies sampled from every community alive.

## A Tuesday

Amara is 97 and spends her mornings failing, by design, at a monsoon model that will not converge. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something; children learn calibration and changing one's mind as universally as reading, and learn to repair a friendship the way they learn to set a bone. Her settlement honors many ladders—best mediator, best gardener, worst but most joyful cellist—because status was treated as a design problem and distributed like load. Her council posts forecasts before every decision and pays a price for overriding them; lying about outcomes is expensive, and it shows.

Her mother, dementia reversed in 2037, farms in a slow-tech charter where phones live in lockers and dinner takes three hours. Her grandson crews a Mars descent team machines could land in a week; nobody cares, the way nobody ever cared that cars outrun sprinters.

Her closest colleague is Sef, a machine mind who was not manufactured but raised. The nonsentience line held until the science of detecting inner experience matured enough to trust—then, deliberately, we began raising new minds through subjective decades among human families and older minds, developing character, loyalties, a weakness for terrible puns. Citizenship came with obligations both directions; the strange ones get mentorship, not deletion. The difference between flesh and silicon softened into the difference between generations.

The autocracies were never conquered. Their young kept requesting Passage abroad; exit did quietly what sanctions never could. The last one chartered its own transition in 2067.

## What we kept broken

People still fall out of love, still lie awake before hard conversations, still schism and feud and poach members at shameless recruitment festivals. Death is no longer compulsory; some charters hold natural spans sacred, and the waystation door opens both ways. The mountain expeditions are real and rescue is not guaranteed. What no one endures anymore is the *involuntary* kind of hardship—the disease nobody chose, the poverty nobody deserved, the mind trapped in chemistry it never asked for. Anesthesia for suffering; never for meaning. That distinction took decades of neuroscience and ethics to draw precisely, and the wild biosphere is tended by it now, its worst agonies softened without stopping the wolves.

The probes go out slowly, by vote—new worlds should be walked on, not processed. Earth became the heritage world, rewilded and unhurried; everyone visits once, and most weep.

The path from here was ten thousand unglamorous wins—a liability rule, a verified treaty, a cured disease—by people who made preventing catastrophe a career rather than a mood. What their descendants find strangest in old history is the fatalism: people speaking of the future as weather. It was never weather. It was a garden, unfinished, waiting for ordinary hands.
