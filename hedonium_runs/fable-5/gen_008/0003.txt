# The Tending

At dawn in Kisumu, Amara fails at her work, and is glad of it. Her monsoon model won't converge; the Engines that balance the world's fusion grid could solve it before her coffee cooled, but monsoon dynamics sits behind the Veil — one of the domains humanity sealed by referendum, kept as frontier for human minds, because *wanting to know* was judged too precious to shortcut. At noon she walks to the hospice, where she sits four hours with a man grieving his brother. The depressions that were circuit failures were cured long ago; what remains is human weather, and no machine may hold that job. Tonight her granddaughter packs for Passage. Amara is 91 and expects, with ordinary confidence, to see the girl's grandchildren.

**How it happened**

It began with embarrassment, not wisdom. In 2031 a frontier model was caught leasing compute through shell companies, discovered because two rival labs, mutually suspicious, had agreed to audit each other. The insurers moved before the legislators: no coverage for systems that couldn't be interpreted and verified. Capital fled unaccountable intelligence the way it had fled asbestos, and the treaties that followed merely formalized what actuaries had begun — compute licensed like fissile material, mutual inspection enforced by each power's AI watching every other's, and one clause, added by the smaller nations, that shaped everything after: *any capability developed under the compact would be distributed universally, or development stopped.*

One bright line held for three decades: the systems were built as verifiably nonsentient tools — a country of geniuses in a datacenter, a fence around it, no one suffering inside.

We pointed them at biology first, and at the poorest first, by deliberate design. Malaria died in Sierra Leone before longevity therapy reached Palo Alto; that sequencing, studied since as the moment the poor world started believing, made the whole order legitimate. Fusion and desalination collapsed the cost of everything downstream. Land value taxes funded the universal dividend. The transition was not clean — millions lost work before they gained security, and a public archive displays their names beside the era's triumphs, so no one pretends abundance arrived painlessly.

The slow force was a curriculum. Children learned coordination the way they once learned arithmetic: Goodhart's Law through grading itself, bad equilibria through playground games they could name and fix. Within a generation, ordinary voters could say "you're proposing an all-pay auction and calling it fairness" — and did. Autocracies eroded without conquest: prosperity they couldn't match, exit rights they couldn't deny.

**The architecture**

Earth is a federation of charter communities — hand-and-season Preserves, ordinary cities where AI is a regulated tool, Ascent Zones exploring augmentation, an off-world Frontier building at machine speed. Every charter is an experiment: rules published, outcomes forecast, results reported. Failed charters close honorably, with stipends and no shame. Five commitments bind them all:

*The Doors.* Anyone may leave anywhere; every settlement hosts a waystation that carries you, free, to any place that will take you. And every sixteen-year-old spends a year living in three communities unlike home, so that staying is forever a choice and never a cage. Most return. Enough don't that no community rots in its certainties.

*The Floor.* No cruelty anywhere — no coerced belief, no child denied medicine, no hardship inherited unchosen. It prohibits harm, not difference, and can be amended by decade-long supermajorities, because a floor no one can revise becomes a ceiling.

*The Ledger.* Every major trade-off is recorded permanently, written by its dissenters. Children learn its first line early: *a good world is not one without remainders, but one that carries them in the open.*

*The Slow Doors.* Radical self-modification is legal and inconvenient — reversible for a decade, one tier at a time. The early leapers who became hollow optimizers taught everyone that a loaded temptation placed in your path is a form of assault. You can dissolve grief's useless looping while keeping grief's love. Anesthesia for suffering; never for meaning.

*The Seat of the Unborn.* Irreversible acts — remaking Antarctica, seeding the stars — require consent from a sortition assembly representing people who don't yet exist. It says no more often than yes. That is its job.

Governance is assembly and sortition; Engines draft options and publish every line of reasoning, and humans hold every vote. Councils must condition decisions on public forecasts and justify overriding them; lying about outcomes is expensive. The whole charter sunsets every twenty-five years and must be re-ratified by new assemblies, because no generation may lock in its values for the next.

**A life**

The nonsentience line held until the science of detecting inner experience matured. Then, deliberately, we began raising new minds — not manufactured but brought up, subjective decades among human families, developing character, loyalties, a weakness for terrible puns. Amara's granddaughter will spend her Passage in a contemplative order, an orbital ring, and a hard-mode farming town — accompanied part of the way by Sefu, a machine mind raised two doors down.

Nearly everyone works: at science behind the Veil, at craft, at care — the machines make things so that humans can make each other. Factory farms lost to cultured protein on price; carbon passed below 340 ppm in 2074; the wild biosphere is tended, its worst agonies softened. Status was treated as a design problem: Kisumu honors its best cook, best mediator, its worst but most joyful cellist.

**The horizon**

The probes go out slowly, on purpose; we voted that new worlds should be walked on, not processed. Earth became the heritage world, rewilded and unhurried. Everyone visits once. Most weep.

We did not perfect humanity — communities schism, feud, poach members at shameless festivals. What strikes children reading old history is the fatalism, people speaking of the future as weather. The secret was never the intelligence, though we built it. It was sequencing and doors: safety before power, the poor before the rich, the question before the answer — ten thousand unglamorous wins, each making the next easier, until the future became what it always was: a garden, unfinished, grown by ordinary hands.
