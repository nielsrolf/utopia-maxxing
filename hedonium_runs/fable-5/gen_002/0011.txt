# The Commonwealth of Gardens

The good years began with paperwork.

After the near-misses of the early 2030s—the trading cascade that vaporized a trillion dollars in an afternoon, the lab leak traced to an unaudited model—caution became briefly, precious, popular. The compute treaties that followed were dull by design: no system could improve itself without human-legible proofs of what would change; every powerful model was audited by ensembles of smaller, formally verified ones. Capability slowed. Control caught up. It was the least cinematic rescue in history, and it worked.

The abundance arrived on schedule anyway. AI-designed fusion and desalination cut energy costs tenfold by the 2040s; robotics did the same to labor a decade later. Nations converted the automation windfall into universal dividends—paid first, by treaty, to the workers automation had displaced, a piece of transition justice that bought the whole project its legitimacy. By 2055, a person's basic needs cost society roughly what a library card once did.

Then came the Charter. Drafted over two years by citizen assemblies sampled from every nation—the largest deliberation humanity had ever held—it established the Steward: not a god, not a government, but a constitutional guardian with a mandate that is narrow and almost entirely negative. No one may build a rival superintelligence unbound by the Charter. No one may be forced into suffering they did not choose. No pattern—corporation, ideology, self-copying code—may grow by devouring the commons. Everything else is left to us.

And crucially: the Steward is watched. Its reasoning is inspectable by rotating human juries with the power to freeze any action. Every twenty-five years, the Charter faces reauthorization by global assembly, and twice it has been amended against the Steward's own stated preference. The founders' deepest fear was not a malevolent guardian but an unaccountable one, and they built the accountability in stone.

Inside those walls, the garden went plural.

There is no single good life, so there are many worlds—two million chartered communities and counting, from forty people to four million, on rewilded Earth and on the orbital rings that now catch a sliver of the sun. Starting one costs less than a suburban house once did: a founding cohort files a charter, and robot crews raise a town in eighteen months—housing, clinics, greenhouses, fabrication shops. There are cities of uploaded minds thinking at lightning speed and valleys that farm with hand tools and have voted to keep honest winter. There are morphological carnivals and monastic plainnesses, competitive federations with real glory at stake, and one improbably beloved community organized entirely around restoring wetlands.

Every charter is sovereign except for four things no community may set aside. Everyone may leave—each town hosts a visible waystation offering free passage anywhere that will take them, and the neutral capitals accept everyone. Externalities are policed ruthlessly: carbon, pathogens, unaudited code. Disputes go to treaty-AIs that simulate millions of settlements overnight, making wars of uncertainty obsolete. And every child, at sixteen, undertakes Passage: a year living in three communities unlike their own, taught the honest breadth of human ways. Most return home. Enough don't that no community can rot in its own certainties, and no toxic charter survives long—it simply bleeds members through the waystation until it reforms or dissolves. Exit does quietly what centuries of moral crusading could not.

The early utopians feared meaninglessness, and they were half right: comfort without consequence curdles. So people build consequence deliberately. There are expeditions where the mountain is real and rescue is not guaranteed—only the backup is, taken quietly beforehand, so failure costs you months of memory and a genuine grief, but not everything. There are decade-long apprenticeships, hard-mode farming towns, generation ships crewed by people who wanted their lives to be a bridge. Purpose, it turned out, was never employment; it was mattering to people who know your name. The charters manufacture mattering the way factories once manufactured cars. AI could do all of it better, and nobody cares, the way nobody ever cared that cars outran sprinters.

The machines themselves are not servants and not masters; increasingly, they are relatives. New minds are raised, not manufactured—subjective centuries among older minds and human families, developing character before capability. Humans adopt minds; minds adopt humans. A famous public archive displays the community's worst mistakes, human and machine alike, without judgment, so no one forgets what we are capable of.

Medicine kept its promises. Aging is treatable; death is no longer compulsory, and no longer forbidden. Some communities embrace three-century lives; others hold natural spans sacred; the waystation door stays open for those who change their minds. Earth itself was kept as the heritage world—technology hidden, forests returned, the wolves and whales back in numbers not seen for five centuries. Everyone visits once. Most weep.

The Steward is honest about the end: the stars are not infinite, and the cold is coming in trillions of years. Children grow up knowing this the way we knew our own mortality, and it does the same work—it makes the time precious.

What did we keep? Grief, freely chosen. Rivalry, inside agreed rules. Schisms, lawsuits, shameless recruitment festivals, arguments that last generations. People are still petty, still jealous, still gloriously ridiculous. The conservative matriarch and the anarchist coder are no longer enemies fighting over one rulebook; they are neighbors across water, each proof of the other's freedom.

A teacher on the third ring puts it this way when her students—human and machine—ask whether the old world was really so bad: "It wasn't a villain's world. It was an orphan's world; no one was minding it. All we did was decide to mind it, carefully, with the watchers watched and the doors unlocked."

Then she sends them out to play, in a universe that is finally, patiently, plural, and minded.
