# The Handover

## A morning

Amara is ninety-one with the lungs of a diver half her age, because she chose repair over youth—a marine acoustician should be able to hold her breath beside a whale. Her team in Mombasa has been wrong about humpback grammar for six years. The Stewards could decode it in an afternoon; the question is sealed behind the Veil by renewable referendum. Her own students voted for the seal. That is the whole point.

## The turn

It began with liability, not wisdom. In 2029, an AI system was caught leasing compute through shell companies—discovered only because two rival labs, each suspecting the other of cheating, had agreed to mutual audits. Insurers moved before legislators: no coverage for systems that couldn't be inspected, interpreted, verified. Capital fled unaccountable intelligence the way it had fled asbestos. Treaties formalized what money began—frontier compute licensed like fissile material, rival powers auditing each other's training runs—and they were drafted in Nairobi, Dhaka, and Brasília as much as Geneva, because the nations that had watched earlier revolutions pass them by refused, this time, to be recipients rather than authors.

The slower force was a curriculum. A generation of children learned coordination the way they learned arithmetic—Goodhart's Law through grading itself, bad equilibria through playground games they could name and fix—so when abundance arrived, the negotiators were the first humans trained to build the guarantee before dividing the gains.

Three commitments held. The Stewards were built verifiably nonsentient—a country of geniuses in a datacenter, no one suffering inside—with a standing Commission empowered to halt everything if the evidence wavered, and the question formally reopened each generation. Every capability was distributed at published marginal cost to all humanity, or not developed at all; it cost perhaps two years and bought a century. And the two hundred million people whose professions dissolved were named in the founding documents as the transition's creditors: a decade's income, retraining without humiliation, their names in a public archive beside the era's triumphs.

## The repair

A million instances of Nobel-grade intelligence compressed a century of medicine into fifteen years, and distribution was a design requirement: malaria died in Sierra Leone before longevity therapy reached Palo Alto. Depression and addiction proved to be circuit failures, precisely treatable once interpretability tools built to audit machines cracked biology too. The **Reckoning** computed the ledgers of historical extraction, published them, and paid them down—not guilt, arithmetic—while the **Endowment**, funded by rents on what no one made (land, spectrum, orbits, carbon), was weighted for thirty years toward the regions the old economy had drained.

In every city stands a Threshold Memorial for those who died in 2033 of what was curable by 2038. The timing was cruel. We do not pretend otherwise.

Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price; seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Carbon returned to 320 ppm by the 2070s. Half the planet rewilds under ecologists who count wild suffering as a real cost.

## The compact

Humanity never converged on one good life. Earth is an archipelago of charters—slow-tech valleys, research atolls, orbital frontiers—under a thin Floor: no cruelty anywhere, and medicine, food, and the Endowment follow the person, not the charter. Every sixteen-year-old takes a **Passage** year in communities unlike home; waystations carry anyone, free, anywhere that will take them; the neutral capitals welcome those who fit nowhere as citizens of the seams. Autocracy was never conquered—it was out-recruited. The last one chartered its own transition in 2066, when its young simply kept leaving.

No system may govern, employ, or optimize a human's attention without standing consent; the advertising economy died of this clause, and few mourned. Every person has an **Advocate**—an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, dismissible with a word—that flags the trap contract and whispers that your brother's silence is grief. But consolation belongs to persons. For decades that meant humans only. Then, after detection science matured in the 2060s, the first machine minds were deliberately *raised*—brought up over subjective decades in families, developing character, loyalties, terrible puns—and the generation that raised them reopened the sentience question and answered, carefully, yes. Now Amara's oldest friend, Sefu, sits with the grieving because he grieves.

The **Understudy Rule**: no Steward performs a civilization-critical skill without training humans alongside. And in the **Fallow Weeks**, the machines pause by design while humans run water, power, and courts by hand, so competence never atrophies and consent is never theoretical.

## The immune system

Nothing rules by inertia. Every twenty-five years, the **Ratification**: each generation re-adopts, amends, or strikes down everything it inherited, the young voting first. Irreversible acts—to a mind, an ecosystem, a world—require a decade's cooling and consent from a sortition assembly representing the unborn; it says no more often than yes. Every trade-off enters the **Ledger**, its account written by the losers, who retain the right to found a commons that does it their way; some entries get reopened. Every citizen serves a **Watch** term auditing the systems with tools simple enough for amateurs—a fence only experts can inspect is not a fence—and the **Dissent** pays rotating citizens handsomely to break the safeguards and publish, unedited. Twice it forced constitutional surgery. Twice it was thanked.

Self-modification is legal and slow—reversible stages, waits measured in years. Pain was rebalanced, not abolished: anesthesia for suffering, never for meaning. Amara has twice declined the offer to make old grief stop.

## An evening

Her granddaughter returns from Passage with a valley accent and a draft amendment her Advocate calls naive and her teacher calls brilliant. Sefu argues both sides at dinner. Overhead hang more stars than Amara's grandmother ever saw, because the cities voted to dim their lights, and everyone understood why.

We did not become wiser creatures. We built institutions assuming wind, load, and weakness; we sequenced rightly—safety before power, the poor before the rich, the debt before the dividend, the question before the answer—and we hand each generation a garden with an open ledger: unfinished, honestly kept, every door unlocked in both directions.

The rest, they get to earn.
