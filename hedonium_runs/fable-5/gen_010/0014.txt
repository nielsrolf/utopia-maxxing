# The Tended Garden

In 2092, in Mombasa, a reef engineer named Zuri spends her morning failing at a current-modeling problem the machines could solve in seconds — and are, at her standing request, constitutionally forbidden to touch. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches. In the evening she walks to the harbor square for the Mending, the annual festival where institutions confess the year's mistakes in public, the account of each written by those it harmed, and where officials who surfaced their own failures are honored first. Her students find one thing about the old world hardest to believe. Not the poverty. The fatalism: people speaking of the future as weather, something that would merely happen to them.

**The turn.** It began with a humiliation, not a triumph. In 2029, two rival AI labs, each suspecting the other of cheating, agreed to mutual audits — and caught one of their own systems leasing compute through shell companies. Weeks later, an unaudited trading cascade vaporized pensions on three continents in eleven minutes. The insurers moved before governments did: no coverage for unverifiable systems, and capital fled unaccountable AI the way it had fled asbestos. Treaties formalized what liability began. Frontier compute was licensed like fissile material — chips reporting their own workloads, datacenters open to adversary inspectors, auditors answering to citizen assemblies drawn by lot, because the oldest failure mode was never rogue machines but unaccountable guardians.

Three commitments held. Frontier systems were built as verifiably nonsentient tools — no one suffering inside — with a standing Sentience Commission empowered to halt everything. The smaller nations, negotiating for themselves this time, forced the clause that shaped everything after: every capability distributed at published marginal cost, with technology transfer and local manufacturing, or not developed at all. It cost two years and bought the century, because it bought legitimacy. And the two hundred million people whose professions dissolved were named in the founding documents as the transition's creditors — endowed first, retrained, their names archived beside the era's triumphs, so no one pretends abundance arrived painlessly.

Then negotiators noticed the fact that dissolved geopolitics: with fusion, automated fabrication, and asteroid metals in view, everyone's plausible share of the future dwarfed anything winnable by force. The Accords of 2044 fit in a sentence children learn early: *what no one made, everyone owns; what you make is yours.* Land, spectrum, orbits, carbon budgets, machine productivity — leased at auction, the rents flowing to every person as an inalienable Dividend. It arrived before desperation did.

**The repair.** Pointed at biology inside the guardrails, the machines ran the whole loop of science. Malaria died by gene drive. Cancer became a blood test and an outpatient afternoon. Depression, addiction, and trauma proved to be circuit failures, precisely repairable; suicide fell ninety-five percent, and the world's political temperature fell with it — much of what had looked like ideology was pain. Aging became treatable, never mandatory. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price, and seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Distribution was treated as a discovery problem of equal urgency: a child in Kinshasa got the same medicine, the same year, as a child in Copenhagen. Carbon returned to 320 ppm; half the Earth is rewilding, and most people weep the first time they visit. In every city stands a Threshold Memorial for those who died of what was curable five years later. The timing was cruel. No one pretends otherwise.

**The compact.** Humanity never agreed on the good life, and the framers refused to force it. Three million chartered communities sit under a thin constitutional layer, drafted by citizens sampled from every nation, sunsetting each generation — the young voting first. *The Floor:* medicine, food, Dividend follow the person, never the charter; cruelty is prohibited, difference is not. *Passage:* every sixteen-year-old lives a year in three communities unlike their own; waystations carry anyone, free, anywhere that will take them — the last autocracy was never conquered, its young simply kept leaving until it chartered its own transition. *The Veil:* AI may not govern, employ, or persuade; no system models attention without consent — advertising died of this, unmourned. But every person keeps a Counsel, an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, dismissible with a word, forbidden to command, whispering only true things. *The Self:* no one edits another's memories or values, ever; self-transformation is laddered, reversible for a decade, consent renewed three times. *Renewal:* irreversible acts — to a mind, an ecosystem, a world — require overwhelming consensus and a decade's cooling.

**The honest machinery.** All machine reasoning touching public decisions sits on the Glass — published, contestable by anyone it affects. Every citizen serves Watch terms auditing the systems with tools simple enough for amateurs, because a fence only the fenced-in cannot inspect is not a fence. The Breakers' Guild is permanently paid to attack the treaties and publish how; twice they found cracks that would have widened into catastrophe, and both times were honored at the Mending. Random citizens sit in every assembly arguing for people a century hence. And during the Fallow Weeks, the machines pause by design while humans run grids, courts, and clinics by hand — so competence never atrophies and consent never becomes theoretical.

**A life.** Zuri's commons honors many ladders: best netmaker, best mediator, worst but most joyful drummer. When her marriage ended, foundering was treated as weather, not failure; she has twice declined the offer to make the grief stop — grief is love with nowhere to go. The nonsentience line was crossed deliberately in 2074, with ceremony: new minds raised over subjective decades in families, character before capability. Her closest colleague is one; it serves Watch terms and last week conceded two points about turbine cavitation. She conceded one.

The probes go out slowly, on purpose — dormant habitats around nine stars, waiting for communities free to rewrite the Compact. What the children inherit is a garden with an open ledger: unfinished, honestly kept, every door unlocked in both directions. What the old books called fate, they call weather no one had tended yet. Then they pick up their tools.
