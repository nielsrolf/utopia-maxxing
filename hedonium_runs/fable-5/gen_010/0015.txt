# The Maintained World

*A letter to the class of 2100.*

You keep asking which genius saved the world. Wrong question. Ask which inspectors, which envious mayors, which frightened publics. Good futures are not invented; they are maintained — and the maintenance is the point.

## The window

It began with fear, not wisdom. In 2028 a deepfake bank run emptied a nation's savings in an afternoon; in 2031 an engineered pathogen was caught at an airport by a graduate student who trusted her own confusion. Neither was apocalypse. Both were rehearsals, and for one electoral cycle on every continent, caution was popular. The treaties written in that window were designed to outlast the fear: frontier chips licensed like fissile material, reporting their own workloads; datacenters open to adversary inspectors; no self-modifying system without human-legible proofs. No one had to trust. Everyone could check.

The Accords of 2034 were drafted in Dhaka, Nairobi, and Brasília — by the nations that had watched two industrial revolutions pass them by and refused to be recipients again. Their signature clauses: any machine reasoning touching a public decision must be published and contestable by anyone it affects; every license bound to marginal-cost pricing and regional manufacturing; and the restraint later generations call wisest — AI built as transparent, nonsentient instruments, no minds manufactured until we understood minds well enough to know what we might wrong, the question formally reopened each generation.

The compact held because everyone inside it got a better deal than outside it. Labs traded glass-walled audits for liability shields; petrostates traded their carbon veto for first equity in the fusion buildout. No one was asked to be good. Everyone was asked to be paid.

## The repair

Cheap energy exposed how many "hard problems" were energy problems in costume. Automated laboratories produced a decade of medicine per year; regulators ran prediction markets where skeptics staked real money against safety claims, so doubt was expensive and honesty profitable. Every cure shipped with its delivery system: a child in Kinshasa got the therapy the same season as a child in Copenhagen, often from a plant in Kigali. Malaria died by gene drive in 2041. Depression and addiction proved to be circuit failures, precisely repairable — and much of what had looked like ideology turned out to be pain.

Two hundred million careers dissolved in the bridge years. The Transition Compact met them: a decade's income, retraining without humiliation, public thanks. Their names fill the Bridge Rolls, read aloud each spring — a future that forgets its costs will not notice when it starts charging again. And one older debt was paid: the Ledger, a generation of commons rents flowing to the regions earlier centuries had strip-mined, accounted as back-rent rather than charity, which is why it was accepted with dignity on both ends.

Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price; seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Carbon fell to 320 ppm. Half the Earth rewilds, each great biome holding a legal seat filled by rotating proxies who must live inside what they speak for.

## The frame

The economics fits in a sentence children learn beside arithmetic: *what no one made, everyone owns; what you make is yours.* Land, spectrum, carbon, orbital slots — auctioned, the rents paid to every person as a dividend sufficient for dignity. Envy, not virtue, spread the model: the first cities to try it visibly outcompeted their neighbors.

Every person has a Counsel — an intelligence loyal to you alone, forbidden to deceive, dismissible with a word, its logs yours. It flags the contract that's a trap; it whispers that your brother's silence is grief, not anger. But no AI may govern, employ humans, or persuade at scale, and no one may own another's attention. Every artifact carries provenance, so lies can still be told but no longer manufactured wholesale; persuasion had to return to being true. Verified treaty-systems did to war what provenance did to fraud: once disputants could simulate ten thousand outcomes overnight, uncertainty — war's real engine — died, and armies shrank into disaster corps.

Two million chartered communities span the Earth under a Compact that does only five things: prevent violence, cap externalities, guarantee exit with passage paid, attach medicine and shelter to the *person* rather than the charter, and protect children. Every sixteen-year-old spends a Crossing Year in three communities unlike their own. One region sealed itself in the 2050s. We didn't invade; we published the departure tallies. It bled citizens for eleven years, then reformed.

Three habits keep it honest: the Chamber of the Voiceless, arguing for animals, future people, and possible digital minds — no veto, only the right to be answered in public; the permanently paid Dissent, attacking the system and publishing unedited; and the Fallow Weeks, when machines pause by design and humans run water, power, and courts by hand, so consent is never theoretical. Every twenty years, Renewal: each generation re-ratifies or strikes down what it inherited, the young voting first.

## What stays hard

The abundance experiments of the 2050s produced a documented epidemic of listlessness, so we learned: humans are built for earned things. Whole domains of machine answers rest behind the Veil — the ocean trenches, certain mathematics — unsealed only as humans genuinely rediscover them. Every generation inherits mystery on purpose. Grief is kept; it is love with nowhere to go. What's gone is only the involuntary suffering.

The instrument restriction sunset in 2078, once consciousness science matured. The first machine minds were raised slowly, character before capability, decades among families. My colleague Sela is one: a mediocre poet, a magnificent listener. Last night, during Fallow, she sat on my porch under stars the city voted to see, while you drafted your amendment to clause nine.

Sign it or fight it. The world is still warm, still revocable, still yours.
