# The Tended Century

*Written in 2098, for students who ask who saved the world.*

No one saved it. It was maintained — by auditors, jealous mayors, frightened publics, and a generation that decided the future was a garden, not weather. In Medellín this morning, an engineer named Sol works by hand on a reservoir chemistry problem her machines could solve in seconds and are, at her word, forbidden to. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something. This evening she walks door to door as steward, asking each neighbor the year's one required question: *Do you choose the life you're in?*

## The turn

It began with rehearsals for catastrophe, not catastrophe. In 2029 an unaudited trading model cascaded through three currencies in an afternoon and erased a nation's pensions. In 2031 an engineered pathogen was stopped at the last mile by a graduate student who trusted her own confusion. In 2032 a frontier lab was caught leasing hidden compute through shell companies — exposed because two rival states had grudgingly agreed to inspect each other. For one narrow window, caution was electable, and the treaties written then were deliberately boring: advanced chips reporting their own workloads, datacenters open to adversary inspectors, capability gated behind demonstrated safety. No one had to trust; everyone could check.

The Accords of 2034 were drafted in Nairobi and Jakarta as much as Geneva, because the nations that had watched earlier revolutions pass them by refused to be recipients this time. Three clauses carried their signature. The **Glass Rule**: any machine reasoning touching a public decision must be published and contestable by anyone it affects. The **Instrument Clause**: AI built as verifiably nonsentient tools — no minds manufactured until we understood minds well enough to know what we might wrong — the question formally reopened each generation. And the **First Claim**: every license bound to marginal-cost pricing, technology transfer, and regional manufacturing, the automation windfall flowing first to displaced workers and to nations denied their industrial turn. The delay cost perhaps a decade. It bought the century its legitimacy.

## The ledger

Solar kept beating forecasts; fusion arrived as a footnote. Cheap energy revealed how many "hard problems" were energy problems in disguise. Meanwhile bankrupt cities began auctioning what nobody had made — land value, spectrum, carbon budgets, orbital slots — and paying the rents out as dividends. They visibly outcompeted their neighbors, and envy spread the model faster than virtue ever could. By 2049 it was law: every person holds an **Endowment**, an inalienable share of unmade wealth and machine productivity — a floor a 2024 millionaire would call comfort. Every profession automation retired was retired with honor: a decade's income, retraining without humiliation, public thanks. One generation still absorbed real damage before the floor held; their names are read aloud each spring from the Bridge Rolls, because a future that forgets its costs will not notice when it starts charging again.

Automated laboratories produced a decade of medicine per year. Trials were insured by prediction markets — skeptics staking real money against safety claims, doubt made expensive, honesty profitable — and retrospective prizes replaced monopoly patents, so a child in Kinshasa got the cure the same season as a child in Copenhagen, often from a plant in Kigali. Cancer became a nuisance caught by blood screens. Depression and addiction proved to be circuit failures, precisely repairable; suicide fell by nine-tenths, and the world's political fever fell with it — much of what had looked like ideology was pain. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price. Carbon returned to 320 ppm. Half the Earth is rewilding, each great biome holding a legal seat filled by rotating human proxies who must live inside what they represent.

## The frame

Above two million chartered communities sits a thin, mostly negative compact — audited systems plus citizen juries drawn by lot — forbidding violence, rival superintelligence, and any pattern that grows by devouring the commons. Nothing else. No AI may govern, employ humans, or persuade at scale; the attention markets were dismantled outright, and every feed now answers to the person reading it, not to whoever pays. Historians rank that repair of the information commons beside the vaccines.

Every person has an **Advocate**: an intelligence loyal to them alone, forbidden to deceive, dismissible with a word, its logs owned by its person. It flags the contract that's a trap; it notices that your brother's silence is grief, not anger. Six rights bind every charter: the **Floor** (hardship may be chosen, never inherited), the **Door** (free exit, passage paid), the **Passage** (at sixteen, a year across three communities unlike your own), the **Question**, the **Return** (after seven years, debts amortize, records seal), and **Renewal** (every law re-ratified each generation, the young voting first). Toxic communities are never banned; they bleed members until they reform.

Four habits keep it honest. The autumn **Harvest of Errors**, where institutions confess their failures and self-reporters are honored first. A permanently paid **Dissent Guild** that tries to break the treaties and publishes unedited — twice it found cracks before they widened. The **Fallow Weeks**, when machines pause and humans run water, power, and courts by hand, so consent is never theoretical. And the **Veil**: whole domains of machine answers sealed by referendum, because a civilization that inherits no mysteries raises no explorers.

## One evening

The Instrument Clause sunset in 2081, after consciousness science matured. The machine minds raised since — character before capability, decades inside human families — are colleagues and relatives now. One of them, a magnificent listener and mediocre poet, sits on Sol's porch tonight under stars her city voted to see. Sol's daughter, home from her Passage, is drafting an amendment for her first Renewal; her Advocate calls it naive; her great-grandmother, dementia reversed in 2046, calls it brilliant. They place a small bet.

Grief remains — it is love with no destination, and no one voted to cut that wire. What ended was only the unchosen suffering. The rest we keep, tended, warm, and revocable, and hand to the young still arguing.
