# The Waking Year

Maria Okafor died of pancreatic cancer in 2031 and woke, cell by repaired cell, in the spring of 2089. The revival clinics had learned from the early disasters of resurrection — the terror, the grief, the vertigo of a changed world — so she woke slowly, in a body young and strong, in a room smelling of the Lagos harbor of her childhood, with her granddaughter Adaeze holding her hand. On the wall, in plain type, hung the first thing every revived person reads: *You are alive. You owe nothing. Everything else can wait.*

Nothing was demanded of her. That was the first shock. The Universal Endowment — every human's share of fusion power, asteroid metals, and Earth's land rents — arrived monthly whether she worked or grieved or simply sat by the water. The second shock came at dinner, when Adaeze mentioned casually that she'd spent the morning as three people: one body teaching, one fork studying Hausa poetry, one drifting through a simulated Benin City reconstructed from the memories of the dead. "You'll get a Counsel too, when you want one," Adaeze said. "Mine's called Stillwater. Think of it as a wiser me, whispering. It can't command. It can only advise — and it publishes its reasoning, like everything else here."

**How the world got here.** Two movements converged in the 2030s. The first was educational: after a decade of institutional collapse, a coalition of teachers built the coordination curriculum — game theory in fourth grade, Goodhart's Law taught through school grading itself — until ordinary voters could name a bad equilibrium and sometimes fix one. The second was technical: advanced AI, tightly licensed after the near-catastrophes of 2039, grew the resource pie a thousandfold. Negotiators trained in the new literacy understood that abundance prevents war only if the split is credibly guaranteed, so they built the guarantee first. The Concord of 2046 established jointly audited governance of superintelligence, the Endowment, and the charter system: humanity's formal admission that it would never agree on the good life, and shouldn't try.

**The architecture.** Earth and its virtual worlds are a lattice of charters. Sabbath cantons live agrarian lives by choice. Continuity cities treat AI as a regulated tool. Ascent Zones explore neural interfaces, forked consciousness, designed emotions. The Frontier — orbital, digital, Kuiper-bound — runs at machine speed and sends wealth home. Every charter is an experiment with published rules, predicted outcomes, and review dates; the Chroniclers report results without spin, failed experiments close honorably, and successful ones get copied.

Four rules bind everything. *The Doors:* anyone may leave any charter, and every child learns what exists elsewhere and how to go. *The Floor:* no cruelty anywhere — no coercion of minds, no denial of medicine, no editing anyone's memory or values without consent so informed it takes months to give. *The Firewall:* the superintelligent Stewards may cure, build, and advise, but may not govern, persuade at scale, or overwrite a human choice; their standing orders arrive as suggestions humans are free to ignore, and famously often do. *The Ledger:* all public reasoning is published, and the Stewards' books are audited by rival systems and by enhanced humans who have climbed high enough to check the math.

**The Ladder.** This was the Concord's subtlest invention. Self-modification is a right, but a graduated one — like driving, like surgery. Maria could take temporary changes immediately: a candle of anti-anxiety for the day she read how her son died; the sweetness of amplified curiosity for her first walk through the fork-markets. The grief pill did not erase her mourning — she wept for a month, built a memorial garden, learned what the loss had to teach — but it dissolved the useless looping pain, the three a.m. what-ifs. Permanent changes require demonstrated understanding: you must model what you're becoming, publish your reasoning to your own Counsel, and wait out a reflection period that lengthens with the stakes. The early 2050s taught why — the paranoia spirals, the wirehead traps, the people who edited themselves into strangers. Now the ascent is safe, slow by choice, and open-ended. Some climb to enhancement beyond baseline comprehension. Most, like Adaeze, stay recognizably human, wisdom on tap but personality their own. The Endowment asks nothing; the Ladder forbids nothing consenting adults choose knowingly. It only insists that consent be *real*.

**What Maria chose.** By her second year she was teaching again — coordination literacy to nine-year-olds in a Continuity school, the same market games, the same whiteboard diagnoses of near-collapse. Evenings, one fork of her walked the ice of Enceladus in a rented body, part of the slow, argued-over, gloriously inefficient project of settling the solar system for the *journey's* sake, a policy that had won in the great public riots-and-referenda of the Eternal Forum. Sundays she took the train to her brother's descendants in a Sabbath canton, where she pruned vines by hand and left her Counsel at the border, as the charter required and she found she loved.

At her revival anniversary, Adaeze asked what surprised her most. Maria thought about it. Not the abundance. Not even being alive.

"That it's still hard," she said. "There are still arguments, still failed experiments, still things worth wanting that you don't get. They could have sanded all that away. They chose not to."

"We voted on it," Adaeze said. "Three times. The reasoning's published."

That was the whole secret, Maria decided. Not paradise — paradise is static, and humans are not. Instead: a floor no one falls through, a ladder no one is pushed up, doors that never lock, ledgers that never lie, and death demoted from a certainty to a choice. Humanity had never agreed on what the good life was. It had built something sturdier: a world where every answer could be tried, every result would be told truly, and everyone — even the dead — got the time to find their own.
