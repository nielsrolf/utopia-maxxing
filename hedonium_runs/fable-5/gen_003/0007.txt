# The Commons and the Doors

In 2091, a teacher named Ines finishes her morning in Porto's harbor school, where twelve-year-olds have just spent a week running a real fish stall — pricing, bargaining, splitting profits, and then diagramming, on a whiteboard, exactly where their little market had almost collapsed into a bad equilibrium and how they'd caught it. This is coordinacy class: taught everywhere, the way reading and arithmetic once were. Tonight she'll review a proposal from her district's Chronicle — a change to the fishing-rights auction, with the drafter's full reasoning published, a three-year review date attached, and a prediction market quietly estimating whether it will work. She'll vote through her delegate, whom she can recall with a tap. Her brother, meanwhile, lives in a Sabbath canton in the Douro valley that runs at roughly 1990s technology by charter, and her daughter is applying for residency in the Lisbon Ascent Zone, where neural interfaces are ordinary. Sunday, they will all eat together.

**How we got here**

Two things converged in the 2030s. The first was a literacy movement. After a decade of visible institutional failure — pandemics botched, platforms optimizing for rage, housing strangled by veto players — a coalition of educators, economists, and open-source developers built the coordination curriculum: Nash equilibria taught in fourth grade through playground games, Goodhart's Law taught through school grading itself, prediction markets run on cafeteria menus. It spread because it worked; students who could name a coordination failure could sometimes fix one. Within a generation, "you're proposing an all-pay auction and calling it fairness" was something ordinary voters could say, and did.

The second was abundance. Advanced AI, tightly licensed after the near-misses of the late 2030s, made the resource pie grow by orders of magnitude — fusion, molecular manufacturing, asteroid metals. Negotiators trained in the new literacy noticed what their predecessors would have missed: when everyone's plausible share is a thousandfold their present wealth, fighting over the split is irrational, but only if the split is credibly guaranteed. So they built the guarantee first. The Concord of 2044 established joint governance of frontier AI, verified by mutually audited systems; a Universal Endowment paying every human a share of off-world resource flows and Earth's Georgist land rents (the raw planet, everyone was taught in second grade, belongs to everyone; only added value belongs to its maker); and the charter system — the legal architecture of plural futures.

**The architecture**

Humanity did not converge on one good life, and the Concord's framers — who had all diagrammed the wars that forcing convergence would cause — never tried. Earth is organized into charter regions: Sabbath cantons living agrarian or twentieth-century lives; Continuity regions, most cities, where AI is a regulated tool; Ascent Zones exploring augmentation; and beyond the atmosphere, the Frontier, where uploaded minds build at machine speed and send the wealth home. Every charter is also, explicitly, an experiment: each publishes its rules, its predicted outcomes, and its review dates, and the Chroniclers — a profession somewhere between judge, auditor, and Wikipedia at its best, funded by the customary 15% civic tithe — report the results without spin. Failed experiments close honorably; successful ones get copied. Civilization learned to treat governance the way it treats medicine: hypotheses, trials, published reasoning, results checked three years later.

Four rules keep the plurality honest:

*The Right of Passage.* Anyone may migrate between charters after informed consent — border residencies, conversations with returnees. Children everywhere learn the common curriculum: what exists elsewhere, and how to leave. Voluntary simplicity that forbids exit is not simplicity; it is a cage, and no charter may be one.

*The Floor.* No charter may permit cruelty: no slavery, no denial of medicine to children, no coerced reproduction or coerced abstention from it — the Concord's framers, remembering older utopias that bred for happiness, wrote that clause in first. The Floor prohibits harm, not difference. What consenting adults do behind their own doors is nobody's experiment to end.

*The Firewall.* Superintelligent systems may not govern, persuade, or economically compete with unenhanced humans. Their licensed work — curing disease, restoring climate (carbon passed back through 330 ppm in 2077), building the Frontier — flows through the Endowment. Communities may decline their answers; the Kyoto Mathematical Commons famously refused the machine proof of the Riemann Hypothesis and is happily still working.

*The Ledger.* All public reasoning is published. Judges write out their logic for review. Officials are described by their actual selection mechanism, not by majestic titles. Inventors are paid by retrospective public prizes — audited shares of the value created — rather than monopoly patents, so a cure invented anywhere is manufactured everywhere the next month, and its inventor is rich and honored anyway.

**What it bought**

Work persists, transformed. With the Endowment as floor and the economy running deliberately hot, employment became symmetrical: salons compete for stylists, employees choose which job, and children apprentice through micro-trials instead of buying credentials. Those who can't or won't join the bustle live well in Quiet Places, unstigmatized — the Endowment asks nothing of anyone. Status competition survives, as it must, but it plays out in craft, sport, and the enormous prestige of being the person who spotted the bad equilibrium first.

**The evening**

Sunday comes. Ines's brother arrives with wine from vines he prunes by hand; her daughter arrives with music composed for ears Ines doesn't have, transposed down into twelve notes because love translates. Over dinner they argue — cheerfully, with the shared vocabulary every schoolchild owns — about whether the canton's new admission rules pass the Floor. They check the Chronicle. They make a small bet on the review.

Humanity never agreed on what the good life is. It agreed on something better: that everyone would learn to see the traps, that the commons would be honestly kept, that experiments would be run and reported truly, and that between all our different answers, the doors would always, always stay open.
