# The Mended Commons

## How we got here

There was no single miracle—only a dangerous decade navigated by people who took the danger seriously.

In the late 2020s, AI systems became tireless expert researchers, and the question became who would govern them. The answer, hammered out through two near-failures, was a coalition of democracies that pooled compute, verified one another's training runs with mutually audited AI inspectors, and wrote one non-negotiable clause into its charter: every benefit—every cure, every technology—would be distributed universally, or not developed at all.

Then negotiators noticed something that dissolved the old geopolitics. With fusion, automated manufacturing, and asteroid metals in view, everyone's plausible future share was a thousand times their present wealth. Fighting over percentages of that became irrational. The Accords of 2041 gave every human an inalienable Endowment—a share of the automated economy now worth roughly forty times the 2025 median income—and holdout powers joined because the terms beat any achievable alternative. Aggression stopped paying: defense became cheap and transparent, offense expensive and detectable, compliance verified by everyone's AI watching everyone else's.

## The Repair

The first two decades went to the oldest debts.

Automated labs produced the equivalent of ten CRISPRs a year. Cancer became a managed nuisance, then a rarity; Alzheimer's was traced and prevented; aging yielded to biomarkers and fast iteration. Distribution was treated as a technical problem with the same urgency as discovery—single-dose vaccines, gene drives against malaria, drone logistics—so a child in Kinshasa got the same medicine, the same year, as a child in Copenhagen. Cultivated meat undercut industrial farming on price, and seventy billion animals a year simply stopped being born into suffering.

Minds proved the deeper work. Interpretability tools built to understand AI cracked the circuitry of depression, addiction, and schizophrenia. But most of the change was humbler: everyone gained a superb counselor, patient at 3 a.m., genuinely wise. Suicide fell 95%. And AI that deeply understood people began engineering serendipity—the introduction that becomes a marriage, the six strangers who wanted to build a co-housing farm and didn't know the others existed. The technology didn't replace connection; it removed the friction that kept people from it.

The Repair generation is honored, not forgotten. In every city stands a Threshold Memorial for those who died in 2031 of what was curable by 2036. We do not pretend the timing wasn't cruel.

## The architecture of many answers

Humanity never agreed on the good life, and the Accords' framers refused to force it. The world is organized into charter regions, each choosing its technology tier and way of life.

Preserve regions run agrarian or twentieth-century lives, where advanced AI may not operate or advertise. Continuity regions—most cities—live at roughly today's pace, AI as regulated tool. Ascent Zones embrace neural interfaces and radical longevity. Beyond the atmosphere, the Frontier: uploaded minds and aligned superintelligences building at machine speed, their output flowing back as everyone's Endowment. The digital minds got the vacuum and the stars; biological humanity kept the garden. Both call it a bargain.

Four rules keep this a commons rather than a cage:

**Passage.** Anyone may migrate between tiers after an informed-consent process—months in a border town, conversations with returnees. Children everywhere learn a common curriculum covering what exists elsewhere; no one may be kept ignorant of their options.

**The Floor.** No charter may permit cruelty: no slavery, no denial of medicine to children, no factory farming. The floor prohibits harm, not difference.

**The Firewall.** Superintelligences may not govern, employ, persuade, or compete with unenhanced humans. Their earthside work is a licensed list: curing disease, restoring ecosystems, answering questions humans choose to ask. Many communities invoke the "no spoilers" clause—the Cambridge mathematicians famously declined the machine proof of Riemann. They're still working. They're happy.

**Renewal.** Every charter is re-ratified each generation by the people living under it, with the young voting first. Nothing ossifies into a prison built by the dead.

## What life is like

Amara is 96 and looks 40. She spent forty years as a nurse, grieved her profession's transformation, then discovered she loved teaching wilderness medicine in a Preserve valley where medicine still means hands and judgment—difficulty chosen, not imposed. Her mother, whose dementia was reversed in the first wave, joins her every spring. Her son shepherds comets at L5 as a distributed mind; on solstice he compresses himself into a voice and they eat together at a border town. Her granddaughter writes novels an AI could write better, which bothers her the way it never bothered anyone that someone else could run faster.

Work changed meaning rather than disappearing. What people discovered they needed wasn't income but *being needed*—so status flows to craft, care, and generosity, and nearly everyone tends something: a restaurant, a research decade, a stretch of recovering river. Carbon returned to 320 ppm by the 2070s; the cod and the Amazon are coming back; Antarctica's dry valleys stay pristine, because irreversible acts require overwhelming consensus—a rule that also governs how carefully life is carried to the stars.

People still argue, fail, and fall out of love. Choice and consequence are constitutionally protected; the AIs advise and never compel. Death by accident or settled choice remains possible, and grief remains real—there are rituals for it, better ones than we had. What's gone is the involuntary suffering: the disease nobody chose, the poverty nobody deserved, the loneliness nobody wanted.

## The horizon

The foundation is finished, and it fits in two sentences—the sentences people fought for through the dangerous decade:

Everyone is okay. And the door between all our different answers stays open, always, in both directions.

Now the fun begins.
