# The Ratified World

Nothing about our world was inevitable, and everything about it is revocable. That, we have come to believe, is why it works.

The path began in the 2020s with near-misses: a pandemic caught late, an AI system that steered an election before anyone noticed, a decade of institutions spending public trust like water. Two unglamorous responses compounded like interest.

The first was the **verification treaties**. The frontier AI labs, facing mutual ruin, submitted to inspection the way nuclear powers once had—grudgingly, with observers and audits and close calls. The systems that emerged from that decade were not gods but negotiators: superhuman at modeling what people actually wanted, constitutionally barred from acting without consent, every consequential decision leaving a trail any citizen can read. The breakthrough was never raw intelligence. It was *legitimacy*.

The second was **coordination literacy**. Schools were rebuilt around a simple premise: every citizen should understand incentives the way every citizen understands arithmetic. Children learn what a commons is by managing one—a garden, a tool library, a creek—and learn what a bad equilibrium is by watching their own playground bargains collapse and recover. This didn't make people saints. It made defection *legible*, which turned out to be most of the battle.

On these foundations came the **Common Inheritance settlement**: the things no one made—land, spectrum, orbital slots, the atmosphere's capacity to absorb carbon—are leased at auction, and the rents flow to every person as a dividend. What you build with your labor remains yours. The dividend isn't lavish, but no one works from desperation, and employers compete for workers rather than the reverse. More people work than before, because work stripped of coercion turns out to be something humans want.

Then, in 2059, the great trade: the **Accords of Terraces**. Humanity's values point in different directions, but they mostly want *different things*. The part of you that wants Sunday dinner with your grandmother needs a kitchen, not a galaxy. So the cosmos was divided by ambition rather than by war.

**Earth remains recognizably Earth.** Children scrape their knees; the knees heal, because some pain teaches. But no child dies of leukemia, no one sleeps under an overpass, and the last factory farm closed decades ago—half the planet is rewilding, and restoration ecology is honorable, well-paid work. Your mother's grave is still on the hill where you left it.

**The inner terraces**—the Moon, the orbital rings, Mars—are where the eager go. Aging is a treatable syndrome there; self-modification is legal but *laddered*: you may change your mind only as fast as you demonstrate you understand what you're changing. Grief is never deleted—only its paralysis, never its lessons.

**Beyond Neptune**, probes carry the seeds of projects we can barely describe—minds optimized for discovery and forms of flourishing our descendants voted into being after a century of deliberation. The utilitarians got the stars they hungered for; the gardeners got the Earth they loved, protected by treaty and by defenses that make home cheaper to keep than to take. Between terraces stand courteous veils: Earth is not bombarded with news of incomprehensible splendor, because meaning is fragile—but anyone may look outward, and anyone may walk the ladder, at any age, at their own pace.

Every person has an **Advocate**: an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, forbidden to deceive them and forbidden to let others deceive them. It whispers true things at the moment of need—that this contract is a trap, that your brother's silence is grief, not anger. You can always dismiss the whisper. It never commands.

And here is what we improved upon every past dream of paradise: **nothing rules by inertia**. Every charter—the treaties, the dividend, the Accords themselves—carries a sunset clause. Every twenty-five years comes the **Ratification**: a season of deliberation, taught to children the way we once taught civics badly, in which each generation must re-adopt, amend, or strike down the laws it inherited. The system survives not because it is entrenched but because, four times a century, millions of ordinary people examine the bridge and choose to keep crossing it. Twice, whole provisions have been torn out. Both times, the world got better.

Daily life is plural—an archipelago of cultures bound by a thin charter: free exit, honest accounting, and the education of children in the common literacies so they can leave if they choose. Disputes go to mediators first; courts publish their full reasoning; restitution is common, prisons nearly empty. The **Chroniclers**, funded by the dividend and sworn to calibrated record, replaced the attention-auction that once passed for news. People still argue furiously. They argue from a shared ledger of facts.

Discoveries are published freely from the open institutes, whose Halls display not statues but lineages—a schoolchild can trace her insulin patch through four named, thanked, and paid generations of researchers. And when any person can gather thirty people whose lives they materially shaped, the community hosts the **Feast of the Household** at public expense: grievances mediated beforehand, the struggling quietly provided for, each guest giving account of what they will build next. It is how we remember that economies are made of people, and people are made by each other.

When a life on Earth ends, the Advocate asks the question its person would have wanted asked—*would you, on reflection, wish to continue?*—and honors the answer either way. The woman who believes death should be final is not resurrected. Her sister wakes on the ring, young again, weeping and laughing.

We did not become wiser creatures. We built our institutions like engineers build bridges—assuming wind, load, and human weakness—then handed every child the blueprints and the vote. There are still unclimbed mountains, unsolved equations, and neighbors arguing over fence lines that no intelligence will settle for them.

Somewhere tonight a sixteen-year-old is signing her first Ratification, frowning at clause nine, drafting an amendment.

Good. It was always meant to be hers.
