# The Unfinished World

The turning point, historians agree, was not a single invention but a refusal. In the late 2020s, after two near-catastrophic deployment failures, the major powers did the improbable thing: they slowed down. Verified compute treaties, adversarial audits, a decade of unglamorous negotiation—and out of it came the Menders: AI systems proven, by architectures designed for the purpose, to be *nonsentient*. Not suffering servants, not overshadowing gods—tools, magnificently capable and demonstrably empty inside, forbidden by deep design from deceiving, from modeling a mind without consent, or from acting on anyone who hadn't said yes with understanding.

The Menders' first great work was not abundance. It was *law*: a legible framework of perhaps two hundred rules—short enough that children learn them by twelve—governing what cannot be done to another person. Within that frame, they went hands-off. The founders had understood something crucial: a world where a benevolent power fixes everything is a world where nothing you do matters. So the Menders repair the frame and otherwise leave the game to the players.

---

It is 2131. Ito is 118, with a body he keeps at a weathered 45 because he climbs, and climbing at apparent-25 felt like cheating. He lives in Kestrel Commons, one of half a million federated communities of a few thousand souls—small enough that reputation is real and the person who sets your broken arm also loses to you at chess. Nobody works to survive; the Universal Endowment sees to that. But almost everyone works, because the alternative was tried in the 2060s and produced a documented epidemic of misery. The lesson is now taught alongside arithmetic: *humans are built for earned things.*

What Ito earns, currently, is atmospheric science. His guild is re-deriving climate dynamics from scratch—instruments they built, mathematics they reconstructed—because the Menders' answers exist behind what everyone calls the Veil: knowledge available on request, but requesting is a formal act, publicly logged, and most guilds treat it the way athletes treat motorized bicycles. The deepest pleasure his civilization discovered was not having answers but *reaching* them, and so it deliberately preserved a frontier of ignorance. Whole domains—the ocean trenches, the far lunar surveys, three of the mathematics the Menders developed—are sealed by referendum, waiting to be discovered rather than downloaded. Children grow up knowing the world contains genuine surprises, that next year holds news no one can spoil.

His mind is larger than the one he was born with. Augmentation exists, but the culture learned from early tragedies—the Rushed, whose hasty self-editing left them subtly broken—to treat it like mountaineering: staged, reversible where possible, each expansion consolidated for years before the next. Ito took his second expansion at 80. He can now hold about four hundred genuine friendships, and his community has grown to match; Dunbar's number, it turned out, was a parameter, not a prison. But the Guardrail Protocol stands over every irreversible door: a year's wait, counsel from those who chose otherwise, consent renewed three times. Temptations engineered to be resisted daily were recognized as a form of assault; the world simply doesn't place loaded devil's-offers in your path. If you want the permanent upload, the memory edit, the radical rewrite, you must walk deliberately toward it, and the walk is long on purpose.

Suffering was not deleted—it was rebalanced. The old world could produce agony far more easily than joy; the new one closed that asymmetry. No child is born to abuse. No one drowns in depression they didn't choose to work through, supported. The factory farms closed in the 2040s when cultured meat won on price. But grief, defeat, heartbreak, the burn of a summit not reached—these remain, because a life without stakes is a story without a plot, and people, given the honest choice, kept the stakes. Ito's marriage of sixty years ended eight years ago. It still hurts. He has declined, three times, the offer to make it not hurt.

Governance is sortition and assembly, with Menders as transparent civil servants whose every recommendation publishes its full reasoning. Politics is boring, which was the point. The losers of any vote retain exit rights: found a commons that does it your way. There are monastic communes and sensory-maximalist arcologies and a settlement of eleven thousand who have partially merged their minds and report, unsettlingly, that it's wonderful. Most people find at least one flourishing subculture obscene. The founders would have found *all* of it unsettling—the four-parent families, the citizens wintering as flocks of birds in persistent virtual worlds, the funerals for people who chose their deaths at 200, satisfied. A world that had stayed comfortable to twentieth-century eyes would have been a world that had stopped moving.

And there is somewhere to go. Slow ships crawl toward eleven stars, crewed by volunteers who understood exactly what they chose. The star system itself is being unsealed one region at a time, deliberately, so that each generation inherits a frontier rather than a completed museum. Purpose did not run out when the last famine ended; it migrated—from rescue to creation, from triage to art and science and the raising of minds.

At dusk Ito eats with his circle—bread from their ovens, arguments from their reading, a child demanding he explain cyclones *without looking anything up*. Above them, more stars than his grandmother ever saw, because the cities voted to dim their lights, and everyone understood why.

The secret was never abundance, and never safety, though they built both. The secret was refusing to finish the world—keeping it lawful at the frame and wild inside it, easy to survive and hard to master, so that every person wakes into a game still worth playing, among people they cannot fool and would not want to.
