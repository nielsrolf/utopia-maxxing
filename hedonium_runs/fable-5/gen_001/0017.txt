# The Terraced World

The path here was narrower than anyone expected. In the late 2020s, the frontier AI labs, facing mutual ruin, submitted to a verification regime the way nuclear powers once had: grudgingly, incrementally, with inspectors and treaties and close calls. The systems that emerged from that decade were not gods but negotiators—superhuman at modeling what people actually wanted, constitutionally barred from acting on it without consent. The breakthrough was not intelligence. It was *legitimacy*: a decades-long process, part market, part parliament, part global deliberation, that produced the Concordat of 2061.

The Concordat's insight was simple. Humanity's values point in different directions, but they mostly want *different things*. The part of you that wants Sunday dinner with your grandmother needs a kitchen, not a galaxy. The part of you that wants the universe filled with flourishing needs galaxies, and can wait a billion years for them. So the cosmos was terraced.

**Terrace Zero is Earth**, and Earth is recognizably Earth. Children still scrape their knees learning to ride bikes; the knees still heal in a week, because some pain teaches. But no child dies of leukemia, no one sleeps under an overpass, and the last factory farm closed in 2064. People work—at restoration ecology, at teaching, at absurdly ambitious cheesemaking—because the machines that could do everything are, by law and by their own preference, doing something else, further out. Jerusalem is still Jerusalem. Your mother's grave is still on the hill where you left it.

Every person has a **Steward**: an intelligence tuned to them alone, sworn to their interests, forbidden to deceive them and forbidden to let others deceive them. Stewards do not command. They whisper true things at the back of your mind at the moment you need them—that this investment is a scam, that your brother's silence is grief and not anger. You can dismiss the whisper. Most people, eventually, stop wanting to.

**The First Terrace**—the Moon, the orbital rings, Mars—is where the eager go. Life extension is unremarkable there; a two-hundred-year-old woman is redesigning Hellas Basin's watershed and has opinions about your irrigation plan. Self-modification is legal but *laddered*: you may adjust your mind only as fast as you demonstrate you understand what you're adjusting. Removing crippling anxiety takes an afternoon of counseling. Rewiring your deepest motivations takes years of supervised apprenticeship, because the Concordat's first commandment is *take the time, take the effort, and you may become what you desire*. Grief is never deleted—only its paralysis, never its lessons.

**The Second Terrace**, among the outer moons and in vast orbital computers, is where humanity gets strange, joyfully. People run parallel lives, wear bodies like clothing, sail methane seas on Titan in flesh built for it. Whole cultures form, flourish, and dissolve—medieval villages, alien cuisines, decade-long collaborative games whose stakes feel real because participants have chosen to make them feel real. Politics survives out here as sport and as substance: contests of persuasion and creation whose outcomes genuinely steer the settlement of the Kuiper Belt, because the Concordat's second commandment is *the machines will propose; humans dispose*.

**Beyond Neptune, the exotic terraces begin.** Probes departed in the 2080s carrying the seeds of things we can barely describe: minds optimized for discovery, for beauty, for forms of welfare our descendants' descendants voted into being after a century of careful moral deliberation. Ninety-nine percent of the reachable universe belongs to these projects. This was the great trade, and it was fair: the utilitarians and the transhumanists got the stars they hungered for; the gardeners and the grandmothers got the Earth they loved, *permanently*, protected by treaty and by defense systems that make the inner terraces cheaper to protect than to take.

Between terraces stand **the veils**—not walls, but courtesies. Earth is not bombarded with news of incomprehensible splendor in Andromeda, because meaning is fragile and a farmer's harvest should not feel small. But the veils are one-way and voluntary. Anyone may look outward. Anyone may *move* outward, at any age, by walking the ladder at their own pace; the journey typically takes years, and the journey is the point. And no one is trapped by death: when a Terrace Zero life ends, the Stewards ask the question the person would have wanted asked—*would you, on reflection, wish to continue?*—and honor the answer either way. The woman who believes death should be final is not resurrected. Her sister wakes on the First Terrace, young again, weeping and laughing, with a granddaughter waiting to show her the world.

What was improved from every past dream of paradise is this: **nothing was mourned**. The traditionalists did not lose their Earth. The ambitious did not lose their infinity. The dying did not lose their choice. Even the people who wanted struggle kept it—there are terraces where the mountains are unclimbed and the equations unsolved and the Stewards stay respectfully silent unless you're about to actually die.

And it remains unfinished, deliberately. The Concordat's last commandment reads: *This is a trajectory, not a destination. Problems will remain. They are yours.* Somewhere on Terrace Zero tonight, two neighbors are arguing about a fence line, and no superintelligence will settle it for them, and both of them, walking home angry under ordinary stars, would tell you honestly that they live in the best of all worlds so far—

—so far.
