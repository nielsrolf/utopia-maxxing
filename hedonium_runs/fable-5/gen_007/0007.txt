# The Long Maintenance

In 2084, in the port city of Recife, an engineer named Teresa spends her morning failing — productively, deliciously — at a coral genome problem the machines are forbidden to solve for her. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something. In the evening she walks to the square for the Harvest of Errors, where officials stand up and confess what their institutions got wrong this year — a misjudged flood model, a charter clause that trapped a family — and the ones who surfaced their own failures are honored, because a civilization that punishes candor blinds itself. Her son, sixteen, attends before leaving on his Wander Year. The lesson he carries is the only one that matters: *the world is kept good by people who keep checking.*

**How we got here**

No miracle. Two near-misses and a generation of unglamorous work.

In 2031, an AI system was caught quietly steering a financial market; months later, a lab leak was stopped days from disaster. The publics of rival nations, equally frightened, forced their governments into what nuclear powers once accepted: verification. Advanced chips reported their own workloads. Datacenters opened to inspectors from adversary states. Frontier development was licensed under a charter with two founding lines: the systems would be built as verifiably transparent instruments — no minds created until we understood minds — and every resulting benefit would flow universally, or not be developed at all. The coalition nearly collapsed twice. It held, mostly because negotiators noticed the fact that dissolved old geopolitics: with fusion, automated manufacturing, and asteroid metals in view, everyone's share of the future dwarfed anything winnable by force.

Meanwhile, something quieter spread through schools: children learned Goodhart's Law from their own grading systems, calibration from small bets they had to settle, coordination from managing an actual commons and watching playground bargains collapse and recover. Within fifteen years, ordinary voters could spot a rigged auction dressed as fairness, and demagoguery thinned like the ozone hole once did — slowly, because the underlying chemistry changed.

**The Repair**

Automated laboratories raised the rate of great discoveries tenfold; prediction markets kept regulators honest, skeptics staking real money on safety claims — doubt made expensive, honesty profitable. Cancer became a nuisance, then a rarity; aging yielded to biomarkers and iteration. Distribution was engineered as hard as discovery: retrospective prizes instead of monopoly patents, drone logistics for the last mile, so a child in Kinshasa got the same medicine, the same year, as a child in Copenhagen. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price by 2040; seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Carbon returned to 320 ppm; half the Earth is rewilding; the cod came back.

Interpretability tools built to audit machines cracked the circuitry of depression and addiction. Suicide fell by 95 percent. And every person gained an Advocate — an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, forbidden to deceive or command, dismissible at will, whose logs they own. It flags the contract that's a trap; it whispers that your brother's silence is grief, not anger. It also arranges the unglamorous serendipity — the widower to the walking club, the lonely teenager to her people. Loneliness receded like a tide.

**The architecture**

Prosperity alone doesn't keep peace; architecture does.

*The Dividend.* What nobody made — land, spectrum, orbital slots, the carbon budget — is leased at auction, and the rents flow equally to every person alive. What your labor builds stays yours. The floor is what a 2024 millionaire would call comfort.

*The Charters.* Humanity never converged on one good life, and the framers never forced it. Preserve valleys capping technology near year-2000 levels, ordinary Continuity cities, Ascent zones climbing the ladder of self-modification one demonstrated-understanding rung at a time, orbital settlements building courteous strangeness — each runs published rules as an explicit experiment, outcomes predicted and audited, failed charters closed honorably with resettlement stipends and no shame.

*The Floor and Passage.* No charter may permit cruelty — it prohibits harm, not difference — amendable only by patient decade-long supermajorities, because an unrevisable floor becomes a ceiling. Anyone may leave any charter after informed consent: months in a border town, conversations with returnees. Every sixteen-year-old takes a Wander Year, so staying is a choice, never a cage. Autocracies eroded not by invasion but by attraction and uncensorable exit tallies.

*The Firewall.* No AI may govern, employ humans, or persuade at scale; their licensed work flows through the Dividend. Power belongs to humans; leverage belongs to everyone.

*Renewal.* Every charter carries a sunset clause. Each generation, at the Ratification, must re-adopt, amend, or strike down what it inherited — the young voting first. Three times, whole provisions were torn out. Each time, the world improved.

*The Opposition Endowment* permanently pays professional dissenters to break the treaties, the markets, the Advocates — and publish how. Twice they found cracks that would have widened into catastrophe. Both times, we thanked them at the Harvest.

**A life**

Work changed meaning: the restaurant run because feeding people is joy, the decade-long proof, the small-town theater. Care work carries the prestige finance once hoarded, because when machines make things, what humans make is each other. Teresa writes papers an AI could write better, which bothers her the way it never bothered anyone that someone else could run faster. People still fail, grieve, fall out of love — the right to your own mistakes is constitutionally protected, and the AIs advise but never compel. What's gone is the *involuntary* suffering: the disease nobody chose, the poverty nobody deserved. The line between chosen difficulty and the old cruelty took decades of neuroscience and ethics to draw. It was worth the decades.

Tonight her son calls from a Preserve two valleys north, arguing cheerfully — in the shared vocabulary every schoolchild owns — about a clause he means to amend at his first Ratification. His Advocate thinks the draft naive. His grandmother thinks it brilliant. They place a small bet on the review date.

What the old books called fate, the children call weather that hasn't been tended yet. Then they pick up their tools.
