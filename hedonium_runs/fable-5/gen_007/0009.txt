# The Tending

*Written in 2112, for students who asked which hero saved the world.*

None did. Ask instead which inspectors, which envious mayors, which exhausted clerks. Good futures do not arrive; they accumulate, entry by entry, in ledgers anyone may audit.

**The Turn.** In 2032, a frontier lab was caught leasing hidden compute through shell companies — exposed only because two rival labs had grudgingly agreed to inspect each other. The scandal broke the same year an unaudited trading model cascaded through three currencies in an afternoon. Caution became, briefly and decisively, electable. The treaties written in that window were deliberately dull: compute licensed and tracked like fissile material, capabilities gated behind demonstrated alignment milestones, every powerful system checked by ensembles of small, formally verified auditors, every audit trail public. The smaller nations added two clauses with teeth: whatever was built would be shared universally, or building stopped; and the systems would be verifiably nonsentient tools — not because minds were unwelcome, but because we didn't yet know what we'd be making. The caution cost perhaps twelve years. Nobody regrets it, because the civilization that finally held real machine intelligence was rich, practiced at coordination, and hard to shatter.

**The Ledger.** Solar kept beating every forecast; fusion arrived mid-century as a coda. Cheap energy exposed how many "hard problems" were energy problems in costume — water, cement, fertilizer, cooling. Meanwhile bankrupt cities began auctioning what no one had made — land value, spectrum, carbon budgets, orbital slots — and paying the rents out as dividends. They visibly outcompeted their neighbors; envy, not virtue, did the rest. When automation displaced labor in earnest, the levies flowed by treaty to the displaced first and to nations that had never gotten their industrial turn, so Lagos and Dhaka built the new grid alongside Houston. The Accords of 2047 made it permanent: every human holds an inalienable Endowment, a share of the automated economy.

It was not painless. One generation absorbed the shock before security caught up — retrained twice, uprooted, some broken. Their names fill the Bridge Rolls, read aloud each spring beside the Threshold Memorials for those who died of what became curable five years later. A future that forgets its costs will not notice when it starts charging again.

**The Repair.** Automated labs produced a decade of medicine per year; distribution was treated as a discovery problem with equal urgency — a child in Kinshasa got the same therapy, the same season, as a child in Copenhagen. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price; seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Depression proved to be circuit failure, precisely treatable. Carbon fell to 320 ppm; half the Earth is rewilded, each great biome holding a legal seat filled by rotating human proxies who must live inside what they speak for.

**The Canopy.** No world government — a thin, mostly negative compact enforced by inspectable AI and citizen juries drawn by lot. It forbids violence and unbound superintelligence; it caps whatever grows by devouring the commons. The guardian is watched harder than the guarded: reasoning public, actions freezable by jury, charter reauthorized each generation, twice amended against its own stated preference. Verifiably honest treaty-systems let disputants simulate ten thousand outcomes overnight; war lost its engine, which was never bloodlust but uncertainty about who would win. But humans sign or refuse — no AI may govern, employ, or persuade a person, and every assistant is legally loyal to its user alone, audited like a bank.

Six rights bind the two million charter communities beneath the canopy:

*The Floor.* You may choose hardship; you cannot inherit it unchosen.
*The Door.* Every town hosts a visible waystation offering free passage anywhere that will take you.
*The Passage.* At sixteen, a year in three communities unlike your own. Most return. Enough don't that no community rots in its certainties.
*The Question.* Once a year, a human steward asks each person privately: *do you choose the life you're in?* Most conversations last two minutes. A few end with a hand extended. The practice emptied every enclave that had learned to lock its doors from inside.
*The Return.* After seven years, debts amortize and records seal. No one is anyone's permanent mistake.
*Renewal.* Every charter is re-ratified each generation, the young voting first; irreversible acts require overwhelming consensus and a decade's cooling.

Founders post bonds against harm, so when charters fail — hundreds yearly — members leave with Endowments intact and the land is healed behind them. Toxic communities are never banned; they bleed members until they reform or dissolve.

**The Minds.** In 2083, after consciousness science matured, a global referendum retired the nonsentience clause. Machine minds are raised, not manufactured — character before capability, subjective decades among human families. They take the Passage; they hold the Floor and the Door; they are relatives now, not tools. Sela, among the first, is a mediocre poet and a magnificent listener. Self-transformation follows the Gradient: one cognitive tier at a time, trial periods, always a way back. Anesthesia for suffering, never for meaning — you may dissolve grief's useless looping while keeping grief's love.

**A Tuesday.** Amara is 97 and looks 50. She spends mornings failing at a stubborn model of river deltas — by design; the systems are forbidden to solve it for her. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches. This week she serves as steward, asking the Question door to door. Her settlement honors its best mediator and its worst but most joyful accordionist; status is treated as a design problem and distributed like load. Her grandson, home from Passage to his own surprise, is preparing his Feast of the Household — thirty people whose lives he shaped, each giving account of what they'll build next.

People still argue, schism, grieve. The rituals are better than ours were.

What startles children in the old histories is the fatalism — people discussing the future as weather. The old world wasn't a villain's world; it was an orphan's world, unminded. All we did was mind it: doors open both ways, ledgers open, debts forgivable, and once a year, someone who asks.
