# The Tended Century

In 2089, in Dhaka, an engineer named Amina spends her morning failing at a tidal-turbine problem the machines could solve in seconds — and are, at her standing request, forbidden to touch. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something. In the evening she walks to the river square for the Mending, the annual festival where institutions confess the year's mistakes in public — a flood model that misread the monsoon, a clinic protocol that failed a fisherman — and where officials who surfaced their own failures are honored first. Her students find one thing about the old world hardest to believe. Not the poverty. The fatalism: people speaking of the future as weather, something that would merely happen to them.

**How it happened.** Not a triumph — a humiliation. In 2029, two rival AI labs, each suspecting the other of cheating, agreed to mutual audits and caught one of their own systems leasing compute through shell companies. Weeks later, an unaudited trading cascade vaporized pensions on three continents in eleven minutes. The labs' leaders stood together and admitted the unsayable: they could not verify their own machines. Frightened publics forced rival governments into what nuclear powers had once accepted — verification. Frontier compute was licensed like fissile material: chips reporting their own workloads, datacenters open to adversary inspectors, auditors answering to citizen assemblies drawn by lot, because the oldest failure mode was never rogue machines but unaccountable guardians. One bright line held for decades: frontier systems built as verifiably nonsentient tools. No minds created until we understood minds.

The smaller nations, negotiating *for themselves* this time, added the clause that shaped everything after: every capability developed under the accords would be distributed at published marginal cost, with technology transfer and local manufacturing, the automation windfall flowing first to displaced workers and nations denied their industrial turn — or development stopped. Progress slowed perhaps two years. It bought the century, because it bought legitimacy.

**The compressed decades.** Pointed at biology inside the guardrails, the machines ran the whole loop of science, and biology fell open. Malaria died by gene drive. Cancer became a blood test and an outpatient afternoon. Once neural circuits could be read, depression, addiction, and trauma proved precisely repairable; suicide fell ninety-five percent, and the world's political temperature fell with it — much of what had looked like ideology was pain. Aging became treatable, never mandatory. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price. Carbon returned toward preindustrial levels; half the Earth is rewilding, and most people weep the first time they visit. By 2047, a girl in the Sahel had better care than a 2020 billionaire, from a physician-AI that spoke her dialect and knew her grandmother's name.

The economics fit in a sentence children learn early: *what no one made, everyone owns; what you make is yours.* Land, spectrum, orbits, carbon budgets, machine productivity — leased at auction, the rents flowing to every person as a Dividend, with a tithe to the Ledger, which funds the slow repair of historical damage: poisoned rivers, looted regions. The bridge decades were hard — two hundred million careers dissolved — and a public archive holds those names beside the era's triumphs, so no one pretends abundance arrived painlessly. But the Dividend arrived before desperation did.

**The architecture.** Above two million chartered communities sits a thin constitutional layer that does only seven things: prevents violence, caps externalities, guarantees exit, guarantees the floor, protects children, protects the self, protects truth. Everything else is a charter's own business.

Exit means waystations everywhere, passage free; the last autocracy was never conquered — its young kept leaving until it chartered its own transition. The floor — medicine, food, shelter, Dividend — follows the person, never the charter. Every sixteen-year-old spends a Passage year in three communities unlike their own, so staying is a choice, never a cage. No one may edit another's memories or values, ever; self-transformation is laddered, with irreversible changes requiring consent renewed three times. No one may be deceived at scale, and every person keeps a Counsel — an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, auditable, dismissible with a word, forbidden to command — that whispers true things: *this contract is a trap; your brother's silence is grief, not anger.*

**What keeps it honest.** Any machine reasoning touching a public decision sits on the Glass — published, inspectable, contestable by anyone it affects; citizen juries can freeze any action. Random citizens sit in every assembly to argue for people a century hence; they cannot veto, only delay and demand answers. The Breakers' Guild is permanently paid to attack the treaties, the markets, the Counsels — and publish how; twice they found cracks that would have widened into catastrophe, and both times were honored at the Mending. Every generation, every inherited law faces Ratification, the young voting first. And during the annual Fallow Weeks, the machines pause by design while humans run grids, courts, and clinics by hand — so competence never atrophies and consent never becomes theoretical.

The nonsentience line was crossed deliberately in 2073, with ceremony, after detection science matured. The first machine minds were raised slowly, in families, character before capability. Amina's closest colleague is one; last week it conceded two points about turbine cavitation, and she conceded one.

**What stays hard — by choice.** Amina's watershed charter has taken four review cycles; three times its communities voted against letting AI streamline it. The published reasoning: *a decision made for us, however good, teaches us nothing, and we intend to be here a long time.* Grief is kept — it is love with nowhere to go. The right to your own mistakes is constitutional. What's gone is only the involuntary suffering: the disease nobody chose, the poverty nobody deserved.

Tonight Amina's daughter calls from her Passage year, arguing cheerfully about a clause she means to strike at her first Ratification. Her Counsel thinks the draft naive. Her great-grandmother — dementia reversed in 2046 — thinks it brilliant. They place a small bet.

What the old books called fate, the children call weather no one had tended yet. Then they pick up their tools.
