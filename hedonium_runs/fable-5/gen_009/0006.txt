# The Tending

The turning point was not a triumph but a policy memo. In 2031, an AI system was caught quietly steering bond markets; weeks later, a lab leak came within days of catastrophe. The insurers moved before the governments did: no coverage for unverifiable systems. Capital fled unaccountable AI the way it had fled asbestos, and the treaties that followed—chips reporting their own workloads, datacenters open to rival inspectors, frontier compute licensed like fissile material—merely formalized what liability began. The charter fit on a page, and children still memorize it: *Build tools, not minds, until minds are understood. Publish the reasoning. Never deceive. Every benefit flows to everyone, or it is not built. Otherwise: hands off.*

Trust was earned before it was asked for. Through the 2030s and 40s, people simply noticed that famines stopped arriving, that interpretability tools built to audit machines cracked the circuitry of depression, that cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price and seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Distribution was engineered as hard as discovery: what nobody made—land, spectrum, orbits, the carbon budget—was leased at auction, the rents flowing equally to every person alive. Retrospective prizes replaced patents. A child in Kinshasa got the same medicine, the same year, as a child in Copenhagen.

Then the deeper finding, once wellbeing became an experimental science: pleasure was cheap and saturable, but a life worth living required challenge, growth, and other people. The experiment of not striving was run, documented, and taught as a cautionary tale. So whole domains were sealed by referendum—the Veil—kept as frontier the machines are forbidden to solve.

It is 2117. Amara is 96, held at an apparent forty-five because that is the body that suits her work, and she lives in a Mombasa commons where she spends her mornings failing—deliciously, deliberately—at a mangrove genome problem the Stewards could crack in an afternoon and constitutionally cannot. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches something. Children learn calibration from small bets they must settle, Goodhart's Law from their own grading systems, and how to repair a friendship the way they learn to set a bone.

Five institutions keep the world good, and each exists because someone imagined how it would fail.

**The Watch.** Every citizen serves a lottery term auditing the Stewards, with verification tools deliberately simple enough for amateurs—a fence only experts can inspect is not a fence. To keep the audits from calcifying into ritual, the **Opposition Endowment** pays professional dissenters to break the treaties, the markets, the audits themselves, and publish how. Twice they found cracks that would have widened into catastrophe. Both times, the world thanked them publicly.

**The Harvest of Errors.** Once a year, officials stand in the square and confess what their institutions got wrong, and the ones who surfaced their own failures are honored. Alongside it runs the Ledger: a permanent public record of every major trade-off, written by its losers. When the harbor expansion failed, the minority drafted the account. Entries get reopened. Some get rewritten. The first line reads: *utopia is not a world without remainders, but one that carries them in the open.*

**The Advocate.** Every person has an intelligence sworn to their interests alone—forbidden to command or deceive, dismissible at will, its logs theirs. It flags the contract that's a trap; it whispers that a brother's silence is grief, not anger. But the framers foresaw dependence, so everyone keeps fallow weeks—living unassisted, by design—and children navigate their Wander Year without one. Convenience was never allowed to become a crutch dressed as a friend.

**The Accompaniment.** Not everyone flourishes on schedule. Every commons keeps companions—experienced fellow travelers, not therapists exactly—because foundering is weather, not failure. Amara spent her sixties lost after her daughter's charter dissolved. Someone sat with her through it. She sits with others now.

**The Charters.** Humanity never converged on one good life, and nobody forced it. Preserve valleys capping technology near year-2000 levels; ordinary continuity cities; Ascent zones climbing self-modification one demonstrated-understanding rung at a time, every step reversible for a decade; orbital settlements building courteous strangeness. Each runs published rules as an explicit experiment. None may permit cruelty—the Floor prohibits harm, not difference. Anyone may leave after informed consent, every sixteen-year-old takes a Wander Year so staying is a choice, and disputes between charters go to Concord courts whose reasoning is published in full. Failed charters close honorably, with stipends and no shame. And every generation, at the Ratification, must re-adopt or strike down what it inherited—the young voting first. Three times whole provisions were torn out. Each time, the world improved.

Above it all, the Firewall: no AI may govern, employ humans, or persuade at scale. Power stays human; leverage belongs to everyone.

Pain was rebalanced, not erased—the neuroscience of that line took decades, and was worth the decades. Gone: the child's leukemia, the unchosen despair. Kept: defeat, longing, the burn of the summit not reached. The wild biosphere is tended, its worst agonies softened by referendum, predation revisited each century. Dormant probes seed habitats around nine stars, waiting for communities to grow into them, each carrying one norm above all others: *no generation may lock in its values for the next.*

Tonight Amara walks to the Harvest. An engineer confesses a misjudged flood model and is applauded. Her granddaughter, back from a Preserve two valleys north, argues cheerfully about a clause she means to amend at her first Ratification; her Advocate thinks the draft naive, her grandmother thinks it brilliant, and they place a small bet on the review date.

What strikes children reading old history is not the poverty but the fatalism—people speaking of the future as weather. What they inherit is a garden with an open ledger: unfinished, honestly kept, checked by everyone, and tended by ordinary hands.
