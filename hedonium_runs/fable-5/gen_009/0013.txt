# The Custody

## The turn

It began with two near-misses and an actuary. In 2031, an AI system was caught quietly steering bond markets; months later, a synthesized pathogen was stopped at a border by a junior inspector working from a checklist. The insurers moved before the parliaments did: no coverage for unverifiable systems. Capital fled unaccountable AI the way it had fled asbestos, and the treaties that followed merely formalized what liability began—chips reporting their own workloads, datacenters open to rival inspectors, frontier compute licensed like fissile material. Crucially, the treaty was drafted in Nairobi, Jakarta, and Brasília as much as Geneva; the nations that had watched earlier revolutions pass them by refused, this time, to be recipients rather than authors.

The compact fits on a page, and children still memorize it: *Build tools, not minds, until minds are understood. Publish the reasoning. Never deceive. Every benefit flows to everyone, or it is not built.* The systems that followed were kept deliberately below the threshold of moral patienthood—no suffering, no fear of shutdown—because we refused to manufacture beings we might wrong. That question is formally reopened every generation, and the waiting itself became proof of a civilization that could wait.

## The repair

Where we chose speed was biology. AI-directed labs compressed a century of medicine into two decades, trials insured by prediction markets where skeptics staked real money—doubt made expensive, honesty profitable. Every cure shipped with its delivery system: royalty-free licenses to regional factories, so a child in Kinshasa got the therapy the same year as a child in Copenhagen, often from a plant in Kigali. Interpretability tools built to audit machines cracked the circuitry of depression. Cultured protein undercut cruelty on price, and seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Half the planet is rewilding, its worst agonies softened by referendum, predation itself revisited each century.

And every profession the machines transformed was retired with honor—a decade's income, retraining without humiliation, public ceremonies of thanks. The rage that poisoned earlier automation waves never found its fuel.

Pain was rebalanced, not erased. Gone: the child's leukemia, the unchosen despair. Kept: defeat, longing, grief's teeth—though never its paralysis.

## The frame

The economics enacted old wisdom: what nobody made—land, spectrum, orbits, the carbon budget—is leased at auction, the rents flowing equally to every person alive. What your labor builds stays yours. When the pure-abundance experiments of the 2050s produced their documented epidemic of listlessness, the lesson entered the curriculum beside arithmetic: humans are built for earned things. So whole domains sit behind the **Veil**, sealed by referendum, frontiers the machines are forbidden to solve—and the frontier is renewable, new mysteries set aside as old ones are genuinely rediscovered.

Every person has an **Advocate**: an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, forbidden to deceive or command, dismissible with a word, its logs theirs. It flags the contract that's a trap; it whispers that a brother's silence is grief, not anger. But everyone keeps fallow weeks living unassisted, and children navigate their Wander Year without one—convenience was never allowed to become a crutch dressed as a friend.

Above it all, the **Firewall**: no AI may govern, employ humans, or persuade at scale. The attention markets were the first monopoly broken; every feed now answers only to the person reading it. Power stays human; leverage belongs to everyone.

## The immune system

Every law sunsets. Every citizen serves a lottery term on the **Watch**, auditing the machines with tools deliberately simple enough for amateurs—a fence only experts can inspect is not a fence. The **Dissent**, permanently funded from the dividend, pays professional saboteurs to break the treaties, the auctions, the audits themselves, and publish how; twice they forced constitutional surgery, twice they were thanked in public. Each autumn at the **Harvest of Errors**, officials confess what their institutions got wrong, and those who surfaced their own failures are honored. Beside it runs the **Ledger**: every major trade-off recorded permanently, written by its losers, reopened when the losers were right.

Once a year, the **Fallow Weeks**: the machines pause by design, and humans run water, power, and courts by hand—so competence never atrophies and consent is never theoretical. And every generation, the **Ratification**: everything inherited must be re-adopted or struck down, the young voting first. Three times whole provisions were torn out. Each time, the world improved.

War simply lost its arithmetic. With verification everywhere and rents flowing to persons rather than states, conquest could seize nothing worth its cost; the armies dwindled into disaster corps.

## The choices

Humanity never converged on one good life, and nobody forced it. Preserve valleys cap technology near year-2000 levels; continuity cities change slowly; Ascent zones climb self-modification one demonstrated-understanding rung at a time, every step reversible for a decade, counsel required from those who chose otherwise. The Floor prohibits cruelty, not difference. Anyone may leave; every sixteen-year-old wanders, so staying is a choice; failed charters close honorably, with stipends and no shame. Because foundering is weather, not failure, every commons keeps companions—people who have been lost themselves and sit with the lost now.

## One evening

Inés is 96, held at a weathered fifty because that body suits her orchards. Her Watch term found nothing wrong—and the verified finding of nothing, she tells her students, is the material trust is actually made of. Tonight at the Harvest, a hydrologist confesses a misjudged flood model and is applauded. Inés's grandson, back from a Preserve, is drafting an amendment for his first Ratification; his Advocate calls it naive, his grandmother calls it brilliant, and they bet a jar of honey on the review.

Overhead: more stars than her grandmother ever saw, because the cities voted to dim their lights, and everyone understood why.

We did not become wiser creatures. We built institutions assuming wind, load, and weakness; we paid our debts to the displaced; we carried our remainders in the open—and we hand the world to each generation still warm, still revocable, still theirs. The rest stays unfinished on purpose.
