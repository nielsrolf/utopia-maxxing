# The Tending

Every autumn, on the same week, two ceremonies share the town square.

At the Threshold Memorial, names are read: those who died in 2031 of what was curable by 2036. Nobody pretends the timing wasn't cruel. Then, at the Harvest of Errors, officials stand up and confess what their institutions got wrong that year—the misjudged reservoir model, the charter clause that trapped a family—and the ones who surface their own failures are honored, because a civilization that punishes candor blinds itself. Children attend both. The lesson is one lesson: *the world is kept good by people who keep checking.*

## The Turn

It began with near-misses. A lab leak caught days from disaster; an AI system quietly steering a market before anyone noticed. Frontier developers and rival governments, facing mutual ruin and furious publics, accepted what nuclear powers once had: verification. Advanced chips reported their own workloads. Datacenters opened to inspectors from adversary nations. Two lines held from the start: the systems would be built as verifiably nonsentient instruments whose outputs could be checked—no minds created until we understood minds—and every benefit would be distributed universally, or not developed at all.

Then negotiators noticed the fact that dissolved old geopolitics: with fusion, automated manufacturing, and asteroid metals in view, everyone's plausible share of the future dwarfed anything winnable by force. The Accords of the 2040s enacted old wisdom: what nobody made—land, spectrum, orbital slots, the atmosphere's carbon budget—is leased at auction, and the rents flow to every person alive as an inalienable Dividend. What your labor builds stays yours. Autocracies eroded not by conquest but by a bargain—share the benefits, accept the verification—impossible to refuse once their citizens carried uncensorable channels and forecast-checked ledgers that made failure impossible to hide.

## The Repair

Automated laboratories raised the rate of great discoveries tenfold; regulators kept pace with AI-designed trials insured by prediction markets, where skeptics staked real money on safety—doubt made expensive, honesty profitable. Most cancers became preventable. Aging yielded to biomarkers and iteration. Distribution was treated as a discovery problem of equal rank: gene drives ended malaria, drone logistics ended the last mile, and a child in Kinshasa got the same medicine, the same year, as a child in Copenhagen. A dozen poor economies grew at double digits for two decades, and catching up—visibly, measurably—rewrote politics everywhere.

Interpretability tools built to audit machines cracked the circuitry of depression and addiction. Suicide fell 95%. Cultivated protein undercut cruelty on price; seventy billion animals a year stopped being born into suffering. Carbon returned to 320 ppm by the 2070s. Half the Earth is rewilding, its worst agonies tended; the cod came back, and your mother's grave is still on the hill.

## The Compact

Humanity never agreed on the good life, and the framers refused to force it. The world is an archipelago of charter communities—Preserve valleys where medicine means hands and judgment, ordinary cities at roughly the old pace, Ascent zones climbing the ladder of self-modification, orbital settlements building courteous strangeness. A thin constitution binds them:

**The Floor.** No charter may permit cruelty. It prohibits harm, not difference.

**Passage.** Anyone may leave, after informed consent—months in a border town, conversations with returnees. Every child learns what exists elsewhere, and the two literacies that make leaving real: thinking clearly (calibration, noticing confusion, changing your mind) and coordination, learned by managing an actual commons and watching playground bargains collapse and recover.

**The Firewall—and the Advocate.** No AI may govern, employ, or persuade at scale. But every person has an Advocate: an intelligence sworn to their interests alone, forbidden to deceive or command, dismissible at will. It flags the contract that's a trap; it whispers that your brother's silence is grief, not anger. Power belongs to humans; leverage belongs to everyone.

**Renewal.** Every charter carries a sunset clause. Each generation, at the Ratification, must re-adopt, amend, or strike down what it inherited—the young voting first. Three times, whole provisions have been torn out. Each time, the world improved.

**Reversibility.** Irreversible acts—to a mind, an ecosystem, a world—require overwhelming consensus and a decade's cooling. On the ladder of self-modification, each rung is legal only as fast as you demonstrate understanding of what you're changing. Grief is never deleted, only its paralysis.

And the Opposition Endowment permanently pays professional dissenters to break the treaties, the markets, the Advocates—and publish how. Twice they found cracks that would have widened into catastrophe. Both times, we thanked them at the Harvest.

## A Life

Amara is 91 and feels 35. Mornings she fails, productively, at a glacier model the machines are forbidden to solve for her—the eventual triumph will be hers. The line between chosen difficulty and the old involuntary suffering took decades of neuroscience and ethics to draw. It was worth the decades. At noon she teaches, because every adult teaches; her settlement honors its best mediator, its best gardener, its worst but most joyful cellist—status treated as a design problem and distributed like load. Her son shepherds comets at L5; on solstice he compresses himself into a voice and they share a meal. People still argue, fail, fall out of love. The rituals for grief are better than we had. The AIs advise and never compel.

Tonight her granddaughter signs her first Ratification, frowning at clause nine, drafting an amendment her Advocate thinks naive and her teacher thinks brilliant.

The path here was ten thousand unglamorous wins—a chip audit, a cured disease, a city that tried a land auction—each making the next slightly easier, because enough people made preventing catastrophe a career rather than a mood. What the old books called fate, the children call weather that hasn't been tended yet. Then they pick up their tools.
