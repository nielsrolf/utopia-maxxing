# The Mending

*Written for the 2099 census, which for the first time asked no one their nation—only their community, and whether they were happy in it.*

Nobody agrees when it began, which historians consider proof that it worked. Good futures don't arrive; they accumulate, entry by entry, in ledgers anyone can audit.

**The first entries were narrow escapes.** In the 2030s, a financial cascade run by unaudited AI models and a bio-lab leak traced to the same negligence made caution briefly, crucially popular. The treaties written in that window were deliberately boring: open-ended AI developed only in licensed institutes, every capability gated behind demonstrated alignment milestones, every powerful model checked by ensembles of small, formally verified auditors, every audit trail public. It cost perhaps fifteen years of speed. Nobody regrets the price, because the world that eventually held real machine intelligence was rich, practiced at coordination, and hard to shatter.

**The second entries were physical.** Solar kept beating forecasts; fusion arrived in the 2050s as a coda, not a rescue. Cheap energy exposed how many "hard problems" were energy problems in costume—water, fertilizer, cement, cooling. It didn't fix politics; it shrank the pie fights until politics could cope.

**The third entries bought legitimacy.** A few desperate cities began auctioning what no one made—land value, spectrum, orbital slots, the carbon budget—and paying the rents out as citizen dividends. They visibly outcompeted their neighbors; envy did the rest. When automation displaced labor in earnest, the levies flowed by treaty to the displaced first and to nations that had never gotten their industrial turn. That act of transition justice is why the whole structure held: Lagos and Dhaka built the new grid alongside Houston and Shenzhen, and nobody had reason to burn the ledger down. By the 2060s, a person's basic needs cost society roughly what a library card once did.

**Then agreement itself got cheap.** Verifiably honest AI negotiators let two nations disputing a river simulate ten thousand outcomes overnight and bargain from resolved uncertainty. War lost its engine—which was never bloodlust, but disagreement about who would win. And with survival and peace handled, the old question dissolved. People stopped asking "what is the best way to live?" and started asking "which way is mine, and who will live it with me?"

**The answer was the frontier, reopened.** Robot crews can raise a town in eighteen months—housing, clinics, greenhouses, fabrication shops—for less than a suburban house once cost. So people founded societies: two million chartered communities now, from forty souls to four million. Hand-tool valleys that voted to keep honest winter. Research atolls. Screen-free villages where dinner takes three hours. Competitive federations with real glory at stake. One improbably successful charter organized entirely around restoring wetlands.

Above them sits the **Compact**—thin on purpose, mostly negative, enforced by inspectable AI and rotating citizen juries. It prevents violence. It caps externalities: carbon, pathogens, self-replicating code, and any pattern that grows by devouring the commons. It faces reauthorization every twenty-five years and has three times been amended against its own stated preference—the guardian is watched harder than the guarded.

And it guarantees three personal rights, where earlier drafts had only two.

*The right to know*: every child at sixteen undertakes the Passage—a year in three communities unlike their own, hosted by welcomers, a beloved profession. Most return home. Enough don't that no charter rots in its certainties.

*The right to leave*: every community hosts a waystation, visible from its center, offering free transport anywhere that will take you. The neutral capitals take everyone.

*The right to be found*: this was the hard-won addition. Exit is worthless to those too crushed, too young, or too controlled to walk through a door. So once a year, every person on Earth speaks privately with a Compact steward—a human, aided but never replaced by machines—who asks one question: *do you choose the life you're in?* Most conversations last two minutes. A few last hours, and end with a hand extended. Charters screamed about intrusion for a generation. Then the practice quietly emptied every cult that had learned to lock its doors from the inside, and the screaming stopped.

Toxic communities are not argued down or banned. They bleed members until they reform or dissolve. Exit, plus the Visit, does what centuries of crusading could not.

**Two customs bind the archipelago.** Children everywhere learn the common literacies—reading, arithmetic, and *how systems fail*: they manage a real commons, watch their playground bargains collapse and recover, and grow up able to see the crack in the bridge. And when any person can gather thirty people whose lives they materially shaped, their community hosts a Feast of the Household at public expense, each guest giving account of what they'll build next. It's how we remember that economies are made of people, and people are made by each other.

**The rest followed.** Aging is treatable; death is chosen, usually late, never compulsory, never forbidden—and charters may hold either custom sacred, with the waystation open to the undecided. Factory farms closed, outcompeted. AI counselors, legally bound to serve only the client, did for mental health what antibiotics did for infection. Half of Earth is rewilded, the heritage world. Fifty million people live on Mars—not for the room, but because frontiers are fun.

The pessimists asked where meaning would come from without necessity. But purpose was never employment; it was mattering to people who know your name, and the charters manufacture mattering the way factories once manufactured cars. Your town needs you—to teach, to judge, to build the thing the charter exists to build. Machines could do it all better. Nobody cares, the way no one ever cared that cars outran sprinters.

We did not become wiser creatures. We stayed petty, jealous, gloriously ridiculous. We built institutions the way engineers build bridges—assuming the wind, assuming the load, assuming human weakness—and made every strut visible.

The old world wasn't a villain's world. It was an orphan's world, unminded.

We minded it—doors unlocked, ledger open, and once a year, someone who asks.
