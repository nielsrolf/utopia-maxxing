The Compact of Circles

In the middle decades of the twenty-first century, humanity learned two hard lessons. First, that forcing three hundred million strangers to share a single culture and moral code produces endless, bitter war. Second, that untethered individualism—every soul adrift in a global market of one—leaves people lonely, anxious, and numb. The Compact of Circles was born from the refusal to accept either fate.

The foundation is territorial and minimal. Cities remain physically mixed. You do not have to sell your house or abandon your family to change your way of life. A light but firm layer called the Commonweal guarantees the basics: no violence, no coercion, a floor of material security beneath every citizen, and strict environmental guardrails. Every child, regardless of their parents’ affinities, attends a Commonweal school until the age of fourteen, studying ecology, practical ethics, governance, and the scientific method. There they learn that the world is wider than their kitchen table.

Above this floor floats the world most people actually live in: the Circles. A Circle is a chartered community of shared values, usually between fifty and three hundred people, linked by mutual obligation. Some Circles demand rigorous ascetic discipline; others organize their lives around collaborative art, competitive sport, contemplative ritual, or experimental child-rearing. A person typically holds membership in two or three Circles at once—perhaps a local food-growing cooperative, a distributed network of molecular biologists, and a choir that meets in virtual concert halls and physical ruins alike. Every Circle maintains a public charter, a schedule of gatherings, and a “stewardship”: a specific responsibility to the broader civilization, such as maintaining a watershed, curating a library, or running a clinic. Purpose is not bought; it is owed to one’s Circle, and through it, to the world.

What makes this structure humane is the architecture of exit. Association is purely voluntary, and switching Circles is as routine as changing a subscription. Portable “liberty accounts”—accrued social credits, education vouchers, and health benefits—travel with the individual, so no one is trapped by a job or a jurisdiction. If a teenager raised in a stern, tradition-bound Circle finds her temperament better suited to a fellowship of chaotic programmers, she walks three blocks to the local charter house, pings her transfer, and begins her apprenticeship the next morning. The Commonweal does not police the internal norms of Circles, except to enforce one iron law: no child may be confined, no adult may be detained, and every member must see and consent to the charter they live under, renewed annually with full knowledge of alternatives.

Because people are no longer fighting over who controls the single national culture, the energy once spent on politics is directed toward the design of flourishing. The Compact learned that human beings do not want ease; they want meaningful challenge. Circles therefore compete—not in wealth, but in the richness of the lives they offer. A martial Circle might require its members to master sailing tall ships; a scholarly one might organize endless, rotating seminars on alien mathematics. There are no artificial superintelligences planning your joys, but neither is anyone forced to spend eight hours performing abstracted drudgery for a distant shareholder. Automation taxes fund the Commonweal floor; above it, people work within Circles on tasks they can see, touch, and understand.

The designers also understood the trap of low-bandwidth existence. Every chartered Circle must convene in the flesh at least twice a week, in spaces built for sensory depth—vaulted gardens, noisy workshops, kitchens that smell of fermentation and woodsmoke. High-fidelity virtuality is permitted, even celebrated, but it is regulated as a tool, not a substitute for the friction and sweat of embodied life. Romance and rivalry remain complicated, because human minds are not simplified to fit algorithms; they are entangled with neighbors whose children attend the same Commonweal schools even while their parents live by different creeds.

Critics in the early days warned that this would shatter into hostile tribes. It did not, for two reasons. First, the Commonweal schools create a shared pragmatic language: you may pray to different gods, but you have all soldered circuit boards, cleaned a river, and sat on a jury with strangers. Second, because exit is guaranteed, oppression is simply unprofitable. A Circle that drives away its young or burns out its members finds itself abandoned, its stewardship revoked, and its charter house reopened by a more vital group.

This world was not ushered in by revolution. It emerged through municipal experiments—charter neighborhoods, portable benefit pilots, and the slow failure of social media to provide the coherence that humans crave. The Compact of Circles is untidy, colorful, and occasionally loud. It is not a heaven of passive bliss. It is a civilization of chosen belonging, where no one is forced to worship at another’s altar, and no one has to face the void alone.
