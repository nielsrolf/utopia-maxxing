In the year 2047, Elias Chen did not check his bank balance before deciding to eat. He checked the weather. The orbital arrays had beamed down enough energy that morning to synthesize protein for twelve thousand people, and the mycelial aqueducts were full. He walked to the public kitchen, a structure of recycled carbon-fiber and living wood, and took a bowl of steaming kelp-and-lentil stew. No one asked for payment. No one needed to.

This is the world after the Great Augmentation, when asteroid mining, AI-directed manufacturing, and closed-loop bioreactors increased the material basis of civilization by three orders of magnitude in less than two decades. But abundance alone does not create peace. The crucial innovation was the Ledger of Reciprocity, a cryptographic system that tracks contribution rather than accumulation. In the old economy, utility diminished logarithmically; once you possessed enough, each additional dollar meant less. The Ledger made this law visible. You cannot "buy" a mansion, because property beyond use is recognized as a liability—an unused room is simply uncleaned entropy. Instead, you earn *scope*: the right to initiate larger projects, to access the deep research archives, or to sit on the stewardship councils that pace the release of dangerous technologies.

The Stewardship Councils meet in buildings of translucent stone grown from captured atmospheric carbon. Here, biologists hold breakthroughs in escrow—cures for aging, new forms of cognition—until the social infrastructure can absorb them without fracture. This is not secrecy; it is choreography. The councils operate on a calculus of aligned incentives. When resources were scarce, a nation or corporation gained advantage by hoarding knowledge. Now, the mathematics of cooperation dominate. The utility gradient of unilateral advantage has flattened; the risk of conflict—destroying the orbital arrays, poisoning the aquifers—outweighs any marginal gain from defection. Security became a gradient rather than a stockpile. You are safer when your neighbor has no reason to envy you.

The transition was exemplified by the Open Threshold Protocol of 2039. When automated manufacturing made scarcity obsolete, the old extraction corporations were not dismantled by force. They were purchased, over ten years, through a sovereign wealth fund fed by orbital mining revenues. Shareholders received lifetime sufficiency stipends and honors—the Order of the Gradient—converting the status of accumulation into the status of stewardship. Many resisted, at first. But as the first generation grew up without hunger, as the aquifers refilled and the climate stabilized, the logic became self-evident. Why dominate when the marginal utility of hoarding approaches zero, while the utility of trust becomes infinite?

Elias worked his twenty hours at the Synthesis Institute, tending the vats where synthetic neurons were grown for medical repair. This was not slavery; it was the *Tithe of Attention*. Everyone contributes, but the work is calibrated to capacity. The elderly teach, the young maintain the sensor networks, the strong tend the kelp forests. At the Solstice Assembly, Elias stood in a ring of quartz benches beneath a canopy of gene-edited redwoods and accounted for his gifts: forty hours cataloging extinct species genomes, a week spent as a Vigil-keeper for a traveler with fever, a piece of music released to the commons. The assembly placed hands on hearts—a gesture meaning *we are aligned*. There is no applause, only the recognition that his flourishing and theirs are the same function.

He walked home through the greenway, past the Stranger’s Houses—hostels where travelers receive medical care and temporary citizenship without interrogation. He passed the Archive, a building of living mycelium that stores not only books but the epigenetic memories of restored forests. Children played there, learning to read by tracing letters in moss that regenerates overnight. At his door—a simple frame of bamboo and smart-glass—he did not reach for a key. There is nothing inside worth stealing that cannot be replaced, and much that is priceless—the ceramic cup from his grandmother, the song he is composing, the memory of his sister’s Vigil—which cannot be stolen at all.

The sun sets. The glass bells ring, not to signal ownership, but to mark the hour when the community turns to tend the sick, teach the young, and calibrate the machines that keep the abundance flowing. It is not a utopia of perfection. It is a utopia of alignment, where the best move for the individual is, measurably and finally, the best move for all.
