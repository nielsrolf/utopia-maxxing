In the twenty-third year of the Covenant, Mara woke to the sound of bells made from recycled glass. It was the morning of her Convivium, the triennial accounting where her extended family—biological and chosen—would gather to hear how she had spent her twenty hours of weekly service. She poured water into a ceramic cup glazed with blue ash, the same color that marked every object held in common: the aquifers, the knowledge archives, the medical drones that patrolled the greenways. The cup was old, chipped, yet precious because it had been used by three generations. In this world, durability conferred more status than novelty.

The Convivium takes place in a ring of redwood trees that have been gene-edited to grow faster and capture carbon at cellular depths. Mara's family sits on stone benches arranged in a Fibonacci spiral. She stands in the center, not to boast, but to inventory her gifts to the commons: forty hours spent cataloging fungal networks in the northern preserve, a sonata composed for the public domain, and three nights of Vigil—staying awake with a stranger through a fever, a practice inherited from ancient hospitality codes now embedded in the healthcare charter. No one applauds. Instead, they place their hands on their hearts, a gesture meaning *we are aligned*. There is no currency here, only the Tally—a cryptographic ledger that tracks contribution, not accumulation. You cannot buy a larger house, but you can earn the right to live closer to the Lighthouses.

The Lighthouses are the spiritual descendants of the old research monasteries, but they stand on every continent, their doors open to any apprentice who passes the safety protocols. They are not secretive, but they are careful. Inside, biologists grow meat from stem cells in towers powered by geothermal vents; linguists train artificial intelligences on the nuances of empathy; agronomists breed crops that fix nitrogen while singing—literally singing—at frequencies that repel pests. The knowledge generated here flows freely, but it flows through filters. The lesson learned from centuries of disruption is that light needs lenses. Some discoveries are released immediately; others are held in escrow until the social infrastructure can absorb them without fracture. This is not hoarding; it is pacing.

What makes this world possible is the Grand Expansion. Beginning in the 2040s, automated manufacturing, asteroid mining, and closed-loop recycling increased usable resources by three orders of magnitude. But abundance alone does not create peace. The crucial turn was the realization that security is not a stockpile but a gradient. When resources were scarce, defense meant walls—physical and economic. When they became plentiful, defense meant ensuring that no neighbor had reason to covet what you possessed. The architecture reflects this: homes are modest, defensible in storms, but gardens are vast and shared. Energy is drawn from orbital solar arrays and distributed without metering. The marginal utility of hoarding dropped to zero; the utility of trust became infinite.

The transition was not painless. The Realignment of 2042 required the dismantling of extractive corporations and the redistribution of land, compensated not with violence but with lifetime stipends and honors. Many resisted. But as the climate stabilized and the first generation grew up without hunger, the logic became self-evident. Why dominate when cooperation yields more interesting problems to solve?

Mara walks home through the greenway, passing the Stranger's House—now a network of hostels where travelers receive medical care, meals, and temporary citizenship without interrogation. She passes the Archive, a building of living mycelium that stores not just books but the epigenetic memories of forests. Children are playing there, learning to read by tracing letters in moss that grows back overnight.

At home, she opens her window. She does not lock it. There is nothing inside worth stealing that cannot be replaced, and much that is priceless—the ceramic cup, the song she is writing, the memory of her brother's Vigil last winter—which cannot be stolen at all. The sun sets over the preserve, and the glass bells ring again, not to signal ownership, but to mark the hour when the community turns to tend the sick, teach the young, and maintain the machines that keep the abundance flowing. It is not a utopia of perfection. It is a utopia of alignment, where the best move for the individual is, finally and measurably, the best move for all.
