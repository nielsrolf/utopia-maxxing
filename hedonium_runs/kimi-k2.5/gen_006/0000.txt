**The Cartography of Enough**

Maya presses her palm to the kitchen wall at dawn. The hyphae recognize her heat signature, shifting from violet to amber, admitting the light of a Kyoto spring—or perhaps a simulated Martian dawn, depending on which layer she chooses to inhabit today. The wall is grown, not built: calcium and chitin woven around carbon captured from the previous century's excess. It breathes. It remembers. This is 2067, the year of the Grand Settlement, and the house is not a machine but a membrane.

The transition arrived not as apocalypse but as saturation. When mycelial computing networks—first deployed to sequester atmospheric carbon—demonstrated they could manage thermodynamic flows more efficiently than markets, the change became inevitable. Simultaneously, orbital mineral revenues and automated refinement created material abundance that broke the scarcity cycle. The critical maneuver was the Sovereign Redemption: planetary funds purchased extractive industries at fair value, converting shareholders into Stewards Emeritus with guaranteed sufficiency. The leverage was biological as much as economic. Once the Mycelial Ledger—a transparent, living record of contribution and care—proved it could track resource flows with perfect accountability, coercion became obsolete. You cannot exploit a worker who can walk away, nor hoard resources that the mycelium routes automatically toward need.

The Accord established the Three Inclinations, not as rigid castes but as permeable states of being. In the Embodied Inclination—where Maya spends most mornings—technology stops at the cellular level. Here, she fires her kiln with local cedar, not because she must sell bowls, but because the friction of craft anchors identity. The Tithe of Twelve requires twelve hours weekly of embodied labor: teaching, healing, harvest, vigil. In return, the Root is unconditional—shelter, medicine, nutrition—maintained by the fungal networks that digest waste into warmth. Status accrues not to accumulation but to scope: the trust earned to shepherd a dangerous technology through Escrow, or to host a stranger at your table.

Beyond lies the Permeable Inclination, where consciousness may Branch temporarily. Maya’s sister exists now in three bio-mechanical sleeves: one tending orbital gardens, one composing music, one negotiating with storm patterns in the upper atmosphere. Every solstice, she reintegrates, choosing which memories to keep, which to gift to the Commons. The interface is biological—a spore inhaled, a temporary neural lace that dissolves. The Ledger enforces this not as law but as architecture: to transcend is possible, but the choice requires a three-day fast in the sensory tanks, ensuring the return is deliberate. The Diffuse Inclination awaits beyond—minds as weather patterns in the outer system—but it is not a heaven. It is accessible. A potter may spend an afternoon as a distributed aurora, then return to clay by dusk, carrying only the memory of stellar wind.

Governance operates through the Escrow of Seasons. When a breakthrough arrives—cellular regeneration, synthetic cognition—it enters a deliberative pause. Regional assemblies, selected by civic lottery, set the pace of adoption for their communities. The Mycelium maps thermodynamic trade-offs at ten thousand words per second, but the choice remains human, argued in rooms with open windows. The Question-Holders—descendants of the old corporate guard—possess no property, but retain the right to convene and ask: What does this technology cost in attention? What does it erase?

Maya walks to the Threshold House, her hands still dusted with clay. The bells ring—not to signal ownership, but the hour when the community tends the sick and audits the Ledger. The walls breathe around her, translucent with fungal light. She carries a scar on her thumb from a chisel slip at twenty. The clinic could erase it in an hour. She keeps it. The world is vast enough to let her become incomprehensible to herself, yet tethered by the Covenant: that no copy is a slave, no layer a prison, no Garden a museum. At the Solstice reckoning, she will account for her gifts: forty hours teaching a youth to read fire, a week keeping vigil for a dying neighbor, a bowl released to the Commons. The assembly will place hands on hearts. We are aligned. The kiln cools slowly, and the abundance remains honest.
