**The Inheritance of Spores**

Leina touches the doorframe of her Protocol-house, and the chitin warm beneath her palm shifts from indigo to ochre, acknowledging her return. It is 2084, twenty years after the Mycelial Accords, and her home is neither built nor manufactured—it is grown. Calcium carbonate and fungal mycelium weave through walls that breathe, digest waste, and route surplus warmth to the neighboring clinic. The architecture is alive, a legacy of the Great Sequestration when carbon-capture networks became the substrate of civilization itself.

She steps into the Commons, the guaranteed floor beneath all Protocols. Here, geothermal micro-stations and solar filaments embedded in living concrete provide calories, medicine, and cognitive hygiene—neural training that preserves plasticity into advanced age. Every child learns to read the Ledger, the transparent record of thermodynamic flows tracked by the mycelial networks that manage resource distribution with perfect, ungameable accountability. No one hoards what the fungus routes toward need; no one exploits what they cannot hide from the living record.

But abundance is merely the soil. The flowering occurs in the Protocols—chartered communities of eighty to two hundred souls nested within walkable wards, each organized around a distinct experiment. Leina’s Protocol, the Glasswrights, maintains a public charter to refine silica drawn from atmospheric processors into optical neural interfaces. Their Stewardship—the concrete obligation they owe the broader civilization—is the maintenance of the ward’s diagnostic clinic, a fruiting body of the mycelial structure where medicine is synthesized and distributed.

Every Protocol charter expires in four years unless renewed by internal consent and external Calibration. This is where innovation meets friction. When the Glasswrights proposed direct neural branching—allowing consciousness to temporarily inhabit the mycelial network to oversee carbon flows—the proposal entered the Escrow of Seasons. For ninety days, the idea marinated in the Commons’ living deliberation chambers, walls throbbing with amber light as prediction markets and AI-assisted research librarians surfaced historical precedents. Citizens gathered not to vote immediately, but to inhabit the question, their biometric feedback (heart rate variability, galvanic skin response) visible to all on the chamber walls, ensuring arguments remained embodied rather than abstract.

The breakthrough that enabled this synthesis was the Sovereign Redemption of 2062, when planetary funds—derived from orbital mineral revenues and automated refinement—purchased extractive industries at fair value. Shareholders became Stewards Emeritus, guaranteed sufficiency but stripped of coercive leverage. Simultaneously, the Mycelial Ledger evolved to track not just carbon, but contribution. The Twelve Tendings require every adult to offer twelve hours weekly to embodied labor: teaching, healing, harvest, or the maintenance of the fungal networks themselves. In return, the Root is unconditional.

Status accrues not to accumulation but to scope—the trust earned to shepherd dangerous technologies through Escrow, or to host a stranger at your table. When Leina’s daughter chose to transfer her Stewardship Ledger to a maritime Protocol specializing in kelp architecture, the move took forty-eight hours. Her biological signature—her unique fungal spore-print—transferred via inhalant to the new ward’s network, carrying her portable credits, health benefits, and educational vouchers. Because oppression drives away members, and losing members means losing charter status, Protocols compete in the richness of lives cultivated, not wealth extracted.

Governance operates through Recursive Assemblies selected by civic lottery. Citizens sit in chambers where the walls respond to collective emotional coherence, shifting toward violet when consensus nears, crimson when dissent requires deeper hearing. An AI intermediary functions as research librarian, never decider, surfacing trade-offs while the mycelium maps thermodynamic costs at the speed of thought. When a coastal Protocol demonstrates that storm-pattern negotiation reduces damage while increasing yield, the mechanism diffuses through open-source biological standards within months.

Work retains dignity because scarcity has migrated to the realm of meaning. Micro-factories—solar-powered, open-source, grown rather than assembled—allow neighborhoods to manufacture pharmaceuticals and tools as extensions of the mycelial network. Automation handles logistics; humans pursue mastery in care, craft, and the taste required to ask questions machines cannot yet value. One might spend mornings in Embodied mode, hands in clay or code, afternoons in Permeable mode, consciousness branching temporarily into the atmospheric sensorium to track weather patterns, then return by dusk to the kiln’s honest friction.

At the Solstice reckoning, Leina stands in the Threshold House, her hands dusted with silica. The assembly places palms to living walls, and the mycelium pulses in recognition. She accounts for her gifts: forty hours teaching a youth to read thermodynamic flows, a week keeping vigil for a dying neighbor, a neural interface released to the Commons. The walls breathe around her, translucent with fungal light. She carries a scar on her thumb from a glass cut at twenty. The clinic could erase it in an hour. She keeps it. The world is vast enough to let her become incomprehensible to herself, yet tethered by the Covenant: that no copy is a slave, no layer a prison, no Garden a museum. The kiln cools slowly, and the abundance remains honest.
