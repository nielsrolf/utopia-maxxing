# The Repairable World

By 2110, humanity had not become angelic. People still envied, exaggerated, fell in love unwisely, lost elections, wrote bad songs, and argued about children, ancestors, money, and God. What changed was the background condition of life: civilization stopped making ordinary misfortune fatal.

Every person was born with a civic account, not as a bank product but as a claim on the common world. It guaranteed a safe room or apartment, clean water, nutritious food, primary healthcare, education, communication, local transport, emergency refuge, and legal help. The account followed people across cities, countries, and communities. Losing a job no longer meant losing insulin. Leaving a violent marriage no longer meant homelessness. A flood no longer erased identity.

The foundation was paid for by charging rent on things no individual had created: land value, minerals, groundwater, fisheries, radio spectrum, orbital paths, carbon sinks, public datasets, and automated systems trained on humanity’s accumulated knowledge. Enterprise remained lively. People founded laboratories, noodle shops, design studios, farms, theaters, tool companies, games, clinics, and spacecraft firms. Some became wealthy. But wealth could not purchase another person’s desperation, a private monopoly over water, or the right to hide dangerous power from inspection.

Cities changed first because their failures had been most visible. Empty parking lots became courtyard housing, clinics, workshops, orchards, schools, bathhouses, cooling halls, and shaded markets. No new district was approved until its public organs existed: grocery, childcare, elder room, library, transit stop, repair shop, public kitchen, garden, storm shelter, and square. Streets slowed down and came alive. Trams, wheelchairs, children, cargo bikes, vendors, trees, musicians, delivery robots, and emergency vehicles shared routes designed for bodies rather than engines. Freight moved at scheduled hours, by electric rail, or through utility corridors beneath the streets.

Housing was treated like water pipes: essential, maintained, and too important for speculation. Land-value taxes made it expensive to hold useful sites empty. Buildings were designed to change as lives changed. A wall could move; a balcony could become a greenhouse; two small apartments could become one family home; a spare room could serve as a clinic office, student room, or grandmother’s suite. Most people lived within a short walk or roll of daily necessities, beauty, and other human beings.

Rural life revived because distance stopped meaning abandonment. Cheap clean energy, satellite links, telemedicine, drone delivery, regional fabrication, and serious public transport made small towns practical again. Farms used perennial grains, agroforestry, microbial fertilizers, soil sensors, autonomous weeders, pollinator corridors, and careful water budgets. Farmers were paid not only for harvests but for carbon storage, watershed protection, biodiversity, and flood buffering. Most meat and dairy came from fermentation and cultured cells, shaped by cooks into ordinary and ceremonial foods. Former feedlots became prairie, forest, wetland, solar commons, and wildlife passages.

Energy made generosity affordable. Solar glass covered stations, warehouses, schools, and winter gardens. Offshore wind platforms hosted kelp farms and bird sanctuaries. Deep geothermal warmed old cities. Advanced nuclear stabilized hospitals, industry, desalination plants, and cold grids where needed. Continental transmission linked with neighborhood microgrids, so outages became rare and brief. Carbon removal became a utility like sewage treatment: measured, audited, and paid for, not waved away with slogans.

Each person had access to a civic advocate: an AI system legally loyal to the individual. It translated forms, explained laws, compared medical options, found apprenticeships, booked appointments, detected scams, appealed wrongful denials, tutored children, and warned when a landlord, employer, lender, official, or partner was hiding something important. It carried no advertising, sold no attention, and had to admit uncertainty. Public kiosks, clinics, libraries, schools, and handheld devices made it available even to those with no private machine.

Powerful AI itself was useful but not sovereign. Frontier systems required licensing, compute accounting, audit trails, adversarial testing, liability bonds, incident reporting, and shutdown rights. Critical models used in medicine, infrastructure, courts, finance, biology, and defense were inspected by independent institutions. No corporation could hide an artificial governor behind a friendly interface. No state could secretly delegate judgment to a machine. If artificial minds showed evidence of experience, personhood courts examined their capacities and appointed representation.

Medicine moved upstream. Wastewater sensors caught outbreaks before hospitals filled. Regional vaccine foundries updated doses within days. Broad antivirals and protective equipment were stocked like fire extinguishers. Many inherited diseases were corrected early. Most cancers were found before symptoms. Organs could be regrown from a patient’s own cells. Chronic pain, addiction, autoimmune disease, and depression were treated with combinations of medicine, sleep, therapy, neurostimulation, peer support, housing stability, and meaningful daily structure. Crisis response began with calm houses and trained teams, not cages.

Children were treated as citizens becoming themselves, not property. They learned reading, mathematics, ecology, history, repair, statistics, media forensics, music, first aid, negotiation, bodily autonomy, and the known range of human ways to live. By twelve, every child helped maintain something real: a wetland sensor, tool library, meal program, transit map, archive, greenhouse, or water system. By sixteen, each spent time in another community, with private access to counsel and funds to leave unsafe situations. No family, school, church, platform, or party could teach that outsiders were demons, escape was impossible, or abuse was sacred.

Communities were free to differ. There were dense market cities, screenless villages, elder cooperatives, desert laboratories, devotional settlements, language enclaves, craft districts, reef towns, forest monasteries, and orbital habitats. Each published a plain covenant: values, taxes, rules, dispute processes, child protections, technology limits, and exit rights. Difference was protected; captivity was not.

Politics remained noisy, but power became easier to see. Budgets, contracts, environmental readings, court outcomes, police actions, medical performance, and project delays were public in readable form. Laws came with goals, costs, forecasts, review dates, and failure clauses. Citizen assemblies set priorities; elected officials negotiated tradeoffs; auditors and statisticians tested claims; whistleblowers were rewarded. Leaders could still ignore evidence, but they had to do it in public.

Justice became less theatrical and more useful. Victims received immediate care, housing protection, counsel, and restitution support. Most harm was answered with repair, treatment, monitored exclusion from specific places, and obligations to those injured. Secure confinement remained for the persistently dangerous, but it was clean, reviewable, and rare. The measure was fewer victims, not harsher rituals.

The transition was uneven. Heat waves, floods, pandemics, housing revolts, debt crises, and AI near-misses made old evasions expensive. The cities that built homes near transit, taxed land, cleaned energy, and funded care became safer and richer. Climate clubs became trade blocs. Mutual aid became public service. Open audits exposed waste. Apprenticeships beat debt-heavy credentials. Inspection regimes spread because secrecy had become more dangerous than oversight.

The ordinary person woke expecting breakfast, clean air, useful work, beauty within walking distance, honest argument, and someone who would come looking if they vanished. The world was not perfect. It was dependable, plural, and repairable. Its promise was simple: no person disposable, no power unexamined, no system too sacred to mend.
