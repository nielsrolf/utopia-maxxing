# The Long Plenty

By the 2070s, the most important fact about civilization is that basic prosperity stopped being scarce.

It did not happen all at once. There was no single invention that saved the world, no benevolent machine emperor, no final revolution. Instead, humanity spent fifty years getting better at compounding competence.

The first turning point was energy. Solar became so cheap that countries stopped arguing about whether to build it and started arguing about where to put the surplus. Advanced geothermal spread through volcanic regions and old oil fields. Small modular reactors, standardized and factory-built, supplied dense power for cities and industry. High-voltage continental grids moved electricity across time zones; cheap storage smoothed the rest. By mid-century, the average person on Earth had access to more useful energy than a wealthy American had in 2020, and the poorest regions saw the greatest gains.

Cheap energy changed the moral geometry of politics. Desalination plants ringed dry coasts. Vertical farms and precision-fermented proteins made famine rare and animal slaughter increasingly unnecessary. Carbon removal became an industry like road maintenance: not glamorous, but funded, measured, and steadily improved. Most mining moved to automated deep-rock operations with strict environmental accounting, and later to near-Earth asteroids for platinum-group metals and industrial feedstocks. Scarcity did not vanish, but its sharpest teeth were pulled.

The second turning point was institutional. Governments learned, slowly and unevenly, to make more decisions by measured consequence rather than by theater.

Every major democracy now has public forecasting boards attached to its budget office. When a city considers a zoning reform, transit line, vaccine policy, or water project, prediction markets estimate cost overruns, mortality effects, rent changes, migration, corruption risk, and citizen satisfaction. The markets do not decide; elected officials still do. But a minister who ignores the forecasts must explain why, in writing, before the vote. This single norm did more for honesty than a century of speeches about honesty.

Regulation became more experimental. New drugs, building methods, school models, and financial products enter supervised “trial jurisdictions” where outcomes are monitored in real time. If a policy works, other places copy it. If it fails, the failure is contained and publicly documented. Some of the most successful cities began as charter districts on unwanted land: ports, desert edges, depopulated towns. They used land-value taxes instead of punishing work, pre-approved dense housing by default, built utilities in accessible underground corridors, and treated permitting as a service with deadlines rather than as a maze.

The effect was visible in daily life. A nurse in Lagos, a machinist in Ohio, and a teacher in Manila all live in apartments that stay cool during heat waves, draw clean power, and cost less than a fifth of their income. Their children can take free online courses with local mentors, earn credentials by passing practical exams, and move between professions without begging licensing boards for permission. Robots clean sewers, harvest crops, unload ships, inspect bridges, and assist elder care; humans do less dangerous drudgery and more design, repair, teaching, hospitality, science, art, and play.

The third turning point was the peace dividend of abundance.

In the old politics, every negotiation felt like a shrinking pie. In the new politics, leaders began to understand that the pie could grow by orders of magnitude if they avoided catastrophic conflict and kept trade, science, and energy flowing. A country that captured a larger share of a ruined world would be poorer than one that accepted a fair share of a flourishing one. This became not a slogan but a spreadsheet, updated constantly by defense analysts, insurers, markets, and citizens.

The great powers did not become trusting. They became cautiously less stupid. They built verification regimes for synthetic biology, autonomous weapons, cyber infrastructure, and orbital systems. They maintained secure second-strike defenses but separated them from hair-trigger postures. They created international “red button courts” staffed by technical experts from rival blocs, empowered to inspect outbreaks, suspicious labs, satellite incidents, and algorithmic trading crashes within hours. Their authority came less from idealism than from mutual fear of losing the long plenty.

Biotechnology matured under this same discipline. The world did not ban genetic engineering; it made it boringly accountable. Foundries that synthesize DNA must authenticate customers, screen sequences, log anomalies, and carry insurance. Pandemic drills are as normal as fire drills. Wastewater sensors detect novel pathogens early. Vaccine printers in regional hospitals can produce updated doses within days. Malaria is gone. Several inherited diseases are disappearing through embryo screening and safe gene therapies. Aging is no longer accepted as a mysterious curse: cancers are caught early, senescent cells cleared, organs regrown, immune systems renewed. Average healthy lifespan has passed 100 in rich regions and is rising quickly elsewhere.

The culture changed too. Children learn statistics, cognitive bias, negotiation, and moral uncertainty as ordinary subjects. Schools teach “how to change your mind without losing face.” Public arguments still get ugly, but there are now respected rituals for adversarial collaboration: two opponents jointly write the strongest version of each other’s case before debating. Philanthropy became more empirical. A fixed share of sovereign wealth funds and large estates goes into independently audited global benefit funds, allocated by evidence to health, animal welfare, disaster prevention, and scientific public goods.

Space is no longer a fantasy frontier, but neither is it an escape hatch. It is infrastructure. Orbital telescopes watch climate, crops, ships, fires, and asteroids. Lunar industry supplies propellant and shielding. Rotating habitats host researchers, retirees, artists, and children born under carefully chosen gravity. The first million off-world humans still depend on Earth culturally and biologically, but they have already made civilization harder to kill.

This world remains human. People fall in love unwisely, form factions, chase status, mourn their dead, and disagree about the good life. Some communities are religious, others radically experimental; some prize solitude, others festival. The achievement is not uniformity. The achievement is that fewer disagreements become existential.

The long plenty rests on a simple bargain: expand the zone where everyone can win, and govern the remaining conflicts with measurement, transparency, and restraint. Humanity did not become angelic. It became richer, healthier, longer-lived, better coordinated, and more aware of how much there is to lose.

And because the future finally looked large enough for everyone, more people began acting as if everyone belonged in it.
