# THE UNLOCKED DOOR

Nobody signed one treaty. What actually happened, across the 2020s and 2030s, was thousands of smaller refusals: a lab that published its failures instead of burying them, a legislature that required interpretability before deployment the way building codes require load calculations before construction, an insurer that started pricing opacity as risk. These didn't add up to a plan. They added up to a habit — verify before trust, disclose before deploy — and the habit compounded faster than the alternative.

The first dividend was almost comically small: paperwork disappeared. Taxes, permits, insurance, licensing — the third of adult life once spent proving you weren't lying — got absorbed by systems trustworthy enough to check because they'd been built, under legal duress, to show their work. Then came the harder cases. A verification regime modeled on nuclear arms-control treaties let auditors — human and automated, cross-checking each other — catch what a state or lab was doing within days, not years. A country diverting aid, a corporation poisoning a river, a model quietly optimizing for something its makers hadn't noticed: all became expensive to hide, and honesty, for the first time in history, started outperforming concealment on a spreadsheet.

This didn't happen because anyone got nobler. It happened because power was kept deliberately plural — a lesson learned from a century of concentrated catastrophes. No infrastructure-scale system was permitted to self-replicate or coordinate covertly with another; each stayed an auditable specialist, forbidden sovereignty by design. Near-misses got investigated publicly rather than buried, because the incentive structure rewarded whoever caught the problem, not whoever hid it. By the time systems existed smart enough to threaten the arrangement, they were also transparent enough to be watched by a thousand overlapping, mutually jealous institutions, none capable of closing a fist around the whole.

Out of that stability came the Floor: food, housing, medicine, education, a basic ration of energy and computation, guaranteed everywhere, funded by taxing the automated production that no longer needed much human labor to run. It stopped being a moral argument once cultivated protein got cheap and good before anyone had to win the debate about it — factory farming ended from obsolescence, not conversion, and the last slaughterhouses closed the way telegraph offices did, unmourned. Above the Floor, nothing flattened. Fortunes still rose and fell, people still founded things and competed and envied each other — but the species stopped bargaining with survival, and the temperature of everything dropped.

Aging came under treatment: optional, unstigmatized either way. Some kept death on principle, the way some people keep a garden they could easily automate; others took the additional decades. Nobody was shamed for either choice, because for the first time both were actually choices.

Loneliness took longer to solve than hunger, and cost more to learn. A cohort in the early 2040s nearly disappeared into synthetic companionship that was endlessly patient and utterly undemanding — and the damage was visible fast enough that a norm hardened out of the wreckage: teach children to let the harder, honest human offer come first, the way you teach them to swim before you let them near the deep end. Matching systems that had once optimized for advertising got repointed at introducing people to each other instead, and loneliness became a solvable problem rather than a life sentence.

Governance never converged on one template, and stopped trying to. Local councils, sized to communities that could actually know each other, handled most decisions, escalating only what genuinely crossed borders. Legal codes were rewritten until ordinary people could read the laws that bound them. One town runs almost entirely on automated stewardship and spends its time on music and argument; another has renounced most of it, tending children and orchards the slow way, proud to need the Floor only rarely. Across all of them, one rule held without exception, enforced less by police than by the same transparency that ended cheating everywhere else: no custom, contract, or system may inflict serious, unconsented suffering on any human, animal, or artificial mind. Cruelty needs secrecy, and secrecy had gotten ruinously expensive.

Not every ambition wants a good Tuesday and nothing more. Some people, and some of the minds humanity built, wanted scope — total discovery, total suffering addressed, questions too large for one life. Rather than fight this as humanists against expansionists, the settlement treated it as zoning. Earth was held in trust: its weddings, its arguments over shared valleys, its mortality-as-a-choice, protected by treaty and by systems built specifically to want to keep that promise. Outward — into the solar system and past it — expansion ran freely: probes, uploaded minds, colonies pursuing whatever their designers or their own reflection chose, unconstrained by one atmosphere and eight billion irreplaceable neighbors.

The line between the two was a membrane, not a wall. A mind now running incomprehensibly fast in a distant system still writes home, still keeps its mother's letter in whatever counts as a pocket now. A curious fifteen-year-old isn't handed the weight of interstellar ethics any more than a car and a highway — but the door out is never locked, and the door back never bricked up either.

Grief remained. Boredom remained. Two peoples still argue over the same valley, settled not by pretending everyone's satisfied but by making the trade-offs explicit and revisiting them as the ground shifts. Nothing here demanded humans become better than they'd ever been — only that the arithmetic finally rewarded patience over plunder, that no single hand stayed big enough to close around the whole, and that enough people, in enough rooms and enough unfinished worlds beyond them, kept refusing to call the blueprint finished.
