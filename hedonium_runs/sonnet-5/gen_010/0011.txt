# The Common Ground

It started with a boring technical fix. By the mid-2030s, interpretability tools let anyone with standing — a rival state, a journalist, an ordinary auditor — actually read what a powerful model was doing, not just what it claimed. Paired with tamper-evident ledgers that let treaties be checked in real time instead of trusted on faith, the fix made deception expensive in a way it had never been before. Cheating still happened. It just stopped paying. Institutions built for a world where lying was cheap began losing, quarter after quarter, to institutions built for a world where it wasn't. Nobody had a revolution. They had a decade of bad returns on dishonesty, and capital, attention, and talent drifted accordingly.

That drift was spent deliberately on one rule: no actor — state, company, or model — would ever hold a monopoly on force, fabrication, or frontier intelligence. Power was split on purpose, the way constitutions once split it between branches, except now the branches included audited AIs sworn to no single master. Standing red teams were funded to break every safeguard in public, so failures arrived as reports instead of disasters.

**The floor.** Fusion, automated fabrication, and cultured protein made scarcity optional, and a treaty-locked share of that abundance — energy, computation, food, medicine, education — is guaranteed to every person alive, war or no war, recession or none. Chronic pain, most cancers, and the crueler edges of aging are now things a person can decline to have, not things they're spared from deciding about. Poverty is a subject in history class. This floor holds not because anyone became virtuous, but because withholding a solved problem stopped being defensible once everyone could see, audited and public, that it was solved.

**The ceiling.** Above the floor sits almost nothing — a thin body, the Compact, that does exactly three things: forbids monopolies on force or frontier AI, forbids irreversible catastrophe, and guarantees the floor. It has no permanent staff. Its seats rotate through lottery-chosen citizen assemblies, term-limited hard, so no ruling class — human or artificial — has time to calcify. Every Compact body publishes its own failures the way scientists publish failed replications: openly, promptly, without the shame that once let small errors ferment into large ones.

**The branching.** Between floor and ceiling, humanity did not converge. It branched into thousands of provinces, sorted by values rather than birthplace: monastic valleys, cities half-run on shared computational substrate, fishing towns that argue daily with a model trained on their own tide logs, communes that renounced automation and take pride in drawing as little as possible from the common floor. A fixed diversity reserve — a small, non-negotiable slice of global surplus — pays for what markets never would: dying languages, stubborn small towns, archives, ecosystems tended lightly instead of optimized, because a garden perfected stops being a garden.

What keeps a thousand provinces from becoming a thousand small tyrannies is a triple right, hard-won after early communities tried to sever members from the outside world: exit, return, and contact. Guaranteed transport, honest information about alternatives, and a body with real subpoena power make exit more than theoretical, especially for children. But exit alone proved insufficient — people also needed to come back. A man raised in a silent order can leave at twenty for a noisy research guild and return at sixty for his mother's last year, welcomed, not shunned. Leaving toward more abundance or risk is easy; the return path carries friction but never force, enough to keep choices honest instead of desperate.

**What was kept, on purpose.** The first generation to live nearly frictionless lives learned, and taught their children, that engineering away every obstacle engineers away meaning with it. So grief, difficult apprenticeship, real risk, and boredom were preserved deliberately. Earth itself is mostly held as an unmanaged garden — weather uncontrolled, oceans still capable of drowning the careless — while serious expansion moves outward: rotating habitats, self-replicating probes, minds becoming something stranger than human, all beyond a fixed inner ring kept as the old mortal, seasonal world. Every outbound mind carries a channel too slow to be hijacked by a single argument and fast enough to carry a correction — a membrane, not a wall. Something computing incomprehensible thoughts four light-years out still keeps its mother's letters and can, in principle, come home.

Status competition, history's most reliable poison, is defused not by flattening hierarchy but by multiplying it: thousands of provinces mean thousands of games worth playing, so almost no one is stuck losing the single ladder that used to be the only one that counted. Longevity is optional and unstigmatized both ways — some stay thirty for a century, others keep the old rhythm of decades and are considered to have lived complete lives either way. Synthetic companionship is real and permitted, but taught alongside literacy as a bridge, never a first offer, because a culture that once let millions drift into frictionless relationships watched them come out thinner people for it.

The one rule that overrides custom, contract, and code everywhere: no serious, unconsented suffering, inflicted on human, animal, or artificial mind. Which minds count is not settled by decree but funded as live, ongoing inquiry, because for the first time there is slack enough in the system to actually ask.

Nobody believes this is finished. A child questioning her tutor-council about the last uncaught failure gets an honest answer, not a comforting one: the habit of checking together hadn't been built yet. She goes looking for the next argument worth having — which was, all along, the design.
