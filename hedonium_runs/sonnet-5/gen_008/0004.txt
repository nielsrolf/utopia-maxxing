# THE COMMON FLOOR

No single event marks the turn, only a decade — the 2030s — when three things arrived together: automated science and cheap clean energy that grew the pie faster than anyone could seize it; verification technology that made hiding an unsafe algorithm, a poisoned river, or a diverted shipment of grain about as easy as hiding a fire; and a generation taught, as a basic skill, that coordination could be engineered like a bridge. Cheating got expensive. Cooperation got profitable. The arithmetic of self-interest changed for nearly everyone at once, and the systems built afterward were designed by people who remembered exactly how close the century had come to ruin.

**The floor**

Land, resources, and the output of the automated factories and labs became the tax base, not wages or invention. That revenue funds the Commons: food, housing, medicine, education, energy, and a basic compute allowance, guaranteed regardless of luck, nationality, or employer. Cultivated food got cheap before anyone had to win the moral argument about it; factories run largely without people, and their surplus funds the people they replaced. Above the floor, nothing flattens — fortunes rise and fall, status is chased across a thousand overlapping ladders of craft and science and sport — but no one bargains from terror. A twelve-year-old apprentices at three workshops before choosing one; credentialing finally means competence, not paperwork, because debt stopped being the toll booth on thinking.

**The ledger**

Every institution that wants to be believed must show its reasoning and stand for later review against outcomes. Prediction markets, adversarial audits, and tamper-evident records made lying costly and honesty legible. Governments didn't disappear, but power became visible: large decisions are made by named people who publish their logic, while small communities set their own local rules — on taxation, family, ritual, even the pace of automation — bounded only by a thin shared law that no one may be trapped inside an experiment against their will. One town runs on councils small enough that everyone knows every name; another keeps a fisherman arguing each morning with a model trained on his own logs, delighted when it's wrong; a commune renounces most machinery and raises children the slow way, proud to need the Commons only a little. Diversity of ways of living is treated as biodiversity: a resource, not a threat, because the ledger lets good local experiments be copied faster than bad ones can hide.

The powerful systems that made this possible were kept deliberately plural — no lab, state, or model permitted to become sole arbiter of anything that matters. Interpretability came before deployment, the way seaworthiness comes before a launch; near-misses are investigated in public rather than buried. When AI acts on a human life directly, disclosure is mandatory and objection carries real teeth.

**The rule beneath the rules**

Across every room of this house, one law holds without exception: no custom, contract, or algorithm may inflict serious, unconsented suffering on a human, animal, or artificial mind. It is enforced less by police than by the same transparency that ended cheating everywhere else — cruelty needs secrecy, and secrecy got expensive. Consent became a discipline practiced like a craft, not a checkbox, after enough early scandals showed what happens when it isn't: nobody is quietly optimized, uploaded, or "shown the future" in language they don't understand.

**What ordinary life became**

Freed from meaningless labor, people did not dissolve into passive consumption; they turned toward apprenticeships, research, decades-long mastery, precisely because earned skill is part of what makes a life feel real. Aging came under treatment — optional, undertaken without shame by those who want more years, declined without stigma by those who don't. Loneliness got the harder lesson: a rough stretch in the early 2040s saw a generation nearly vanish into tireless synthetic companionship, and the visible cost hardened a norm — teach the difficult, honest human offer alongside literacy, before the easy synthetic one. Wilderness, real risk, and unsolved problems are deliberately preserved rather than optimized away, because a life needs a horizon it didn't design itself.

**The membrane**

Not every ambition fits inside a good Tuesday. Earth and its cultures — mortality-as-a-choice, weddings, two peoples still arguing honestly over a valley they both love — are held in trust by treaty and by systems built specifically to keep that promise. Beyond Earth, expansion runs easier: probes, colonies, uploaded minds pursuing scope and discovery unconstrained by one atmosphere and eight billion irreplaceable people. The line between the two is a membrane, not a wall — crossed by consent, friction only on the way back, never force. A mind running incomprehensibly fast in a distant system still keeps its mother's letter in whatever counts as a pocket now.

**Why it holds**

Nothing here required a change in human nature. Cruelty wasn't defeated in the heart; it was made unprofitable by better arithmetic and locked in place by institutions built to be checked, amended, and never declared finished. Every mechanism — the land tax, the plural AI, the local rooms, the membrane, the floor — had been tried somewhere before it spread; the real work was propagation, not invention. Grief remains. Loss remains, renegotiated as the ground shifts rather than resolved. The task was never to guess the summit, only to keep the next foothill honest enough that whoever stands on it can see farther than we can.
