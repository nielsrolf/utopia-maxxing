# The Widening Path

By the 2040s, the old arithmetic of scarcity had broken. Advanced automation and abundant clean energy meant that the pie could grow faster than anyone could fight over slices of it. This is the hinge on which the new world turned: not moral conversion, but a change in the payoff matrix. When a nation or corporation could see, with reasonable confidence, that cooperation yielded a thousandfold return within a decade while conflict risked losing everything, the rational choice became the generous one. Diplomats and CEOs did not become saints; they became better accountants of their own self-interest.

This did not happen automatically. It required foresight: institutions that modeled these expanding-resource games explicitly, treaties that locked in minimum guarantees so no party feared being left behind, and verification technologies — powered by the same AI systems driving growth — that let rivals confirm each other's restraint without demanding blind trust. The great powers signed onto a framework nicknamed "the widening path": binding commitments that any transformative technological windfall would be shared broadly, with baseline provisions (health, housing, education, computational resources) guaranteed to every human being, while allowing enormous variance above that baseline. Nobody had to accept equality. They only had to accept that their absolute gains would be vast, which turned out to be enough.

## The Shape of Ordinary Life

What did this world feel like to live in, a generation later?

Material want had been solved the way smallpox was solved — not perfectly, but so thoroughly that people had to be reminded it once existed. Automated systems handled agriculture, manufacturing, and logistics with minimal human oversight; the resulting surplus funded a universal basic provision generous enough that no one worked from desperation. But contrary to old fears, people did not sink into passive consumption. Idleness without purpose turns out to have been a symptom of scarcity-era jobs — meaningless labor — not of freedom itself. Once freed from that labor, people turned toward harder, more interesting problems: original research, art, education, exploration, the raising of children with real attention, the building of communities at human scale.

Governance had learned from Fun Theory's central insight: a good life needs obstacles worth overcoming, not their absence. Societies didn't optimize for ease; they optimized for meaningful difficulty. Cities preserved "high-friction zones" — wilderness, competitive sport, craft guilds, difficult apprenticeships — precisely because people wanted to earn their satisfactions. Legal systems became radically simplified and stable, so that ordinary citizens could actually know the rules governing their lives and plan long-term projects with confidence, rather than depending on specialists to navigate opaque bureaucracies. Predictability, not paternalistic optimization, was the gift that power gave to the less powerful.

Artificial intelligence played a curious, deliberately limited role in daily experience. The most capable systems were kept transparently non-sentient by design — extraordinary problem-solvers with no interior experience to protect or placate — precisely so that humans would not be reduced to spectators in their own civilization. These systems handled existential risks (pandemics, asteroid deflection, climate stabilization) and infrastructure, but individual humans retained the "protagonist" role in their own lives: making their own art, raising their own children, solving their own chosen problems, sometimes badly, and learning from the failure.

Communities reorganized around human cognitive limits rather than against them. Instead of nations of hundreds of millions governed by distant capitals, federated networks of smaller communities — scaled to roughly the size groups humans evolved to navigate socially — handled local decisions, federating upward only for matters that genuinely required it. People knew their local representatives personally. Status hierarchies became more numerous and less totalizing: instead of one global ladder of wealth and fame that almost everyone loses, thousands of overlapping ladders — in crafts, sciences, sports, arts, local reputation — meant nearly everyone could be excellent at something that mattered to people who mattered to them.

Security among unequal actors was maintained not by parity but by transparency and defensive dominance: verification technology made surprise aggression nearly impossible to conceal or execute, so vast disparities in wealth or capability stopped translating into existential threats. A billionaire-scale enterprise and a modest cooperative could coexist without the latter fearing conquest, because conquest no longer paid and could not be hidden.

Boredom, that old enemy, was respected rather than merely medicated away. Novelty was cultivated deliberately — new arts, new sciences, new modes of exploration (physical and virtual) — but people were also taught, culturally, to find depth in repetition: a garden tended for forty years, a friendship sustained for a lifetime, a skill still being refined after a thousand hours. Relationships remained gloriously complicated; no one had been offered a shortcut around the entangling difficulty of loving other real, unpredictable people, because that difficulty was understood to be part of the point.

Purpose did not run dry even as suffering diminished. With aging slowed, poverty abolished, and existential risks radically reduced, the "emergency work" of civilization shrank — but people did not run out of things to care about. They turned toward frontier problems: understanding the universe, reaching other worlds, building art and institutions worthy of the abundance they'd inherited, and tending the remaining hard edges of life — grief, growth, the raising of new generations — that no amount of abundance erases.

## Why This Is Reachable

None of this required utopian humans. It required, first, technology capable of radically expanding the total resource pie; second, enough foresight among competing powers to recognize that cooperation dominated conflict once gains became large enough; and third, a deliberate, unglamorous effort to design institutions — legal, psychological, technological — around what actually makes a human life worth living, rather than around what sounds impressive in a press release. The scarcity mindset that drove millennia of conflict didn't need to be defeated in the human heart. It needed to be made, quite simply, obsolete.
