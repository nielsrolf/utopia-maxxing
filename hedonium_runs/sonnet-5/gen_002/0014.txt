# The Widening Garden

Imagine standing on Earth in 2150. Factory farming ended decades ago, replaced by cultured food indistinguishable from the original at a fraction of the cost. Poverty is a historical curiosity, taught in schools alongside smallpox. People still go to work, fall in love, raise children, and argue about politics — but the work is chosen, the arguments are conducted through institutions that actually resolve disagreements, and no one dies of a disease we knew how to cure. This is not the endpoint. It's the foundation.

The path here was unglamorous: better forecasting and coordination tools reduced the chance of catastrophic war; a costly but survived series of near-misses with pandemics and AI systems taught the world to take safety seriously before it was too late; global institutions slowly learned to price in the interests of animals, the poor, and future generations, not out of sudden moral awakening but because the tools to do so — verification, transparency, abundant compute — became cheap enough that decency stopped requiring sacrifice.

From this foundation, humanity did something unusual: it stopped assuming that one vision of the good life had to win. Instead, it built a civilization shaped like a garden radiating outward from Earth, with different beds tended for different things — and, crucially, paths between them that anyone could walk.

**The near garden** stays recognizably human. Earth, the solar system, and eventually the nearest stars remain home to biological and post-biological people living at human scale: friendships, competition, art, risk, mortality for those who want it, radical life extension for those who don't. Bodies are customizable — some people choose enhanced biology, others choose mechanical or hybrid substrates — but the texture of life, its dramas and stakes, is preserved by choice, not by ignorance. No one is trapped here; anyone may leave.

**The middle garden**, spreading through the galaxy over the following millennia, is where humanity's more expansive projects unfold: civilizations of trillions of augmented and artificial minds pursuing science, art, and forms of flourishing we don't yet have words for. This is where "more" starts to matter — more minds, more knowledge, more kinds of joy — because the people here have, through free and informed reflection, come to genuinely want more, rather than having "more" imposed on them by a value system they never chose.

**The far garden** is where the civilization's longest-term, most scope-sensitive values are given room to run. Self-replicating probes, built only after a century of rigorous, adversarially-tested research into the neuroscience and computational signatures of sentience, spread outward at a fraction of lightspeed. They construct Dyson swarms and computational substrates whose purpose is to instantiate vast amounts of flourishing — not naively "hedonium" in the crude sense of undifferentiated pleasure-stuff, but whatever the best available science and the most carefully idealized values determine is actually good: rich, structured, valuable experience, produced at civilizational scale, for beings whose existence is itself considered and consented to by proxy through the deliberative process that built them.

This is not decided once and locked in blindly. Every probe carries an update channel: a slow, deliberately-throttled way for improved ethical and empirical understanding to propagate outward, so that a mistake made in year 200 doesn't get frozen into year 200,000. The throttling is intentional — fast enough to correct errors, slow enough that no distant power can hijack the whole project through a single clever argument or attack. Call it a firewall with a mail slot.

Crucially, the boundaries between gardens are permeable by choice, not fixed by decree. A person living an ordinary human life on Earth can, upon death or by request, be uploaded and migrate outward into the middle or far garden if they'd endorse that upon reflection; someone who wants an ending, wants one. Someone in the far reaches who becomes lonely for dirt and weather and a human-scale life can, in principle, come home — the probes reserve a fraction of captured resources for return trips, communication, and rescue, so expansion is never entirely one-way.

Why does this arrangement hold together instead of collapsing into one value eating the others? Partly because the economics favor it: the near garden's inhabitants don't want the same resources the far garden's projects want — no one is fighting over the same galaxy for opposite purposes, so there's little to fight over. Partly because the process that builds the probes is itself pluralistic: it's overseen by institutions descended from today's — improved, but recognizably accountable — rather than by a single actor's private vision of the good, which makes both capture and catastrophic error less likely. And partly because the whole system was built incrementally, each stage tested and only cautiously scaled: first curing diseases, then verifying AI alignment on small stakes before large ones, then testing sentience-detection science for centuries in low-stakes settings before entrusting it with galaxies.

The result is not a utopia that demands anyone abandon what they love in exchange for astronomical abstractions, nor one that sacrifices the scale of what's cosmically possible for the sake of comfort. It is a civilization that took seriously both the parent who wants an ordinary evening with their kids and the researcher who wants to know how much good the universe can hold — and built room for both, connected by open doors, correctable by design, and expanding outward not as a conquest but as a garden tended by people who remembered, at every stage, that the point was never the resources. It was always the flourishing.
