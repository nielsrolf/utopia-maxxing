The Porchlight Covenant

You know it when you see it: a thumb-sized lamp etched on tile, a warm cone of light spilling onto a threshold. It hangs by side doors of libraries, on the wall of a bus depot, at the edge of a market. Porchlight means the same thing everywhere: if you’re in trouble, come in.

Inside, the air smells like soap and tea. There’s a washer churning, towels that actually dry, a shower that steams, a drawer with real clothes in common sizes, and two small rooms with locks on the inside. A short counter hums with chargers that fit whatever you brought. A person looks up—blue vest, name pinned to a felt strip—and asks the questions that have saved whole weeks: What would make the next hour easier? Who should know you’re safe?

This is a Porchlight Room. It is not charity. It is the visible edge of a promise we made to each other and wired into law, software, and buildings: no single bad week ruins a life; any force that would move many must announce itself, leave a trail, and wait a decent pause. That promise is the Porchlight Covenant. It’s short enough to memorize and stout enough to hold up cranes.

Harbor first, then horizon

A Porchlight visit comes with a small bundle that works in any city that signed the Covenant. You don’t apply; your civic key unlocks it.

- A bed with a door that locks for a month, relocatable with you if you must move; movers and deposits are budgeted already.

- A pause on bills that would avalanche while you cross your private river: rent, utilities, minimum payments.

- Staple groceries you can serve with pride and water that never tastes like an anecdote.

- A transit pass and a compute credit you can spend on study, translation, automation of care, or just a chess engine that doesn’t cheat.

- A clinic appointment that starts with sleep, pain, and safety before forms.

- Three names: a person who will answer at 2 a.m., a place you can work tomorrow if you need cash without a saga, and an Advocate—a digital agent with a duty of loyalty to you, not to whoever bought the ad slot.

The Advocate lives on a device in the room and moves with your key. By law it is a fiduciary. If it sells your attention or steers you for its own gain, a judge treats it the way judges treat embezzlement. Alongside your Advocate sit three old-new professions that practice together:

- The Clinician who deals with bodies and minds with the quiet competence that calms a room.

- The Longview, who holds what future-you wants while present-you is on fire.

- The Quartermaster, who turns a tangle of errands into a map you can carry and checks the small things that trip us—“if you asked for potatoes, do you have a pot?”

They lift, then vanish until you call. Sixteen-year-olds can come through a side door with two neutral adults present, no permission slip required, because exits that only start at eighteen leave too much harm inside.

The floor and how it’s paid

Below the Porchlight sits a floor we underwrote together. It isn’t deluxe. It is dependable.

- A small, decent apartment that locks. If floods or family pull you across town, the lease moves and so does the help.

- Power to light rooms, keep food cold, and hold temperatures inside “living” without checking the meter twice a day.

- Transit on headways you can plan a life around.

- Primary care and mental health coaching that come to your door when that’s smarter.

- Secure identity and messaging that don’t peel and sell you.

- Hours each month in public workshops and studios, with instruction if you want it.

- Compute alongside electrons—so the tools of the day aren’t a luxury.

The money comes from boring places it should have come from a century ago: the value of ground we stand on, the airwaves and rights-of-way we all cross, leases on orbits and mineral seams, a small fee on very large model runs, and a levy on heavy automation that hums while cities sleep. In practice:

- A commons dividend rides in on your utility bill. It scales up automatically when your income dips without a second humiliating form.

- Every budget carries an option to compete. When a market behaves like a club, the city spins up a co-op or public competitor that sells at cost until the kink relaxes, then winds down with a note on the door.

- Each adult allocates a handful of Long Bet credits each year. These feed patient work: reef stitching, institute-grade tool safety, choirs that teach breath, math monasteries. Money follows sustained desire, not just whoever shouts today.

- When automation eats a trade, displaced people receive equity in the gains and training budgets rise like an airbag. That’s statute, not a memo.

The promises in plain speech

Covenants matter when they’re shorter than a sermon and longer than a slogan. Ours reads as ten vows, printed on ceramic in Neighbor Houses and on the welcome page of your civic key.

- Exits are real. You can leave a home, job, school, group, or district without losing shelter, identity, or medicine. You land on your feet.

- Reasons before power. If you wield public authority or sell necessities, you post Policy Receipts: the guess, the rehearsal, the contingencies, the appeal. In emergencies you can move first; the math and the pause arrive tomorrow.

- Consent breathes. Agreements that touch your body, attention, memory, or data expire on visible clocks, renew in plain language, and include undo where physics allows and insurance where it doesn’t. Significant shifts climb a Consent Stair: disclosure, cooling-off, a second key held by someone loyal to you, practice rooms that preview ordinary mornings, and ceremony when a choice is for keeps.

- Quiet is a right. A physical Quiet Switch on your wall makes devices sleep. Uninvited pings pay a tiny toll to your block unless you whitelisted the sender. Strong persuasion sits behind gates that ask you-today, you-last-week, and you-tomorrow-morning.

- Safety is legible. Systems that can maim or widely steer—bridges, vats, models, replicators—carry safety cases an aunt with a curious mind can follow. Sharp tools wear visible belts. Kids test the “Off” in fifth grade.

- Borders are agreements. Neighborhoods post norms in copper and trees as well as online: dark at ten, loud till two. Your devices learn defaults at seams and ask. A standing Constraint Corps interrupts domination with shipments before injunctions and, if necessary, with reversible force that ruins a bulldozer’s day without ending a life.

- Truth is a utility. Forecast benches sit by budgets. Proof Seasons require teams to persuade and build—demo loops you can ride, rituals people keep, budgets that close. Red Seasons pay citizens to break critical systems safely twice a year and then fund the fix.

- No masquerade. Everyday assistants schedule, translate, and draft. They do not pretend to be your lover, your therapist, or your dead. Minds that can suffer are treated as such or prohibited.

- Reversible-first innovation. Proven patterns for homes, heat pumps, microgrids, ventilation, bridges get fast permits and sixty-day inspections. Deviate inside sandboxes where failure cannot cascade. Compute that can persuade or plan harm at scale runs with Run Passports: who asked, what for, wattage paid, where outputs may travel.

- Markets, with guardrails. Wealth buys time, tools, and choice. It doesn’t buy vetoes, legal immunity, or other people’s attention at breakfast.

Seams you can feel

We stopped insisting on one texture of life. Some want windows open and dinner at one long table. Others love a boulevard that glows till dawn. We made difference legible and safer with curbs you can feel.

- Hearth Wards scale to bodies and nerves. Feeds end. Devices are guests. A night switch kills radios unless someone you’ve named says your name.

- Forge Belts hum with heavy craft. Wind sleeps on migratory nights. Small fission wears neat jackets under crews who can chant their safety briefs half-asleep. Declared nonhuman minds with unions and contracts work here if invited. If you want tools that didn’t begin in breath, you sign in and sign what that means.

- Dream Gardens are shared, lucid worlds you sit into on studio chairs. Rules float in the corner of your eye: what senses are borrowed, whether memories write back, where the exit is. Ads appear only if you ask to study them. Jump-scares require consents that expire.

- Far Works hold power we cannot use without breaking nearer promises: orbits that tempt monopoly, ice that tempts extraction, models that tempt extortion. Their charters bind into treaties that force the world to notice in daylight.

Edge Houses sit on seams: maps, shop counters, a place to ask “what does that light mean?” Edge Meters count the few flows we agreed matter across lines: decibels after quiet hours, particulates downwind, floodwater pulses, nitrates at dawn, spectrum clutter. Where money tells truth, it flows either way. Where price would lie—neonatal ward air, species loss, sacred quiet—we set hard limits.

Belonging as infrastructure

Loneliness is not a personal failing. It is a design flaw. Every district keeps a Neighbor House that feels like a library with a kitchen, or a workshop with a garden gate. A professional Binder—trained like a teacher, paid like an engineer—keeps the place warm. They run practical crews that replace handrails and true wheels and choirs that keep an open chair. With consent, they keep a gentle connection ledger that watches isolation without monetizing it. When the graph bends the wrong way—after a plant closes, a flood, a wave of divorces—a budget unlocks automatically. Invitations sound like friends, not funnels: “You came late to bread night and laughed at a mushroom joke. Friday we’re walking the ravine. Omid and Raven are going. Want a thermos?”

Kin Circles form around households that ask: twenty to thirty neighbors who pledge to be accountable kin for a year. A code word on a chat means a hot pot appears on your stoop. If you miss open-table supper three weeks in a row, someone knocks with a loaf and no lesson.

Work, time, and pride

With a floor underfoot, work regained taste. We talk about missions: things you can point at and say, “I’m glad I did that,” and others nod. A Pride Ledger travels with you. It holds hinges copied ten thousand times, a winter of bedtime you ran for single parents, a drain that hummed through a storm, a barley cultivar that smiles at salt.

Guilds thrive. Apprenticeships replaced most paper licenses: one thousand supervised hours where mistakes waste material; ten thousand where a mistake can suffer. If your chair design makes kitchens smile in five cities, a trickle of credits follows it through an open registry anyone can audit. Studios fail softly: tools slip into lending libraries, debts shrink to sizes you can sleep with, and your reputation file carries a note: tried; told the truth.

Attention and agency

We treat attention like water. Devices ship with a Quiet Switch on the wall. Flip it, and everything sleeps except a fountain-pen list of names you preapproved. News and scheduling agents owe a duty to you alone. If they steal your agency, courts treat it like theft. Identity and broadcast divorced for good: speaking is free, flooding others at scale is not. To spray a message across breakfast tables you spend reach stamps tied to your past good acts or burn a small monthly allotment. Bot swarms don’t vanish; they simply cannot vote where flesh is required.

Belts on sharp things

Very large model runs file a flight log before they begin: who is running what, trained on which data, for which declared purpose, with which auditor. We watch with three instruments: software attestation, substation meters tied to racks, and a heat witness that samples waste heat. Hiding a run above threshold is treated like flying without a transponder: an expensive mistake with consequences that arrive without shouting. A Civic Brake can slow a risky deployment for days. It must publish its math: scenarios, disagreements, and how it resolved them. It has used the brake sparingly; even the pauses taught.

DNA printers above a class report by default. Labs inventory feedstocks like jewelers track stones. Replicators ship with visible lineage tags and dead-man cords you can pull with a wrench. Making pathogen parts without a license is first unprofitable, then prosecutable.

We keep bright lines with long clocks to change them: no scaled nonconsensual harm; no targeted persuasion at children; no boxed minds that can suffer; no self-replication in the wild. Changing a line requires years and councils weighted toward citizens, not specialists alone.

Clean air, power, water, shelter, food, networks

We stopped making clean power hard on purpose. Neighborhood geothermal and small walk-away-safe reactors got certified like airplanes. Rooftop and desert solar clicked like floorboards. Wind tuned for birds and bats. High-voltage DC lines followed old corridors; compensation used formulas posted in bus shelters. Once a quarter, neighborhoods practiced crescent mode: streetlights glowed softer, fridges hummed, oxygen machines ran, stoves cooked. Blackstarts became festivals—stew and brass and sweaters.

Air got boring-good. Filtration that would embarrass a 2020 hospital became code. Far-UVC on buses and in classrooms humbled bioaerosols at safe doses. CO2 dots and mold counts sat next to the date. Wastewater labs read viruses before hospitals had to.

Permitting stopped being a riddle. Codes flipped to performance: hit targets for light, sound, energy, safety; the shape is yours. Public builders stamped warm, handsome modules that assembled like grand furniture near trains. Pattern books were open, witty, local. Land value charges debited from parcel registries; hoarding empties became a costly hobby. Every city kept a yard of finished units by the tracks. When a camp formed, the answer was keys, not cops.

Food stopped hurting. Fermentation hubs in remade warehouses made proteins as cheaply as beans and as richly as feast. Cultivated meats carried recipes forward. Farmers still farmed: mixed perennials, shade-tithing agroforestry, grains that loved drought. Schools, hospitals, airlines, and militaries signed procurement that made slaughterhouses into museums and then studios. An Ocean Trust paid coastal communities to restitch reefs and anchor kelp.

Governance you can read

With your civic key, you may be called to serve on a council drawn by lot. The work isn’t theater. Big calls—bus lanes, marsh-and-wall, storage that hums—go to Civic Trials. Teams bring five roles: a narrator who explains like a beloved aunt; a modeler with code you can poke; a builder who makes a small version work; a reconciler sworn to carry the best objections forward; a risk captain who names what can go wrong and how to tear small. Policy Receipts post in the same font whether they announce an experiment or an apology. Proposers post reciprocity bonds that pay those affected if they miss their claims. Agencies gain safe harbor if they try what forecast benches say is likely to work. When they overrule and miss, their discretionary pots take the haircut, not the parks.

Institutions recharter on surprise clocks within five-year windows. They must win a slim vote on a plain mission and show receipts. Every capital project carries a visible line for human care—training, spares, time to sit with those displaced. Cut that line and cause more trouble than you saved, and the name over your office carries a mark.

Justice that mends

We unbundled force. Those who detain don’t interrogate and don’t prosecute. Most wrongs end in restitution and repair: lies become public notes and a season of work; broken windows turn into weekends mending houses. Serious violence still sometimes means walls; those places are small, inspected, and designed for return. Predictive tools point care, not punishment. A time-bound, appealable registry of banned roles replaces whisper networks. On the wall of a Repair Studio hangs a time-lapse of a mistake that was fixed, and next to it a Policy Receipt from the day an agency apologized.

Nature as co-sovereign

Rivers, reefs, savannas, and old-growth hold budgets and guardians with teeth. A wetland that holds a storm and throws birdsong counts as a ledger victory, not romance. Wind farms sleep on migratory nights. Dirty work moves away from bird paths and seagrass. Wilderness the size of countries is left alone on purpose and wins applause. A Primelands Trust sets a finite number of resident-years, auctioned for money and care—hours of tending, ecological skill, art that repairs. Some time is pilgrimage: come, tend, learn, be still.

Security without empire

Mostly we enforce promises with physics and procurement, not sticks. Risky streams pass narrow waists: firmware for machines that can bite; orders for odd chemicals; replication outside bounded zones. Keys to those waists sit with courts and communities; the enforcement lives in copper and light. On hard days, the Constraint Corps reads out an injunction and the terms investors signed into mud that turns to pudding until tracks fill and contracts die. Lives don’t.

A day here

Morning in Lagos. A Porchlight Room smells like thyme. A teenager comes through the side door with a friend. She wants to leave an uncle’s house without burning the city down. The Advocate prints three addresses. A Clinician treats a bruise, a Longview listens without sifting for charges, a Quartermaster checks that her suitcase’s wheels turn. By afternoon she has a key that turns a lock, a stipend that works at the market she knows, and two adults on call who owe loyalty only to her.

Midday in Kraków. The bridge bends wrong. Sensors that learned to sound worried sing. The span bows and closes with a countdown anyone can read. A Red Season team at the university tries to make the closure system fail and earns a small prize when their attack finds a bug. Commuters grumble, ride the detour, and read the Policy Receipt by the bus stop. A muralist paints trout on the plywood and nobody loses a paycheck.

Afternoon in Recife. The wind is loud and so are the turbines, but migratory nights have begun, so half sleep. A child points at a tower that spins lazily and says, “it’s letting the birds go.” In the permit office something odd happens: a workshop tower’s plan clears in twenty-three days under a performance code. The builder used a pattern book for ventilation. You can smell the ocean through the louvres.

Evening in Nagpur. A Neighbor House hums. The Binder’s loneliness dashboard ticked up when a plastics plant closed. Their budget unlocked: “Try a midweek supper with childcare and an early-shift table?” Three months later the line bent down. The Binder’s bonus felt like civic applause. Across the street a Dream Garden studio lists tonight’s rules in the corner of your eye: no ads unless you ask; memories write back only to a sealed notebook; one exit in view at all times.

Night on a retreating shore. A family meets a migration ombud in a room that glows. Before the seawall fails, they have keys two hundred kilometers inland, school seats, co-op shifts, and a person to meet them at the station. They cry, then write lists. Transit follows people, not the other way around.

High above, on a basalt ledge turned factory, a refinery lists its grandparents to an auditor: two licensed lines from three licensed lines from a pilot whose key expired last year. A violation pings. A subcontractor tried to flash firmware without a key. The line pauses. The subcontractor goes dark on the network until a human face appears with an apology. The Commons Court will fine them a week’s profit. Work resumes. No one writes a florid essay about freedom.

How we got here

From far away, it looks like a leap. Up close, it looks like clocks, receipts, and shameless copying.

In two to three years
- Cities kept heat shelters open as Porchlight Rooms year-round. Insurers learned that dignity is cheaper than triage and began to pay for Advocates and simple Steward boxes with real off switches.
- Legislatures wrote duty-of-loyalty into software that mediates choices and banned dark patterns. Judges learned to say “you stole their agency” the way they had always said “you stole their cash.”
- Filters, CO2 monitors, and upper-room UVC left pilot status and entered code next to fire stairs. Wastewater warnings went routine. Blackstart drills turned into stew-and-sweater festivals.
- Permit portals grew APIs and clocks that fine agencies for stalling. Performance-based codes cut approvals from years to weeks. Pattern books for warm, modular homes spread like songbooks.
- Three mid-sized cities shifted taxes toward land value, cut parking minimums, and ran buses every eight minutes on seven routes. When rents calmed and small buildings rose, five more cities copied them.

In five to seven years
- Lightweight compute registries tied to meters became normal. Watt tariffs from big training runs flowed into floors and safety. Vendors refused to ship sharp tools without child-pausable “Off” and Proving Grounds quarters; vows became regulations.
- Libraries took on tool clinics. Apprenticeships replaced swathes of licensing. Children learned forecasting and fallacies in games; adults learned to argue in Proof Seasons where building outranked vibe.
- A small commons dividend tied to land, spectrum, orbits, and model licenses launched across patchwork borders. People stopped taking soul-nibbling jobs; small enterprises bloomed.
- Food procurement did what sermons hadn’t. Schools, hospitals, and airlines bought fermentation proteins and cultivated cuts until slaughterhouses dwindled into museums and then studios.
- Rights-of-way deals for HVDC and neighborhood geothermal used compensation formulas that felt fair, not fast-talk. “Dark week” drills got boring.

In eight to twelve years
- Sortition councils and Civic Trials replaced committees that never decided anything. A transit chair thanked a high school whose model beat her hunch and changed course on camera. Culture shifted.
- DNA printers above threshold and replicators shipped with lineage and dead-man cords by default. Making pathogen parts without a license became a way to go broke and then to court.
- Nature guardians won budgets that bit. Rivers sued and got standing times money. Builders learned to schedule around birds and moths.
- Weaving Ports—Porchlight Rooms scaled for receiving—became default for climate and care migration. Hostlines answered in your language. The phrase “managed retreat” picked up the word “welcome.”

In fifteen to twenty years
- Modes and seams signed into treaties. Quiet towns asserted their right to tame machines at the edge. Hot frontiers ran on consent with triple-key belts. Story studios watermark rules in the corner of your eye.
- The right to rejoin after a fork grew beyond families into law. Undo insurance turned from a boutique rider into a floor.
- The Proof Guild—the dull, beloved guild of auditors and field measurers—became a first-choice career for stubborn, curious kids who like being right slowly.

Why this is good, and why to believe it

It is good because the first question on hard days is human and close. Because breath is not a gamble, attention is not a strip mine, and leaving a life that hurts does not mean hitting a floor that breaks. Because power prints its reasons before it acts, not after it fails. Because when a machine grows teeth, there is a switch on the wall a child can throw and an adult must respect. Because rivers have budgets, elk have corridors, oysters have guardians, and machines respect the edges we draw.

It is specific. You can hold the wrench that pulls a dead-man cord. You can see the watt line item that funded the studio where your neighbor learns to weld. You can flip a switch that makes a phone truly sleep. You can read the line in a budget that leased an airwave and paid for vines over a bench. You can follow the model—posted with code—that justified painting a bus lane that saved a thousand mornings. You can search a ledger of lessons to see what failed and what we learned.

It is plausible because nearly every piece exists in miniature. Performance codes have cut approvals from years to weeks. Land value charges have calmed rents where tried. Insurers pay for prevention when arithmetic is plain. Wastewater labs catch outbreaks. HVDC hums across continents. Microreactors are certified. Whistleblower bounties work. Courts have run parallel opinions citizens chose between. Proof-of-personhood is mature. Child-usable off tests can be written tomorrow. A physical night switch is a hinge and a promise.

The improvement was not to paste kindness atop systems that weren’t built for it. We turned welcome into security: receiving people in motion with rooms and kin lowered street harm more than any curfew. We wrapped sharpness in ceremony and code: a kettle is not metaphor, a brake with three keys is not theater, a firmware gate that slows floods to streams is not a gimmick. We wrote neurodiversity into architecture: sensation maps at doors, a quiet nook never more than a minute’s walk away, cafés that whisper at certain hours, trains that dim deal-lights on winter afternoons. We funded undo and taught grief. We measured friendship half-life and repair lag because a place you cannot mend or love will break you in ways air tests don’t catch.

A small promise

At civic beginnings—a Binder taking a Neighbor House, a teenager receiving a key, a family widening to add one more—we warm a kettle and say a promise small enough to carry:

- I will ask before I reach.
- I will leave a path back.
- I will show my reasons when they touch others.
- I will answer fair requests and decline without guilt.
- I will make one thing a stranger will quietly thank me for.
- I will remember that neat is not the same as true.

On a street at dawn, a baker lifts her gate. Ovens pull power that hums like a river you can trust. Across town, a Customs Ring slows a torrent to a stream neighbors may choose to drink. In a clinic, a nurse pours tea and names three paths without hurry. On a ridge, a child ties a knot and laughs. A porchlight glows above frosted glass. The chime trembles, soft soles whisk, and a voice kind to nerves asks the two questions that change a day.

The edges are bright. The doors are open. You can go far. You can come home. That is what the Porchlight is for.
