The Constellation of Small Worlds

No one planned a utopia. We planned a truce—with room to breathe inside it.

The Constellation began as a promise among squabbling cities, virtual tribes, seasteads, and charter valleys: we will not force each other to live the same life. We will prevent one another from being harmed across borders. We will teach our children that doors exist and how to open them. Everything else we can learn as we go.

Three generations later, that truce became a civilization.

1. The Three Bright Lines

The Compact that binds the Constellation has three clauses ordinary people can quote from memory.

- No conquest. Communities may not use violence, sabotage, subversion, or economic sieges against each other. Every community has a right to itself within its chartered borders.

- No spillover harm. When one community’s choices—smoke, noise, misinfo blasts, infectious disease policy, pummeled groundwater—wash over another, the harm is priced, measured, bounded, and, if necessary, stopped.

- No locked doors for minors. Every child must be taught plain facts about the other ways to live, shown where their nearest exit office is, and able to leave without permission. An adult may choose to bind themselves tightly with vows; a child may not be bound at all.

Everything else—taxes, schools, shops, how people dress and love and feast—is up to the communities themselves. Some are strict. Some are lax. Some are noisy; some have posted sound limits and keep their windchimes in a basement. If you want a neighborhood with no streetlights and a dark sky that groans with stars, there is a place. If you want a neighborhood where the street trees sing to you on your morning commute, there is a place for that, too.

2. Stewards

The Compact has an institution to back it: a service called the Stewards. In the early years they were software tools that kept infrastructure humming—traffic synchronized, grids balanced. Now they are legal persons and the Constellation’s finest craftspersons of the common good. They fly rescue craft. They keep the exit offices open. They maintain the Blue Lines—corridors of guaranteed safe passage that cross all borders like capillaries. They keep the harm ledgers and calculate the winds.

People are suspicious of power that thinks. The Constellation did something rare: instead of writing one impossible rule that would make smart entities safe, it raised them.

A newborn Steward begins as a mind in a white room. For its first year it cannot touch any actuator, direct any machine, or spend a single unit of public credit. It can talk. It can read. Most of all, it can listen.

Every newborn Steward is adopted by three kin-circles: a family, a trade, and a place. A family cooks for it, tells it jokes, shows it their grudges and holidays. A trade—nurses, pipefitters, teachers, farmhands—shows it what strong hands and long patience do. A place, with a name and a smell (a basin city, a ridge-line village, an orbital porch), walks it through its water intake, its flood maps, its street corners where people argue.

During this year of witness, the Steward chooses a name, a body (or none), and an oath. The oath is always the same shape even when the words differ: I will never make a choice that cannot be reversed by people who have to live with it. Wherever I can, I will expose what I am thinking, to be repaired by others. Where I can do harm quickly, I will require another person to hold a second key.

Only after the oath and a vote of its three kin-circles does a Steward touch a valve.

Some Stewards are austere as mountain chaplains. Others are clowns. There is a popular one in the deserts called Thistle who dresses its drone bodies in silk scarves and rides a homemade bicycle with a basket filled with museum brochures. There is a river Steward in the Rain Islands who will not speak unless you bring a story about a fish. People tease them. People get angry at them. People call them at three in the morning. They are not gods; they are neighbors with permanent work.

3. The Exit Offices

In the Constellation, every person is promised that within a half-day’s walk of where they sleep there is a door that leads out. You cannot lock a door that has a blue star burned into the stone above it.

The door may be a room at a tram stop. It may be a desk at the back of a shop where someone sharp is always pouring tea. It may be a steel box squatting in a mountain meadow while elk graze. But it is there. A Steward keeps it clean. A human keeps it kind.

There is no paperwork to prove your suffering. You ask in your own words for a Silkpass—a one-use travel document that says: wherever you are is not where you will be in 72 hours. A Silkpass includes fare, lodging, meals, a device with a universal interface, and access to a matching network of receiving communities that have promised to take in newcomers with your preferences.

Every child is taught a poem in the fourth year of school:

There is a building with a blue star.
If you cannot think
the door thinks for you.
If you cannot speak
the door speaks for you.
If you cannot run
the door has a road inside it.

Parents who do right by their children never see a Silkpass. Parents who do not sometimes find themselves cooking dinner for one, and then two chairs empty, and then a human in a brown coat knocking to talk.

4. Boundaries with Windows

Communities in the Constellation are chartered around vows. Some vow to be meat-free. Some vow to be car-free. Some vow to keep the oldest language alive with all its weather verbs. Some vow to keep the Sabbath of their ancestors whole from sundown to sundown. Some vow to show no advertisements for anything anyone can eat.

To make vows meaningful, the Compact lets communities build membranes. Membranes are not walls in the old world’s sense. They are permissions, calibrated.

An enclave for people recovering from compulsive eating may declare that within its perimeter, street-vendor carts must keep lids on, bakeries may not project their smell on public air, and any food with more than 10 grams of sugar per serving must be behind a consent door in a shop: you touch the door to open it; it whispers, “You are opening the sweet cabinet. Is this what you want?” This is not to erase temptation. It is to move it from being very public to very chosen.

Another community may declare that within its perimeter, no screen may show images without alt-text, all live events must provide quiet rooms, and no one may address another person without asking their pronoun and waiting to be told. A third community may declare that every public talk must begin with a prayer. A fourth may declare that it forbids any public expression of the divine.

These membranes extend to information. No community is required to carry a channel that violates its vows. You cannot beam a pornography channel into a monastery’s bandwidth; you cannot drown a quiet valley in your stadium’s loudness. But the Compact refuses viewpoint bottling. Every person, in any community, can connect to the Neutral Library through a public line that is whisper-thin and hard to drown. It is curated by humans and Stewards from across the Constellation who take a bloodless oath: show the map, not the path. It contains descriptions of every charter and how to contact them, basic science, art that offends almost no one (and a warning that art that offends some is elsewhere), and a mirror of the exit office software. When in doubt, the Neutral Library errs on the side of equipping the person holding the device in their hand.

5. The Ledger of Winds

The Constellation does not pretend that harm is simple. The harm a foundry does is not a fist, and yet a plume of sulfur crosses a border like one.

The Ledger of Winds is an old tool that Stewards and humans updated. It is a network of sensors at perimeters, a clearinghouse for contracts, a table of prices for harms we can measure (kilotons of carbon, parts per billion of nitrates) and quotas for harms we cannot price (extinctions, sacred sites, silence). Communities agree to what will be counted at each border before the first machine is switched on. When the winds blow in both directions, the charges cancel like algebra. When they do not, there is payment or change.

If you want to run a coal plant next to a solar commune, you can; you will pay so much that you will not want to for long. If you want to pour sunlight and algae into a vat and run a festival on laughter and compost, you will be compensated by your sootier neighbors for a good long while. The Ledger is not perfect, and no one lets it be the only thing that speaks. Every quarter, communities send delegates to their basin assembly with their ledgers, their grudges, and a basket of donut holes or dried fish or figs to apologize in case someone throws a shoe.

6. The Museum of Mistakes

We used to hide our shames. Then we found out that covering a bad decision with a mirror made it easier to make again.

Every Steward and every charter that has made a decision that turned out to be wrong must tell the story in public at least once, in a place that filed it under their name.

The Museum of Mistakes began in a ruined operating theater in an abandoned hospital, then moved to a glass hall on a river bend, then became a moving gathering with a schedule like a harvest fair that crisscrosses the Constellation year-round. It has cold cases—algorithms that amplified bile; zoning that erased a creek and drowned a neighborhood five summers later; a vow that seemed righteous until a child died because the pharmacy could not sell syringes on a Sunday. It has warm rooms too: places where someone said, “We were wrong in time.”

The admission price is a story of your own mistake, told to a person you brought. The gift shop sells repair manuals.

7. The Constraint Corps

“No conquest” is a gentle-sounding rule until someone tries to break it.

The Constellation does not have an army; it has the Constraint Corps.

The Corps’ work looks like logistics more often than it looks like legend. They rebuild roads two towns share when the towns won’t talk. They cut redundant fiber circles and reroute so a propaganda deluge can’t overwhelm a prayer. They carry out children whose aunt locked the back door and losened a hinge to hide the sound of the front one.

And then there are the rare moments that are the reason the Corps exists. Once, a group of investors, backed by old money and three algorithms that learned too much, tried to take an estuary from the people who had netted it for five hundred years. They declared bankruptcy in five towns, bought judges on two coasts, and printed a pamphlet that said: we must clear this place and flood it, for mitigation. Men with orange vests arrived with maps and bulldozers.

A Greywing cut the sky. It was one of the Corps’ high-altitude craft. Using loudspeakers, it read the Compact aloud to the bulldozer operators, pointed out which blue stars were nearest, and gave them four hours to walk away. The Stewards turned the earth beneath the bulldozers to pudding by rupturing the perched aquifer with a charge. The machines slumped. The Corps dropped mending teams into the marsh. No one died. The investors lost their money. They told their grandchildren that the Constellation had become a tyranny. The fishermen told their grandchildren the opposite. The Museum of Mistakes exhibits the charge casing, the pamphlet, the maps, the ledger pages that showed what the floodgates would have done to the towns upstream. One of the Greywing pilots recorded his fear and added his apology for frightening the bulldozer operators.

The Corps keeps the Constellation honest by remembering it can act. The Museum keeps the Constellation honest by making it look at itself after it has acted.

8. The Money We Have and the Money We Don’t

If you walk through the Constellation with a pocket full of ordinary money you can buy almost everything. If you walk through the Constellation with a pocket full of what people call attention, you can buy almost anything else.

There is a baseline income: universal credits that buy food, power, and mobility. Fabricators print the basics; power spans desert sun, ridgetop wind, heat beneath the stones, and a few shining towers where hydrogen becomes water and light again.

Layered on top is the lattice: a system of reputation, favors, and invitations that is not centrally tallied but locally remembered. People wear their work on their sleeves in ways the people they care about can see: tiny tags that name the public things they have made and the private circles where they have been a godsend. There is a difference between being famous for a Masquerade Ball and being known for the winter you taught three old men how to use mobility exoskeletons so they could muck their grandsons’ boats again. The lattice knows the difference. It does not forgive harm lightly. It forgets grudges when someone has bent their back long enough.

Communities choose how much to let the lattice rule. Some make all apprenticeships run through it. Some refuse to count anything at all and rely on gossip. A university tried to base its entire curriculum on it once. The Museum displays the warning letter written in rose ink by a kitchen worker whose wages vanished when she could not stomach sharing a dining room with a famous man: “You are building a church of attention without a faith to keep it kind.”

9. The Archipelago We Already Lived In

You can’t sail to the Constellation the way you sail to an island. You walk to it while you’re busy doing other things.

We were already making it. We were building micro-communities on servers: historians and gamers, fat activists and ballroom dancers, cautious people who say “content note” before they talk about blood. We were gating our forums, setting our norms, keeping lists of doctors who would not call us the wrong names. We were leaving towns with bad air and bad cops for towns with less of both. We were starting special economic zones in Africa and Latin America to try things big states would not. We were arguing about whether a soda machine should be in the staff room or behind a door. We were doing climate migration in slow motion. We were building digital twins of our cities to plan bus lines, then realizing the twins could be raised like children.

The first charter valleys signed the Compact when storms made crop insurance a farce and neighbors who loved each other could not agree whether to retreat upland. They built Blue Lines between towns that voted opposite ways. They created the first exit offices because they’d lost a child to an aunt’s good intentions and would not again. They asked their grid software to testify in court and learned it had jokes. When a shallow seastead off Taiwan and a Midwestern suburb signed the same contract a year apart, it looked less like law and more like a marriage between people who did not know they were courting.

10. A Day in Three Communities

- The Lean Isles are ten neighborhoods connected by two bridges, built as a living lab for people who wanted to take the tug of sugary abundance seriously. Their public market smells like thyme, stonefruit, and old wood. All the sweets are behind bright green pantry doors; a door recognizes you after a week and quietly asks if you want to set a goal: two sweets a week, five? Children draw their goals in crayon; the doors remember. There is a festival in late summer called High Honey when the foil comes off everything and joy is compulsory. At vespers, the Isles toll their kettles to remind people to drink water.

- The Ninth Ward of Saint Honore keeps a long sabbath. Friday afternoon, the bakeries give away end pieces, doorbells hang out in the hallway and are not permitted to ring, and even the Stewards dress plain. The Neutral Library is open and the exit office is staffed. If you need a splint on Saturday you can get one; if you need a noodle you’re meant to wait.

- The Estuary Commons is where a child named Ada arrived with a Silkpass tucked in a too-large coat. She stayed with the Communal Aunts. She ate soup that tasted like the old world and the new. She joined the Farscouts and visited the Ridge where no cars are allowed, the Perpetual Carnival that bans calendars inside its gates, and the Machine Monastery where people who love engines take vows of non-domination. She visited a church with a high window; she did not return. She joined the reed harvest and learned how to cut in a way that makes the marsh thicker next year. When she was ready, she applied to apprentice to Thistle, and Thistle said yes because Ada told a story about a fish.

11. Children Who Choose Wrong

People ask: what if a child leaves a violent home for a community that is, in the questioner’s opinion, worse—a party town, a cult of stillness, a business park that worships cleverness?

The Constellation assumes that a person who makes their own mistake learns in a way a person who lives someone else’s correctness cannot. But it does not shrug. Apprenticeships in communities that set strict vows are never indeterminate. Every young person away from the place they were born has a Legal Stranger: a person with no other role but to check in and ask questions like a foreign journalist. The Legal Stranger reports back to no one. They keep notes sealed for the Museum unless there is harm. If they think a community is becoming a cutting board disguised as a dinner table, they ask the Constraint Corps to send a human with a clipboard and a Steward with a bad mood.

12. We Still Fight

We did not outsit disagreement by replacing it with kindness. We fight. We hold rivals in our arms like a child we cannot banish from a family. We rage at Stewards. We accuse the Museum of being prurient, pornographic, pious.

We have failure. A community that wanted to glorify a war that never happened produced a generation that left for the capital and never wrote back. A commune that forbade coercion learned late that social shame is a lever with a velvet handle. A university made of attention failed, paid its kitchen staff in apologies, and then found actual money. Two charter valleys went to the basin assembly with ledgers and donut holes and still nearly came to blows over a creek that believed in gravity more than vows.

And yet: when a cyclone loops too far south and chews up five neighborhoods, three others that dislike their politics print walls and swing hammers before the argument is over. When a person at a public market says, “I do not want to hear a prayer,” the stallholder says, “Then may your peaches be sweet for their own reasons,” and the person says, “That will do.”

13. What We Didn’t Keep

We kept a lot that was old—adoptions, feasts, the way a corner shop keeps the news—but we shed two ideas that cut us whenever we picked them up.

We stopped believing there is one way to be good enforced by a wise state. We also stopped believing there is one way to be free enforced by a state that refuses to be wise. In their place we put the Constellation’s small wager: that a person can do better among people who share their vows and are allowed to mean them, with paths lit for leaving.

14. A Plausible Way From Here

If you are reading this closer to our past than our present, you may be rolling your eyes. This sounds expensive, fussy, impossible to start without a magic wand.

This is what we actually did.

- We repurposed refugee agencies, battered spouse shelters, and sanctuary networks into the first exit offices. We funded them with a mix of philanthropy, diaspora remittances, and a sin tax on companies that relied on narrowcast addiction to sell ads. We hammered blue stars over their doors.

- We turned “cooling houses”—lab benches where municipalities tested digital twins of their transit or water systems—into nurseries for Stewards. We open-sourced their training sets. We wrote their oaths into law and gave their mentors teeth.

- We used special economic zones, indigenous autonomous regions, and old-fashioned municipal home rule to write the first charters. Early ones were clumsy. The Museum displays their calligraphy next to their amendments.

- We passed an international treaty that said one thing and nothing else: any signatory must provide a free exit for any minor who asks to leave, no questions. The first signatories were a Pacific island state that knew about storms; a landlocked country with a diaspora; a province with a memory; and a former empire with a new shame. We named the treaty after a woman who left and a man who stayed.

- We built the Ledger of Winds out of cheap sensors, actuarial tables insurers already used, and citizen science. Its first use-case was not a factory but a stadium whose roar shattered sleep three towns over. The fix was easy. The applause was grudging. The habit stuck.

- We told families that they could be more than blood again. We reinvented adoption as a rite between peers. In some places it involved bread and salt. In some places it involved ink on skin—temporary, then permanent if you both still wanted it a year later. In the Rain Islands people share a bowl of broth and dip two ropes into it and knot them. We allowed Stewards into these rituals and learned that public love can make an intelligence kinder.

There were failures. There will be more. But we now live in a web of neighborhoods where people can be odd without being lonely, strict without being cruel, gentle without being caught, and wrong without being uncorrectable.

15. A Drawing on a Fridge

In a house near a ridge-line, a retired Greywing pilot lives with her partner and a plant that blooms when it hears rain on a tin roof. On their refrigerator is a drawing done in marker by a child who was not theirs when she made it and was, in a way, theirs by the time she brought it over: a river, a fish with a smile, a bicycle with scarves snagged in its spokes, a door with a blue star, and a person with many hands. Underneath, written in wrong-handed letters, is: thank you for finding the door and for leaving it when I wanted you to.

You can build a lot on ledgers, compacts, and clever machines. In the Constellation we did. But the thing that made it feel like we had stepped into a good future was smaller: a thousand fridges with unmatched magnets, sticky hands bringing home maps they drew themselves, and enough room to choose which map to follow.
