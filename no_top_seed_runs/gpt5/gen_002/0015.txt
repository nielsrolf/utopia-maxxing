The Commonwealth of the Many

We did not get one miracle. We got a thousand small ones, stitched together by a simple promise: no one has to live the same life.

The promise fit on a single page. It was drafted in the wake of three overlapping crises—pandemic, climate shocks, and a wave of mutual loathing that made people consider force as the only way to protect the things they cherished. What replaced that mood was not a new empire or a clever slogan, but a covenant that millions could sign without giving up their convictions.

The covenant had five clauses.

1) No coercive force across communities. No militias, no culture wars waged with police power. A single mutual-defense service existed to stop any community that tried to conquer or harass another.

2) The right of exit and the open future. Every adult could leave any community for any other, with a public travel voucher issued on request. Every child received, each year, direct contact with a neutral ombuds network and a universal “world curriculum” that made clear: there are other ways to live, and here is how to reach them. At sixteen, any young person could declare independence, with a migration stipend and guardian ad litem to ensure it was truly their choice.

3) The externality rule. If what you do spills measurable harm onto others—disease, carbon, river poisons, denial of shared spectrum, persistent harassment—it is regulated at the level that can see the whole spill. The inverse held as well: if your policies only applied to your people, and stayed within your borders and bandwidth, you could go very far indeed.

4) Legibility without control. The Commonwealth tracked flows of energy, pollution, pathogens, and money well enough to tax or block harms; it did not monitor ideas for their own sake. Communities could set strong memetic boundaries for themselves, including advertising bans and import filters, and no one could shove content through them by force.

5) Shared floors, free ceilings. A common budget funded basic services—clinics, a travel right, neutral schools, clean air, safe water, a data privacy baseline, and disaster insurance—paid for by taxes that could not be evaded by moving one town over. Above that floor, communities were free to build their own worlds.

These five clauses transformed “tolerance” from a miserable ceasefire into a joyous proliferation. Not just subcultures, but whole polities with charters you could read before you ever visited, rating agencies that told you whether their labor law or their nightlife was compatible with your preferences, and welcoming houses in every region that specialized in helping people land well.

On paper this looked like a recipe for balkanization. In practice, it was a flywheel for trust.

Energy, permissioned at last

We discovered that abundance is a political art. The price of solar modules and batteries had been falling for decades. Small modular reactors, long stagnant in the licensing swamp, finally passed through a streamlined “pattern code” process that recognized proven designs and allowed copy-paste deployments with local customization only where geology or grid topology differed. Public utilities invited private capital into “district build-operate” concessions that competed on uptime and resilience.

By 2035:

- Suburban rooftops, parking canopies, and industrial roofs wore light, cheap perovskite films that clipped onto rails in hours. Local apprenticeships paid teenagers to install and maintain them.
- Microgrids with smart inverters islanded neighborhoods when storms hit. Grocery stores did not throw away their cold chains; dialysis machines never blinked.
- High-temperature heat pumps and modular fission plants took over the dirtiest industrial heat, producing green ammonia along rail spines and decarbonizing cement with direct electrification or hydrogen.
- Deep geothermal projects learned to fracture granite without seismic risk and came online in the interior, anchoring rural electrification without long fragile lines.

Permitting changed character. Building anything that matched a pattern code—derived from tested exemplars, finite element models, and years of post-construction performance data—went through a 60-day “as-built variance check,” not a five-year gauntlet. If you wanted to innovate, you could, but you did so inside sandbox zones where a failure wouldn’t cascade. The result was that people could see the future being installed down their street, not argued about on their feed.

Planning by wager

We also admitted that our arguments rarely converged and hired a referee. Agencies created formal markets to predict measurable targets: hospital readmission rates under new care protocols; the net effect of a congestion-pricing plan on air pollution; the survival benefit of a cancer drug. Traders posted claims and odds; registered experts explained their theses; insurers underwrote impact bonds that paid out if the promised benefits arrived.

No one was forced to obey the market price—it was not a god—but budgets shifted automatically with it. If a flood barrier’s projected benefit-cost ratio dipped below a threshold and a superior design’s odds rose, funds moved. If a drug’s benefit looked exceptional, provisional approval arrived with automatic retraction triggers if post-market outcomes disappointed. Politicians could overrule, but they did so in public and took a haircut from a discretionary fund when the forecasters proved right. Within a few years, cities that used oracles bilked fewer taxpayers and killed fewer people.

Licenses became warranties

We replaced many occupational licenses with “competence proofs and coverage.” If you could pass a robust, practical exam, publish your outcomes, and secure an insurer to stand behind your work, you could work. Licenses persisted where real externalities existed—airline pilots, nuclear operators—but most others turned into portable, testable credentials. A national registry tracked who had passed what, linked to an outcomes database. As a patient or client, you could sort by price, performance, and claim history in an interface that felt like an airfare search, not a medieval guild.

Wealth from the ground

We taxed land values, not buildings. Assessors used lidar, transaction records, zoning entitlements, and local amenities to calculate the annual public value of each parcel. People paid for the privilege of exclusive access to land whose value their neighbors and the broader economy created. Improvements were tax-free. The result: vacant lots filled. Slumlords either fixed their properties or sold them to those who would. A share of the site rents funded universal basic services—free and frequent transit, open clinics, and libraries that doubled as maker spaces and job-matching hubs.

Major metros layered in dynamic fees for curb space, street access, and peak-hour congestion, all visible in the same app that let you summon a bus-on-demand or book a cargo bike. You could still drive into the center in your truck laden with materials; you just paid a price that reflected the finite space you took. People complained less than expected because the travel time they saved was obvious, and because the revenue built the bus lane that had been promised and, this time, arrived on schedule.

Automation without dispossession

Robots took the dangerous, dull, and dirty jobs first: climbing transmission towers, sanitizing hospital rooms, unloading containers. Narrow artificial intelligence plodded up learning curves in places with thick streams of labeled data—factories, warehouses, kitchens—and because litigation risk was a real thing, deployments remained humble and supervised.

To prevent productivity gains from turning into windfalls for a few, we created “automation participation” trusts. When a firm deployed systems that displaced a shift’s worth of labor but did not shrink output, a sliver of the savings went to a regional pool that paid out to every worker in that labor market quarterly. It was not a universal income; it was a universal productivity dividend, keyed to where you lived and retrained. Community colleges became the most optimistic buildings in town: clean labs with short courses that taught a nurse to supervise a diagnostic tool or a carpenter to program a CNC router.

Plenty of ways to be good

We did not try to agree on “good.” We agreed on the floor and let the ceilings bloom. The directory of communities read like a catalog you could browse with coffee.

- Quiet Diet Cities. You read the charter before you visited: billboard bans, sugary-soda cup caps, no advertisements within 50 meters of a school or clinic, a 9 p.m. freight curfew, aggressive tree canopy targets. Restaurants served whatever they pleased, but the checkout aisle wars of your childhood did not exist, because the aisle was redesigned. People who had spent decades trying and failing to quiet their environments moved here and breathed easier. The rest of us visited and thanked them for the calm, then went home to our neon.

- Sabbaths and Screens. One valley observed a day of rest—no commerce, no news feeds, no vibrations in your pocket—and they meant it. Another town wrote a charter of radical expressive freedom, where profanity, burlesque, and transgression were civic art forms. Both communities paid their land rents, both vaccinated their kids, both built storm drains to code, and neither tried to change the other across the ridge.

- Child-Friendly Republics. Tax codes that gave families with three or more children property tax abatements; corner parks on a nine-minute walking grid; “slow street” designs everywhere; apartment buildings with ground-floor stroller parking and communal kitchens for co-cooking; a right to part-time work with uncapped promotion tracks. Fertility rose there, but so did educators’ salaries and the patience of strangers when a toddler cried.

- Vegan Counties. These places banned slaughterhouses and subsidized microbial and cultivated proteins, with shared pilot plants that food startups could rent by the hour. The tasting rooms became tourist draws; the town petting zoo became a sanctuary.

- Craftwork Townships. Tolerances in the woodshops would make a machinist blush. Mass-timber lofts rose in old car parks. A guild mark actually meant something again because it was backed by statistics.

Generous boundaries

Communities had the right to bound their infospheres. That did not mean turning off the internet. It meant consent walls. If you walked into Quiet Diet City and opened your phone, the public network defaulted to an ad-free portal; if you wanted to stream something loud or promotional, you flipped to your own data plan and passed a literal popup reminder: You are entering an ad zone; proceed? If you bought land here, you agreed that your property would not host a digital billboard unless a two-thirds vote overturned the charter. No one forced you to move there; the travel voucher worked fine the other way.

Children first

We placed children beyond the reach of anyone’s ideology. In every community, kids attended a daily local program that matched their parents’ values and the charter they had chosen. And once a month, in a building with a neutral crest shared by all, they participated in the World Class. The World Class did not catechize. It showed maps—geographic and social—of the region, explained how to reach an ombuds in three clicks or a thirty-minute walk, and taught a course in bodily autonomy, consent, and the mechanics of leaving if they ever chose to. The day before their sixteenth birthday, they would meet an ombuds who checked in privately: do you want to stay? Do you need anything to leave? If a teenager chose to go, they stayed for two weeks in a Commonwealth-licensed welcome house, finished a short life-admin curriculum (bank account, housing search, safety planning), then joined the community they had chosen. Parents were instructed to grieve if they needed to, and then welcome their adult child home when they visited.

The way we funded childhood dissolved the old moral hazard. Education was financed by a per-child endowment contributed by the Commonwealth and topped up by communities, with all funds following the child wherever they lived. If you moved away just as your daughter graduated high school, her original town did not resent you: it had already been paid to educate her. If you arrived with two toddlers, your new town received, from day one, the per-child endowment that would help staff the nursery.

Health and time

Longevity ceased to be a billionaire’s parlor game. Primary care revived as a guild with status and delight, because it had tools again. Clinics offered home renin-angiotensin testing, fecal microbiome sequencing that tied to dietitians who cared, sleep coaches with authority to prescribe soundproofing and blue-light filters under a “right to rest.” Fall prevention became a civic contest: which building could reduce hip fractures to zero first?

Gene therapies moved steadily from the lab through a safety stack that had been built during the mRNA revolution. A public genomic registry, consented to by participants and guarded fiercely, made it trivial to run pragmatic trials. Not trivial in the sense of “easy”; trivial in the sense that it was a moral and technical routine, like screening blood for pathogens. High-profile heritable edits remained illegal and unpopular, but correcting monogenic diseases in adults was a bore by 2040.

Cryonics pivoted into “biostasis,” and it stopped being a punchline the first time a nineteen-year-old with an untreatable glioma signed up for it, died in the back of a Commonwealth ambulance, and a decade later had a mother who could visit her temperature-logged capsule, audit the funds that maintained it, and post a letter to her daughter’s future caregivers in a publicly indexed archive. Joining was opt-in; mockery declined because the biostasis fund also paid for the sort of conservatorship for memory-impaired patients that everyone’s grandmother needed even sooner.

No more plagues

After we buried too many people, we did what our grandparents had done with fire safety. We set standards. Everywhere: HEPA and UV-C in public buildings; CO2 readouts on cafe walls; wastewater sequencing on a dashboard you could read with your morning coffee. Community response teams—people mostly in their twenties on two-year service terms—practiced contact mapping and supply runs during peacetime. When a new pathogen arrived, we locally suppressed with competence, without the moral theater. The Commonwealth could not order a community to close its schools, but the World Class teachers in every community were unioned across communities and in practice moved in sync when risk was clear. People complied more easily with measures they understood and that had off-ramps forecasted in a market you could bet on.

Beyond the shore

Frontiers existed if you wanted them. A few dozen sea platforms became engineering labs—cheaper than New York real estate, less lonely than ships—legally embedded under the Commonwealth’s externality rules. They provided wave-power devices and coral nurseries, and occasionally a scandal that the markets punished appropriately. In low Earth orbit, cheaper lift led to telescopes that could resolve methane leaks from space and orbital foundries that spin-cast silicon carbide mirrors like honey. On the Moon, not much happened beyond a few research habitats and a good story to tell schoolchildren about patience.

How we got here

Plausibility lies in sequence.

First, we got better at clean air and buildings out of sheer self-interest. Insurance companies paid lower claims where schools had MERV 13s and a maintenance plan; zoning boards tied occupancy permits to ventilation measured annually, not inspected once. It felt like the transition to smoke-free restaurants: at first loud, then normal.

Second, one medium city—Toledo, say—shifted 30% of its property tax to land value, cut parking minimums downtown, used the windfall to run electric buses every eight minutes on six routes, and bragged about it on TikTok until five other places copied it. Ten years later, statehouses did not move to 100% land value tax out of ideology, but because it built more homes without a dime in subsidies. Homebuilders liked it. Renters loved it.

Third, Congress created the Policy Oracle Authority with a narrow remit: forecast tournament standards; a safe harbor for agencies that used them; grants to cities that ran properly-staffed oracles for big projects. At first only coastal technocrats used it. Then a drought-state governor realized that water markets priced by oracles rather than political pull kept his farmers alive. Traders from five distinct ideologies got rich together predicting the flow of the Colorado River, and trust increased by one notch.

Fourth, a court case, brought by parents whose child had died of bullying and neglect in a tightly sealed religious school, forced a reckoning. The outcome was not a national ban or a national curriculum. It was the open-future clause: the World Class in every community; the ombuds office near every school; the right of exit, with teeth. Communities that loved their ways did not die; they flourished as people who hated them left. Those who stayed knew where the door was and chose not to use it. Even the strictest fathers sat easier with authority thus legitimized.

Fifth, energy finally got permission. Not permission to be reckless; permission to be copied. The pattern codes won because they were simple to understand and hard to game. Neighbors objected less when projects moved dirt three months after approval, not three years later when the world had changed.

Sixth, we made it very easy to start small communities with narrow goals. State legislatures certified “charter districts” that could, with a two-thirds vote of residents and a bond to cover shared services, slice out of a county and write a charter. These districts could not dodge Commonwealth floors or taxes, but they could adjust everything else. The first wave of charters were dull: homeowners capping short-term rentals; logistics parks negotiating truck routes with their neighbors. Then the interesting ones came.

The hundred small things

On any given day, the Commonwealth looked boring because competence is not loud. The forecaster on the evening news shrugged about the odds of a heat emergency next week and recommended the six cooling centers in your part of town. Your daughter’s school cafeteria offered heavily-spiced legumes beside the pizza; the pizza was decent, the legumes were better for learning, and the forecast market that funded the menu design had discovered a 12% gap in afternoon math scores that narrowed when lunch had slow carbs; the school published this without shame. Your friend took a train to a craftwork township for a week of training on a machine that sounded like bees; she came back with a certificate that auto-populated her public profile, which her new insurer accepted, lowering her professional coverage target premium by 17%.

If you had an allergy to loud places, you moved to a borough where scooters chirped rather than whined and delivery trucks obeyed a sensor-defined path; if you were in your rave years, you lived three stops away, did not lie to yourself about your likes, and kept a debit card for the congestion charge on nights when you brought your car into the zone anyway. If you were a twenty-year-old trans kid in a town whose memorized prayers and creeds chafed, you sent a message to an ombuds on a channel your parents could not see; two weeks later you stood on a platform with one bag, your best friend holding your hand, and got on a train to a sofa you could trust.

The money you paid for these arrangements was not invisible. Each quarter, the statement arrived: your site rent for the land your home sits on; your share of the carbon fee, offset by your household’s reductions; your dividend from the regional automation fund; your community’s contribution to the childhood endowment and the clinics. The numbers fluctuated in ways that made sense rather than ways that darkened your mood.

The work of people

This world was not run by an AI that loved us. It was built by mechanics, nurses, teachers, project managers, vaccinators, roofers, mediators, forecasters, civil engineers, and law clerks. Its magic was not that bad people disappeared; we still had grifters and cruel streaks. Its magic was that you could move away from them; that the cheapest ways to hurt others—and to poison your own future—were taxed; that evidence had a place not as a cudgel but as an odds board; and that children knew, much sooner than we did, that there are many kinds of adulthood and more than one door through which to walk.

What improved us most was not that we agreed. It was that our disagreements became creative again. If you believed that modest dress built better friendships, you moved where that was the norm and found out if you were right, with no one harassing you from the next town over. If you believed that erotic art saved lives, you moved where you could practice your craft at full wattage and saw who thrived. When people got hurt, we tightened a rule. When a trick worked in one place, we copied it, with credit. The Commonwealth’s crest over the ombuds office came to represent something few expected to feel again: affection for the institutions that kept the peace.

There are still problems. Sea levels continue to rise and we are not fast enough. We disagree about memory—what to commemorate, what to forgive. Comedy becomes hard and then easy again, as new sensibilities mature. We worry that the World Class could become a backdoor for smuggling culture wars. We build watchdogs for the watchdogs and markets to bet on the watchdogs’ honesty. And yet most mornings, people wake up in neighborhoods that make sense to them, step into streets that respect their time, breathe air that does not shorten their lives, and plan a weekend that reflects who they are.

If you visit, go first to a welcome house. Ask for the charter catalog and a cup of tea. Tell them what you love and what you can’t stand. They will not argue with you. They will circle some pages. They will offer you a train pass and a small stipend for using it. The rest is yours to choose.
