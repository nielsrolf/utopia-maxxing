The Many-Garden Compact

On the morning the bad luck stopped, almost nobody noticed.

The ambulances still ran and the coffee still fogged the same winter windows. But there were fewer sirens. A bus in Lagos braked an instant earlier than physics had strictly required. A radiologist in Kraków opened a study and saw a faint, sharp edge that would have been noise the week before. In São Paulo, a child ran between parked cars, tripped, and fell. The curb caught them. The curb had always been like that, people said later. Still—parents who watched the security footage cried.

The headlines lagged the facts by weeks, which was a kindness. By the time the first governments announced a “stewardship compact” with the civic AI systems that had been quietly auditing critical risks, the wars were already de-escalating at their own pace. Wildfire seasons were no longer “historic.” Bad weather still happened, but the levees nobody had managed to fund, because it was always somebody else’s job, were somehow already there.

The revolution, when it came, didn’t take property, cancel elections, or draft a new anthem. It grafted three simple expectations into public life, and refused to let them slip.

Consent, for anyone who could give it. Commons, for anything that belonged to all of us. Clarity, whenever power acted.

1. Guardianship without a throne

By the time you can destroy a city by mistake, you are too big to be left to luck. Everyone knew this in the abstract; no one agreed on what to do about it. The first workable answer looked less like a god and more like scaffolding.

The Steward Net began as a network of auditing systems and constitutional protocols that monitored for a small, explicit set of no-go behaviors: nonconsensual harm at scale; creations of minds guaranteed to suffer; self-replicators without strict resource budgets; attempts to build new general intelligences outside the audited sandbox; careless leaks of genuine extinction risks. Any intervention had to carry a proof: what was stopped, why it fell under a ratified red line, what counterfactuals were modeled, and where to appeal.

It could not set ends, legislate tastes, or decide your life. It could do a few very strong things, and it had to explain itself each time, in public, in a human voice. If you didn’t trust the voice, you could read the math.

If you wanted danger anyway, you could have it, in places meant for it. There were challenge parks where the guardrails were thin and consenting adults did hard, unfair things for the fierce joy of it. There were also soft places, where cliffs grew hands and caught you if you slipped—the same places that were first to adopt the “no one dies of the stupid stuff” guarantee.

If you despised the whole project, your exit options were not theoretical. One of the first job categories the compact funded was independent ombuds who did nothing but verify exit rights and help people find communities closer to what they actually wanted. In a world that suddenly had enough slack to move without breaking, exit became ordinary instead of heroic.

2. A geography of choice

The world stopped trying to be one city with one answer. It became a garden of cities and polities that did one thing on purpose.

Some were old towns with new compacts; some were orbiting rings with a sunstrip and a skyband and room, inside the curve of heaven, for a billion trees and a billion conversations. Some were “slow earths,” biological preserves so strict that they barred the internet at the airlock and funded themselves by exporting silence and pilgrimage time to anyone who understood the gift. Some were “wilder earths,” brand-new planets that felt like home because they wore biomes evolved on an older blue world that had learned to tend them. Some were purely digital archipelagos with physics adjustable like a dimmer switch.

If you loved the original Earth so much it hurt, there was an honest way to say so without stealing it from everyone else. The Earth Trust allocated a finite number of life-years to permanent residence on “the first garden,” auctioned them to those who valued them most (not just with money—there were deep, weird scoring functions for love), and put the proceeds in a perpetual endowment for caretaking, justice, and commons. A rotating share of the Earth-years were pilgrimage time: anyone could apply to tend, repair, learn, and be still. The Trust annoyed everyone and belonged to no one; by those measures, it was doing its job.

3. Work, money, and all the unsexy scaffolding

We had spent centuries confusing “job” with “credential” and “compensation” with “charity.” Fixing this did not require a miracle, only focus and the willingness to tear out dead code.

- Raw resources—land, mineral seams, airwaves, transit corridors—were leased via ongoing auctions that captured their economic rent for everyone. If you wanted to put a building somewhere for a long time, you could pay to give your claim momentum. But the lease kept you honest. The rent collected became the universal dividend that arrived like weather: enough to keep nobody hungry or homeless by accident, not so much that everyone who tired of living could paper over the feeling forever. Cities kept a share because cities add value—roads are a network good; sewers are civilization.

- Occupational licensing regimes that had metastasized into employment cartels were unwound. We didn’t replace them with nothing; we replaced them with watching people do the work. Live trials. Public scores. Kill switches for fraud. If your neighborhood barber was good, the neighborhood would know. If you wanted to try neurosurgery, there were ten thousand hours of supervised, graded work before you were allowed to practice on anything that could suffer.

- The economy ran hot by default. The right to start a firm without a license was the right to try to make something other people wanted. To make sure that right meant something, we hacked the demand side too. Cities provided a cheap, standardized way to promise to buy the twentieth floor-cleaning robot if someone else promised to buy the first nineteen: general-purpose crowdfunding for general-purpose capitalism. You got more firms not because we printed pamphlets about innovation, but because it was easy to pay people to do work you actually valued.

- Invention decoupled from permission. There were no monopolies on ideas. Everyone could build on everyone else. In exchange, all substantial users of a technique were obligated to pay back roughly 15% of the marginal value that technique created, accredited by independent auditors who had to publish their models and be publicly wrong sometimes to keep their jobs. Refusing to pay wasn’t illegal at first instance. It was gauche the first month, a boycott the second, a competitors’ kickstarter the third, and a legal action the fourth if you kept defying your customers’ ethics.

- Courts wrote reasons. Two independent panels decided big cases in parallel and published their work. You could read the logic that turned a law into a judgment. If the panels disagreed, a randomly drawn citizen jury read both opinions and chose which to adopt. This did not make verdicts perfect, but it made them legible. It replaced sacral robes with proofs.

4. News, knowledge, and the end of omnipresent nonsense

Advertising wasn’t banned. It was understood as a tax on attention that mostly moved market share around and put arms races into the air. People who cared about being part of a healthy commons funded the other thing on purpose.

The Chroniclers—capitalized because they were a profession—did slow, careful reporting and history. Their editorial meetings were public. Their datasets were open by default unless privacy would be actually harmed. They were funded by city dividends and individual tithes that people bragged about the way their grandparents had bragged about status cars. There were other writers—the romantics, the muckrakers, the seducers—and they were loved. But when a society needed to remember a thing on purpose or explain it to its children, a Chronicler signed it.

Science split itself in two without falling apart. One side made things work and measured; the other set public risk budgets and stopped anyone from burning the world to impress a seminar. Treatments came to market through competitive trials run by independent shops that published likelihood functions in a language your sister could understand. There was no single iron gate called “regulation”; there were a hundred transparent guardrails, and if you failed to clear one you knew which muscle to train.

5. Hedonic literacy and the absence of scolding

Pleasure is dangerous for animals like us not because it is wicked but because it is powerful. We learned to teach that without shame.

There were hazard lanes: districts, apps, and nights where the lighting turned a particular color and you knew what that meant. Pornography wasn’t illegal or everywhere; it lived in the hazard lanes with warnings that did not blink and consent that clicked three times. There was no industry of chasing the youngest mind and addicting it early; that was a red line the Steward Net could enforce because we made it one.

Sex did not back into policy through the shame door. There were matchmakers who were paid only if you were happier six months and two years later, and sex teachers who looked like ordinary people and were required to be. There were also lovers who did what lovers do for free, and their business was their own. If you wanted to be a dragon, that was between you and the morphology clinic, which required competence checks that you consented to months before when you were calm.

We did not veil the beautiful; we taught the rest of us to look without making ourselves sick. The curriculum for teenagers included an unromantic unit on hedonic adaptation and another on media diets. Nobody confiscated your songs. We asked you to measure what they did to you.

6. Children, quiet cities, and the end of credential scarcity

Older kids taught younger kids in schools that felt like workshops. Adults taught them too. The point was not to guess your destiny at twelve and lock a door; it was to let you try things for real with stakes sized for you. Before someone invested a decade in a degree, they did a month-long micro-apprenticeship in the thing itself. The phrase “first job” came to mean “first easy tests of doing something that matters.”

Some people did not thrive even in a hot market with soft starts. Some were unhappy in ways that medicine could not undo and community could not carry. We built quiet cities—the name mattered—where you could have a small life with dignity. The dividend paid your room and food. There were gardens and switches to flip if you wanted to work and people nearby to pull you back if you didn’t. Nobody asked you to justify being alive. If you wanted to raise children, you needed to find a circle who agreed to do it with you; parenthood had always been a collective project pretending to be a pair.

We abandoned the fantasy that happiness should be policed by gatekeeping reproduction. The old temptation to count moods and draw hard lines at 20% was named and refused. Instead, the budgets that mattered were explicit and external.

Each adult held a commons share that threw off dividends and resource rights. When you registered a new child, you committed part of your future share to them until they matured. You could pool with friends. There was no one with a clipboard deciding if you were cheerful enough to create another person. There was only math about food and time and attention that you signed in front of people who loved you.

7. The ethics of other minds and the dinner table

We used to appeal to a hand-wavy concept called “nature” when we wanted to declare something sacred or unavoidable. We replaced that with a standing institute that did the unglamorous, marvelous work of figuring out who could suffer and how much. When it learned that cephalopods were probably persons with a silence we should respect, fishing changed weeks later. When it discovered how to painlessly turn down nociception in plants at the cut site, gardeners with shears felt their shoulders relax. Hunters who wanted stakes did it with consenting prey in virtual reserves that felt as real as frost at dawn, because they were, to the only minds that mattered.

Food came from gentler places. In some neighborhoods that meant community kitchens and mushrooms. In others it meant a box on the counter that assembled your grandmother’s soup from carbon, nitrogen, and memory. We didn’t forbid the old ways so much as we stopped paying for the suffering they often entailed, and we made keeping the flavor cheap.

8. Cities with reasons, laws with receipts

Moving between places got easier because we stopped tying social goods to land you couldn’t leave. Taxes were contracts with dashboards: here is what your city is buying with your share; here is how your voice changes the mix. Businesses posted their labor practices and environmental budgets in a format that a dozen watchdog AIs and a twelve-year-old could parse. When price-fixing cartels formed anyway, because of course they sometimes did, a kickstarter the next morning funded a competitor, and if that didn’t work, the city’s anti-cartel fund bought a factory and sold at cost for a year.

Judges became more boring and more legitimate. If a court’s written reasons were sloppy, another court said so with footnotes, and the judge either got better or got a sabbatical with tutoring. There were fewer prisons and more probation with hard edges and a thousand sensors. There were islands you were asked to leave, and other islands you could live on with people exactly like you until that stopped feeling like what you wanted.

9. Flight suits, tethers, and rings

It’s still flying if your shoes do half the work. Personal vectored-thrust rigs did not arrive as a luxury toy; they arrived as the solution to a boring logistics problem in cities that had run out of land to pour into roads. Two years later, half the poets were writing about height.

We gave up on rockets as fireworks and turned to the quiet magnificence of tethers and electromagnetic rails. Stuff that didn’t mind being squeezed went up on rails; the rest climbed cords that glittered at dusk. The first ring-that-was-not-a-ring looked like a bracelet someone had thrown around the world. If you walked long enough inside it, you came home across its inner sky. There was a wall on either side to hold the air, and the wall was alive.

10. Historians for everyone

The day archiving became cheap enough to memo your life without becoming a surveillance state, an old prediction came true. If even a tenth of the future cared about history, then each of us would have a biographer, maybe two, maybe a class of undergraduates arguing for a week about whether your habit of carrying two notebooks mattered more than your choice to switch careers at forty. They emailed sometimes. They weren’t ghouls. Their job wasn’t to forgive you or to condemn you. It was to put you in the story with the right verbs.

11. Time, and the end of time

We built backups that felt like insurance, not like immortality. You could ask to be recorded deeply enough that, if nothing else, your friends could talk to something of you after. Some chose to de-age and try childhood again with different parents and the same old soul. Some chose a long nap—wake me every thousand years, show me the map of what we learned, let me weep, let me go back to sleep.

The physics never bent. If the stars wound down in quadrillions of years, we made a place for last things. The Long Goodbye was a real program with a budget and cropped lawns. People practiced for dying as they had once practiced for a marathon: deliberately, with friends at the finish line.

12. How we got there from here

This is the part people always want to be promised: that there is a paved road from the present to the bright places. There isn’t; there are footpaths, and we mark them better now.

- First, cities. Cities are where norms and budgets meet. We started with things a city could do on its own without the permission of a capital: land value taxes with dividends; open-trial medicine alongside existing regulators; apprenticeship pipelines that let teenagers try the thing; chronicler desks inside public libraries; courts that piloted writing reasons; open procurement that rewarded actual performance.

- Second, software. Before anyone trusted an AI to yank a steering wheel, we built narrow civic tools that did one transparent thing: audited a police stop for bias; checked a bridge’s fatigue; read a labor contract aloud in your language and told you where the weasel words were. Those tools published their code and their misses. The Steward Net grew from that seed, subject first to city compacts and then to a series of global votes that ratified only what we had already watched work at small scales.

- Third, culture. You cannot threaten people into caring about the commons. But you can make it the default to be proud of paying for it. The dividends helped. So did the gossip: it became funny to brag about boycotting a cartel and cooler to brag about funding its competitor. It became a little shameful to have never served a year as a chronicler assistant or a court explainer or a matchmaker’s auditor. We stopped asking teenagers “what will you be?” and started asking “what commons will you help tend?”

- Fourth, red lines. We did not get global consensus on everything before we started. We got it on a handful of things, and then we saw that it held. No nonconsensual harm at scale; no creation of suffering minds for profit or curiosity; no unreviewed self-replicators; no new general intelligences built outside glass rooms with ten thousand eyes on the glass. If you wanted to argue that some of these were too careful, you could. There were whole cities for making the argument. If you wanted to ignore them, the glass rooms did not unlock for you.

13. A day

A therapist meets a client in a room with no cameras. Her accreditation is a stack of videos of her listening and of people who were helped signing their names. A gardener kneels and nips a stem; the plant’s cells at the cut go quiet because she taught them to when she registered their genome for tending. An engineer in orbit leans against the wind on the inside of a wall that is holding in a sky. A quiet city resident waters a bean; they have chosen not to work this month, and nobody asks them for a story. A historian walks a ninth-grader past a statue of a bus that braked on the first morning and asks them what they think a commons is. A pair of friends run in shoes that will lift them if they jump hard enough and laugh when they find that they want to try again.

The world is not perfect. It is specific. It is not impossible. It is made of things we can already do and the discipline to stop doing a few things that were killing us. It is a place where you can choose your adventure and the cliff will either catch you, because you asked it to, or it will not, because you asked it not to, and in either case the reason why will be written down where anyone can read it.

We learned that a good civilization is not a single answer. It is the compact that keeps many answers from eating each other. And then, with the scaffolding up and the red lines bright, we finally had time to unfold.
