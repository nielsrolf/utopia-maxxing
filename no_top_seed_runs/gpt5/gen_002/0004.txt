The Neighborly Abundance

On summer evenings, the block fills up before the cicadas warm their chorus. The children from Number 12 roll a ramp into the street and take turns with scooters; the elders from the corner house bring chairs and oranges; two neighbors who met at last month’s match salon practice a Bach duet on the stoop. A woman in her second century limbers up before a pick-up game, laughing when the ten-year-olds insist on calling her “rookie.” A few people wear slender headbands; if you looked with the right light, you’d see their shared dreamspace sketched faintly above them like a soap bubble, the story they’re crafting together—tonight’s is a mystery set in a Marseille bistro—available to any passerby who asks to join.

Everyone on this block is okay. No one is hungry. Nobody is cold, unless they chose an alpine holiday. The man with the violin has a brain that should have failed him a long time ago; instead, a necklace of living cells hums quietly beneath his skin, whispering corrective messages into his nervous system. The woman who limbered up beat a vicious cancer eighty years back and has not hosted a malign cell since. One of the kids used to crumble under certain kinds of social stress; their care team—and they would tell you this themselves with pride—helped them practice until the hard parts were manageable. Tonight they are darting between groups, asking who wants to be the detective and who wants to be the thief.

We built this, and it is good. It is not perfect; people still quarrel and make mistakes. But the conditions are engineered so that almost every stumble lands on soft ground, and everyone has a clear path back to firm footing. The way it works is concrete. The path from here to there is not a miracle. It is a sequence of choices we could make.

How it works

The first layer is the abundance stack—energy, food, shelter, networks—provisioned as a public utility and managed as a commons.

- Energy: We solved cheap, clean power by overbuilding what already worked—solar, wind, geothermal—and firming it with next-generation fission and deep storage. Every region added at least one dependable baseload source and a dozen storage types. The last coal plant became a museum. Abundance meant we stopped rationing imagination.

- Food: Factory-farm meat ended without a fight because better options were cheaper and tastier. Precision fermentation and cellular agriculture made proteins and fats at the cost of beans. Vertical greenhouses slid into city blocks like extra spines, growing salad, herbs, and strawberries as a shared service. Farmers kept farming, but the cruel arithmetic of animals in cages vanished. The animals themselves—elephants, wolves, ants—saw less human footprint and more habitat corridors. Where wild suffering was obvious and solvable, we intervened gently: contraceptives for deer to prevent mass starvation winters; vaccines laid in bait for foxes.

- Shelter: Housing stopped being an investment asset and became a utility. Regions spun up public builders that stamped out dignified, beautiful modular homes at scale, then let neighborhoods customize. Zoning flipped from “protect value” to “guarantee access to a great life.” Blocks kept their character by choice—gardens, stoops, courtyards—not by exclusion.

- Networks: Bandwidth was declared a civil right. Latency fell beneath perception, and secure identity became universal. The network’s spine carries not just entertainment but governance and care.

From this base, we built the living layer: health, time, connection, and meaning.

Health and the end of involuntary death

Biology surrendered to patient intelligence, not brute force. We learned to read and write the body with kindness.

- Clinics of renewal: Each city hosts open clinics where care is free in fact, not just in slogan. They use continuous monitors—a bracelet for the circulatory system, a patch for glucose, a subdermal bead for chronic inflammation—to spot trouble before it hurts. When they find it, they program it away: precisely targeted antivirals, senescent-cell clearance, immune retraining, organoids grown on demand from a tiny sample. Pain still exists—stubbing a toe still makes you hop—but the long drowning in agony ended. We keep an emergency gear of pain for those who want honest feedback from their bodies; it never spirals beyond what someone consents to.

- Brain care: Neural prosthetics used to be science fiction headgear; now they look like jewelry. They do not control minds. They stabilize attention, quiet panic at the source, and help memory find what it’s looking for without swallowing your past whole. Therapy became what it should have been: a set of practiced skills, guided by compassionate experts—many human, many synthetic—who have sworn a fiduciary oath to your flourishing. A thousand approaches are tuned to your nervous system like a luthier to a cello. The floor rose for everyone: panic becomes manageable, depression shorter and less dark, intrusive thoughts less sticky. People still have hard days; the days stop being hopeless.

- Death as a decision: The law changed words first. We stopped calling it “natural causes” when a person’s body stopped because of age alone; we called it a failure we would one day prevent. Then, step by step, we did. People are free to end their lives, but compulsion and emptiness are no longer the usual reasons. Those who choose the long path design centuries like chapters: a practice of medicine that becomes a millennium-long art of teaching; a fifty-year expedition through coral dreamscapes; a hundred seasons tending a mountainside.

- Guardrails against regret: For changes that might permanently alter one’s mind—deep mood polishing, memory thinning, radical personality updates—we adopted a right to reversibility. Big choices require cool-down periods and practice modes. No one can be edited while they are compromised or coerced. Your future self must consent on the other side of rehearsal.

Universal companions and the right kind of AI

Everyone gets a companion by default: an AI that lives in a small, beautiful box in your home and on a shard in your pocket. It is not your boss and not your content pusher. It is your advocate, trained only on data you grant it under a “personal model vault” that never leaves your control. It reads your calendar and heart rate with the same ethic as a doctor: to help you, never to sell you.

This companion does the drudgery modern life used to impose—paperwork, appointments, searching for services—and a better thing besides: it hums along with you in crisis and in joy. Should you wake at three in the morning with dread, it will sit with you and breathe. If you want, it will also point you to a human who knows this terrain and has a free hour today. You can turn it off. Your neighborhood is required to support you even if you do.

Above the level of individual companions, there are larger systems. We learned the hard way to wrap them in law and oversight.

- Fiduciary AI: Every AI that touches a human life is bound by fiduciary duty. If it matches you on a date, it optimizes for your long-term well-being, not engagement. If it suggests a job, it explains tradeoffs in plain language. If it advises a city on flood control, it must open its reasoning and data for audit. Fiduciary violation is a crime like embezzlement.

- Compute accountability: The world keeps a ledger, maintained by independent monitors, of large computing runs. Any training above a threshold registers for safety checks, like a chemical plant must pass inspection. We did not outlaw curiosity; we banned building monsters by accident. Democratic bodies can halt a training run that looks reckless. Whistleblowers are paid and protected.

- Red-team leagues: We made sport of breaking our own systems. Every significant model faces red-team seasons where professionals and citizens try to make it cheat. Successes pay prizes and set fixes in motion. It is fun. It catches things when they are small.

Loneliness ended without abolishing privacy

We stopped treating friendship and romance as side quests you’re supposed to figure out alone. We built infrastructure for belonging the way prior centuries built railroads.

- Hearths: Every neighborhood has a hearth—a real, physical place that anchors a web of care. In some cities they are libraries with kitchens; in others, gardens with workshops. Hearths host welcome circles for newcomers and longitudinal circles that meet for years. A hearth’s staff are professionals of human glue: they know who lost a spouse, who loves board games, who wants a hiking buddy tomorrow morning. None of this information lives in a corporate ad stack. It lives in the hearth’s encrypted memory and in the minds of humans you can look in the eye.

- Circlecraft: Once a week, the district runs a thousand little salons: poetry nights, soldering dens, dance classes, Dungeons & Dragons campaigns, small business co-ops, cycling clubs that actually wait for you on the hills. Invitations are not manipulative. If the system thinks you might enjoy the speculative-fiction potluck, you can see exactly why: you bookmarked a certain author; you lingered at last week’s talk on time travel; these five people you might like will be there. You click yes or no; nothing nags.

- Matchmaking with agency: Romantic matching learned restraint. Algorithms propose possibilities, then step back. Each suggestion comes with a manifest: what values you share, where you differ, what red flags to test by talking about them, two or three good first-date ideas. Triads and other constellations are normal for those who want them. Some people never match because they’re happier single; the system respects that. It never tricks you into a choice. It never hides your history from you or from those you choose to date.

- Universal counsel: Anyone can request a therapist and be seen the same day by someone good. There are as many therapy styles as there are nervous systems; part of the species-level investment in AI excellence went specifically to counseling. Counselors also form a web with your friends, your mentor, your soccer coach, your meditation app. With your consent, the web communicates so you do not fall through holes.

Work, status, and meaning

We did not abolish work. We unbound it from fear and narrowed its shadow.

- The foundations: At birth, everyone receives an unconditional dividend funded by the productivity of public AIs, public lands, and automated industry. The dividend covers good housing, staple food, network, health, transit, and a studio membership at your hearth. It is not a pittance. It is enough that “doing something I hate for money” stopped being a plan.

- Missions instead of jobs: People contribute through missions—bounded projects tied to outcomes rather than hours. Some are civic: redesign the square for flood resilience, catalog the fungi of the county. Some are scientific: culture a new crop for saline soils, probe a conjecture in topology. Some are in care: accompany end-of-life journeys, run a summer for new parents. Missions accrue to your ledger of pride, not a miserable résumé. You can build a life by stringing together missions that belong to the story you want to tell.

- Guilds and studios: Crafts came back big. Fabrication studios will cut, carve, and print almost anything; if you design a chair others love, the pattern spreads and a micro-royalty arrives. Writers form houses; labs form guilds. Apprenticeship is honored. People who want to be ferociously excellent can be. We structured prestige into seasons: you can be the MVP of your guild this fall and a learner next spring without shame.

- Many games: Status sought the sun and burned. We made many little suns instead. Recognition is local and plural—it comes from your choir, your esports league, your reef restoration crew. No single leaderboard rules your self-worth. This was culture work more than policy: we learned to point admiration at skill, kindness, rigor, humor, not at money—because money no longer told an interesting story.

Governance without exhaustion

We gave ourselves voice without demanding full-time politics.

- Proof of personhood: You have a civic identity that is truly you, not your phone number or your face. It was built to resist fraud and protect privacy. With it, you vote in neighborhood assemblies, district councils, global referenda that decide things like ocean treaties and model governance. No one can buy your identity. No one can sell it.

- Citizens’ assemblies at scale: Complex questions move first through citizens’ assemblies—a random sample of people briefed by independent experts, then advised by models whose code is public. Their proposals go to the broader public. Your companion can walk you through the arguments at your own level, showing what values are in play and what tradeoffs are real.

- Quadratic public goods: We funded shared projects—parks, open-source software, microschools—by letting many small voices outweigh a few big ones. You contribute five credits, I contribute seven, a hundred neighbors contribute one each; the fund multiplies according to how many people care. Suddenly, niches bloom that never had a patron.

- Right to analog and to exit: Slow zones exist; you can live without headbands and companions in a valley that prizes hand tools and silence. Communities write charters and traditions. If yours slides into oppression, leaving is easy and supported: other communities must, by law and custom, offer welcome to exiters, and the foundations follow you.

Virtual is real, with consent

We built shared dreamspaces that feel like lucid dreams you can walk through with friends. They are not the default life for most people; for some, they are a cherished medium.

- Dreamspaces: A dreamspace feels like this: you and three friends decide to spend the weekend in a coral reef at dusk. The system coaxes your senses; your body stays in a safe room at the hearth; you swim through catenary archways of light. You can import your grandmother’s sourdough recipe and bake on a raft. You can also set rules: no jump scares, no ads, no mind hooks. A consent overlay hovers like a HUD, telling you exactly what the space can and cannot do to your mind.

- Meeting of minds: We made high-bandwidth communication safe by designing it like a well-run kitchen: clear stations, good knives, nothing that slices fingers without eye contact and a “ready?” Teams can temporarily link parts of their thinking for shared tasks—an orchestra’s timing, a lab’s intuition—then go back to being themselves. Mergers of identity exist, but they’re rare, ritualized, and reversible for a time. Most people find the sweetness of being a person amplified, not erased, by these tools.

Memory and time

We stopped losing our lives to forgetting.

- Lifelogs: Consent-first lifelogs record what you want: your kid’s first jokes, your hikes’ smells, the way your lover’s eyes widened that afternoon you finally said yes. It’s not a surveillance reel. You can delete. You can share. You can leave messages for your future self that unlock like wildflowers at mile 23.

- Seasons of life: Cultures revived rites of passage that mean something. At forty, you might take a sabbatical-season to go apprentice on a boat, or study the night sky from a place that sees it full. At ninety, you might take vows of teaching. At one hundred and eighty, you might throw a party called The Rechoosing.

The path from here

This world is not a wish list; it is a staircase.

- This decade: We put legal teeth into data rights. We banned dark patterns and mandated fiduciary duty for digital intermediaries. Cities piloted hearths by converting libraries and underused schools into care-and-craft centers, then funded them based on how many friendships formed, not foot traffic. Insurance paid for continuous monitoring and prevention because it saved money. We funded a crash program in alternative proteins and subsidized them as we once did staple crops. We wrote the first compute accountability laws, requiring registration and safety audits of very large training runs. Citizen assemblies ran in dozens of places, first on small questions, then on zoning, then on climate adaptation.

- The 2030s: Precision therapies for cancer became routine; a few forms of dementia blinked out. Organ scarcity ended with bioprinting. The grid hit overcapacity and power prices turned negative on sunny afternoons; we built storage with an artist’s eye and a recycler’s ethic. Social work got a glamour upgrade: we paid and celebrated connectors. Smart therapy models passed hard trials and rolled out alongside humans, never replacing them wholesale. Foundation dividends started in a handful of cities funded by automation taxes and public equity in AI infrastructure. More nations signed a compute registry treaty; whistleblower rewards were tested and proved cheap relative to the disasters they averted.

- The 2040s: The foundation dividend spread regionally. Pets lived longer, partly because their people did. People stopped buying factory meat because nobody wanted to. First-generation longevity protocols that mostly worked were refined by second-generation ones that worked astonishingly, and the ethical debate about indefinite life quieted from an abstract panic into a series of practical norms and rites. Schools transformed into studios and quests. Proof-of-personhood matured enough to run referenda without bot invasions. Cities competed—not for corporate headquarters, but for which could orchestrate flourishing best.

- The 2050s and beyond: Children began to assume as a baseline that their great-great-grandparents would dance at their graduations. A huge portion of adult time returned to care, craft, and science, not because robots took jobs, but because we remembered humans never wanted those jobs. Some groups left Earth for orbitals and asteroids; the rest rebuilt rivers, planted belts of forest that stitched continents. We kept the oceans in common forever.

Two design choices made everything else easier.

First, we declared that a person’s time is precious, and the systems we build must respect it. That meant defaults that worked without homework. A companion that does real tasks off-camera and rings you when there’s a meaningful choice. A government that explains itself in three minutes for those who need three minutes and in thirty hours for those who want thirty hours. Public spaces where children can roam without a spreadsheet of permissions.

Second, we took seriously the idea that psychological safety and dignity are as basic as food. We asked every new piece of technology to pass a consent test: can this harm a mind without that mind’s knowledge? If the answer was yes, we redesigned it. That slowed some things and made others better.

The world remains plural. In the coastal city you can be fully digital by day and an ecstatic dancer by night. In the mountain valley you can live slow and wide and carry water by hand. In the desert you can join a research city without private cars, where children learn to sail sand. The baseline is the same: you are healthy; you are fed; you have a place to sleep and a place to be known; you can leave; and if you wake at three in the morning, there will be someone, human or not, who will sit with you until your breath finds itself again.

We did not edit away all anguish; we rearranged the conditions so that anguish is no longer a sentence and never a business model. We did not end all competition; we put it into fields where the losers can laugh and try again. We did not tame the whole world; we left room for wilderness and for risk chosen on purpose, with a rope secured and a friend holding the other end.

On the block, the duet wraps to laughter, and the mystery in the bubble above the sidewalk has a twist so clean it makes the children gasp. Down the street, a hearth window glows; inside, three people are learning to fix bicycles; in the back, two more are quietly preparing a memorial for a neighbor who chose the long path’s end. The city hums, the reefs—both virtual and ocean—brighten, and humanity has given itself the one gift worth the trouble: the expectation, justified by the way we live, of pleasant surprises.
