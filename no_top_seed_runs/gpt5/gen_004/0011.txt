The Lantern Compact

The first time people noticed the lanterns was not on the news. They appeared on doors you already knew: a back room at the bus depot, a window in a church that had never closed, a desk tucked into the corner of a rural post office. Each carried the same small glyph—a little lamp with three rays—and a hand-painted sign that said what the lamp meant in that place.

By the end of the month, the lamp had meaning everywhere. It meant that the world outside your block was not rumor. It meant that, if you needed to go, a corridor would open that did not require permission from the person who was hurting you. It meant that the people who ran your city, your water district, your hospital, and your school kept their reasons in a place you could read and argued in public when they were wrong.

None of it happened because a single parliament woke up generous. It happened because we wrote down three obligations that institutions had to us, ratified a handful of red lines that our tools could enforce, and then got to work copying what worked.

Three obligations

- Safe exit: Any person can ask to leave a situation or community and will be moved, protected, and provisioned without having to prove they deserve it. Children have a direct channel that does not pass through the people who raise them.

- Shared burdens: Anything you do that spills into other lives—carbon, pathogens, noise, river poisons, spectrum hogging, harassment—must be counted across boundaries and paid for or stopped.

- Reasons in the light: If you act with public power, you publish your reasoning in language a teenager can read and say where appeal lives. If events force you to act faster than you can explain, you write the reasons the next day.

Five red lines

These are etched into law, contracts, and code.

- No unconsented harm at scale. You can experiment on yourself and your volunteers. You do not roll dice with strangers.

- No creation of minds engineered to suffer.

- No unbounded self-replication, physical or digital.

- High-risk experiments live in “glass rooms”: logged, air-gapped where relevant, and overseen by a thousand eyes that can stop the process.

- No locking knowledge about exits. You may hold your beliefs close; you may not hide maps.

That’s the scaffolding. On it we raised tools with names and faces so we could love them when they behaved and correct them when they did not.

Civic Companions

Before we let any code touch valves or budgets, we grew it like we would a junior colleague.

Every region sponsors a handful of Civic Companions—narrow, auditable systems trained on local laws, infrastructure models, and open minutes. A newborn Companion spends a year in a sandbox. It reads the city code, sits in on committees, interviews nurses and plow drivers, and watches intersections. It has no actuators and no access to money.

Three circles mentor and license it. A household circle invites it into everyday life—smells, grudges, jokes—and keeps it from becoming sterile. A trade circle—pipefitters, teachers, paramedics—teaches patience and stakes. A place circle—a watershed, a row of drought-prone farms, a neighborhood of high-rises—walks it through what can break and how slowly to turn the knob. Each circle has veto power.

At the end of training, if all three circles and a public board vote yes, a Companion takes an oath you can print on a postcard. It promises to never make changes that cannot be revoked, to require a human co-signature on anything that could hurt someone fast, to publish its reasons in plain words with links to the models beneath, and to admit uncertainty in public.

Every Companion has to appear once a year without precomputation. We call it the Humbling. They get asked questions by a room that does not announce the topic in advance. They are allowed to say, “I don’t know.” They are required to say, “I was wrong,” when they were. There is a bounty for anyone who catches a Companion hiding a reason. Companions who misbehave lose access and go into the Museum with an engraved description of the miss and the repair.

Lantern doors

Within a half hour’s walk of where you sleep, there is a door with a lantern above it. Inside is not magic; it is logistics with manners.

If you are an adult and you need to go, you say so, verbally or not, digitally or on paper. Within seventy-two hours a caseworker—one whose reputation you can inspect—sits with you to decide between speed and stealth. You get a travel voucher good on any carrier, a month of hosted housing in a welcome house in the place you choose or the place that fits what you asked for, health coverage that roams, and your records in a personal data safe you control. If you leave debts that you did not consent to or that would follow you abusively, the membrane cuts their reach; creditors who object have to present in a forum with reasons and appeals.

Children have their own lanterns. Once a month, in a building with a neutral crest that belongs to no creed or party, every child attends a World Hour taught by people whose contracts do not depend on local politics. They learn maps—of the region, of the ways to live, and of their own bodies. They meet an ombuds whose contact never appears in a parent’s inbox or a community’s network. At sixteen, any teenager can file to leave. If they do, they spend two weeks in a welcome house finishing the dull, vital lessons—bank accounts, safety planning, how to use the clinics—then go where they chose. Their old community is expected to grieve and is forbidden to punish.

Membranes with windows

Communities are built around vows. One borough might outlaw billboards, cap the sugar content of drinks sold near schools, and set a 9 p.m. freight curfew. Another town might hold morning prayer in council chambers and ban civic talk of the divine. A third may design streets for toddlers first. A fourth might celebrate burlesque as civic art. These choices live inside membranes.

Membranes are not cages. They have windows you cannot paper over. A thin neutral line—call it the Trail Map—runs through every network and physical space. It points to other ways to live, publishes basic science and civic manuals, and keeps exit information fresh. Communities can filter their infospheres for themselves—no one can shove ads through your walls by force—but the Trail Map passes.

When you cross a membrane, your phone rests on the region’s defaults. If you want to blast neon or stream porn in a quiet zone, you flip to your own data and tap through a reminder: you are choosing a different norm. People put up with much more when it is a choice and when they can move.

Boundary meters and the ledger

We stopped pretending that externalities were a theory. We counted them.

Cheap sensors and old-style contracts knit together a boundary layer wherever policies change. The layer measures what we know how to measure—CO2, NOx, pm2.5, nitrates, decibels after midnight, pathogen loads in wastewater, spectrum congestion—and tracks what we will not price—species loss, sacredness, silence—with quota tables set in charter. If a stadium keeps a county awake, the meter says so and triggers a payment or change. If a fabrication lab feeds three blocks and halves ambulance calls, a credit flows from neighbors whose habits increase those calls. If you want to run a coal unit beside a solar commune, you can pay the carbon fee for a season and then do the math.

The Civic Dividend

We acknowledged that raw places—land with pipes under it, mineral seams, port berths, street space, spectrum—get valuable because all of us exist. We switched to long leases with rent tied to the public value of exclusive use, no matter who holds the deed. Assessors use lidar, transaction records, and zoning entitlements to calculate site rents; improvements are tax-free. Bids refresh on a calendar; no forever claims.

The rent flows to a Civic Dividend. It is enough that no one is involuntarily cold, hungry, or unhoused; not so much that you can disappear if you do not choose to. Cities keep a share because cities add value. Quiet districts exist where you can live gently on your dividend and flip a switch when you want work. Busy districts exist where the dividend buys time to launch a shop or finish an apprenticeship.

Work, markets, and boring competence

We replaced most occupational licensing with competence proofs and coverage. If you want to cut hair, people can see your cuts and your outcome statistics. If you want to fit prosthetics, you log a thousand supervised hours with clear rubrics and carry an insurer who stands behind your work; the premium drops with your outcomes. Airlines and reactors still have licenses; brain surgery still demands ten thousand hours.

Starting a firm is a right not just in rhetoric. Procurement turns promise into pipeline; if nineteen neighbors commit to buy your sidewalk scrubber, the city becomes the twentieth. If a sector turns into a club—broadband, insulin, waste pickup—the public option stands up at cost, publishes its ledgers daily, and exits when competitors behave.

We stopped paying for monopolies on ideas. In their place lives a standard license: if you build on published work, you pay a small set percentage of the marginal value created. Independent auditors certify the models that calculate that value; they publish their misses and lose customers if they hide.

Justice with reasons, repair with teeth

Judges write reasons. Big cases get two panels who rule blind to the parties and publish their logic. If the panels disagree, a citizen jury chooses the reasoning they accept, citing sentences. Sloppy opinion writers get tutoring and sabbatical; the court’s explainer staff writes what happened in language a teenager can follow; datasets are open by default.

Most wrongs do not need prison. Repair courts bind restitution to measured harm. Apologies become floors swept, lies become public notes plus a season of work for those you wronged, grief becomes a schedule and a tray of food left weekly at a door. There are hard edges: you cannot talk your way out of continued harm. Sensors make probation precise instead of vengeful, and they are not allowed to follow you when the term ends.

Children and first stakes

Schools feel like workshops. Teens teach younger kids. Before anyone takes on a decade of debt for a degree, they do micro-apprenticeships: four weeks in a kitchen, six in a clinic filing charts and learning privacy, three in a lab turning turbidity into a story, a summer in carpentry, a fortnight apprenticed to a Companion to see how a model fails. “First job” means “first small stakes that matter.” Every child who lives away from their birth home has a Legal Stranger: a single adult whose only job is to check in without reporting to anyone else unless harm appears. The Stranger’s notes go to the Museum of Errors only if needed.

The Museum of Errors

We stopped hiding our institutional mistakes. We catalog them. Any Companion who misfires, any charter that wrote a vow that had a terrible downstream, any university that tried to pay kitchen staff in prestige—tells the story in a place you can find. Admission costs a story of your own told to someone you brought. The gift shop sells repair manuals and a booklet called How to Change Your Mind On Time.

Pleasure, gates, and hazard lanes

Pleasure is not wicked; it is powerful. We teach it like we teach fire. Streets and apps mark hazard lanes clearly—colors, sounds, times of day—where things bite if you walk in. Erotic materials live behind consent gates with non-blinking warnings. There is no industry that targets children with infinite novelty; that is a red line and Companions enforce it because we made it one.

On the other end lives abstinence with dignity. Boroughs with quiet nights, prayer bells, and restrictions on visual noise have the right to be left alone for as long as their members wish. You can go there to recover from your own appetite and no one will mock you at the boundary.

Other minds, other bodies

We finally funded the boring science of who can suffer and how much. When ethicists and biologists published that cephalopods likely experience a kind of silence worth protecting, fishing rules learned new shapes. When someone figured out how to turn down nociception at plant cut sites, gardeners put away an old shame. Morphology clinics exist—height, hormones, scales, quiet—for those who want them, but consent is checked months before when you are calm, not on the day when your nerves are made of glass. Matchmakers only get paid if their couples are still content a year and two years later. Sex teachers look like normal people and are required to.

Energy and the permissions we fixed

Abundance came from policy, not a miracle. Solar films clip onto roofs and parking canopies in hours; microgrids island neighborhoods during storms; high-temperature heat pumps and small reactors replace dirty heat where wires make sense; and deep geothermal pulls steady warmth from granite without quakes.

Permitting stopped being a maze. We wrote pattern codes based on proven designs. Build to pattern and the check takes sixty days. Innovate, and you can, inside sandboxes where failures do not cascade. We did not bless recklessness; we blessed copying what works fast. Public utilities invite private capital into district concessions that compete on uptime and resilience, with audits in public.

Forecasts with skin in the game

We admitted that debates rarely converge. We built oracles—formal prediction markets for policies. Will this bus lane reduce EMS response time? Will this cancer drug extend median survival six months? Traders post odds; experts explain why; impact bonds pay out if the promised numbers arrive. Budgets shift automatically toward policies with better forecasted yield. Politicians can override, but they sign a receipt; if the markets prove them wrong, a chunk of their discretionary fund goes to what they ignored.

Health and time

We resurrected primary care as a guild with status. Clinics prescribe ventilation and blue-light filters under a right to rest, sequence your microbiome with dieticians who care, and run pragmatic trials through a public genomic registry that is consented and guarded. Gene therapies for adults with single-gene diseases became routine. Exotic heritable edits remain rare and unpopular. Biostasis shed the old tone; it is opt-in, transparent, and its fund also pays for the care a thousand grandmothers need today.

No more plagues

We learned from funerals. Everywhere: HEPA and UV-C in public buildings; CO2 numbers beside the pastry case; wastewater sequencing posted with the weather. Community response teams—mostly people in their twenties doing two-year service terms—practice supply runs during quiet months and contact mapping without moral theater when it matters. Schools close and open according to thresholds you can bet on.

Lifelines and quiet magnificence

A network of guaranteed safe corridors crosses boundaries—trains and buses and roads where the lantern glyph buys passage and protection. When a storm bruises a coast, Lifelines move walls and food into the wind. When heat eats a city, they pour shade, water, and people who know how to listen.

Freight hums at night; the buses come when they said they would; curb space is priced dynamically and the money pays for the bus lane you were promised and got this time. Personal thrust rigs never replaced bikes. Cheap lift went to telescopes that catch methane leaks from orbit and to sky gardens that made a neighborhood love a lattice of vines.

The Constraint Service

We keep no army that conquers. We keep a Service that constrains. Its job is to stop coercion, not engineer souls. Most days they are logisticians who cut fiber loops so a propaganda firehose cannot drown a prayer, or they rebuild the two-lane road that feeds two towns that glare. On the few days they confront bulldozers hired to drown a marsh, they put a grey wing overhead to speak the Compact aloud, and Companions turn the ground to pudding long enough for operators to walk away. No one dies. Investors lose money. The charge casing and the pamphlet go to the Museum.

Reputation and attention without a panopticon

We keep a lattice of favors, histories, and invitations. It is not central. It is remembered by people and networks that care, and you can turn parts of it off. Your sleeve can say “I taught elders to use exoskeletons this winter” or “I built that seawall.” The lattice knows the difference between clever and kind. It has brakes: shame cools for a week before it posts; ombuds can erase an old stumble if you bent your back long enough; budgets exist to move people out from under rumors.

Earth, kept honest

If you ache for the old Earth, there is an honest path. A First Biome Trust locks in a finite number of permanent residence years for the oldest places. Bids are made in money, time, and care metrics; a rotating share is pilgrimage time anyone can hold. The Trust annoys everyone in roughly equal measure. That is, perversely, its job.

Data and identity

We built personal data safes you actually own. Your health records, your credentials, your purchase history, your location pings—sit in a vault behind keys you hold. When a Companion needs access to plan the sewer upgrade or your insurer wants to price your risk, they ask through a standard that lets you say yes, no, or “only this slice for this purpose for this long.” We banned data brokers who build dossiers you cannot see. We required that any model that governs your fate be explainable to you in the time it takes the bus to come.

How we got here

This can sound expensive, fussy, impossible to start. We started anyway, small.

- Refugee agencies, battered-spouse shelters, and sanctuary networks became the first lantern doors. Philanthropy, diaspora remittances, and a levy on engagement-driven ads funded them. We put the lamp over their doors and then wrote the right of exit into state constitutions and a treaty.

- Cities turned their digital twins into nurseries for Companions. We open-sourced training sets, wrote oaths into charters, and gave mentor circles legal teeth. A family can still say no to a valve.

- We built boundary meters out of cheap air-quality sensors and flood gauges. The first target was not a factory; it was a stadium whose roar broke sleep. The fix was modest. The habit stuck.

- One mid-sized city moved thirty percent of its property tax to land value, killed parking minimums downtown, used the windfall to run electric buses every eight minutes, and live-streamed the commute time savings until copycats sprouted. A decade later, statehouses increased site rents because it built homes without subsidies.

- Congress created a narrow Forecast Authority: standards for prediction tournaments, safe harbor for agencies that used them, and grants for cities that ran oracles. At first, only coastal technocrats cared. Then a drought-state governor priced water with oracles, farmers lived, and five ideologies made money together predicting the Colorado.

- A court case brought by parents whose child died in a sealed school with no exit forced the open future into law: monthly World Hours in every community, ombuds networks near every school, the right of teenagers to leave with a stipend and a guardian ad litem to check consent.

- Energy got permission to be copied. Pattern codes outcompeted bespoke fights. Neighbors objected less when projects broke ground three months after approval, not three years later when facts had changed.

- Legislatures certified charter districts. With a two-thirds vote and a bond for shared services, you could slice out of a county and write a charter. The first wave was boring—truck routes, rental caps. Then the interesting worlds bloomed: vegan counties with microbe protein pilot plants, child-friendly republics with stroller parking and right-to-part-time without career penalty, craftwork townships with tolerances that made machinists smile.

- We made it easy to serve. A Civic Year paid stipends for court explainers, matchmaker auditors, pipeline testers, and ventilation mechanics. People bragged about service the way their grandparents bragged about cars.

- We put the red lines to a vote, one at a time, twice on purpose. No suffering minds passed in a landslide; again a year later after debate. No unbounded self-replication was hot, then normal. No large-scale harm without consent took cartoons and patient translation.

- We taught teenagers to read cravings and contracts. We did not seize their songs; we showed how media bends mood and what to do when bent.

- We kept score for institutions, not souls. Courts have dashboards; Companions publish misses; companies display ledgers a seventh-grader can parse. Liars still lie. The air they move through is thinner.

A day inside the Compact

Morning. In a borough that keeps a long sabbath, the bakeries give away end pieces on Friday. Phones default to quiet. The Trail Map still hums. The lantern door is staffed. If you need a splint on Saturday, you get one. If you want a noodle, you wait.

Midday. A sixteen-year-old who has loved their aunt and cannot stay writes a message to an ombuds on a channel their household does not see. Two weeks later they stand on a platform with one bag and a book, ride to a harbor town, eat soup that tastes like old and new, and apprentice with a Companion named Reed because they tell a good story about a storm drain and a fish.

Afternoon. A man who chose to live quiet this month waters beans, flips a neighbor’s bookshelf level because the bubble going still soothes him, and doesn’t have to justify breathing. His dividend covers rent in a small, green building with thick walls and quiet vents; when he wants, he flips his work switch and picks up three days supervising upholstery robots who move like cats.

Dusk. A Companion stands in a plaza and gives its Humbling. People ask why three lanes became two with trees. It quotes crash statistics and cortisol curves and opens a model that a teenager can poke. A street vendor hands over a skewer and an angry letter about a stall rating that dropped a notch. Both end up in the record. A forecaster on the local station shrugs about a 40% chance of a heat emergency next week; six cooling centers open on a schedule the markets already predicted.

Night. A cargo tram slides up a humming guideway. Somewhere the Constraint Service cuts fiber to slow a deluge of coercive rumor. Somewhere a judge finishes reasons and posts two opinions; a citizen jury will choose which logic to adopt in the morning. Somewhere a child falls asleep in a bunk that squeaks; the lantern’s glow leaks a thin line under the door.

What we kept and what we put down

We stopped insisting that there is one way to be good that a wise state must enforce. We also stopped pretending that freedom means never telling anyone what to do. We made a small wager instead: people do best among people who share their vows when the doors are easy to find, the winds are kept honest, and reasons are written down.

We put down two tools dressed as gifts. We let go of the idea that shame must be instant and permanent. We let go of “innovation” as a license to roll dice with strangers. In place of the first we put slow shame with exits and ombuds whose job is to restore. In place of the second we put glass rooms, pattern codes, and a museum that pays people to remember.

There are problems still. The sea comes in faster than our seawalls. We argue about what to commemorate. Comedy becomes hard and then easy again. We worry the World Hour could one day smuggle dogma. So we built watchdogs for our watchdogs and markets to bet on their honesty.

Most mornings, though, people wake in neighborhoods that make sense to them. They walk streets that respect their time and their lungs. They plan weekends that reflect who they are. The institutions with lanterns on their doors feel like something we could love because they show their work and change their minds in time. The point of the Compact is not utopia. It is a promise to try many ways to be human without letting them devour one another. It is a chair well made: it holds weight without creaking and leaves your hands free.
