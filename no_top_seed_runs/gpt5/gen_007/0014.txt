The Loom Accord

Ask people what changed first and most, and they point to the teal lanterns.

One hangs within a short walk of almost anywhere: above a sturdy door, open late, with someone inside who knows your name and puts water on the stove when the wind comes off the river. There are sockets, a quiet table, and a person whose only job is to help you leave well or stay better. We call these places Lantern Houses. They are the human face of a promise we wrote in plain speech and then wove into pipes, budgets, and software: no one stays trapped because the door is expensive, and power has to show its thinking before it moves.

The promise is short enough to memorize and hard enough to hold a town up. We call it the Loom Accord.

- Open doors, safe landings. You can step out of a job, school, household, or district without permission; you can show up somewhere that promised to catch you. Shelter and basic care are a floor, not a favor. Every child is quietly and regularly taught how to use an exit.

- Commons rent, shared dividends. Land, spectrum, orbits, and high-end compute are leased, not owned without end. Their rents split into a universal dividend and public goods we voted to buy. If your work helps beyond its walls, it is paid; if it harms beyond its walls, it pays.

- Reasons before rules. Agencies and companies publish a plain Action Receipt before acting: what they expect, how they’ll back up, where to appeal. Big calls require two independent rationales; a citizen panel picks which logic proceeds.

- Compacts with windows. Communities post their vows—quiet nights, big parades, no billboards—and enforce them inward. They do not blast their signals over other walls. Every compact keeps windows: an information backbone, a little blue book of rights, and the address of the Lantern Houses.

- Count the spill, guard the sacred. A public Spillbook tallies measurable help and harm across compacts and invoices openly. Some lines are not for sale: extinctions, burial grounds, midnight lakes.

- Reversible-first progress. Deep changes start with pilots, time delays, and Keepers paid to be your floor. No self-replicators without hard stops. No sentient pain on purpose.

- Customs for attention. Outbound messages can shout; inbound streams arrive at filters citizens control. The Quiet Right protects dinner and sleep. Public feeds slow by default; emergencies cut through plainly.

- Soft enforcement, firm edges. When someone tries to impose across compacts, a standing corps applies logistics and lawful friction long enough for people to walk away. No one dies. Investors eat the loss. The apologies and case files go to the House of Errata.

Those eight lines do not make a single way to live. They make room for many ways to touch without grinding each other down. The world took that shape: hearthlands where daily life is kind and legible; workshop reaches where big and strange are allowed by consent; and between them a customs estuary that lets ideas travel without letting tides flatten towns.

Neighborhood compacts

A compact is a posted agreement you opt into—how to live together and what to fund. Joining takes a tap and thirty days to leave, longer if you took a subsidy that runs to the season’s end. A village, a block, an apartment stack can carry a compact. Your windows always remain: the neutral backbone, emergency calls, rights, Lantern House addresses.

A riverside city might have:

- A Slow Ways compact that guarantees you can cross any arterial without your heart jumping. Outdoor ads are banned. Noise maps are public. Crosswalks speak with light, not beeps. Storefronts push out pianos and chess sets when dusk cools the stones.

- A Night Forge compact where workshops hum till midnight. Noise budgets are posted. A giveback norm holds: if your tool rests on an open component, you send 15% of the marginal value to the inventor’s fund. The Attribution Board’s “Who Paid” board is public; lying on it is social suicide.

- A Green Sabbath where one day a week commerce sleeps, screens dim, and the default is neighborly visiting and a telescope in the lane. The Quiet Right is strong there: feeds run at letter pace, except for real alarms.

Compacts are not tribes. People move as their lives change. New parents like the Nine-Minute Parks compact—every home within a nine-minute walk of a small playground and a clinic; part-time roles with real promotion tracks. Later they try a Violin Quarter. In time they take a flat in a Garden Quiet compact where the loudest night is a book fair and a brass band at solstice.

Lantern Houses and safe landings

The Lantern promise is plain: if you ask for a Wayleave, the answer is yes. Within seventy-two hours you land in a Welcome Home that agreed to hold you, with a three-month bed, a stipend pegged to local prices, and a device whose blue mode floats you through paywalls and ads on the neutral net. An ombud takes a chair and listens. Do you want to go? Do you want to stay better? What would help?

Kids learn the triplet by eight:

If your thoughts feel tangled, the Lantern helps you comb them.
If your words won’t come, the Lantern speaks with you.
If your feet are stuck, the road begins inside.

Once a month every child attends a World Session. There is no catechism. There is a checklist: how to reach an ombud in three taps or thirty minutes; what compacts live nearby and what vows cost; what the dividend is; what consent means and where to go if it’s broken; how to leave and how to come home. On the eve of their sixteenth birthday, an Outside Aunt or Uncle—an adult bound by confidentiality and nothing else—meets them in private. If a teenager chooses to step out, they spend two weeks in a Welcome House to sprint the life admin: bank, tenancy, consent law, medical file, credit freezes. Parents weep in a room where someone understands both love and doors.

Exit doesn’t erase roots. Benefits walk with you. Endowments follow children the day they land. Debts you didn’t consent to are cut at the compact boundary; a creditor can argue in a court that demands sentences a teenager can understand.

Counting the spill

We count what crosses the line. The Spillbook is a civic utility that ingests sensors and contracts and produces case files that stand up in court. If your fans send decibels into five blocks of sleepers after midnight, last month’s invoice arrives with an audit trail you can follow. You can change or you can pay. If your kitchen knocks 40% off ambulance calls around it, your ledger fills with money from the accounts whose choices made those calls more likely.

Some things we refuse to price. Extinctions are hard stops. Sacred quiet at a midnight lake, a burial ground’s integrity—compacts can declare and defend these. The ledger shows where we stand against those lines and who is learning to tread softer.

Records, foresight, and plain requests

Governance learned to show its work. Recorders do the old work of reporters plus the honest work of bookkeepers. When a bus-only lane promises faster trips, Recorders hire people with clipboards and publish numbers in prose a high-schooler can follow. The best-known Recorders are those who change their minds in public and show why. Their budgets are slices of commons rent with multi-year protection and receipts you can drill to the line item.

On top sit Foresight Pools: policy tournaments where anyone can post claims and odds within limits that keep whales out. Registered experts write clear theses. Noisy bettors have caps. Agencies earn safe harbor if they do what Foresight says will work; if they overrule and lose, the haircut hits their discretionary pot, not the clinic or park. Budgets tie a slice of money to measured targets: cut child asthma 30% in five years; lift bus speeds to 15 miles an hour. Competing designs win implementation shares proportional to predicted lift; if they hit, they renew; if they miss, they sunset, and the reasons go to the House of Errata.

Floating over all this is a simple piece of civic software called Askline. Anyone can post a Request to a relevant actor: please swap male-only street trees; pollen counts will drop; here’s the model; bond posted. Please move this curb two meters; ten kids will cross safely next week. Please mentor this kid; their math completion odds will jump. You can ignore any Ask. If you accept, you publish a short note. Recorders keep a league of Askers with high hit rates. We honor them quietly and give them a bigger nudge pot next season.

Work, learning, and the craft of being with each other

We cut credentials where they were fig leaves and kept them where they kept fingers attached. Twelve-year-olds shadow cooks and coders in two-week blocks. To cut hair you show five clean cuts and pass a hygiene module. To wire a house you apprentice and pass a test run by external examiners who don’t sell the course. Hazardous trades kept exams. New crafts earned them.

Matching people to work became a profession. The Placement Guild is paid for fit: if you’re still in a role and content six months and three years later, your placer gets paid; fake outcomes cost them their license. A hot job market—a vacancy ratio above one—is a civic target we manage like water pressure. When slack creeps in, compacts issue work orders for public tasks at wages that force private employers to compete.

We teach relationships rather than guessing at them. Schools run Friendship Studios where kids practice listening, apologizing, and setting boundaries. Adults have Relationship Clinics with therapists and coaches who make practice normal and diagnosis rare. Intimacy tech obeys a food-safety rule: consent must be legible, logged, and revocable. No device can override it.

Block Halls—bright rooms with tools, soundproof booths, a piano someone plays badly on Thursdays—anchor most blocks and hamlets. Each has a Steward whose craft is hospitality. A slice of the land dividend funds weekly open-table dinners. You show because not showing leaves a small, friendly debt you feel. Around these halls, Neighbor Rings formed—twenty to thirty households who sign to be kin. A child acquires ten aunts by default. An adult who texts the code word gets soup at the door that night.

Bodies, minds, and memory

We didn’t outlaw altering bodies and minds; we made it decent. Caps are temporary mods you can toggle that make you steadier with toddlers, calmer under pressure, sharper with numbers. They don’t habituate; exits are gentle and come with debriefs that sand off the strangeness. Deeper changes—surgery, lenses, rewiring—require practice runs with shallow versions, a cooling-off period, and a Keeper: a human or human/AI team whose work is to sit across from you as an older, kinder version of yourself. Keepers can’t say no; they can say “sleep on it in a different compact and ask me tomorrow.” Forking—running versions of yourself virtually—is allowed behind signed doors with etiquette: you can’t push a memory into someone who didn’t choose it; you can hash-link forks for later fusion; courts have templates for custody and consent across forks.

We wrote grief into policy. Paid leave covers funerals and slow sorrows—a miscarriage, a fork’s farewell, a reef’s bleaching. Clinics keep a Civil Registry that holds your will, health directives, and the people allowed to say stop if you forget.

Tools that made abundance boring

Energy stopped being a fight. High-voltage direct-current trunks run like nerves from deserts and offshore wind to cities and farms. Walk-away-safe microreactors—small and standardized—are licensed like aircraft: design families certified centrally, site approvals in weeks with public dashboards that show who signed what. Roofs shine with photovoltaics that outlast their warranties. Storage is boring: iron–air in municipal vaults, salt caverns for seasons. Prices fell below the cost of arguing.

Water followed. Coastal desalination runs on excess power; inland utilities hunt leaks with listening fibers and drone mappers. Wastewater is a mine: phosphorus and lithium pulled back into loops. Precision greenhouses feed cities without squeezing farmers dry: hedgerows for pollinators, mesh against pests, drip roots glittering like beads under sunlamps fed by midnight wind.

We made air a utility. Codes require MERV-13 or better in buildings, whisper-quiet HEPA on buses and trains, and upper-room far-UVC in schools. A public dashboard shows your block’s air in a color you understand. When a virus tugs at the map, you see it like weather and respond like weather: crack windows, nudge settings, postpone the indoor concert. The last global pandemic is a campfire story teens tell to feel brave.

Housing stopped being punishment. Cities flipped to performance codes: hit targets for noise, light, seismic, energy, and access; everything else is yours. Permits live in a portal with clocks and fines if an agency stalls. Modular factories in Surabaya, Tacoma, Accra, and Timișoara turn out panels with craft care and line reliability. A land value tax—debited automatically from parcel registries—made hoarding land a hobby you keep for sentiment, not profit. Streets slow, trees shade, rain runs into bioswales alive with dragonflies. The ugliest empty lot in your town is now a courtyard with a swing.

Proof tags anchor the supply chains that matter: bridges, cranes, centrifuges, sequencers, reactors. Every critical part ships with a cryptographic pedigree and a visible mesh you can check with a loupe. If it can’t pass inspection by a rural co-op, it doesn’t belong near a biosphere.

Compute and biology grew up. Big compute is rented like land. Runs above a threshold carry passports: signed, rate-limited tags that say who called them and what outputs may touch. General persuasion or code-generation at scale stays sequestered with public oversight. Home and hospital automations carry the Offswitch Right: a child could learn the test that proves you can pause, audit, and resume without ghosts in the state. DNA printers above a line need licenses; orders get screened through cloud tools with no memory. Community labs flourish because guardrails make fear less necessary.

The customs estuary

Messages have customs. Workshop reaches can shout; towns reserve the right to hear softly. Outbound streams route through Signal Quays at system edges. There, short-lived boards—a mix of trained citizens and clear-eyed auditors—inspect, summarize, and release. You can subscribe to the raw feed; your right to receive is yours. What customs blocks is mass mind hijack—memetic dazzlers, sky-billboards, unstoppable ads. Curiosity remains elective. The Quiet Right is law at home; spoiler tags flag big discoveries for slow lanes.

Workshop reach, far drift

Across the estuary we keep customs for matter, too. The Workshop Reach—orbital yards, spun valleys, cislunar refineries—runs on the Accord with extra lines to match the drop-offs. Self-replicators boot with Boundaryware: tiny, verified code that enforces budgets and genealogy. A swarm sculpting an asteroid counts its grandchildren; if proliferation is too fast, grandparents refuse parts and power. Mirrors near Mercury can’t throw clouds over Oaxaca. Keys burn at factory and rotate through random orbital notaries. Anyone doing business past low Earth orbit signs, or gets no fuel, no repairs, no insurance. Physics and contracts do more than police ever could.

Choices in the far drift cost resources that belong to many. We built a way to steer without pretending to agree on the highest good. Every adult receives nontransferable Cause Tokens to delegate to public trusts. Some chase exoplanets at coastline resolution. Some run joy gardens for sentient beings who pass agreed tests and consent to be there. Some keep monasteries for goals not easy to say but bounded by the Accord. Trusts publish plans; markets predict outcomes; audits check results. Budgets tilt as conviction flows. Forks are normal and dignified: with explicit consent, a copy of you wakes in a spun valley to grow radiation shields while you coach soccer. Integration clinics guide the ache of merging or the grace of staying separate forever.

Law with reasons; security without empire

Police don’t prosecute; prosecutors don’t run detention; judges don’t count cases closed. Each is measured by what matters—convictions that hold, corroborated facts, less harm without recidivism. Judges publish plain-language reasons with citations. Big cases get two panels in parallel; a jury chooses which logic to adopt. Court explainers rewrite decisions for teenagers. Most of what old codes criminalized—selling food without a permit, sleeping on a bench—moved into compacts or clinics. Repair Panels convert remorse into mops, money, and months; they forgive when a back has bent enough.

The Buffer Corps is conscience with logistics. Most days they fix roads and pumps that knit towns who haven’t spoken in years. Some days they put a quiet drone over a marsh and read the Accord aloud to a crew hired to bulldoze it. If a platform starts shouting into compacts that voted for quiet, they sever a fiber loop long enough to clear the air. No one dies. Investors eat the loss. The casing and the remorse go to the House of Errata.

Between polities and between Earth and orbit runs the Between-Court with tight powers: quarantine a node, cut a fuel line, freeze dividends to repeat cheaters. Militaries exist for rescue and contemptible failures, not conquest. Most enforcement is ostracism with teeth: ports close, insurers walk, neighbors refuse to sell you bolts.

Daily life on the Loom

Morning in a Slow Ways quarter. The baker gives away end pieces on Fridays. A CO2 meter on the door glows pale green. The Block Hall’s bulletin lists tonight’s dinner: curry, three gluten-free, a river talk with a jar holding dragonfly larvae. The Lantern House is staffed. If you need a splint on Saturday, you’ll get one. If you want a package, you wait till sundown and the bike train’s quiet hours.

Midday at a workshop by a canal. Mira, a joiner, loves old wood and new stairs. A thirteen-year-old on her second micro-apprenticeship is cleared for the table saw and oil finish but not the planer. An Ask pings Mira’s sleeve: a neighborhood audit shows three intersections feel unsafe for small kids; would she join a one-hour walk Tuesday? The issuer posted a bond; Mira accepts. She remembers when moving a curb took five years and a thousand pages.

Across town, a teenager named Danilo uses a Wayleave to step out of an uncle’s house. The uncle loves him and can’t stand who he is. An ombud in a brown coat makes tea for the uncle, talks through grief, and asks if he’ll volunteer one Saturday a month at the Welcome House. Danilo lands with the Communal Uncles in a harbor quarter, eats soup that tastes like both worlds, and apprentices with a Steward named Piper who tells a fine story about a fish and a floodgate.

Afternoon in a quiet county. Eliza wheels a barrow to the sheepfold. Phones sleep in a wooden box by the General Store door, next to postcards and a mending sign-up. The clinic has a diagnostic model, but it lives behind a counter and a nurse with gentle eyes makes sure it speaks softly. When Eliza’s knees grind, a doctor offers three paths: surgery, a walking stick and slow hills, or tea and pain control that is effective and kind. She decides. No algorithm argues.

Evening in orbit. Ji-won steps from a lab into the inside of a cylinder. Orchards stripe the hillside; the sky is painted but good. He logs the last run of a microgravity alloy that will make transformers back home run cooler. On his bunk waits a letter: “I planted the plum. You will miss the first bloom. I will not.” He writes back, “Send a photo when it slides toward purple.” Later he serves on a Signal Quay board, watching a summary from a collective mind singing a proof into a resonator. He stamps it to the public archive and finishes his coffee.

Night on a reef trust’s edge. Neema moves along a rope under a fat moon, swapping lines bacteria don’t love, checking shrimp-listeners. Her pay is good, her work is beautiful, and parrotfish sleep as if people never could reach them.

How we got here

From far away it looks impossible. Up close it was sequence and shameless copying.

- Clean air first. Insurers shaved premiums for schools with good filters and maintenance plans. Zoning boards tied occupancy to annual air numbers. It felt like the smoking turn: loud, then normal.

- Land rent and dividend pilots. A scatter of midsize cities shifted a slice of property tax toward land value and auctioned leases for spectrum. Half the proceeds paid a small dividend. The rest bought buses every eight minutes, parks within nine, and pipes. Property values rose because the city got better; speculation cooled because sitting on empties cost money. Managers bragged until others copied.

- Receipts before rules. Regions set aside one to two percent of commons rent to fund independent Recorders with multi-year terms and public audits. Agencies had to publish Action Receipts before moving budgets or code. The first time a mayor overruled a Foresight call, missed the promise, and had to cut her discretionary pot instead of a clinic, a habit was born.

- Compacts inside cities. Volunteer compacts formed. The first Slow Ways negotiated quiet hours with delivery fleets. The first Green Sabbath dimmed phones and the world didn’t end. People moved between compacts; compacts borrowed each other’s best bits.

- Lanterns, repurposed. Refugee networks, domestic violence shelters, and sanctuary churches became the first Lantern Houses. Philanthropy lit the first lanterns. A levy on the noisiest ad farms helped keep them on. The crest spread faster than planned because parents like sleeping.

- Pattern codes and Foresight. Statehouses wrote laws recognizing pattern codes for solar films, heat pumps, and microgrids. Agencies got safe harbor for using Foresight Pools. A drought state learned that water markets priced by forecasts kept farms alive. Traders from five ideologies got rich together predicting a river’s flow. Trust rose, one click at a time.

- Giveback instead of hoards. Regulators carved a path for giveback royalties and short copyrights—fourteen years, one-and-done. The Attribution Board’s dashboard settled into daily life. Open components exploded. Lawsuits cooled.

- Unbundled justice and Repair Panels. One county paid arrestors by later convictions upheld, interrogators by corroborated facts, custodians by harm reduction without recidivism. Repair Panels reattached restitution to harm you can measure. Judges were paid for reasoning that held three years later. Teenagers saw the chain and laughed it took so long.

- Child endowments, portable care. Funding flipped from districts to children. Per-child endowments followed kids wherever they slept. A family moving away wasn’t theft; the town had been paid to teach the years that happened there. Welcome Houses became civic pride.

- Compute passports and proof tags. Foundries shipped attested hardware for bridges and biolabs; clouds added run passports by default. Bad actors hid worse. Good actors slept better.

- Customs and the Workshop Reach. A near-disaster wrote the frontier compact into law: a mirror swarm tried to goose power prices by shadowing a competitor’s farm. Neighbors reduced it to dust; insurers walked. Legislatures codified what everyone had just done: geofences, generational limits, signal customs, shunning by contract.

- The Between-Court treaty. A handful of provinces and one embarrassed former empire signed a minimal treaty: no coercion across compacts; mutual friction for those who tried; Lantern Houses for any child who asked. When teenagers who had left came home more often, not less, more places signed.

- Loneliness as infrastructure. Block Halls and Neighbor Rings were funded like waterworks. Crime fell by accident. Dinners got better bread.

Two quiet shifts mattered as much as laws. One was the Quiet Right, which began as a village quirk and spread to a human rhythm: public feeds slow to letter pace during meals and at night unless an alarm rings true. People slept again. The other was an attention tax on unsolicited pings that fed the Commons unless you whitelisted the sender. Phones learned to be quiet. Night remembered how.

Why this world is good

It is good because energy, air, shelter, and time—the ordinary miracles—are boring, abundant, and fair. It is good because you can be short on money but rich in time and no one can starve you as punishment. It is good because the adventurous have runways and the cautious have porches, and neither can conscript the other. It is good because we find pride together in things you can touch: we halved asthma in five years; we moved a curb and three kids cross safely; we reopened storm drains and watched water go where water wants. It is good because when someone cheats, consequences are contained and dull. No empires rise. No ads stain the sky.

It is specific. You can hold the proof tag that shows a replicator can’t make a centrifuge. You can read the ledger that logs laser orders. You can follow a court’s chain from summary to raw data. You can turn the mute switch on your wall. You can borrow a drill and a recipe from the room at your block’s end.

It is plausible. Nearly every piece existed as a draft. Solar fell in price. Safe reactors sat in binders; we fixed the process that kept them cold. HEPA and UVC already saved lungs. Wastewater already spotted outbreaks. Land value taxes worked where courage lived. Forecast tournaments already beat pundits. Cryptographic attestation already secured chains. Rockets already landed on tailfire. None required magic. All required fidelity to boring miracles.

Better, it spreads by paying. Insurers like lower risk. Utilities like fewer blackouts. Cities like faster permits. Parents like ten aunts. Orbital yards like not being sued into dust for a neighbor’s nightmare. Everyone likes dinners that start on time with good bread.

We still argue—about how much brain-to-brain bandwidth is wise at a table, whether to send Cause Tokens toward sentient joy or physical discovery this quarter, whether the Commons should fund a second open meal night or more rehearsal rooms. We keep hard lines bright and few: the right to quiet isn’t the right to batter; the right to mystery isn’t the right to enslave. Hateful compacts that deny others’ humanity don’t pass consent. When people cross lines, enforcement is firm and boring: dividends freeze; ports close; nodes go dim. Most change. A few leave. There is no empire to join.

On a spring morning near you, a baker unlocks her shop. The ovens pull power quietly; the dough remembers her hands. Across town, a port worker taps a screen and the customs feed downshifts for the night. In orbit, a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth and goes to sleep. On a ridge, a teenager ties a perfect knot and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens more than she speaks. The day runs on rails we built on purpose. The horizon stays wide. The tide comes, the tide goes, and no wave knocks the houses down.
