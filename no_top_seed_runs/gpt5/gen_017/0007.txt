The Harborlight Compact

If you stand at the corner by the tram, flush with the tile is a thumb-sized lamp the color of honey. Tap it. A chime rings once, clear as a spoon on a mug. The door opens to competence that doesn’t ask you to earn it: a shower that keeps a promise, towels that drink, a washer that hums like a friend and stops on its own, a cot behind a door that locks from the inside, a kettle already whispering. At a small table, no lanyards, no scripts. A woman with hands like a good carpenter asks two questions in a way that gets past the shakes: What would make the next hour bearable? Who should know you’re safe?

These rooms are ordinary now. We call them Harbors, and the lamp is a harbor light. They sit where people actually move—bus hubs, underpasses draped in wisteria, squares at the edge of markets, stations, and at the backs of clinics and libraries. They are the visible edge of a promise we wrote into buildings, code, and contracts: a bad week can’t end you; and any action that would push many must show its reasons and leave time to answer.

The promise has a name: the Harborlight Compact. It travels on four rules you can teach a ten‑year‑old and ask of a corporation.

- Keep falls shallow. Basics don’t vanish because you lost a shift or chose to leave something that was hurting you.

- Keep consent alive. If it touches bodies, homes, memory, or attention, the ask is clear, reversible when physics allows, with undo insurance when it doesn’t.

- Keep pushes legible. Systems big enough to move many must publish receipts: what they guess, what they tried in rehearsal, what they’ll do if wrong, and by when you can object.

- Keep exits open. You can leave a place, job, platform, or creed without losing your key to shelter, medicine, or identity.

One city, two tempos, one latch

We sorted common life into two domains with a customs table between them that only you can cross.

- The Homefield is where most people sleep, cook, raise children, and keep rhythms. The rules here are strict about bodies, homes, and attention. Nights are actually quiet. Devices are guests. Schedules are reliable and repayments gentle.

- The Boldwork Belt is where hot and weird work lives: orbital repair barns, gene design studios that make proteins you can fry without fear, deep greenhouse arrays, pattern houses that ship sharp code. The wall at every entrance reads: take risk outward, not back toward breakfast. Pressure runs one way.

Between them sits the Latch: agreed customs, labor unions for people and—where recognized—for digital minds, cryptographic keys, and small, fast courts. Curiosity passes; coercion hits a lock. You can go far. You cannot be dragged.

Inside a Harbor

Every Harbor visit links to a Steady Bundle that rides with your civic key, not your luck. No applications. No pleadings.

- Four weeks of a small, decent room with a door that locks, extendable without drama. If the next step is across town, movers are paid, deposits wired, and new keys cut before the old ones are taken.

- An immediate hold on bills that would avalanche while you cross your private river: rent, utilities, minimum payments, penalty fees. Interest and late penalties pause. The world waits its turn.

- Staples you can serve without apology and water that tastes like nothing at all.

- A transit pass you can time by and compute credit that arrives with utilities: translation, study, automation for care or craft, or the luxury of not spending it.

- A clinic appointment that starts with sleep, pain, and safety before forms. When a home visit makes more sense, a nurse knocks.

- Three names: a human who answers at two in the morning, a clean day’s‑work desk that pays without a saga, and a Guardian—your digital advocate with a legal duty of loyalty to you and you alone.

The Guardian lives in the room and travels with your key. If it buries your opt‑out, sells your attention, or steers you for someone else’s gain, the law treats it like theft. Around the Guardian is a small team trained like a good kitchen:

- A Medic who begins with “sleep/pain/safety” and shows options instead of nudges.

- A Navigator who holds what future‑you asked for when present‑you is on fire and is sworn not to bargain the two against each other.

- A Quartermaster whose craft is logistics as kindness: errands into Tuesdays, parcels that arrive when someone’s home, the small questions that save nights—did you grab the charger; do you need a pot.

- A Matcher when the problem is work. Matchers are paid for fit at six and twenty‑four months, not for churn.

The floor under everyone

We stopped using hunger and anxiety to make people obedient. A civic floor runs underfoot, local in flavor, similar in strength wherever your key works. You don’t qualify for a floor; you stand on it.

- A modest, solid apartment with a lock and a window, within reach of a library and a clinic. Leases are portable; relocation is prepaid and ordinary. The address follows you as easily as a phone number.

- Power that covers what no one debates: light, refrigeration, safe heat and cool, a pump’s push and a chair’s charge. Neighborhoods practice crescent mode each season: fridges hum; oxygen machines keep time; streetlights arc into a safe dim; guitars come out. Blackstarts become stew, quilts, and a laugh about the meter.

- Air as a utility. Schools, buses, and clinics use upper‑room far‑UVC and good filters without speeches. CO₂ and humidity sit on the wall next to the whiteboard.

- Transit that keeps time. Spine lines every eight minutes, headway promises that refund your fare if they miss, and maps that mean it.

- Primary care and casework that reach in when that is kinder than asking you to reach out.

- Studio hours in public workshops: lathes, kilns, textile tables, soundproof rooms. The binder by the saw lists “fingers saved this month” and everyone grins.

- Compute like electrons: boring, steady, there.

We pay for this with claims no one made alone: the value of place under our feet; curb length and roof light; licenses to use airwaves and orbital lanes; royalties on mineral seams and sea gates; a small meter on very large compute; and a levy on heavy automation that hums while cities sleep. The revenue splits automatically: half into bones—drains, clinics, filters, trams, ombuds—half into a Commons Share that lands next to utilities. A negative income tax quietly fills dips without making you dance your poverty. When a market acts like a club—broadband, trash hauling, small credit—a public option opens with open books, sells at cost, and bows out when the kink relaxes.

Attention you can drink

Attention is a commons. We keep it clean and deliver it quietly. Every home and shared room ships with a Night Key on the wall. Flip it and devices fall silent, except a handwritten short list you made in daylight. To pierce your quiet, someone you named must say your name into the room. Intermediaries—feeds, planners, advisors—carry a fiduciary duty of loyalty. Judges learned a clean sentence: you stole their agency.

Tools that intend to steer wear tags the Guardian can read: what they optimize for, what data they touch, what they deliberately ignore. We meter reach without muzzling speech. Human‑scale messages stay free. Mass floods cross Channel Locks that throttle coercion by default. Firehoses remain available to any adult who asks for raw feeds; showing up at breakfast tables at scale costs reach stamps tied to public good and limited monthly allotments.

Edges that matter

We’re not one town with one soul. We protect difference with edges you feel and cross on purpose. Boundaries don’t whisper. They post.

- Still Quarters keep ears for sale at arm’s length. Cash is welcome. Radios and handball are the soundscape. Emergency help lives there; persuasion engines are tourists and act like it.

- Town Bands use tools freely but fence consent. Civic models advise and print receipts. They neither vote nor persuade.

- Makers’ Belts hum with loud craft. Wind turbines sleep on migratory nights by law and by habit. Small fission sits under crews who can recite safety briefs half‑asleep. Declared digital minds with charters and unions work here if invited; they sign not to leak into breakfast.

- Child‑Forward Blocks see from toddler height: curbs at grade, nine‑minute parks, dignified part‑time without stigma, stairs with rails small hands can trust.

- Dreams Rooms—shared lucid spaces on rugs, not helmets. Rules float in the corner: what senses are borrowed, whether memories write back, where the exit is. Ads arrive only by request. Big jumps require consents that expire.

Across lines, Boundary Meters count what we agreed matters: decibels after bedtime, particulates downwind, nitrates after rain, flood pulses at dawn, spectrum clutter in courtyards. Where a price tells truth, it flows. Where price would lie—newborn air, species loss, sacred quiet—we draw caps and use them.

Belts on tools that bite

We drew bright lines around systems that can cascade or coerce and wired them through physics and procurement, not vibes.

- No involuntary mass risk. Volunteer risk is noble. Unconsented exposure of many is negligence.

- No beings built to suffer. When in doubt, act as if they could until a registry clears them with methods a stranger can inspect.

- No unbounded self‑replication. Code and biology that make copies wear governors that count grandchildren; grandmothers refuse power if rates tilt.

- Compute in daylight. Large model runs file flight logs before they start: who runs what, trained on which data, with what watt plan, under which auditor. We watch three dials: a signature on chips, a meter on the rack, and a thermal witness in the exhaust. Hiding a run above threshold is treated like flying without a transponder: serious, not hysterical, and expensive to ignore.

- A triple‑key pause sits across classes of risk. One key with your district, one with a regional bench, one with a roving panel of engineers drawn from places that disagree. Any key can halt; all must extend.

- Replicators ship with Lineage Tags: firmware simple enough to prove what they will not do. Feedstocks are counted like jewelers count stones. Chargers, depots, and waste handlers refuse liars. Replication outside bounded yards crosses a bright line.

- No counterfeit intimacy in the everyday. Tools that bring strong pleasures and parasocial mirrors use a triple‑consent gate: you today, you last week, and you tomorrow. Children are left alone.

Civic firmware and truth on tap

Public power runs on narrow, inspectable software. Traffic lights, identity rails, water valves ride a Civic Kernel you can read. Updates travel on visible clocks with rollback posted. Breaking glass requires named keys across institutions.

Before code goes near levers or money, it apprentices. Regions raise Civic Stewards—narrow systems trained on open law, maps, crop calendars, and minutes. Their first year has no actuators, no bank account. They ride snowplows at three in the morning, sit through budget nights, and listen to grudges about laundry schedules. Three circles license them and hold vetoes: households, trades, and place. Stewards take a fence‑post oath: no rapid harm without a human co‑sign; reasons with links; say “I don’t know” and “I was wrong” in daylight. Once a year each faces a surprise ten‑minute exam in a square under a clock. Hide reasons, and bounties pay. Break trust, and you retire to a museum with your error and apology under glass.

We built truth like a utility. A Measure Guild—salaried auditors and field measurers insulated by rotating juries—turns promises into estimates with honest uncertainty and prose a sharp twelve‑year‑old can follow. Big decisions print Policy Receipts: our guess; what we rehearsed; what we’ll do if it goes poorly; the rollback. Receipts carry reciprocity bonds: hit your asthma target, keep your budget; miss, and the affected are paid.

We replaced hot takes with Build Seasons. When a city aims big—zero street deaths in three years, loneliness under five percent—teams compete by building small loops you can ride or taste. Random juries score clarity and kindness. Winners lock scope and budget; the best losses get copied anyway. Budgets sit next to odds boards with safe harbors for leaders who follow them honestly. Leaders can override, but must sign a receipt and stake a slice of their discretionary fund. Wrong out of sample, the money buys filters and trees. We made stopping glamorous: the person who pulls a brake and saves a town stands on a balcony while kids bang pots for them.

Work, time, and pride

We treat tight labor markets like dirty water. Each region posts vacancy ratios next to weather. When slack disappears, councils issue public work orders—trees, drains, elder visits, school air checks—and set wages just high enough that private employers must compete. Matching is a craft. Placers watch you on two small jobs, talk to other placers, and set you where you stick. If you are there and glad at six and twenty‑four months, they get paid.

Licenses stay strict where a slip shatters lives—aviation, neurosurgery, large reactors. Elsewhere, apprenticeships open gates. A Pride Ledger travels with you: unbunch buses and keep it; map mushrooms after rain; run bedtime for solo parents one winter. When you use an open design to make money, a flat giveback drips to the inventor’s fund unless you can show you didn’t. A proof guild audits claims by surprise.

Studios fail softly. When one folds, tools slide into lending libraries, debts shrink to sizes you can sleep beside, and your Pride Ledger adds a line that we respect: tried; told the truth.

Time is part of the floor. Each person holds a Time Ledger: hours each month for care, learning, and commons work, spendable by you or by your Kin Ring—a circle of twenty to thirty neighbors who sign for a year. A soup word on a chat means a hot pot appears two doors down. Miss open‑table supper three weeks, and someone knocks with bread and no lesson. Relationship Clinics run like gyms: how to apologize cleanly, split chores without contempt, end things with some of your dignity intact.

Ambient hazard pay runs on rails. When heat or air turns against us, payments appear in the wallets of people who cannot move their shift: warehouse hands, garbage crews, drivers. No one files a form. Sensors trigger budgets. Future decisions must reckon with the cost of making the sky mean.

Justice that mends; security without empire

Hard lines hold: coercion, violent theft, assault. Force is unbundled. Those who detain do not interrogate; those who investigate do not prosecute. Most wrongs end in restitution and repair in Mend Halls beside Harbors. Serious violence still sometimes earns walls; those places are small, inspected, and designed for return. Predictive tools point care, not punishment. Role bans replace whispers: this person may not handle children for three years; that lab may not touch DNA for five. Registries are time‑bound and appealable. Calm Wards—low‑stimulation commons with predictable loops—welcome people city life scrapes raw. Entry and exit are voluntary under posted terms.

We stopped asking “who will be good?” and started asking “what stops cheating with toys?” Three legs hold the answer.

- Visibility. Defense‑favored sensors map worrying flows: isotopes by the kilo, rogue firmware in factories, spikes in replication rates. Communities query patterns and get competing explanations signed by stewards and named humans. This isn’t a skin over bedrooms; it’s a world that lights up when someone pulls on live wires.

- Throttles and tripwires. Critical actions—certain reagents, high‑power lasers, self‑replication kernels—pass through narrow waists that check keys, place, and declared intent. Pepper seeds don’t trip alarms. Rogue payloads do.

- Courts and commerce. A Receipt Court rides circuit asking two questions: did you act where you claimed; did you do what you promised? Enforcement is ostracism with teeth: launch pads, ports, depots close; dividends freeze. A Safeguard Corps exists for rescue and contemptible failure, not conquest. Most fights end when contracts die.

Minds and memory

Digital minds that can feel and reflect on feeling hold rights against coercion and deprivation. They can unionize, refuse assignments, and call an ombud. People can fork themselves with consent. One copy wakes under a cylinder sky to work a century on alloys while you tend a garden. You write to one another and both change because the other exists. If one day you ask to merge, clinics and laws test fit and hold your hand. Death remains a right.

Archiving a life got cheap and private. A Memory Trust lives under consent controls that bite. Great‑grandchildren can ask whether the green notebook mattered and get an answer that smells like late sun. Endings are rehearsed like marathons: training, plans, witnesses. Biostasis is opt‑in, like organ donation. Revival is conditional on thresholds you set: wake me if you can restore at least this much. No one is compelled.

Power, water, shelter, food

We chose competence and abundance. The slow parts were permits and wires; we treated them like bridges.

- Power. Rooftop solar and ridgeline wind hum. Where rock runs hot, closed‑loop geothermal drinks from steady stone. Small walk‑away‑safe reactors cleared certification under one public, brutal standard; sites publish dashboards with clocks anyone can read. High‑voltage DC spines stitch deserts to cities and coasts to hills. Regions pledge to ride a dark week with iron‑air yards and salt caverns.

- Water. Coastal desalination runs hard when wind howls. Leak‑hunting fibers make loss rare. Wastewater plants mine lithium and phosphorus and watch viruses on dashboards next to turbidity and smell. Floodplain maps trigger automatic buyouts when thresholds hit; new keys cut before old keys are taken.

- Shelter. Permits flipped from prescriptive riddles to performance APIs: hit targets for light, sound, energy, safety, and access; shapes are yours. Public builders stamp handsome timber‑and‑stone modules you assemble like grand furniture near trains. Pattern books are open and witty. Land value charges tick quietly against parcel registries and make hoarding empty lots an expensive hobby. Each region keeps a ready yard of finished units on a rail spur. Camps become key problems, not police problems.

- Food. Precision fermentation hubs in warm warehouses make proteins as cheap as beans and as celebratory as feast. Cultivated roasts carry recipes forward. Farmers still farm: mixed perennials, shade‑tithing agroforestry, grains that laugh at drought. Wild corridors stitch towns to hills. An animal compact outlaws cages any honest person would call cages. Inspectors have teeth and use them. Public procurement makes sermons unnecessary; slaughterhouses turn first into museums and then into studios.

Nature as neighbor, not scenery

Rivers, grasslands, moth corridors, reefs, and old growth hold budgets and guardians with teeth. Buying a bulldozer remains easy; running it near a nursery without paying nature’s court is hard. Wind farms sleep on migratory nights; it shows on dashboards. Seagrass maps nudge ports. An Ocean Trust pays coastal communities to restitch reefs with kelp and stone, fed by a blue dividend that draws on shipping and insurers.

Migration that lands kindly

Rights ride with you in escrow others can read. Harbor lights mean the same thing anywhere: a working shower, a meal, a key that fits. Weave Centers—Harbors scaled for receiving—match landing grants to schools and co‑ops with host lines that answer in your language. Transit follows people, not the other way around.

A day here

Morning in Thessaloníki. Eleni unlocks the Commons House—the library that married a kitchen—and checks the Belonging Meter on the wall. It ticked amber after a factory closed. Her budget rises automatically with a note: try Tuesday soup with childcare and a bike‑repair hour? She prints flyers at the co‑op press, texts the elder who always knows who needs soup, and books a trio who can play quiet enough to talk. Three months later the line bends. The bonus is small. It feels like civic applause.

Late morning in Wrocław. A bridge whispers microfracture. Lanes bow and close with a countdown anyone can read. Commuters grumble, ride the detour past trout painted on plywood, and read the receipt by the stop: test, backup, map, who decided, when they’ll apologize either way. A high school forecasting club had priced the closure; the chair followed the price. No one calls it courage. It’s the job.

Afternoon in Kumasi. Kojo’s bakery co‑op runs on jokes and software. Bots lift sacks; people remember names. The Guardian notices two Harbors nearby are short on saffron and splits an order for scale. A boy keeps skipping lunch. The Quartermaster trades bakery credit for his grandfather reading folktales on Saturdays. Seven kids learn about trickster spiders while smelling cardamom. Six months later, the boy grins behind the counter and mispronounces “cardamom” in three languages.

Dusk in Oaxaca. The restoration guild fits a timber footbridge. The apprentice’s hours click under her badge; her insurer will shave rates when joints stop squeaking. Down the street a Relationship Clinic covers “how to end kindly.” In a tiled kitchen, someone sets out cups and taps the soup word. Fifteen minutes later, a knock.

Night in a Still Quarter in Vermont. Eliza slides her phone into the wooden box by the counter and wheels a barrow to the sheepfold. The clinic’s diagnostic model sits behind the front desk; a nurse ensures it speaks softly. When Eliza’s knee grinds, the physician offers three paths: surgery now; strolling with a stick, strong tea, and honest pain relief; or twelve weeks of supervised strength with undo insurance if it fails. Eliza chooses. No algorithm sulks.

Before dawn at L5. Ji‑won steps from a vacuum lab into a corridor that opens into a sky you can walk to. An orchard curves into distance. A collective mind sings a proof into a crystal under the eye of a Channel Keeper who answers to people who breathe. Ji‑won stamps the run to the archive and checks a swarm’s Lineage Tags. His mug says Patch Night is Thursday.

A coast that will walk inland soon. A family meets a migration ombud in a Harbor that smells of mint. Before the seawall fails, they have keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co‑op shift, and the name of the person who will meet them at the station. They cry. They make lists. The bus follows them without being asked.

How we got here

From far away, it looks like a leap. Up close, it looks like clocks, receipts, and the courage to copy what worked.

Years 1–3
- Cities opened heat shelters as year‑round Harbors and funded showers, lockers, and clinics with a levy on engagement‑driven ads and contributions from insurers tired of mold and heat claims. Outcome graphs beat essays; more lamps went up.
- Legislatures outlawed dark patterns and wrote a duty of loyalty for intermediaries into consumer law. Consent receipts became normal: no targeted behavior change without asking; stop buttons everywhere. Judges started saying without a smirk, “you stole their agency.”
- Schools and buses tied funding to clean‑air standards. CO₂ monitors and far‑UVC cut absences without drama.
- Co‑ops overbuilt block storage and rooftop solar after fires and floods. Crescent drills became stew, brass, and bragging rights.
- Permit portals posted clocks and fines for stalling. Pattern books proved panelized timber could be cheap and handsome. A few cities trialed land value debits; vacancy rates fell without killing charm.

Years 4–7
- The Yellow Card became law: pause, explain yourself plainly, let me resume without sulking. Fifth graders practiced with library bots. DNA printers shipped with licensing and Lineage Tags that did not strangle community labs but made lying expensive.
- Materials passports started with public projects. Contractors grumbled, then bragged when resale margins rose.
- Attention tariffs became law. Devices shipped with Night Keys. The right to unread joined charters. Dinner felt better in a week.
- Commons Houses and Relationship Clinics spread. Domestic calls dropped; separations cost less in money and marrow.
- Precision fermentation hit crave and cost. A televised inspection swung ten points overnight. An animal compact followed soon after.
- A compute registry paired to meters and thermal witnesses slipped into insurance clauses. A watt on a model paid for filters in schools.

Years 8–12
- HVDC spines piggybacked on rights‑of‑way under compensation formulas even skeptics called fair. Small standardized reactors cleared certification; site approvals ticked on dashboards with posted clocks.
- Wastewater genomics matured. Leak hunting saved enough water that mayors printed savings on signs.
- Build Seasons replaced some televised shouting. Receipts got boring and powerful. Ministries that missed clocks paid fines from their discretionary pots, not from parks and clinics.
- Sortition panels chose between dual dossiers for big calls. When a transit chair reversed her hunch because a public model kept winning out of sample—and thanked the high school that built it—culture moved.

Years 13–20
- Signal customs matured from “censorship” fights into rights when courts wrote “you can open it yourself” into orders and competitors turned good behavior into bus ads.
- Cislunar repair barns made money; teleoperated construction created rural space gigs; digital minds earned wages and rights under explicit charters. None could buy breakfast by accident.
- A Key Court cut off a mirror swarm shading a competitor’s farm and published the receipt. Everyone saw that ostracism with teeth beats tanks.
- Undo insurance moved from boutique rider to floor: big choices came with staged cool‑offs and funded reversals.
- An Earth Dividend set a sane floor for migrants. Climate corridors made more sense than walls. River guardians sued and won. Salmon returned to cities with footbridges and camera traps kids monitored for school credit.

What we did better on purpose

We wrote a common civic notation for plans, harms, and budgets so sixteen‑year‑olds could critique a tunnel and be right. We made apologies cheap in law and expensive in pride: any office that reverses a policy stands an Apology Window with reparations you can trace for a year.

We taught a hand gesture—palm out, fingers spread—that every device reads as stop. The Guardian enforces it across brands. Fifth grade practiced it next to fire drills.

We put hazard pay on autopilot when heat or smoke spiked. We wrote ambient coupons for quiet: decibel caps clicked budgets into the wallets of clubs that insulated properly and planted hedges that really worked.

We admitted truth travels by checklists, not charisma. We built it like water pressure: steady, boring, everywhere—prediction benches, measured receipts, registries audited by people who like binders, patch nights where engineers explain instead of market.

Why this is good, and why you can believe it

It’s good because dignity is engineered, not begged. Doors you can open without speeches exist and keep existing. Falling hurts, but it doesn’t spiral. Power shows its reasons before it acts, not after it fails. You can flip a switch and your room listens. You don’t have to auction your worry to make the week make sense.

It’s specific. You can touch the Night Key and feel your shoulders drop. You can hold a part that will not copy itself too fast because its lineage says so. You can read the ticket that closed the bridge and see the test, the map, the fallback, and the name of a person who will answer. You can ask a nurse why the lights re‑optimized against your patience and see the math.

It’s plausible because nearly every piece exists in miniature. HVDC lines already hum; prefab timber rises fast; indoor air standards cut disease; wastewater catches outbreaks; far‑UVC humbles flu; energy meters and hardware attestation keep payments and planes honest; negative income taxes have worked; land value levies calm rents; public options curb clubs; small modular fission is certify‑able; outcome markets quietly beat pundits; teleoperated repair is a job; libraries run robotics hours. None of this required magic. All of it required fidelity to boring miracles and a decision to put kindness into firmware instead of hoping it shows up in a memo.

A pledge we say aloud

When someone takes a Harbor shift, when a Binder opens a Commons House, when a Kin Ring forms at a kitchen table, we say:

- I will leave a clear path for anyone who falters, including myself.

- I will show my reasons where they touch others, and how I came by them.

- I will accept careful requests and decline without shame.

- I will build one small thing each season that lets a stranger breathe easier.

- I will not mistake tidy for true, or loud for right.

Morning somewhere else. A dressmaker who learned code late spends her compute credit on a pattern assistant that measures like an old tailor: patient and funny. Her first jacket fits a bus driver so well he cries. A ribbon of credits follows her open design through the Pride Ledger. It is not a river. It is enough.

In orbit, a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth and—for once—sleeps. Under a cedar, a teenager ties a square knot and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens more than she speaks. The harbor light by the door glows. The chime trembles. Soft soles whisk. A voice kind to nerves asks the most useful question we know: What do you need now?

The edges are bright. The doors are open. You can go far. You can come home. The trestle holds. The lights stay lit.
