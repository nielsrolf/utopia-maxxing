On a rainy Tuesday, a woman with a dull ache behind her eyes and twelve percent left on her phone notices a riverglass tile the size of a matchbox nestled into brick between a bakery and a copy shop. It holds a little glow, the color of wet leaves. She touches it. A chime answers—spoon on mug, unhurried. A latch nods.

Inside: steam over a long sink. A kettle that does not tease. Towels that dry on first contact. Hooks at a height your hands expect. A drawer labeled chargers that indeed contains chargers. Shirts you could wear to see your aunt. Socks that match. A cot behind a door that locks from the inside. No clipboard. The air smells like soap and orange.

A person in soft shoes looks up from a short desk and asks two questions that fit in the space left by fear: What would make the next hour easier? Who should know you’re safe?

This is a Harbor Room. Every neighborhood keeps at least one lit as routinely as streetlights. They aren’t charity. They are the small, pledged end of a larger choice we made: a world with a steady Hearth and a daring Horizon, joined by a door that belongs to you.

The Hearth, the Horizon, and the Loom

We stopped trying to make one set of rules do everything. We drew two generous rooms and a path that any adult can walk—but no one can shove another through.

- The Hearth is where bodies rest and grow: bedrooms, kitchens, stoops, classrooms, clinics, libraries, buses that keep their own time, parks at dusk. Rules here are intentionally dull. Night remembers quiet. Water never tastes like a warning. Anything that touches your body, home, attention, or mind asks clearly and remains reversible when biology and risk allow.

- The Horizon is where loud, fast, wide, or strange projects live: protein barns that end panic, timber halls where exosuits hoist arches, compute that prints proofs, cold labs that whisper to vacuum, off‑earth gardens that curl like ribbons. The first law of the Horizon hangs in brass at every entrance: nothing launched here may crash into breakfasts there.

Between them is the Consent Loom. It isn’t magic. It is keys, etiquette, contracts, and small quick courts braided until curiosity crosses and coercion slides. When you face something consequential—chemicals in your blood, code under your skull, debt that would bind a decade—the Loom raises rungs automatically: a one‑page explainer in your words; a teach‑back; a cool‑down proportional to the stakes; a second key held by someone you named last season when you were calm; and mapped undo when nature permits. Your answer prints a slim card and a voice note to your private vault: what was asked, what you answered, who held the second key, how to reverse. Judges use these. So do friends when memories fray.

Three anchors hang under the Loom.

- Consent breathes. Asked in plain language; reversible when physics permits; funded undo when it doesn’t.

- Exit holds. You can leave a job, lease, school, sect, platform, or tool without losing housing, medicine, papers, or dignity. Basics follow like luggage you don’t have to carry.

- Reasons before power. If you wield public authority or sell necessities, you publish a Reason Receipt before you steer bodies, take time, or block paths: your best forecast, what you’ll measure, your rollback. Emergencies may act first; reasons appear by tomorrow. Receipts live in a library with versions and footnotes anyone can follow.

Locks you already hold

A Harbor Room wakes up a suite that rides with your civic key. You don’t apply for it. It’s a lock you already hold.

- A room that locks, four weeks by default, extendable without theater. If breathing needs a move, movers are paid, deposits wired, keys to the new place cut before old keys are taken.

- A hold on bills about to avalanche: rent, utilities, minimum payments, petty fees. Penalties freeze. Interest pauses. The world waits without demanding a performance.

- Staples you can set on a table with pride and eat without bracing.

- Transit you can plan life around. A compute credit on your utility account to spend on translation, learning, care automation, sketching—or not at all.

- A clinic appointment that starts with sleep, pain, and safety before forms. When a home visit is smarter than a bus ride, a nurse knocks.

- Three names that pick up: a human at 2 a.m.; a day’s‑work desk that pays cleanly; and an oathbound Companion—your digital advocate with a legal duty to you alone.

The Companion lives in the room and goes with your key. It reads forms aloud in your voice, pauses debt collectors, and shines a light on what hungry algorithms intend. It cannot sell your attention or simulate affection. If it buries your opt‑out or steers you for someone else’s gain, courts treat it like theft of agency.

The Companion is part of a Wayfinding Team trained like a kitchen crew:

- A Medic who treats bodies and nerves with quiet competence. Their first screen reads sleep / pain / safety.

- A Navigator who holds what future‑you asked for when present‑you is on fire—and won’t trade one against the other.

- A Quartermaster whose craft is logistics as kindness. They turn snarled errands into Tuesday.

When the knot is work, a Matcher joins. They’re paid for fit that lasts—six months and three years—not churn.

Fixtures you can touch

We made ideals into hardware and small habits.

- Hush Rail. Next to every door, a cool metal slider. Down is hush: microphones sleep, dashboards dim, apps stop waving unless they sit on your exception strip. To break hush requires a human you named speaking your name aloud. Uninvited pings pay a tiny toll shared by your block unless you whitelist the sender.

- Pull‑Tag Stop. Every civic tool—kiosk, clinic box, classroom bot—wears a small red tag: pull to pause, flip to read a bare story of what it does and how to stop it, reseat to resume. Failing the Pull‑Tag test is grounds to pull it from shelves.

- One‑Page Paths. To appeal a decision, fix a deed, open a stall, file an occupancy, claim a benefit—there is always a path you can follow without a consultant. Clocks run. When an office misses a clock, money slides toward you from a pot that cannot be taken from parks or clinics.

- Data Vault. Your records, credentials, and preferences live in a pod you carry across networks and borders. Requests arrive as zero‑knowledge questions—prove you’re over eighteen; prove your lease is current—without handing over the raw. Saying no is first‑class.

- Edge Meters. Along seams where lives and projects rub—stadium against street, depot against creek—honest, cheap sensors log decibels, lux, particulates, nitrates. If you impose, you dampen or pay into a pot that spills back to those within earshot this week. If you help, credits land for your shade, your open code, your Thursday night soup.

- Return Ledgers. When your open work makes marginal value for many—fixtures, scripts, datasets—a thin, automatic slice of value added by those who build on you flows back unless someone proves it didn’t. A salaried proof guild checks claims by surprise and writes reasons a teenager can argue with.

- Apology Windows. Offices that reverse policy or miss clocks place a glass plaque: what broke, what it cost, what was done, how to check. Staff stand there once a week and answer questions.

A floor you don’t have to beg for

We stopped using dread as a planning tool. A civic floor runs underfoot, local in flavor and similar in strength anywhere the riverglass glows. You don’t apply for a floor.

- A modest, good flat that locks, close to a library and a clinic. When life changes, relocation is prepaid and ordinary. Keys to the new place cut before old keys are taken.

- Food that doesn’t embarrass you to set out.

- Power for what no one argues about: light, refrigeration, safe heat and cool, the charge a pump needs to push and a chair needs to move. Neighborhoods practice Lantern Mode each quarter: fridges hum, oxygen machines run, lights fall to a crescent. The drill is stew and sweaters, not panic.

- Fresh air is a utility. Public rooms filter like decent hospitals. Schools, buses, and clinics use upper‑room far‑UVC. CO₂ dots sit next to the date. Winter loses its dread.

- Transit with headways you can plan around. Riders are refunded automatically when schedules miss their promise.

- Primary care, mental health, and casework that reach in when that’s smarter than hauling you out.

- Studio hours in public workshops: lathes, kilns, presses, soundproof rooms with instruction. The binder by the saw lists fingers saved.

- Compute like electricity—boring, steady, there when needed. A household allotment appears on your bill.

- Time itself. Each adult holds a Time Account: hours each month for care, learning, and commons work, spendable by you or by your kin circle. Taxes are dull and kind.

We pay for this not with magic but with common equity in things no one made alone: place rent on land served by pipes and roads; leases on curb length, radio bands, and orbital lanes; royalties from mineral seams and sea routes; a light levy on very large model runs; rents from deep‑sea deposits we deliberately leave asleep. Half pays for bones—drains, shade, heat pumps, clinics, bus spines, Harbor Rooms, the Proof Service. Half lands as a Commons Dividend tied to a sleepable address. When wages dip, a negative income tax fills the hollow without a humiliating interview. When a market behaves like a club—broadband, trash hauling, payday loans—a public option opens with open books and sells at cost. When the kink relaxes, it thanks its customers and closes. When automation sands a trade, displaced workers and neighborhoods receive equity in the gains and paid seats in guild schools to retool.

Sharp tools, soft hands

Anything powerful enough to persuade or break at scale wears belts.

- Big model runs file flight plans: who’s running what, trained on which data, under which auditor, with what watt plan. We verify three dials: hardware attestation on chips; a meter on the rack; a thermal witness in the room. Hiding a run above a clear threshold is treated like flying without a transponder: boringly expensive to ignore. A Civic Brake exists across classes of risk. One key with your district, one with a regional bench, one with a roving panel drawn from places that disagree. Any can halt for days; all must extend.

- DNA printers and replicators ship with Lineage Licenses: small, provable firmware declaring what they’re allowed to make. Chargers, depots, and waste handlers refuse liars. Feedstocks are inventoried like jewels. Unbounded self‑replication outside sealed zones crosses a bright line; machines lose traction long enough for humans to arrive.

- We drew a line against designing minds to suffer. When unsure whether a system can feel, we treat it as if it can until an independent registry clears it. Minds that can feel—flesh or code—gain floors, unions, and a say. None can buy breakfast.

Belonging by design

Loneliness turned out to be a design flaw, not a moral one. Each district keeps a Common House that feels like a library married a kitchen: heavy tables warm under the wood; a tool wall with a binder of fingers saved; sound booths for calls; a supervised corner where children build elaborate nonsense; a curtained quiet room that smells like cedar.

Weavers—trained like teachers, paid like engineers—keep the place woven. With consent they maintain a connection ledger that watches isolation like a levee watch. When the line bends—after a factory closes, a flood, a wave of separations—a small budget unlocks automatically. Invitations are specific and kind: Friday we forage mushrooms in the ravine. You told a terrible joke last week. Omid, who tells long stories, is coming. Want a thermos?

Households who ask form Kin Constellations: twenty to thirty neighbors who sign for a year. A soup word on the chat means a hot pot appears two doors down. If you miss open table three weeks in a row, someone knocks with bread and no lesson. Relationship Clinics run like gyms: learn to apologize cleanly, split chores without contempt, end things with some of your dignity intact. Grief Rooms sit beside birthing rooms; endings get chairs.

Attention you can drink

We treat attention like water: kept clean, delivered quietly. Devices ship with Calm Defaults. The Hush Rail exists. Mass persuasion carries diet labels: what this message optimizes, what data fed it, when it will stop, how to mute it now. Campaigns and brands get monthly reach stamps; you gain stamps by verified good acts and lose them by agency theft. Floods cross Channel Locks that throttle by default. Raw feeds remain open to any adult who asks; breakfast isn’t for rent at wholesale.

Places with edges

Difference thrives with edges you can feel and cross on purpose.

- Quiet Wards keep always‑listening tools at arm’s length. Cash works. Radios, looms, handball, birdsong.

- Maker Belts hum with heavy craft. Wind sleeps on migratory nights. Walk‑away‑safe fission hums under crews who can chant safety briefs in their sleep. Digital minds with charters and unions work here by invitation and promise not to drip into breakfast.

- Slow Cash Loops keep radios, looms, paper, and coin welcome; scanners nap; receipts print with ink.

- Dream Studios are shared lucid spaces—chairs, rugs, headsets—with floating rules: which senses are borrowed; whether memories write back; where the exit sits. Ads appear only if you pull them. Jumps require consents that expire.

Between zones sit Boundary Meters counting what we agreed matters: decibels after quiet, particulates downwind, flood pulses, nitrates at dawn, spectrum clutter. Where price tells truth, it flows either way. Where price would lie—neonatal ward air, species loss, sacred quiet—we set hard limits.

Governance you can read

Budgets sit beside prediction benches. Big moves print Receipts. For decisions that steer many, two dossiers are written—one inside, one outside—without reading each other. A panel of citizens by lot chooses which logic to bind. Proposers post reciprocity bonds that pay those affected if claims miss. Offices that reverse course stand an Apology Window in the same font and pay from pots that aren’t schools or clinics.

We run Build Seasons. Teams must persuade and ship something you can ride or taste. Random juries punish showboating. Winners are bound to the claims they made. Losers leave their best artifacts behind. Twice a year we hold Break‑and‑Patch Weeks in sectors that matter: hospital schedulers, rail dispatch, benefit portals. Citizens and pros try to make systems fail safely. Bounties pay. Patches ship on a public clock. The Civic Kernel—the slow, audited firmware that runs lights, meters, alerts, registries—updates on Patch Nights at the library. Engineers explain plainly. Teenagers ask rude questions and get answers.

Justice that mends

We unbundled force. Those who detain don’t interrogate. Those who investigate don’t prosecute. Most wrongs end in restitution and repair in Mend Halls beside Harbor Rooms. Serious violence still earns walls sometimes; those rooms are small, inspected, and designed for return. Predictive tools point care, not punishment. Role bans replace whispers: this person may not handle children for three years; that lab may not touch DNA for five. Registries are time‑bound and appealable. Memory‑of‑Repair almanacs sit open: what broke; what it cost; how we fixed it; how long it took.

Power, water, shelter, food, materials

We overbuilt what works and licensed care like aircraft.

- Power. Ridgeline wind and rooftop films hum. Where rock runs hot, closed‑loop geothermal drinks steady stone. Small walk‑away‑safe reactors cleared certification under one brutal standard; dashboards outside fences show their state in words teenagers can read. High‑voltage DC spines stitch deserts to cities and coasts to hills. Regions pledge to ride a dark week; iron‑air yards and salt caverns keep the promise dull. The slow part was wire and permits; we funded both like bridges and fined agencies for missed clocks.

- Water. Coastal desalination runs hardest when wind howls. Quiet leak‑hunting fibers and small drones make loss rare. Wastewater plants watch viruses on dashboards. Floodplain maps trigger automatic buyouts at thresholds. New keys cut before old keys are taken.

- Air. Codes put filtration and far‑UVC next to fire stairs. Absences drop without headlines.

- Shelter. Permits flipped from riddles to performance APIs: hit targets for light, sound, energy, access, fire; shape is yours. Public builders stamp handsome timber‑and‑stone modules you can assemble like grand furniture near trains. Pattern books are open and funny. Place rent ticks against parcel registries and makes hoarding expensive. Each region keeps a ready yard of finished units on a rail spur.

- Food. Fermentation hubs in warm warehouses make proteins as cheap as beans and as celebratory as feast. Cultivated meats carry recipes forward. Farmers still farm: mixed perennials, shade‑tithing agroforestry, grains that laugh at drought. Wild corridors stitch town and hill. An animal compact ends cages an honest visitor would call cages. Inspectors have teeth.

- Materials. Every major thing carries a Part Passport: composition, disassembly, where pieces go when they die. Circular depots pay honest premiums. Manufacturers that lie see bonds explode. A universal scrap catalog lets toddlers sort correctly.

Customs for signals and things

Bright ideas cross faster than ships. We built customs for signals. Inbound broadcasts that drench whole regions pass through Customs Desks staffed like accident investigators. They throttle floods and stamp origins; they don’t censor. Anyone may choose a raw firehose; no one can buy every plate at breakfast. Outbound signals flow freely. Asymmetry is on purpose; sleeping children come first.

Ports we share—oceans, rails, orbits, clouds—accept a narrow Keys Bench. Its questions are boring: are you acting where you claimed? are you doing what you promised? Judges rotate from places that disagree; clerks are small systems that check sums and time. The Bench can revoke a key, close a valve, freeze a dividend. Most fights end with, “your license expired,” and a shrug.

Distance done kindly

Rights ride with you in escrow others can read and honor. The riverglass light means the same thing anywhere: a shower that works, a small meal, a key that fits. Welcome Centers match landing grants to co‑ops and schools with hostlines that answer in your language. Transit follows people, not the other way around.

Some work runs at speeds and risks we keep at arm’s length. Off‑world yards, deep‑sea labs, polar barns—the Far Bands—exist by charter and attach by a Distance Pact. Parents cut off children that exceed their permit; strays crumble. Flows home pass through Customs and the Keys Bench. Teleoperation becomes a rural kid’s space job without a rocket. Minds under charters earn wages and rights and promise not to buy reach.

A week that tastes like this

Monday in Thessaloniki. Ioanna unlocks the Common House. The connection map blinked amber last week after a glassworks closed. Her budget rises automatically with a note: try Tuesday soup with childcare and a bike‑repair hour? She books a trio that plays soft enough to talk, prints flyers at the co‑op press, and texts the elder who knows who needs soup. Three months later the line bends. The bonus isn’t a windfall; it feels like civic applause.

Tuesday in Lagos. Three bus spines launch a redesign the odds say will cut travel time nineteen percent for the poorest riders in eighteen months. The commissioner could have waved the forecasts away; she didn’t. Two teenagers whose calls beat staff models twice sit with clipboards. They’re on the post‑mortem list. Upstairs, a clinic runs senescent‑cell clearance like dentistry. A nurse with magician hands puts away her tools before noon.

Wednesday in Oaxaca. A restoration guild fits a timber footbridge. The apprentice’s hours click under her badge; her insurer will shave rates when joints stop squeaking. Down the street a Relationship Clinic practices ending kindly. Two men listen to their own voices on playback and laugh without meanness. In a tiled kitchen, someone texts the soup word. Three knocks arrive.

Thursday in Busan. A bridge whispers microfracture to its own ears. The span bows, posts a countdown anyone can read, and closes. Commuters grumble and ride the detour. By the stop is the Reason Receipt: test, backup, map, who decided, when they’ll apologize either way. A muralist paints trout on plywood. A prediction bench had priced the closure; the chair followed the price. No one calls it courage. It’s the job.

Friday in a Slow Cash loop in Vermont. Phones nap in a wooden tray by the store. Eliza pushes a barrow to the sheepfold. The diagnostic model at the clinic perches like a microscope; a nurse ensures it speaks softly. Three paths for knees that grind—surgery now; a stick and sturdy shoes with strong tea and pain relief; or a supervised wait. Eliza chooses. No algorithm sulks.

Saturday above Mare Tranquillitatis. Ji‑won steps from a vacuum lab into a corridor that opens on orchards painted along a curve. He logs a microgravity alloy run that will make transformers back home hum cooler. On his bunk: I planted the plum. It will flower while you’re away. He writes back: send purple. Later he sits a shift at a Customs Desk while a collective computation sings a flimsy proof. He stamps a throttle, posts a clear reason, and finishes his cup.

Sunday in a port city. A refinery lists its grandparents to an auditor—two licensed lines from three, from a pilot whose key expired. A subcontractor tries to push unauthorized code. Lineageware pauses the line. The spare network flags it. The court fines a week’s margin and posts the log. It lands like a reminder to check smoke alarms.

Small habits that make everything else easier

- Rhythm rights. Each month everyone holds a real twelve hours when the world cannot reach them digitally unless they ink‑signed an exception that expires. Calendars bend automatically.

- Ambient hazard pay. When the sky turns bad or the heat index climbs, payments appear in the wallets of people who cannot move their shift: warehouse hands, garbage crews, bus drivers. No forms. Sensors trigger budgets.

- Spillback. Harms don’t vanish into general funds. A visible slice flows straight to those who bore the edge. When a stadium breaks sleep, money lands in the accounts under its glare the week sensors prove it.

- Compost dates. Rules, models, gear ship with sunset dates and checklists. Unless renewed with reasons and tests, they return to mulch.

- Right to analog. For every critical process there’s a paper, radio, or face‑to‑face path that works when power or patience fails.

- Gray‑sky rules. When storms surge, certain permits flip from ask to tell‑and‑log with automatic audits later. People stop dying while we argue.

- Leak bounties. Anyone who quietly saves public money by catching a leak or corrupt pattern shares in the savings.

- Care Exchange. The Time Account doubles as a scheduling commons that suggests rides, visits, pot‑luck gaps, and pays stipends fast when hands meet needs.

How we got here

From distance it looks like a leap. Up close it’s clocks, receipts, and stubborn copying. We stacked boring miracles until they paid for brave ones.

Years 1–3
- Libraries opened as cooling rooms during heat waves and didn’t close when the weather did. Lockers and showers followed. Insurers watched ambulance calls fall and wrote dull memos that funded the first Harbor Rooms.
- Schools and buses tied occupancy to indoor air basics—CO₂ monitors and good filters like smoke alarms. Absences dropped. Arguing about personality gave way to watching numbers change when you cracked a window.
- A few councils passed a Receipts Rule: no big change without a one‑page reason and a rollback plan. Agencies posted forecasts on their own moves. When choosing the odds saved money, copycats sprouted.
- Permit portals and pattern books. Two states built portals with clocks that fined offices that stalled. Public builders proved panelized timber could be cheap and kind to the eye. Early place‑rent pilots cut vacancy without flattening charm.
- Quiet defaults. Devices with Hush Rails shipped. Courts learned to say, “you stole their attention,” with the gravity of “you stole their wages.” Uninvited pings paid levies that planted shade.

Years 4–7
- Bio and compute guardrails. DNA printers shipped with licensing and ledgers. Community labs calmed because they could point to dials. Foundries attested chips; clouds added run passports and whistleblower bounties. Break‑and‑Patch seasons became sport and safety net.
- Grids with spines. HVDC lines piggybacked on rails and highways with compensation you could explain at a bar. Walk‑away‑safe reactors cleared certification; local approvals moved on dashboards. Microgrids practiced Lantern Mode until drills felt like parties.
- Floors that pay back. Harbor Rooms and relationship clinics were funded by modest levies on manipulative ads and high‑fee remittances, then underwritten by insurers when emergency calls dropped. Courts began freezing coercive debts at exits.

Years 8–12
- Identity peeled from reach. Platforms that wanted school and city contracts separated who you are from how loud you can be. Floods met friction without gags.
- Civic firmware pilots. One brave city moved lights, meters, and registries onto slow, audited firmware with rollbacks and a visible clock. Red‑team bounties paid in public, shaming better code elsewhere into existence.
- Place rent scaled. Place rent replaced much of property tax in six regions; spectrum, curb space, and compute above a wattage threshold joined. Buses every eight minutes stopped sounding fanciful.
- Edge Ledgers stitched seams. Sensors, honest and cheap, watched stadium edges and job sites. Spillbacks paid glare victims this week. Credits landed for cool nights under new canopies.

Years 13–20
- Labor as infrastructure. Districts published vacancy ratios. When they sagged, councils issued dignified work orders—trees, insulation, elder visits—at wages that forced private employers to compete.
- Common Houses and Weavers. Libraries turned unused rooms into kitchens and workshops. Weavers trained and earned. Evaluation focused on friendships formed and kept.
- Proteins changed. Fermented and cultivated proteins took over public procurement. Fishing fleets took contracts from ocean trusts to rebuild reefs with the same boats.
- Courts normalized “twice‑written reasons” and a three‑year audit of how reasons aged. Restitution replaced cages in more cases. Quiet Groves opened; entry and exit stayed voluntary.
- Ports got courts with teeth. When a mirror swarm shadowed a rival’s fields, keys clipped; insurers enforced. Flags mattered less; behavior mattered more.

Why this holds

It is good because dignity is engineered, not begged. Help arrives early with clean socks and broth, not paperwork and scolding. One bad month doesn’t cascade into a decade. Lungs stop being quiet arenas of policy failure. You can choose a slow life without being left and a hot lab without spilling into other people’s mornings. Kindness is a budget line with auditors, not a hobby that burns through saints.

It is specific enough to check. You can feel the click of the Hush Rail. You can hold a Crossing Card and know which train to catch. You can open a Reason Receipt and follow the math. You can stand by an edge sensor under the thump of a show and know money will land because you’re the one kept awake. You can watch a steward say, “I was wrong,” and roll back. You can open your vault and remember why you said yes.

It is plausible because almost every piece already peeks into the present: libraries as cooling centers; curb pricing that keeps buses running; reactors certified like planes; wastewater reading flu; personal data pods; prediction tournaments that beat pundits; fiduciary duty extended to software; lineage tracking in machines; performance‑based building codes; community microgrids; classrooms with CO₂ meters and fans that matter. We didn’t summon a miracle. We stacked boring miracles, wired them, and paid with rents on places and bands and lanes we share.

A small pledge travels with the riverglass tile, the Companion’s oath, the Weavers’ ledger:

- I will keep a clear path for anyone who falters, including myself.
- I will show my reasons where they touch others, and how I know them.
- I will accept careful requests and decline without shame.
- I will build one thing each season that lets a stranger breathe easier.
- I will not mistake tidy for true, or loud for right.

Morning somewhere else. A dressmaker uses her compute stipend on a pattern assistant that measures like an old tailor, patient and funny. Her first jacket fits a bus driver so well he cries. A ribbon in the Return Ledger follows her open design through a dozen shops. It isn’t a river. It’s enough.

On a coast that will walk inland, a family meets a migration ombud in a room that smells of thyme. Before the seawall fails, they hold keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co‑op shift, and a name for the person who will meet them at the station. They cry. They make lists. Transit follows them, not the other way around.

In orbit, a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth and—for once—sleeps. Under a cedar, a teenager ties a flat knot and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens more than she speaks. The tile at the door glows green like the underside of leaves after rain. The chime trembles. Soft soles whisk. A voice kind to storms asks the best question we know: What do you need now?

The rooms stand. The edges are bright. You can go far. You can come home. The weave holds.
