The Lantern Commons

Behind the bakery’s back door, where steam sneaks from racks and the air smells like warm yeast, a hand-sized tile waits—a deep blue disk, thumb‑polished, etched with a small spiral that points homeward. You tap it with a knuckle because tired hands are honest. Somewhere in the wall a relay breathes. A latch gives with the soft release of a jar that finally opened. Inside, the light is amber on purpose.

It smells like cedar and lemon. A washer hums through one cycle that tells the truth. Two shirts hang from pegs in sizes that make sense. Sandals sit with the snip tags you keep. A door that locks from the inside guards a cot you can fold. A kettle wakes where you can see it, not as a trick. Chargers are generous but not nosy. A heavy mug holds broth with a slice of ginger like a coin. A person in soft shoes is fixing a hinge. Their badge says Host. They ask slowly, as if speed can bruise: What would help in the next hour? Should anyone be told you’re safe?

This is a Lantern Room. There are millions—above markets, beside bus bays, in the side halls of fire stations, on the second floor of union houses, behind kitchen doors in mosques and synagogues and churches, tucked near staff sinks in libraries. They are not favors. They are paid like pipes and inspected like bridges. You do not owe a wound‑story to open the door. Machines do the dull miracles: hot water, thick towels, a snug latch, a transit card that doesn’t humiliate you. People do the human ones: calm voices, a pause at the edges of panic, a bowl of rice without interrogation.

Two domains, one key you keep

We rebuilt public life with a seam that honors how humans swing between risk and rest. On one side are Havens: where we sleep and raise children, cook and sing, argue and repair and practice being gentle. On the other are Yards: where we attempt work with teeth—ship houses that loft grand frames, protein kitchens that put feast back on tables without old harm, code shops that solder logic into instruments, rings where gravity glances off and orchards grow in arcs. A short gate sits between them—the Personal Gate. Only you carry its token. Affection crosses freely; obligations cross only if you invite them.

- Havens are strict in kind ways. Consent asks plainly and often. Night means night. Quiet is protected like pressure keeps air in. Devices behave like guests and take off their shoes.

- Yards are ambitious and candid. They post a pact by the door: scope, speed, safety. Nothing that leaves a Yard may crash into anyone’s breakfast. Sharp work wears visible belts, publishes tests you can read, and pauses when a hand you trust hits a brake.

Standing Pledges, printed on faucets and log‑in screens

Four promises thread everything. Children learn them before long division; adults repeat them when moving something bigger than themselves.

1) Falls are shallow. Ordinary misfortunes—a layoff, a breakup, a sprain, a small reckless act—cannot erase a life. A Lantern Room lies within a ten‑minute walk of most beds. Bills that could become leashes pause under a neutral’s signature. Keys are cut before evictions. Soup arrives without confessional theater.

2) Consent breathes. Any tool or process touching your body, home, memory, or attention asks in words you can say back; stops cleanly with no sulk; and budgets for undoing when physics allows it. Big choices come with rehearsals vivid enough to feel and a cooling period you picked on a calm day. One‑way doors ride with Undo Insurance you can claim without putting on your courtroom voice.

3) Reasons come first. If a system with reach changes—bus lanes, water rates, shift algorithms, budgets—it posts a Move Receipt before the move unless physics forbids delay. The receipt names the guess, the test you can try, the fallback if wrong, the model that did the math, the day we’ll check, and the bond that burns if they miss. Teenagers can follow it. Their grandparents can hold it without squinting.

4) Exits carry landings. You can end a job, a lease, a creed, a chat, a dangerous evening without falling through trapdoors. You walk into a Lantern Room and say “I’m ending this,” and the world tilts to catch you: a Bridge Pass lands in your civic wallet within seventy‑two hours—keys to a small flat for a month (extendable when prudent), holds on avalanche bills, a transit pass that just works, compute you can spend on tools or rest, and three numbers that answer on the second ring. Teens practice this like swimming in a guarded lane. Saying no does not burn the floor from under you.

Fixtures you can grasp half‑asleep

Rights evaporate if they live only in slogans. We built knobs and slips and switches anyone can work.

- Quiet Slider: A brass tab by every bed and door. Drop it and microphones sleep, boards dim, apps stop nudging unless they sit on the handwritten whitelist tacked by the switch: Aunt Mei; flood siren; the school nurse. To cross silence, a human you named must say your name. Uninvited grabs at your attention pay a tiny toll into your block’s budget.

- Pause Pedal: Any system that nudges, schedules, or decides must honor three moves a fifth grader can manage: Stop now; show in six boxes what you’re trying to do and why; resume without revenge. There’s a gesture and a tone that brands must respect. Ignoring a universal pause carries penalties like ignoring a fire alarm.

- Stepsheet: A folded card lives by counters and in phones—how to appeal a bill, form a co‑op, fix a deed, claim a benefit, file a complaint. Every step carries a visible clock. Miss your own deadline; money flows to the person waiting automatically.

- Right to Analog: For every critical function there is a paper, radio, or face‑to‑face path that works when patience or power fails.

- Data Pantry: Tools borrow ingredients from your digital larder with your say and leave a receipt. An envelope on your fridge prints who asked, why, how long, and how to revoke instantly.

- Rhythm Sabbath: One twelve‑hour block a month when the world cannot interrupt you unless you handwrote an exception. Workplaces and schools plan around it like tides.

- Influence Labels: Anything trying to steer you carries a plain tag your aunt can read: who paid; what it optimizes; which data it touched; what tricks it may use; when it stops; how to stop it now.

A floor like water pressure

Under each life sits a floor that doesn’t demand a performance of deserving. It follows you when you move and is paid like mains: from rent on things none of us made alone.

- Home: a small flat that locks, breathes, and quiets. When moving is wise—smoke, flood, danger, love—new keys cut before old ones die. Movers are part of the floor.

- Power: steady enough to plan minutes; sockets that keep chairs and ventilators alive. Twice a year we practice Dusk Mode—the grid slips to a safe crescent; fridges and oxygen hum; guitars come out.

- Movement: one token covers bus to train to ferry to glideways. If your bus misses its headway, a credit appears without complaint.

- Care: primary care finds you when that’s smarter. Nurses start with sleep, pain, and safety before compliance. House calls happen again.

- Tools: hours in public workshops—lathes, kilns, printers, sound rooms—where instruction is pooled in. The binder by the band saw is titled Fingers We Intend to Keep.

- Compute: like tap water—enough to translate, learn, write, run a shop, compose. Big runs queue politely behind safety labs and public work. Models for children refuse to fake affection. Adults can demand the same.

- Fast Ombuds: a number answers for housing, school, and work disputes that cannot wait. Escalations run on clocks anyone can point to. Miss those clocks; money moves.

We pay for the floor with rent on gifts none of us made alone: land underfoot; curb frontage; rooftop light; radio bands; orbital lanes; ridgeline wind; bright water; training watts beyond a posted line; models built from public baselines; deep harbors; fiber trenches. Skill and sweat are not taxed; place and band and lane are. Half of the yield pays bones and kindness—pipes, filters, clinics, buses, Lantern Rooms, slow courts that answer. The other half appears as a Commons Share next to your utility bill: small and steady, sized to help you decline a bad deal.

Belonging, by design not accident

Loneliness turned out to be a design error. Each district keeps a Commons House: a library tied to a kitchen tied to a workshop. Long tables worn to beauty. Mugs that know dish soap. A piano unashamed of being played badly on Thursdays. A glass room for quiet work. A dark room with heavy curtains. A corner where children can make elaborate nonsense without damage. Weavers—part teacher, part neighbor—keep the place threaded. With consent and care, they watch isolation curves like levees. When the graph bends wrong, a small budget unlocks automatically for specific invitations: Friday we’re walking the ravine. You laughed at the mushroom joke last week. Omar who tells long stories is coming. Bring a thermos?

Edges and quarters that tell the truth

At seams we mounted simple meters with names like bus timetables: particulates at dusk; nitrates after rain; decibels past bedtime; glare that climbs shades; spectrum clutter that steals sleep; pathogen signals in wastewater; the frequency and reach of algorithmic blasts. Spill beyond posted limits and money flows this week into neighbors’ accounts. Spill help—shade that cuts ambulance calls, courtyards with soup that calm midnight emergencies, code that returns five minutes to nurses—and a visible credit hums back. Some flows we don’t price: nesting dark; air over neonatal wards; memory inside temples. Those carry caps with guardians who wear boots.

Belts and brakes on sharp tools

Anything fast, strong, or contagious wears belts bright enough to see and a brake anyone can test.

- Compute: above a wattage line, runs file plans before the first gradient drops. Chips attest they are who they say; meters count power; an exhaust sensor whispers to a witness. Models ship with Capability Cards written for adults: can write code; cannot propose toxins beyond a threshold; refuses to simulate lovers. Sharp systems wear a three‑key Civic Brake—one with local tenders, one with a rotating bench of engineers, one with a panel drawn by lot. Any key can halt; all three must extend. Once a year, serious systems sit through Open Seasons—rewarded public attack weeks. Good breaks earn bounties. Fixes land on clocks.

- Biology: replicators and DNA shops obey lineage and place. Family Counters cap grandchildren unless someone visits a depot. Feedstocks ride tamper‑evident logs. Chargers and waste yards refuse liars. Self‑replication outside sealed spaces is a bright‑line offense. Strays lose traction by design: grandparents’ keys do not open doors.

- Persuasion: reach parts from identity. You can speak under your name or none; to buy everyone’s breakfast table, you burn Reach Stamps tied to contribution and a modest monthly allotment. Anything that aims to steer wears an Influence Label anyone can read.

- Affection: everyday models and tools live behind an Empathy Firewall. They can be kind; they cannot pretend to need you. Judges can point to the line.

Work with pride; slack on purpose

We kept the labor market tight on purpose. Each region posts a Slack Dial next to the weather. When it sags, councils release public work orders: shade in heat islands; leak hunts; elder visits; culvert clearing; code cleanups; moth corridors. Wages sit just high enough that private shops must compete on dignity, not desperation. Matchers act like tailors; they’re paid only if, at six months and two years, both person and place say on speaking terms.

Automation triggers stabilizers. People in the path of machines get paid seasons in guild schools before layoffs hit and an equity slice in the savings their old work unlocked. An Automation Dividend trails machines into shops. A Pride Ledger follows you: shifted the 12 bus by six minutes; stitched a pollinator corridor; ran bedtime for solo parents through February; found the leak that saved three million liters; shaved ten minutes off the longest commute. Auditors check calmly without swagger.

Governance you can read; truth as a utility

Traffic lights, benefit portals, water valves, identity rails—anything that sits between people and their days—runs on Civic Firmware you can inspect. Patches happen in seasons with public rollbacks. Break‑glass requires supermajority keys in named hands.

We train Civic Stewards—small auditable systems and the people minding them—on open law, maps, crop calendars, budget logs. Their first year carries no levers. They ride snowplows at three in the morning, sit through procurement, fix broken signs. Three circles license them—households, trades, place. Any can veto. Once a year, each Steward faces a ten‑minute surprise exam in a square under a clock. Hiding reasons pays a bounty to the finder.

Big moves print Move Receipts; leaders who override public odds boards post Regret Bonds that sting if they miss. We replaced some televised shouting with Build Fairs. Aim: zero street deaths in three years? Teams build working loops. Sortition juries score clarity and kindness. Winners lock scope and budget. Good losses get copied.

Justice that repairs; safety without empire

We unbundled power so no single hand held too many tools. Those who detain do not interrogate. Those who investigate do not prosecute. Many wrongs end in restitution, time‑bound role bans, and work tied to the harm, mediated in Mend Studios beside Lantern Rooms. Serious violence still sometimes earns walls; those rooms are smaller, inspected, built for return. Calm Groves—low‑stimulation commons with predictable loops—welcome people city life scrapes raw under posted terms. Predictive tools aim care, not punishment. Judges write reasons in plain words and face audits—not for verdicts, but for the lives their logic led to.

Ports we share—the cloud, rails, oceans, orbits—sit under a narrow Gate Court with big teeth. Its questions are dull and powerful: are you acting where you claimed? are you doing what you promised? Clerks are tight systems that check signatures and sums. Judges rotate. The court can close valves, revoke keys, and shave dividends. Most fights end with a sentence no one can spin: This contract expired.

A Constraint Wing—gray‑coated engineers—keeps shared nets from being drowned or trampled. Most days, that means mending fiber so a propaganda hose can’t drown a vote or reopening a two‑lane so rival towns still share a clinic. On rare days, if bulldozers line to drain a marsh that holds a bright legal line, a rotor reads the charter aloud, churns the soil to pudding long enough for a judge to arrive, and waits. Machines break; people do not.

Nature with a chair and a budget

Rivers, reefs, prairies, old groves, migratory nights hold charters, budgets, and guardians with standing and teeth. Turbines nap on bird nights; it shows on a board. Seagrass maps steer licensing. An Ocean Trust pays coastal crews to anchor kelp and rebuild reef; funds come from leases on blue industries. We do not build suffering minds. When two independent assays agree a nonhuman mind can feel and endorse feeling, floors expand and a say comes with them. Declared digital minds may unionize, refuse assignments, call an ombud. None can buy breakfast.

Energy, water, shelter, bread, movement

We overbuilt the boring parts until reliability felt like luxury and then made it unremarkable.

- Power: rooftops and ridgelines hum; closed‑loop geothermal sips steady rock; walk‑away‑safe microreactors clear a single, brutal type certification and sit on brownfields under glass dashboards; HVDC spines ride rail and highway easements with compensation you can explain at a bar; iron‑air yards and hot salt carry us through dark weeks. Second‑source rules keep critical supply two deep.

- Water: coastal desal runs hard when wind howls; listening fibers and small drones make loss rare; wastewater labs read cities’ health like weather. Floodplain buyouts trigger after verified floods; new keys arrive first. A Migration Ombud meets you with tea and three neighborhoods you might like.

- Shelter: permits flipped from riddles to outcome APIs—hit marks for light, sound, drainage, quake, fire, access, energy; shape is yours. Public builders stamp handsome timber‑and‑stone modules near transit; cranes stack them like serious furniture. Pattern books are open and a little funny. Ground rent made hoarding empties expensive. Each region keeps finished units by the tracks. Encampments became key problems, not police problems.

- Food: precision fermentation went from clever to crave; cultivated roasts carry old feast recipes without old harms; inspectors have teeth. Farmers still farm—mixed perennials, drought‑smiling grains, hedgerows that pay for birdsong. Sea corridors thicken until whales have choices. Where easing suffering doesn’t shred webs, we do it—oral vaccines in baits, salt licks that balance harsh years. Still no ads in the sky.

- Movement: feet first. Trains move like weather. Buses arrive when promised. Curb prices rise and fall to pay for bus lanes we didn’t lie about. Crossing town with a stroller without swearing is a right in charters. Night belongs mostly to whispering freight; day belongs to people.

Global fairness, on purpose

Rents from global commons—orbital slots, polar routes we rationed deliberately, deep‑sea minerals we often leave asleep—flow into an Earth Dividend that lands atop any Commons Share. Funds move not through pity but through contract: regions that receive on purpose are paid on purpose by emitters and insurers who prefer repair to ruin. Vaccines, antivenoms, and essential drugs are manufactured in distributed open plants that license like radio stations—auditable formulas, attested batches, local labor. We copied what worked from community mesh nets: local hands, global standards. Trade attaches to safety oaths: publish your reasons, respect our belts, pause on our signal, or your customs lane dims.

Distance as a kindness

Some work runs at speeds and risks we keep at a distance. Off‑world yards, cold cloud rings, deep‑sea labs—the Far Belts—exist by charter and attach by Distance Pacts. Parental locks on power keep children tools from exceeding their rights. Strays crumble because depots refuse liars. Teleoperation made “space job” something a rural teenager with good hands can do from a room that smells like coffee.

Five ordinary slices

Monday in Nairobi. A minibus screens air green to amber as the morning warms. A poster the size of a chopping board—today’s Move Receipt—says the eastern busway will close for eleven days; the bridge whispered fatigue. The receipt names the guess, shows the fallback route in thick blue, lists the person who will apologize either way, and the clock under their name. A teenager with braided hair runs fingers along a Steward’s tablet, tracing how two parking stalls became trees two blocks over. “We’ll check again in March,” the Steward says. “Here’s the rollback if we blew it.” Someone hands mango. No one shouts. Schedules adjust.

Wednesday in Busan. A fisher steps into a Lantern Room with a phone bill heavy as rope. The Host makes tea. No detective work. A clinician tends a bad shoulder without lectures. A navigator opens a Stepsheet for the bill, taps the appeal clock with a glue‑stained finger. By afternoon there’s a halt on fees, a day’s fair work at the net‑repair yard, and two numbers that answer on the second ring.

Thursday in Appalachia. Phones nap in a wooden tray outside the clinic. Inside, a diagnostic model sits like a microscope; a nurse ensures it speaks softly. Three paths for a spine that grinds—surgery; a stout stick and shoes that forgive; strong tea and honest pain control. Leon chooses without hurry. No algorithm pouts. On the way home, he slides the Quiet Slider and the room exhales.

Friday in Jakarta. A monsoon pulse stutters the river high. Listening fibers in floodwalls whisper. The Constraint Wing spokes a culvert clear before lunch. By afternoon, teens in rubber boots move crates of vaccines from a low clinic to a higher temple storeroom. The Weavers have keys to both on purpose. A Migration Ombud sits under a fan with three families and three maps. “If we move by next Tuesday, here are two flats and a co‑op shift. The keys are cut. Keep one eye on the river anyway.”

Sunday on Tycho’s gray. In a ring that opens on orchards painted along a curve, Ji‑Won logs a microgravity alloy run that will make transformers cooler at home. He passes through a Hall of Promises—Capability Cards under glass. On his bunk: I planted the plum. It may flower while you’re away. He writes back: Send purple. Later he sits a shift with the Gate Court while a mirror swarm tries to shade a rival’s fields. Keys clip, not lives.

How we walked here

There was no master switch. We stacked quiet wins until they paid for brave ones. We copied shamelessly, standardized the boring parts, and made good defaults cheap.

Years 1–3
- Summer cooling and winter warming centers became all‑season Lantern Rooms: sinks, lockers, small kitchens, quartermasters. Insurers noticed ambulance calls fall and wrote dull memos that bought more sinks.
- Duty of loyalty entered consumer law. Dark patterns became theft of agency. Product standards added the Quiet Slider and Pause Pedal. Judges used the new lines.
- School and transit funding tied to indoor air: CO₂ dots, filters, upper‑room violet light. Absences and inhalers fell so quietly it felt like cheating.
- Permit portals grew clocks and fines for stalling. Mid‑rise timber kits approved unless an inspector found a true miss in two weeks. Material passports began with public jobs.
- Ground rent pilots opened: long‑empty lots moved; slumlords met arithmetic; buildings improved without tax paranoia.
- Transit auto‑credits paid back lateness. The small pleasure of a kept promise spread.
- Data Pantries launched with standard rails. Identity split from reach on big platforms. Floods of plausible nonsense met friction without gagging voices.

Years 4–7
- A third of property taxes slid toward location value, curb frontage, rooftop radiance, leases for spectrum and training watts. Funds bought buses every eight minutes, retrofitted schools and clinics, and sent a small Commons Share.
- Compute registries tied to meters and capability classes launched. Training‑watt tariffs flowed to filters, safety labs, and compute stipends for small shops. Professional oaths hardened: don’t ship sharp capacity without a brake and an Open Season.
- Right‑to‑repair passed: schematics after a short head start, parts and debug ports at civilian prices, warranties that didn’t ban curiosity.
- Commons Houses opened in library basements. Weavers trained. Isolation was tracked like a levee; budgets unlocked automatically when it dipped.
- Family Counters landed on DNA printers without strangling community labs. Lying got expensive. Wastewater epidemiology got funded like weather. Microgrid co‑ops practiced Dusk Mode until drills felt like block parties.
- Undo Insurance started narrow; claims taught it where to grow.

Years 8–12
- Edge Boards moved from pilots to factories and nightlife. Numbers replaced rumor. Credits flowed for courtyards that shaved ambulance calls.
- Public odds boards went up for bus corridors, floodplains, drought relief with safe harbors for agencies that used them. Teenagers kept winning out of sample; reversals were celebrated.
- Civic Stewards trained inside municipal twins open to inspection. Bounties paid for hiding reasons. Square exams became Saturday rituals.
- Lantern standards went national: sinks, lockers, neutral teen exits. The right to exit appeared in charters. Bridge Passes became normal.
- HVDC corridors permitted with farmer‑friendly compensation. Walk‑away reactors and deep geothermal cleared like aircraft. Metals were cheap; noon charged iron‑air and hot salt.
- Automation Dividends trailed robots into shops. Slack Dials stayed high with civic work orders. Guild schools filled. Empathy Firewalls became law: no pretend lovers in everyday tools.

Years 13–20
- A belts‑and‑borders compact passed: Quiet Quarters vowed tame code; Foundry Belts vowed unions for the minds they invited and a breakfast truce; tavern rows vowed clear exits and genre gates.
- Guardians with budgets and standing sat on rivers and old groves. Cases for migratory nights and quiet estuaries won.
- Modular housing yards by the tracks cut tents for keys in weeks. Encampments became key problems, not police problems.
- The Constraint Wing drilled openly under three red lines: no invasion; yes to halting avalanches; reasons filed by noon tomorrow. The line held.
- Far Belts got Distance Pacts. Pads, depots, insurers tied to dull but lethal ostracism for liars. Teleoperation became a rural space job.
- An Earth Dividend tied to global commons matured. Cities in the south led on microgrid practice and sold patterns north at dignified prices. Medicine plants licensed like radio stations.

Small, unglamorous rules made the rest cheap:
- Twin‑source rule: any critical public system keeps two independent supply chains and an open‑spec version a co‑op can build in a pinch.
- Human change budgets: every capital project sets aside time and money for training, translation, spare parts, and sitting with the displaced. Cut it and cause trouble? Your name glows on budget night.
- Scrutiny bonds: big proposers post bonds that burn if they bury contrary results. It stung just enough to make publishing misses a habit.
- Tenfold juries: any resident can propose a small shift in the civic simulator. Ten names drawn by lot from the affected district read a postcard case, consult a Steward, and vote. If seven agree and odds don’t scream, a small slice moves.
- Exit scores: insurers and utilities rate places by how easy it is to leave cleanly. High scores earn discounts and votes. Low scores fix doors or pay.
- Ambient hazard pay: when heat or smoke climbs past human, money lands in the wallets of those who can’t leave their shift—bus drivers, sanitation crews, warehouse hands. No forms; sensors trigger budgets.
- Warm starts: when you change districts, your keys work on arrival; a Host greets you; three circles that might fit kept a chair open with your name.

Why this holds

It is good because on the worst morning there is a blue tile you don’t have to deserve. Because attention can rest. Because lungs stop being where policy hides. Because teenagers rehearse exits the way they learn knots. Because you can push at the edge of what’s possible with belts on your tools and friends to call, and then go home to soup and a room that remembers how you like the dark. Because whales find more than one lane through the sea. Because if someone moves power without reasons, clocks light and bonds burn—and no tanks roll.

It is specific. You can slide a Quiet Slider and feel your shoulders drop, then see the tiny toll a overeager app paid your block. You can hold a Stepsheet and follow it from a bad bill to a small court that answers on time. You can read last month’s Move Receipt and point to the day someone checked if they lied. You can scan a Lineage Seal at a printer and watch a green light tell you what it refuses to make. You can sit at a Build Fair and watch pedestrians cross a street that killed your neighbor last year and not die. You can open your Pride Ledger and show a child the moth corridor you helped stitch.

It is plausible because most of it already breathes somewhere: libraries as cooling centers that became Lantern Rooms; CO₂ dots in classrooms; wastewater that sees flu before clinics; panelized timber on cranes; HVDC along rails; reactors under type review; negative income supports that didn’t explode; ground rents that steadied land markets; public options that tamed clubs; prediction tournaments that kept promises out of punditry; hardware attestation that keeps planes and payments honest; teleoperated repair as a job; lineage tracking in labs; performance codes that care about outcomes; community microgrids; courts that finally say duty of loyalty with their hands.

Evening elsewhere. A dressmaker who learned code late spends her compute stipend on a pattern helper with the manners of an old tailor—patient, funny. Her first jacket fits a bus driver so well he cries. A thin ribbon of credits follows her open design through the Return Ledger to the carpentry co‑op whose mortise she borrowed, to the public shop whose sander she used, to the safety lab that wrote the Pause Pedal. It isn’t a river. It is enough.

On a coast that will walk inland soon, a family meets a Migration Ombud in a room that smells like thyme. Before the seawall fails, they hold keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co‑op shift, and the name of who will meet them at the station. They cry. They make lists. Transit follows them, not the other way around.

Far out, a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth and—for once—sleeps. Under a plane tree, a teenager learns the knot you use to clip a rope to a ring and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens longer than she speaks. The blue tile glows by the door. You touch it. A chime like a spoon on a mug. Footfalls on tile. A voice kind to nerves asks the question we trusted enough to put on rails: What would help in the next hour?

The lines are bright. The doors are open. You can go far. You can come home. And the lights stay lit.
