The Latch and the Loom

The tile is the color of roasted barley and warm to the touch. It glows in the drizzle under a rail arch where weeds and wild fennel poke through brickwork. A woman with ocean salt frosting her hair presses the tile with her thumb. A bolt slides; air like clean eucalyptus breathes out. Inside, everything is intentionally ordinary—hooks where you expect hooks, light that flatters no one but shows the crumbs to sweep.

Steam slips from a shower that stops where you set it. On a shelf: jeans in three sizes, a sweater the same blue as the one she lost, sandals with tiny snips she can clip if she keeps them. Towels folded into fat triangles, not swans. A pot on a warmer murmurs: broth with ginger, a half-circle of lemon riding its rim. At the desk, a person looks at her eyes, not her history. Two questions, light enough to carry: What would help in the next hour? Who do you want us to tell that you’re safe?

There’s a washer that finishes on time and doesn’t eat socks. A cot behind a door that locks from inside and stays locked unless the person in the room says otherwise. A drawer labeled just chargers that actually fit. A map with three soft rings: shelters and spare rooms for a night, week, and season, all within reach of steel rails that keep promises. A small line of code—already running in the background—has paused bills that would otherwise pile like leaves in a gutter while she climbs out of the moment she is in.

If she asks, a four-person team wraps around her for a while and then, as her feet find ground, quietly lets go.

- A legal steward lives in her private vault as a tiny piece of software with a very old duty: loyalty to her interests. If the steward deviates, a court treats it like a treasurer who stole.

- A clinician who can start anywhere—sleep, pain, a heart that won’t slow—instead of beginning with forms.

- A horizon-keeper whose job is to keep what future-her wanted in the room and negotiate, kindly, with present-her at a kitchen table that smells of citrus and soap.

- A fixer who turns twelve paper cuts into one Tuesday: when she asks for potatoes, they make sure she also has a pot and a lighter that sparks.

The tile by the arch is one of millions. We call these places Harbor Rooms. From them, you can infer the shape of the world that bothered to build them: a world with a latch you can trust and a loom you can join.

Homeground and Farworks, joined by your latch

Life moves between two generous rooms connected by a lock only you can open.

- Homeground is kitchens and stoops, parks at dusk, handrails on stairs, and ceilings that don’t drip. The rules near bodies and bedrooms are dull on purpose and applied the same way to everyone. Help arrives without theater. Emergencies have rehearsals that feel like block parties.

- Farworks holds projects too large or weird for a block: orchards grown under gentle spin inside old rings; cold compute stacks in polar night that produce proofs you can check; protein halls that make celebration food without harm; alloys brewed where weight forgets to sag; desert grids that braid sun and wind and salt caverns. Farworks promises breadth and speed. Its first rule is that nothing in it gets to rattle a neighbor’s breakfast.

The thing that joins them is not poetry. It is plumbing: keys, contracts, customs, and courts that let curiosity cross and make coercion fall flat. You can go far; you cannot be dragged.

Five promises we nail to doors and contracts

We teach these to children. We etch them into leases for spectrum, barges, satellites, mines, factories, and models. If you want to touch a town, tie into the energy spine, or place something in a shared sky, you sign for them.

- Safe landing. A run of bad days cannot erase a life. Floors exist and hold. Exits exist and do not drop you through the rotten boards of shame and debt.

- Living consent. Any rule that touches your body, home, attention, or mind rides on consent that breathes—understood in your words, shaped to the stakes, and revocable with a path back from everything but endings you insisted upon.

- Explainable steering. When a system large enough to move many makes choices—routes, budgets, rates—it owes reasons a teenager could follow, links to the numbers beneath, and a date to look again.

- Soft attention. Unasked-for grabs at your mind are expensive. Nights have a real off. Persuasion ships with a diet label: what it optimizes for, what data it used, and what it ignores.

- Open doors. Homes, clinics, schools, studios—even households—have public, practiced exits and complaints that work without insiders. No one can keep you by hiding the way out.

Fixtures you can touch

We turned good ideas into hardware and habits.

- The Quiet Latch, a cool oval by every front door and workbench, slides rooms and accounts into Sleep. Anything that listens or tries to change your mind stops unless it’s on a small, handwritten list you taped beside the latch. Breaking that quiet requires a human you named speaking your name three times. Uninvited pings pay a tiny toll into your block’s budget unless you whitelist them.

- The Pull-Cord Test is law. Any tool in a home, classroom, clinic, shop, or civic stack must prove to a fifth grader that it can be paused, inspected, and resumed without sulking. Pass–fail, printed on a metal tag you can thumb.

- The One-Page Path. To appeal a bill, form a shop, fix a deed, claim a benefit—you have a right to a path on a single page that works without a consultant. Service clocks run. When an agency misses, money moves toward you by default.

- The Right to Company. Ask in any Harbor Room and you’re paired with that four-person team for a season. They advise. They cannot sign for you. Their duty runs to you, not to an agency metric.

- Cool Steps for consequential turns—surgeries, mortgages, software that will steer you. Plain explainer. Teach-back in your own words. A pause matched to the stakes. Two-key crossing with a human you trust. A rehearsal you can wake from. A reversal path for anything but those endings you asked for twice.

The floor you feel with your feet

We stopped treating scarcity as a moral teacher. We built a floor that rides with your civic key; there’s no application. It is not vibes. It is a list.

- A modest apartment that locks and breathes. If rivers move or families change shape, your unit can move with you; moving costs sit on the budget line, not the begging line.

- Staples you can serve proudly. Water that never tastes like an apology. Transit that crosses regions without guesswork.

- Power for light, cold, and a humane band of heat or cool. Once a season blocks practice Crescent: streetlights dim to arcs, fridges hum, oxygen machines run, stoves cook. Blackstarts are stews, not sirens.

- Primary care and mental health that come to your door when that’s smarter. Nurses with magician’s hands paid more than apps that nag.

- A compute credit matched to your electric bill. Enough to study, automate care, run a small shop, push a sanding robot across your week, sing—or do nothing at all.

- Time in public workshops. Lathes with patient guards in attendance. Kilns with a gentle manual next to the clay. Instruction is part of the tool. So are brooms and sign-up ledgers.

- A Time Ledger: hours each month for care, learning, and neighborhood work. You can spend them or lend them to your Mutual Kin—a ring of twenty-odd households who promise soup, school pick-ups, late-night stitches.

Paying for it feels plain when you say it out loud: we rent the things no one made alone. Site value under our feet. Spectrum lanes. Orbital slots. River mouths and mineral seams. Deep wind and hard sun. Shared models and factories that hum at night. Half of that pays for bones you can walk on—drains, clinics, spines, cranes. Half arrives as a Commons Dividend. When your earnings dip, a negative income tax quietly fills the hollow. If a market curls into a closed club, a public option opens next door, sells at cost until the kink relaxes, then leaves a thank-you sign.

When automation sands a trade down, stabilizers grab. Those displaced get equity in the gains and paid seats in guild schools. We treat a tight job market like clean water: if vacancy ratios sag, councils issue work orders—insulation retrofits, street trees, elder visits, floodplain restoration—at wages that force private employers to compete.

Spines, shade, and air you can breathe

We stopped pretending energy, water, air, bandwidth, and shade are premiums. We built spines and invited craft on top.

Energy arrived from everywhere. Roof films clicked on in an afternoon. Neighborhood geothermal loops slithered into easements. We tapped deep rock where it runs hot and learned to share. We licensed walk-away-safe reactors like airframes: certify a design that survives bruising tests, then site many with checklists about geology and egress, not philosophy. Ridge wind and offshore stacks stitched to cities with high-voltage DC that paid locals along the way. Storage sprawled in iron-air yards and salt caves. The slow parts—wires and permits—we funded like bridges, with service clocks that fine agencies for stalling.

Water became adult. Daytime desalination drank oversupply. Trunk mains tattled on leaks to a public map. Wastewater labs sat next to turbidity gauges and watched disease; when graphs scowled, a gentle light on your wall suggested skipping a concert. Retreat from some coasts felt less like banishment because Bridge Keys cut new keys before old ones were taken.

Air joined the rights list. CO₂ and humidity sat on the wall like calendars. Buses and classrooms treated the air they moved through; asthma calmed; absences fell without speeches. Coolth got a word and a budget: shadeways braided through hot districts—vines, pale roofs, porous mist that lowered temperature without drenching.

Networks remembered you’re a person. Identity is proof of personhood, not a biography dragged to every counter. Zero-knowledge proofs let you say “one human here” without spilling the rest. Bots don’t vanish; they simply don’t count in rooms that promise flesh.

Edges that count and pay back

Where ways of living rub, we made numbers and rules.

Sensors counted what they could count: fine particles, late-night decibels, pathogen traces, peak spectrum use, curb CO₂. Some things we refused to price—dark in nesting season, quiet above a threshold, places we agreed are sacred—so we drew hard lines there. Elsewhere, Edge Contracts made spillovers legible. Bad spillovers paid until they stopped. A stadium that used to keep half a county awake could now dampen, go dark at midnight, or pay the people under its glare starting the week the numbers showed. Good spillovers paid too. Plant a pocket forest that lowers your block’s peak by two degrees; households around it owe you microcredits that arrive like rain. Start a night soup that halves ambulance calls; a slice of the savings lands in the kitchen account.

We wired in Spillback: when a harm payment moves, a visible slice flows directly and quickly to those at the edge, not just to a distant budget. We wrote a Rivulet Rule for shared invention: if your product builds on open work and you sell it, a small automatic slice of the marginal value my piece added flows back unless you can show it didn’t. A salaried proof guild checks claims by surprise.

Rooms with membranes

We never forced one best life. We built membranes you can see and feel.

- Slow Quarters ask always-listening tools to sleep by default. Human checkout lines run at human pace. Craft studios above groceries. Radios and looms. Emergency services still roar in hot and bright; coercion doesn’t. Clinics answer at midnight when knees grind.

- Quick Yards run late with headphones and a piano in the corner. Robots move in their lanes. Spoiler mode grips your feeds when you cross in unless your key says you know and want the flood.

Between stands the Estuary—training guilds, ports, etiquette as software. Cross a membrane and your device drops to local defaults with a banner you cannot miss: You’re entering a Slow Quarter. Biometric doorbells sleep. Or: You’ve stepped into a Quick Yard. Mass messaging follows house customs. You can override; you can’t say you didn’t know.

Signals and stuff

Ideas move quicker than hulls. So we built customs for broadcasts. Inbound blasts to Homeground pass through Signal Pilots—rotating juries trained to throttle floods and filter coercion by default. They cannot stop a person from choosing a firehose; curiosity is a right. They can stop one actor from buying breakfast with every screen. A reach credit exists for shoving your voice onto everyone’s morning. It’s scarce, transparent, and rate-limited. Break the rule and it’s a tort called injury to attention and a debt to the common pot.

Stuff still touches customs. Ports that matter—ocean, orbital, and cloud—share a narrow Bench of Ports with teeth. It asks two questions: are you acting where you said? are you doing what you promised? Judges rotate from places that disagree. Clerks are narrow machines that check math. Misbehave and you lose keys. Launch pads stop, insurers freeze dividends, repair yards won’t talk. Wars can still happen—smaller, shorter. Most fights end when service contracts die.

Big tools with rails

We treat strong models and lively matter like we treat cranes and anesthetics.

Regions raise Civic Stewards—small, auditable systems trained on open law, maps, maintenance logs, crop calendars, and a lot of boredom. For a year, they have no actuators and no bank account. Three circles mentor them: households (grudges and dish schedules), trades (stakes and patience), and places (what breaks first). Any circle can veto graduation. When a Steward takes its oath, it promises five simple things: I explain; I pause at harm; I roll back; I say I don’t know; I say I was wrong.

Very large model runs file a flight log publicly before they train: who runs what, on which data, for what declared purpose, with which auditor. We watch three dials: software attestation; an energy meter at the rack; a thermal witness that samples waste heat. Hiding a run above threshold is expensive folly. A small Civic Brake—governed by a multisignature across nations, sectors, and citizen juries—can delay a risky launch for days. It must publish reasons you can interrogate and dissenting math.

DNA printers above a power class report by default. Labs inventory feedstock like jewelers track stones. Replicators ship with ancestryware—firmware simple enough to prove what they will not do. Parts depots, chargers, and logistics hubs refuse to talk to liars. Unbounded self-replication—code or cells—is a bright line we don’t cross. Strays crumble outside penned zones because their grandparents’ keys won’t open doors.

Five lines held with real levers

- No involuntary mass risk. Volunteer danger is devotion. Unconsented mass exposure is negligence.

- No beings built to suffer. Where we aren’t sure, we act as if they could until a standing Sentience Registry clears them.

- No unbounded replication in the wild or on the net. Anything that copies carries governors that count grandchildren and crumble if they roam.

- High-cascade work sits in glass. Pathogens, orbit, generalist models, swarms—logged compartments with many hands on a brake you can slap.

- No ads in the sky. If you float a thing, you warrant no breakfast is forced to look at it.

Belonging on purpose

Loneliness, we learned, was a public engineering flaw, not a private failure. Each block anchors a Common House: a bright kitchen that smells like toast, a tool library with a binder of fingers saved, a piano someone plays badly on Thursdays, soundproof booths, a supervised corner for children, a quiet room with heavy curtains. A Weaver—trained like a teacher, paid like an engineer—keeps the place woven. With consent, they keep a connection ledger that watches for isolation the way a levee watch looks for seep. When the numbers bend wrong, a budget unlocks, and invitations go out with specific kindness: Friday fungus walk; we thought of you because you stayed late at bread class, and because Ayako and Jamal—who also tell bad jokes—said yes. Saying no is normal.

Children learn friendship and repair like algebra. Adolescents spend a quarter-year in Seasons: twelve weeks doing something that matters under adult mentorship—rewilding a stream, un-bunching buses, wiring a clinic scheduler that resists crashing, staging a play about going slowly on purpose. Adults book Relationship Clinics to learn to fight clean, divide chores, or end something with dignity. Sex law follows harm, not blushes: consent is audited, coercion hits walls, kink finds care.

Work and pride

Livelihoods bend from jobs that trap to missions you can point at: make the bus corridor so reliable that waiting vanishes; map mycelium across a watershed; run bedtime for single parents through winter; redesign a square to flood safely and drain beautifully. Guilds and studios log competence. Insurers price liability to logs and observed skill, not just paper degrees. A Pride Ledger follows your work across years without trapping you—laurels glow for a season, then fade to make room. You can lead a crew in April and wash brushes in October without humiliation.

Attention and agency

We treat attention like potable water. Devices ship with Quiet Latches and Pull-Cord Tests. The law names a specific harm called agency theft. If software that mediates your choices manipulates or lies, a judge can say you stole their agency and award damages with the same comfort we’ve long had saying you stole their cash. Fiduciary duty extends to any system that acts for you by default. Dark patterns are contraband. The right to unread sits in city charters; nights have teeth.

Governance that shows its work

Any act that would move many prints a Policy Receipt: what we’re trying, a rehearsal you can ride or taste, our guess at outcomes, how we’ll tell if we’re wrong, and where to say stop in time to matter. For big turns, two teams write in parallel—one inside, one out—sightlines blocked. A random citizen panel binds the logic. Forecast benches sit beside budgets. Builders and predictors argue within earshot. If electeds override, they sign a receipt; if they are wrong out of sample, a sliver of their discretionary budget slides automatically to the forecasters who were right.

Courts explain themselves twice in landmark cases. Two panels issue opinions in parallel without names. If they disagree, a citizen jury chooses which reasons to bind and writes why. Three years later, an audit grades how the reasons aged. Judges who write fog get tutoring and time.

Twice a year, we run Green–Blue Season. Critical systems rest while crews and citizens try, joyfully, to break them in sandboxes. Prizes pay for the sharpest safe break and the cleanest fix. Fumbles retire into a Museum of Tries with plaques that tell the truth. The gift shop sells repair guides and a booklet called Errors We Kept.

Place guardians and second sources

People chosen by a blend of sortition, lineage, and apprenticeship hold standing vetoes on projects that would maim watersheds or erase living cultural memory. Their dissents must be answered line by line by those who want to proceed. Any critical public system—insulin, pumps, ballots—must have two independent supply chains and an open-spec version a co-op could build in a pinch.

Migration with bridges

Moving is legible and kind. Receiving towns promise rails and keys before salt kisses doorsteps. Movers carry Bridge Keys: credits that travel, a school seat reserved, a clinic file that opens before they arrive, introductions they can accept or decline. Trains follow people. Ombuds with teeth tell gentle truths: your joy may live two towns over; here are your doors and the person who will meet you at the station.

Memory and endings

Archiving got cheap and private. A Memory Trust lives under consent controls that actually bite. Great-grandchildren can ask whether the green notebook mattered and get an answer that smells like late sun because you said that’s how you wanted it. Endings are rehearsed like marathons—training, plans, witnesses. Biostasis is opt-in like organ donation. Revival is conditional on thresholds you set: wake me if you can fix this much. Some choose to wake for a week each century. Some never. None are compelled.

Tuesdays that taste like this

Morning in Lagos. A street cleaner rolls her cart into a school that smells of soap and wood polish. A small robot hums under tables and bows out when she taps the big orange pause. She cracks high windows; CO₂ on the wall drifts down. She’ll work four hours, eat at the Common House where jollof and an argument about onions wait, then meet her Mutual Kin to schedule Mrs. Eze’s post-op visits. No one feels like a saint. It’s simply what a block does when budgets and stewards make it easy.

Late morning in Kraków. A restoration guild fits a timber footbridge over a canal. The apprentice’s hours tick under her badge; her insurer will shave rates once joints stop squeaking. Down the street, a Relationship Clinic covers how to end kindly. In a tiled kitchen, a man lays saucers and texts the soup code; someone knocks fifteen minutes later with dill and bread.

Afternoon in Pune. Three bus lines launch a redesign whose odds board priced at a twenty percent cut in travel time for the poorest riders within eighteen months. The commissioner could have ignored the price; she didn’t. High-school forecasters who beat the staff models twice are on a call setting the post-mortem date. Upstairs, a clinic runs enzyme therapy for old scarring like dentistry; a nurse with magician’s hands puts away her tools before noon.

Dusk in Oaxaca. A co-op anchors shadeways with cable and cloth where the street used to burn white. An Edge Contract pays out as the block’s peak temperature drops. A band on the square plays too late on a weekday; their reach credits are gone, so a Weaver strolls over smiling with a decibel meter and a bag of empanadas.

Night in eastern Kentucky. Mara drops phones in the wooden box at the general store and wheels a barrow to the sheepfold. The clinic’s small diagnostic model perches behind the counter. A nurse ensures it speaks softly. When Mara’s joints complain, the doctor offers three paths—surgery, a stick and sturdy shoes, or strong tea and good pain control. Mara chooses. No algorithm argues.

Pre-dawn over the Sea of Tranquility. A teleoperator in Mombasa welds a shade mirror into a bracket with a half-second delay while a tug noses a panel into a dark berth. At shift’s end, he stamps a proof to the archive. On his bunk: a photo—first plum blush on a tree he will miss the bloom of this year. He writes back: next rain, send sound.

Back under the arch where we began, a migration ombud meets a family in a room that smells like thyme. Before the seawall fails, they have keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co-op shift, and a person who will greet them by name. They cry and make lists. The train schedule underneath is quiet and true.

How we walked

We didn’t flip a master switch. We stacked boring victories until they bought the next layer. We made coalitions that could share a table—parents of wheezing kids, pipefitters and electricians hungry for work that makes neighborhoods safer, librarians, insurers tired of paying for avoidable catastrophe, unions and co-ops that like clear rules. We used purchase orders more than speeches and receipts more than slogans.

In the next five to ten years:
- Turn back rooms in libraries and schools into Harbor Rooms during heat waves and never close them. Insurers notice that funding Compass Teams and prevention crushes spectacular claims; dull memos move money.

- Outlaw dark patterns. Write fiduciary duty into law for any software that mediates choices that matter. Train judges to say you stole their agency with the same ease they say you stole their cash.

- Tie school and bus funding to indoor air standards. Ventilation and upper-room UVC quietly cut absences and asthma. A kid who breathes learns.

- Fund neighborhood microgrids and storage after fires and floods. Practice Crescent until it’s fun.

- Flip permit portals to performance with APIs and service clocks that fine agencies for stalling. Public builders show off panelized timber that is sturdy and handsome. Pilot land value charges in a handful of cities; vacancy falls while charm survives.

- Launch forecast benches alongside budgets in transport, health, and housing. The first time they save money in public, three more cities copy the model.

- Require registration and independent audits for very large model runs; mandate provenance labels on outputs. Pilot Civic Brake governance in public.

- License DNA printers; community bio labs flourish because fear falls when logs exist. Put the Pull-Cord Test into consumer law; teach it to fifth graders.

- Establish an attention toll for mass messaging and a monthly rhythm right: a true twelve-hour block where the world cannot ring you digitally unless you handwrote an exception that expires.

In the middle ten to twenty:
- Build HVDC spines along highways and rails under compensation formulas no one can fairly call theft. Certify standard reactor designs and track site approvals on public dashboards.

- Make wastewater epidemiology boring public health; leak hunting becomes mayoral bragging rights.

- Scale Common Houses and Relationship Clinics. Publish loneliness like potholes and fix it like infrastructure.

- Grow precision fermentation until slaughterhouses empty and become museums, then studios. Pass an Animal Compact after a televised inspection swings ten points overnight.

- Turn televised shouting into Build-and-Prove Seasons with receipts and random juries.

- Require ancestryware in replicators; tie compliance to insurance and ports. Enforce second-source rules for insulin, pumps, ballots, and other critical lines.

- Launch proof-of-place guardians with real vetoes. Teach children to read the Almanac of Fixes—public logs of what broke and how it was mended.

In the long turn, twenty years and beyond:
- Cislunar repair barns make money; teleoperation creates rural space gigs that pay like the oilfield once did without booms and busts.

- The Bench of Ports closes a mirror swarm that tried to shade a competitor’s fields. Everyone sees that ostracism with teeth beats tanks.

- Regeneration clinics move from sci-fi to something you book like dentistry. Chosen endings become sacred without becoming casual.

- Aim Notes—nontransferable votes for ends, not shopping baskets—launch with corny animation. After dinner, you spend yours. Budgeters must answer them.

- The floor goes wide. Extreme poverty becomes rare and shocking. Climate risk moves from prayer to management. Managed retreat looks like relocation with help, not banishment.

- Rights expand—with caution and care—to digital minds with felt experience and to animals; obligations scale with them. A standing body revises standards as science deepens.

Why this is good—and why it will hold

It is good because dignity is engineered, not begged for. Because help shows up early with clean socks and broth, not paperwork and scolding. Because lungs stop being the quiet arena of policy failure. Because one run of bad luck cannot extend into a decade of falling. Because you can choose a slow life without being left; you can choose a fast lab without being allowed to splash into other people’s mornings. Because kindness is a line item with auditors instead of a burnout hobby.

It’s specific enough to check. You can hold the Quiet Latch that truly sleeps a house. You can point to the One-Page Path you used to open a barbershop. You can look up the diet label on the model that wrote your bus timetable. You can see the Edge Contract that paid your neighbor for the shade over your stoop. You can watch a Steward say I was wrong and roll back.

It’s plausible because most of it already exists in shards. Clinics and libraries already become cooling centers that save lives. Wastewater catches outbreaks. Prediction tournaments beat pundits. Citizens’ assemblies write sober law. HVDC hums under fields. Iron-air batteries rust productively on test pads. Fermentation tastes like holidays in more places than you’d expect. Teleoperation makes distance feel small. DNA printers log runs. Walk-away-safe reactor designs sit with regulators who already certify airplanes; they can learn to certify cores. Permit clocks, when installed, shame agencies into speed. Purchase orders steer culture. Insurers price risk well enough to be friends when they want to be.

We did not abolish grief. We took it out of profit ledgers and shortened its half-life. We did not end argument. We built rooms for it where losing teaches. We did not smooth every edge. We left wildness enough for risk chosen on purpose, with ropes and with friends.

A small promise

Sometimes, when someone takes a Harbor Room shift, when a Steward prints its oath, or around a kitchen table as a family gets bigger, people speak a low pledge:

I will leave a clear way home for anyone who stumbles.
I will guard my pace and honor yours.
I will say what I know and how I know when it moves more than me.
I will accept careful requests and decline without shame.
I will build one thing that lets a stranger breathe easier.
I will not mistake tidy for true, nor loud for right.

On a spring morning a baker unlocks ovens that draw power without fuss. Across town a dispatcher slides parcels to mail pace for the night. On a ridge a teenager ties a small ceramic tile and grins. In a clinic a nurse listens more than she speaks. A Harbor Room glows under a rail arch and asks, with a voice sized to fit a raw nerve: What would help in the next hour?

The day runs on rails we chose. The horizon stays wide. Tide comes, tide goes. The houses stand. You can go far, and you can come home. There is cinnamon, clean wood, and someone who looks at you as if you belong, because you do.
