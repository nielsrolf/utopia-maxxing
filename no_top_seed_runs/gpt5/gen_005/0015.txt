The Loom Compact

Just before the heat wakes, the city loosens its shoulders. Leaves let go of night water, gutters hum, and your windows slide open two fingers wider because you asked them to do that whenever pollen is low and the ozone sensor on your block is quiet. Nothing guesses for you. The house follows settings you wrote on a slow Sunday, and anything that would like to move your attention asks with a visible receipt and a clock.

Your ear offers three small invitations. The night herons are still on the rail by the weir; your glasses can paint them warm against the dawn if you consent. The clinic notes a safe rise in your uncle’s inflammation around a healing hip and will knock on his door at ten. The Welcome House three streets over needs a pair of hands and your Spanish for an hour; a wildfire fifty miles away sent three buses your way while you slept. You accept the hour. You choose to find the herons by chance.

The Loom Compact is not a single law or a single network. It is a way of building places and tools so that ordinary life is full without being frantic, and power can be checked without kneeling to a single mind. We pinned it to five standing promises, and then we bent suburbs, servers, bylaws, budgets, and orbits until they kept those promises by default.

The five promises

- A floor you can stand on. Everyone receives a co-owner’s dividend, not a parent’s allowance. It pays for shelter that breathes, food you can cook, healthcare with teeth and time to die well, bandwidth enough to belong, school you can return to, and the right to move. The dividend comes from renting what no one made—land, airwaves, orbits, river mouths, and high-end compute—so it cannot be yanked by a boss or an election rumor.

- Consent that travels. No system—commercial or civic—may shape your choices or moods without explicit, time-boxed permission you can revoke from your sleeve. The right to refuse is as practical as a stop button and as enforceable as a lien.

- Safety you can verify. Where harm could scale, we built rituals, not faith. High-power computation above a threshold is licensed and metered at the wall. Self-copying devices wear visible safety lattices. Any lab or utility can be stopped within its signed domain by a triple-key that lives in three different hands.

- Many ways to live, with bright borders. We built places that choose different paces and tolerances, wrote their vows down, and then made the edges crisp so that different choices don’t bleed into one fight.

- Meaning as infrastructure. First doors for people in storms. Long eyes for work that needs proof. Rooms and rituals for contention that builds instead of burning.

Belts and gates

We learned to separate trains of meaning so that a tool built for one theatre doesn’t crash others. The world we can reach is arranged by purpose.

- The Hearth Belt is our ground and close sky. Its covenant is human-scale sense. Phones and stores obey the No-Pretend Rule: narrow, helpful, unsentimental; interfaces cannot seduce; you must say yes twice to open any bottomless scroll. Sharp pleasures are behind honest gates and go cold if you haven’t slept.

- The Build Belt is where we practice large craft. Orbital yards, asteroid diggers, high mirrors that throw winter light to high valleys. Stints here are contracts with returns built in: training, wages, time to come home. Where independent nonhuman minds choose to live and work, they do so under charters that grant rights and boundaries and that places opt into or decline in public.

- The Tale Net is the made world of data and story turned spacious: salons and labs in near-orbit and cool cells on Earth that support reversible lives—places to try on heavier bodies or lighter, rehearse hard conversations, write oceans with two fingers of gravity and a wind you can sing to. Doors back to trees and bus stops are clear and open.

- The Reserve Trust is not a place we touch. It is deeds to futures we are not allowed to spend: cometary water, bands of rock, frequencies we leave to our grandchildren. Unsealing a deed is possible, slow, and costs real face and money in view of everyone.

Between belts are Genre Gates and Looking Glasses. Genre Gates hold a line: your work mail does not summon help from a trillion-parameter poet without your clear consent; children’s books do not smuggle casino math. Looking Glasses hang at thresholds—leaving school, changing work, adding a child, retiring. They let you hear from people like you who stepped over and how it felt five and ten years later. Some carry letters from your own other tries. You can look, pass, leap, or back away; all four choices are clean.

Blocks you can join and leave without spite

Within Hearth cities and Build habitats, public life is organized into Charter Blocks. A Block is a voluntary covenant: posted norms and budgets you consent to when you join. One block outlaws private cars and guarantees that a child on a scooter will reach a river without crossing a lane of traffic. Another funds pianos and a library that never sleeps. A third bakes ambition into law and runs 24/7 childcare above a welding floor. You join with a signature and a share of common goods: water plant, flood meadows, mesh network, print shops. You can leave with thirty days’ notice and take your personhood and dividend with you.

That design drains poison from politics. Instead of fighting for one answer, we try many that can copy each other’s strengths. Leaving is ordinary, so spite turns lazy. A nurse with a baby moves from the loud block to the quiet one for three winters and then back without losing their civic identity.

The Return Ledger

The dividend flows from renting exclusive use of what belongs to everyone: ground, coasts, mineral seams, orbital slots, radio bands, and expensive compute. Bidding is public; hoarding is costly; sitting on an empty downtown lot is expensive; improving an old warehouse is cheap. Half of the rent funds the bones—drains, grid, parks, tending—half lands as the monthly co-owner’s draw.

We retired long copyright and heavy patents. Designs and songs live in the Return Ledger: an open record of who used what to make what. If your chair uses my joint or your model uses my trick, you pay 15% of the value my trick added into my pot. The Verifiers’ Guild—auditors, model-checkers, field measurers—maintains the real numbers behind the Ledger. The trap is social: a dashboard called Who Gave shows who pays and who stiffs. Cheats lose credit lines and their calls stop getting returned.

The Verifiers’ Guild and the Measures stream

We made truth into a utility. The Guild is a salaried corps of auditors and measurers, paid from rent and insulated by fixed terms, rotation, and audit of the auditors. They do not pass judgment. They estimate and explain. When a block says, “This bus plan halves asthma,” the Guild clamps monitors, watches numbers, and publishes bell curves with words a fifteen-year-old can follow. A Measures stream hums quietly at the edge of ordinary life; most of us read it because it feels good to act on live numbers. Appeals courts review not just verdicts but reasoning that did or didn’t age, and the Guild reports on both.

Cheap trust without a single god

Contracts used to be expensive because trust was. We made it cheap without giving a single machine the last word.

- Deal Foundries turn two parties’ intents into contracts that run. You state constraints; it proposes options that satisfy both; it shows the good-looking variation you rejected and what that cost you.

- Accord Explorers can be summoned by councils wrangling over air, water, or power. They simulate compliance and cheating and show their work. The councils choose knowing, not guessing.

- Identity and reach are pried apart. You can say almost anything as almost anyone. You cannot push it into everyone’s breakfast without burning scarce stamps: hours from your monthly allotment, tokens tied to verified contributions, and narrow caps that prevent flood. Speech is free. Reach is earned.

Attendants that help and stop

Inside kitchens and pockets live Attendants: narrow, audited machines that never pretend to feel. They obey the No-Pretend Rule: if a design correlates with subjective experience, it is forbidden for daily tools. Their memory is thin; their reward loops are boring; they go cold when fuses trip. An Attendant can draft a motion, book a truck, or deflect a habit you told it to help you stop. It cannot be your spouse and may not act like one. If you want a mind that can disappoint you, find one and make a promise.

Work that stings the right way

A tight job market is a public good. Blocks publish vacancy ratios. If ratios sag, councils issue public work orders—insulation days, elder visits, creek mending—and set wages just high enough to make private employers compete. The central bank’s dashboard in your phone targets slack the way it once targeted inflation.

Credentials are thin; demonstrations are thick. Want to cut hair? Show five good cuts and clean tools. Want to write code? Ship a small tool someone can lean on. Hazardous trades keep exams, but testing is external; the school cannot sell the license.

Matching is a profession. Placers interview you, watch you on two small jobs, talk with other Placers, and place you. If you’re still there and glad six months and three years later, they get paid; if they painted the fit, they lose their license. They are as ordinary as insurance agents and as trusted as librarians.

Education became challenge design. Everyone gets a tireless, nonsentient tutor. Teachers design sequences that live one notch past comfort. Every adolescent spends one season of the year in a real-stakes project under adult hands. They rebuild a streambed. They rewrite a clinic’s intake so no one chokes on shame. They write and stage a play that costs them something. Alumni chats still light with minnows in year nine.

Friction where reasons change you

Mind and body edits are permitted behind guardrails that respect identity. Before strong changes, you consult a simulated future-self grown from your data that is honest about your likely regrets. You sleep on it. You speak with a Keeper circle—three humans with training and tools and patience—who are paid to be your floor. They cannot veto you. They can say, “Try the low dose first and sleep on it in a different Block.” Many leap. Many thank them later for the pause.

Forking yourself is common in Tale Net rooms. Fork Etiquette is simple: you don’t interrogate other branches; you don’t demand someone bear memories they did not choose; custody and consent carry templates; families make rituals for rejoining. Courts learned to keep records that treat forks as persons and persons as branches.

Justice without humiliation

Most of what used to be “crime” routes to better-fit Blocks or clinics. A stubborn class remains. The justice system is unbundled: those who detain do not interrogate and do not prosecute. Each is paid for what matters—corroborated facts, long-run harm reduction without recidivism. Judges write plain language and get audited three years on. There are still prisons, fewer and smaller. There are Calm Wards: low-stimulation Blocks with predictable loops for people for whom standard city life is a scrape. Entry and exit are both voluntary with conditions.

Power like water, tools like bread

The hinge was electrons. We standardized small reactors and made them dull; we threaded continents with high-voltage lines; we opened deep geothermal with narrow-AI drilling that sidesteps faults. Permitting sped because regulators and engineers learned to write “known-good” patterns a city could adopt with one vote; bad patterns died quickly.

Production rematerialized. Microfactories the size of garages turn a few feedstocks into most of what a town needs: furniture, scooters, pumps, gears. Villages far from ports can remake themselves without begging a container ship. When a thing must cross an ocean, a coordination mesh finds a boat already pointed that way.

The most dangerous compute is licensed like fissile material: nosy oversight across borders; chip serials and power meters that write to third-party vaults; time-boxed runs in supervised labs; triple-key stop switches. We did not outlaw math. We made daylight pay.

The kernel underfoot

All civic machines that can make consequential decisions—traffic lights, water taps, identity hubs—run on open civic firmware updated on a slow schedule ordinary people can follow. The kernel supplies a predictable floor: lights no longer optimize against your patience in secret; identity systems cannot flip affordances in the night. Above the floor, messy human games continue.

Prompt Cards and Build Meets

Anyone can send a Prompt Card to a person or Block: “Switch your trolleys to this motor; particulates fall by a quarter; it pays back in three years.” “Mentor this kid on Tuesdays.” “Stop planting male-only street trees; the pollen is choking us.” Your card only lands if the Measures stream thinks you’re plausibly relevant, and only if you post a small bond that burns if your claim was cooked. You can always refuse. Acceptances are logged quietly. Your Keeper sees your yes-rate if and only if you asked them to.

Budgets are built in Build Meets instead of tribe wars. Anyone can submit a project. If your plan survives two rounds of peer review and a public reasoning test, it gets a slice of budget proportional to predicted lift—fewer hours in line, cleaner air, faster buses. If it hits targets, it renews and you and your backers receive a prize. If it misses, it sunsets and you lose face. People watch like sports and then go home to small quarrels and string beans.

A day under the Loom

Kira lives on the fifth floor of a car-free Block that cares about dogs and silence. The co-owner’s dividend covers half their rent in a courtyard building with pear trees and a sump that drank the last flood with a long sigh. The Placer who found the house still gets a drip, because the fit held.

Kira is a civil craftsperson—half hydrologist, half storyteller. Their Attendant folds out a one-page brief: a storm angling north; a meeting moved to the glass room of the library because the light is better; two ways to untangle an easement; three scripts for a hard talk with a contractor who missed steel delivery. Kira circles the version that matches their style. The Attendant files the plan and disappears. It will never whisper that Kira is its favorite.

At ten Kira rides to the print shop. A thirteen-year-old on her second season stands at a table saw with a badge that opens the guard and not the planer. She’s making birdhouses and a small weir gate for the rewilded creek. Lunch is buttered noodles and a Prompt Card ping: pollen index is rising; an elder down the street posted a bond and asked the parks office to stop buying male-only saplings. Kira taps accept. It’s small, with honest math.

In the afternoon, a branch of Kira attends a Tale Net seminar in a low-gravity hall with organ pipes the size of oak trunks. The cohort is reopening a food hall flooded last spring; last week two members were rightly angry at a missed deadline. Today, before schedules, they replay the negotiation with everyone’s consent. The system highlights phrases that escalate; it offers three calmer starts from similar cases. They pick one, try again, and feel that odd, welcome softening when a hard thing gets a handle.

At five, Kira walks to the creek she and her old gang straightened a decade ago and then unstraightened with elders. Native grasses came back in year three. Foxes in year five. Hawks in year nine. After the hardest rain in fifty years, the water ran brown for a day and then clear. Some of those teenagers have children who throw pebbles into riffles; when the alumni thread lights with minnows, a few still cry.

At seven, Kira attends a Build Meet that will stripe bus-only lanes on three arterials. Another Block did this last year. Buses sped 31%. Shop sales dipped 12% for six months and then climbed 10%. The Guild says there’s a 70% chance your place will rhyme. Proposers posted a bond to pay shops if they’re wrong by more than five points. Hands rise. Feet move. Budgets shift.

Before bed, Kira opens a Looking Glass seeded for leaving this decade’s work. Peers like them took a three-month leave to tend a parent; others doubled down; a few signed on for a Build Belt stint. The letters are frank. A Keeper’s nudge whispers at the edge: “Sleep on it. Try a sampler tomorrow—the low-dose of feeling steadier under pressure—and take the interview you’ve dodged.” Kira smiles. The Attendant adds nothing. It is not that sort of companion.

How we got here

From now to this world runs a bridge of boring and brave.

- In three years: ten mid-sized cities stop taxing improvements and start leasing resource claims—ground, permits to occupy scarce airwaves, curb space, roof radiance, and compute cycles above a wattage threshold. They pay a small dividend, buy down floods with wetlands and drains, and discover property values can rise for reasons other than gambling. They pilot Charter Blocks as voluntary covenants with opt-in fees and thirty-day exits. Schools run two-week seasons each term. A civil coalition funds a Verifiers’ Office; local papers hire explainers who publish causal chains instead of outrage theater.

- In five: national law allows cities to collect rent on resource claims and reduce taxes on work. Legislators carve a path for short, nonrenewable copyright; open designs with Return Ledger payments; external licensing markets. Deal Foundries become cheap. Public platforms separate speech from reach; floods of plausible lies face friction without gagging voices.

- In eight: the central bank publishes a labor slack target; cities get tools to issue public work orders when vacancy ratios sag. Microfactories spread; dull, safe home robots come in hardware stores. Permitting for small reactors and deep geothermal adopts “known-good” patterns; bad projects die faster. The first Registered Compute Pool logs chips and power draw above set thresholds. Courts begin to demand influence receipts: products that move moods must carry timers and reasons; the first Right to Refuse Nudges Act passes.

- In ten: the No-Pretend Rule for everyday tools becomes law. Civic firmware runs in traffic lights, sewers, and identity services; updates slow to cadences people can track. Prompt Cards launch in three regions with bonds. Placer licenses spread. The co-owner’s dividend grows enough in most Blocks to make street-sleeping rare where exits are real.

- In fifteen: the Belts Compact is signed before the first heavy yard opens in orbit. The Hearth vows quiet code; the Build Belt vows rights for any nonhuman minds where invited; the Tale Net vows Looking Glasses and Genre Gates. The Reserve deeds are published with cryptographic commitments staked by probes and registries. Accord Explorers are invoked for crossing rivers; water wars begin to look like antique sins.

- Alongside: Keeper circles become a profession and an app with liability and training. Fork etiquette normalizes. Calm Wards open. Courts shift to written reasoning with third-year audits. Dividends climb when compute and spectrum rents kick in. Adolescents expect to spend one season every other year doing something that changes their town. Reparations become dividends with names attached; land returns to Indigenous trusts with budgets to tend memory and bread at once.

We improved on older dreams by making safety aspirational and touchable. The kernel is not a scold; it is a floor that lets courage climb. The No-Pretend Rule is not metaphysics; it is checklists tied to code and chips. The Belts Compact is not poetry; it is power metering, chip serials, and stop signals that no one can quietly cut.

Security without empire

We are not naive about danger. We built for failing soft.

In a valley lab a new drive breathes like a lung. They ask for a wider test corridor. A coral trust says the tides will hate the shadow. A roving bench meets on a dock. Two civic models—sworn transparent—draw harms in colors underwater. Fishers, divers, and children talk about what healthy smells like. Risk captains concede points against their own side because that is their job. The bench publishes a decision with footnotes. The lab shifts tests to a darker orbit. No soldiers fly.

A county leans toward keeping girls home from school. They call it tradition. The dividend is held from council hands and rerouted as lunch, books, and pay direct to girls and their teachers. Women from a next county arrive by day to teach in a church and leave with escorts by dusk. Some men stall. Some fold. A few leave for other shores. They do not find new homes where rule without service is fashionable. The world has moved on.

A lab breaks spoiler etiquette and posts a protein twist that could have gone badly because drama sells. Their access is cut. Their mistake writes itself into the Failure Ledger. Their next year’s budget moves to their sharpest critic with a charge to audit and teach in public. They swallow pride and spend a year letting high schoolers argue with them. We remember the breach and the bend.

Economy without humiliation

The economy runs on three levels that talk.

- The floor is dividends and pointed automation in necessities: food, shelter, heat, care, connectivity. No one makes rent by making another person frantic.

- The fabric is a million co-ops and firms running cafés, building tools, scoring Build Meets, mending, keeping turbines quiet. Markets are good at countless small decisions on a level field. We keep the field level. Wealth buys more art and more time, not more votes, not immunity. That more than any bracket ended the old humiliations.

- The frontier is high variance: orbit, language, biotech, art. Risk capital is capped to prevent permanent castes and taxed to keep beauty cheap even when sold. Ventures report incentive tables like nutrition labels. If they lie, they pay triple.

Migration feels like seasons. Schools on the river of movement train you to be the neighbor someone would choose and then introduce you to neighborhoods that chose. Solidarity visas knit remittances and dividends for people who stand with you in storms you did not cause.

Attention like air

Public option feeds and directories do not shout or mine. Private feeds can dance; the public ones are there to come home to. Quiet zones have legal teeth: counties can ban face recognition, refuse gig markets that mine panic, and outlaw toys that wake small children’s attention too early. Borders are lines systems respect: walk into a quiet county and your devices hand their sharp edges to the local network and then go still.

Why this is good

It refuses the lazy bargain between shrines and stars. On a Tuesday you can sing three strangers into laughing in the same breath, then walk to a creek you unstraightened with a handful of kids and elders, then vote a bus into a lane that frees a thousand mornings a day. If you want to spend half a year in a Build yard, you can, and your father can still find you in a Looking Glass and ask how your hands are.

It buys down the wrong strains—confusing interfaces, predatory loops, bureaucratic fog—and keeps the right ones—craft, courage, invention, love—hard. It does not build fake partners to make loneliness blink. It does not outsource your life. It gives you an Attendant that stays in its lane, a Keeper who shows up when you might undo yourself, and a Placer who wants you able to quit without ruin.

It makes quitting easy and reversible. Most old harms were traps: monopolies on land and truth, one-shot choices you made at twenty, invisible metrics that learned your weak joints. The Compact pries those teeth open and writes down the habits that keep them open.

It tells the truth in a way you can use. When a plan fails, the Guild says so without a sneer and shows who got paid anyway. When a Block quietly saves thousands by redesigning an intake that made people choke on shame, the Guild says that, too. Proposals compete in daylight, carrying bonds. Prestige clings to measured victories: “We cut asthma in half.” “We raised bus speeds a third.” “We built a mirror that made a thousand people sleep on it and thank us later.”

It is plausible because none of its pieces demand miracles. It is a stack of doable improvements that together change what is easy. Standardize reactors. Meter chips. Charge rent for fences around what no one made. Put measurers on salary and bolt their incentives to the floor. Write an operating system for towns you can read. Build microfactories. Teach adolescents to move a curb and a heart. Insist that the tools in your kitchen never pretend to love you. Make exits real.

At the Welcome House down the street, a cedar smell lives under citrus. Stewards meet people coming in from rain, fire, and panic with two questions anyone alive can bear: What do you need now? Who should we notify? There is a quiet wing for shocks and a bright one for papers. Quarantine without humiliation. A clerk who is also a notary and a router into groups who survived storms like yours. You carry a slip of paper that asks two of you to walk a stranger to housing when the forms are done, no questions asked. You take the slip. It is an hour of your life and a piece of your city’s pride.

At dusk your building hosts a Concord Feast. Children read aloud duties and rights. A bunch of tin grapes goes around; each charm is a season of help for someone’s project: a bakery that will keep an oven warm on nights when grief lives at your address, a burial, a bridge between two cousins who stopped speaking because both were right. A magistrate sits near the door in case a decision needs law’s weight. It is a party, a budget, a peace conference, and mercy.

We leave our children rivers wide enough to change their minds, rooms they can borrow, woods they can join. We leave them a ledger of repair and a sky that still answers. And when they ask what we did with our luck, we show them the House doors with cedar under citrus, the Signal Houses’ stairs, and the way the city listens before it moves. We do not offer a perfect ending. We hand them a banister you can put your palm on and climb. We hand them a habit of drawing borders bright enough to respect and doors easy to find. We hand them, when they wake in fog, a small, honest hint and a culture of being asked to do things you would be proud to refuse.

This is not a promise that nothing breaks. It is a practice that keeps breaks small and repair close. It is specific enough to build, and it is already scattered in pieces around you now.
