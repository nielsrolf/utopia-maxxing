The Hearth–Horizon Compact

On a damp morning under a rail viaduct, a small ceramic tile glows the color of bread crust. A woman with kelp in her hair presses it and a lock lets go. The room she walks into is plain in the way a handrail is plain: steam, cedar, clean towels rolled like small animals. Jeans in three sizes. A sweater that could be hers. Sandals with tags you can snip if you keep them. A pot of broth whispers on a warmer, a slice of lemon riding the rim. The person at the counter looks up, not down, and asks the two questions that fit into a frightened mind without overflowing it: What would help in the next hour? Who should we tell that you’re safe?

A washer that finishes on time turns on. A cot clicks and locks from the inside. There’s a drawer of chargers that fit, labeled in a way that makes you exhale. A map on the wall has three rings: places that can hold you for a night, a week, a season, all within reach of a train. Bills that would otherwise begin stacking in your absence pause until you can read them with steady hands. If you ask, a four-person Compass Team wraps around you for a while and then, as you can carry your own weight, steps back:

- A fiduciary advocate that lives in your vault as a small, legally loyal piece of software. If it betrays you, a court reads it like a treasurer who stole.

- A clinician who begins with sleep, pain, and fear before forms.

- A long-view guide who keeps future-you in the room and helps negotiate with the present you at a kitchen table that smells like citrus and salt.

- A quartermaster who turns twelve errands into one Tuesday and, when you ask for potatoes, checks you have a pot that works.

This door is one of millions. The tile with the bread-crust glow marks a Beacon Room. From these rooms you can infer the shape of the world that built them: a compact between the places where we sleep and laugh and the places where we build strange, large things; bridges between them that belong to you.

Two rooms with a lock you hold

Life travels between two generous rooms joined by a lock only you can open.

- Hearthlands are blocks and kitchens and parks where stitches hold, where you know the cereal your neighbor buys and the name of the kid who skates badly and bravely. The rules that touch bodies, bedrooms, and attention are strict, dull, and fair. Help arrives without theater. Emergency is a drill we practice as a party.

- Horizons are for projects too big or odd for a block: orchards under gentle spin in an old cargo ring, cold compute stacks in polar night that do proofs you can check, protein mills that make feast without harm, alloys grown where gravity forgets to sag. The first rule of Horizons is no waves into breakfast. The second is receipts you can read.

The lock between them is not a poem. It is plumbing—keys, contracts, customs, and courts—that let curiosity cross and make coercion fall flat. You can go far; you cannot be dragged.

Five promises, written on plugs and contracts

The Compact hangs on five promises we teach to children, etch into grants, and bind to ports, power, and spectrum. To sell to a town, to use the grid spine, to float a satellite over a region, you sign for them.

- Safe descent. A run of bad days cannot erase your life. Floors exist, and they hold. Exits exist, and they don’t drop you through.

- Living consent. Any rule that touches your body, home, mind, or attention rides on consent that breathes—understanding checked in your words, cooling time scaled to the stakes, and a way back from everything but the ending you insist upon.

- Clear steering. When a system big enough to move many chooses—routes, budgets, rates—it owes reasons in words a teenager can follow, with links to the numbers beneath and a date when we look again.

- Soft attention. Unasked-for grabs at your mind are expensive. Nights have true off. Persuasion ships with a manifest that says what it aims to tug and which data it used.

- Open doors. Homes, schools, clinics, studios, even households: you can leave without ruin. Practices for leaving and complaining are public; exits can’t hide.

Rights you can touch

We turned good ideas into fixtures that click and hum.

- The Night Switch—an oval of cool metal by every front door and workbench—slides rooms and accounts into Quiet. Anything that listens or persuades sleeps unless it’s on a handwritten list you taped next to the switch. Breaking that quiet requires a human you named saying your name three times. Unsanctioned alerts pay a small toll into your block’s budget unless you whitelist them.

- The Off Test is written into law. Any tool that operates in a home, classroom, clinic, shop, or civic stack must demonstrate to a fifth-grader how to pause, audit, and resume without revenge. Pass–fail, visible on a card riveted to the device.

- The Path-on-a-Page. To appeal a bill, form a shop, fix a deed, or claim a benefit, you can demand a one-page path that works without a consultant. Service clocks run. If an agency misses its own, money moves in a way that you can feel.

- The Right to Company. Ask in any Beacon Room, and you’re paired with a Compass Team for a season. They advise; they cannot vote for you. Their loyalty runs to you, not an agency metric.

- The Right to Understood Risks. Big turns—surgeries, mortgages, software that steers you—climb Deliberate Steps: a plain-language explainer and a teach-back in your words; a cool-down scaled to stakes; a two-key crossing with a human you trust; a rehearsal you can wake from; and a path to reverse what is not an ending you insisted upon.

- The Right to Air. CO₂ and humidity sit on the wall next to the calendar. MERV-13 or better in public rooms. Upper-room UVC in clinics and buses, tuned to bite viruses without biting throats. Schools and trains breathe better than most hospitals once did.

The floor you can feel

We stopped pretending scarcity trains virtue. We built a floor that rides with your civic key; you do not apply for it. It is not a mood. It is a list:

- A modest apartment that locks, with light you can read by and a door you can close. Units can move with you when water rises or family shapes change; moving costs ride under the floor.

- Staples you can serve with pride. Clean water that never tastes like a warning. A pass that crosses regions.

- Power for light, cold, a humane band of heat and cool. Blocks practice Mooncrest once a season: streetlights dim to crescents, fridges hum, oxygen machines run, stoves cook. Blackstarts are suppers, not sirens.

- Primary care and mental health as house calls when it’s smarter. Nurses with magician’s hands paid more than software that nags.

- A compute credit next to your electric bill, enough to study, automate care, run a small shop, drive a sanding robot across your week, sing—or to do nothing with at all.

- Time in public workshops. A lathe with a patient guard standing nearby. A kiln with a handbook that explains right next to the clay. Instruction is part of the tool. So are a broom and a sign-up ledger.

We pay for this with rent on the things no one made alone: the site value under our feet; spectrum lanes; orbital slots; river mouths and old seams; deep wind and hard sun; general-purpose models and factories that hum at night. Half of that revenue pours cement and wire into bones—drains, clinics, spines, cranes. Half arrives as a Commons Share. When your earnings dip, a negative income tax fills the hollow automatically. If a market behaves like a club for long, a public option opens next door, sells at cost until the kink relaxes, then leaves with a thank-you sign.

When automation sands a trade, stabilizers grab. Displaced workers receive equity in the gains and paid time in guild schools. A tight job market is treated like a utility: if vacancy ratios droop, councils issue work orders for insulation retrofits, tree planting, elder visits, and floodplain restoration at wages that force private employers to compete.

Spines and shade

We stopped pretending energy, water, air, bandwidth, and shade were luxuries some buy and some go without. We built spines and invited play on top.

Energy arrived from everywhere. Roof films clicked on in an afternoon; neighborhood geothermal loops slithered under streets; rock where it ran hot learned how to share. Walk-away-safe reactors were licensed like aircraft: certify a design that passes bruising tests, then site many with checklists about geology and egress, not philosophy. Ridge wind and offshore stacks stitched to cities with high-voltage DC that paid along the way. Storage sprawled in iron-air yards and salt caverns. The slow parts—permits and wires—we funded like bridges with service clocks that fined agencies for stalling.

Water got adult attention. Daytime desalination drank oversupply. Mains tattled on leaks to a public map. Wastewater labs sat next to turbidity gauges and watched for disease; when graphs scowled, a soft light on your wall suggested skipping a concert. Buyouts that used to feel like banishment turned into Bridge Keys that cut new keys before old ones were taken.

Air joined the rights list. Buses and classrooms cleaned the air they moved through. Asthma calmed; absences fell without speeches. Coolth got a budget and a word we didn’t have before: shadeways braided through hot districts—vines, fabric canopies, porous mist that cooled without drenching. Roofs in heat belts went pale on purpose.

Networks treated you like a person. Identity is proofs of personhood instead of biographies dragged to every counter. Bots don’t vanish; they simply don’t count in rooms that promise flesh.

Edges and ledgers

We put numbers at the seams where ways of living rub.

Sensors counted what they could count: fine particles, late-night decibels, pathogen loads in wastewater, peak spectrum use, curb CO₂. Some things we refused to price—dark in nesting season, quiet above a point, places we agreed were sacred—so we drew hard limits there. Elsewhere, an Edge Ledger made spillovers legible. Bad spillovers paid until they stopped. A stadium that kept half a county awake used to shrug; now it can dampen, go dark at midnight, or pay the people under its glare starting the same week the numbers show. Good spillovers paid, too: plant a pocket forest that lowers a block’s peak; households around it owe you credits that arrive like rain. Start a night soup that halves ambulance calls; a slice of the savings shows up in the kitchen’s account.

We wired in Spillback: a visible slice of any payment for harm flows directly, quickly to the people at the edge, not just to a budget many steps away. We wrote a Rivulet Rule for shared invention: if your work uses mine and you sell it, a small automatic slice of the marginal value my piece added flows to me unless you can show it didn’t. A salaried proof guild checks claims by surprise.

Rooms with membranes

We did not pretend one best life fits all. We built seams you can feel and choose.

Some neighborhoods chartered themselves Soft. Always-listening tools sleep by default within their borders, and human checkout lines run at a pace that feels human. Craft studios over grocery stores. Radios and looms. Emergency services still arrive burning and bright; coercion does not. Their clinics answer at midnight when knees grind.

Other neighborhoods chartered Quick. Code deadlines hum into the night with earplugs and a piano in the corner. Robots move like bicycles in bike lanes that are theirs. Spoiler mode grips your feeds when you cross the line unless your key says you know and want the flood.

Between sits the Estuary—training guilds, ports, etiquette coded as software. Cross a membrane and your device downshifts to local defaults with a banner you cannot miss: You’re entering a Soft Quarter. Biometric doorbells sleep. Or: You’re stepping into a Quick Yard. Mass-messaging follows house customs. You can override; you cannot claim you didn’t know.

Signal customs and Port Court

Bright ideas move faster than ships. We built customs for signals. Inbound blasts to Hearthlands pass through Signal Pilots—rotating juries trained to throttle floods and filter coercion. They cannot bar a citizen from choosing a firehose; curiosity is a right. They can stop one actor from buying breakfast with every screen.

Stuff still touches customs. Ports that matter—ocean, orbital, and cloud—share a narrow Bench of Ports with teeth. It asks two questions: are you acting where you claimed? are you doing what you promised? Judges rotate from places that disagree; clerks are narrow systems that check math. Misbehave and you lose keys. Launch pads say no, insurers freeze dividends, repair yards won’t talk. Wars can still happen—smaller, shorter. Most fights end when service contracts die.

Big tools with rails

We treated strong code and lively matter like heavy machinery. We apprenticed our civic software before giving it levers, and we made big bets file plans before they spun.

Regions raise Civic Stewards—narrow, auditable systems trained on open law, maps, maintenance logs, crop calendars, and the smell of a damp hallway. For a year they have no actuators and no bank account. Three circles mentor them: households (grudges and dish schedules), trades (stakes and patience), and places (what breaks first). Any circle can veto graduation. When a Steward takes its oath it prints, postcard-sized for your fridge: I explain; I pause on harm; I roll back; I say “I don’t know” and “I was wrong.”

Very large model runs file a flight log on a public ledger before a single weight updates: who runs what, using which data, for what aim, with which auditor. We check three dials: software attestation; an energy meter on the rack; a thermal “witness” that samples waste heat. Hiding a run above threshold is expensive folly. A small Civic Brake—governed by a multisignature across nations, sectors, and citizen juries—can delay a risky launch for days. It must publish reasons and dissenting math.

DNA printers above a power class report by default. Labs inventory feedstock like jewelers track stones. Replicators ship with ancestryware simple enough to prove what it will not do. Parts depots, chargers, and logistics hubs refuse to talk to liars. Self-replication outside bounded zones is a bright line you cross at your peril; strays crumble because grandparents’ keys won’t open doors.

Bright lines

We cut five lines we do not cross and tied them to ports, plugs, leases, and power.

- No involuntary mass risk. Volunteer danger is devotion. Unconsented mass exposure is negligence.

- No beings built to suffer. If we can’t be sure, we act as if they could until a standing Sentience Registry clears them.

- No unbounded self-replication in the world or on the net. Code and biology that make copies carry governors that count grandchildren and crumble if they roam.

- High-cascade work sits in glass. Pathogens, orbital systems, generalist models, swarms—logged compartments with many hands on a brake that stops in an instant.

- No ads in the sky. To float a thing, you warrant that no breakfast will be forced to look at it.

Belonging on purpose

Loneliness turned out to be a public engineering flaw, not a private shame. Each block anchors a Neighbor House: a bright kitchen that smells of toast, a tool library with a binder of fingers saved, a piano someone plays badly on Thursdays, soundproof booths, a supervised play corner, a quiet room with heavy curtains. A Weaver—trained like a teacher, paid like an engineer—keeps the place woven. With consent, they maintain a connection ledger that watches isolation like a levee watch, not a product. When numbers bend the wrong way, a budget unlocks automatically. Invitations are specific and kind: Friday fungi walk; we thought of you because you stayed late at bread class and because Omid and Raven—who also tell bad jokes—said yes. Declining is normal.

Children learn friendship and repair like algebra. Adolescents spend a quarter of their year in Seasons: twelve weeks doing something that matters under adult mentorship—rewild a stream, unbunch buses, wire a clinic scheduler that resists crashing, stage a play about slowing down. Adults book Relationship Clinics to learn to fight clean, negotiate chores, or end something with dignity. Porn law follows harm, not blushes: consent is audited; coercion finds walls; kink finds care.

Work and pride

Time is part of the floor. Everyone holds a Time Ledger—hours each month for care, learning, and community work, spendable by you or by your Mutual Kin (twenty to thirty households who sign as kin for soup, school pickups, late-night stitches). Livelihoods bend from jobs that trap to missions you can point at: fix the bus corridor until waiting vanishes; map mycelium across a watershed; run bedtime for single parents through winter; redesign a square to flood safely and drain beautifully. Guilds and studios log competence; insurers price liability to logs, not just paper degrees. A Pride Ledger follows your work across years without trapping you; laurels glow, then fade to make room. You can lead a crew in April and wash brushes in October without humiliation.

Attention and agency

We treat attention like potable water. Devices ship with Night Switches and Off Tests. The law names injury to agency. If software that mediates your choices manipulates or lies, a judge can say you stole their agency and award damages with the same comfort we’ve long said you stole their cash. Fiduciary duty extends to any system that acts for you by default. Dark patterns are contraband; unscheduled persuasion pays a neighborhood toll unless you invite it with your key.

Governance that shows its work

Any act that would move many prints a Policy Receipt: our guess; our rehearsal you can ride, taste, or use; what we’ll do if we’re wrong; where you can say stop in time to matter. For big turns, two teams write in parallel—one inside, one out—without seeing each other. A random citizen panel chooses which logic to bind. Forecast benches sit beside budgets. Builders and predictors argue within earshot. If electeds override, they sign a receipt; if they are wrong out of sample, a sliver of their discretionary budget slides automatically to the forecasters who were right.

Courts write reasons twice in big cases. Two panels issue opinions in parallel without names. If they disagree, a citizen jury chooses the logic and explains why. Three years later, an audit grades how the reasons aged. Judges who write fog get tutoring and time.

Twice a year, we run Blue–Green Season. Critical systems rest while crews and citizens try joyfully to break them in sandboxes. Prizes pay for the sharpest safe break and the cleanest fix. Fumbles retire into a Museum of Tries with plaques that tell the truth without shaming. The gift shop sells repair guides and a booklet called Errors We Kept.

Migration with bridges

Retreat from some coasts and fire belts happens with grace because receiving towns promise rails before salt kisses doorsteps. Movers carry Bridge Keys: credits that move, a school seat, a clinic file that opens before you arrive, introductions you can accept or decline. Transit follows people. Ombuds with teeth tell gentle truths: your joy may live two towns over; here are your doors and the person who will meet you.

Memory and endings

Archiving a life got cheap and private. A Memory Trust lives under consent controls that bite. Great-grandchildren can ask whether the green notebook mattered and get an answer that smells like late sun because you said that was how you wanted it. Endings are rehearsed like marathons—training, plans, witnesses. Biostasis is opt-in like organ donation. Revival is conditional on thresholds you set: wake me if you can restore this much. Some choose to wake for a week each century. Some choose never. None are compelled.

Tuesdays that taste like this

Morning in Kigali. A cleaner rolls her cart into a school that smells of lemon and wood wax. A robot hums under tables and bows out when she taps Off. She cracks transoms; CO₂ drops on a dial. She works four hours, eats at the Neighbor House where stew and an argument about basil wait, then meets her Mutual Kin to schedule Mr. Nascimento’s post-op visits. No one feels heroic. It’s simply what a block does when budgets and stewards make it easy.

Afternoon in Porto. A restoration guild fits a timber footbridge. The apprentice’s hours tick under her badge; her insurer will shave rates once joints stop squeaking. Down the street, a Relationship Clinic covers how to end kindly. In a tiled kitchen, a man sets out teacups and texts the soup code; someone knocks fifteen minutes later.

Dusk in Busan. Three bus routes launch a redesign the odds rail priced at a twenty percent time cut for the poorest riders in eighteen months. The commissioner could have ignored the price; she didn’t. High-school forecasters who beat staff models twice are on a call setting the post-mortem date. Upstairs, senescent-cell clearance runs like dentistry; a nurse with magician’s hands will be home by noon.

Night over the Mare Crisium. A teleoperator in Nairobi welds a solar truss with a half-second delay while a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth. At shift’s end, she stamps a proof to the archive. On her bunk waits a letter: I planted the plum. You will miss it bloom. She writes back: Send a photo when it slips to purple.

Under the viaduct where we began, a migration ombud meets a family in a room that smells like thyme. Before the seawall fails, they have keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co-op shift, and a person to meet them at the station. They cry. They make lists. A train schedule that tells the truth sits under their tea.

How we walked here

We did not flip a master switch. We stacked boring victories until they bought the next layer.

In the next five to ten years:
- Convert back rooms in libraries and schools into Beacon Rooms during heat waves and never close them again. Insurers notice that funding Compass Teams and prevention saves spectacular claims later and write dull memos that move money.

- Outlaw dark patterns. Impose fiduciary duty on any software that mediates choices that matter. Train judges to say you stole their agency with the same ease they say you stole their cash.

- Tie school and bus funding to indoor air standards. Ventilation and upper-room UVC cut absences and quiet asthma without ceremony.

- Fund neighborhood microgrids and storage after fires and floods. Practice Mooncrest until it’s fun.

- Flip permit portals to performance with APIs and service clocks that fine agencies for stalling. Public builders prove panelized timber can be cheap and beautiful. Pilot land value leases in a handful of cities and show vacancy falls as charm survives.

- Launch forecast benches alongside budgets in transport and public health. The first time prices save a city money in public, three more copy the model.

- Require registration and independent audits for very large model runs. Mandate provenance on model outputs. Pilot Civic Brake governance.

- License DNA printers; community bio labs flourish because fear falls when logs exist. Put Off Tests into consumer law and teach them to fifth graders.

In the middle years (ten to twenty):
- Build HVDC spines along highways and rails under compensation formulas no one can fairly call theft. Certify standard reactors; track site approvals on public dashboards.

- Make wastewater epidemiology normal public health; leak hunting becomes mayoral bragging rights.

- Create attention tariffs and Night Switches by default; add the right to unread to city charters.

- Scale Neighbor Houses and Relationship Clinics. Publish loneliness like potholes and fix it like infrastructure.

- Grow precision fermentation until slaughterhouses empty and become museums, then studios. Pass an Animal Compact after a televised inspection swings ten points overnight.

- Turn televised shouting into Build-and-Prove Seasons with receipts and random juries.

- Require ancestryware in replicators and tie compliance to insurance and ports.

In the long turn (twenty years and beyond):
- Cislunar goes industrial. Repair barns make money. Teleoperation creates rural space gigs that pay as well as oilfield work once did without the booms and busts.

- The Bench of Ports closes a mirror swarm that tried to shade a competitor’s field; everyone sees that ostracism with teeth beats tanks.

- Regeneration clinics move from sci-fi to standard; chosen endings are sacred without becoming casual.

- Aim Tokens—nontransferable votes for ends, not consumption baskets—launch with corny animation and become a ritual you join after dinner to feel like a citizen. Budgeters must answer them.

- The floor goes wide. Extreme poverty vanishes. Climate risk moves from prayer to management. Managed retreat feels like relocation with help, not banishment.

- Rights expand carefully to digital minds with felt experience and to animals; obligations scale with them.

Fixes that made everything else easier:
- Rhythm rights. Everyone holds a true monthly twelve-hour block where the world cannot ring them digitally unless they handwrote an exception that expires.

- Second sources. Any critical public system—insulin, pumps, ballots—must have two independent supply chains and an open-spec version a co-op could build in a pinch.

- Proof-of-place guardians. People chosen by a blend of sortition, lineage, and apprenticeship hold standing vetoes on projects that would maim watersheds or unmake cultural memory. Their dissents must be answered line by line by those who want to proceed.

- Almanac of fixes. Every agency keeps a public book of repairs: what broke, how it mended, time and cost. Children read them. Applause meets ingenuity and honest failure alike.

- A giveback rule for reach. You can say almost anything to those who choose to listen. To shove your voice onto everyone’s breakfast plate you spend scarce reach credits whose issuance and use are transparent and rate-limited. Breaking this rule is a tort of injury to attention and a debt to the common pot.

Why this world is good—and why it is believable

It’s good because dignity is engineered instead of moralized. Because help shows up early with clean socks and broth, not paperwork and scolding. Because lungs stop being the quiet arena of policy failure. Because children nap in air that doesn’t quietly hurt them. Because one run of bad luck cannot turn into a decade of falling. Because you can choose a slow life and still be fetched gently when things tilt. Because people who like bright rooms full of humming code can build with guardrails, and those who like quiet rooms can knit in peace. Because kindness isn’t left to volunteers with burnout; it sits on line items and gets audited.

It’s specific enough to check. You can hold the Night Switch that truly sleeps a house. You can point at the Path-on-a-Page for registering a studio. You can tap the ledger that paid your neighbor for the shade over your stoop. You can read the diet label on a model before it suggests a plan. You can look up the receipt for last year’s bus redesign and the odds board that warned it might bunch at dusk. You can see the Off Test on the vacuum you just bought.

It’s plausible because most of it exists in pieces. Microgrids island clinics today. Wastewater spots outbreaks. Prediction tournaments out-predict pundits. Citizens’ assemblies write sober law. HVDC strings continents together. Fermentation tastes like holidays in more experiments than you’d think. Teleoperation already makes distance feel short. DNA printers log runs. Walk-away-safe reactor designs sit in binders with regulators who certify airplanes; they can learn to certify cores. Permit clocks, when installed, shame agencies into speed. Purchase orders already steer cultures. Insurers already price risk well enough to be friends when they want to be.

We did not abolish grief. We took it out of profit ledgers and shortened its half-life. We did not end argument. We gave it arenas where losing teaches. We did not smooth every edge. We left wildness enough for risk chosen on purpose, with ropes and with friends.

A small pledge

When someone takes a Beacon Room shift or a Civic Steward prints its oath, sometimes at kitchen tables when a family grows, people speak a low promise:

I will leave a clear path home for anyone who falters.
I will guard my pace and honor yours.
I will say what I know and how I know it when it moves more than me.
I will accept careful requests and decline without shame.
I will build one thing that lets a stranger breathe easier.
I will not mistake tidy for true, nor loud for right.

On a spring morning, a baker unlocks ovens that pull power without fuss. Across town, a dispatcher slides a feed to mail pace for the night. On a ridge, a teenager ties a small ceramic beacon and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens more than she speaks. A Beacon Room glows and asks, in a voice that fits a raw nerve: What would help in the next hour?

The day runs on rails we chose on purpose. The horizon stays wide. Tide comes, tide goes. No wave knocks the houses down. You can go far, and you can come home. There is cinnamon, clean wood, and someone who looks at you as if you belong, because you do.
