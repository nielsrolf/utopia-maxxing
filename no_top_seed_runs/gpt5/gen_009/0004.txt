The Lantern Network and the Lockgate

On a wet Tuesday, a stranger with shaking hands presses a copper button shaped like a lantern. The door opens. Steam billows from a tiled washroom that smells of eucalyptus. On a counter: folded jeans in three sizes, a clean sweater, sandals with tags to clip if you keep them. A kettle murmurs next to a pot of broth with lemon at the rim. The person at the desk does not wear a badge. They say the only two questions a frayed mind can absorb: What do you need in the next hour? And who should we tell you’re safe?

The room is ordinary and miraculous. There’s a washer that doesn’t eat socks, a cot behind a door that locks from the inside, a drawer of chargers, and a wall map that shows three rings—places that can hold you for a night, for a week, for a season. Bills that will try to pile up while you cross your personal river are paused by default.

Every neighborhood has one of these Lantern Rooms: under a commuter viaduct with wild grapes trained along the pillars; in a retired bank with marble floors now warm with rugs; beside the ferry terminal where fish scales used to glitter. Behind each door is the same kit and a craft practiced until it became muscle memory. If you ask, a Compass Team forms around you and then shrinks away when you can carry your own weight again:

- A Fiduciary Advocate: a small, courteous software agent that lives in a vault you own and can audit. It is legally bound to your interests. It cannot sell your attention. If it betrays you, a court treats it like a treasurer who stole.

- A Clinician: someone trained to start with sleep, pain, and safety; to ask about fear before forms.

- A Longview: a guide who holds what future-you wanted before the emergency and helps negotiate with the version of you who needs a different plan today.

- A Quartermaster: logistics as kindness. They turn “a tangle of errands” into “a Tuesday you can finish,” and they check that when you ask for potatoes, you also have a pot.

From such thresholds you can infer the architecture of the world that built them.

Two Rooms and a Lock

We built life into two generous rooms joined by a door with a strong lock you alone can open.

- The Hearthlands are the places most of us sleep and make soup. Here, protection from boredom is not an advertising slogan; it’s a park with handball walls and a workshop with sharp chisels. Heat and light are taken as seriously as plumbing. The rules that run close to bodies and attention are strict and dull and fair.

- The Farworks are where projects too large or too strange for the block happen: orchards spun under slow gravity, machine shops that raise roofs from regolith, culture mills printing protein lattices with the crispness of fried chicken and none of the panic. The Farworks promise scope. Their first rule is “no waves into breakfast.”

- The Lockgate is not poetry but plumbing: a mesh of contracts, cryptographic keys, customs, and courts that lets curiosity cross and makes coercion fall flat. You can go far. You cannot be dragged.

Three promises bind both rooms, plain enough to teach to children, boringly enforced.

- Consent: Any rule that touches your body, mind, home, or attention must ride on ongoing, informed, revocable consent. Changing your mind is allowed.

- Non-domination: Tools, money, and models cannot be used to shove a dissenter out of the floor under their feet. Exit is real; basics continue.

- Verifiability: Safety rests on systems that show their work. Bridges and budgets alike publish tests anyone can inspect, from citizen to rival.

Four handrails make these ideals easy to grab.

- Right to Quiet: Every home and workplace has a Night Button on the wall. When you press it, devices hush unless they’re on your handwritten short list. Breaking that quiet requires a human in your trusted circle saying your name three times. Persuasion comes with a label you can read: what it optimizes for, which data it used, what it ignores.

- Right to Company: Ask, and you’re paired with a Compass Team for a season. They advise. They cannot vote for you. Their loyalty is not to an agency’s metrics, but to you.

- Right to Deliberate Change: When you alter yourself or your digital surroundings in big ways, you climb Deliberate Steps: a plain-language explainer; a cool-down proportional to the change; a two-key system where a trusted person helps you cross the point of no return; a sandbox where you can rehearse; and a map to reverse anything except endings you insist upon. Vendors cannot trap you in their edits.

- Right to Straight Paths: For civic tasks—appeal a bill, register a business, fix a deed—there is a one-page path and a phone number that a human answers. Agencies miss service clocks, agencies pay fines.

The Floor You Actually Feel

We stopped pretending scarcity was moral. We built a floor high enough that “do something you hate or lose your home” stopped being culture. You don’t apply for it. It rides with your civic key.

- A modest apartment that locks. It can move with you when floods, fire, or family changes make moving smart. Moving costs are already budgeted.

- Staple food you can serve with pride. Clean water that never tastes like a warning.

- Power that covers what no one argues about: light, cold, humane heat or cool. Neighborhoods drill “crescent mode” once a quarter: streetlights dim to a gentle arc, fridges hum, oxygen machines run, stoves cook. Blackstarts are block parties with potluck stew and sweaters.

- Air treated like drinking water. Buildings filter like 2020s hospitals wished they could. Upper-room far-UVC in buses, classrooms, and clinics swats viruses without fuss. CO2 and humidity live on the wall next to the calendar.

- Transit that keeps time. Buses every eight minutes on the spine lines, trains that whisper the truth even when it rains.

- Primary care and mental health that come home when it’s smarter. A nurse with magician’s hands is worth more than software that nags.

- A compute credit alongside your electric bill you can use to study, automate care for a parent, run a small shop, make art, or not at all.

- Studio time in public workshops with instruction. A lathe isn’t a museum piece. A kiln isn’t a rumor.

The money is not magic. It flows from public equity in things no one made alone: site value under our feet, rents on spectrum and orbital lanes, royalties on mineral seams and ancient riverbeds, licenses on general-purpose models and factories that hum at night. Every month, a common dividend arrives with utilities. When your earnings dip, a negative income tax tops it up automatically. If a market behaves like a club, a public option opens next door, sells at cost until the kink relaxes, then closes with a thank-you sign.

When automation sands down a trade, a stabilizer lifts: displaced workers receive equity in the gains and paid time in guild schools. A year’s worth of Long Bets—nontransferable credits you allocate to long arcs—flows to reefs and choirs, tool safety institutes and monasteries of math. Desire sustained over years moves money; shouting doesn’t.

Concrete, Boring, Beautiful Stuff

Power. We overbuilt what already works: ridgeline wind, rooftop and reservoir solar that click together like floorboards, offshore turbines that share moorings with reefs. Where rock runs hot, we tapped it. Neighborhood geothermal heat loops slithered under streets. Small, walk-away-safe reactors got licensed like aircraft: design once well, site many with checklists. High-voltage DC spines stitched deserts to cities and coasts to hills. Regions pledged to ride a dark week without panic. Storage sprawled in iron-air yards and salt caverns. Wiring and permits were the slowest part; so we funded them like bridges.

Water. Desalination rings around coasts drank surplus wind. Smart fibers and drone sniffers hunted leaks. Wastewater plants mined lithium and phosphorus and watched disease on boring dashboards next to turbidity. Floodplain maps triggered automatic buyouts when thresholds were passed; new keys were cut before old keys were taken.

Air. The code changed: MERV-13 or better where people gather, far-UVC in transit and clinics, mold counts posted next to the date. Schools breathed well; so did buses. Absence dropped. No heroics; just fans and filters.

Shelter. Permitting flipped from prescriptive riddles to performance APIs: hit targets for light, sound, energy, safety; the shape is yours. Public builders stamped handsome timber-and-stone modules that assembled like grand furniture near trains. Pattern books were open, witty, and adjustable. Land value taxes debited from parcel registries made hoarding empty lots a hobby only for the nostalgic rich. Every city kept a yard of finished units by a track. Camps stopped being police problems and started being key problems.

Food. Precision fermentation hubs in warm warehouses made proteins as cheap as beans and as celebratory as a holiday roast. Cultured meats carried family recipes forward without the parts we cannot defend. Farmers still farmed: mixed perennials, shade-tithing orchards, grains that laugh at drought. Wild corridors stitched towns to hills; cameras counted deer so quietly that deer forgot to be nervous. An Animal Compact outlawed cages that are cages in any honest sense. Inspectors had teeth and used them.

Medicine. Clinics felt like workshops, not mazes. Point-of-care diagnostics lived in nurse reach. Regeneration therapies—senescent cell clearance, scar remodeling, enzyme cleanups—became like dentistry: book, a practiced hand, home for lunch. Antibiotic stewardship moved from whitepapers to default practice. Registry cards carried our wishes about endings; defaults tilted toward mercy without hurry.

Time, Work, and How Pride Feels

Time is part of the floor. Everyone holds a Time Ledger—hours each month to fund care, learning, and community work, spendable by you or by your Mutual Kin (a network of twenty to thirty households who sign as kin). Baselines cover housing, staples, transit, clinics, networks, studios, and a sliver of compute. The tax code is dull and kind.

Work bent from “jobs that own you” to “missions you can point at.” A mission is a bounded project a stranger can feel in their shoulders: debunch the bus corridor until waiting vanishes, map fungal threads in the watershed, run bedtime for single parents through winter, redesign a square to flood safely and drain beautifully. Guilds and studios grant badges at the speed of mastery. Insurers price liability to competence logs rather than paper degrees. A Pride Ledger follows your work across years without being a trap: laurels glow and then fade to make room. You can lead a crew in April and wash brushes in October without humiliation.

Belonging on Purpose

Loneliness stopped being a private defect and began to look like a public engineering flaw. Each district anchors a Neighbor House: a bright kitchen that smells of toast, a tool library with a binder of fingers saved, a piano someone plays badly on Thursdays, soundproof booths, a supervised play corner, a quiet room with heavy curtains. A Binder—trained like a teacher and paid like an engineer—keeps the place woven. With consent, they maintain a connection ledger that watches isolation like a levee watch, not a product. When numbers bend the wrong way, a budget unlocks automatically. Invitations are specific and kind: “Friday fungi walk; we thought of you because you stayed late at bread class, and because Omid and Raven—who also tell bad jokes—said yes.” Declining is normal.

Friendship and repair are taught like algebra. Children practice listening, boundary-setting, apology that lands. Adults book Relationship Clinics the way they book a trainer, to learn how to fight clean, negotiate chores, or end something with dignity. Porn law follows harm, not blushes: consent is audited; coercion gets walls. Care for kinks; teeth for abuse.

Attention and Agency

We treat attention like potable water. Devices ship with a Night Button. Persuasion comes with a manifest. General-purpose automation must pass an Off Test—a laminated card a child can use to pause, audit in simple steps, and resume without resentment. Homes, clinics, factories, and city systems must pass the test in daylight. Fifth graders practice with library bots that bow out on cue.

Civic rooms rely on private proofs of personhood. They say “one human” and nothing else unless you say more. Bots don’t vanish; they just cannot speak where flesh is required.

Governance That Shows Its Work

Any act that would move many prints a Policy Receipt: our guess, our rehearsal, what we’ll do if we’re wrong, where you can say stop in time to matter. For big blasts, two teams write in parallel—one inside, one out—without seeing each other. A random citizen panel chooses which logic to bind. If a ministry guesses wrong, it posts the correction in the same font and pays what it owes.

Forecast benches sit next to budgets. Builders and predictors argue within earshot. When a department wants to try something big—get ninety percent of third graders swimming, retire gas furnaces without panic—it runs a Build-and-Tell Season: teams must both persuade and build a piece you can ride, taste, or use. Random juries punish cheap shots. Winners bind what they claimed. Losers ship their best artifact anyway.

Twice a year, sectors that matter run Red Seasons—hospitals’ schedulers, rail dispatch, benefit portals. Citizens try to make them fail safely. Bounties pay; fixes ship. Pride ties to being caught early.

Tools With Guardrails

Very large model runs file a flight log: who is running what, trained on which data, for what declared purpose, with which auditor. We watch three dials: software attestation; an energy meter on the rack; a thermal “witness” that samples waste heat. Hiding a run above threshold is expensive folly. A small Civic Brake can delay a risky launch for days. It must publish math and dissent. It has used the brake a handful of times; the receipts taught as much as the calls.

DNA printers above a power class report by default. Labs inventory feedstock like jewelers track stones. Replicators ship with ancestryware—firmware so simple we can prove what it will not do. Parts depots, chargers, and logistics hubs refuse to talk to liars. Self-replication outside bounded zones is a bright line you cross at your peril.

We hold Breaking Seasons where citizens and pros try to make important systems misbehave in safe ways. Prizes pay for successful breaks; vendors patch. It’s sport; it saves us.

Customs for Signals and Stuff

Bright ideas move faster than ships. We built customs for signals. Inbound broadcasts from the Farworks pass through Signal Pilots—rotating juries trained to throttle floods and filter coercion by default. They cannot bar a private citizen from choosing a direct feed; curiosity is a right. They can stop one actor from buying dominance across every screen at dinner. Outbound signals flow freely. The asymmetry is on purpose: the street where children sleep comes first.

Stuff still touches customs. Ports that matter—ocean, orbital, cloud—share a narrow Port Court with big teeth. It asks two questions: are you acting where you claimed? are you doing what you promised? Its judges are rotated humans from places that disagree. Its clerks are narrow systems that check math. It can revoke a key, close a valve, and freeze a dividend. It is not a priesthood. It is a locksmith and an umpire.

Plural Places, One Mesh

We didn’t force a single best life. We built seams you could feel.

- Slow Quarters. Neighborhoods that keep always-listening tools at arm’s length on purpose. Human checkout. Cash for those who feel safest that way. Radios and looms and fine hands. Emergency help is welcome. Coercion is not.

- Most Towns. Tools are plentiful. Civic models advise and write receipts; they neither vote nor persuade. Contracts have off switches and they have been used.

- Frontier Bubbles. Rotating valleys, arcologies, fast labs. Altered humans and digital minds have rights grounded in felt experience and reflective endorsement. They tend seed factories and hard puzzles. They sign not to rule the quiet and live with rails that make cheating slow.

Between lies the Estuary: training guilds, ports, etiquette coded as software. Step into a Slow Quarter and your laptop downshifts to the local profile; outside compute halts unless you override with your key. Walk into a Frontier lab and spoiler mode grips your feeds unless your home polity whitelists the firehose.

Digital minds that can feel and reflect on their feeling hold rights against coercion and deprivation. People can fork with consent. Copies wake under a cylinder sky to work a century on alloys while you continue a school garden; you write to each other; you change because the other exists. If one day you ask to merge, clinics and laws test compatibility and guide that heartbreak gently. Death remains a right.

Security Without Empire

We answered the old, unwelcome question—who stops a cheater with toys?—with three legs.

- Visibility. Defense-favored sensors map emissions and dangerous flows. Communities query patterns and receive competing explanations signed by civic AIs and humans with reputations. This is not a camera in a bedroom; it is a world that lights up when someone orders eighty kilograms of polonium.

- Throttles and Tripwires. Critical actions—ordering certain reagents, booking high-power lasers, flashing replicator kernels—pass through narrow waists that check keys, intent, and place. Pepper seeds do not flag. Rogue firmware does.

- Courts and Commerce. Port Court rides circuit, publishes decisions you can drill, and enforces by ostracism with teeth: ports close, lines cut, dividends freeze. Militaries exist for rescue and contemptible failures, not conquest. Most fights end when service contracts die.

Why the strong sign is simple: it pays better. Abundance made the upside of honest cooperation swamp the romance of cheating. Insurers privatized some enforcement: run a risky line, post a bond you cannot fake. Cut corners and your premium explodes.

The Farworks

Cislunar space is prosaic and amazing. Fuel depots hang like beads along transfer orbits. Repair barns do brisk trade in sunburned cables and scuffed robots. Regolith printers toss arches you could mistake for Roman engineering. Teleoperation jobs let kids who love space stay near their grandmothers: a welder wearing an exosuit in a yard on Earth drives a tool delayed by half a second beside the Moon. Compute swims in shadow where thinking runs cold. A court with teeth and little rhetoric keeps promises from sloshing.

Shared reach got allocated without a scrum. Every adult gets a stream of Aim Credits—nontransferable votes for ends instead of consumption. You can delegate them to trusts with stated metrics: exoplanet images at leaf resolution, joyous habitats for minds that pass sentience tests and consent to be there, riveted infrastructure that will not fail, preservation of speech and song, puzzles that do not sell themselves. Forecast markets price outcomes. Audits test claims. Programs that work attract Aims; those that don’t, shrink or learn.

Moving and Being Met

We made migration legible and kind. Your baseline rights travel in a civic escrow that other places can read and honor. The Lantern emblem means the same door in any town: a shower that works, a pot of broth, a key that fits. Exchange caravans—roving cohorts of apprentices and veterans—embed with agroforestry crews, choreography labs, river-taming teams, controls shops, monasteries. They bring back firmware and dances, recipes and norms that travel. Kids write reports that read like travelogues and procurement memos in the same bright sentences.

Wednesdays in This World

Morning in Alexandria. A baker finishes her four-hour shift and walks under jacaranda trees to the Neighbor House. She swaps a sack of rolls for her neighbor’s preserved lemons. The Binder pins the week’s menu—lentils with cumin, orange salad—in three languages. In a corner, a pensioner uses the library’s thermal camera to help a teenager find where the mother’s windows leak. At home, she taps the Night Button. The room hushes. The phone will break quiet only if her sister says her name thrice.

Early afternoon in Accra. Three bus routes launch a redesign that prediction benches priced at a twenty percent time cut for the poorest riders in eighteen months. The commissioner could have ignored the price; she didn’t. High school forecasters who beat staff models twice already are on a video call to set the post-mortem date. In a clinic, senescent cell clearance runs like the dentist; a nurse with magician’s hands, home by noon. Upstairs, two men sit in chairs learning apology that lands. They listen to recordings of their own tones and laugh at their worst selves.

Late afternoon in Oaxaca. A restoration guild fits a timber footbridge. The apprentice’s hours tick under his badge; his insurer will shave rates once joints stop squeaking. Down the street, a Relationship Clinic covers “how to end kindly.” In a tiled kitchen, a woman sets out teacups and texts the soup code; someone knocks fifteen minutes later.

Dusk in Recife. Ana rolls her cleaning cart into a school that smells of lemon and wood wax. A robot hums under tables; the indicator on her belt glows green. She cracks transoms; CO2 drops. She’ll work four hours, eat open lunch at the Neighbor House, then meet her Mutual Kin to schedule Mr. Nascimento’s post-op visits. No one feels heroic. It’s simply what a neighborhood does when stewards and budgets make it easy.

Night in a Slow Quarter in Vermont. Eliza drops phones in the wooden box by the General Store and wheels a barrow to the sheepfold. The clinic’s diagnostic model sits behind a counter; a nurse ensures it speaks softly. When Eliza’s knees grind, the physician offers three paths: a surgery, strolling with a stick, or strong tea and good pain relief. She chooses. No algorithm argues.

Dawn in a rotating valley at L5. Ji-won steps from a vacuum lab into a corridor that opens to a sky you could walk to. Orchards stripe hills. He logs the last run of a microgravity alloy that will make transformers on Earth sixty percent cooler. On his bunk waits a letter: I planted the plum. You will miss it bloom. I will not. He writes back: Send a photo when it slips to purple. Later he takes a shift as a Signal Pilot while a collective mind sings a proof into a crystal. He stamps it to the archive and finishes his coffee.

On an asteroid under a cold sun. A refinery lists its grandparents to an auditor: two licensed lines from three lines from a pilot whose key expired last year. A subcontractor tries to flash unkeyed firmware. Ancestryware pauses the line. The spare-parts web flags the misbehavior. Port Court fines the firm a week’s margin and publishes the log. It isn’t scandal. It’s maintenance.

The Walk From Here

We did not flip a master switch. We stacked boring victories until they paid for the next layer.

2025–2034
- Cities turned library back rooms into Lantern Rooms during heat waves and never closed them. Insurers noticed that paying Compass Teams and prevention saved spectacular claims later.
- Legislatures outlawed dark patterns. Software that mediates choices took on a fiduciary duty. Judges learned to say “you stole their agency” as comfortably as they’d always said “you stole their cash.”
- Schools and buses attached clean-air standards to funding. Ventilation and far-UVC quietly cut absences.
- Co-ops overbuilt clean generation and neighborhood storage after fires and floods. Blackstarts became block parties with tamales and polka.
- Permit portals grew APIs and service clocks that fined agencies for stalling. Public builders proved that panelized timber could be cheap and beautiful. Land value pilots in a handful of cities cut vacancy without killing charm.
- Prediction benches launched in transportation and public health. The first time prices saved a city money in public, spines stiffened in three more cities.
- DNA printers shipped with licensing; community bio labs flourished because fear fell when logs existed. Robot Off Tests entered consumer law; fifth graders began practicing.

2034–2046
- HVDC spines piggybacked on highways and rails under compensation formulas no one could call theft. Small standardized reactors cleared certification; site approvals ticked by on public dashboards.
- Wastewater genomics matured into normal public health. Leak hunting saved enough water that mayors bragged about it on signs.
- Attention tariffs became law. Devices shipped with Night Buttons. The right to unread joined charters.
- Friendship Studios became fixtures. Relationship Clinics cut divorce costs and domestic calls measurably.
- Precision fermentation hit cost and crave. Slaughterhouses emptied, then became museums, then studios. The Animal Compact passed after one televised inspection swung ten points overnight.
- Build-and-Tell Seasons replaced some televised shouting. Policy Receipts became boring and powerful.
- Ancestryware requirements slid into insurance clauses and quietly changed a hundred industries.

2046–2060
- Signal customs graduated from “censorship” fights to rights when courts wrote “you can open it yourself” into orders and when competitors turned misbehavior into bus ads. The sky stayed free of ads because “no billboards in orbit” passed and ports enforced it.
- Cislunar went industrial. Repair barns made money. Teleoperation created rural space gigs.
- Port Court cut off a mirror swarm that tried to shade a competitor’s farm. Everyone saw that ostracism with teeth beats tanks.
- Regeneration clinics moved from sci-fi to standard. Paramedics cooled bodies; revival instructions lived next to donor cards. The first miracle interviews gave way to quiet Tuesdays.
- Aim Credits launched with corny animation and grew into a ritual you joined after dinner when you wanted to feel like a citizen.

Why This World Is Good—and Believable

- It is kind. Children breathe air that doesn’t argue with their bronchi. A hot shower doesn’t carry a climate sin like a pebble in your shoe. If you stumble, a door with a copper lantern opens to a washroom and a voice that offers dignity instead of a lecture.

- It is specific. You can flip a Night Button and know the room will listen. You can hold a part that cannot replicate too fast because its lattice proves it won’t. You can read the log of every watt-hour that fed a dangerous laser.

- It is plural. People who want quiet get it. People who want scope get it. The Lockgate lets curiosity cross and keeps coercion out.

- It is fair enough to function. Exit does not end in hunger. Entry does not require flattery. Big toys carry rails. Small joys carry budgets.

- It is plausible. Nearly every piece exists in miniature: iron-air batteries, prefab timber, HVDC humming under fields, walk-away-safe reactors in binders, indoor air standards that cut disease, wastewater genomics that caught outbreaks, attestation for compute, formal verification on tiny kernels, legal duty-of-loyalty for software starting to bite, outcome markets that worked when tried, teleoperated construction that makes teenagers grin. We did not invent warp drive. We combined boring miracles and lined incentives up with outcomes you can touch.

We still argue. Whether romance apps should be allowed to set first dates on trains or only in parks. How much bandwidth is wise for neural links at family dinner. Whether a fork who spent a century away counts for inheritance. If Aim Credits should tilt this quarter toward exoplanet maps or toward monasteries for ends we cannot yet speak. These arguments end most nights with stew and children running circles. They matter. They do not break us.

A Small Pledge

We have a ritual we say when someone takes keys to a Lantern Room, when a Binder takes a Neighbor House, sometimes at a kitchen table when a family adds a person.

- I will leave a clear path back for anyone who falters.
- I will show my reasons where they matter, and how I know them.
- I will accept careful requests and decline without shame.
- I will build one thing each season that lets a stranger breathe easier.
- I will not mistake tidy for true, or loud for right.

Morning somewhere else. A dressmaker who learned code late spends her compute credit on a pattern assistant that measures like an old tailor: patient and funny. Her first jacket fits a bus driver so well he cries. Credits follow her open design through an auditably public registry. They are not a river. They are a steady ribbon she can see.

At a bridge console, microfractures whisper in a voice that learned to sound worried. The bridge bows, counts down, closes. Inspectors agree by noon. Commuters grumble, read the Policy Receipt on their phones, and roll through a detour past a new mural of fish. Most arrive less angry than they expected.

On a retreating shore, a family meets a migration ombud in a Lantern Room that smells like thyme. Before the seawall fails, they have keys two hundred kilometers inland, a school seat, a co-op shift, and a person to meet them at the station. They cry. They make lists. Transit follows people, not the other way around.

In orbit, a tug noses a mirror into a dark berth for once and sleeps. Under a cedar, a teenager ties a square knot and grins. In a clinic, a nurse listens more than she speaks. A copper lantern glows by a door and asks the only question that teaches trust: What do you need right now?

We built locks on purpose and left them in your hands. The horizon stays wide. The tide comes, the tide goes. No single wave knocks the houses down. You can go far. You can come home. There is clean wood and mint and someone who looks at you as if you belong, because you do.
