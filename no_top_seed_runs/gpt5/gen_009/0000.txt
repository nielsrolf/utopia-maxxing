The Waystone Compact

The little hexagon appeared first on doors you already trusted. A worn worker center that kept its kettle on. The side entrance to a library where the outlet was always free. A window in a bus depot where the clerk sometimes kept dry socks. Someone had etched a small stone symbol with a notch—like a marker from an old trail—into the jamb, and next to it taped a card: Here, you can ask to go. Here, you can ask what is being decided in your name and see the reasons before the gate swings.

By the time the newspapers named it, most of it was already daily. Buses began arriving when the display said they would. A public pool stopped smelling of rot because the ventilation spec was published, plain and copyable. You learned that any door with the hexagon stone—any waystone—meant the same thing anywhere: you could leave, with help; you could ask power to explain itself; you could take a shower without answering questions.

The Waystone Compact is not a master law or a single network. It is a stack of small, human-sized fixtures and habits that make exits real, decency routine, and speed safe. It is boring where it should be and glorious where it can be.

Four standing promises

- A lit exit, without tests. Any adult may leave a home, job, or group without first winning approval from that group. When they do, they receive a Waypass usable on public and private carriers, a guaranteed month in a Welcome House in a place of their choosing, roaming health coverage, and a bridge stipend set to local prices. Children have a direct line that never touches their guardians’ devices. Starting at sixteen, a teenager can activate their own exit through a quiet channel staffed by neutral adults. No institution may hide where exits are or how they work.

- Influence receipts and reasons you can read. No civic system or commercial service may steer your attention, alter your patience, or shape your mood without timed, revocable permission. Every feed and nudge must carry a receipt that says: what it is trying to do, for how long, how to turn it off, and why it was shown to you. Anyone exercising public power—or selling essentials like heat, water, transport, identity—writes reasons in words most teenagers can follow and links to the model beneath. If emergencies force action first, reasons and appeal routes arrive by tomorrow.

- Boundaries that count and pay. What you push across a boundary—exhaust, late-night noise, spectrum clutter, harassment at scale, river salts—is measured where it crosses. If you impose, you pay or you stop. What you spill that helps—cold nights from new canopy, a courtyard kitchen that keeps ambulances home—earns visible credit.

- Floors, not favors. Feeding and washing are not conditional. Every person can find a quiet bathroom called a First Sink without paperwork, a kitchen that never asks who you are, a clinic that will see you, and a guaranteed way to reach them. Benefits follow you across employers and towns. Children’s supports follow children, not buildings.

Six lines we do not cross

- No non-consensual mass risk. Trial on yourself and volunteers; do not roll dice with strangers.

- No making minds to suffer. If we are unsure whether a system can suffer, we treat it as if it could until an independent registry clears it.

- No unbounded self-replication, in matter or code.

- Glass rooms for cascade risks. Work that can ripple—bio, grid, orbital, city-scale code—operates in logged, compartmented spaces with independent hands able to pause.

- No bottling maps. Teach what you believe; you may not hide the existence of exits or the routes to them.

- No reach by force. Communities may enforce their vows within; no one projects coercion into a place that did not consent.

Wayhouses and the First Sinks

Within a short walk of where most people live, a door with the hexagon waystone opens daily. Behind it is logistics with manners.

If you ask to go—spoken, signed, tapped—a caseworker whose record you can inspect sits with you within seventy-two hours to choose speed or stealth. You move. Your debts that can be used as chains have their reach frozen until a forum hears both sides. You are not made to explain your soul to a stranger.

Near the desk are First Sinks: clean showers and toilets you can use without giving a name. A wall lists communal kitchens open that night, their addresses, and where to file a safety complaint if respect fails. A small kiosk can switch any phone or card into “quiet rails,” a mode that gets you where you need to go without ads or lures.

Children learn about waystone doors during Open Hour: once a month, in a neutral building with no party’s crest, adults with no stake in keeping kids home teach maps of nearby ways to live, practice how to ask for help, and rehearse—gentle and serious—both leaving and staying.

Vow districts and windows that stay open

Most public life lives in districts bound by voluntary vows. Joining takes a signature. Leaving takes thirty days. Vows are posted large at every entrance and portable across towns.

- A hush borough bans outdoor ads, caps midnight noise, and guarantees shade on footpaths people actually use.

- A workyard district runs twenty-four-hour studios over a welding floor and pays for piano practice and earplugs.

- A child-forward ward designs for toddlers first: stroller parking at grade, nine-minute parks, and a right to part-time work without a promotion penalty.

- A devotions precinct rings bells at dawn but keeps council votes secular and reasons public.

Membranes around vows are not cages. A thin, neutral “window” runs through every network and physical place: basic science, civic manuals, charter catalogs, exit routes, lifeline timetables. Communities curate their infospheres for themselves. No one can shove signals through a wall by force. When you cross, your device offers local defaults—“This place bans biometric doorbells. Accept?”—and reminds you if you override them. You can override. You cannot say you were not told.

The Edge Ledger

We stopped arguing about externalities like sermons and counted them at the edge.

Cheap, boring sensors knit into a layer wherever one set of vows meets another. The layer measures what we can measure: CO2, NOx, fine particulates, nitrates at dawn, decibels after bedtime, pathogens in wastewater, spectrum congestion, heat islands in afternoon sun. It enforces what we refuse to price—dark skies, breeding seasons—through quotas and hard caps written into vows. If a stadium cracks sleep, meters say so and payment or change triggers. If a makerspace’s repair bench keeps ambulances idle, credit flows in the clear. Dashboards show numbers a seventh-grader can parse. It is not perfect. It is better than rumor and fists.

Place rent and the Civic Dividend

We recognized something obvious: sites—land with pipes under it, curb frontage, orbital slots, radio bands—gain value because we exist together. Exclusive claims sit on public foundations.

We moved from taxing improvement to charging place rent based on public value. Assessors use lidar, transaction data, and zoning entitlements to set the yearly rent of location value. Improvements are untaxed. Long-neglected lots fill. Slumlords sell or fix. Spectrum, curb space, and orbital paths follow similar leases refreshed often enough that forever claims evaporate.

Half the revenue funds bones: drains, shade, heat pumps, clinics, buses, ombuds, Welcome Houses, the Museum of Tries, and the teams who enforce constraints. The other half lands as a Civic Dividend to every resident with a right to be here. It buys warmth, food you can cook, a room with a door, connectivity, school you can return to, and the right to move. It is not a parent’s allowance. It is a co-owner’s draw from shared assets, paid no matter your vote.

Alongside lives a compute stipend—enough time on strong models to learn, work, mentor, or run your shop without kneeling to a platform—and an Automation Participation Share that skims a sliver of savings when machines replace hours and pays it where the jobs changed.

The Commons Return Engine and the Proof Guild

We stopped paying for fences around obvious ideas. In their place we run a simple license. If your chair uses my joint or your model uses my trick and you sell it, a small fraction of the marginal value my contribution added flows to my pot automatically. If you claim independence, you point to proof. The Commons Return Engine tracks flows. A salaried corps—the Proof Guild—audits claims, model-checks contributions, runs field measures, rotates often, and is itself audited by teams it does not know. Their work comes with bell curves and plain words. Hiding adverse results burns a scrutiny bond you posted when you proposed the thing.

Tools that do their job and do not pretend

Everyday tools obey a simple rule: no simulation of feeling. Your kitchen helper can plan a menu, write a grocery list inside your budget, schedule a plumber, and nudge your calendar with persuasion you have read and accepted. It cannot act like your lover or your long-lost friend. For tools near appetite, we built triple-consent gates for strong pleasures: you today, you a week ago, and you tomorrow. Kids are left alone. Quiet districts ask code to wait in a glovebox; abstinence walks in with equal dignity.

Civic Stewards

Before any code sits near budgets, gates, or valves, we train it like a junior colleague.

Each region raises a few Civic Stewards—narrow, auditable systems trained on open law, infrastructure models, and local minutes. A newborn Steward spends a year with no actuators and no accounts. It reads codebooks, sits through budget nights, rides in a plow at 3 a.m., and watches intersections from different heights.

Three circles mentor and license it:

- A household circle folds it into ordinary life: grudges about the laundry schedule, the way a new rule touches a kitchen, jokes that would be flat on paper.

- A trade circle—nurses, pipefitters, school secretaries, machinists—teaches patience and the smell of competence.

- A place circle—river mouth, high-rise cluster, aquifer—walks it through what breaks first and how gently to open a tap.

Each circle has veto power. When all three and a public board say yes, the Steward takes a postcard oath: no rapid harm without a human co-sign; no change without a rollback plan; reasons with links to models; a duty to say “I don’t know” and “I was wrong” in public. Once a year each Steward faces a ten-minute surprise test on a street corner under a clock. Anyone can try to stump it. Hiding reasons pays bounties to the finder. Break trust and you lose access and go to the Museum, where your error, fix, and apology live under glass.

Public odds and receipts on overrides

Arguments rarely converge alone. We do not pretend they will. We built public odds boards: formal prediction markets for policy. Will bus-only lanes cut EMS response times? Will this flood plain buy save more than it costs? Traders post odds, experts write theses, and impact bonds pay if promised numbers arrive. Budgets tilt toward options with better forecasted yield. Electeds can override but must sign a receipt and risk part of their discretionary fund if the markets later prove them wrong.

Data you hold; speech and reach pulled apart

People keep keys to their data in personal safes: health records, credentials, purchases, pings. When a Steward needs slices to plan sewers or your insurer wants to price risk, they ask through standard rails that let you say yes, no, or “this narrow slice for this long for this purpose.” Proof of personhood for voting and petitions exists, privacy-preserving and revocable. We pulled identity apart from reach: you can say almost anything as almost anyone, but you cannot push it to everyone’s breakfast without burning stamps—tokens tied to verified contributions, hours from a monthly allotment, and narrow caps that prevent floods. Influence receipts show how, and how often, someone tried to steer you this week.

Work, matching, and slack

We treat a tight job market as a public good. Each district tracks vacancy ratios. When ratios sag, councils issue public work orders—shade trees, creek mending, elder visits, school air checks—and set wages just high enough to make private employers compete. The central bank’s dashboard targets slack the way it once targeted inflation.

Licensing shrank. Life-and-death trades kept exams. Most others use demonstrations and apprenticeships you can find in a public directory. Matching became a vocation. Placers watch you on two small jobs, speak to other Placers, and place you. If you are still there and glad at six months and twenty-four, they get paid. Paint the fit and you lose your license.

Health, air, and endings with dignity

Primary care regained rank. Clinics prescribe ventilation and light under a right to rest. A guarded genomic registry runs pragmatic trials with strong consent. Diagnostics moved into the bathroom mirror and the cheap watch. Home robots lift, fetch, and sanitize while humans listen and care. We made quitting addictions ordinary and kind. Endings are scheduled with ritual; a Time Fund lets you prepay a small share of tomorrow’s wonders—biostasis where lawful or simple remembrance—and pays for hospice and elder care today.

Power, patterns, and permission

Abundance came not from miracles but from copyable competence. Solar films clip onto rails in days; high-temperature heat pumps replace dirty burners; district microgrids island clinics and groceries when winds hit; small reactors sit on brownfields under many eyes; deep geothermal pulls steady warmth from granite without shakes. Pattern codes based on proven designs moved approvals to sixty days for conformity checks. Deviate and you can—inside sandboxes where failures cannot cascade. Utilities invite private capital into district concessions that compete on uptime with ledgers in daylight.

Dangerous compute runs like fissile material: registered labs, chip serials and power meters writing to third-party vaults, time-boxed runs, and triple-key stop switches held by separate groups. We did not outlaw math; we made daylight pay.

Mobility, lifelines, and quiet magnificence

Trains, buses, roads, and ferries honor a Waypass that buys passage and a floor of protection. Night belongs to freight with whispering brakes. Day belongs more to feet and bikes than it did in a century. Curb space is priced in real time to pay for the bus lane you were promised and got. When heat eats a city, Lifelines pour shade, water, and people who know how to listen. When storms bruise a coast, modular walls roll to low streets and kitchens scale calmly.

Justice and repair

We unbundled justice. Those who detain do not interrogate or prosecute. Payment ties to long-run harm reduction. Judges write reasons and are reviewed three years on not for guilt or innocence but for the quality of their reasoning and the life of their rulings. Most wrongs end not with cages but restitution: lies become public corrections and a season of work for those you misled; assaults become schedules, money, and bans on rooms. Prisons exist, fewer and smaller. We built Calm Wards—low-stimulation commons with predictable loops for people city life scrapes. Entry and exit are voluntary under clear terms.

Guard the compact, not an empire

We keep a Constraint Service, not an army of conquest. Most days they are logisticians: re-knitting fiber so a propaganda firehose cannot drown a vote, or rebuilding the two-lane that feeds two towns that glare. On the rare day when bulldozers line up to drain a marsh, a grey wing reads the compact aloud, makes the soil pudding long enough for operators to walk away, and logs everything for a court. Machines break. People do not.

The Museum of Tries

We stopped pretending institutions never err. We cataloged our misses and paid people to read them. Any Steward that misfired, any vow that had terrible downstreams, any hospital that tried to pay kitchen staff with prestige told the story in a room you could find. Admission often costs a story told aloud. The shop sells repair manuals and a little book called How to Change Your Mind While You Still Can.

First Gardens Trust

We accepted that the oldest places on Earth belong to no one and to everyone. A First Gardens Trust sets a finite number of permanent resident-years, auctioned for money and demonstrated care—hours of tending, ecological skill, art that repairs. A slice lives as pilgrimage time. The Trust belongs to no one and irritates everyone equally; that is a sign it works.

A day under the Waystones

Morning is blue and cool. The curb tile shows a small smile for air. Your window slides open the two centimeters you told it to when pollen sits below your threshold and the ozone sensor agrees. Nothing guesses. Your home follows settings you wrote on a slow Sunday. Anything that would like to tug your attention sends a receipt with a clock.

Three notes arrive. Egrets have landed where the creek bends; your glasses can paint them warm against the dawn if you want. A clinic flag says your aunt’s knee is flaring in a safe way; a nurse will knock at ten. A Welcome House two doors down needs two pairs of hands and a bit of Urdu for an hour because a fire one county over sent buses in the night. You accept. You choose to find the egrets by chance.

Your pocket helper unfurls a one-page brief. A storm angles north. A meeting moved to the library’s glass room because the light helps. Two ways to untangle an easement. Three scripts for a hard talk with a contractor who missed steel delivery. You circle the version that fits your style. The helper files the plan and vanishes. It does not act like your friend. It will never say it loves you.

At noon, your neighbor’s teenager—on her second season of real-stakes work—stands at a table saw in the microfactory. Her badge opens the guard and not the planer. She’s building a weir gate for the rewilded creek. Lunch is buttered noodles and a prompt card from the odds board: a bond to stop buying male-only saplings so pollen stops spiking. Someone you trust flagged the math as sound. You tap accept. Small and right.

At three, you stop by the square where a Steward faces questions. Why did three lanes become two with trees? It shows crash stats and cortisol curves, opens the model so a teenager can poke assumptions, and points to a rollback plan if promised savings don’t show. A street vendor passes a skewer and a letter about a stall rating that dipped; both go on the record. No one shouts. Some laugh. The Proof Guild gets a note to watch night noise one block south; the new plaza might be too fun.

At five, you visit the creek you straightened a decade ago and then unstraightened with elders. Native grasses returned in three years. Foxes in five. Hawks in nine. After the heaviest rain in fifty years, the water ran brown for a day and then clear. Some of those teenagers have children who throw pebbles into riffles. When the alumni thread lights with minnows, a few still cry.

At seven, you attend a Build Meet that will stripe bus lanes on three arterials. Another district did it last year. Buses sped thirty percent. Shop sales dipped twelve for six months and then climbed ten. The odds board says there’s a seventy percent chance your place will rhyme. Proposers posted a bond to pay shops if they’re wrong by more than five points. Hands rise. Budgets move.

Before bed, you open a Looking Glass about whether to leave this decade’s work. Peers wrote frank letters: three-month leaves to tend a parent, doubling down, a stint in an orbital yard. A Keeper’s nudge whispers at the edge: sleep on it; try a sampler tomorrow—the low-dose of feeling steadier under pressure—and take the interview you’ve dodged. You smile. The helper adds nothing. It is not that sort of companion.

Getting there from here

This all sounds expensive and solemn. It costs less than ambient dread. The bridges are boring and brave.

- Years 1–3. Five mid-sized cities move a third of property tax onto place rent and leases for shared claims—curb frontage, roof radiance, airwaves, compute above a wattage threshold. They run buses every eight minutes, convert schools and clinics to fresh air and light, and pay a small Civic Dividend that makes sleeping on the street rare where exits are real. They pilot vow districts as voluntary covenants with posted vows, windows you cannot paper over, and thirty-day exits. Refugee agencies, shelters, and sanctuary networks put waystones over doors they already staff; a levy on engagement-driven ads and diaspora money fund the first Welcome Houses. Local papers hire explainer desks that publish causal chains instead of outrage.

- Years 3–5. Legislatures allow cities to collect rent on resource claims and cut taxes on work. Courts begin to demand influence receipts. A civil coalition funds the Proof Guild and “reasons weeks” where agencies publish models in plain language and answer ten questions that fit on a postcard. A first batch of Civic Stewards trains in municipal twins open to inspection. Permitting for pattern energy designs adopts “known-good” codes; bad projects die faster. Personal data safes with standard rails go live in two states.

- Years 5–8. Central banks publish a labor slack target; cities receive tools to issue public work orders when vacancy ratios sag. Microfactories spread. Dull, safe home robots show up in hardware stores. The first registered compute pools log chips and power draw above set thresholds; triple-key stop switches become law. The public odds board runs pilot tournaments on buses, air rules, and drought relief with safe harbors for agencies that use them. Separate identity from reach on the big platforms; floods of plausible lies meet friction without gagging voices.

- Years 8–10. The No-Pretend Rule for everyday tools passes. Civic firmware—open, audited code for lights, meters, and identity—goes on a slow, visible update clock with built-in rollbacks. Placers get licenses. Welcome House standards and First Sink specs go national with funds from place rents. Surprise recharter windows roll out for public bodies; within a five-year horizon, any given agency faces a slim, public vote on its mission and a plain ledger of what it did.

- Years 10–15. A compact on belts and borders passes: hush districts vow quiet code; build yards vow rights for any nonhuman minds they invite; tavern rows vow clear exits and genre gates. Edge Ledgers become normal at stadiums and factories. The Proof Guild becomes a national profession with fixed terms and audits of the auditors. The Constraint Service trains openly with neighboring countries under shared red lines: no invasion, yes to pausing cascades.

Improvements we insisted on

- Slow switches for fast things. Any change to civic firmware, emergency alert logic, or identity rails travels through a visible countdown unless a supermajority of authorized people break a glass cover. Dry runs in public sandboxes are mandatory before live flips. Rollback plans come with the proposal.

- Scrutiny bonds. Anyone proposing a major project posts a small bond that burns if they suppress adverse results or hide their reasoning. It does not ruin; it stings. It built a habit of publishing misses.

- Contingency dividends. Every capital project sets aside a visible line for “human change”—training, spare parts, translators, time to sit with the people a project displaces. If you cut that line and your project causes more trouble than it saved, the dash next to your name turns red wherever budgets are debated.

- Micro-juries on the shadow budget. Any resident can propose a budget shift in the civic simulator. Ten names drawn by lot from the affected district read the postcard case, consult a Steward, and vote. If seven of ten agree and the odds board does not warn of steep costs, a small slice of discretionary funds moves. It is not a plebiscite. It is a practice of trust.

Why this is good and why to believe it

This world is good because it treats dignity as a default and competence as a habit. It drains poison from politics by giving people exits that do not ruin them and vows that fit their lives. It refuses lazy bargains: no pretend partners in your pocket; yes to small robots that take the trash out so you can make soup and arguments. It makes influence show a nutrition label. It stops asking you to live at the mercy of guesswork and starts making numbers you can look at without learning a new language.

It is specific and buildable. There is nothing mystical in a waystone door; shelters, churches, and unions already do pieces of this. Cloud providers already log compute; we signed that habit into registries with oversight and receipts. Cities already lease curb space and spectrum; we raised the rent on hoarding and lowered the tax on building. Apprenticeships already teach most of what a human really learns; we published directories and paid Placers when fits last. Libraries already moderate better than platforms; we paid them to run quiet feeds. Courts already write reasons; we paid readers to check whether the reasons aged.

It is plausible because we chose frictions that pay back: pattern permits that turn “three years” into “sixty days,” trains that arrive when signs say they will, budgets that lean toward options with better odds, and rules that put daylight where it hurts. We did not abolish conflict. We gave conflict rooms and rituals where it can cool into accord or sharpen into a clean no. We did not promise that nothing breaks. We keep breaks small and repair close. We left our children thresholds that are lit and windows that do not close.

Most mornings now people wake on streets that respect their lungs and time. They plan weekends that feel like themselves. The small hexagon near back doors stays honest. Systems with oaths speak in words you can check. When you fall, hands appear. When you build, the rails run straight. When someone tries to pull you into a trap, the border hums and asks you twice. When you sing, the room listens before it moves.

The path here was not magic. Insurers tired of paying for mold moved first. A parent’s lawsuit forced a right of exit into constitutions. A mid-size city bragged about buses and shade until its neighbors copied its place rent. Pattern codes copied competence; public odds paid out in drought and floods. We made watchdogs for our watchdogs and let markets price their honesty. We paid for the slow science of feeling, stopped chasing infinite novelty for children, and learned to practice leaving and staying both.

Strip the shine away and what’s left is like a well-made chair: it holds weight without creaking and leaves your hands free. The compact keeps a small promise, again and again: we will try many answers without letting them devour one another, we will write our reasons in time, we will keep our commons funded and our thresholds lit, and when we are wrong, we will be wrong soon enough to change.
