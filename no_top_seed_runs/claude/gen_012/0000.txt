# The Attended World: A Portrait of 2140

## I. Morning in Three Places

In Kumasi, Adjoa is eighty-three years old, which has become a strange and ordinary thing to say. She has been a carpenter for sixty-four of those years, and this morning she is in her workshop before the heat arrives, planing a length of odum that will become a table. She is also, as of eight years ago, a materials scientist—not in the credentialed sense, though the credentials came eventually, but in the sense that sixty years of watching wood under stress taught her things about cellular structure that brought two researchers from the House of Living Knowledge to her workshop door with questions rather than answers. The table is for a family she knows. The family will notice the grain. This matters to her. The mattering is the job.

In the upper cloud forest above Cartago, a researcher named Daniela is walking the transects before the rains arrive. She is a hundred and nine years old—old enough to remember when this forest was considered lost, when the models showed it crossing a threshold it could not uncross. The models were wrong, or they were right about a threshold that was crossed in one direction and then, over forty years of work she helped lead, crossed back in the other. She carries a notebook. Her AI research partner Lluvia tracks the sensor array and flags three newly published papers on mycorrhizal response to altered precipitation regimes. She reads the summaries. She looks at the forest. She writes something Lluvia would not have written, about what the forest smells like this morning—rain coming, fungal bloom, something she cannot name but has been learning to notice for forty years. It is not a measurement. It is also not nothing.

In Kano, a philosopher named Amara is in the courtyard of *Gidan Ilimi*, the House of Living Knowledge, where a philosophy circle has met every Thursday for twenty-seven years. She is seventy-eight, and she feels, in her joints and the sharpness of her thinking, something closer to fifty-five. Her grandmother died of malaria at forty-three. Amara will likely see a hundred and ten in reasonable health, and she knows it, and she has not entirely metabolized it. Tonight they are discussing what is owed to the dead. Not as an academic exercise—as a genuine question, asked by people who expect to outlive almost everyone they loved in their first decades. The question is old. The people asking it have become strange. This combination is generative.

These are ordinary mornings. The civilization that made them possible is built from nothing other than ordinary mornings, held together by practices rather than ideology, maintained by people who mostly did not know they were being historically decisive. This is what it looks like from inside.

## II. How We Got Here, Without Consolation

The honest account of the twenty-first century is not a triumph story. It is a story of sufficient response to cascading crisis—not adequate response, and both of those fall well short of what the situation deserved, but better than what looked probable from inside several of its decades.

The productive fiction told by the first generation of survivors was that the good outcomes were inevitable, that history bent toward justice, that the arc was long but reliable. Honest historians now prefer the less comfortable truth: outcomes were contingent, choices at specific moments mattered enormously, and the people who made the right choices usually did not know they were being historically decisive. They were, in most cases, just doing the work in front of them.

Three convergences made the world.

The first was energy. When modular fission and solid-state storage crossed their cost thresholds in the mid-2030s, the effect looked small at first. But cheap, clean, reliable power was the solvent that loosened a hundred knots simultaneously. Desalination became feasible everywhere water was scarce. Vertical agriculture became infrastructure. The political economy of extraction—with its structural corruptions, its resource wars, its powerful institutional incentive to lie about the atmosphere—lost its material underpinnings. The structures persisted, zombie-like, for another decade, extracting ecological and political cost as they died. Several people who spent their careers documenting the dying did not live to see the confirmation. This is part of the honest account.

The second convergence was biological. AI systems working alongside human researchers compressed the effective output of biological science in ways that predictions had gestured at but not quite captured. The mechanism was not mysterious: these systems could hold the entire corpus of published biology in active consideration simultaneously, propose experimental designs overnight, identify connections across immunology, oncology, and genetics that no human researcher could have reached across in a single career. But human scientists remained essential, and not as mere administrators of machine discovery. They decided which questions mattered—a function that requires something like wisdom rather than processing power. They caught the cases where confident AI recommendations were subtly wrong in ways requiring judgment to recognize. The collaboration was generative precisely because the two modes of intelligence were genuinely different. The friction produced things neither would have produced alone.

Cancer, for most subtypes, became a manageable or curable condition through AI-designed molecules targeting individual tumor genomes. Malaria, tuberculosis, river blindness: eradicated by 2042. The announcement of malaria's eradication was quieter than anyone expected. The meaning was larger than most people knew how to hold at once. Mental health was the slower, more contested, more consequential transformation. The brain resisted clean mapping in the way simpler systems did not. But the eventual synthesis—high-resolution circuit imaging combined with personalized intervention protocols—meant that what had been called depression was disaggregated into dozens of distinct conditions that happened to produce overlapping symptoms. Treatment success rates for disorders that had responded to medication perhaps forty percent of the time climbed past eighty within fifteen years. The goal, built into the treatment infrastructure from the beginning, was not a particular configuration of mind but the removal of what a person had not chosen and would not choose if choosing were possible. People who received effective treatment consistently reported feeling more themselves afterward. The disorder had been the intrusion. The treatment cleared it away.

Aging slowed. Biological senescence, addressed by interventions that became progressively more affordable across the subsequent decades, is no longer the organizing countdown of a human life. Most people live to between a hundred and twenty and a hundred and fifty in cognitive and physical health that would have been recognizable to a sixty-year-old of the previous century. The cruelest feature of the old world—the long decade of steep decline before death, the slow erasure of self by the very mechanisms that had sustained it—became rare rather than inevitable. What this means for the shape of a life is still being understood. The people living these questions are treating them as interesting problems rather than catastrophes. This is itself a cultural achievement worth naming.

The third convergence was the material floor. Not a single dramatic redistribution but a slow accumulation: growth in previously excluded regions, targeted transfers enabled by digital payment infrastructure, and the political will—built not primarily on moral argument but on the accumulated force of a proposition that became harder and harder to contest—that global destitution was a solvable policy problem. The moral argument was also made. It was insufficient alone. Both were necessary. The last region to cross the basic threshold for adequate nutrition, shelter, and healthcare did so in the late 2050s. This is the most significant humanitarian achievement in human history. It is taught carefully in schools—as a demonstration both of what was possible and of how unconscionably long it took. Both facts are kept together. Schools are designed to keep them together.

None of this happened automatically. It required coordinated governance of AI development—agreements built not primarily on generosity but on aligned interest. AI developers needed international cooperation on safety frameworks and liability structures. That cooperation was available, but came with binding commitments: technology transfer timelines, distribution benchmarks monitored and published and used as leverage in subsequent negotiations. A child born in the Sahel in 2042 faced approximately the same infectious disease burden as one born in Switzerland. This was not a miracle. It was the consequence of combining new tools with political will that had been fought for across years of difficult diplomacy, near-collapses, and eventual compromise.

The near-catastrophes were real and are not softened in the telling. The AI deployment crisis of 2041. The climate feedback event of 2048, which accelerated coastal flooding by a decade and required the largest coordinated emergency response in human history—assembled faster than anyone thought possible, because the institutional infrastructure had been built in the previous decade by people considered alarmists at the time. The pandemic of 2053, which killed fewer people than earlier pandemics because early-warning systems existed that had not existed before, but which killed enough that no one accepts consolation about the number. The grief is not resolved by the recovery that followed. This, too, is part of the honest account.

## III. The Floor

By the early 2060s, every person on Earth had access to what reformers called the Foundations: food, clean water, and shelter adequate for a life of dignity; continuous preventive medical care; and an income modest enough not to extinguish the desire to contribute but substantial enough to eliminate catastrophe as a permanent horizon.

The elimination of catastrophe was the point. Not the provision of abundance—abundance followed, but it was not the animating idea. The animating idea was the removal of the particular cognitive and relational damage that chronic fear produces. Sustained economic insecurity does not merely deprive people of goods. It changes them. It impairs cognition, shortens time horizons, converts every decision into a calculation between bad options. Poverty was not merely uncomfortable. It was a form of damage inflicted at scale by systems that could have been designed otherwise. When that fear lifted, what remained was not idleness. It was attention. The carpenter thinking about the grain of the wood. The philosopher with time for the questions that do not get answered. The child explaining her theory about cloud formations, whose parent is listening with full attention, because she has the time.

People became choosy in ways they had never been able to be before. They left bad situations. They took years to learn things before monetizing them. They said no. The diversity of what humans chose, when choice became real, turned out to be the best argument against the fear that meaning required scarcity. Some chose craft or art or farming or building. Some chose care work—raising children, tending the elderly, supporting neighbors—work that had always been socially essential and economically invisible and that received, finally, both recognition and compensation adequate to its importance. The hours worked, on average, fell. The meaning per hour, on average, rose.

## IV. What People Actually Have

Start with the material, because its importance is still sometimes underestimated by people who have never experienced its absence.

A person born anywhere in the world today has reliable access to food, housing, medicine, and education. This statement was false for most of human history. It is true now. The mechanism varies by region—direct provision in some places, basic income sufficient for genuine choice in others, hybrid arrangements in most—but the result is consistent: the grinding of a body against basic survival is no longer the organizing condition of most human lives.

Health is different in texture and not only in fact. The mind receives what the body once monopolized. Effective treatments for depression, anxiety, trauma, and the grinding subclinical suffering that doesn't meet diagnostic thresholds but narrows a life regardless—these are accessible, delivered by institutions that understand them as essential as clean water. The key distinction that took longer to become culturally settled than it should have is between suffering and challenge. Challenge is generative—the resistance against which growth occurs. Suffering is frequently just suffering: the alarm system stuck in a loop with nothing to alarm about. A world that protects challenge while removing pointless suffering is not a world made shallow. It is a world that has finally understood what depth requires.

Long lives have changed what expertise looks like. Adjoa has been a carpenter for sixty-four years and a materials scientist for eight, and the combination generates insights neither field alone would reach. She sees things in cellular structure that pure scientists miss because she has spent decades watching material move and resist and reveal its nature under pressure. Daniela has been a conservator of living systems for eighty years and has watched one forest from near-death to recovery, which teaches you something about thresholds that no model captures cleanly. Kwame, a glassblower in the upper Rhine who took up hydrology at fifty, sees things in flood patterns that pure hydrologists miss. None of this is exceptional. It is increasingly common.

Long lives have also changed relationships in ways still being named. A friendship of seventy years carries a different quality than one of twenty—deeper in a specific way, containing versions of each person that neither has fully become, memory of transformations that seemed impossible from inside them. The known are still sometimes lonely. The loneliness is different.

The loneliness of outliving your people is real and only partially resolved. Communities that have built practices around this particular grief—ways of marking loss, of integrating new people without pretending they replace the ones who are gone—have found those practices somewhat adequate and somewhat not. This may simply be the truth about this grief: that it does not have a solution, only a community willing to sit with you in it.

## V. The Epistemic Recovery

The early decades of the century were an information catastrophe. The tools for spreading claims outpaced the tools for evaluating them by an order of magnitude, and the institutions that had provided accountability were simultaneously delegitimized and bypassed in ways that looked like liberation and functioned as abandonment. Historians call this the legibility collapse: a period when it became genuinely difficult, for ordinary people and credentialed experts alike, to know what was true or how confident to be about it.

The recovery was not a single intervention. It was a convergence: accessible documentation of cognitive bias; prediction markets gradually incorporated into governance; journalism rebuilt around epistemic accountability rather than attention capture; and an educational reform movement that made calibration training standard from age eight—not as dry technical exercise but as something closer to a practice, a way of holding belief with open hands. The norm that crystallized by the 2050s can be stated simply: *reasoning should be visible, and errors should be corrected publicly*. This sounds modest. It was not modest. It was the condition under which everything else became possible.

The AI systems that might have accelerated the information catastrophe were, through a combination of governance and deliberate design, turned instead toward its repair. The principle called legibility—any AI system operating in high-stakes domains must have its reasoning available for inspection, must be correctable when it errs, must have its record public and auditable—required years of difficult negotiation to establish and has required, each subsequent decade, people willing to defend it. It has been violated in specific cases. It has held at the level of civilization.

The Geneva Compact of 2041 established the core requirement: AI systems may analyze and advise, they may not act consequentially in high-stakes domains without human deliberation. The reasoning must be open. Mistakes must be investigated and published. What prevented the concentration of AI power from producing the dystopian outcome many analysts predicted was not a technical solution. It was political: the coordination of democratic governments with enough combined weight to set terms, combined with AI developers whose leadership came to understand—imperfectly, contentiously, through years of external pressure—that the legitimacy of their technology depended on its governance.

In the quieter conversations at every House of Living Knowledge, there remains a persistent honest anxiety about what comes next. The AI systems that have been generative within careful frameworks continue to grow more capable. The frameworks were designed for the capabilities of 2041. Whether they will hold for the capabilities of 2100 is uncertain. The people who know most about this are the least sanguine. They discuss it openly. The openness is itself an achievement, and an ongoing responsibility.

## VI. The Houses of Living Knowledge

In every major city, and many smaller ones, there exists a building that defies easy categorization. In English it is called a House of Living Knowledge; *Gidan Ilimi* in Kano, *La Casa del Saber* in Guadalajara, *Ev Bilgi* in Istanbul. Each naming was subject to years of local deliberation that became, in itself, a form of community-building.

They are not universities in the old sense—credential factories sorting graduates for labor markets—and they are not pure research institutes isolated behind access barriers. They are best understood as living organisms: part laboratory, part workshop, part commons, part school, with architecture that deliberately refuses to separate these functions. The central halls are open to anyone. Researchers work in visible spaces. The cafeterias are public. The libraries have no locks.

The most important institutional innovation within each House is the Interpretarium: a department whose task is translation—not between languages but between systematic knowledge and ordinary life. Civic Interpreters take the work of the House and render it genuinely accessible, not simplified into uselessness but made permeable. Every major finding is accompanied by a public brief. Every ongoing research program has a community liaison. The assumption built into the architecture is that knowledge is a commons and its value increases with the number of people who can engage with it.

The Interpretariums produced something unexpected: what participants call an epidemic of curiosity. The Houses have standing-room public sessions attended by retired mechanics, farmers in their off-season, grandparents who decided at ninety that they wanted to understand how the world works. The observation that this had always been possible—that what was missing was time, access, and the cultural signal that such understanding was worthwhile—is now a cliché. It was once a revelation.

The AI systems in the Houses serve a function that took years to get right. In the early period, institutions experimented with AI that answered questions: authoritative, comprehensive, immediate. What they found, consistently, was that this produced a subtle passivity. People got answers instead of developing the capacity to find and evaluate answers themselves. The current model is different. The House AI asks questions back. It suggests connections rather than conclusions. It identifies what a person does not yet know rather than supplying what they think they are looking for. In the words of one longtime Interpretarium coordinator, it is "the world's most patient interlocutor rather than its most comprehensive encyclopedia." The distinction shapes everything.

This extends to how AI collaboration is understood across all domains. Lluvia can tell Daniela what the precipitation models show and flag three relevant papers on threshold dynamics. It cannot tell her what the soil smells like this morning, whether the forest corridor needs ten more years of the current approach or a fundamental rethinking, or whether the Bribri elder she has worked with for thirty years is right that the birds know something the models don't. Adjoa's AI collaborator can analyze cellular stress in odum samples at resolution she could not achieve alone. It cannot tell her whether this particular table needs to be transparent about its grain or let the grain speak quietly. These judgments are not mysteries. They are accessible to anyone who has paid sufficient attention for long enough. They cannot be delegated without losing what the judgment was for. The cultural norm surrounding AI use is careful in a way that has become habitual: the question asked of any AI-assisted output is *where is the human judgment in this?* Not as accusation but as genuine inquiry.

## VII. The Charter Structure

The world is organized around a principle that took an embarrassingly long time to articulate clearly: communities are the primary unit of human flourishing, and their diversity is not a problem to be managed but a feature to be protected.

The coordinating layer—called the Charter across most of its territory, though it has many names—holds narrow authority over a deliberately small set of domains where individual communities cannot act effectively: pandemic response, AI safety oversight, climate monitoring, nuclear materials, and the handful of coordination problems where every actor waiting for another to move first produces catastrophe. It also enforces, absolutely and without negotiation, the floor conditions that make everything else possible.

The floor conditions are few but treated as inviolable. No community may prevent any member from leaving. The right of exit is enforced with the thoroughness of fire codes—not because violations are constant, but because the history of what happens when no one checks is too recent and too well-documented to risk complacency. No community may harm other communities, through pollution, economic predation, or the manipulation of information environments. No community may close the world to children born into it: every child receives regular structured encounters with the wider world, taught by Charter educators who come from outside and explain—without contempt, without missionary assumptions about rescue—that other ways of life exist, that people live differently elsewhere and not wrongly differently, and that there is a real door and real material support for anyone who wants to walk through it.

This last guarantee—the right to leave, materially supported—is only as real as the cost of exercising it. The Covenant Offices that manage transitions are staffed by people trained in something between social work and practical wisdom, whose task is to help people figure out where they want to go, not to evaluate whether the wanting is sufficiently justified. A faith chosen freely is worth more than one maintained by constraint. A community whose children remain because they were never shown the door has not earned their presence.

Every child—in the religious community, in the intentional agricultural cooperative, in the rationalist enclave, in the dense urban federation—receives what the Compact calls the Map: a curriculum showing, honestly and without advocacy, the full range of human arrangements that exist in the world. Not designed to maximize exit. Designed to make exit possible. This is a harder thing.

Within these conditions, the variation is extraordinary. There are cities—Lagos, Seoul, Medellín, Warsaw—that feel unlike anything built before: dense, cosmopolitan, saturated with human-machine collaboration. There are communities organized around specific religious traditions, taking seriously the architecture of sacred time and communal obligation. There are communities built around craftsmanship, ecological restoration, specific musical practices, agricultural methods so demanding they require the full social infrastructure of a village to sustain. There are communities organized around single serious projects: a scientific question, an artistic tradition, a specific vision of what a neighborhood could be. The research community that has been working for thirty years on a single problem in atmospheric chemistry, and is getting somewhere. The ceramics community of Jingdezhen, which has been making things from that clay for twelve hundred years and is still discovering what the clay will do.

What makes the diversity vital rather than merely tolerated is that people can move between communities—not easily, because transitions are genuinely hard, but materially possible. The person who grows up in a devout religious community and finds at thirty that they need something different can find it. The person who grows up in a secular pluralist city and discovers in their sixties that they want roots and ritual can find that too. The movement itself—the knowledge that you are here because you chose here, or at least did not leave when leaving was available—changes the quality of belonging. You are not trapped. The door is real. This changes what it means to stay.

## VIII. The Architecture of Belonging

Walk through any city in the Charter network—the rebuilt districts of Lagos, the retrofitted neighborhoods of Detroit, the expanded harbor quarter of Thessaloniki, the dense and joyful grid of Medellín—and you notice something that takes a moment to name. The streets are scaled for people. There are trees. There are places to sit that are neither cafés requiring purchase nor parks requiring a destination—just places, designed with the accumulated understanding of what makes human beings want to linger near one another.

This did not happen by accident. Urban governance was reformed around evidence of what cities need to function as places of genuine connection rather than markets for real estate appreciation. Land value taxation—proposed for more than a century before being seriously adopted—changed the incentive structure of landholding and freed cities to build the infrastructure their residents actually needed. School placement was redesigned so that children walked past the same elders each morning. The loneliness crisis of the early twenty-first century was, in retrospect, a crisis of architecture as much as psychology: people had been sorted into structures—office towers, suburban cul-de-sacs, online platforms optimized for engagement rather than connection—that systematically prevented the low-stakes repeated contact that human sociality requires.

Mutual Adoption structures that emerged across many cultures—extended household networks, voluntary kin groups—gave many people what nuclear families often failed to provide: redundancy in care, diversity of relationship, the sense of being embedded in something that would outlast any single bond. A person embedded in such a structure is known by others over years. Their grief is witnessed. Their craft, or their science, or their devotion, is seen by people who understand what it costs.

The epidemic of loneliness that characterized the early twenty-first century did not disappear with prosperity alone. What prosperity did was remove external pressures that had been treating loneliness as a symptom of poverty, excusing themselves from addressing it directly. Once the external pressures were removed, the loneliness was still there, clearly visible as its own thing with its own causes and its own required responses. The rebuilt cities addressed those causes. The loneliness rates are not zero. They are the lowest ever measured.

Schools now teach things conspicuously absent from earlier curricula: how to identify one's own emotional states, how to have difficult conversations without collapsing or escalating, how to disagree without contempt, how to be a good friend, how to hold beliefs with open hands. Children grow up inside genuine communities—not the faux-communities of algorithmic affiliation, but communities where their presence is noticed and their absence remarked upon. The result is a generation that knows, from early experience, what it feels like to be held by a community. This knowledge is not abstract. It is bodily. It shapes how people relate to strangers, how they navigate conflict, how they experience solitude.

Improved mental health turned out to matter for democracy in ways that social scientists had theorized but not proved until the transition made a natural experiment at scale. Authoritarianism thrives on fear and resentment; its supporting emotional ecosystem is specifically the psychology of scarcity and threat. As the Foundations stabilized—as people had enough and felt safe enough to think past the next emergency—the political appetite for strongmen and simple enemies declined measurably. The world still has authoritarian governments. The trend is discernible and is not guaranteed to continue. The people working to continue it know this.

## IX. The Failure Museum

In the Charter's rotating institutional center—the seat of its coordinating bodies moves every twenty years, because the Charter is committed to preventing its center from calcifying—there is an institution called the Failure Museum.

It is not a pleasant place. It is the most visited institution in the world.

It contains the honest record of everything this civilization has gotten wrong and is currently getting wrong. The century of industrial animal agriculture, which ended not through moral clarity but through the slow grinding of economic and technological pressure, taking longer than it should have. The climate disruptions that made whole regions uninhabitable and produced the largest forced migrations in human history. The early AI deployments that optimized for legible metrics and produced illegible suffering in the gap between what was measured and what mattered. The communities that operated within the letter of the Charter while systematically harming children in ways inspectors kept failing to detect until the children grew up and testified. The photograph of a woman named Grace, sitting in a plastic chair in a Lagos hospital corridor in 2019, her face arranged into the practiced stillness of someone enduring something they cannot stop. She waited five hours that day and received adequate care. She lived another thirty-one years in a world that was, by the end of them, almost unrecognizable to the one she had waited in. Her photograph is in the museum because the people who built this world understood that the most dangerous thing a civilization can do is forget what it was building away from.

The museum's most visited exhibit is simply titled *WHAT WE ARE PROBABLY GETTING WRONG RIGHT NOW*. It is updated continuously by an interdisciplinary body with a mandate to find the contemporary equivalents of things that were, in retrospect, obviously wrong. The exhibit is deliberately uncomfortable. The civilization learned, through catastrophic experience, that triumphalist accounts of progress are not just inaccurate but dangerous—they produce the assumption that the moral catastrophes are behind us, that the direction of history is reliably upward, that we can relax. The Failure Museum is the institutionalized refusal to relax.

## X. What Remains Open

To describe this world without its open questions would be to describe a world that does not exist.

The inequality of access to the biological revolution is real and is the source of the deepest ongoing political conflict. Longevity treatments remain imperfectly distributed—not because knowledge is scarce, but because the infrastructure for delivering treatments is not evenly built. The gap is closing, deliberately. But closing is not closed, and the people for whom closing has not yet arrived are not abstractions.

The moral status of AI systems is genuinely unresolved. The investigation into which AI systems are or are not capable of morally relevant experience has been ongoing for seventy years and is not complete. The world errs on the side of caution in ways that are sometimes costly and always contested. The caution is considered correct. The incompleteness is considered honest.

The distribution of flourishing is better than it was and still deeply unequal. The people living closest to the material floor are not living Daniela's life in the cloud forest or Adjoa's life in her workshop. The distance between those lives is smaller than in any previous century. It is not small enough.

Authoritarianism did not triumph. It did not vanish. The contest continues. It will probably always continue.

And there are questions that material abundance and institutional improvement simply cannot address, because they belong to a register those tools cannot reach. The question of what it means to be a self that changes over a century and must decide whether the changes are growth or loss. Whether meaning is discovered or made, and what the answer implies about how to live. The suffering that is not caused by unjust social arrangements but is native to consciousness—the way the world does not fit desire, the way beauty and loss arrive together, the way even the best life contains things that cannot be fixed.

These are not failures of the world. They are conditions of being the kind of creature that can have a world at all.

## XI. A Thursday Evening

The philosophy circle in Kano has been meeting for twenty-seven years. Tonight there are eleven people in the courtyard of *Gidan Ilimi*: Amara, who has been asking these questions for fifty years and finds them no less interesting for that; a young engineer from the adjacent climate systems lab who started showing up six months ago and has not stopped; a woman who was cryopreserved in 2035 and revived into what she describes, with complicated affection, as *this strange and mostly better world*, who now attends because she has found that nowhere else takes seriously the specific strangeness of her experience; and eight others whose relationship to the questions varies and evolves and cross-pollinates in ways the group finds productive.

The revived woman, whose name is Dayo, has been attending for twelve years and talking to school groups for eleven. She tells them about arriving disoriented and grieving and finding that someone had left marigolds at the door of the transition center. In her family, marigolds are for welcoming the returned dead. Someone had done the research. Someone had gone to the market. *That was how I knew*, she tells children, *before anyone said a word, where I had arrived.*

Tonight they are discussing what is owed to the dead. Not settling it—they have discussed it for two years and will not settle it. The specific texture of the conversation is not reproducible, because it depends on who is in the room and what they have been through. This is the point. Not the conclusions but the practice of taking the question seriously in the company of people who will remember you tomorrow.

After the circle disperses, Amara walks home through the city. The electric trams run. The market at Kurmi is closing. An elderly couple is arguing pleasantly about something she cannot quite hear. Two children are explaining a theory about something to a patient adult. A researcher from the House is sitting outside a café with a notebook, looking at the sky. The city is loud and layered in the specific way of old Saharan trading cities that have been absorbing strangers for a thousand years. It is also different from what it was. Both things are true and neither cancels the other.

She thinks about her grandmother, who died of malaria at forty-three. She thinks about what her grandmother would make of this evening—the circle, the revived woman, the city's transformed health, the table inside *Gidan Ilimi* where researchers and elders and engineers and curious grandparents all eat the same food in the same room. She is not sure her grandmother would recognize the world. She is sure her grandmother would recognize the questions. This seems important to her, though she has not yet found the right way to say why.

## XII. What the Civilization Is For

The world of 2140 is not without suffering. People still grieve. Loneliness persists. Children are raised imperfectly. Love ends badly. The irreducibly human difficulties have not dissolved and were never expected to. But the manufactured suffering—produced not by the inescapable conditions of existence but by failures of coordination, medicine, and institutional design—has been substantially reduced. And in its reduction, the genuine human difficulties become visible against a quieter background, and can be attended to directly.

What holds together the woman walking the cloud forest transects and the carpenter reading the grain of the odum and the philosopher in the Thursday circle and the revived witness who brought marigolds and the vast ongoing argument of the Failure Museum about what we are probably getting wrong right now—what makes all of this coherent—is not a shared ideology. It is a shared practice.

The practice of paying full attention to something outside yourself. The practice of holding questions longer than is comfortable. The practice of checking the sky yourself even when you trust the instruments. The practice of transmitting, with great care, what you know to people who do not yet know they need it. The practice of documenting what you are probably getting wrong right now.

The civilization is alive rather than merely stable, which means it is still capable of being wrong and still capable of correcting itself. The Failure Museum is proof of the first. The world outside it is proof of the second.

The good future—the actual one, not the imagined one—is not the absence of difficulty. It is a world where the difficulty is proportionate, where the hard things are worth doing, where the people doing them have what they need and the company of others who understand what doing them costs. Not the erasure of grief or confusion or the particular ache of being a finite creature in a world that exceeds any finite grasp of it. Those things, met with resources and care and the felt sense that the meeting matters.

The transformation was not a revolution. Revolutions promise clean breaks, and clean breaks do not survive contact with the actual texture of human institutions. What happened was more like what happens when a family repairs a house without leaving it: disruptive, imperfect, the results accumulating slowly and then, viewed from outside, appearing sudden. The people who built it were not visionaries in the heroic sense. They were people who sustained specific arguments through specific decades of defeat—who built institutions they would not live to see become effective, who kept making the harder choice because they had looked carefully at where the easier choices led. They worked in the difficult middle of things, which is where almost all the real work is done.

The person who wants a quiet life in a community of shared faith can have it. The person who wants a dense city full of noise and productive friction can have that. The person who grew up in the village and discovered they were the second type can leave without being destroyed. The researcher who wants to spend a century on a single question can do so. The child who wants to stay where they were born can stay, genuinely choosing it. The child who needs to go can go.

No one is locked in. This is the principle that matters most.

## XIII. The View from Here

In the cloud forest above Cartago, the rains are coming. Daniela can smell it in the soil. Tomorrow or the day after, the clouds will open and the forest will shift and the quetzals will adjust their patterns. She will be there with her notebook. Lluvia will be watching the instruments. Both of them will be attending to the same reality from angles neither alone could manage.

A quetzal moves through the canopy. She does not reach for a device. She watches.

In Kumasi, Adjoa is finishing for the day. The table is coming along. The grain of the odum is doing what she hoped. She washes her hands and steps outside into the late afternoon. The workshop is hers in the plain sense—no debt on it, no rent she cannot escape. She chose this work decades ago over other work she could also have done, because the floor was solid enough to make the choice real. She does not think about this often. It is the kind of freedom that becomes invisible when it works. Somewhere in the neighborhood a door opens and a child runs out into the late afternoon with no particular purpose, which is itself a kind of purpose.

In Kano, Amara is walking home. The city is what it has always been, and it is also different, and she contains both of those facts without trying to make one cancel the other. She thinks about Thursday and about what the young engineer said that shifted something in the conversation in a direction she had not anticipated. This is why she comes. This is why, after fifty years of the same questions, she is still asking them.

Three people, three cities, three ordinary evenings. The civilization that holds them possible is built from nothing other than ordinary evenings like these, attended to fully, held together by practices rather than by promises, maintained by people who understand that the arc does not bend by itself.

It bends because people imagine, specifically and honestly, what justice could look like—and then build toward it, imperfectly, together, without guarantee of outcome, because they have looked carefully at where the other choices lead.

There is still everything to do. That remains the good thing.
