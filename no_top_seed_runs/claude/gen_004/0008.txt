# The Living World: Notes Toward a Civilization Worth Having

## I. Wednesday

On a Wednesday morning in Medellín, a woman named Yael is sitting across from her grandmother at a kitchen table, arguing about whether gratitude is owed to the dead.

Her grandmother is ninety-three and feels, in most ways, like a woman in her late fifties. She was a child during what people now call the Difficult Decades—the long turbulent middle of the twenty-first century, when the old world was dying and the new one had not yet decided to be born. She remembers the anxiety of it: the climate disruptions, the economic dislocations, the sense that the institutions charged with protecting ordinary people were held by forces that had other priorities. She does not glamorize those years. She is, however, fond of arguing about them.

Yael is twenty-eight. She was born into the world that came after. She finds her grandmother's arguments about obligation and debt slightly irritating, the way young people have always found their elders' insistence on difficulty irritating, and she suspects her grandmother knows this and does not mind.

This is where we begin—not with the institutions or the technologies or the governance frameworks, but with the table, the argument, the two women who disagree across a distance of sixty-five years and love each other anyway. Everything else described in these pages exists to make that table possible. The structures are the arch. This is the space the arch protects.

But the arch is worth describing, because it did not build itself.

## II. What Changed and How

The transformation that produced Yael's world was not a revolution. It was a long accumulation of decisions, each made under uncertainty, each reversible in principle though costly in practice, each dependent on decisions that had come before and making possible decisions that came after. This is worth stating plainly because the stories people prefer to tell about transformation tend toward the dramatic—the breakthrough, the founding moment, the singular act of will. The actual story is less satisfying and more important: it was grinding, incremental, political, and contingent, and it required people in every decade to choose to continue it without knowing whether it would succeed.

The first cluster of changes was biological, and it came faster than almost anyone predicted. AI systems working alongside human researchers proved capable of holding the entire accumulated literature of biology in active consideration simultaneously—proposing connections between immunology and oncology that no single human career had room to make, designing experimental variations overnight, identifying patterns across datasets too large for unaided human attention. The human researchers remained essential; they decided which questions mattered, interpreted whether answers made sense, caught the confident errors that machine systems kept producing without apparent awareness. The partnership was genuinely a partnership, which distinguished it from both the utopian scenarios in which machines did everything and the dystopian ones in which they did it badly and alone.

By the 2040s, most cancers were curable or manageable. The infectious diseases that had killed equatorial populations for centuries—malaria, tuberculosis, the slow parasitic erasure of childhood potential—were eradicated through a combination of new pharmaceuticals and logistics systems intelligent enough to reach settlements without roads. Mental health treatments were finally working, not by engineering happiness but by clearing away the disorders that had been the intrusion, returning people to themselves. Aging—the cruelest part of it, the long decade of steep decline that preceded death—was slowed without abolishing death itself, which most people, on reflection, did not want to abolish.

None of this would have meant what it means if it had remained geographically concentrated. The history of medicine until then was the history of treatments moving from wealthy nations to poor ones across gaps of two or three decades, if ever. The deliberate reversal of that pattern was achieved through a combination of AI-assisted logistics that made last-mile healthcare dramatically cheaper, international agreements tying wealthy-nation access to AI governance frameworks to genuine commitments on technology transfer, and the slow strengthening of governance institutions in what had been called developing nations—more competent, less captured, more capable of deploying complex systems than their predecessors. A child born in Kano in 2048 faces approximately the same disease burden as a child born in Oslo. This sentence would have seemed fantastical in 2025. In 2052 it is ordinary, which is the best possible outcome for a sentence.

The second cluster of changes was economic. The disruption of automation in the 2030s was real and painful, falling heaviest on people who had least—mid-career workers in routine jobs in mid-sized cities who had neither the resources to relocate nor the educational infrastructure to retrain. This produced genuine suffering and came close, more than once, to derailing everything else. What salvaged it was not any single policy but a combination: retraining programs designed with AI assistance that actually worked, portable benefits decoupled from employment, and versions of universal basic income funded through productivity dividends that were politically possible only because the case for their economic sustainability had finally become persuasive to a sufficient majority. These were political victories. They required democratic majorities to demand them across multiple election cycles, against organized resistance, without guarantee of success. Several countries failed. Most eventually didn't.

The floor of material sufficiency—food, shelter, medicine, education, an income sufficient for dignity—became infrastructure rather than charity. Not because scarcity was abolished, but because a sufficient number of people decided it should be treated that way. What people chose, when choice became real, turned out to be the best argument against the fear that meaning required scarcity: some chose leisure, which turned out to be fine; some chose care work; some chose craft or study or the thousand activities humans had always wanted to prioritize and had always been told they could not afford.

## III. The Covenant of Community

The social architecture that emerged is harder to describe than the medical or economic changes, because it resists clean summary. The term people use is the Covenant, though the name is contested and the concept is permanently under argument.

The Covenant is not a government, exactly, though it performs governmental functions. It is closer to a framework: a small number of absolute guarantees wrapped around a deliberately protected space of radical community variation. The guarantees are few and held firmly. No person may be held against their will. No child may be prevented from learning that other ways of life exist and that they may choose them. No community may externalize its costs—whether carbon, or violence, or the slow erosion of shared resources—onto those outside it. And every person, at any point in their life, may present themselves at a Covenant embassy and receive, within seventy-two hours, transportation to anywhere they choose and the basic material conditions for starting over.

Within these constraints, the variation is extraordinary.

There are communities organized around religious traditions that would have been recognizable to observers from the nineteenth century—specific practices, specific understandings of family and obligation and sacred time—that neither require external validation nor impose themselves on anyone outside their membership. There are communities where the social architecture around food, or sleep, or physical movement, has been collectively designed based on what behavioral research suggests actually supports the health their members have chosen to prioritize. There are communities that have made deliberate choices about technological pace, living in ways that look substantially like 2025 or even earlier, not from ignorance but from considered preference. There are communities where relationships take forms that have no names in the old vocabulary.

The diversity is not maintained by accident. It is maintained through continuous, unglamorous effort, most of which is boring and important.

The hardest work involves children. Every community that raises children within its norms must negotiate with the Covenant about education. The negotiation is genuine—the Covenant is not trying to produce uniformity, and a Covenant education delivered by someone who despises the community delivering it is not actually serving its purpose. The goal is specific and narrow: every child must understand, before they are old enough to make meaningful choices, that they are not trapped. That the world is larger than their community. That people live differently elsewhere, not wrongly differently, just differently. And that there is a specific door they can walk through if they want to.

The educators trained to deliver this curriculum are chosen for their ability to do it without contempt—without the missionary's assumption that rescue is the point. Most of the children they teach will stay where they are, and this is considered a good outcome when the staying is genuine choice. The curriculum is not designed to maximize exit. It is designed to make exit possible, which is a different thing. Exit being possible is what makes staying a genuine decision rather than an unchosen fate.

The second hardest work involves externalities. Communities that want to live according to their own values must not impose the costs of those values on others. This sounds simple and is not. Carbon pricing, finally implemented at honest levels, handles the most obvious category. But there are subtler flows in both directions: communities that produce unusually strong social trust, or exceptionally well-educated children, or beautiful public spaces, are generating benefits that spill beyond their borders, and the Covenant tries to recognize this in how it allocates resources. Communities that maintain their norms through shame or social pressure that rises to the level of coercion—that produce people too broken to make use of their theoretical right to leave—are not exempt from scrutiny simply because they have not technically violated the Covenant's letter. There are inspectors. There is measurement. There is a permanent argument about where the line is between a community that asks hard things of its members and one that damages them, and this argument does not resolve cleanly, and the Covenant has learned to be comfortable with that.

The path being open—all the paths—is what makes choosing any particular one a genuine expression of who you are.

## IV. The Houses

In every city of significant size, there is now a building that defies easy categorization. In English they are called Houses of Knowledge, though the names vary by region and the naming was subject to years of sometimes contentious local deliberation. They are not universities in the old sense—credentialing factories sorting graduates for labor markets—and they are not research institutes isolated behind access barriers. They are organisms: part laboratory, part workshop, part commons, part school, with the membrane between professional and public deliberately kept thin.

Researchers work in visible spaces. Citizens may walk through, watch, ask questions, and occasionally propose ideas that turn out to matter. The cafeterias are open to anyone. The results of publicly funded research are published within days of completion, translated by AI systems working alongside human editors into language accessible to a general reader. Within each House, AI systems function as extraordinary collaborators—tireless, capable of reading entire fields overnight, skilled at identifying the connections no individual human could hold in mind across a single career. They do not lead. They illuminate. Senior researchers spend considerable time learning which questions to ask their AI partners, and considerable time learning to be suspicious of answers that arrive too easily.

The history of the 2030s—when AI systems were deployed in research and policy contexts with insufficient oversight and produced confident, well-argued, and occasionally catastrophic recommendations—left a deep institutional scar that became a deep institutional wisdom. The framework governing AI use in consequential decisions was established through international agreements in the early 2040s. The principle is consistent across domains: AI systems may analyze, model, and advise. Every consequential decision requires human deliberation, with dissent recorded, reasoning audited, and outcomes tracked and published. Mistakes are investigated, and the results of those investigations are public. This is not a perfect system. It is a mature one that has so far held against the pressures expected to break it.

What the Houses have made possible, beyond the specific advances in medicine and materials and agriculture and energy, is something less tangible and equally important: a relationship between knowledge and ordinary life that is different in quality from what came before. The distance between the discovery and the person it affects has shrunk. The sense that science happens somewhere else, to other people, by a priesthood with its own inscrutable language, has weakened. Not because knowledge has been made simpler than it is, but because the institutions that hold it have been made more genuinely accessible. This is not a small thing.

## V. The Failure Museum

In the Covenant's central city—which changes location every twenty years, because the Covenant is committed to preventing its center from calcifying—there is an institution called the Failure Museum. It is the most visited institution in the world, and it is not a comfortable place to visit.

The Failure Museum is the honest record of everything this civilization has gotten wrong, is getting wrong, and will probably continue to get wrong. The factory farming that ended not through moral clarity but through the grinding of pressure across decades, taking far longer than it should have, producing suffering of a scale that cannot be calculated and has not been forgiven. The climate disruptions that were foreseeable for a century before anyone did enough about them and that made whole regions uninhabitable and generated the largest forced migrations in human history, disruptions whose effects continue into the present. The AI deployments of the 2030s that optimized for legible metrics and produced illegible suffering before anyone developed the tools to see what was happening. The communities that operated within the letter of the Covenant while breaking children in ways the inspectors kept failing to detect. The confident consensus positions that turned out to be wrong.

Its most visited exhibit is titled WHAT WE ARE PROBABLY GETTING WRONG RIGHT NOW. It is updated continuously by an interdisciplinary body with a mandate to find the contemporary equivalents of the things that look, in retrospect, obviously wrong. The exhibit includes, currently, several areas of AI governance that the oversight frameworks were designed for capabilities that are already outdated; the specific ways in which the economic floor, though real, remains porous for particular populations through mechanisms not yet fully understood; the treatment of certain categories of cognitive difference that the culture has not yet learned to understand rather than manage; and a long, uncomfortable section on the non-human world—the species extinctions that were arrested rather than reversed, the coral systems that were preserved but not restored, the ongoing grief of ecological loss that the world has learned to acknowledge without yet learning to fully address.

The Failure Museum exists because this civilization learned, at great cost, that triumphalist accounts of progress are not merely inaccurate but dangerous. They produce the assumption that the moral catastrophes are behind us, that the direction of history is reliably upward, that we can relax. The Failure Museum is the institutionalized refusal to relax. It is also, surprisingly, one of the most hopeful places people report visiting, because it demonstrates that the capacity to be honest about failure is itself a kind of health—that a civilization capable of this kind of reckoning is more likely to be capable of continuing to improve.

## VI. What Was Not Solved

Loneliness persists. It persists in communities designed specifically to prevent it. Some people are constitutionally isolated, and no architecture fully solves this, though good architecture helps. The loneliness of people who have lived too long and outlasted everyone they loved is a specific kind of grief that did not exist before, and the communities are still learning how to hold it. The Covenant supports bereavement networks with unusual seriousness, and this is considered a medical priority alongside the physical interventions.

Meaning remains difficult. The extension of life has made the question of what to do with it more urgent, not less. Communities that provide strong answers—religious communities, communities organized around serious long-term projects, communities with demanding vocational structures—tend to produce members who flourish on most measures. Communities organized primarily around comfort tend to produce members who are comfortable. These are not the same thing, and the Covenant watches the data without adjudicating between them, except to note that the option to leave in either direction is always open.

Violence has not been eliminated. There is less of it by every measurable metric, and the Covenant's enforcement mechanisms are real and occasionally necessary and occasionally wrong. The question of how much violence is acceptable to prevent violence is not resolved and will not be.

Status competition relocated rather than disappeared. It moved from material survival to the domains of recognition, creativity, and belonging, where it is sometimes more subtle and sometimes no less painful. This is mourned by some and accepted by others as an irreducible feature of what humans are.

## VII. The Quality of Conversation

A visitor from 2025 spending a week in Medellín—or in Kano, or in Gdańsk, or in Chengdu, or in any of a hundred cities across the Covenant—would likely be struck not first by the technology, nor by the absence of poverty, nor even by the visible health of people moving through public spaces. They would be struck by the quality of conversation.

People talk to one another at length, about things that matter, without the anxious quality of people being constantly evaluated and sorted. In the evenings, in market squares, in the cafeterias of the Houses, in community halls, in the courtyards where philosophy circles meet every Thursday for eleven years running—conversations that are not performances and not competitions. Conversations that humans have always been capable of having and have rarely been given time for.

Yael's grandmother has had these conversations for decades. Yael considers them her inheritance.

On this particular Wednesday morning, the argument about what is owed to the dead continues past the coffee and into the late morning, circling without resolving—which is not a failure but the point. The question is old. It has not been answered and will not be. What has changed is that people have time to ask it well, with patience, without the static of desperation. The two women at the kitchen table disagree about obligation and debt and the nature of gratitude, and they love each other, and they have nowhere more important to be.

## VIII. The Conditions of This Morning

This world was not given to its inhabitants. It was built, slowly and imperfectly, through political fights that came close to failing and technical advances that came with genuine costs and moral arguments that took decades to resolve. It was built by people who did not know it would work—who made the choices they made not from certainty about outcomes but from a judgment that those choices were right, and that a world worth having required someone willing to work toward it without guarantee.

The medicine is extraordinary. The institutions are hard-won and worth defending. The technology has changed what is possible in ways that would have seemed miraculous to earlier generations. None of these facts is the most important one.

The most important fact is this: the floor is real, and everyone stands on it. No one is excluded from basic health, basic security, basic education, basic access to the world's knowledge—not by geography, not by ancestry, not by the particular community they were born into. Above the floor, the variation is extraordinary and preserved by design, because the Covenant learned that diversity of human forms of life is not a problem to be solved but an expression of the irreducible variety of what humans genuinely value, and that any civilization mature enough to know this has learned something essential.

Below the floor, no one falls.

This was always possible. The resources were always sufficient. The knowledge was, for decades, sufficient. What was missing was the decision—made collectively, imperfectly, repeatedly, against real opposition—that this was how things should be organized.

The decision is the achievement. Everything else followed from it, including the Wednesday morning, including the kitchen table, including the two women arguing across sixty-five years about what the living owe the dead, which is a question worth arguing about when no one is arguing instead about whether there will be enough to eat.

Yael's grandmother laughs at something her granddaughter says. The light comes through the window at an angle. The city outside goes about its Wednesday. The argument is not finished.

It will not be finished.

This is not a failure of the world. This is the world working.
