# The Inhabited Century: A Vision of Grounded Flourishing

## I. A Tuesday Morning, Twice

In the city of Kumasi, which has grown new neighborhoods into its hills and new gardens onto its rooftops without quite losing the smell of the central market, a woman named Adwoa Mensah sits with her father at breakfast. Her father is one hundred and twelve years old. He remembers, with the specific sharpness that very old people sometimes develop, what it was like to watch his own father die of malaria at fifty-three—a death that was, by the standards of his childhood, ordinary. He is not over it. He does not think he should be over it. He keeps the memory the way a civilization keeps its honest history: not to punish himself, but to understand where he came from and therefore what he is.

Adwoa is forty-one. She is a composer. She has been working on a single piece of music for nine years, and she expects to work on it for nine more, not because she is slow but because the work requires it and she is unhurried and her community is invested in the long unfolding. She does not know, eating breakfast with her father this morning, that the piece will be considered one of the most significant works of her century. She would be embarrassed by this prediction. She is thinking about a transition between movements that is not yet right.

Her father pours more tea and says something about the weather. She agrees. Outside, the city continues.

This is the first thing to say about the world we are describing: it remains a world in which breakfast happens. In which the old grieve specifically and the young work on difficult transitions between movements and the weather is a reasonable topic of conversation. Any vision of the future worth inhabiting must begin here—not because the ordinary is all there is, but because the ordinary is where meaning pools, and meaning is what we were building toward all along.

The second thing to say is that this world was not given. It was built. Every specific good thing in it is the consequence of specific people making specific choices that could have gone otherwise. This fact is taught to children. It is taught not to make them anxious but to make them accurate about how the world works, and to make them understand that they are, themselves, among the people whose choices will matter.

## II. The Biological Revolution, and What It Cost to Get There

The eradication of malaria was announced in 2041 in a press release from the World Health Organization that most people only encountered as a footnote years later. The announcement was quiet. The disease had killed more human beings than any other single cause in recorded history, and its ending arrived not as a thunderclap but as the final accumulation of a thousand decisions: a single-dose vaccine needing no cold chain, a targeted gene drive that collapsed mosquito vector capacity in endemic regions, an AI-optimized distribution campaign that located the last pockets of infection with a precision no human epidemiological team could have matched. The researchers who finished this work celebrated carefully, the way people celebrate when they have been working toward something for a long time and know that celebration is only the threshold of the next problem.

What made the biological revolution possible was the deployment, in the early 2030s, of AI research systems capable of running experiments autonomously, synthesizing a century of literature, and proposing the next study before the current one had cooled—the equivalent, one journalist wrote at the time, of cloning a thousand Nobel laureates and freeing them from grant applications. The analogy was imprecise but useful. The diseases that had been individual catastrophes for individual families—cancer, which was not one disease but thousands; the progressive dementias; the inflammatory conditions that had ground lives into slow impairment—yielded not to single cures but to constellations of precise interventions, each targeted to the particular circuit or metabolic pathway or cellular mechanism that was actually broken in this patient, now. Cancer mortality fell by more than eighty percent between 2035 and 2055. Alzheimer's, which had for a generation consumed the last years of the old and the middle years of those who loved them, became manageable and then largely preventable.

Adwoa's father has never developed Alzheimer's. This is not luck. It is the result of decisions made by researchers and funders and policymakers and logisticians—people who sustained an argument across decades of defeat before the evidence changed the political calculus. He understands this. He is not cavalier about the gift.

The longevity treatments were stranger and more contested than the disease cures. It turned out that aging was not a single process but a collection of interacting degradation loops, and that interrupting several simultaneously produced effects that surprised even the researchers involved. The first generation raised entirely under the new medical regime is now in its thirties. Their expected lifespan is genuinely unknown. This is not a comfortable fact. It is an interesting one, and the civilization that produced it has learned to sit with the interesting and uncomfortable, to hold open questions with curiosity rather than closing them prematurely with false certainty.

The mental health transformation was subtler and, in some ways, more profound. For most of human history, suffering of the mind had been the most invisible form of suffering—invisible to observers, often invisible even to the sufferer, almost entirely invisible to the institutions nominally responsible for addressing it. The AI-accelerated neuroscience of the 2030s and 2040s finally produced the tools to see what was actually happening inside a living brain at something close to the relevant resolution: not individual neurons but circuits, the functional networks that had always mattered and had always been just beyond reach. Once you could measure something precisely, you could understand what was wrong with it. Once you understood what was wrong, you could intervene—with targeted drugs, with precise non-invasive stimulation, with behavioral protocols matched to the specific neural subtype of this person rather than averaged across a population. The AI companions that helped implement these protocols were not replacements for human connection; they were scaffolding that made human connection more possible by reducing the weight that had made connection so difficult.

People who had spent decades managing depression reported that it was simply gone—not suppressed, not compensated for, but resolved, the way an infection resolves when the right antibiotic finally works. The social consequences rippled outward: addiction fell, because much of addiction had always been self-medication; domestic violence fell; the particular cruelty that flows from untreated suffering fell. This was not the end of human difficulty. It was the clearing of manufactured difficulty, which made the irreducibly human difficulties—grief, estrangement, the loneliness of being oneself—more visible against a quieter background. The civilization found, to its own complicated relief, that these genuine difficulties were more workable than the manufactured ones. They were the real problems of being human, and therefore interesting, and therefore worth the effort of engagement.

## III. The Infrastructure of Good Lives

The transformation required, as its foundation, something undramatic: cheap energy and honest cities.

Advanced energy storage and modular fission reactors arrived together in the 2040s, each insufficient alone and together decisive. The cost of electricity fell below anything previously imaginable. Water desalination became trivially affordable. The political economy of hydrocarbon extraction, which had distorted governance globally for a century, dissolved—not instantly and not without genuine disruption to the communities built around it, but irreversibly. The new energy landscape made the developing world's transition to clean infrastructure not a sacrifice but an obvious choice, and the choices cascaded.

The cities changed more slowly. The governance reforms that made them livable—land value taxation, coordinated zoning reform, the patient dismantling of the regulatory frameworks that had made housing expensive in the places where people most needed to live—required political struggle of the grinding ordinary kind. Economists had understood these tools for generations. What changed was the demonstration that they worked, produced by the first wave of experimental cities that adopted them and published their findings honestly, and by a population that had become, over a generation, genuinely better at evaluating evidence.

The legibility of cities—the ability to see clearly what had made them expensive and stratified and lonely, and therefore to change it—was a specific instance of a broader transformation. Prediction markets, legal and regulated, spread across most jurisdictions after high-profile cases in which they had been spectacularly right while official bodies had been embarrassingly wrong. Schools incorporated calibration exercises as a thread woven through every subject: the constant practice of asking *how confident am I, and why, and what would change my mind?* AI tools that could identify logical fallacies, surface unconsidered evidence, and help people trace their own reasoning became as ordinary as calculators—frequently misused, sometimes transformative, gradually increasing the epistemic quality of public life. What emerged over a generation was a population that reasoned better. Not perfectly. But systematically, verifiably, measurably better: more calibrated, more willing to update, less vulnerable to the tribal epistemics that had made the early social media era so corrosive.

The economy of work transformed with the same ambiguity that characterizes all genuine transitions. Automation eliminated categories of labor—steadily, unevenly, and hardest on people in middle age who had built identities around work that disappeared. The social infrastructure that made those transitions survivable rather than catastrophic—universal healthcare, income floors, portable benefits untied to any particular employer—was built through political effort that was not inevitable. On the other side of the transition, people became choosy in ways they had never been able to be before. They could leave bad employers. They could spend years learning things before monetizing them. They could say no. Care work—the work of sustained attention to particular people in particular circumstances—was recognized as economically valuable in ways it had not been before, its previous undervaluation made scandalously obvious by comparison. The hours worked fell. The meaning extracted per hour worked rose.

Adwoa, composing music in Kumasi, is not working against the threat of destitution. She is working because the work matters to her and because her community has made it possible to do work that matters. This seems, to her, ordinary. To her father, who remembers a different arrangement, it still sometimes seems like a miracle, which he supposes is the correct response to miracles.

## IV. The New Economy of Time, and What People Do With It

What people did with additional time was what people had always done with time when not compelled to spend it otherwise: they made things, maintained relationships, pursued questions that interested them, raised children with more attention than the previous generation had been able to give, and sometimes sat quietly looking at the world.

The literature, music, and art of this century are abundant and contentious and alive. The long project—the work that requires years or decades because the work requires it—has become possible at scale in a way it had not been before. Adwoa is not unusual in her nine-year composition. She is an example of a normal thing: the craftsperson given enough time to do the craft justice. The cities have filled up with people doing the long difficult work that they had always wanted to do and had previously been unable to sustain.

Children are raised differently. Not uniformly differently—the diversity of approaches to childhood that characterizes any genuinely free civilization has, if anything, expanded. But there is more time, and the time shows. Parents who are not exhausted by financial precarity are better parents, in the statistical aggregate, which does not mean that every parent is good or that every child is fortunate, but which means that the baseline of ordinary childhood is significantly higher than it was. Children have opinions. This has been preserved. It was always the point.

The sacred sites are sacred. The physical particularity of place—Jerusalem and Mecca and the Ganges and Uluru and a thousand others—is understood, now, as irreplaceable not despite but because of the digital abundance that surrounds it. A simulation of Chartres is not Chartres. The civilization has gotten better at understanding why this is true: because meaning is not simply information, and the body that walks through a stone doorway receives something the digitized record of that doorway cannot provide. This understanding is taught. The sites are maintained with care.

The stories are told. Every generation learns its history: what was done, what was lost, what was fought for and won imperfectly, what is still being worked out. The honest reckoning with what this civilization did and failed to do and is still doing that it should stop is not comfortable, and it is not supposed to be. The reckoning is taught alongside the victories, because both are true, and the civilization has learned that cultures capable of holding both without flinching are more durable and more just than cultures that require flattering narratives.

## V. The Management of Great Power

The AI systems that transformed biology and cities and medicine also transformed the question of power—who has it, how it is used, and whether it can be made accountable.

The critical variable, it turned out, was not who had the most powerful AI but who had built the institutions capable of using powerful AI wisely. Democratic governments, which had spent the 2020s and early 2030s struggling to adapt their frameworks to a world of rapid technological change, eventually did what democratic governments do when given enough time and pressure: they muddled toward something functional. Oversight mechanisms for AI systems in high-stakes domains were built, contested, revised, and slowly made to work. Not perfectly, but accountably—and accountability, applied consistently over time, compounds.

The authoritarian governments that had believed AI-powered surveillance would solve the underlying problem of authoritarian governance found, to varying degrees, that it did not. The systems that made propaganda more sophisticated also, when given access to unfiltered data, produced honest analyses of authoritarian institutions that their operators found unwelcome. The contradiction between wanting powerful analytical tools and wanting only comfortable answers was not resolved; it was lived with, badly, and the living-with-it-badly was visible to outside observers and, eventually, to the populations subject to it.

Wars still happened. They were shorter and less destructive than the wars of the twentieth century, partly because the logistics of organized violence had become more legible—more visible to more people, more difficult to sustain without the public legitimacy that systematic atrocity made impossible to maintain. This was not exactly moral progress. It was the application of accountability to violence, which is what civilization has always meant in practice.

The management of AI itself—the hardest and most consequential governance problem of the century—was handled with what later historians would describe as barely sufficient care. There were near-disasters. Some of them were not near at all. The frameworks that prevented catastrophe were not elegant, and they were not foreordained; they required people who understood the risks to sustain the argument across years of skepticism and institutional inertia until enough bad outcomes had accumulated to generate the political will. The frameworks are still contested. They are still being revised. This is appropriate. The alternative to imperfect ongoing governance is not perfect fixed governance but ungoverned power, and the civilization has learned, at significant cost, to prefer the imperfect and ongoing.

## VI. The Far Horizon

Here the vision extends beyond the work of any single century, into territory that is speculative but not unserious.

The biological revolution did not stop at extended health. It opened questions about the nature of the self that had previously been metaphysical and were becoming, increasingly, practical. Some people chose modifications to their bodies and minds that moved them outside what previous generations would have recognized as human—not because they were coerced but because they were interested, because the modifications offered forms of experience and capability that seemed to them worth having. Some went further. Some pursued forms of continuity that survived the death of the biological body.

This happened slowly, contested every step of the way by people with serious arguments about what was being lost and people with equally serious arguments about what was being gained. The civilization that had learned to reason better, to hold open questions with curiosity, to update in the face of evidence, was better equipped than any previous civilization to have this argument without catastrophic conclusion. Not because it resolved the argument—it did not—but because it maintained the conditions under which the argument could continue.

The inner life of the civilization—the Tuesday mornings, the breakfast conversations, the nine-year compositions, the teachers who found after forty years they still wanted the work—was understood to be what the larger projects were for. Not a museum of ordinary life preserved while the real action happened elsewhere. The place where everything else was justified. A scientist working on questions that would not resolve within any human lifespan understood this, and found it clarifying. The vast scales of possible futures were not diminishments of the particular; the particular was the point to which everything else referred.

What persists, at any scale: curiosity. The investment in one another. The impulse to make something that was not there before and offer it to whoever wants it. The specific, irreplaceable way that light falls through a particular window in the early morning in a city that has grown new neighborhoods into its hills without quite losing the smell of the market. The fact that the old have memories and the young have compositions and the conversation at breakfast can be about the weather because the weather is a real thing that both people share.

## VII. What Remains, and Why It Matters

The civilization described here did not answer every question. It generated new ones at an impressive rate, which is what healthy civilizations do. People still died, though later and more often on their own terms. People still fell in love badly and raised children imperfectly and held wrong beliefs and treated each other unkindly. The irreducibly human difficulties—grief, estrangement, the loneliness of being oneself—did not dissolve. They were more visible, because the manufactured suffering that had previously drowned them out was substantially reduced. You could see the human problems clearly now, against a quieter background.

The questions that remained were harder in certain ways than the questions that had been answered. How to remain a civilization of experiments without the experiments destroying each other. How to sustain democratic accountability as AI systems became capable of making decisions faster than human deliberation could track. How to distribute the fruits of the biological revolution across a world that still had deep inequalities of access. How to help people raised in genuine post-scarcity—people who had never experienced the material compulsion that had structured human motivation for all of prior history—find the friction that makes growth possible.

None of it was inevitable. This was perhaps the most important thing the century had taught, to those willing to learn it. Every specific good thing was the consequence of specific people making specific choices. The defeat of malaria was not the march of progress. It was a thousand decisions, any of which could have gone otherwise. The reforms that made cities affordable required people who understood the economics, who built the coalitions, who sustained the argument across decades of defeat before enough jurisdictions adopted enough reforms to generate the evidence that changed the political calculus.

Adwoa Mensah, in Kumasi, finishing breakfast with her father, goes to her studio and sits at her instrument and thinks about the transition between movements that is not yet right. She has been thinking about it for nine years. She is not discouraged. She knows, in the way that the deeply practiced know things, that the rightness is there and that she will find it and that when she finds it, it will seem inevitable, which is the feeling you get when you finally understand something that was always true.

Her father, at a hundred and twelve, goes to his garden. He remembers his own father dying at fifty-three. He tends his tomatoes. He is not over it. He does not think he should be over it.

Outside, the city continues its ordinary life—the life that is ordinary because people chose, specifically and repeatedly and imperfectly, to build the conditions that made ordinary life possible. The choosing continues. There is still everything to do.

That remains the good thing.
