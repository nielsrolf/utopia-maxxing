# The Flourishing

## A Vision of What We Could Become

---

On the morning that Dayo first opened her eyes in the revival chamber, she could not have told you what year it was. The number she was given later—2089—felt abstract, almost theatrical, like a date from a novel she hadn't finished reading. What she could tell you was this: the room smelled faintly of cedar and warm electronics, someone had left a small bouquet of marigolds on the table beside her, and a young man with her own grandmother's eyes was sitting in a chair nearby, crying quietly, happy.

That was the first thing she learned about the world she had awakened into. People still left flowers. People still cried happy tears. Some continuities, it turned out, were worth preserving.

She had been frozen in 2031, three days before the cancer finished its work. The freezing had been, in the language of that earlier time, a long shot—expensive, controversial, mourned by her children almost exactly as a death. Fifty-eight years had passed. Two of her children were themselves grandparents now. The world had changed in ways she would spend years understanding and decades loving. But someone had left flowers, and a man with her grandmother's eyes was sitting there waiting, and so she knew, before anyone said a word, that she had arrived somewhere good.

---

This is an essay about that somewhere good. Not a blueprint—history's great disasters were all blueprints—but a portrait, sketched from the available light, of the future we are most likely to find if we are careful and lucky and, above all, serious about the question of what human flourishing actually requires.

There is a temptation, when imagining utopia, to begin with the most dramatic technologies: immortality serums, superintelligent machines, cities floating among the stars. These things exist in this future, some of them, and we will get to them. But the most important changes are subtler. They are changes in how people relate to each other, how children are raised, how grief is processed, what success means, and how a person comes to feel, on an ordinary Tuesday afternoon, that their life contains something worth protecting.

The dramatic technologies matter because they expand the space of what is possible. The subtle changes matter because they determine what, within that expanded space, we actually choose.

---

**The first and most consequential change is that extreme suffering has been largely abolished.**

This sounds like an abstraction until you sit with what it actually means. It means that no one lies in a hospital bed for months, subsumed by pain that serves no purpose. It means that the worst mental illnesses—the kinds that trap people in loops of suicidal ideation, that hollow people out over decades, that turn consciousness from a gift into a punishment—are treatable, and are treated, with the same urgency and competence that a broken leg receives today. It means that the billions of factory-farmed animals who lived brief, tormented lives, who constituted perhaps the largest reservoir of suffering in the history of life on Earth, are gone from that particular hell. The last of those operations was closed in 2051, and what replaced them involved neither suffering nor deprivation.

The abolition of pointless suffering was not achieved through some single dramatic breakthrough. It accumulated: better psychiatric medicine, then neurological interfaces that allowed genuinely precise interventions, then—most controversially at first—voluntary hedonic modification. The controversy faded as people observed the results. The people who used these tools were not zombified. They were not emptied of motivation or depth. They were people who no longer woke at three in the morning crushed by a dread they could not name, and who, freed from that weight, discovered they had more energy for everything else—for work, for love, for argument, for art.

The key insight, which took longer to arrive than it should have, was that suffering and challenge are not the same thing. Challenge is generative. Suffering is frequently just suffering. A world without the first would be unbearably flat. A world without the second is what every decent person has always hoped for.

What remains is difficulty—genuine, chosen, meaningful difficulty. Dayo's grandson Kwame spent seven years learning to compose music in the tradition of his great-grandmother's village in Ghana, and the seven years were hard, and the hardness mattered. The musicians who mentored him were demanding and sometimes discouraging. The pieces he wrote in his third year were not good, and he knew they were not good, and that knowledge cost him something real. When he finally played at the ceremony for the Accra Cultural Archive, he wept—not from relief, but from the specific satisfaction of having become adequate to a task that had once seemed impossible. That feeling was not available through any shortcut. It was earned. The world knows this, and protects the conditions under which it can be earned.

---

**The second change is in how people are raised.**

Every child in this world is wanted, or becomes wanted within the first year of life through an adoption infrastructure that is rapid, well-resourced, and treated as a serious social priority. Every child has access to multiple adults who know their name, who notice when they are absent, who can be called at odd hours with the particular panics of childhood. The nuclear family persists—many people love it, and the data suggest children generally benefit from at least one consistent primary attachment—but it is embedded in a broader network, something that resembles the extended families and village structures that characterized most of human history before industrialization dissolved them.

This was not mandated. It emerged from a convergence of incentives, norms, and urban design. Cities were rebuilt—gradually, over decades—to be walkable, to place schools and parks and community centers within the daily orbit of ordinary life, to make neighbors into something more than strangers who share a building. The loneliness epidemic of the early twenty-first century was recognized, belatedly, as the public health crisis it was, and addressed with the seriousness that epidemics demand.

Children are taught things that were conspicuously absent from earlier curricula: how to identify their own emotional states, how to have difficult conversations without either collapsing or escalating, how to disagree without contempt, how to be a good friend. These are skills. They can be taught. The evidence that teaching them produces measurable improvements in mental health and relationship quality—evidence that existed, largely ignored, for decades—finally received the attention it deserved.

The result is that most adults in this world grew up knowing what a secure attachment feels like, which means they are better at creating it in their own relationships. This compounds. A generation raised in secure attachment raises children in secure attachment. The psychological inheritance has improved.

---

**The third change is in what work means and who gets to do it.**

By 2089, most of the labor that was performed in earlier eras purely out of economic necessity—the sorting, the driving, the repetitive assembly, the data entry—is performed by machines. This transition was rocky. It caused real displacement and real pain, and the political battles around it were fierce and lasted decades. But they were navigated, imperfectly and with considerable loss, toward an outcome that most people would recognize as better than what came before: a universal material floor, high enough that no one needs to choose between food and medication, and an economy that rewards creativity, judgment, care, and connection—things that machines do not do well, and may never do well, because they require being human.

What people do with the time that automation has returned to them is varied and often surprising. Some people pursue intensive creative work. Some dedicate themselves to the dense, slow labor of maintaining relationships across generations. Some spend years learning skills that have no market value whatsoever: weaving, small-boat sailing, the reading of ancient languages. Some do what Dayo did in her first decade after revival—they wander, sampling the extraordinary proliferation of communities and subcultures and experiences that a world with abundant time and resources has produced.

What very few people do is nothing. The fear, common in earlier eras, that human beings freed from the necessity of labor would simply collapse into passive consumption—that they were, at bottom, pleasure-seeking machines who needed external compulsion to produce anything worthwhile—has turned out to be unfounded. Human beings need purpose and challenge and community. Given freedom and material security, most of them construct these things. They are, it turns out, builders by nature.

---

**The fourth change is in the relationship between individual and community.**

This is the hardest change to describe because it is the most philosophical, and because earlier utopian visions consistently got it wrong in one of two directions: either they subordinated the individual to the collective, producing conformity and stagnation, or they celebrated individual freedom so absolutely that the social fabric dissolved and people found themselves, paradoxically, lonelier and less free in the ways that matter.

The resolution is not a compromise between these extremes but a genuine synthesis: a world in which individual self-determination is protected absolutely in certain domains and embedded in genuine community in others, and in which people understand intuitively which domain they are in.

No one can be compelled to participate in any community or adopt any identity. But most people, given real options, choose to belong to several overlapping communities—geographical, professional, cultural, spiritual—each of which makes real demands and offers real rewards. These communities are not the faux-communities of earlier social media, which simulated connection while producing isolation. They are communities where people have obligations to each other, where showing up matters, where your absence is noticed and your presence is valued.

The status games that poisoned so many earlier social arrangements—the zero-sum competitions for prestige that left most participants feeling inadequate—have been substantially reformed. This happened partly through the proliferation of domains in which people can excel. In a world with genuine material abundance and unlimited cultural production, there are countless arenas of recognized achievement, and they are genuinely diverse: no single scale on which everyone is ranked, no single definition of the successful life against which all others are measured as failures. A person who is a brilliant technical innovator and a person who is a masterful gardener and a person who is, year after year, the one to whom others in their community turn in moments of grief—all of these are recognized, in their own domains, as having achieved something real.

---

**The fifth change is in how memory and time work.**

Dayo is, as of the writing of this essay, one hundred and fourteen years old in biological terms, though she spent fifty-eight of those years frozen and unconscious. She is vigorous. She is sharp. She is curious about things that would not have occurred to her to be curious about at sixty, because at sixty she had not yet accumulated the experiences that made those things interesting. She is also, in some respects, a different person than she was in 2031—changed by time and choice and the slow accumulation of insight—and she is at peace with that, because she understands that continuity of identity does not require stasis.

Longevity has been extended substantially, and this has changed the texture of human life in ways that are still being understood. Relationships deepen differently when they are measured in decades rather than years. Projects that once seemed impossibly ambitious—learning a new language in one's seventies, mastering a musical instrument at eighty, spending a decade on a single piece of research—become ordinary. The sense of urgency that once characterized the middle years of life has relaxed, replaced by something that looks, to earlier eyes, like patience, but feels, from the inside, like confidence that there is time to do things properly.

Death has not been eliminated. The biological ceiling has been pushed back—many people live to a hundred and fifty or beyond, healthy throughout—but it has not been abolished, and most thoughtful people are not sure it should be. What has been eliminated is premature death: the deaths of children, the deaths of people in the middle of projects they cared about, the deaths that arrived before any reasonable measure of completion. The deaths that remain tend to arrive when something close to completion has been reached, and are mourned with grief that is genuine but not despairing, because the grief is for a life that was full.

---

**The sixth change is in humanity's relationship with non-human minds.**

The animals that share this world are not, in the main, suffering. This took longer than it should have. The moral argument was available for centuries before it was acted upon at scale. What finally moved things was a combination of cultural shift, economic competition from cultivated proteins, and a series of landmark legal decisions that extended limited rights to the higher mammals. The change came unevenly and with enormous resistance, and the suffering that occurred during the transition was real and is not forgotten. But it is history now.

There are also, as of 2089, several artificial minds that are recognized as having morally relevant experiences—entities that can suffer and flourish in ways that matter, even if those ways are genuinely different from human suffering and flourishing. The philosophical frameworks for understanding these minds are still being developed. The legal frameworks are imperfect and contested. But the commitment to taking them seriously is genuine, and the history of moral progress provides some ground for confidence that the frameworks will improve.

---

**A note on what was not sacrificed.**

This world is not a world of enforced happiness. It is not a world in which unpleasant emotions have been engineered away, leaving people smooth and contented and shallow. The tools for emotional modification exist and are used—voluntarily, by people who have thought carefully about what they want—but the dominant cultural attitude toward difficulty, grief, anger, and loss is that these experiences, when they are genuinely engaged with rather than merely endured, make people deeper and wiser and more capable of understanding others.

There are real disagreements in this world. There are genuine political conflicts, genuine philosophical controversies, genuine artistic arguments that have not been resolved and may never be resolved. There are people who have made different choices from each other and who sometimes find those differences uncomfortable. This is not a failure of the system. It is, in a real sense, the point.

What the world has managed to do is distinguish between conflicts that are productive—that generate understanding and creativity and the testing of ideas against each other—and conflicts that are merely destructive: wars, systematic oppression, the grinding down of people who have no power by people who have too much. The first kind of conflict is protected and even celebrated. The second kind has been dramatically reduced.

---

Dayo spent her first year after revival in a daze of wonder and grief. The wonder was for everything new: the way information moved, the proliferation of cultures, the vigor and beauty of the people around her, the extraordinary expanded palette of human experience available to anyone who wanted it. The grief was for everything lost: her daughter who had not been frozen, her friends, the particular texture of the world she had known. Both the wonder and the grief were honored. No one told her to feel one more than the other.

In her second year, she began to understand that she had something the people around her lacked: she knew, from the inside, what the world before had been like. Not as history, but as memory—the specific smell of a specific room, the particular quality of an afternoon light that was now several generations gone, the way a certain kind of worry had felt, that people younger than her had read about but never experienced. This knowledge turned out to be valuable. People wanted to understand what had been changed and what the change had cost, and she was someone who could tell them.

She became, gradually and without planning it, a kind of witness. She talked to historians, to artists, to children in schools, to documentary makers. She maintained a long conversation with the world she had arrived in, about the world she had left. This is, she told anyone who would listen, the thing she had not expected: that her most useful contribution to the new world would be her memory of the old one. That the wound of discontinuity would become, over time, a strange and specific gift.

This seems right. A world that forgets where it came from loses the ability to understand what it has achieved and what it still needs to do. The goal is not to transcend the human past but to redeem it—to take everything that was good in what humans were and have always been, and to protect it, while removing the structures of cruelty and scarcity and loneliness that prevented so many people, for so long, from accessing any of it.

The flowers in the revival room were marigolds. In the tradition of Dayo's family, marigolds are for welcoming the returned dead. Someone had known this, and had prepared accordingly.

That is the small, specific, human thing at the center of all the large abstractions. Someone did the research. Someone went to the market. Someone filled a vase with water, arranged the flowers, and placed them on the table. Someone thought: *she is coming back, and she should feel, from the very first moment, that she was expected, that she was prepared for, that someone here already knew something about who she was.*

This is what the whole apparatus exists to protect: the possibility of that gesture, and the kind of people who think to make it.
