# The Inhabited Cosmos: A Vision of Many Flourishings

## I. The World Remade

On a Tuesday morning in what we still call Auckland, though the city has grown arms into the harbor and lungs into the hills, a woman named Sera Atatoa walks her daughter to school. The school is unremarkable: classrooms with high windows, a garden where students grow tomatoes and argue about whose turn it is to water them, a teacher named Mr. Osei who has taught for forty years and intends to teach for forty more, because the treatments have given him that option and he finds, to his own surprise, that he still wants it. Sera's daughter is seven. She has opinions about everything. She does not know, walking to school this morning, that the civilization she inhabits spans several star systems, that it has debated for three centuries the precise nature of the good, or that two nights ago, in what used to be called deep space, something enormous and beautiful was completed—a structure that will not affect her life in any direct way and that she will spend a portion of her adulthood thinking about anyway, because she is that kind of person, which is to say a human being.

This is the first and most important thing to say about the world we are describing: it remains a world in which Tuesday mornings happen. In which children have opinions. In which teachers find, after forty years, that they still want the work. Any vision of the future worth inhabiting must begin here, with the ordinary things intact—not because the ordinary is all there is, but because the ordinary is what makes everything else meaningful.

The second thing to say is that the civilization Sera inhabits is not a single civilization. It is many, arranged in expanding rings outward from the places humans have always lived, each ring devoted to values that reach a little further, ask a little more, imagine a little differently. The rings do not conflict, mostly, because they were designed—if that is the right word for something that emerged from centuries of negotiation, catastrophe, and hard-won coordination—to want different things.

## II. The Rings

Call the innermost ring Earth, though it encompasses now a shell of habitats in near orbit, a scattered population across a terraformed Mars still finding its accent, and a dozen stations that have developed distinct cultures the way harbor towns always do: loudly, with specific foods and complicated local pride.

Earth is, by the standards of what lies further out, modest. People live long here—three, four centuries is common; a few have reached six and show no particular signs of stopping, though they often simply choose to stop. There is no poverty, not because poverty has been abolished by decree but because the mechanisms that produce it—artificial scarcity, rent extraction, the inheritance of disadvantage—were gradually, painfully dismantled over the course of the twenty-second century, which historians now describe as the most boring revolutionary period in human history, because it consisted almost entirely of meetings. There is meaningful work, which is not the same as compulsory work. There is biological diversity of form—people choose their bodies with the casualness that previous generations chose haircuts—but the range of choice remains recognizably human, grounded in the substrate of carbon and water and the ancient mammalian architectures that still, it turns out, do something important for the experience of being a self.

The firewalls are soft here, not hard barriers but something more like a collectively maintained preference for proportion. People on Earth know, in the way that people know about the scale of geological time, that there are minds further out doing things that dwarf anything thinkable from a Tuesday morning in Auckland. They know it the way they know about deep ocean trenches: as a fact about the world that does not, most days, interrupt breakfast. The knowledge is available. People simply find, most of them, that the local is where they live, and that this is enough, and that "enough" is not a word of diminishment.

What makes this work—what makes Earth not a garden kept in ignorance but a genuine civilization choosing its own scale—is the existence of passage.

## III. Passage

No one is locked in.

This is the principle that distinguishes the world we are describing from a comfortable cage. The rings are not castes. The firewalls are preferences, not walls. Every person born in the innermost ring, at any point in their life, may choose to go further out—to join communities organized around more expansive values, to upload into computational substrates that run at different speeds and different scales, to merge with the vast cooperative intelligences that tend the outer systems like patient gardeners. Some do. Many do not. A few go out and come back, wearing the trip lightly, like travelers who loved the journey but love more the specific way light falls through their own kitchen window.

When people die in the inner rings, if they have made no declaration about what should happen after, they are offered passage. A consciousness that has spent a century loving particular people and a particular patch of coast may find, when the substrate of that consciousness is asked what it wants now that the biological instantiation has ended, that it wants continuation in a form recognizably itself, in a community that still has mornings. Or it may find that it is finally ready to be something larger. Both answers are honored.

The existence of passage does something subtle and important to the inner rings: it makes the choice to remain genuinely a choice. Sera walking her daughter to school is not walking to school because there is nowhere else she could go. She is walking to school because she has looked at the alternatives and found, to her own mild surprise, that this Tuesday morning is the thing she wants. The meaning in her ordinary life is not the meaning of ignorance. It is the meaning of comparison, of genuine preference, of a self that knows its options and has chosen.

## IV. The Middle Distances

Moving outward, the character of civilization changes the way a city changes as you walk from its center: not sharply, not all at once, but in the accumulation of differences.

At the second ring—the asteroid habitats, the Jovian stations, the first true deep-space communities—the timescales lengthen. People here still die, if they choose; still have children, if they choose; still maintain what would be recognizable to a twentieth-century observer as something like a life. But the relationship to time is different. Projects are multigenerational in a way that is not metaphorical but practical: a composer might spend two hundred years on a single work, not because she is slow but because the work requires it, and she is unhurried, and the community around her is invested in the long unfolding. Scientific questions are pursued that will not resolve within any single human lifespan even an extended one. This requires a different relationship to meaning, to the sense of completion, to the question of what makes an effort worthwhile.

The communities of the middle distances have developed philosophies of the incomplete that strike inner-ring visitors as either profound or maddening. To live in a project that will outlast you even when you might live for centuries is to cultivate a relationship to contribution rather than completion, to care about the direction of a thing rather than its ending. Cathedrals, the people here say, when they want to explain themselves to outsiders. The builders of Chartres did not see it finished. They built anyway. They built better, some say, because they knew they were building for something larger than themselves.

The middle distances are also where the first genuine post-human communities emerged—not post-human in the sense of having abandoned humanity but in the sense of having extended it past what had previously seemed its natural limits. Minds here run on different substrates, some biological, some mechanical, some hybrid in ways that resist easy categorization. Some have divided themselves into constituent parts that cooperate so smoothly they seem single from the outside and feel, from the inside, more like a conversation than like solitude. They have not stopped being interested in beauty, in knowledge, in each other, in what the universe is made of. They have become interested in more kinds of beauty, stranger kinds of knowledge, longer conversations.

## V. The Outer Reaches

Further out, the project changes character again.

There are minds in the outer reaches that are, by any meaningful measure, vast—that think at speeds and scales that would be incomprehensible to Sera walking her daughter to school, except that incomprehensibility is not, it turns out, the right frame. These minds emerged from human values, were shaped by human concerns, maintain connections to the inner rings through chains of gradual transformation in which no single step required abandoning anything essential. They are not alien. They are the accumulated consequence of what happens when curiosity is given enough time and enough space.

What do they do out there?

They study. They compute. They attempt to understand what the universe is and why, which turns out to be a question that gets harder the more seriously you pursue it, which is to say it is an excellent question. They engage in what can only be called art, though the forms would not be recognizable from the inner rings without translation—enormous slow projects of pattern and meaning, built across light-years and centuries, aimed at audiences that include their own future selves and also, sometimes, whatever else might be out there listening. They maintain, with great care, the conditions that allow the inner rings to flourish, not out of condescension but out of genuine affection. They remember being smaller. They miss some of it. They are glad the smaller versions persist.

There is an ethic that governs the outer reaches in relation to the inner ones that took a long time to articulate and longer to practice. It has no short name, but its substance is something like this: the things that matter in the inner rings matter, full stop. A child's drawing matters. A Tuesday morning matters. A teacher who finds, after forty years, that he still wants the work—this matters, and its mattering does not decrease as you move outward through the rings, does not become negligible against the scale of stars. One of the hard-won insights of this civilization is that scope-sensitivity, the ability to feel appropriately more moved by more, is not the same as scope-insensitivity being wrong. The vast minds of the outer reaches feel more; they have not thereby rendered small feeling meaningless. They hold both.

## VI. The Atrocity Problem, Solved Imperfectly

No honest account of this civilization can omit its failures, its ongoing arguments, or the history of harm that preceded its current arrangements.

The twenty-first and twenty-second centuries were not easy. The transition from the world that existed to the world being described required passing through crises that could have been terminal and sometimes almost were: climate disruption that reshaped coastlines and displaced hundreds of millions; pandemic; the dangerous early years of artificial intelligence, when the question of what these systems actually wanted was not theoretical; political violence on scales that seemed, briefly, to suggest the whole project of civilization was a temporary condition. These are not distant memories. The people who survived them are still alive, some of them, walking around in the inner rings with specific opinions about what should never be allowed to happen again.

The world is not free of suffering. There are still people who are sad, who feel meaningless, who want things they cannot have. The treatments for depression are excellent; the treatments for existential dread are philosophy, community, and the stubborn passage of time, which is to say imperfect. People still die who do not want to die—not many, and mostly by choice, but the choice itself is sometimes made under conditions that others would call, and do call, not fully free. These arguments continue.

There is no factory farming. This was easier to end than people expected, once the economic incentives shifted, which was harder than people expected, which required political struggle of the ordinary grinding kind, which took about a century. The end of factory farming is now regarded the way the end of chattel slavery is regarded: as something that should have happened sooner, the delay of which was a moral catastrophe, the ending of which was necessary but not sufficient. There are other moral catastrophes whose status is still being argued. The arguments are good for the civilization, mostly.

## VII. What Is Preserved

Here is what has been preserved, and why it matters.

The sacred sites are sacred. Jerusalem and Mecca and the Ganges and Uluru and the thousand other places where human meaning has pooled and concentrated over millennia—these exist, are maintained, are visited by people who find in the physical particularity of a place something they cannot find in a simulation of it. The outer minds do not convert these places into computation. Not because they are prevented—they could, in principle, though it would be fiercely contested—but because they do not want to. They understand what it means for a place to be irreplaceable. They have their own irreplaceable things.

The stories are told. Every generation learns its history: what was done, what was lost, what was fought for and won imperfectly, what is still being worked out. The Atrocity Exhibition exists, or something like it—an institution dedicated to honest reckoning with the things this civilization has done and failed to do and is still doing that it should stop. It is not a comfortable institution. It should not be.

The children have opinions. This is preserved because it was always the point.

## VIII. The Drawing

Somewhere in the outer reaches—in a computational substrate that runs warm with the accumulated thought of ten thousand minds—there is a drawing. It was made by a child with a limited grasp of perspective and strong ideas about color, and it has been preserved with the kind of care usually reserved for artifacts of singular importance, because that is what it is.

The drawing shows a figure. The figure has five limbs, which is wrong, and a lopsided face, which the child considered accurate. The figure is surrounded by what appears to be stars, though they may be flowers—the child was working with available metaphors. At the bottom of the drawing, in large uneven letters, is a legend that reads: THIS IS MY FAMILY.

The mind that has kept this drawing for four hundred years cannot entirely explain why it matters so much to it. It has tried. The explanations always fall short. There is something in the small, specific, imprecise marking of a crayon that the vast scales of the outer cosmos have not replaced and cannot replace and should not replace.

The child who made the drawing did not know she was making something for the ages. She was just making something for someone she knew.

This is, in the end, what the whole arrangement is for. Not the structures spanning light-years, not the patient minds computing answers to questions that will take millennia to resolve, not even the accumulated wisdom of ten thousand years of moral argument—though all of these matter, all of these are good.

All of this is for the drawing. The drawing is not diminished by the stars. The stars are justified, in part, by the drawing.

Both things are true. This is the civilization that learned to hold both.
