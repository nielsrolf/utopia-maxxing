# The World as It Could Be

There is a kind of knowing that comes only in certain moments. You are watching your child sleep, or you have just finished the last page of a novel that broke you open, or you are standing at the edge of something vast — an ocean, a mountain range, a piece of music that you cannot explain — and for a few seconds the membrane between you and the world goes thin. Everything seems lit from within. You think: *this is it. This is what it's actually like to be alive.* And then, too soon, it fades.

We have all had moments like this. That is where we should begin.

Not with a blueprint. Not with a political program or a technology roadmap. But with the honest acknowledgment that those moments are real — that they are, in some important sense, *evidence*. Evidence that the range of human experience extends much further in the good direction than most of us spend most of our time. Evidence that the distance between an ordinary Tuesday and the best day of your life is not fixed. And evidence, most importantly, that the best day of your life is probably not the ceiling of what is possible — only the highest point you personally have reached so far.

This essay is about what it might look like if we took that evidence seriously, and built a world around it.

---

## What We Are Building Toward

Let me be honest about the difficulty of this project. The future worth building is not simply our present world with the problems removed. It is something genuinely different — different enough that any detailed description risks being, as someone once put it, the equivalent of an ape imagining paradise and picturing a larger pile of bananas. Our concepts will bend and distort under the weight of what we are trying to describe.

And yet the description matters. Not because we will get it right, but because the attempt keeps us honest about the scale of what is at stake — and because, even now, there are things we know. We know that loneliness is not necessary. We know that children can be raised with safety and wonder instead of fear and deprivation. We know that whole categories of suffering — disease, hunger, war — are not inevitable features of existence but problems with solutions we are actively finding. We know that institutions can be designed to serve people rather than to grind them down. And we know, from those illuminated moments, that consciousness itself can be so much more awake, more present, more suffused with meaning than it usually is.

We are not guessing at a paradise we cannot imagine. We are extrapolating, carefully, along a path we have already begun to walk.

## The Shape of an Ordinary Day

Imagine it is an ordinary morning, several generations from now, in a city that has been designed around a single animating question: *What do people actually need to flourish?*

The city is dense and walkable, organized around neighborhoods small enough that most residents know each other by name. This was not an accident. Early in its design, planners and community members worked together using tools that allowed genuine participation — not the performance of consultation, but the real thing, with the influence to match. The result is a place that feels, improbably, both like a village and like a metropolis: you are almost always within ten minutes of a library, a workshop, a park, a café, a medical clinic, a school. The built environment says, quietly and constantly: *you belong here. This was made for you.*

The morning begins without an alarm. Work, in the old sense — the grinding obligation to sell your hours or go hungry — has largely dissolved. Automation handles most of what the 20th century called "jobs." What remains is something closer to vocation: the things people do because they cannot help doing them, because the work calls to something in them. A woman spends her mornings with the neighborhood's children, teaching them mathematics through games she has spent years designing. A man spends his afternoons engineering the microbiome of the local watershed. Neither of them experiences what they do as labor in the old sense. Both of them experience it as the thing that makes a day worth having.

This is not because drudgery has been legislated away, but because sufficient material security was established — through mechanisms hammered out over decades of democratic argument, through the gradual extension of automation's benefits as a shared inheritance rather than a private windfall — to give people genuine choice about how to spend their hours. The resulting culture still values effort, difficulty, growth. But it is effort chosen, difficulty embraced, growth that is personal rather than compelled.

## The Architecture of Belonging

The loneliness epidemic of the early 21st century — and it was an epidemic, with mortality statistics to match — ended not through any single intervention but through a slow accumulation of changes in how communities were organized, how children were raised, and how the adults of a society were taught to think about relationships.

What emerged, gradually, was something like a rediscovery of the fact that human beings are not naturally individualistic. The hyper-individualism of the 20th and early 21st centuries was not an expression of human nature but a particular cultural configuration, shaped by specific economic pressures and historical accidents, and it made most people miserable in ways they often could not articulate. The antidote was not a return to some imagined premodern community — which was often suffocating, exclusionary, and unkind to those who did not fit — but the construction of something new: communities that were small enough for genuine knowing and large enough for genuine diversity.

In practice, this looks like a world where most people have several overlapping circles of deep connection. There is the immediate neighborhood. There are the people who share your vocation or your passionate interest. There are the people who have known you long enough to remember who you used to be and have watched you become who you are now. There are chosen families — not replacing biological ones, but supplementing them, adding to the web.

Children, in this world, are not raised in isolation. Every child is surrounded not just by parents but by a wider community of adults who know them by name, who are genuinely interested in who they are becoming, who will be there on a Tuesday afternoon when a parent is overwhelmed and a child needs someone to talk to. This is not communal in the sense of abolishing family bonds — those bonds remain, and are valued — but in the sense of insisting that no parent should raise a child alone, and no child should grow up without knowing that the world, at some basic level, is on their side.

The result is adults who are better at love. Not because they are neurologically different — though some voluntary cognitive tools help — but because they were raised in it, taught it explicitly as a skill worth developing, and have spent their lives in communities that model and reward it. The anxious avoidance that characterized so much 21st-century intimacy has not disappeared entirely, but it is understood as a wound to be healed rather than a personality trait to be accommodated.

## On Suffering and Its Limits

This world is not without pain. That is important to say clearly, because a utopia without honest accounting of difficulty is not a utopia but a fantasy — and fantasies do not sustain effort.

People still grieve. They grieve because they love, and love entails vulnerability, and vulnerability entails loss. This has not been eliminated and should not be. Grief is not pathology; it is the shadow cast by everything that matters.

People still struggle. Some of this is the productive struggle of difficulty chosen and embraced — the long project, the hard conversation, the thing you are trying to build that refuses to be built easily. Some of it is the friction of living with other people, who have their own needs and blind spots and histories.

But a very large category of suffering — the suffering that was never chosen, never productive, never anything other than a grinding diminishment of human possibility — has been dramatically reduced. The diseases that shortened and blighted lives: the treatments came, slowly and then faster, through sustained investment and international cooperation that finally understood pandemic preparedness and research funding not as costs but as the most basic infrastructure of human welfare. The mental anguish that was mostly invisible for so long: taken seriously at last, with the same resources and urgency that were once reserved for physical illness. The violence — intimate, political, structural — that was woven through the old world: not eliminated, but progressively curtailed by the same mechanisms that have curtailed it throughout history, which is to say, by the slow, contested, frequently reversed but overall inexorable expansion of the moral circle and the rule of law.

The suffering that remains is not dismissed as trivial. Rather, there is a shared, serious project of reducing it further — of asking, always, whether what appears to be an inevitable feature of existence might actually be a solvable problem in disguise.

## The Life of the Mind

It became clear, gradually, that intelligence is not a fixed quantity and that the quality of our thinking depends enormously on our tools, our institutions, and our education.

In this world, education has been reimagined from the ground up around the question of what it actually means to understand something. Children learn to think — to reason carefully, to hold uncertainty without anxiety, to find their way through a disagreement toward what is actually true — not as an abstract skill but as a practice embedded in everything they study. They learn mathematics by discovering it, history by interrogating it, science by doing it. They learn to disagree with each other, and with their teachers, and to find that process generative rather than threatening. They leave school not with a collection of facts but with something harder to acquire and more valuable: genuine intellectual agency.

The resulting culture is one in which ideas are taken seriously. People argue about things — about ethics, about aesthetics, about how communities should be organized — with a rigor and a good faith that was once rare. The conversation is louder and stranger and more generative than anything we currently have. And it is continuous: a species-wide project of trying to understand more, get it less wrong, and act better on what we learn.

Alongside this, technologies that amplify cognition — not replacing human thought but extending it, as writing extended it, and as the internet is still awkwardly, incompletely extending it — have been developed with enough caution and enough democratic input to make them genuinely beneficial rather than merely powerful. People can think in ways and at scales that were unavailable before. This does not make them post-human; they are still human, still shaped by everything that makes us human. But they are, in some important sense, more fully themselves — the person they were always trying to become, capable of the understanding they were always reaching for.

## Governance and the Problem of Power

The ugliest truth about the old world was not its poverty or its disease, bad as those were, but the way power concentrated and corrupted. The mechanisms that were supposed to prevent this — elections, constitutions, checks and balances — were necessary but not sufficient. They were designed for a world that no longer existed and were endlessly gamed by those with the resources to game them.

What replaced them was not a single system but a layered set of innovations: decision-making tools that enabled genuine participation at scale without degenerating into mob rule; transparency technologies that made concentrated power harder to hide; civic institutions redesigned to give ordinary people meaningful influence over the decisions that shaped their lives; and, crucially, a culture that understood itself to be in an ongoing, unfinished experiment with governance rather than in possession of a final answer.

The world government — such as it is — is less a government than a coordination mechanism: a set of shared agreements about certain baseline rights and certain collective problems that require collective action. Climate stabilization. Pandemic preparedness. The governance of technologies powerful enough to affect everyone. These are handled with something approaching adequacy, which is more than could be said before. Everything else is handled locally, by communities that know their own needs.

This is not a world without conflict. It is a world with better tools for resolving it — tools that are slower, messier, and more demanding than simple coercion, but that produce agreements people can actually live inside.

## What We Cannot Yet Imagine

Here is where the honest account must acknowledge its own limits.

There are dimensions along which human life might improve that we cannot currently describe, because the improvement would require concepts we do not yet have. Consider: a medieval peasant asked to imagine the goods of modern life could conceive of having more food, more warmth, more safety. They could not conceive of recorded music, or of the particular kind of connection that comes from reading a novel written three hundred years ago by someone who understood exactly what you are going through right now, or of the specific joy of a video call with someone you love who is on the other side of the planet.

The goods available to our descendants will include things like that — not mere increments of what we have, but new categories, made possible by new capacities. New ways of knowing each other. New forms of beauty that require new forms of perception to appreciate. New modes of consciousness that are not alien to what we are, but deeper expressions of it — the same fire, enormously larger.

We cannot describe these goods. But we know, from the evidence of those moments when the membrane goes thin and everything is lit from within, that they are in the same direction. And that direction is the one we are, imperfectly, already moving in.

## The Only Real Requirement

None of this requires a master plan. It does not require a vanguard, or a revolution, or the overthrow of everything that currently exists. It requires something harder and more ordinary: the sustained, collective decision to take human flourishing seriously as a goal — and to build, institution by institution, neighborhood by neighborhood, relationship by relationship, a world that acts like it means it.

The work is already underway. It is happening in the laboratory where someone is trying to cure a disease that kills children. It is happening in the classroom where a teacher has decided that every student deserves to feel genuinely capable. It is happening in the conversation between two people who have decided to tell each other the truth. It is happening in every act of governance that chooses accountability over expedience, and in every community that makes space for the person who does not quite fit.

This is not naivety. It is the reading of the evidence. The evidence of history, which shows a long and uneven but real movement toward less suffering and more freedom. The evidence of science, which shows that the causes of human misery are mostly tractable. And the evidence of those illuminated moments, which show that whatever we are capable of feeling and knowing and being — we have barely started.

The world described here is not guaranteed. It is not inevitable. It is, in the language that matters most, *possible* — physically possible, morally possible, humanly possible. It is a real place that does not yet exist, the way the ocean was a real place before the first person who had never seen water was told that it existed: unimaginable in detail, but reachable, and worth reaching.

That is enough to begin.
