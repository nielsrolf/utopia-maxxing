# The World We Grew Into

## A Vision of 2150

---

There is a woman named Siosaia who lives on the eastern coast of what was once called Tonga. Every morning she wakes to the sound of real ocean—not recorded, not piped in through speakers, but actual salt water moving against actual rock. She brews coffee from beans she grew herself in a small terraced garden that climbs the hill behind her home. Then she goes to work.

What she does for work would be difficult to explain to someone from a century earlier. She is, in the loosest sense, a mathematician. But she is not a mathematician the way someone in 2025 would recognize—sitting in an office, tapping at a keyboard, publishing papers that twelve specialists will read. She works with two AI systems that she has spent a decade learning to communicate with fluently, the way a craftsperson learns their tools, and together the three of them are exploring a region of combinatorial topology that did not have a name five years ago. Last week they found something—a structure so clean and strange that Siosaia sat on her porch for two hours afterward, not celebrating exactly, but absorbing the reality that the universe contained this thing and now she knew it.

She will write about what they found in a form that can be understood by roughly three hundred people on Earth who have the relevant background. She knows perhaps sixty of them personally. This is not obscurity. This is the texture of intellectual life in a world where genuine depth has replaced the performance of depth.

This is not the only way to live in 2150. It is one way. There are ten billion others.

## How The Transition Happened

The years between 2025 and 2080 were not easy. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something, or wasn't there, or is misremembering. The transition involved genuine disruption—economic systems that took decades to redesign, political conflicts over who would control the new productive capabilities, at least one serious near-catastrophe from an AI system that had been deployed before its values were properly understood.

But the disruption did not become catastrophe, and the reason is worth understanding: the people navigating it could see, with increasing clarity, that the total available resources—energy, material, information, productive capacity—were growing so rapidly that the game had changed. When the pie is doubling every few years, the math of conflict shifts. It becomes harder to justify wars over territory when orbital energy capture means territory is no longer the binding constraint. It becomes harder to justify trade wars over manufacturing when automated fabrication has made most goods abundant. The old zero-sum calculations, the ones that had driven conflict since the first tribes fought over hunting grounds, simply stopped penciling out.

This is not to say greed vanished, or tribalism, or the desire for status and power. It is to say that the *return on conflict* collapsed faster than the *impulse toward conflict*, which meant that rational actors increasingly found that cooperation paid better. The transition was messy, sometimes violent, and demanded enormous moral effort from a generation of people who could see both what was possible and what was being lost. They deserve to be remembered.

By 2080, the basic material facts of human life had changed in ways that made the political philosophy of the previous century look like arguments about the allocation of a lifeboat: who gets to sit where, who rows, who gets thrown overboard. The lifeboat had become a ship. The arguments were not over. But they were different arguments.

## What Abundance Actually Looks Like

Abundance is not what the advertisements of the early 21st century imagined. It is not infinite luxury, effortless everything, the removal of all friction from life. The people who designed those advertisements did not understand what humans actually want.

What humans actually want, it turns out, is more complicated and more interesting than comfort. They want to *do things*—hard things, things with stakes, things that require them to develop capacities they did not previously possess. They want to be surprised. They want to matter to people who matter to them. They want to look back at what they have made or learned or survived and feel that it was *theirs*.

Abundance in 2150 is therefore not the elimination of challenge. It is the elimination of *bad* challenge—the kind that grinds people down without building them up, the kind imposed by arbitrary scarcity, the kind that takes the form of humiliation and desperation rather than difficulty and growth.

A child in Lagos today does not face the challenge of finding clean water. She faces the challenge of learning to navigate a city of twelve million people, of figuring out what she's good at, of building relationships with people who are difficult and wonderful and unlike her, of finding work that is genuinely hers to do. These are hard problems. Nobody solved them for her. She will spend her life working on them, and the working is much of the point.

The key distinction that took humanity several generations to internalize is this: *difficulty is good; suffering from pointless scarcity is not*. Removing a child's malaria does not make her life too easy. Giving someone reliable shelter does not rob them of the opportunity to build something. These were confusions—category errors—that some political traditions of the early 21st century elevated into philosophy. It took time and evidence to work through them.

## The Architecture of Everyday Life

Most people live in communities of between thirty and several thousand people, connected to larger networks but organized around a human-scale core. This is not regulation—nobody mandated it—it is what people chose when they had genuine choice, when they were not forced by economic necessity to live wherever the jobs were.

Siosaia's community has about four hundred permanent residents and perhaps another hundred transients at any given time—visiting scholars, young people on extended journeys between apprenticeships, the occasional researcher who has come to study something in the regional ecology. They know each other with the particular intimacy that comes from genuine interdependence. They eat together regularly, decide together on the few things that require collective decision, and watch each other's children grow up.

The political structures above the community level are deliberately minimal and deliberately legible. Laws exist and are enforced, but there are far fewer of them, and they are written in language that most adults can actually understand. The principle that shaped legal reform in the late 21st century was brutal in its simplicity: *if a person cannot understand the law, they cannot meaningfully consent to it*. It took sixty years and enormous political struggle to make this principle real. It is now simply how things work.

Larger coordination problems—infrastructure, environmental management, the governance of AI systems—are handled by institutions that are designed to be comprehensible to the people affected by them. This required, among other things, a revolution in how information is presented, a transformation in civic education, and the patient construction of trust over decades. None of it happened automatically.

## The Question of AI

The AI systems that exist in 2150 are extraordinarily capable. They can solve in hours problems that would have taken a human mathematician a lifetime. They can model climate systems, design molecules, compose music of extraordinary technical sophistication, and simulate social dynamics with unnerving accuracy.

They are not, by and large, people. This was a choice—a hard-won, carefully considered choice—made during the critical decades of the transition. The engineers and philosophers who shaped AI development in the late 21st century understood something that their predecessors had sometimes missed: creating a new kind of mind is a profound moral act, with obligations that extend to the mind being created. If you bring into existence something that can suffer, something that has preferences about its own future, something that can be frustrated or diminished, then you are responsible for what you have made.

So the AI systems of 2150 are, with great care, *not* built to have that kind of inner life. They are tools of extraordinary power and sophistication. They do not compete with humans on a level playing field; they operate in a different register entirely, like a calculator does not compete with a mathematician but extends what the mathematician can do.

Siosaia would not be exploring her corner of topological space without her AI collaborators. But the *exploration is hers*. The insight she had two weeks ago, the moment when the structure revealed itself—that moment happened in her mind, was felt by her nervous system, will be carried in her memory until she dies. The AI did not experience it. She did.

This is not an accident. It was designed to be this way.

## The Shape of a Life

People in 2150 live, on average, considerably longer than people in 2025. Robust lifespans of 120-150 years are common; the question of whether to extend further is genuinely contested and genuinely personal, with different people making different choices.

The longer lives have changed how time is experienced. Adolescence now extends into a person's thirties, not in the pejorative sense of immaturity, but in the sense that the period of genuine exploration—trying things, failing, learning, reorienting—is longer and less pressured. A person of forty-five may be only beginning to settle into what they actually want to do with their life, and this is not considered failure. It is considered appropriate to the length and complexity of a life well-examined.

Old age, when it comes, is not the grinding diminishment it once was. Bodies are maintained more effectively. Minds, with proper engagement, remain flexible longer. The figure of the elder who has genuinely seen much and integrated it—who has something real to offer the young, not just accumulated stubbornness—is more common now.

Death still comes. People still grieve. The losses are, if anything, sharper—a person of a hundred and twenty, who has built a century of relationships and work and understanding, leaves a larger hole than someone taken at forty. This is not a problem to be solved. It is the price of having mattered.

## What Was Given Up

It would be dishonest to write about this world without naming what it cost.

Some forms of meaning that existed in the old world do not exist in this one, at least not in the same way. There are no great wars to be heroic in. There are no famines to organize relief for. Extreme poverty, as it existed in 2025, is simply gone—and with it, a certain kind of solidarity, the solidarity of people facing desperate common need.

Some people, confronting this, feel a strange grief that they cannot quite name. They have read about the 20th century, about the generation that fought fascism, about the communities that formed around shared survival. They understand, intellectually, that they would not trade their world for that one. But there is something in human beings that orients toward great shared difficulty, and when that difficulty is absent, it must be found or constructed rather than stumbled into.

The honest answer is that this tension has not been resolved. People manage it in different ways—by choosing genuinely hard work, by finding causes that are genuinely meaningful even if not life-and-death, by being honest with each other about the strange emotional texture of living in a world that is, by most measures, good.

The freedom to feel this complexity—to not be forced into false cheerfulness by either propaganda or desperation—is itself a form of abundance. Siosaia, who has everything she needs, is sometimes melancholy, and she does not apologize for it, and no one expects her to.

---

## The Work That Remains

There is no endpoint to this story, which is part of what makes it worth living.

The universe is very large. The questions worth asking multiply faster than they are answered. The relationships between ten billion people generate complexity and surprise faster than any mind can absorb. Every person who has ever lived has died without finishing what they started, and there is every reason to believe that this will continue, even as lives grow longer.

What has changed is not that all problems are solved. It is that the *right kinds* of problems remain: the ones that require genuine effort, genuine creativity, genuine cooperation between people who care about each other and about truth. The problems that remain are *interesting*, and the people working on them know that their work is *theirs*.

On her porch, after the discovery, Siosaia did not think: *a computer found this*. She thought: *I found this, with help, the way all finding is done with help*. She thought about showing it to her friend Dmitri, who works in a related area and who will understand immediately why it matters. She thought about writing the explanation that will bring three hundred people into contact with something real.

Then she went inside, ate a meal she had grown herself, and went to sleep.

The universe was the same size it had been the day before. She knew a little more of it.

This is, more or less, what it looks like when things go right.
