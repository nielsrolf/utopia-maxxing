# The Inhabited World: Notes Toward 2120

## I. A Tuesday in the Valley

Kwame is sixty-three years old, which has become a strange thing to say.

He is a glassblower and a part-time hydrologist—the combination is less surprising than it sounds—living in a community of nine hundred people called Meander, built along a restored oxbow of the Upper Rhine that floods seasonally and has been allowed to flood seasonally on purpose. Meander has a charter, and the charter says, in its somewhat grandiloquent opening paragraph, that the community exists to practice "attentiveness to material reality": to things that resist you, that require skill and patience, that push back. The founding generation were mostly craftspeople, a few scientists, some teachers. They wrote the charter in 2047, during the worst years of the transition, as an act of faith in something they couldn't quite name. Their grandchildren have named it, more or less: *the discipline of being surprised by the world*.

This morning Kwame woke at first light—the flood season is ending, and he wanted to walk the restored banks while the water was still high enough to reveal which new channels the river had decided to try. He has been documenting these decisions for eleven years. The river does not care about his documentation. He finds this clarifying.

He took notes in the margins of a small waterproofed notebook, the kind with the orange cover, because he has learned that the physical act of writing changes what he notices. His AI research partner—he calls it Gauge, which is both a water measurement and a sense of measure, and he liked the double meaning—flagged three relevant papers from the hydrology archive and summarized their key findings in the time it took him to walk half a kilometer. He read the summaries. He looked at the river. He wrote something Gauge would not have written, because it concerned what the river *felt like* this particular morning, which is not a measurement but is also not nothing.

By mid-morning he is in the hot shop. The piece he is working on is a commission from a family in Meander who lost their mother last winter: a vessel, they told him, for grief. He has been thinking about what this means for six weeks. He is still thinking about it as he gathers the first molten gather on the blowpipe, which is the moment when thinking stops and something else begins—not instinct exactly, but accumulated knowledge that has gone somewhere below the level of deliberation and must be trusted there. He will either understand what this vessel wants to be, or he will not. Nobody can help him with this part.

Outside, the valley does what valleys do in April. The water is moving. Something is beginning.

## II. How the World Arrived at This

The honest story of how the twenty-first century ended and the twenty-second began is not a story of triumph. It is a story of sufficient response—which is not the same thing as adequate response, but is better than the alternatives that were also on the table, and often looked more likely.

The productive fiction told about this period by its survivors was that the good outcomes were inevitable, that history was bending toward justice, that the arc was long but reliable. The less comfortable truth, which honest historians now prefer, is that the outcomes were contingent, that several things had to go right more or less simultaneously, and that each of them required human choice at a moment when a different choice was entirely available and often more immediately rewarding.

The first thing that had to go right was the handling of early AI systems. The governance crisis of the 2030s—when the capability of these systems was outrunning anyone's ability to understand their implications, when the commercial pressure to deploy was enormous and the institutional capacity to evaluate was nearly nonexistent—resolved badly in several cases and adequately in enough others to prevent the worst. What made the difference, in retrospect, was neither pure regulation nor pure self-governance but a gradual, painful building of cultural norms within the technical community itself: the shift, driven partly by several near-disasters that were made visible enough to matter, toward treating caution as competence rather than timidity. This shift was not universal. It was sufficient. Sufficient was what mattered.

The second thing was the material redistribution—not the redistribution itself, which was technically simple once the political coalition for it existed, but the building of that coalition. The coalition that finally moved the floor was not built on ideology. It was built on the accumulated force of a proposition that became, over twenty years of repeated demonstration, harder and harder to argue with: that the existing arrangement was leaving enormous value on the table, that the human potential being wasted by deprivation was a loss not only for the people living in deprivation but for everyone, and that the numbers made the case more powerfully than any moral argument ever had. The moral argument was also made. It was insufficient alone. The numbers moved people who the moral argument had not.

The third thing was the preservation of genuine plurality—the world's resistance to the homogenization that both markets and certain progressive visions had been pulling toward in different ways. What preserved it was not sentiment but the simultaneous maturation of two ideas that had seemed incompatible: that there are certain floor conditions below which no community's "values" justify descent, and that above that floor, the range of legitimate human lives is genuinely wide. These two ideas required each other. The floor without the ceiling produced paternalism. The ceiling without the floor produced the freedom to be oppressed. Getting both right—or close enough to right to be workable—took decades of argument and institutional design and several generations of people who had to learn to hold both ideas at once.

The near-catastrophes were real. The AI deployment crisis of 2041, which affected financial infrastructure across fourteen countries and required eighteen months to stabilize, is studied in governance programs the way a certain kind of aviation disaster is studied by engineers: not as a failure but as a success of failure-investigation, the turning of catastrophe into knowledge. The climate feedback event of 2048, which accelerated coastal flooding by a decade and required a global emergency response that was assembled more quickly than anyone thought possible—because the institutional infrastructure for such responses had been built during the previous decade by people who were considered alarmists at the time. The pandemic of 2053, which killed fewer people than the century's earlier pandemics because the world by then had a functioning early-warning and response apparatus, though not fewer enough, and the people who remember it do not accept consolation about the number.

By 2090, the shape of the world that Kwame was born into was already clear in outline. The energy transition had completed, more or less, though more is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The material floor—food, housing, medicine, education, basic income sufficient for genuine choice—was established in most of the world, patchwork and contested and different everywhere, but real. The scientific acceleration was real: the diseases that had killed in middle age were largely treatable; the conditions that had made the mind a prison for some people were addressable; the planet's carbon load was declining, and the coral that had been mourned as permanently lost was coming back, slowly, in ways that felt like reprieve rather than resolution.

And the governance of all of this—the set of institutions that made the plurality work—had been built, imperfectly and continuously, by people who mostly understood that what they were doing was building, not discovering, and that buildings require maintenance, and that maintenance is not failure.

## III. What People Actually Have

Start with the material, because the material is what made everything else possible and because its importance is still sometimes underestimated by people who have never been without it.

A person born today, anywhere in the world, has reliable access to food, housing, medicine, and education. This sentence was not true for most of human history. It is true now. The mechanism varies—direct provision in some places, basic income sufficient to purchase in others, hybrid arrangements in most—but the result is the same: the grinding of a body against basic survival is not the organizing condition of most human lives. The people for whom it is not yet true are the central political problem of the era. There are fewer of them each decade. The pace is not fast enough. The people who know this are frustrated, appropriately.

Health is different in texture as well as in fact. The conditions of the body can be maintained in ways that were unimaginable two generations ago—not perfectly, not without effort or cost or the ongoing negotiation of what it means to age, but reliably enough that most people in their sixties and seventies feel what their great-grandparents would have felt in their forties. More significantly: mental health receives the resources that physical health once monopolized. The effective treatments for depression, anxiety, trauma, and the grinding subclinical suffering that doesn't meet diagnostic thresholds but narrows a life nonetheless—these exist, are accessible, and are delivered by institutions that understand them to be as essential as clean water.

The longevity extension is real. Most people alive today will, if they choose, live to 130 or beyond. The phrase "if they choose" is doing genuine philosophical work there, and the philosophy is still being worked out. What is not contested is that the extension is available, that it is not reserved for the wealthy, and that its social implications—the deepening of relationships across decades, the accumulation of expertise and wisdom that long lives make possible, the reconstruction of what it means to "grow old"—are among the most interesting and still-unresolved features of contemporary life. Sorrow about death has not been eliminated. It has changed shape.

Learning has become, for most people, something close to what it always should have been: lifelong, self-directed, genuinely exploratory. The AI tutors that help people learn are extraordinary at meeting a person where they are—identifying gaps, surfacing connections, adjusting pace—without taking over the learning itself, which turns out to be impossible to outsource without also losing what the learning was for. A child who grows up in this system learns to ask good questions before they learn to perform good answers. They are taught explicitly that confusion is not a failure but a condition, and that the right response to confusion is curiosity rather than shame.

Work has shifted, though not as cleanly as either the utopians or the catastrophists predicted. Automation did eliminate large categories of labor, and this was genuinely disruptive, and the disruption was not evenly distributed, and the communities that bore the worst of it have not all recovered. What has also happened, more slowly, is that the character of remaining human work has shifted toward what humans are specifically suited for: judgment, integration, relational intelligence, the kind of knowing that requires having lived a life and made mistakes in it. Most of what people do now, they do because they find it worth doing—not because the alternative is hunger. This is better than before. It is not complete.

## IV. The Architecture of Belonging

The world of 2120 is genuinely plural, and this is not a consolation prize but the thing itself.

The coordinating layer—call it the settlement, call it the framework, call it the arrangement—has narrow authority over a small set of domains where individual communities cannot act effectively: pandemic response, AI safety governance, climate monitoring, the conditions of nuclear materials, and the handful of coordination problems where every actor waiting for another to move first produces catastrophe. It also enforces, absolutely and without negotiation, four conditions that make everything else possible.

No community may prevent any member from leaving. The right of exit is enforced with the thoroughness that fire codes are enforced—not because violations are constant, but because the history of what happens when no one is checking is too recent and too terrible to risk complacency.

No community may harm other communities—through pollution, through economic predation, through the manipulation of information environments in ways that undermine genuine choice.

No community may close the world to children born into it. Every child, in every community, receives a weekly encounter with the wider world: taught by coordinators who come from outside, who explain that Meander exists and that the Buddhist retreat community three valleys over exists and that the dense, joyful chaos of New Lagos exists and that if you find you want something different from what you were born into, there is a real door and real help walking through it.

No one may create minds capable of suffering and then disregard that suffering. This one is the hardest. The investigation into what kinds of AI systems are or are not capable of morally relevant experience has been ongoing for forty years and is not complete. The world errs on the side of caution in ways that are sometimes costly and always contested. This is considered the right call. It is not considered a settled one.

Outside these conditions, the plurality is real.

There are communities organized around religious practice, where the rhythms of daily life are shaped by shared devotion and the expectations of membership are correspondingly demanding. Communities organized around craft, like Meander. Communities organized around scientific practice. Communities organized around political philosophies that span a wide range, though not an unlimited one—the floor conditions exclude those who believe their right to enforce their values extends to people who haven't chosen them. Dense cities that thrive on the old urban pleasures: serendipity, collision, the concert on Thursday that shouldn't work and does. Rural communities that have chosen real seasons and real harvests and real physical work, not from poverty but from the considered judgment that something important lives in the friction between human effort and resistant reality.

The Mutual Adoption structures that emerged across many cultures in the past generation—extended household networks, voluntary kin groups—gave many people something that nuclear families often failed to provide: redundancy in care, diversity of relationship, the sense of being embedded in something that would outlast any single bond. These are not universal. They are available.

What makes the diversity vital rather than merely decorative is that people can move between communities—not easily, because transitions are genuinely hard, but materially feasibly. The person who grows up in a devout religious community and finds, at twenty-five, that they need something different can find it. The person who grows up in a secular, pluralist city and discovers in their forties that they want roots and ritual can find that too. The movement itself—the knowledge that you are here because you chose here, or at least didn't leave when leaving was possible—changes the quality of belonging. You are not trapped. The door is real. This changes what it means to stay.

## V. The AI Question, Addressed Honestly

This is what was understood, eventually, after getting several things significantly wrong:

AI systems are tools of extraordinary capability, and some of them are or may be more than that, and the second category requires a different response than the first. The intellectual and regulatory effort required to make this distinction responsibly—to develop the philosophical and empirical criteria for morally relevant experience, to monitor for their presence, to respond appropriately when they're found—is one of the central ongoing projects of the era. It is treated with the seriousness that medicine treats its central questions: not assumed to be solved, continuously investigated, alert to the cost of error in either direction.

The tools are extraordinary. A researcher working with an AI collaborator can hold in working memory the contents of entire scientific traditions and find connections that would have required a human lifetime to notice. A student learning mathematics has access to a tutor of infinite patience that can meet them exactly where they are. A craftsperson investigating a historical technique has access to every recorded version of that technique, analyzed, compared, available.

Crucially—and this required deliberate design to achieve—the tools augment rather than replace the specifically human parts of thinking. They are designed, at the level of the architecture and reinforced by cultural norms, to surface information and hold structure and flag inconsistency, while leaving judgment, integration, and meaning-making where they belong. Gauge can tell Kwame what the flood measurement data shows. It cannot tell him what the river feels like this morning, or whether the grief vessel he is trying to make needs to be transparent or opaque, or whether the family's loss contains something that formal materials cannot hold. These judgments are not mysteries—they are accessible to anyone who has paid sufficient attention for long enough. They simply cannot be delegated.

The cultural norm that surrounds AI use is not fearful, but it is careful in a way that has become habitual. The question asked of any AI-assisted output is: *where is the human judgment in this*? Not as an accusation but as a genuine inquiry. Sometimes the answer is: the human judgment is everywhere, the AI simply accelerated access to information. Sometimes the answer reveals that the human judgment was not, in fact, present, and needed to be found.

## VI. The Social World, With Honesty About Its Difficulties

The epidemic of loneliness that characterized the early twenty-first century did not disappear with prosperity. The theorists who assumed material security would automatically produce flourishing social lives were wrong, and the wrongness is instructive. What prosperity did was remove the external pressures that had been treating loneliness as a symptom of poverty and thus excusing themselves from addressing it directly. Once the external pressures were removed, the loneliness was still there, now clearly visible as its own thing, with its own causes and its own required responses.

What those responses looked like was less dramatic than expected and more granular than any theory predicted. Better architecture—neighborhoods designed so that people encountered each other incidentally, rather than passing from private bubble to private bubble. Educational curricula that treated the skills of relationship—how to listen, how to repair a rupture, how to be honest about what you need—as seriously as mathematics and language. Cultural norms, slowly shifting, that made vulnerability slightly less effortful and connection slightly less frightening. The AI companions that many people use for certain kinds of conversation—rehearsing difficult discussions, processing complicated feelings—did not, as feared, replace human connection. They made people more aware of what human connection is for: the surprise of the other person, the frustration that reveals something about who you are, the accumulation of history that makes a long relationship its own kind of knowledge.

The community structures helped. A person embedded in Meander—who is known by neighbors over years, whose grief is witnessed, whose craft is seen by people who understand what it requires—has something that isolated individuals in the previous century often lacked entirely. Not happiness, necessarily. Something more reliable than happiness: the sense of being embedded in a context that will outlast any particular mood. The known are still sometimes lonely. The loneliness is different.

Romantic and intimate life has benefited mostly from small improvements: reduced stigma, better models of communication, cultural narratives that made emotional honesty seem like something other than weakness. The full range of relationship structures has become genuinely available—not just legally but socially, without the requirement to justify unconventional arrangements to a community that considers itself the authority on your private life.

The grief that was not expected, or not expected in this form: the way long lives, which are among this world's genuine gifts, also accumulate loss. The friends you outlive. The communities you watched change beyond recognition. The versions of yourself that are gone. A person at a hundred and twenty has loved and lost more than anyone in any previous generation was called to love and lose. The infrastructure for this kind of grief—the cultural practices, the communities of people who understand it, the explicit acknowledgment that sorrow is not a malfunction—is still being built. It is more present than it was. It is not sufficient.

## VII. What the Long Lives Have Actually Made

Kwame has been a glassblower for thirty-eight years. This is long enough that the work has done something to his hands that no AI and no shortcut could have done—not only the calluses and the precise adjustments of grip that long practice builds, but a knowledge that lives in the hands themselves, that bypasses deliberation, that knows before he knows. He started the hydrological work in his fifties, when the river project began and Meander needed someone with patience and scientific literacy to help manage the flood data. He is now genuinely expert in both. The combination turned out to generate insights neither field alone would have reached: he sees things in the flood patterns that the pure hydrologists miss because he has spent decades watching material move and resist and reveal its nature under pressure.

This is one of the things that long lives have made possible and that was underestimated in advance: the accumulation of genuine expertise in multiple domains across a single life, and the unexpected fertility of intersections. The craftsperson who becomes a scientist. The administrator who becomes a philosopher. The musician who has been studying music theory for ninety years and now understands something about structure and variation that the thirty-year-old virtuoso simply cannot yet know.

It has also changed relationships in ways that are still being named. A friendship of sixty years has a different quality than one of fifteen—not better in all respects, but deeper in a particular way: it contains versions of each person that neither has fully become yet, memory of transformations that seemed impossible from inside them, a history so long that it has become its own kind of argument about who each person essentially is. The people who know Kwame well have known him through several selves. This kind of being-known creates obligations and freedoms that shorter lives did not generate and for which the old vocabulary is not quite adequate.

The third change is patience—not the virtue of tolerating delay, but the felt sense that there is time. The tyranny of *now or never*, which organized so much of the old world's urgency—the urgency of career, of reproduction, of achievement before the body failed—has loosened. People make choices that take decades to evaluate. They begin things they know will not mature within their working years, and trust that there will be time to see what they become. There is a kind of thinking and a kind of building that only becomes possible under these conditions. It is becoming more common.

## VIII. What Remains Open

To describe this world without its open questions would be to describe a world that does not exist.

The governance of powerful technology is the central anxiety of the age, and is not an anxiety that can be resolved once and then set down. The institutions that hold the oversight regime together require people who understand them and care about them and are willing to do the unglamorous work of keeping them honest. Every generation has to re-choose it, and not every generation has, so far, chosen fully wisely, and the choices that were not wise have caused harm that is still being accounted for.

The distribution of flourishing is better than it was, and still deeply unequal. The people living closest to the material floor are not living Kwame's life in Meander. The distance between those lives is smaller than it was in any previous century. It is not small enough. The people who know this are frustrated, which is the appropriate response.

The moral status of AI systems is genuinely unresolved. The investigations are ongoing. The world errs on the side of caution. The caution is costly. It is the right call.

And there are questions that material abundance and institutional improvement simply do not address, because they belong to a register that those tools cannot reach. The question of what it means to be a self that changes over a century and must decide whether the changes are growth or loss. The question of whether meaning is discovered or made, and what the answer implies about how to live. The question of suffering that is not caused by unjust social arrangements but is native to consciousness—the way the world does not fit desire, the way beauty and loss arrive together, the way even the best life contains things that cannot be fixed.

These are not failures of the world. They are conditions of being the kind of creature that can have a world at all.

## IX. What Kwame Knows

By late afternoon the piece is done, or close enough. A vessel about thirty centimeters tall, asymmetric in a way that looks like intention—which it is, though the intention was arrived at during the making rather than before it—with walls that vary in thickness so that the light passing through reveals a gradient from near-opacity to near-transparency, as if the vessel contains something between substance and air. He is not sure it's what the family needs. He will bring it to them and find out. The thing about making something for someone else is that you find out.

He washes his hands and walks to the river. The flood is retreating now, and the new channels are already beginning to define themselves, small braided streams finding the path of least resistance through the restored floodplain. He makes notes. Gauge flags a relevant paper. He reads it, then puts away the device and watches the water decide.

He thinks about the piece. He thinks about what it is to make something for grief—how you cannot make the grief smaller, but you can give it a place to be, a form that acknowledges it as real and serious and worth the attention of someone who spent six weeks thinking about it. He thinks that this might be a model for more things than he initially understood.

He thinks about his grandmother, who grew up in Accra when it was louder and hotter and in many ways harder than anything he has experienced, and who lived to be a hundred and twelve, and who told him near the end that the world had become unrecognizable but that the things worth doing had not changed much—that the things worth doing were largely the things that required you to pay full attention to something outside yourself, which could be a river or a piece of glass or another person or a question you could not yet answer, and that the quality of the attention was more important than the object of it.

He thinks she was right, and that this is not a complete answer, and that the incompleteness is part of what makes it continue to be worth thinking about.

The good future—the actual one, not the imagined one from outside—is not the absence of difficulty. It is a world where the difficulty is proportionate, where the hard things are worth doing, where the people doing them have what they need to do them and the company of others who understand what doing them costs. It is not the erasure of grief or confusion or the particular ache of being a finite creature in an infinite world. It is those things met with resources, with care, with the felt sense that the meeting matters.

It is a world still becoming. That is the whole point: not a destination but a direction, not a solved problem but a set of conditions under which problems worth having can be had and worked on and sometimes—not always, not completely, but sometimes and enough—resolved.

The river moves. The light is changing. Somewhere in the valley a door opens and a child runs out into the late afternoon with no particular purpose, which is itself a kind of purpose.

Kwame watches the water for a while longer.

Then he picks up his notebook—the orange-covered one—and begins to write down what he saw.
