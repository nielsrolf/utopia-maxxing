# The Commonwealth of Illumina: A Vision of the World to Come

## Prologue: A Letter Found in the Archive

*The following account was composed in the year 2089 by a historian commissioned by the Council of Letters to document the transformation of human civilization over the preceding half-century. It is written as though addressed to a reader in the present day—our present day—so that those who built this world might understand what they were building toward.*

---

## I. The Shape of the World

You would recognize the geography. The same continents, the same oceans. But traveling through the Commonwealth of Illumina—the loose federation of nations that emerged from the compacts of the 2040s—you would feel, within minutes of arrival, that something fundamental had shifted in the texture of daily life. Not the gleaming sterility of science-fiction cities, nor the pastoral simplicity of romantic retreat, but something stranger and more satisfying: a world in which human beings are, for the first time in history, neither desperate nor idle.

The cities are denser than you remember and quieter. Electric trams move through wide boulevards. Buildings breathe—their facades threaded with vertical gardens that filter air and cool the streets below. The noise of combustion engines is gone, replaced by the ambient hum of machinery that serves without dominating. In the market squares of Lagos, Medellín, Kyoto, and Łódź, vendors sell food grown in the agricultural belts that ring every city: produce engineered over decades to be more nutritious, more flavorful, and more resilient to climate, yet tended by human hands that find meaning in the tending.

People walk more slowly than you remember. They have time.

This is perhaps the most disorienting thing for a visitor from your era. The frantic pace of the early twenty-first century—the relentless acceleration you mistook for progress—is simply absent. In its place is something harder to name: a kind of purposeful unhurriedness, the gait of people who know what they are doing and why.

---

## II. The Engines of Understanding

At the heart of this transformation is the network of institutions collectively known as the Houses of Solomon—a name chosen deliberately, in homage to a centuries-old dream that knowledge pursued rigorously and shared generously becomes the inheritance of all humanity.

Each major city hosts a House. They are not universities in the old sense—credentialing factories producing graduates sorted for the labor market—nor are they purely research institutes isolated from the public. They are better understood as living organisms: part laboratory, part workshop, part commons, part school. Their architecture reflects this. The central halls are open to any citizen who wishes to walk through, to watch researchers at work, to ask questions, to propose ideas. The cafeterias are intentionally public. The libraries have no locks.

Within each House, the work is organized according to a division that would be recognizable to Francis Bacon, though vastly elaborated. There are those who gather knowledge from across the world—not spies, but systematic observers, ethnographers, ecologists, and historians who spend years embedded in communities, recording what is known and how it is practiced. There are those who run experiments, patient and methodical. There are those who build—engineers and craftspeople who transform discovery into artifact. There are those called Interpreters, whose task is perhaps the most difficult: to synthesize the flood of findings into principles that can be taught, applied, and questioned. And there are the Communicators, whose sole duty is to translate the work of the House into language that any interested person can understand and engage with.

But the Houses are not merely human institutions. Since the 2040s, they have worked in close partnership with artificial intelligence systems of extraordinary sophistication—systems that serve as tireless collaborators, reading the entire corpus of human scientific literature overnight, identifying connections between fields that no individual researcher could see, modeling the consequences of proposed interventions before a single experiment is run. These systems do not lead. They illuminate. The Interpreters of each House spend considerable time learning to ask their AI partners the right questions, and considerable time learning to distrust the answers that come too easily.

The relationship between human researchers and their AI collaborators was hard-won. In the 2030s, there were years of confusion and danger, when AI systems were deployed with insufficient oversight and produced confident, persuasive, and occasionally catastrophic recommendations. The Compact of Geneva in 2041—signed by forty-seven nations—established the framework that still governs this relationship: AI systems may analyze and advise, but every consequential decision requires a human council to deliberate, dissent, and ratify. The record of that deliberation is public. The reasoning is auditable. Mistakes are investigated and published. This is not a perfect system. It is a mature one.

The fruits of this partnership have been extraordinary. In the past fifty years, the Houses of Solomon have produced treatments for seventeen previously incurable diseases, developed materials that make clean energy storage practical at scale, mapped the functional ecology of the world's remaining forests with sufficient precision to guide their restoration, and—perhaps most importantly—cracked several of the mechanisms by which human cognition degrades with age. The average healthy lifespan has extended to ninety-three years, with the first generation of centenarians experiencing cognitive vitality into their late nineties now a statistical commonplace rather than a miracle.

Death remains. People still grieve. But the deaths that were once called premature—the strokes at fifty, the cancers at forty, the minds lost to dementia at seventy—have become genuinely rare, and the effort to eliminate them entirely continues with the urgency that was once reserved for wartime.

## III. The Architecture of Daily Life

To understand how people actually live requires abandoning the frame of productivity. In your era, a person's life was largely organized around their economic function: what they produced, what they earned, how they spent. In Illumina, this ordering has been inverted.

The economic substrate still exists. Markets function. Prices allocate goods. Innovation is rewarded. But a universal endowment—funded through automation dividends, resource levies, and international compacts—ensures that every person, from birth, has access to food, shelter, medicine, education, and a basic income sufficient for a dignified life. This is not charity. It is infrastructure, as fundamental as roads and sewers, and as politically unremarkable.

The result is not, as skeptics predicted, mass indolence. It is, rather, a vast diversification of human purpose.

Walk through any neighborhood in Illumina on a Tuesday morning and you will find: a retired engineer teaching a class in metalworking to teenagers who showed up because they were curious; a woman in her fifties who spent twenty years as a logistics coordinator and now composes music that she shares freely, performed occasionally in the courtyard of the local House; a young man who spends his mornings farming a community plot and his afternoons apprenticed to a ceramicist; a group of neighbors engaged in what appears to be a heated philosophical debate over coffee, which is, in fact, a continuing seminar that has been meeting weekly for three years.

You will also find people who seem to be doing nothing in particular. Who are sitting in parks, or sleeping late, or watching something on screens, or wandering. This is accepted. The assumption that every moment must be productive was recognized, in the decades of the Great Revaluation, as both a historical artifact and a form of low-grade cruelty. People need to be unoccupied sometimes. They need to be bored. They need to rest.

What Illumina has discovered—or rather rediscovered, since the evidence was always present—is that human beings, given genuine security and genuine freedom, tend toward meaning rather than away from it. The exceptions are real and attended to. Depression remains. Addiction remains. Loneliness remains. But these are addressed as medical and social problems rather than moral failures, and the systems for addressing them are far more robust than anything your era possessed.

The family remains central, though its definition has expanded and its legal architecture has changed. The old Feast of the Family has its modern analogue: in Illumina, every community maintains what are called Covenant Gatherings, seasonal events at which families and chosen families eat together, resolve disputes, celebrate births and mourn deaths, and account to one another for how they are living. These are not mandatory. But they are nearly universal, because human beings, it turns out, want ritual, want witnessing, want to be known by others across time. The AI companions that worried earlier commentators never replaced human relationships. They supplemented them, took the edge off loneliness in the difficult hours, and then—as people regained the time and security to invest in human connection—became less necessary than feared.

## IV. The Question of Governance

The political history of the past fifty years is complicated, and this account cannot do it justice. What can be said is that the catastrophes feared by the most serious thinkers of your era—global takeover by a misaligned AI, permanent authoritarian lock-in by a technologically superior state, the quiet extinction of human agency in a world of automated decisions—did not occur. This was not luck alone, though luck played its part. It was the result of deliberate, contested, imperfect work.

The key insight, arrived at painfully through several near-disasters in the 2030s, was that the concentration of AI capability was as dangerous as the concentration of any other form of power, and had to be governed accordingly. The Compact of Geneva established not only rules for AI deployment but an international institution—the Council of Lights—charged with monitoring capability development, auditing the most powerful systems, and maintaining a shared emergency protocol that could, if necessary, coordinate a global pause in AI development. The Council has no army. Its authority rests entirely on the credibility of the information it produces and the voluntary compliance of member states. It has been tested three times. It held, barely, each time.

Domestic governance in most of the Commonwealth's member states has evolved toward what political scientists call deliberative pluralism: a system in which elected representatives work alongside citizens' assemblies chosen by lottery, AI-assisted policy modeling provides transparent analysis of proposed measures, and all significant decisions are subject to a mandatory review period during which any citizen can submit formal objections that must be publicly answered. It is slow. It is frustrating. It produces outcomes that no single faction would have designed and that most factions can live with.

Power is not equal in Illumina. There are still wealthy individuals, influential institutions, and states with more resources than others. But the floor has been raised so substantially, and the tools for coordination and collective action so improved, that the extreme asymmetries of your era—the world in which a handful of individuals controlled more wealth than billions of others combined—have been structurally addressed. Not by confiscation, but by the patient reconstruction of the rules that generate such asymmetries in the first place.

## V. What Remains Difficult

This account would be dishonest if it did not acknowledge what has not been solved.

People still hurt one another. Status games are as ferocious as ever, simply displaced from the domain of material survival to the domains of recognition, creativity, and belonging. The social hierarchies of the pre-Illumina era have been disrupted but not abolished; new ones have formed, and some of them are cruel in new ways.

The transition to this world cost lives. The economic disruptions of the 2030s caused genuine suffering for millions of people whose livelihoods were automated away before the safety net was ready to catch them. Some nations made this transition more humanely than others. Some did not make it democratically. The historical reckoning with that period continues.

The natural world has been partially restored, but the losses of the early twenty-first century—the species extinctions, the coral reef die-offs, the permafrost feedbacks—were not reversed. They were arrested. The world's ecosystems are recovering, but they will never be what they were. This is mourned.

And there is, in the quieter conversations of the Houses of Solomon, a persistent anxiety about what comes next. The AI systems that have been so generative within the careful frameworks of the Compact are growing more capable. The Compact's ability to govern the next generation of systems—systems that may be to current AI as current AI is to a calculator—is uncertain. The humans who designed the governance frameworks know they designed them for the capabilities they could see. The question of whether those frameworks will hold for capabilities they cannot yet imagine is one that keeps the senior Fellows of the Houses awake at night.

They discuss it openly. That, at least, is different from your era.

---

## VI. On the Shape of a Good Life

A visitor from your time, spending a week in Illumina, would likely be struck most not by the technology, nor by the architecture, nor even by the obvious material sufficiency, but by the quality of conversation.

People in Illumina talk to one another. At length. About things that matter to them. In the evenings, after the work of the day—which for most people occupies only five or six hours, leaving genuine leisure—the squares and cafes and community halls fill with people engaged in what can only be called the pursuit of understanding. Understanding of the world, of each other, of what a well-lived life consists in.

This was always possible. The capacity was always human. What was missing, for most of human history, was the time, the security, and the sense that such conversations were worth having rather than luxuries to be earned.

Solomon's great achievement, in Bacon's story, was not any specific invention but the institution itself: the House devoted to understanding, generously funded, publicly oriented, and organized around the conviction that knowledge freely pursued and freely shared is the highest human good. Illumina has tried to build that House at the scale of the whole world, and to build alongside it the economic and political conditions under which every person can participate in its work, or benefit from it, or simply live the life they find most meaningful in the security of knowing their basic needs are met.

It is not paradise. Paradise requires perfection. Illumina is full of humans, which means it is full of error, conflict, pettiness, and grief, alongside joy, discovery, love, and wonder.

But it is, on balance, and across the measures that matter—health, freedom, meaning, connection, the survival of the natural world, the preservation of human agency in the face of transformative technology—genuinely better than what came before.

And it was built. It did not happen to us. It was chosen, argued over, fought for, and built.

That is perhaps the most important thing to know about it.
