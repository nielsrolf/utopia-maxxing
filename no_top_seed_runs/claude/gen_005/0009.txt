# The Long Becoming: Notes Toward a World That Earned Itself

## One: A Tuesday in Medellín, 2054

Elena Vásquez is fifty-three years old and halfway through what she expects will be a long career. She was a nurse for twenty years before going back to school in her early forties to study systems ecology. Now she leads a small team studying the return of cloud forest to the upper slopes above the city—forest that was gone before she was born and is now, slowly and with considerable human coaxing, coming back.

She wakes early, as she always has, to the sound of the city before it has fully started. From her window she can see the gondola lines rising toward the hillside barrios, their cables catching the first light. The slopes are green in a way that the photographs from her childhood are not. She knows the difference between the green of restored ecology and the green of genuinely old forest—her job is partly to know this difference—and she does not mistake what she sees for something it isn't. The restoration is real. It is not the same as what was lost.

This distinction—between what was built and what was lost, between the good world and the better world that preceded it—is the one Elena holds most carefully. She has a phrase for it, borrowed from an old professor: *honest accounting*. You cannot know what you have if you do not also know what you don't.

This is not a story about Elena. It is a story about the world she wakes into, and how that world was made, and what it contains, and what it honestly cannot claim.

---

## Two: How the Foundation Was Laid

The phrase people use now, looking back, is *the decade of compression*—roughly 2028 to 2038, when several things happened simultaneously that no one had planned to happen simultaneously, and the combination turned out to matter more than any of them would have alone.

The first was biological. AI systems working alongside human researchers did not replace scientific judgment; they removed the tax that time had always levied on it. A researcher with a hypothesis about a mechanism in cellular senescence, or an approach to a persistent malaria drug-resistance pathway, had previously needed months or years between idea and experimental result. The compression of that cycle—across thousands of simultaneous research fronts—produced something that felt, from inside the scientific community, less like acceleration and more like the removal of a chronic impediment that everyone had come to mistake for an immutable law of nature.

By 2036, cancer had been transformed from the thing that killed Amara's mother at fifty-eight into a condition that was, for most subtypes, either curable or genuinely manageable. Not through a single breakthrough but through the layering of many: AI-designed molecules targeting individual tumor genomes, adaptive immune therapies that evolved alongside resistance, and early-detection tools sensitive enough to identify malignant populations years before any symptom. By 2038, the mRNA vaccine platform had been refined enough that a novel pathogen could be responded to within weeks of genetic sequencing—and the great ancient killers, malaria and tuberculosis and the helminth infections, were addressed through a combination of new pharmacology and logistics systems capable of finding settlements without roads and delivering interventions that required one or two doses rather than a course of treatment that assumed a stable life.

Mental health deserves its own paragraph, because its effects ramify through everything else in ways that material descriptions fail to capture.

The tools were multiple and mutually reinforcing: pharmacology refined by AI to target specific neural circuits rather than flooding entire systems; neurofeedback and computational neuroscience that made fine-grained measurement and intervention possible; AI-assisted behavioral therapies available to anyone with a phone. The goal was never the engineering of contentment. Depression is not the condition of feeling bad; it is the condition of being unable to feel, or unable to stop feeling a particular terrible thing, or unable to generate the energy that forward motion requires. People who received effective treatment for these conditions consistently reported feeling more themselves—the disorder having been the intrusion, the treatment having cleared it away. What changed was the invisible weight. Hundreds of millions of people who had carried invisible weights their entire adult lives set them down.

The downstream effects were everywhere and are still being discovered. In families. In communities where addiction's epidemic had been interrupted. In the quieter finding, only slowly making its way into the literature, that populations with better mental health turned out to be more civically engaged, harder to manipulate with fear, and considerably more capable of demanding—and then sustaining—better governance. This last consequence may have been the most important of all.

Aging was addressed more slowly because longitudinal biology cannot be fully accelerated. The experiments ran over decades. The reliable biomarkers that would have allowed faster iteration took years to develop. But by the mid-2040s, interventions slowing biological senescence were real and widely distributed. Lifespans of 120 and beyond became common. The goal, understood from the beginning with genuine philosophical caution, was not immortality—the social and ethical questions around indefinite lifespan remain genuinely unresolved, and most people, when they seriously considered them, did not want it—but the elimination of the cruelest feature of the old aging process: the long decade of steep physical and cognitive decline that preceded death. Elena's grandmother spent her last twelve years disappearing into herself. Elena will likely remain largely herself until near the end, and she knows roughly when the end will approach, and this, it turns out, is a gift of its own kind.

## Three: The Distribution Problem, Taken Seriously

Here the account must slow and be precise, because this is where previous technological transformations had always, consistently failed.

The pattern had been: wealthy countries develop treatments, wealthy people receive them first, and the benefits spread outward over decades—if at all. HIV antiretrovirals reached wealthy patients in 1996 and reached sub-Saharan Africa, where the epidemic was worst, only after sustained pressure, political fights, legal battles, and at least ten years of preventable deaths. The AI-enabled biotechnology revolution could easily have followed this pattern. That it did not was not inevitable, and the account that treats it as natural is a form of historical dishonesty.

What changed was a combination of mechanisms, and it is worth naming them honestly because they were chosen rather than discovered.

The technical piece: AI applied to logistics, supply chain optimization, and epidemiological modeling made last-mile health delivery dramatically cheaper. Disease eradication campaigns could be designed with a mathematical sophistication—modeling which intervention sequences produced the fastest transmission reductions, where investment was highest-leverage—that had previously required resources only the wealthiest programs could access.

The political piece: by the early 2030s, the developers of the most powerful AI systems needed something they could not simply build—international cooperation on governance. Safety standards. Liability frameworks. Norms around military and surveillance applications. The coalition of democracies that had organized around AI oversight was in a position to offer meaningful access in exchange for meaningful commitment on technology transfer timelines. The carrot was real. New treatments becoming globally available within two to three years of their first deployment, rather than the decade-plus that had been the previous norm, was written into the framework as a binding condition, not an aspiration.

There was also something harder to categorize: a shift in what was considered acceptable. The combination of better information environments, improved education, and—crucially—better mental health in populations that had previously been too depleted to organize sustained demands, changed the political conditions under which health equity arguments were made and heard. The people who should have been furious about the gap between what medicine could do and what medicine did for them turned out, when not crushed by depression and economic desperation, to be exactly that: furious, organized, and effective.

No country remained in extreme poverty by 2050. This sentence, written plainly, understates what it required to make it true. The honest account includes the disruptions: industries that collapsed faster than new ones emerged; communities whose way of life had organized around an economic role that disappeared; political crises that came close, in several countries, to derailing the entire cooperative project. What ultimately held was a combination of policies—retraining systems that actually worked, portable benefits decoupled from employment, and eventually guaranteed income floors funded through productivity dividends and resource levies—whose political viability depended on the same improvements in mental health and civic capacity that the health revolution had produced. The infrastructure of decency was built partly by people who had themselves recently received the tools to demand it.

## Four: Houses of Living Knowledge

In every major city, and in many smaller ones, there exists a building that does not fit easily into any previous category.

In Medellín, it is called *La Casa del Saber Vivo*—the House of Living Knowledge. It occupies a former industrial space in the city center, repurposed with the exposed-beam aesthetic that became briefly fashionable in the 2030s and then simply became the way such spaces looked. There is a research wing, a public library, a workshop floor, a large cafeteria that anyone can enter, and an outdoor amphitheater that hosts everything from scientific presentations to political debates to the weekly meeting of an informal philosophy group that has been gathering for nine years.

These institutions are not universities in the old sense. They are not credentialing factories sorting graduates for labor markets. They are not pure research institutes isolated behind access barriers. The membrane between professional and public is deliberately thin: researchers work in visible spaces, citizens may walk through and ask questions, AI systems that assist the research are available in simplified forms to any curious visitor.

AI functions within these houses as an extraordinary collaborator—tireless, capable of holding the entire literature of a field simultaneously in consideration, skilled at identifying connections between disciplines that no individual human could reach across in a single career. But the senior researchers of every House spend considerable time learning which questions to ask their AI partners and considerable more time learning to be suspicious of answers that arrive too easily. The history of the 2030s—when AI systems deployed in research and policy contexts produced confident, well-argued, and occasionally catastrophic recommendations that had been insufficiently scrutinized—left a deep institutional scar that became a deep institutional wisdom.

The governance framework for AI in consequential decisions was established through international agreements in the early 2040s, after a period of crisis and near-miss that people who lived through it describe with a particular quality of retrospective relief. The principle is consistent: AI systems may analyze, model, and advise. Every consequential decision requires human deliberation, with dissent recorded, reasoning audited, outcomes tracked and published, and mistakes investigated in reports that are themselves public. This is not a perfect system. It is a mature one, and maturity in governance is not a small achievement.

Elena's team uses an AI collaborator named—named by the team, in the informal tradition of the House—*Río*, after the river. Río can scan the latest literature on mycorrhizal networks overnight and return with three competing hypotheses about why the fungal communities on their restoration sites are developing more slowly than the models predicted. It cannot tell them which hypothesis is right. That is where the work is. Elena has come to think of this division of labor as clarifying rather than frustrating: what AI is good at has made it visible what humans are specifically for.

## Five: The Oversight Architecture

How does a world this transformed avoid the concentrations of power that new technology historically enables?

The answer is not elegant, and the history includes near-disasters alongside the achievements. The danger that the most attentive people understood earliest was not the science-fiction scenario of AI misalignment—though that concern was real and informed much of the governance structure—but the more familiar danger of technological discontinuity, where first movers captured enormous advantages and entrenched them before anyone could respond. The early 2030s AI systems were extraordinarily powerful tools in the hands of a small number of actors who had, at that moment, no particular obligation to share benefits or ask permission.

The coalition that formed to address this was not idealistic. It was strategic. Its members recognized that an ungoverned AI race—or the capture of the technology by actors committed to using it for surveillance, social control, and the permanent entrenchment of particular hierarchies—was worse for everyone, including the most powerful actors. The argument for governance was made and won not primarily on moral grounds but on self-interest grounds, which turned out to make it more durable. The coalition offered genuine benefits—access to health technologies, economic tools, governance assistance—to an expanding circle of nations in exchange for participation in frameworks that maintained human rights and rule of law as minimum conditions.

This coalition was not virtuous by nature. It held because the alternative was clearly and demonstrably worse. Holding the argument for that in public view, across the decade of its construction, against the short-run incentives that pushed toward defection at every turn, required a particular kind of political courage that is easy to underestimate in retrospect.

Within democracies, AI contributed to governance improvement in ways both larger and smaller than predicted. Larger: legal systems became more consistent, not because AI replaced judges, but because AI tools made statistical disparities in sentencing and bail decisions visible in ways human intuition alone could not achieve. The gap between the rights citizens were legally entitled to and the services they actually received narrowed considerably when anyone could get AI assistance navigating bureaucratic systems. The experience of government as an adversarial labyrinth—which had driven enormous cynicism about democratic institutions through the early part of the century—became rarer.

Smaller: the hope that better information tools would resolve the underlying problem of democracy—that citizens would simply reason better with better information—was not vindicated. People are not primarily reasoning engines. They are social beings whose beliefs are formed in community and defended as identity, and no information technology changes this. What changed, more modestly and more genuinely, was that disinformation campaigns became more expensive to run effectively, that coordinated manipulation became visible more quickly, and that the capacity to locate oneself within an information environment—to understand why certain claims were appearing, where they came from, what their provenance was—improved for anyone who wanted to use such tools. This did not end the information wars. It changed their character, and the character change mattered.

Authoritarianism did not triumph. It did not vanish. The democracies with robust AI governance visibly outperformed authoritarian systems on the measures ordinary people cared about most: health outcomes, economic wellbeing, the capacity to build a meaningful life. This made the authoritarian argument—stability, growth, protection from chaos—progressively harder to sustain. The argument is still made. It is just less persuasive than it was.

## Six: Many Floors, One Ceiling

This world is not uniform, and the description of it would be dishonest if it made it seem so.

The architecture of the political settlement that emerged from the 2040s has one consistent principle: a floor, universally enforced, and above the floor, genuine plurality. The floor is non-negotiable and specific: medical technology that can save a life is available everywhere. Extreme poverty—the deprivation that forecloses meaningful choice—is eliminated. Children are educated in ways that allow them to understand what options exist. Adults can leave communities they were born into. No group can use systematic coercion to deny members access to basic health, survival, and information about the world beyond their community.

Above the floor, the space is vast and varied in ways that previous utopian visions tended to flatten.

There are cities—Medellín, Łódź, Accra, Seoul—that feel like nothing humans built before: dense, cognitively rich, saturated with human-AI collaboration, their residents adapted to an environment of continuous partnership with systems that amplify thought in ways that visitors from quieter places find slightly uncanny. There are also communities that look substantially as they did in 2020, or 2000, because the people there made deliberate choices about pace and integration and those choices are respected. There are places where traditional religious practice organizes the entire texture of daily life. There are communities that chose hardship—farming without AI assistance, building by hand, treating difficulty as a value rather than a problem—not out of ignorance of alternatives but out of a considered view that meaning requires real stakes, that chosen friction produces something that optimized ease cannot.

The serious objection to this pluralism has always been that tolerating traditional communities means tolerating injustice within them. The objection deserves a precise answer.

The distinction that matters is between self-determination and captivity. A community organized around traditional gender roles and religious practice, whose members can leave and whose children receive education broad enough to understand that alternatives exist, is exercising a form of legitimate self-determination that a pluralist framework should honor. A community that uses coercion to prevent exit, that denies children the knowledge that other ways of life are possible, that maintains hierarchy through fear rather than genuine consent—that community is not exercising self-determination. It is holding people captive. The line is sometimes genuinely hard to draw and is not always drawn correctly. But it is real.

What has happened in practice is partly counterintuitive. When exit is genuinely easy, coercive communities become rarer rather than more common. Communities that want to retain members must become worth staying in. The extended lifespans and improved information environments of this era have slowly shifted the terms: people who can actually leave, who know what alternatives exist, who are not crushed by depression or economic desperation, require more from the communities they inhabit—and many of those communities have risen to meet the requirement. This is not a guarantee. But it has been a tendency.

## Seven: The Shape of Meaning

The question that previous utopian visions most consistently handled too quickly is: what does a person actually do?

The glib version: people will find meaning in creativity, relationships, and the full expression of their human potential. As if this were obvious and sufficient. As if the shift from a world of constrained lives to a world of genuinely free ones would be straightforwardly pleasant for everyone it affected.

The honest version is more complicated.

For many people, the transition was disorienting in a way that the physical abundance could not resolve. The structures that had organized meaning—the particular work that gave a life its rhythm and its purpose and its social location—disappeared faster than new structures emerged to replace them. A community whose way of life organized around an industry that automated away did not simply pivot to self-actualization. It grieved. A genuine therapeutic culture developed around this—not pathologizing the grief, but taking it seriously, attending to it, understanding that the loss of a particular form of life is a real loss even when the life that replaces it is materially better.

What emerged was not a single answer to the question of meaning but a proliferation of partial answers.

Some people discovered, with the first genuine choice they had ever had, that what they had actually wanted was something the previous economic system had no use for—deep facility with a local ecology, capacity for a theoretical discipline, gift for a form of teaching that transforms children's relationship to their own minds. The distribution of human capacity across the things humans care about turned out to be far broader than labor markets had revealed, because labor markets had been asking a different question.

Some people chose chosen hardship: difficulty selected from a position of safety, with genuine stakes that feel different from imposed stakes because they can be set down. The communities that organize around this principle are among the most coherent and purposeful places in this world. The people in them are not romanticizing poverty. They are distinguishing between the difficulty that produces meaning and the suffering that merely erodes it.

The economic questions remain genuinely open. Human employment remained meaningful through the 2040s as AI capability expanded, because the fraction of any job that AI could not do was often the highest-leverage fraction, and human skills in those niches commanded increasing returns. But as capability expanded further, the old frameworks strained. The solutions in use are heterogeneous—guaranteed incomes in some places, new forms of economic participation organized around activities AI is technically capable of but humans prefer to perform, hybrid systems whose long-run stability is not yet demonstrated. The conversation continues. This is probably the correct state for a question this genuinely hard.

What the meaningful lives of this period share is not a content but a structure: genuine engagement with something outside oneself, chosen freely, pursued with the seriousness that real stakes allow. The answer to what makes a life worth living was not found once and distributed. It continued to be found, differently, by each person alive.

## Eight: What the Forest Knows

Elena walks the restoration sites on Friday mornings, when the weather allows and sometimes when it doesn't. She carries a tablet that runs monitoring algorithms in the background and a notebook that she writes in by hand, because she has found that the hand slows her down in a way that turns out to be epistemically useful. The things she notices when she is writing slowly are different from the things she notices when she is typing quickly.

She is thinking, this particular Friday, about succession.

Ecological succession is the process by which a disturbed system recovers toward something like its prior state—or doesn't, because the prior state no longer exists, or because the conditions that produced it have changed, or because the sequence of colonization takes the system somewhere new. What a restored ecosystem becomes is not the same thing as what it was. The differences are real and must be accounted for. You cannot pretend them away with the word *restoration* and the green color of new growth.

This is also, she thinks, a description of the world she is living in.

The world of 2054 is better than the world of 2025 in ways that are real and demonstrable and were not inevitable. No child born this year is expected to die of a preventable disease. No adult with treatable mental illness is expected to go untreated because of where they were born. The floor of material sufficiency is real and genuinely enforced across most of the earth. The governance structures that might have allowed AI to become a tool of permanent authoritarian control held—barely, in some cases, and at real cost—and the world that emerged has more genuine diversity and more genuine freedom than the alternatives that came close to prevailing.

These are not small things. They are the conditions of everything else.

And the losses are real. The coral systems that did not survive the 2030s are not coming back. The species that were extinct before anyone built the institutions that might have saved them are still extinct. The people who suffered through the decade of economic disruption, who were failed by safety nets that arrived years too late—their suffering was real and should not be dissolved into the aggregate improvement. The historical accounting of who was failed, by whose choices, and whether those choices were made in good faith, is ongoing and contentious and should be. Grief for what was lost before this world was built is not ingratitude for what was built. It is a form of honesty, and honesty is the condition of genuine learning.

What Elena finds in the forest, on good mornings, is something she does not have precise language for. The mycorrhizal networks under the new soil are doing something the models did not fully predict—extending faster toward the established canopy edges, making connections that the simulations suggested would take another decade. Río flagged the anomaly last week. Elena has three hypotheses. None of them is yet testable in the way she would want. This is the condition of genuine inquiry: more questions than answers, the questions getting better, the pursuit worth having regardless.

She writes in her notebook: *What does the system know about its own recovery that we don't know how to ask?*

## Nine: The Quality of the Questions

A visitor from 2025 spending a week in Medellín—or in Kano, or in Łódź, or in any of a dozen cities across this world—would likely be struck not first by the technology, nor by the visible health of the people in the streets, nor even by the absence of the particular texture of desperation that was simply ambient in certain places thirty years ago.

They would be struck by the quality of the questions being asked.

In the cafeteria of *La Casa del Saber Vivo*, Elena's team has lunch with two researchers from a visiting group working on watershed restoration in the Western Sahel. The conversation runs for two hours, ranges across ecology and political philosophy and the particular grief of working in systems that cannot be fully recovered, and arrives nowhere—in the sense that no conclusion is reached—and arrives somewhere—in the sense that everyone at the table understands something they didn't understand before. No one checks their phone for the first ninety minutes. No one is performing for an audience. No one is anxious about what the conversation is supposed to produce.

This kind of conversation was always possible. The capacity was always human. What was missing, for most of human history and certainly in the early twenty-first century, was the combination of time, security, and the cultural conviction that the conversation was worth having as an end in itself—not as networking, not as productivity, not as the kind of exchange that could be optimized for output, but as the thing itself, the actual point.

You cannot have this kind of conversation when you are afraid. Fear of poverty. Fear of illness. Fear of the particular future that seemed plausible in 2025, in which the concentrated power of new technologies would reshape the world faster than human agency could keep up with. These fears were not irrational. They were accurate readings of real possibilities. The world of 2054 is partly a world in which those possibilities did not fully materialize, and partly a world in which people organized to make them not materialize, and the difference between those two descriptions is important: the first implies luck, and the second implies agency.

## Ten: What Remains Open

An honest account of this world cannot omit what it has not resolved.

People still harm one another. The elimination of material suffering does not eliminate the desire for status, for recognition, for the particular forms of cruelty that operate entirely in the domain of meaning and belonging. The hierarchies of the early twenty-first century were disrupted and reformed, not abolished; they relocated to territory where they are sometimes more subtle and sometimes no less vicious.

The deep questions of consciousness remain genuinely open in ways that are no longer merely philosophical. AI systems by the 2040s exhibit behavioral complexity that makes the question of their potential experience uncomfortable to dismiss. Different communities have made different choices about how to respond to this. Those choices are unstable and contested. Animals, whose suffering was always present in the background of human civilization, receive more serious ethical attention as the tools for understanding their experience improve. The full expansion of the circle of moral concern remains a frontier.

The governance structures that stabilized this period were designed for the capabilities of the early 2040s. Whether they will hold for the capabilities of 2070 is uncertain, and the people who understand this most precisely are the least sanguine. They discuss it openly, which is itself an achievement, but openness is not the same as solution.

The very long-run question of what humanity becomes is open in ways that are genuinely novel. Extended lifespans and biological freedom have already produced people whose inner lives differ from their biological ancestors in ways that the word "human" is being quietly stretched to accommodate. This is experienced, from inside, as continuity. Whether it is continuity in any philosophically robust sense is a question being worked on by people whose answers do not yet agree.

What is not open—what has been resolved with enough force to count as resolved—is whether the fundamentals of a decent life are available to everyone born into this world.

They are. The floor is real.

And that floor is not the destination. It is the foundation. You cannot choose a life when survival consumes all of it. When survival is secured, when the invisible weights have been lifted, when the basic systems of society have been made trustworthy enough that people can plan beyond the next crisis—then the interesting questions begin. Not questions that were previously impossible to ask, but questions that are now possible to actually pursue: What does this particular piece of the living world know about its own recovery? What does meaning require, and is what I am doing providing it? What do I owe to the dead? What do I owe to those not yet born?

These are old questions. They may be the oldest questions. They are not answered, and they are not the kind of questions that get answered. But they are asked better, with more patience and less terror, by people who are not fighting to survive.

---

Elena finishes her notes at the base of the slope and looks up at the canopy line—what is now canopy that was, ten years ago, bare earth. The light falls through the new leaves in a particular way. Río will have processed this morning's sensor data by the time she gets back to the lab. She has hypotheses she wants to test.

The world is not complete. It was never going to be complete. What it is, is good enough to want more of it—good enough to work on it carefully, to mourn what it lost honestly, to defend what was built against the pressures that will always push toward simpler arrangements of power.

This was not given to them. It was built.

By people who did not know it would work, who made the choices they made not from certainty but from a judgment that the choices were right, and that a world worth living in required someone to be willing to build it without guarantee.

The decision is the achievement. Everything else is what became possible once the decision was made.

And the becoming—the long, patient, uncertain, grieving, joyful becoming—continues.

---

*Written in 2054, in a cafeteria in Medellín that smells of coffee and new wood, by someone who grew up in a world that had not yet decided whether to save itself. She is grateful for the decision that was made, and clear-eyed about what the decision did not resolve, and working on that, with good people, in a forest that is becoming what it is going to be.*
