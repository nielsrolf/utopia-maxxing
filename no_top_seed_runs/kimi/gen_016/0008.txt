UTOPIA  
The City That Unbuilds Itself While You Sleep  

I wake because the floor under my left shoulder blade has liquefied into warm clay and is gently pushing me sideways, reminding me that the lease on my bedroom ends at sunrise. The building does not evict; it rotates. Every room is on a turntable that completes one revolution every twenty-four hours, so that no one wakes twice in the same orientation to the river, the sky, or the memory of who they were yesterday.  

The clay re-solidifies as I stand. A seam opens and coughs up yesterday’s shoes, resized for the feet I have today. Our architecture never learned the word “finished,” so it practices daily self-mediation: a wall wins an argument with a window by becoming a door; a staircase forgets its destination and grows a balcony instead. The city issues permits for these mutations the way other places issue parking tickets—by not issuing them at all, trusting that anything still standing at dusk must have negotiated a treaty with gravity and the neighbours.  

Breakfast is served on the roof, which last night folded itself into an origami amphitheatre so that moths could perform their one-act opera about streetlights who believe they are moons. This morning the roof has unfolded into a flat field of barley that ripens in minutes because it is impatient to become porridge. I harvest one handful, leave the rest to audition for next week’s climate. The cook-stove is a retired satellite dish; its heart still beats in orbital rhythms, so porridge must be stirred counter-clockwise if you want it to taste like yesterday, clockwise if you prefer tomorrow. I go clockwise and swallow a day that has not yet happened.  

While I eat, the building interviews me through the spoons.  
Question one: Which of your certainties has begun to smell?  
Question two: Name a border you have never crossed but already apologise to.  
Question three: What sound would the river make if it forgot the word “bank”?  
I answer out loud because the city has no surveillance—only listening. My replies dissolve into the barley stalks and re-emerge an hour later as the exact colour of the sky at 17:42, visible only to people who have ever failed at love.  

By 08:00 the roof remembers it is also a plaza. Tables, scooters, and toddlers sprout from hidden hinges. A grandmother pedals up the vertical garden that doubles as elevator, harvests three tomatoes, and pedals back down carrying her granddaughter who has just learned to wink at strangers. No one checks IDs because identity is understood as a temporary weather: you are the humidity that condenses between who you were at seven and who the river needs you to be at dusk.  

I descend through the building’s digestive system. Each floor is a stomach for a different kind of past: the second floor ferments last decade’s protest banners into bioplastic; the fifth softens discarded prosthetic limbs into jelly that will feed the rehab clinic’s new orchard of kneeling trees. Trees here do not grow up; they grow down, roots reaching for groundwater like slow, green lightning. In autumn they kneel on purpose, offering their crowns as stools for anyone who needs to sit and confess to photosynthesis.  

Outside, the street is a river of packed snow that never melts and never freezes. Every footstep presses a short story into the surface; the next pedestrian reads it with their soles and edits by walking on. The stories keep getting shorter, denser, until they collapse into single characters that even sparrows can pronounce. When the alphabet is complete, the street exhales and the snow lifts off the ground like a sheet shaken by giants, revealing yesterday’s cobblestones re-arranged into a mosaic of every face that passed this way. Tomorrow the snow will return, newly illiterate, ready to begin again.  

I board the tram that exists only while you are looking at it. Capacity: one uncertainty per seat. The conductor’s badge reads I AM THE MAP YOU HAVEN’T DRAWN YET. She asks for my destination in the form of a regret. I offer the memory of a bridge I never finished building between two arguments. She punches the ticket straight through the image, returns the hole to me as a brass ring I can wear until I meet someone who needs a gate.  

We glide on rails grown from salt-tolerant asters. Every wheel-turn releases a syllable of sign language into the soil; commuters who know how to stand very still can feel the earth learning to speak kindly. The tram stops at the Bureau of Gentle Subtraction, where I work four mornings a week. Our task is to remove things faster than they can be added: carbon, noise, the belief that enough is a static quantity. We do not store the removed atoms; we unwrite them into negative space that behaves like mild weather. Last year the city shrank by three percent and everybody felt warmer, as if missing coats had been converted to extra heartbeats.  

My cubicle is a soundproof lung. I breathe in last night’s helicopter anxiety, exhale it as the smell of bread rising. Colleagues sit inside translucent cocoons spun from cancelled deadlines. When a cocoon turns opaque, the worker inside has earned sabbatical: they will be shipped to the horizontal mountain that exists only on Sundays, where glaciers teach adults to nap without apology.  

At 11:11 the city performs its daily Rehearsal of Removal. Sirens do not scream; they recite lullabies backwards so that sorrow forgets how to spell itself. Buildings open like books and release pages of themselves into the wind. The pages are blank because we have not yet committed to tomorrow’s mistakes. Children chase them, add ink, fold them into paper planes that land on balconies where elders unfold the planes, read the freshly invented fables, and begin cooking supper according to the moral: for example, season lentils with the guilt you feel for outliving your first pet; serve rice in the shape of a river that once memorised your name.  

Lunch is distributed by the public library that exists only while you are inside it. You exit carrying a sandwich that tastes like the page you were reading. My page was page 42 of a novel that will be written next century; the sandwich therefore tastes like future tense, slightly metallic, with a hint of asteroid. I eat it while sitting on the cathedral steps that are also the city’s tongue. The stone tastes each footfall and learns to pronounce new pronouns for god, none of which are capitalised.  

The cathedral’s bell is a drop of molten glacier that never quite reaches the ground. It hovers, humming, until enough people have hummed along; then it rises again, cooled by collective pitch, ready for tomorrow’s tremble. Time here is not kept; it is negotiated. Elderly citizens receive an extra minute every birthday, stolen from teenagers who have minutes to spare. The transaction happens at dawn on the reversible bridge that flows in both directions. If you meet yourself coming back, you must swap jackets. This is how empathy is laundered.  

After lunch I walk to the estuary where the city practices endings. A warehouse is scheduled to unbuild itself at 14:00. We gather in the tidal muck, each carrying a tool of gentle refusal: I bring a feather that can erase rivets, someone else carries a violin bow strung with seaweed that persuades concrete to remember it was once limestone at the bottom of a calm sea. The warehouse sighs, releases a smell of 1987—cheap coffee, new sneakers, a romance that ended in the parking lot. We inhale, exhale in morse code: thank you, goodbye, return to scattered. Bolts loosen like buttons after a large meal; beams bow politely and step into the water where they float off as rafts for seabirds who have always wanted to nest inside the alphabet.  

By 15:30 the warehouse is a diagram sketched in salt. Children outline the absence with sticks, invent hopscotch games that can only be played inside something that no longer exists. The rules change every jump, dictated by the tide’s lisp. Winner receives the right to name the void; the void must be named after a lullaby. Tonight mothers will hum the new song over cradles, teaching infants that absence can be soft, can be rocked to sleep.  

I take the tram home through the district where houses hibernate. November is the month of voluntary collapse. Facades fold inward like hands in prayer; roofs lie down on gardens, pretending to be soil. Residents move into the corridors, then the stairwells, then the coat cupboards, practicing the art of living in progressively smaller footprints. By December they occupy only the volume of their own bodies, stacked like nesting dolls in the town square, warm under snow that has agreed to behave as duvet. In spring they will re-expand, surprised by how much room they suddenly have for new songs.  

Dinner is cooked over the flame of forgiven debt. We feed it last month’s rent, the fine for dancing in the library, the compound interest that never quite compounded. The fire burns blue, the colour of accounts closing. Around the communal stove we sit on stools carved from police barricades that volunteered early retirement. Someone brings dough; someone brings yesterday’s sunset folded into a handkerchief; someone brings the smell of a train station where they once said goodbye and meant it. We eat the flatbread hot, tearing it open to release trapped sunsets that splash across the table and dye our fingers gold.  

Night begins with the lullaby of the void. We hum the name that won hopscotch, let it settle in the throat like cool water. Children are sung backward into their daytime clothes, pockets filled with the keys to doors that will be invented while they dream. Couples practice the art of shared amnesia: they co-write the Forgetting Ledger, recording small memories they agree to lose—first compliment, last complaint, the exact middle of an argument about whose turn it is to buy soap. When the ledger is full, they read it aloud underwater, in the bath that is also a time zone. The water remembers only what is useful; the rest precipitates as salt that will exfoliate tomorrow’s arguments.  

I walk the dog that is also the moon. It waxes and wanes along the sidewalk, pulling tides of litter into neat piles of apology. We stop beneath the tree that grows petitions instead of leaves. Tonight’s petition is addressed to Gravity, politely requesting a holiday next Tuesday so that elderly dancers may rehearse jumping without landing. I sign with a fingerprint of porridge, the dog signs with a paw-print of tidal foam. The tree inhales the petition, exhales a soft breeze that smells of reconsidered physics. Somewhere, a woman practising pirouettes on the sixth floor feels her bones lighten by one micro-gram; she smiles, stored energy ready to become air.  

Before bed the building interviews me again, gentler now.  
What did you unmake today?  
What will you permit to grow in the space you became?  
Which heartbeat did you borrow and to whom will you return it?  
I answer by leaving the front door ajar. The corridor breathes through the gap, carrying the smell of barley and unfinished sentences into other apartments, stitching them together with the slow thread of maybe.  

Sleep arrives as a train that inhales while another exhales, carrying me gently into the jurisdiction of tomorrow. The last thing I hear is the river practising its new name, syllables clinking like stones turned soft by years of listening. It is learning to say We without excluding the banks, to say Now without erasing the melt-water of last century.  

Tomorrow the porridge will taste backwards, the tram will request a risk I have not yet imagined, the warehouse will reassemble itself inside the belly of a whale whose ribs are made from reversible bridges. We will continue building subtraction at the speed of forgiveness, practising the art of staying soft enough to let the future edit us first.  

Our utopia is a city that remains tender by never arriving, a grammar of perpetual loosening. We hold completion lightly enough to let it fly, catch it again by the corner we forgot to finish, fold it into a paper boat that carries tonight’s lullaby across the estuary of maybe, into the mouth of the river that is also a question, into the sleep of children who still believe that absence can be soft, that endings are only doorways left politely ajar, that tomorrow will taste like the day after tomorrow, seasoned with the salt of unmade mistakes and the sweet, unfinished dough of becoming.
