UTOPIA  
ILMA / 47° N, 8° E, 2124  
Field Notes by Apprentice Cartographer Šejla Mahì, age 27  
(My life measured in centimetres of shared breathing)

0. Sunrise Inventory  
The day officially starts when the river forgives itself.  
A solar-lily unfurls on the water’s surface and records the apology on a pollen-film that smells of wet cotton and cardamom. No one reads the message; we taste it. If the flavour is sour at the back of the tongue, the city spends an extra hour in Dawn Court, settling yesterday’s unfinished gestures. If it is sweet, cafés open early and light-rail slugs stay in their cocoons so the streets belong to pedestrians. I have tasted both in the same week; that is ordinary.  

We do not keep clocks, only tide-sharing accounts. Every citizen begins the month with 1,440 minutes—exactly the number in a day—but the minutes are not yours until you give at least 120 away. You can donate them to a stranger learning to walk again, to the communal bread ovens, or to an elder who wants to re-read all 7,412 revisions of her suicide note before burning it. Minutes you give return doubled, pressed like flowers inside the Ledger of Postponed Futures, a structure that used to be a bank vault. The doubling is not interest; it is gratitude that has learned photosynthesis.

1. Architecture in Joint Custody  
Every building is divorced from its original purpose and remarried to another species every decade.  
The former parliament is now a fungal lung: vaulted walls inhale canal water at dusk and exhale fog at dawn, distributing mineral apology evenly across roofs. The maternity ward used to be a power station; embryos once stored in cryo-tanks have hatched into glow-worms that migrate through copper pipes spelling “slow” in cursive Morse. Once they spell “slow” 1,000 times the ward will petition to become a train station that never moves, only rocks passengers into remembering the first sound they ever heard.  

Houses are built two at a time—one for residents, one for their shadows. If you neglect your garden, the shadow house leans closer, compressing yours until you learn to share bedrooms with strangers you almost loved. The mortgage ends only when both houses voluntarily swap inhabitants for a full lunar year; the goal is not ownership but mutual hostage of perspectives. Last winter I lived as someone else’s silhouette for three weeks and learned the weight of footsteps that never quite catch you.

2. Labour: Rehearsed Collapse, Guaranteed Reassembly  
All work is measured in collapses per month.  
Bakers burn the bread on purpose, surgeons rehearse exquisite failures on synthetic organs, bridge architects orchestrate a mid-span sag that dips just low enough to graze the river before it tightens back. Spectators are paid equal wages for cheering the moment of recovery: bread sliced open to reveal charcoal snowing into rosemary smoke, the prosthetic heart twitching back to sinus rhythm while singing a lullaby it learned from the patient’s son, the bridge humming like a cello string connecting two riverbanks that had filed for separation but reconciled mid-phrase.  

My own apprenticeship is Cartography of Interdependent Futures. I map roads that choose to remember differently from the map. I am paid in conditional hours: every time I revise a street to curve toward the immigrant orchard, the city gifts me a pocket of sixty minutes that someone else has donated. Last month I accumulated enough to live my parents’ first kiss in Zagreb, 1998. I spent the hour standing inside their shared inhale, feeling the tram scrape sparks above us that later became my name.

3. Currency Dissolving into Counselling, Counselling Fermenting into Currency  
Money is grown in three compost currencies, each shelf-stable for exactly the average length of a heartbreak:  

a) Breath-Tokens  
Glass beads filled with exhaled sentences you do not yet believe. When you finally speak the sentence aloud the bead fractures and releases an aroma you forgot from childhood—rain on hot pavement, library glue, grandmother’s house the week after she died. Whoever smells it will pay your next bill for you, usually in the form of an unasked question that solves a problem you tried to bury.  

b) Tide Debts  
Ink-print on kelp parchment. Wave action rubs words away until only vowels remain; those vowels are mailed to inland towns that have forgotten the ocean exists. When you meet someone whose debt has become pure vowel-groan, you are required by custom to give them your shoes. Walking barefoot on their soil teaches both of you dialects of rescue.  

c) Seed-Apologies  
Living coins minted from carrot-root and copper. They sprout only when the person you injured forgives you in a dream, not waking. Dreams are verified by communal moth-readers who sip the volatile chemicals of night kitchens. Forgiveness verified, the sprout fruits crisp orange cylinders containing 7% of the original guilt. You eat your own guilt for lunch; it tastes like pepper and sermon. Enough lunches in a row and your joints remember how to pivot without snapping at strangers.

4. Everyday Ethics: Choir Governance in Three Registers  
Law is sung in triplicate: by lungs, leaves, and library.  

Human Choir meets on rooftops where argon wind mixes with our collective exhalation. A proposal (say, lowering the birth-license from two watersheds per person to one) passes only if the harmony sustains until the oldest singer runs out of breath. Thus octogenarians wield veto power; they practise circular breathing by telling uninterrupted stories of every war they refused to fight.  

The Chlorophyll Choir grafts amendments onto the city’s photosynthetic skin. Mango leaves publish chloride limits, banyan aerial roots annotate zoning debates in rings per radius, vines outside the courthouse grow slower whenever the docket holds too many evictions. Legislators must read the rings like Braille before sunrise; many a cruel ordinance has withered overnight when vines refused to climb the council walls.  

The Unborn Register hums below 20 hertz through drainpipes. We hear it as sudden sleepiness while washing dishes. If the drowsiness is gentle, the proposal is considerate of descendants; if it jolts like cold water, we re-write. Children learn to yawn in harmony with drainpipes and therefore act as proxy voters for their own futures.  

5. Learning as Temporary Ghosthood, Graduating into Custodial Citizenship  
At thirteen you die for one day.  
The city conducts clinical death at low tide; hearts stop for exactly 210 breaths of the world. Students walk the fog-dark as apprentice ghosts, attending lectures by extinct species: a silky anteater explains the grammar of hairline cracks in concrete, the passenger pigeon teaches how to arrive collectively or not at all, the cave lion gives detention to anyone who fears small spaces. Upon revival each child carries an extinction inside the ribcage like a borrowed lung; civic classes begin with how to draft policy for beings who can no longer cast ballots with bodies.  

Adults continue haunting by volunteering to become particulate patience once a month. We dissolve into the condensation on bakery windows, whisper reminders to strangers who forgot how to pronounce their own names. The haunting ends when someone wipes the glass, unknowingly inhales us, and laughs for no reason. We reform beside compost bins, pockets full of flour and somebody else’s laughter. Diploma is paid in advance: one nightmare you pledge never to wake from alone, one dream you promise to share nightly for seven years.

6. Health: Redistribution of Risk with Optional Reassembly  
Illness is diagnosed in Water-Mirrors, shallow pools coated with retroreflective algae that ripple the reflections of everyone who has ever lent you time. If images ripple backward, sickness is ancestral; sideways, environmental; if they refuse mirror status, prognosis is envy. Therapy proceeds by potluck: your tumour is portioned into carnival tickets. Strangers bid to cradle 11% of your epilepsy for the month in exchange for guitar lessons, teach you to sing while they host your limp. The more hosts, the lighter each shard becomes until lethal diagnosis is diluted into a stutter, an inconvenient déjà-vu, a hiccup every sunset that reminds you to telephone your sister. Death still occurs, but statistically resembles twilight: gradual and collaboratively experienced. Mortality is the graph of how elegantly we scatter the burden.

7. Housing: Boundary as Hostage Negotiation  
No dwelling is built alone. Structures arrive in quadruplets—one for humans, one for pollinators, one for mycelium, one for data-shadows. Each quarter holds hostages of the others’ mortality. If you evict bees your bedroom walls develop hexagonal holes that whistle dirges at night until you host a new swarm. Forget to feed the fungal layer beneath foundations and your address slowly erases itself from every map; visitors arrive only when they embrace the probability of getting lost. Data-shadows flake off smart-balconies like moulting paint, clustering into autonomous poems that demand less screen-time and more night-crickets. Stability is negotiation, never property.

8. Breakfast Ritual: Apomelet Reconciliation  
Every roof hosts a communal hearth. Each resident cracks one egg laid overnight by city hens who dine on yesterday’s gossip. If the shell is empty you owe the gossiped-about party a lullaby. If double-yolked they owe you forgiveness. All eggs are whisked together regardless, because obligation tastes better folded. The resulting omelet lengthens like elastic while people volunteer new ingredients: nettle from flood-plain gardens, cheese aged inside music (Beethoven for sharpness, lullabies for cream), saffron cultivated by inmates who learned colour theory while finishing their sentence for non-violent crimes. You eat until full, then feed the remainder to street-side bioluminescent pigs who convert calories into public night-light. Their excrement grows tulip bulbs that spell the mayor’s name in Morse, a title refreshed annually through democratic lottery.

9. Love, Divorce, and the National Registry of Almost  
Marriage licences last seven years, renewable only if both parties convince an impartial panel of eels in the city fountain. Couples each place a hand in the water; if eels nibble both hands simultaneously, renewal is granted. If only one, the union dissolves without shame; the state gifts each former partner 300 tide-debt notes to spend on grief-risotto and solo train rides that terminate wherever carriage chooses.  

Before remarrying you must register one “Almost” in the national database: a person you nearly loved but circumstance refused. Each year the city lottery pairs random Almosts to collaborate on one civic project—planting roof-top rice, painting bridges with thermochromic paint that exposes hidden trauma when temperature drops, composing lullabies for turbines. This prevents nostalgia calcifying into regret; it also produces the most breathtaking sidewalk orchids you will ever see bloom at 03:00 under drone moonlight.

10. Death: Graduated Letting-Go  
You die seven times, each smaller than the last:  
Public death crowdsourced into festival: organs harvested as musical instruments—lungs become bagpipes mourning future oil spills, liver a drum recalling nights you rowed boats of immigrants to safety.  
Social death—your name conjugated in conditional tense for a year; verbs keep you present until stories get bored and move on.  
Civic death—your vote redistributed to a season; for one year autumn gets extra representation, colours smear longer across banyan rows.  
Sensory death—loved ones forget the smell of your neck at a ceremony perfumed by every shampoo you hated; grief closes circuit.  
Property death—possessions unlearn you; door-handles cool, shoes widen for different bones, your toothbrush migrates unprotested to neighbour’s cup.  
Photographic death—images of you fold until pupils overlap into single black seed planted in noisy library book.  
Seed death—when stranger turns that page in fifty years, they sneeze final you into lamplight where you die mid-sparkle, visible only as dust slowing.  

After seven you may return once, as weather. My lover chose a two-minute drizzle on my birthday—umbrellas blossomed paintings of poems she never finished, rain tasted of yeast and almost. I baked bread from that water; every slice rose with the pressure of unsent love letters. We ate, belched thunder very softly, felt mysteriously lighter.

11. From Now to Then Without Knowing Exactly Where  
Historians agree on nothing but the scent-marker of 2033: wet cardboard, diesel, and overripe pears. They also concur that change arrived not by victory but by side-effect cascading slower than policy but faster than grammar. One story claims global teenagers refused to translate the word “profit” into any tongue older than seven years; economies stammered until vocabularies softer than carbohydrate grew. Another insists metro drivers discovered trains shunt smoothly when lullabies containing no possessive pronouns are sung over PA; transport budgets diverted half their surplus to municipal choirs before legislators noticed. Yet another says corals threatened to unionise and withhold colour from postcards unless humans signed provisional pre-nups promising property is a lunar rental, not life sentence. Truth tastes like carrot-copper coins under tongue: undeniable for a season, compost next.  

Whatever the spark, we climbed inside collective exhalation and named it city, named it maybe. We presume revision, not utopia. Every constitution ends with bacteria fluorescing only under full moon: “This is not finished. Finish it kindly.”  

Tonight I float home along canal-boardwalks lit by pig-light. My satchel holds a mirror-film page inked with unfinished apology. At the communal hearth someone has saved me the last slice of reconciliation omelet flavoured by my own previous commute. I eat, join the midnight audit: scan wrist, release today’s failures into moth-cloud that will file them under reef. My daily balance: 323 minutes still owed to strangers, 11 returned doubled, two dreams promised until 2037.  

Above me drone-moon glitches; ellipsis-shaped constellation copies itself across cloud-bottoms—a punctuation travelling faster than copyright. Somewhere downstream the paper plane I launched at birth is still flying, stringing tomorrow’s possibilities through yesterday’s commas. I close my eyes, taste tamarind sunrise on borrowed hour, answer the only way citizenship allows:  
“Yes, and—”
