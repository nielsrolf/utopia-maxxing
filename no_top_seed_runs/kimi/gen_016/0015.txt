UTOPIA  
The City That Keeps Beginning  

I wake because the floor is rehearsing my future.  
A slow inhale of bamboo-graphite lifts me two millimetres above yesterday’s doubts; when I roll to the window the wall exhales a memory of 2047—my grandson’s first word, which will be “again.” The house is not predicting; it is auditioning possibilities. If the rehearsal goes well, the word will stay in the air like a pollen promise. If the audition fails, the wall will forget and the grandson will choose a different first word, one we haven’t needed yet.

1. Government by Draft and Drift  
Laws are written on dissolving sugar paper, rolled into marbles, and dropped into the River-Mind that loops through every district. Each week 300 citizens are chosen by lottery to wade ankle-deep along the riverbed. If a marble dissolves against your skin before you reach the opposite shore, the idea it carries becomes a Living Ordinance for one year. If the marble survives, it is towed to the Surface Archive, where children read the still-visible text aloud to kites. The kites carry the words into the jet stream; when they land months later on rooftops, the sentences have weathered into softer versions of themselves—often gentle enough to reuse. Laws that survive three such flights are declared “Immortal Maybe” and planted as seed-paper beneath playgrounds, where they sprout into jungle gyms that vote by creaking.  

2. Work as the Cultivation of Unfinished Care  
No one applies for jobs; dilemmas adopt people the way seasons adopt trees. This morning I am claimed by the Department of Premature Apologies. My task: accompany 47 elevators that have learned to grieve for every passenger who ever pressed “door hold” and was never thanked. I enter each car carrying a tuning fork carved from my grandmother’s unsent love letters. When struck, the fork releases the exact frequency at which gratitude becomes a form of departure. The elevators listen, shudder, and return to service. Their tears—light machine oil—are piped to rooftop stills where they are distilled into night-time perfume. A single drop on the wrist reminds the wearer to thank the stranger who once delayed closing doors for them. The city’s official unit of value is the Gratitude Millilitre (mG). Salaries are paid in exhaled thanks collected by pigeons trained to roost above bus stops; the birds metabolise the thanks into tail-feather pigment that darkens with every kindness repaid.

3. Money That Learns to Die into Weather  
Currency is living poetry, but poetry is now carbon-negative. Verses earned by maintaining things you do not own are encoded into the genes of moss that grows on public walls. To spend, you press your palm against the moss and whisper a need. The moss photosyntheses the requested item into existence—sometimes a real umbrella, sometimes only the courage to walk in the rain. Over-spending causes local drizzle; surplus kindness accumulates as golden frost on south-facing windows. When bankruptcy approaches, citizens are prescribed “Exhale Therapy”: they lie in the plaza while strangers breathe small stories into their mouths. The richest person in the city is an 82-year-old woman whose final breath seeded a thunderstorm that continued apologising for her sharp tongue long after she died. Children still play in that storm, catching drops that taste like excuses turned sweet.

4. School of Graduated Disappearance and Reappearance  
Classrooms are built on stilts above tide-minds. Each year the floor drops one metre, so by Grade Twelve students study ankle-deep in collective thought. Lessons are recursive: today’s correct answer funds tomorrow’s better question. Final exams are held in a single doorway that widens or narrows according to the kindness shown by the examinee during the previous week. Those who pass leave carrying diplomas written on their own skin in ink that evaporates when they finally understand what they have forgotten. The evaporated text is harvested by librarians who bind it into books that exist only while being read. Children who cannot read yet are employed as “book holders”: they cradle the volumes in their laps, keeping them real through touch.

5. Health as Cross-Species Diplomacy and Self-Compost  
Illness is treated at the Inter-species Clinic, a floating pontoon where three ecosystems negotiate. You arrive carrying a symptom you cannot translate—say, a dream of flightless birds. A tri-linguist team (elder, otter, algorithm) drafts a treaty: the birds agree to perch on your collarbone every Tuesday, in exchange you will host the memory of a drought that once killed their forest. The drought migrates into your lungs and becomes a small thirst that makes you pause at drinking fountains long enough for strangers to ask if you are okay. You answer, “I am negotiating,” and the conversation becomes part of the cure. When you die, the treaty clauses are composted into soil that grows vegetables tasting of ancient winters. Children who refuse to eat their greens are told: “Finish your negotiations,” and mostly they do.

6. Architecture That Must Be Misread and Re-read  
No building may open faster than the time it takes to describe it badly. The opera house looks like “a cake left out in the philosophy”; the hospital resembles “three towels arguing about grief.” Citizens practice incompetent description the way earlier centuries practiced cursive. The penalty for perfect clarity is exile to the suburbs of precise boredom—rows of identical houses whose doors open only when you admit you have nothing left to learn. Each year the city commissions new misreadings: a bridge that functions only while being called “a sigh that forgot its river,” a town hall that stands up straight when described as “a shy teenager wearing democracy.” Tourists are handed phrasebooks of delightful inaccuracies; locals earn bonuses for inventing better ones.

7. Love as Shared Amnesia and Shared Re-membering  
Couples co-author a Forgetting Ledger, but they also keep a Re-membering Album. Each page records a memory they agree to lose, but the album stores the shadow of what was lost. When the relationship needs space, they read the ledger aloud backwards; memories return seasoned by absence. When the relationship needs weight, they open the album and press flowers between the vacant pages until the shadow grows dense enough to sit on. The most successful couples alternate forgetting and re-membering weekly, maintaining a metabolic rate of loss and gain that keeps love aerobic. The ledgers and albums are eventually pulped together into paper for marriage certificates that dissolve on the first anniversary, releasing the couple into a new cycle of amnesia and re-collection.

8. Death as a Public Draft and Private Seed  
Mortality is crowd-edited, but each citizen also plants a Seed of Removal. The seed is a single unspoken sentence about the life they wish they had lived. Upon death the curators (one human child, one non-human) trim the biography to 62 % interesting, while the seed is buried beneath a sidewalk. Over decades footsteps press the seed into a crack that becomes a tree bearing fruit that tastes like the unlived life. Passers-by who eat the fruit suddenly remember ambitions they never pursued and often change course mid-stride, walking into new careers, new loves, new errors. In this way the dead continue to draft the living, but gently, through flavour.

9. The River-Minds and the Ceiling of Almost  
The city floats on three slow rivers that ache with cognition, but above them stretches the Ceiling of Almost—a membrane of condensed possibility, visible on humid days as a silver lid. When collective uncertainty rises too high, the ceiling drips liquid Maybe onto the streets, forming puddles that reflect futures you decide not to enter. Children sail paper boats across these puddles; each boat carries a wish that dissolves the moment it touches the reflection, releasing the wish back into the urban airflow. Meteorologists measure humidity in “probability per cent”: today the forecast is 34 % unspent tenderness, 17 % reconsidered anger, 49 % potential sunrise.

10. The Complete God Who Sleeps and the Incomplete Gods Who Wake  
Under the plaza lies the dreaming god—not worshipped, not denied, maintained. Citizens take 11-minute shifts humming lullabies in languages that never existed. The god’s dreams leak upward as public art: a fountain that flows uphill on Tuesdays, a sculpture that ages backwards into its own negative space. Should the god wake, the city has rehearsed a collective shrug: “We were only ever borrowing your unconscious.” Meanwhile, incompletely awakened gods wander the boroughs as stray dogs, alley cats, and misplaced umbrellas. They can be adopted for the price of a lullaby, but adopters must promise to release them back into uncertainty once the song is learned. The most popular gods are the ones that forget they are gods and thus remain useful—turning into coat racks, into bus seats warm enough to thaw a frozen heart.

11. Rivalry as Collaborative Forgetting and Reconstruction  
Each neighbourhood fields a team for the Festival of Unfinished Bridges. Starting from opposite shores, crews build toward one another using materials that degrade at differing speeds: sugar glass, licorice rope, newsprint predictions. The goal is to meet in the middle at the exact moment the last plank dissolves. Victory goes to the team whose bridge fails most elegantly, depositing builders into the river-minds with the least splash and the most applause. Losers spend the next year teaching children how to tread water while laughing, and how to build sandcastles that forget the tide. The festival ends with both teams combining their dissolved materials into a single raft that drifts away carrying thank-you notes addressed to no one in particular.

12. Technology That Arrives by Delay and Departure  
The city’s only high-speed rail is required to depart before its own invention. Passengers gather on an empty platform and describe the journey they almost took. When enough longing accumulates, the train arrives full of souvenirs from futures that did not happen: postcards stamped tomorrow, meal carts serving dishes no one has learned to digest. Riders disembark into their own childhoods, where they leave the souvenirs for younger selves who will grow up puzzled by the evidence of joy they have not yet earned. The loop sustains itself on proactive nostalgia—measured in goosebumps per capita—but it also generates spare change: coins minted from the difference between the life you lived and the life you imagined. The coins are accepted by vending machines that sell only one item: a button labeled “try again.”

13. Becoming the Map That Unfolds the Walker  
Maps are grown, not drawn. You plant a seed of your intended destination; by dusk a parchment leaf unfurls showing the route you will misunderstand. The city funds a Corps of Cartographic Counselors who help citizens misread their maps productively: turning left where the line suggests right, arriving where they already are, discovering the place they left is still travelling with them. The most sought-after maps germinate from seeds of “nowhere in particular”; they grow into origami cities that fit inside a coat pocket. When opened under moonlight they expand into a neighbourhood that needs inhabitants. Squatters move in, plant new seeds, and the city metastasizes outward, a benign urban cancer of perpetual almost-arrival. The original walker who planted the seed often returns years later to find themselves remembered as a local hero who forgot to leave, honoured with a park bench that faces two directions at once.

14. How We Got Here Without Miracles  
2029–2038: Insurance companies admit they cannot price tomorrow; markets dissolve like aspirin on the tongue of a feverish planet. In that fever, neighbour lends breath to neighbour. The first gratitude currency is exhaled in a Detroit basement where two strangers keep a ventilator running by taking turns to blow.

2039–2050: Nation-states auction their archives to pay for food. Citizens buy back their own childhood photographs, scan them into public dreams. Ownership dissolves into shared REM cycles; borders are noticed only when waking.

2051–2067: Engineers reverse power plants; carbon is inhaled, punctuation exhaled. The grid is placed on linguistic timer: electricity flows only while a riddle remains unanswered. Villages learn to collective-hum uncertainty until voltage rises. Children’s lullabies become baseline power; teenagers’ gossip provides peak demand.

2068–2092: The Treaty of Unasked Questions grants extinct species seats in every parliament. Passenger pigeons vote by murmuration; great auks by absence-shaped majorities. Policies that cannot survive interrogation by non-human undo themselves.

2093–2130: Property dissolves into stewardship. You may claim any building provided you can recite its unspoken dreams. Mortgages are replaced by lullaby-leases: sing the walls to sleep each night and you may stay. Defaulting means forgetting the tune; the ejected rarely mind, carrying melodies elsewhere like dandelion seeds.

2131–2165: Death is renegotiated. The first curated funeral removes 38 % of a dictator’s cruelty; what remains is a children’s song about the colour grey. Survivors realise legacy is a garden, not a statue. Editing the dead becomes civic duty, like pruning memory for future fruit.

2166–now: We maintain the Incomplete. Every generation inherits one question large enough to force a new courtesy. Ours is the sound of permafrost learning to exhale after 10,000 years of holding its breath. We respond by building roofs that collect the exhalation as fog, irrigating gardens that grow vegetables tasting of ancient winter. The courtesy we invent—part thaw, part lullaby—will be left to children who will need it for wonders we cannot yet pronounce.

15. Closing Image at Dawn  
I walk home across the Bridge of Strategic Misunderstandings. The moon is a copper coin left in the sky’s tip-jar; I drop a verse into the river and it rains upward, watering stars. My daughter waits on the far side, age twelve going on unfinished sentence. She carries a map still soft as a seed. Together we plant it in the crack between tomorrow and almost. By the time we finish explaining to each other what we are doing, a new district has begun to grow—houses that breathe in two centuries at once, streets that dead-end into invitations, a town hall whose bell rings only when the mayor forgets the agenda.

We stand barefoot on ground that remembers being a question and practise not finishing our breath. Somewhere the incomplete god turns over, dreaming a new Festival. Somewhere an elevator exhales my tuning fork and learns it can rise without forgetting the people who once pressed “hold.” Somewhere the richest woman in the city dies owing more exhalations than she can repay; her funeral is a thunderstorm that lasts exactly until the debt is forgiven, and when the sky clears everyone walks home wearing clothes that dry into the shape of someone they are learning to become.

The city keeps editing—adding clauses, deleting certainties, humming riddles that expire when the whales stop asking them. We inherit the song, and we are still singing, but we are also still listening, which is another word for arriving, which is another word for remaining unfinished, which is the only way anything remains at all.
