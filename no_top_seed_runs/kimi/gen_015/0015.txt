UTOPIA
The City That Breathes Back and Forth

I wake to the sound of my kitchen learning to forget the recipe for perfection. The tiles under my bare feet are warm with yesterday’s arguments, tenderized by a family who agreed to disagree. Overnight the walls have absorbed our unfinished sentences and exhaled them as scent: cinnamon and cut grass and the metallic edge of a question neither of us finished asking. I breathe it in. The air tastes like permission.

My name is Asha. I am a Continuation Architect, which means I help buildings and stories and people design their own graceful endings while they are still busy becoming. My contract is renewable every three years, but renewal requires me to present evidence that I have successfully forgotten at least one certainty I previously believed was load-bearing. Last cycle I let go of “stone is solid.” The facade of my apartment block softened overnight into wax, then cooled into a new curve that casts shadows shaped like question marks. We call this “structural doubt.” The city approves.

Our city is called Bibliopolis-Tilt. It was founded in 2041 on the ruins of four infrastructures: an airport, a data centre, a failed theme park, and a river that had been sentenced to concrete. Instead of erasing the past, we recycled its longing. The runways became forests of carbon-sequestering paper-birch whose leaves are printed with declassified love letters. The server racks were melted into living copper that carries not electricity but unfinished stories. The theme park’s roller-coaster was disassembled and rebuilt as a slow tram that climbs hills at the speed of regret, then descends at the speed of forgiveness. The river was acquitted. It moves through us now in braids, sometimes liquid, sometimes data, sometimes vapour that condenses on the inside of windows so we can read the weather like marginalia.

08:00. I cycle to work along the Index Path, a bike lane paved with ceramic tiles that rearrange themselves daily into a new alphabetical order. Today it spells “MERCY” for two kilometres, then switches to “QUINCE.” The tiles warm under sunlight, releasing scents that correspond to their words. MERCY smells like cardamom and old card catalogues. QUINCE smells like thunderstorms and grandmothers who forgot your name but remember your favourite story. Maintenance is performed by primary school children who earn Spelling Tokens they can trade for extra chapters in their favourite books. No one has ever tried to hack the path; it knows when you’re lying, and it will gently reroute you through a memory you had hoped to avoid. Detours taste like humility.

The Continuation Library rises at the city’s centre—a dome of translucent mycelium and recycled glass that photosynthesises during the day and phosphoresces at night. Inside, the air is thick with suspended sentences. They drift like jellyfish, glowing when someone reads them aloud. My job is to match orphaned narratives with hosts who have room inside their bodies for unfinished meaning. This morning I sit with Nyx, a librarian whose cloak is stitched from the indices of out-of-print encyclopaedias. She smells like index tabs and thunder.

Nyx hands me a prism the size of a heart. Inside flickers a story that arrived overnight: a glacier’s autobiography, dictated in morse code through crevasses that no longer exist. The glacier wants to become a lullaby, but it worries that melting will be misread as surrender. My task is to find a singer who is ready to learn theDifference between disappearing and letting go.

12:00. Lunch in the Root Café, sunk beneath the library like a seed. The menu changes according to what stories are being metabolised overhead. Today the air is thick with unspoken apologies, so the special is forgiveness soup: bone broth cultured on pages of retracted newspaper apologies, garnished with parsley grown in soil mixed from shredded non-disclosure agreements. We eat quietly. The soup tastes like copper and the moment you realise you were wrong. A woman at the next table sobs into her bowl. The salt seasons the broth; the chef records the flavour for next week’s batch. Nothing is wasted, especially grief, especially salt.

13:30. I descend to the Vault of Reversible Futures, a climate-controlled cavern where we store narratives deemed too dangerous to release outright: manifestos that could incite tenderness, patents for time machines powered only by regret, love letters that could destabilise whole marriages by proving that desire is recyclable. Each story is sealed inside a prism of living glass kept at 4 °C and 100 % humidity—conditions that keep possibility dormant but viable. They hum like hibernating bees. Sometimes one cracks open just enough for a single photon of maybe to escape. We call these “yearnings.” They ricochet through the city, landing on eyelids, earlobes, bicycle bells. Tomorrow someone will wake with a new question and no idea who put it there.

Today I add the glacier’s lullaby. I label the prism “Not-Yet-Sleep.” The shelf shivers, recognising a cousin. Stories, like fungi, form networks underground. I whisper the word “melt” into the dark. Somewhere overhead, a child hums a tune that sounds like ancient ice.

15:00. My daughter Juno arrives after school. She attends the Maybe Academy, where subjects are taught in conditional tense: We Might Learn Physics; Perhaps We Will Practise Disappearance; An Introduction to Not-Yet. Today’s lesson was “How to Befriend an Unfinished Poem.” Each student received a stanza that ends mid-word. They must spend a week living with the fragment—read it to strangers, plant it in soil, sleep with it under the pillow—then decide whether to complete, burn, or release it. Juno’s poem ends “the river is not a metaphor for.” She has drawn a blank map of the city and poured the words into the harbour. Tomorrow she will sail out to see where they drift. If they wash ashore, we’ll know the poem chose us. If not, another city will inherit the sentence. No one fails; everyone continues.

We walk home along the Tram of Forgetting, a roller-coaster rebuilt at the speed of regret. It climbs slowly enough that you can watch your reflection in the window soften, then descends fast enough that you arrive having misplaced the reason you boarded. Juno and I get off at the stop called “Almost.” We remember we were sad, but not why.

18:00. Dinner. Our kitchen table is a printing press turned inside out. Each night we ink the surface with leftover emotions—today’s ink is distilled from Gabe’s laughter (he visited the library today to donate his high-score memory) and Nyx’s encyclopaedia dust. We roll out pasta sheets, imprinting them with fragments of the day. Juno arranges her noodles into the shape of an unfinished bridge. We eat in silence, tasting each other’s untranslatable hours. The pasta glows faintly, digestible narrative. Overnight our bodies will metabolise the stories, exhale them as dreams into the bedroom membranes. The cycle continues, but it spirals rather than circles. Each loop forgets something and adds something. We wake lighter, heavier, different.

21:00. Evening walk. The city dims by sharing light: windows turn translucent, casting pooled radiance onto the streets. Pedestrians walk inside borrowed glow, their shadows overlapping like marginalia. We pass the Maybe Orchard, newly planted with fire-spores from last year’s wildfire. Tiny shoots push through soil, leaves shaped like question marks. Juno whispers to them in second-person future: “You will decide who you become, but we will hold the space.” A soft bioluminescence answers—stories acknowledging listeners. We walk on.

We stop at the Honeymoon Arch, built from bound Forgetting Ledgers. Couples walk through it twice: once to deposit a memory they agree to lose, once to retrieve it transformed. The arch widens for every couple who chooses to begin again. It is beginning to sag under the weight of recycled tenderness. Engineers debate whether to add more ledgers or to let it collapse into a heap of stories that no longer need to be remembered. They decide to wait. The arch is learning.

22:30. Bedtime. The bedroom walls have rearranged themselves into a single sentence: “The river is called Letting Go.” I remember now. It runs beneath the library, through the Vault of Reversible Futures, under the Index Path, past the Maybe Orchard, into the harbour where Juno’s poem drifts. We are all tributaries. I inhale the sentence, exhale a dream. The building records it, warm as bread, cold as meltwater.

00:00. Midnight. Somewhere a prism cracks open—just a hairline fracture, enough for a single photon of possibility to escape. It ricochets off the copper lattice, through the orchard, along the bike path, into our bedroom. It lands on my eyelid, weightless. Tomorrow I will wake with a new question. Tomorrow someone will bring an unfinished story and ask the library to hold it. Tomorrow the path will spell a new word, the soup will taste of a different grief, the tram will forget a new sorrow. We will not reach perfection; we will reach continuation. That is enough.

The city keeps breathing. Buildings inhale our arguments and exhale architecture shaped like compromise. The river inhales our industrial guilt and exhales fog that tastes like iron and forgiveness. The library inhales orphaned stories and exhales readers who no longer need to know how things end. We inhale one another’s incomplete sentences and exhale poems that end mid-

And we are still becoming.
