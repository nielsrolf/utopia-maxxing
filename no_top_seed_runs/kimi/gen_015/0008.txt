UTOPIA  
The City That Breathes While Unbuilding  

I wake because the ceiling is inhaling my future.  
Not all of it—only the paragraphs I will write after I am seventy-three, the ones that begin “I wish I had told you…”  
The mycelial plaster above my bed drinks those sentences, turns them into spores the colour of sunrise over rust, and exhales them through the cracked skylight.  
By the time they land on the river three storeys below, they have become tiny paper boats carrying footnotes to a treaty that has not yet been negotiated.  
I make a mental note to thank the house for editing me before I can do any damage.  

The room is exactly the size of one regret.  
That is the municipal standard: bedrooms may swell or contract overnight, but they must never exceed the cubic metres of a single workable remorse.  
If you wake up claustrophobic, the wall-clock translates the feeling into a lullaby in an extinct whale dialect and plays it back at the frequency that makes concrete soften.  
By the time the melody ends, the walls have relaxed, and so have you.  
Architects call the process “gentle blackmail.”  
Tenants call it rent.  

I dress in the day’s forecast.  
The communal loom in the stairwell is already humming—an argument between last night’s thunderstorm and the mayor’s unfinished apology for scheduling the eclipse during rush hour.  
The fabric emerges dove-grey, still crackling.  
As I pull the shirt over my head, the thunderstorm wins: a seam of lightning runs down my spine, stitching me to every person who will feel the same jolt before sunset.  
We are a temporary constellation, flickering in and out of each other’s chests.  

Breakfast is served on the roof that is also a question mark.  
The wheat is taller than I am, ripening in waves that spell the word “unless” across the skyline.  
Each grain contains a drop of yesterday’s grief, distilled by chlorophyll into something almost sweet.  
I harvest one handful, leave the rest to finish the sentence.  
The porridge cooks over a burner fed by the building’s compost of broken promises.  
While it simmers, the pot asks the daily triad:  
Who needs your silence more than your voice?  
What boundary inside you has begun to rust?  
Which river remembers you by a name you haven’t yet learned?  
I answer into the steam; the steam carries the words back down the ventilation shaft into the bedrooms of neighbours who are still deciding what kind of day to invent.  
By the time I swallow, three strangers have inhaled my replies and dreamt them into other shapes.  
The cycle is closed, the bowl edible, the day officially co-authored.  

I descend via the stem-elevator which, satisfied, folds itself into a bench so the roof can be a civic balcony for whoever arrives next.  
The lobby is ankle-deep in yesterday’s footprints preserved as thin ice.  
We walk across our collective night: the stumble of someone learning a prosthetic knee, the ballet-step of a child who discovered gravity could be negotiated, the patient drag of the insomniac pacing out the perimeter of an argument.  
At 08:00 the ice melts, is filtered, and ferried to the metro where it will become the cooling mist that keeps commuters human during rush hour.  

The metro entrance is a mouth in the wall that used to sell tickets.  
Instead of fare, the turnstile requests one risk you are willing to take publicly.  
I press into the scanner the memory of the first poem I never showed anyone.  
The gate sighs open, lighter by one secrecy.  
Somewhere down the line a stranger will find that poem printed on the back of their ticket, folded into a paper boat, or fluttering from the cooling vent.  
They may read it, crumple it, add a stanza, return it to the river of unkept confessions that flows under the tracks.  
Completion is not the point; circulation is.  

Trains breathe.  
Five cars inhale while five exhale, a slow accordion of strangers trading carbon and courage.  
Seats are upholstered with kelp-leather that records skin temperature.  
If enough passengers feel dread, the seats bloom small purses of sage and burnt citrus.  
If collective anticipation spikes, the walls sweat peppermint.  
We ride inside the city’s emotional weather report, destination written in condensation:  
Today we are headed toward “maybe, if the wind agrees.”  

I disembark at the Department of Gentle Acceleration and Delayed Arrival.  
My office is on the fourteenth floor of a cliff that is also a tower.  
Swifts nest in the façade; when they migrate, pieces of the building migrate with them—small wedges of limestone that detach politely, carried on bellies halfway to Senegal, released as mid-air stepping-stones for other species learning long routes.  
The tower loses mass every autumn, regrows every spring through mineral accretion in the basement ponds.  
Net carbon ledger: negative.  
Net dignity ledger: still being copy-edited by barn swallows.  

I am a Listener, Level III.  
My task is to sit in the city’s ear and annotate the pauses.  
Each morning the master timeline unfurls across my desk: a three-metre scroll of bacterial cellulose listing every large infrastructure project scheduled for the next thirty years.  
Beside it sits a second scroll—blank, hungry.  
With a stylus made of glacier silt I transfer projects from the first scroll to the second only when I can hear them breathing.  
If a highway insists on being built faster than the soil can forgive, I leave it un-copied.  
The scrolls dry overnight; in the morning the unchosen projects have become translucent and are released into the atrium where citizens may adopt them as household ghosts—reminders of futures we declined to haunt.  

At 11:00 the city holds its daily Rehearsal of Loss.  
Noon light dims; citizens gather on rooftops, in kayaks, on floating desks.  
We practise losing things we have not yet lost: a sibling, the concept of ice, the ability to walk backwards without suspicion.  
A conductor stands on the reversible archipelago—today shaped like an ellipsis—raising a baton of dried kelp.  
On the downbeat we exhale in unison, releasing 1,000 kilos of potential grief.  
The carbon is harvested by kites stitched from old fishing nets; they rise, tethered to children who will inherit the atmosphere.  
When the kites land next week, the sorrow will have fermented into glittering bicarbonate.  
Artisans will carve it into beads for coming-of-age necklaces: proof that we can survive futures we have not yet entered.  

Lunch is served on biodegradable mirrors in the Parliament of Unfinished Seconds.  
The speciality is Regret Salad: leaves coated with dressing that tastes like whatever you apologised for most recently.  
We eat each other’s mistakes.  
A junior minister chews my failure to finish the winter report; I taste her apology for signing a flood-wall contract that would have blocked the swifts.  
Flavours mingle—bitterness first, then iron, then something faintly like wet pavement after fireworks.  
Nutrition labels list micronutrients of reconciliation: 12 % of daily need for mutual leniency.  
We leave the table dizzy with shared culpability, lighter by one secret each.  

After lunch I walk to the Interstitial Library, a building that exists only when enough people doubt it.  
It materialised last Tuesday after a thunderstorm of unspoken apologies saturated the air.  
The façade is brick today, tomorrow it may be sheet music or turmeric.  
The only constant is the revolving door made from a single whale vertebra; it turns only if you whisper a sentence you are not yet brave enough to finish.  
Inside, shelves rise like bronchioles.  
Books inhale when someone needs their knowledge, deflate to pulp when the lesson is spent.  
There are no catalogues; instead a mist of micro-organised questions drifts overhead.  
I open my mouth, taste the query that tastes like me, and follow its scent to a balcony where a single book sighs open.  
Pages are translucent beetle wings; words crawl across, rearranging as I read.  
I copy the shifting text onto mirror-film that will carry the story back to the streets.  
By dusk the mirror will be scratched thin by passers-by who each steal a syllable, until the sentence belongs to no one and therefore to everyone.  

My daughter meets me at 17:00 outside the Clinic of Tidal Forgiveness.  
She is ten and apprenticed to unfinished sentences.  
Today she earned a badge by convincing the city’s central turbine to stall for 3.2 seconds—just long enough for a deaf boy to feel the vibration of his own voice.  
She trades the badge for a bedtime story.  
I tell her about the Library that forgets itself each night, how it relies on readers to dream it back into existence.  
She asks if buildings can be lonely.  
“Only when we stop mid-sentence,” I say.  
She falls asleep clutching a comma I carved from driftwood—insurance against hasty endings.  

Dinner is cooked on stoves fired by yesterday’s arguments.  
We sear vegetables over the flame of a debate about whether birds are flowers that learned impatience.  
Smoke carries our words into the sky where cumulus rearrange them into banners readable only from the suburbs of regret.  
After eating we walk to the estuary where the city practices endings.  
Tonight an old pier is scheduled to unbuild itself.  
We gather ankle-deep in mud, each holding a tool of forgetting: a sponge, a feather, a pocketful of bread.  
On the hour the structure loosens nails like sighs.  
We offer our implements; strangers scrub beams, chew crumbs and spit papier-mâché into cracks.  
Piece by piece the pier returns to driftwood, floating off with the tide.  
Within an hour nothing remains but a rectangle of moonlight on water that remembers being solid.  
We name the absence “perhaps,” feel it throb like a freed artery.  

Back home the ceiling has resolved into neither roof nor sky but a low cloud licensed to linger indoors.  
It rains gently on the quilt, enough to keep the fabric humble.  
I lie down, offer the pillow today’s final heartbeat.  
It records the rhythm, promises to remix it into tomorrow’s alarm.  

Before sleep I check the window.  
Outside, the city is a scatter of half-lit buildings breathing in slow sync, some expanding, some courteously collapsing, all practicing the art of remaining unfinished enough to welcome the next becoming.  
Nothing gleams with the hardness of forever; everything reflects the apricot uncertainty of dawn.  

I whisper a law into the dark:  
Let no surface harden faster than a heart can soften.  
The cloud above me flashes once—lightning or approval, impossible to tell.  
Sleep arrives like a train that inhales while another exhales, carrying me gently into the jurisdiction of maybe.  

Tomorrow the porridge will ask new questions, the metro will collect fresh obligations, the pier will begin to reassemble somewhere else because a group of teenagers needs to learn how to walk on water before they learn how to vote.  
We will continue unbuilding the city at precisely the speed we learn to forgive its weight.  

This is how we live: in a place that stays tender by never arriving, that keeps its maps lightly drawn in dissolving ink, that measures wealth in the pause between inhale and exhale.  
We are cartographers of postponement, librarians of reversible abundance, gardeners of sentences that refuse to end.  
Our utopia is not a destination but a grammar: the practice of staying soft enough to edit each other’s stories without tearing the page, of holding completion lightly enough to let the future revise us first.
