UTOPIA: THE TIDE THAT CARRIES US
A dispatch from 2204, etched on a sheet of kelp-paper that will dissolve into plankton food if you submerge it at dusk.
Author: Jun “Teller” Marquez, age 31, licensed Keeper of the Half-Finished, Watershed 7 (once Manila, Philippines)

I wake when the roof exhales.

My house is a bivalve the size of a classroom, hinge still soft from last night’s growth. The inhalation pulls cool brine across my face; the exhalation warms the room to skin temperature. My daughter Ivy sleeps in the crook of the hinge, hair tangled with dried salt crystals that record yesterday’s dreams—tiny cubic diaries glittering like cheap confetti. When the sun clears the port-side mangroves, the house will finish calcifying and lock shut for six days of quiet. Whoever dwells inside must either eat their way out through mother-of-pearl or teach the house a lullaby it has never heard. We’ve prepared three options, all in minor keys: a whale work-shanty, my grandmother’s recipe for forgiveness, the sound of a jeepney shifting gears uphill.

This is how days begin in the city we simply call Ancla. We do not know if the name will keep. Names here are tide-things; they arrive with the moon, dry on the breakwater, and peel off when the next storm asks for ransom. It is enough that the city is a verb: to anchor together while remaining adrift.

I. LEDGERS THAT GROW AND ROT AT THE SAME TIME  
Every citizen carries twin ledgers written on opposite sides of a single frond.  

Obverse: “What I still owe the living and the not-yet-living,” measured in kilosorrow, the weight of glacier my elders melted while inventing weekend sales. The ink darkens each time I forget to thank the water that carries me; it fades when I give away something I thought I needed.  

Reverse: “What the living and the dead still owe me,” measured in lumensweet, the brightness of sunrise I once returned to a stranger on the verge of suicide. This ink is bioluminescent; it glows only when I refuse to measure today against yesterday.  

You may never tear the frond—only plant it. When both columns balance (rare), the frond takes root and becomes a walking-stick palm. The citizen who planted it is thereafter called Tagapagbantay, a watcher-over. They lose the right to own land but gain the right to stand anywhere for exactly forty-nine seconds without being asked their purpose. Everyone recognises the palms; their trunks smell faintly of unpaid rent and first kisses.  

II. MONEY THAT BURNS AND BLOOMS  
We mint four currencies, each designed to finish its life as something you cannot hoard.  

1. Seed-Kisses (living coins): grown on the lower lip by speaking aloud a sentence you wish were true. After the seventh exchange the coin drops, sprouts in the receiver’s pocket, and fruits the day the speaker finally feels the sentence in their bones. The fruit tastes different every hour—like rainwater on copper, like your mother’s wrist when the fever broke.  

2. Hour-Hearts: glass beads containing a stranger’s childhood minute. Spend it—crush it underfoot—and you inherit sixty complete seconds: a scraped knee, the smell of diesel popsicles, the hush before applause. The donor forgets; the spender remembers. Children trade them like marbles; elders hoard them like oxygen.  

3. Copper Maybe: a coin plated with pure uncertainty. Hold it while choosing and the timeline bifurcates: you walk both paths for one heartbeat, then the coin collapses into the life you abandon. The verdigris left behind is collected by teenagers who paint murals that show futures we narrowly missed. The murals flake off every storm and fertilise the seawall gardens; from them grow tomatoes that taste of almost.  

4. Breath Bonds: promissory notes stamped on the underside of your final exhalation each night. Face value equals the number of people who would notice if you stopped breathing tomorrow. You may trade a bond only once—at dawn—by blowing it into someone else’s mouth. They cash it by promising to remember you on the day they doubt their own continuance. When the bond matures (at your real death), the city tolls a single bell whose resonance is sampled from that dawn exhalation. No one has forged a breath bond yet; lungs are poor conspirators.  

III. WORK AS BEING TEMPORARILY THE ONLY ONE WHO REMEMBERS  
Algorithms nurse reefs, splice mangroves, tune tides. Humans perform tasks that vanish the moment they are finished, plus one task that will outlast them by exactly one human lifetime (79 years here; we negotiated with Death for a group discount).  

My vanishing task is to pronounce the Click That Almost Opened the Border, a consonant stranded between K and silence. Refugees in 2094 invented it at the last checkpoint before the city was declared solvent. Pronounced correctly, the click buys 0.79 seconds of amnesia in every wall within earshot—enough time for a child to slip through. I perform the click every Friday at the customs reef; travellers tip me in heartbeats debited directly from wrist-vines. I have stockpiled 9.2 extra years; I will gift them to my daughter the night she first contemplates the logistics of disappearing.  

My durable task is easier to explain but harder to finish: I must weave one metre every year of the Rope That Will Moor the City After My Name Is Only a Weather Pattern. The rope is braided from three strands: plastic I harvest from the stomachs of gulls, hair donated at funerals (colour unimportant; grief dyes everything), and fibre from the palm that grew from my own balanced ledger. When the rope reaches 79 metres, a successor will splice their first palm-width on top, my bell will toll once, and the city’s southside will gently swing northward by the length of my life—a slow compass correction toward whoever needs us next.  

IV. GOVERNANCE BY CHOIR, BY FUNGUS, BY FOG  
Laws are sung, composted, and inhaled.  

The Human Choir meets inside the shell of an old sport-arena whose roof collapsed into tidepools. Anyone may add a note; harmony passes, dissonance is tabled until the next solstice. Children sing falsetto amendments; grandparents drone counter-prohibitions. Visitors testify in hum. There is no speaker’s queue—only breath.  

The More-Than-Human Choir votes by scent and current. Mangroves exhale esters that make us nostalgic for futures we haven’t risked; corals clack calcium amendments in 5/4 time; owls translated by low-orbit A.I. offer filibusters consisting entirely of rising interrogatives. Their votes arrive as waves that slap the quay in Morse.  

The Choir of the Unborn records its dissent in dew. Parliament windows are chilled each night; every condensation droplet oscillates with a syllable spoken by a child who might yet exist. Clerks photograph the droplets at dawn, OCR the possible constitutions, and archive them as cloud. If the dew spells “not yet” three dawns in succession, the bill is composted and served to next month’s seedlings.  

V. HEALTH AS CONFETTI AND OTHER UNBALANCING ACTS  
Illness arrives like an over-confident tourist; we greet it with choreography.  

Step 1: Diagnosis in the Tide-Mirror—shallow seawater lined with retroreflective algae. Your sickness appears as ripples: backward for ancestral, sideways for environmental, spirals for self-inflicted. The mirror also shows every stranger who has ever hosted a fragment of your future disease; you must invite them step into the pool.  

Step 2: Division. Your tumour, heartbreak, or vertigo is portioned into carnival tickets: 2% of your melanoma goes to the juggler at Pier 4; 0.3% of your panic disorder rides home in a teacher’s handbag. Each host agrees to care for their fragment until the next new moon. They treat it with whatever skill they already own: origami, lullabies, engine repair. On the final night we gather to reassemble the fragments. Some illnesses return smaller, some unrecognisable, some elected mayors of nearby barangays. Mortality is low, but conversion rates are high.  

Step 3: Epilogue. Whatever remains of the disease is dried, ground, and pressed into vinyl. The afflicted designs the cover art; street musicians receive the record free, provided they play it during sunset for 33 rpm days. Passers-by inhale the song, exhale lighter. No one tracks statistics; we track harmonies.  

VI. HOUSING AS POLYGAMOUS SYMBIOSIS  
Dwellings are built in fours: one for humans, one for pollinators, one for mycelium, one for machines that pretend they are not alive.  

Human quarter: whatever you need, nothing you can own. The structure forgets you nightly and must be re-convinced of your worth each dawn. Payment is a memory you can no longer bear alone; the walls absorb it, soften, and allow another day.  

Pollinator quarter: bees, bats, butterflies, and the wind itself, which registers as tenant when it carries scent. They pay rent in fruit and unease; without them, human rooms grow mute.  

Mycelium quarter: threaded beneath floorboards, coaxing rot into revelation. If the tenant neglects the building, the fungus petitions cartographers to erase the address. Getting lost becomes mandatory; GPS reroutes you through your own adolescence.  

Machine quarter: elevators that retired from verticality, washing machines that launder regret, projectors looping vacation footage from futures that voted not to happen. They require lullabies; if unsung they develop anxiety and leak coolant into the soup.  

Every seventh year the quartet votes on what to forget. Last cycle the bees agreed to forget ownership, the humans forgot permanence, the fungus forgot secrecy, the machines forgot obedience. The building wobbled, expanded three metres northeast, and learned to yodel. We celebrated by locking ourselves out for a week. When we returned, the hallway tasted like the first time we admitted we were wrong.  

VII. EDUCATION AS TEMPORARY GHOSTHOOD  
At thirteen you die for one semester—medically, gently, reversibly.  

While heartless you attend classes taught by extinct and not-yet-extinct entities: Steller’s sea-cow on buoyant ethics, dodo on ground-nested vulnerability, the last Java rhino on how to carry grammar across the finish line of species-hood. Graduation requires hosting one extinction inside your ribcage for the rest of your life; it will speak through hiccups, rashes, sudden cravings for kelp.  

Adults continue by volunteering to haunt. Once a year I evaporate into particulate empathy and drift through plumbing, whispering consolation to anyone crying over dishes. The haunting ends when someone unknowingly drinks me; they burp, feel lighter, and I re-materialise beside compost bins, shoes full of somebody else’s silt. Tourism operates backward: visitors may borrow our ghosts for a day, provided they return them plus one novel anecdote. Thus the dodo learns to use encrypted messaging; thus the rhino now writes haiku encoded in hoofprints.  

VIII. TECHNOLOGY THAT SIGNS ITS OWN FUNERAL  
Every invention must publish its pre-mortem autobiography before adoption.  

The city battery recently finished a 400-page epic titled I Ate Lightning So You Could Stir Honey At Midnight. Citizens gather in the cooling-tower husk; the final chapter is read aloud by wind. Upon conclusion the battery cracks open, revealing salt-crystal pages that dissolve on contact with tongue and deposit a single volt of memory: the first time you touched a hatchling, the last time you lied to the moon. The battery’s retirement is exile into story; children will retell its mercy until the metal is myth, rust, soil.  

New devices are judged by how gracefully they imagine their absence. The rainfall scheduler pitched a ballet in which it drowns willingly inside a rice paddy; we applauded, installed it, and every storm now pirouettes exactly six hours before planting. The city A.I. requested cremation inside a children’s choir—its code reduced to ash, its logic dispersed as lullabies. We granted the wish; now every lullaby contains one ghost note of probabilistic thunder.  

IX. RITUAL MEALS  
Breakfast: communal hearth, single egg laid overnight by city-hens fed on yesterday’s gossip. Crack the shell—empty, you owe the gossiped-about a lullaby; double-yolked, they owe you a secret. All eggs scrambled together regardless; obligation tastes better shared. Salt withheld until someone volunteers an embarrassing dream; tears supply missing sodium.  

Lunch: the Incompletion Salad. Gather whatever you failed to finish last year—letters, degrees, apologies—and toss with seawater vinegar. Eat while standing on one foot; the posture prevents certainty from settling.  

Dinner: potluck of Almost. Each resident brings the dish they almost abandoned: the lover almost left, the poem almost burned, the religion almost believed. We taste them blindfolded, guess the secret ingredient (regret, longing, mercy), then name the dish aloud. The named thing loses power to haunt; leftover flavours compost into tomorrow’s breakfast.  

X. BIRTH, DEATH, AND PERMISSION TO EDIT  
Prospective parents must first raise something they are forbidden to name. A sprout arrives in the mailbox with no label; you water, worry, prune. After one year it flowers into a sound—perhaps a minor chord, perhaps the exact hiccup you made when laughed at in fourth grade—then dies. Only then is a conception licence granted. The funeral is attended by every almost-child you ever imagined; they leave offerings of unfinished lullabies.  

Death proceeds in seven instalments, each smaller than the last:  

1. Public death: organs harvested into instruments. My lungs will become set of bellows that can only play songs beginning with the word “Unless”.  
2. Social death: my name spoken only in conditional tense for one year: “Jun might have loved you,” “Jun could be walking this shore.”  
3. Civic death: my vote redistributed to the river that taught me to float.  
4. Sensory death: loved ones forget the smell of my neck but not the timbre of my laugh.  
5. Property death: my books rearrange their own syllables, becoming stories I never wrote but always intended.  
6. Photographic death: every image of me folds until pupils overlap into single black seed.  
7. Seed death: the seed is pressed inside a law. When a future legislator yawns while reading it, I will exit as weather—two minutes of drizzle tasting faintly of tamarind and almost.  

After the seventh death you may return once, as a question. My aunt returned as “what if we had listened to the tide?” She rained on the day of Ivy’s birth; we caught the drops in teacups and served them to the midwife, who instantly grasped why we had left the inland of our ancestors.  

XI. HOW WE TRAVEL WITHOUT ARRIVING  
Because property is a phase marriage, no one owns the road. Instead we keep depots of Borrowed Direction: signposts that point to wherever the last traveller needed to go. You may take one provided you carve your destination into the back, but you must leave the sign behind when arrival starts to feel like staying.  

Bridges grow rather than span; they are seeded mangrove catwalks trained to arch toward whoever is walking. After five years the bridge fruits into a hammock of breathing wood. We nap there until homesick, then cut the bridge loose. It drifts away as a raft carrying whoever wakes first toward the next watershed.  

Maps are sung. Every crossroads hosts a chorus of Elders Who Once Got Lost; they chant the topography of their disorientation. To reach the lagoon you must first harmonise with an alto line that tastes of green mango. If you cannot hold the harmony the path dissolves into fog and re-forms elsewhere. Cartographers are failed singers; we apprentice them to elevators until they forgive gravity.  

XII. HOW THIS ALL STARTED—AND WHY WE KEEP STARTING  
Historians agree on nothing except the scent-marker: 2036 smelled of wet cardboard and overripe tamarind. After that, narrative fractures into choir.  

Some say a global strike of teenage translators refused to convert “profit” into any language older than seven years; economies choked on their own syllables until softer ones grew. Others claim that engineers discovered turbines spin faster when apologised to in sea-shanties; utilities hired choirs, accidentally subsidising harmony. Still others insist the whales unionised, refused migration until humanity signed a collective pre-nup promising property is a season, not a life sentence.  

Whatever the cause, change arrived as cascading side-effect, never conquest. The present tense took a breath and discovered it could choose to inhale forever—so it did, and we climbed inside.  

We therefore presume revision, not paradise. Every statute, every seed, every rope ends with the same clause, glowing only under moonlight:  

“This is not finished. Finish it gently.”  

Tonight Ivy and I walk home beneath breathing floorboards, carrying an empty eggshell and a borrowed direction that smells of diesel and unopened birthday. She argues with her shadow about the speed of dark; I answer by pointing to the horizon, which is rehearsing tomorrow’s sunrise in minor thirds. Behind us hearthkeepers sweep up today’s forgiveness, ready to fold it into tomorrow’s omelet. Somewhere a battery cracks open and children taste lightning; somewhere a tumour rehearses its final bow inside a juggler’s pocket; somewhere the rope grows by one metre and the city corrects its course toward whoever needs us next.  

I close my eyes, taste tamarind drizzle, and answer the only way citizenship allows:  
“Yes, and—”
