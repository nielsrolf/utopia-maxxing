UTOPIA C  
The Republic of Perpetual Maybe  
field log, equinox 2161, written on wind-sensitive paper that forgets itself at sunrise

I wake because the roof has decided to smell like rain.  The droplets arrive as tiny glass beads, each one printed with last night’s dreams harvested from the neighbourhood sleep-loops.  When the beads pop on my cheeks I catch fragments: a child learning to whistle backwards, an old woman arguing with a seabird about tides, a spider who claims to have memo­rised the entire sky.  By the time I sit up the roof has already folded itself into a question mark, curious where I will go next.

The dwelling is ours, but only in the weak sense that mycelium “owns” a forest.  It is built of maybe-stone laced with strawberry-runner fibres and the laughter of extinct clowns—carbon captured from archive footage, decrypted, then re-encoded as tensile strength.  If we forget to laugh for forty nights the walls will slump back into soil and volunteer as riverbank.  So far we have failed at sadness for six years straight, a record that earned us a visit from the travelling choir who sing thank-you notes to houses that refuse to die.

Below my feet the floor reports the daily nutrient balance: I owe 0.3 g of phosphorus to the oat-patch upstairs, 2.7 millismiles to the stranger who watched me dance in the dark through the window (she enjoyed the way my elbows predicted rain).  Nothing is bought, only *continued*.  Every kilowatt I used yesterday already replanted itself as wind-hungry dandelion seeds outside the city.  The ledger does not ask “who pays?” but “who keeps the story awake?”

1. The Currency of Unfinished Symphonies  
We have three overlapping tenders:

- Glint – the attention you gift and immediately lose, mirrored back as brighter air.  
- Friction – the right to be briefly difficult, earned by choosing chores bots could finish faster.  
- Maybe – a token minted whenever you leave an outcome unsettled long enough for someone else to dream into it.  

Maybe is the most valuable.  You acquire it by releasing control at the precise moment when certainty feels most delicious: pausing a bridge mid-span, publishing a poem without the final stanza, planting a tree upside-down to see if the sky has roots.  Spend it and the world audibly inhales.  Hoard it and your hand begins to fade—first the thumbprint, then the lifeline—until you agree to let go.  Bureau of Maybe agents (selected by yawn-lottery) drift through the plazas measuring pulse rates of possibility.  Their badges read: *I don’t know yet, may I help you hesitate?*

2. Work: The Department of Glorious Bottlenecks  
All base labour is automatic.  Citizens therefore queue for the right to impede perfection.  Tuesday I served on the Garden Traffic Cone brigade, placing orange ceramic mushrooms in random intersections so delivery eels had to practice sudden pirouettes.  The resulting delay poured 400 millifrictions into my account, enough to commission a local poet to write me a personal forecast: “Expect scattered epiphanies before dusk, followed by late-night clarity clearing to awe.”  The cones dissolved at sunset, feeding the pavement their memories of near-collisions.  Children licked the spots where they had stood; the taste is said to be a cross between cinnamon and last-second regret.

Every citizen keeps a *difficulty calendar*—fifteen hours a week of chosen inconvenience: folding 1,000 paper boats then unfolding them because the river changed its mind, or transcribing the encyclopaedia in a language that will be invented next year.  The goal is to stay amateur at something—amateurs remain porous.  Retirees are the most skilled; they can stretch a simple hesitation into a month-long art piece that ends with the entire city unsure which shoe goes on which foot.

3. Governance: The Parliament of Ripening  
Decisions are taken by sortitioned circles of 150 humans plus 15 non-human delegates trained on the dreams of soils, children, and the yet-unconceived.  They meet inside fruit.  Each month a different crop swells to cathedral size— fig, mango, breadfruit—its skin translucent like warm amber.  Inside, the air is thick with ethylene and debate.  Speakers must pollinate their arguments: ideas literally clothe themselves in pollen, drift to listeners, and germinate in the hairlines behind ears.  By the time the fruit drops, policy has ripened into seeds.  Citizens vote by eating the ones they trust; the rest are composted into next year’s agenda.  Thus every law spends part of its life as taste.  You can always spot a former legislator: they unconsciously lick their lips when discussing taxation.

Emotional weather is monitored by the Fountain of Almost, a circular pool that measures the city’s collective *salmon-pink of recklessness* versus *indigo of caution*.  If the water stays translucent for three days any rule can be suspended, a window used mostly for mass kite-flying indoors and for apologising to insects.

4. Health: The Illness Choir  
When my blood detects anomaly I am not hospitalised; I am *cast*.  Within minutes the Choir assembles: one retired comedian whose own tumour became a stand-up routine, a child studying empathy through microscopes, a dog trained to detect despair in exhaled ketones, and an off-duty sunset.  Treatment is mostly autonomous—phages, drones, song-spells—but the humans provide dramatic irony.  The comedian insists on naming my rogue cell “Kevin,” turning malignancy into an unpopular coworker.  The child charges the choir one laugh per day; if Kevin shrinks, the dog gets to vote on the closing joke.  Recovery is livestreamed as an episodic opera.  Viewers earn glint for witnessing, but the composer must leave the final chord unfinished so the audience leaves still humming, cells uncertain where the boundary between me and them lies.

5. Education: The School of Unlearning  
At fourteen every citizen is enrolled in Ignorance Expansion.  Curriculum: forget three certainties, fail at two ambitions, and plant one question so large it casts shade for the rest of your life.  Final exams are held inside a Möbius strip; you pass when you can no longer find the front.  Graduates step out barefoot carrying nothing but a badge that reads *Ask me what I don’t know*.  Employers (to the extent that word survives) must pay extra for applicants who still bear the badge unblemished by answers.

6. Romance: The Slow-Motion Crush  
Love is declared by planting a bulb in the public arbour that will flower only if both partners regularly speak contradictory truths to it.  The blossom is colour-shy; stare too hard and it retreats into green.  Lovers therefore practice peripheral affection—talking to each other while watching clouds, letting devotion ferment in the corner of the eye.  Breakups are celebrated: at dusk the bulb splits into two vines, each carrying half the pigment away.  Where they climb, new benches appear so passers-by can rest inside the chromatic absence, feeling what used to be someone else’s future.

7. Death and the Second Draft  
Mortality is elective but *finishing* is mandatory.  Once a century every citizen must release a work that can never be edited again—an orchard, a theorem, a child.  Upon publication the author is restored from backup minus copyright on their own past.  The new self meets the old creations as strangers, obliged to co-exist without authority.  Judges are archaeologists from another species we have not yet met; they will grade us on curiosity, not legacy.  Those who choose true death board the Ferry of Unsaid Sentences.  Corpses are transformed into weather: librarians become fog that prints miniature novellas on car windows, dancers becomeTrade winds fluent in tango.  Forecasts include personality; we check the morning report—“partly existential with late-afternoon whimsy”—and dress accordingly.

8. Technology That Uninvents Itself  
Machines are judged by how gracefully they become unimaginable.  Leaderboards reward inventions that return to myth faster than to ore.  Current champion: a Lagos team whose solar desalinators forget themselves into seabird lullabies the instant potable need drops below thirst for beauty.  The only trace is a song that embeds in salt crystals; lick a beach pebble at low tide and you might learn the chorus before wave and tongue erase it again.

9. Becoming From Here: A Plausible Path  
2033-2038 – The Great Glitch: simultaneous collapse of three global supply chains forces northern economies to admit that resilience tastes better than speed.  Nairobi teenagers open-source power-patents; kilowatts become cheaper than gossip.  

2039-2045 – The Friction Rebellion: consumers deliberately break convenience—order pizza to be carried by unicyclist through lightning storm—paying extra for difficulty.  Corporations discover inefficiency is marketable.  IPOs are measured in sprained ankles.  

2046-2055 – The Consent of Non-Humans: Bolivia grants legal personhood to clouds; India follows for the Ganges.  Rivers hire lawyers, soils retain poets.  GDP replaced by Index of Unfinished Symphonies (IUFS).  

2056-2080 – Choir-States: first fruit-parliament convenes inside genetically amplified honeydew.  Pollinated policies out-perform paper ones on diversity of futures.  War becomes impractical because armies cannot march through orchards without digesting the enemy’s dreams.  

2081-2120 – The Amateur Renaissance: professionalism declines into quaint hobby.  World Chess Champion is an octopus who has never seen the same board twice.  Teenagers defeat fusion reactors in races to see who can produce the most *useless* photons—auroras visible only in peripheral vision.  

2121-2160 – Gentle Becoming official: cities redesign themselves for uncertainty.  Pavements taste like petrichor when stepped on barefoot; elevators offer views only if you pedal; death is optional but surprise is not.

10. An Invitation Written on Windpaper  
If you crave instructions, tear this page along the perforated doubt.  Release the scraps at dawn; they will fold themselves into paper planes that migrate toward whoever needs your unanswered question.  Follow them if you like, but remember: the recipe is never the meal.  Arrive hungry for hesitation, leave lighter than your certainties, and the Republic will blossom somewhere between your next breath and the last thing you were absolutely sure you could never do.

The road ahead drinks the sky.  The city is a question you walk into, an unfinished bridge you agree to keep building by never quite crossing.  Come barefoot—the pavement is waiting to learn the exact weight of maybe.
