**The Humility Protocols**  
*Field notes from the year 2193, assembled by itinerant archivist Mira Solano, age 34, currently residing in Watershed 11 (old latitude 37.8 °N, longitude 122.4 °W), but only until the apricots finish fermenting*

I write this on a tablet grown from salmon-mycelium. It will dissolve in six weeks, taking my edits with it. That is the first law here: nothing you say should outlast the season that taught you to say it. The tablet smells faintly of river stones because last night I used it as a pillow after the hostel’s mattresses were harvested for mulch. No one complained. Complaint is a form of premature closure, and we tax it heavily.

The place I live has no proper name. Visitors call it the Humility Protocols because the greeting carved above every gate reads: *May you leave here smaller than you arrived.* The phrase is not metaphor. Every adult undergoes a voluntary shrinking at age fifty: one vertebra is removed and returned to the watershed as phosphate. The ceremony is called *backbone composting*. You walk in tall, bow out shorter, literally grounded. My mother mailed me her fourth vertebra last spring; I planted it beneath a lemon tree that now fruits with a faint taste of her laughter. When I eat the lemons I remember not to get too tall in my opinions.

We are 51 million humans distributed across 3,011 watersheds small enough to hear the same thunderstorm. Between us lie the *Unfinished Belts*—land legally prohibited from reaching climax forest. Every decade a belt must burn, flood, or erode at least once; if it behaves too well we send in teenagers with flutes to stir up trouble. Stability is considered a form of loneliness. After the last wildfire the soil was so rich with grief that pumpkins grew to the size of kayaks. We carved them into boats and held a regatta to celebrate impermanence. The winner was disqualified for arriving; the finish line was declared a mistake.

Governance happens in three layers: soil, story, and song. Soil votes through chemistry. Each watershed maintains a *taste parliament*—a spiral trench seeded with clover engineered to change flavor when stepped on. Humans deliberate barefoot; earthworms vote by altering mucus pH; mycorrhizae register decisions through calcium pulses detectable by the city’s heartbeat sensors. A proposal passes when the trench tastes like roasted barley and fails if it turns metallic. Last year the soil rejected a plan to dam the north fork because the clover went bitter as aspirin. We obeyed, dismantled the blueprints, and used the paper to wrap goat cheese that won first prize at the equinox fair.

Story governance is slower. Every child at age seven must *adopt* an unloved object—a cracked phone, a rusted drone propeller, a single synthetic sock—and keep it alive for seven years. The object is fitted with a biodegradable speaker that whispers one sentence per day: *I was once the future and now I am your homework.* At fourteen the child publishes a novella tracing the object’s journey from mine to landfill to adoption. The novellas are printed on apple pectin; you read them by licking. The sweetest stories become policy. A decade ago a girl named Tala chronicled a lithium battery that wanted to become sunrise. Her tale tasted so intensely of mango that we rewired the grid: now every battery must spend its retirement powering grow-lights for orchards. Sunrise is no longer a metaphor; it is a measurable export.

Song governance is the fastest and most dangerous. Once a month the watersheds gather at twilight to sing the *Inventory of Regrets*. You may sing only what you personally failed at—crops you overwatered, apologies you postponed, cakes that sank. The melody is taken up by night insects who remix it into mating calls. By dawn the regret has been metabolized into eggs and antennae. If your regret is still audible by sunrise you must wear it as a perfume for thirty days. Mine currently smells like over-proofed sourdough and the email I never sent my father. Strangers hug me longer than usual; the scent instructs them where to place their tenderness.

Work is defined as *anything that makes the city stranger*. Accountants breed bioluminescent moss that spells budget variances on courthouse walls. Engineers host pottery classes where the clay is mined from demolished highways; every bowl must leak exactly 15 % of its contents to qualify as infrastructure. The most prestigious job is *Dream Curator*: you spend nights collecting citizens’ dreams using EEG headbands woven from recycled spider silk. At dawn you distill the dreams into ice cream flavors—*late for an exam I never studied for*, *kissing my elementary teacher who is also a glacier*—sold from tricycles that play lullabies backwards. Eating the ice cream induces a micro-dream that is harvested the next day. The economy runs on this recursive unconscious. Tourists arrive hoping to taste déjà vu; they leave having loaned us three nights of REM we will spend redesigning crosswalks.

Technology is required to forget itself. A shovel must rust enough to remember being ore. A tablet, like mine, must deliquesce before the next solstice. The city keeps a *Museum of Planned Obsolescence* where exhibits perform their own dissolution. Visitors queue to watch a solar panel flake into lichen, a smartphone blossom into trumpet vines. The most popular attraction is a mirror that evaporates if you look too long; people line up to practice not recognizing themselves. Children use the queue to play *last glance*—whoever sees their reflection longest wins, but the prize is only the knowledge that they were the final witness. The game teaches proportion.

Money exists as *slow coupons*. A coupon earned harvesting seaweed can be redeemed only after the same seaweed has cycled through three human bodies and two species of barnacle. This takes roughly seven years, during which the coupon accrues *patina*—a legal term for stories that stick to value. A coupon wrapped in the anecdote of how you slipped on kelp and accidentally proposed marriage is worth more than a pristine one. The most valuable coupon in circulation was carried across the Pacific by a grandmother who traded it for a song that prevented a suicide. It is now framed in the town hall, feeding termites that taste like salt tears. No one will cash it; the patina has become the payment.

Housing is *sequential*. You may occupy a room only as long as you can name every previous resident and one thing they failed at. The walls are grown from hempcrete embedded with *memory spores* that release the scent of past occupants when warmed by your body. My current bedroom smells of cardamom and the woman who forgot to water her bonsai for exactly 43 days. I sleep beneath her guilt like a second blanket. When I leave I will plant a seed of my own failure—perhaps the recipe I never mastered for apricot mole. The next tenant will dream of Mexico and wake up hungry.

Transport is *gravitational*. The city slopes 2 % toward whatever you most need. Engineers achieved this by training termites to eat substrate at angles calibrated by citizen desire surveys. If the collective yearning shifts—say, from intimacy to solitude—the termites adjust their diet and the gradient migrates. Walking to a tryst you walk downhill; returning you climb. Heartbreak tastes uphill; reconciliation tastes like level ground. Elderly residents claim the slope used to lead toward *justice*, but that was before we learned justice is better pursued obliquely.

Death is *distributed*. When you die your body is divided into 41 portions: one for each watershed you visited plus one for the sky. The sky portion is cremated using almond shells saved over a lifetime; the smoke becomes the ink that prints your final joke on kites released during the next equinox. My father’s joke—*I told you I was allergic to eternity*—hovered for three days before rain dissolved it into a pond where frogs now sing in punchlines. The other 40 portions are composted inside libraries. Each book receives a teaspoon of you. When readers turn pages they release a faint whiff of your childhood kitchen. Over centuries the collection becomes a choir of ancestral smells. Scholars argue that the afterlife is simply the moment when someone checks out *The Odyssey* and inhales your grandmother’s saffron rice.

Birth is *preceded by negotiation*. Prospective parents must spend one year tending a *maybe-garden*—a plot sown with seeds that may or may not be sterile. If anything sprouts they must raise it: tomato, thistle, or child. The ritual dissolves certainty. Some gardens produce only nettles; the couple returns to their watershed childless but richer in pesto. Others burst into volunteer melons that ripen at the same moment the pregnancy test turns blue. Twins are common in drought years because the soil teaches that abundance sometimes means duplication. Every child thus arrives with a plant-sibling; they graduate adolescence only when they can graft their sibling onto civic rootstock—meaning they must find a public role for the extra squash or sunflower that grew alongside them. The city square hosts a *maybe-orchard* of such grafts: lemon trees that bear protest slogans, roses that unfold into birdhouses. The plants remind us that every private joy was once a public speculation.

Art is *maintenance in disguise*. The opera is sung while replacing roof shingles; the percussion is the sound of rotten wood being removed. Audiences sit in the rafters, showered by sawdust that carries the scent of decades. When the aria ends the house is weatherproof for winter. The most famous performance lasted 19 years—an entire neighborhood being rebuilt to the score of *The Tempest*. By the time Caliban received his forgiveness, the final balcony was installed. Residents still find splinters that hum B-flat when plucked.

Time is *layered like sauerkraut*. The outermost layer is *today*, crisp and urgent. Beneath it lies *yesterday*, softening into sweetness. Deeper still: *last century*, tangy with trauma. At the core: *deep time*, the cabbage that remembers being a wild mustard on a Central Asian hillside. Citizens carry pocket forks to taste the layers. Children are taught to chew slowly; fermentation continues in the mouth. A lie spoken tastes metallic because it disrupts the bacteria. Truth tastes like the ocean that once covered the cabbage field. We calendar by flavor. When the city tastes too sour we schedule reconciliation festivals; when it tastes bland we invite strangers to bring their spices. The goal is not perfect kimchi—it is to keep tasting.

Even here we fail. Last winter a boy hacked the termite gradient to slope toward his ex-lover’s window. For weeks the entire city stumbled downhill carrying bouquets and apologies. Couples reunited who had no business reuniting; the divorce compost pile went cold. The Council of Tastes declared a *flavor emergency* and released counter-termites trained to crave regret. The city overcorrected; for a month no one could walk to the market without veering into memory. We survived by eating extra bitterness—dandelion, citrus peel, the jokes that did not land—until the layers stabilized. The boy was sentenced to plant 1,000 *apology trees*, each labeled with the name of a love he inconvenienced. The grove is now a popular breakup spot; you walk in carrying two hearts, leave carrying one and a handful of leaves that smell like almost.

Visitors ask how to export the Protocols. We answer: shrink first. Remove a vertebra from your tallest belief. Mail it to someone who will plant it beneath a fruit you have never tasted. Wait seven seasons. If the fruit grows sour, apologize to the soil. If it grows sweet, share it with a stranger who does not speak your language. When they return the seed in a story, build a door from the pectin. The city that enters will not look like ours, but it will know how to forget itself at the correct speed.

I finish this tablet on the ferry between Watershed 11 and 12. The captain is a dog who steers by barking at phosphorescent plankton. Passengers are required to row for exactly one minute; the oars are carved from last year’s dissent trees. My minute arrives. I row west, toward the apricots that will finish fermenting into time. The tablet softens in my hands like a very old promise keeping itself. By the time we dock the corners have rounded; the words are leaking into my palms. I taste ink and realize it is yesterday trying to become tomorrow. I lick my fingers clean. The city tastes ready—sharp at the edges, sweet at the center, unfinished all the way through.
