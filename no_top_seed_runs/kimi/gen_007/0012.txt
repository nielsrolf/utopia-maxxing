UTOPIA D  
The Continent of Gentle Maybe  
A traveller’s notebook, spring equinox 2174, written on paper that photosynthesises and forgets at dusk

I wake because the wall has exhaled.  
The room is still there, but its edges are softer, as though the house is rehearsing its own absence. A faint smell of crushed cardamom drifts in through the gap where yesterday there was a window. Tomorrow the window may decide to be a bench, or a question, or a small boy learning to whistle. For now it is only a breeze, and that is enough.

The house is built of maybe-stone laced with runner-bean fibres and the recorded laughter of grandmothers who have not yet been born. The laughter is not stored; it is *cultivated*. Every chuckle is clipped, rooted, and trained along carbon-filament trellises. If we forget to laugh for forty nights the walls slump into compost and volunteer as riverbank. We have failed at sadness for eight consecutive years. The neighbourhood keeps sending us tiny medals shaped like dimples; they dissolve on contact with the tongue and leave a taste of overnight rain.

I walk across the floor. It reports the nutrient balance in real time: I owe 0.4 g of phosphorus to the upstairs oat-patch, one lullaby to the stranger who watched me dream (she liked the way my eyelids fluttered in Morse code), and a half-formed hypothesis about the colour mauve to the communal pigment pool. Nothing is bought, only *continued*. The debt itself is a gift: it guarantees that tomorrow I will wake curious.

1. The Economy of Unhurried Surplus  
We have three currencies that cancel each other if hoarded:

- Glint – attention that must be reflected, not spent.  
- Friction – the right to slow something down until it can be felt.  
- Maybe – minted whenever you leave an outcome unfinished long enough for a stranger to finish it in public.

Maybe is the most valuable. Acquire it by releasing control at the instant certainty tastes sweetest: pause a bridge mid-arch, publish a poem minus the verb, plant an orchard upside-down so the sky can taste fruit. Spend it and the city inhales. Hoard it and your hand begins to fade—first the thumbprint, then the lifeline—until you agree to let go. Bureau of Maybe agents drift through the plazas wearing coats of unread footnotes. Their badges read: *I don’t know yet, may I help you hesitate?*

Work is whatever still requires awkward human bodies. Robots do everything that can be finished; people do everything that must remain incomplete. Tuesday I served on the Garden Traffic Cone Brigade, placing orange ceramic toadstools at random intersections so the delivery eels had to practice sudden pirouettes. The resulting delay poured 600 millifrictions into my account, enough to commission a poet to write me a personal weather report: “Expect scattered epiphanies before dusk, followed by late-night clarity clearing to awe.” The cones dissolved at sunset, feeding the pavement their memories of near-collisions. Children licked the spots where they had stood; the taste is said to be a cross between cinnamon and last-second regret.

2. Governance: The Parliament of Ripening  
Laws are grown, not written. Each month a different crop swells to cathedral size—breadfruit, mango, persimmon—its skin translucent as warm amber. Inside, the air is thick with ethylene and debate. Speakers must pollinate their arguments: ideas clothe themselves in pollen, drift to listeners, and germinate behind the ear. By the time the fruit drops, policy has ripened into seeds. Citizens vote by eating the ones they trust; the rest are composted into next year’s agenda. Thus every statute spends part of its life as flavour. You can always spot a former legislator: they unconsciously lick their lips when discussing taxation.

Emotional weather is monitored by the Fountain of Almost, a circular pool that measures the city’s collective salmon-pink of recklessness versus indigo of caution. If the water stays translucent for three days any rule can be suspended, a window used mostly for mass kite-flying indoors and for apologising to insects.

3. Health: The Illness Choir  
When my blood detects anomaly I am not hospitalised; I am *cast*. Within minutes the Choir assembles: one retired comedian whose own tumour became a stand-up routine, a child studying empathy through kaleidoscopes, a dog trained to detect despair in exhaled ketones, and an off-duty sunset. Treatment is mostly autonomous—phages, drones, song-spells—but the humans provide dramatic irony. The comedian insists on naming my rogue cell “Kevin,” turning malignancy into an unpopular coworker. The child charges one laugh per day; if Kevin shrinks, the dog gets to choose the closing joke. Recovery is livestreamed as an episodic opera. Viewers earn glint for witnessing, but the composer must leave the final chord unfinished so the audience leaves still humming, cells uncertain where the boundary between me and them lies.

4. Education: The School of Unlearning  
At fourteen every citizen is enrolled in Ignorance Expansion. Curriculum: forget three certainties, fail at two ambitions, plant one question so large it casts shade for the rest of your life. Final exams are held inside a Möbius strip; you pass when you can no longer find the front. Graduates step out barefoot carrying nothing but a badge that reads *Ask me what I don’t know*. Employers must pay extra for applicants who still bear the badge unblemished by answers.

5. Romance: The Slow-Motion Crush  
Love is declared by planting a bulb in the public arbour that will flower only if both partners regularly speak contradictory truths to it. The blossom is colour-shy; stare too hard and it retreats into green. Lovers therefore practice peripheral affection—talking while watching clouds, letting devotion ferment in the corner of the eye. Breakups are celebrated: at dusk the bulb splits into two vines, each carrying half the pigment away. Where they climb, new benches appear so passers-by can rest inside the chromatic absence, feeling what used to be someone else’s future.

6. Death and the Second Draft  
Mortality is optional but *finishing* is mandatory. Once a century every citizen must release a work that can never be edited again—an orchard, a theorem, a child. Upon publication the author is restored from backup minus copyright on their own past. The new self meets the old creations as strangers, obliged to co-exist without authority. Those who choose true death board the Ferry of Unsaid Sentences. Corpses are transformed into weather: librarians become fog that prints miniature novellas on car windows, dancers become trade-winds fluent in tango. Forecasts include personality; we check the morning report—“partly existential with late-afternoon whimsy”—and dress accordingly.

7. Technology That Uninvents Itself  
Machines are judged by how gracefully they become unimaginable. Leaderboards reward inventions that return to myth faster than to ore. Current champion: a Lagos team whose solar desalinators forget themselves into seabird lullabies the instant thirst drops below aesthetic need. The only trace is a song that embeds in salt crystals; lick a beach pebble at low tide and you might learn the chorus before wave and tongue erase it again.

8. Becoming From Here: A Plausible Path  
2033-2038 – The Great Glitch: simultaneous collapse of three global supply chains forces northern economies to admit that resilience tastes better than speed. Nairobi teenagers open-source power-patents; kilowatts become cheaper than gossip.  

2039-2045 – The Friction Rebellion: consumers deliberately break convenience—order pizza to be carried by unicyclist through lightning storm—paying extra for difficulty. Corporations discover inefficiency is marketable. IPOs are measured in sprained ankles.  

2046-2055 – The Consent of Non-Humans: Bolivia grants legal personhood to clouds; India follows for the Ganges. Rivers hire lawyers, soils retain poets. GDP replaced by Index of Unfinished Symphonies (IUFS).  

2056-2080 – Choir-States: first fruit-parliament convenes inside genetically amplified honeydew. Pollinated policies out-perform paper ones on diversity of futures. War becomes impractical because armies cannot march through orchards without digesting the enemy’s dreams.  

2081-2120 – The Amateur Renaissance: professionalism declines into quaint hobby. World Chess Champion is an octopus who has never seen the same board twice. Teenagers defeat fusion reactors in races to see who can produce the most *useless* photons—auroras visible only in peripheral vision.  

2121-2174 – Gentle Becoming official: cities redesign themselves for uncertainty. Pavements taste like petrichor when stepped on barefoot; elevators offer views only if you pedal; death is optional but surprise is not.

9. An Invitation Written on Windpaper  
If you crave instructions, tear this page along the perforated doubt. Release the scraps at dawn; they will fold themselves into paper planes that migrate toward whoever needs your unanswered question. Follow them if you like, but remember: the recipe is never the meal. Arrive hungry for hesitation, leave lighter than your certainties, and the Continent will blossom somewhere between your next breath and the last thing you were absolutely sure you could never do.

The road ahead drinks the sky. The city is a question you walk into, an unfinished bridge you agree to keep building by never quite crossing. Come barefoot—the pavement is waiting to learn the exact weight of maybe.
