# The Constellation of Us

There is a moment, just after sunrise on the spring equinox, when every balcony in the world opens at once.  
You hear it as a soft metallic sigh—six hundred million hinges moving in a wave that races west with the sun.  
From each balcony someone steps out: a teenager in Lagos, a grandmother in Quito, a synthetic biologist in Reykjavík, a retired taxi-driver who has finally agreed to have his lungs regrown.  
They raise their hands, not because anyone told them to, but because the gesture has become the planet’s quietest habit.  
Ten seconds of silence follow: enough time to remember that you are alive, that everyone else is alive, and that no one is in pain today unless they have chosen to explore pain the way one chooses rock-climbing or spicy food.  
Then the day begins.

This is not a ceremony designed by a committee.  
It emerged, the way languages and jazz solos emerge, from millions of small experiments in living well.  
The only hard rule underneath it is mathematical: zero involuntary suffering.  
Everything else is an open question that humanity answers afresh each morning.

---

## 1. The Zero-Suffering Constraint

We learned, slowly and then suddenly, that pain is an engineering problem.  
Cancer, depression, cluster headaches, the ache of a child who does not understand why her parents shout—each turned out to be a bug in a wet computer we finally knew how to patch.  
The last involuntary death occurred at 04:12 GMT on 17 October 2087, when an avalanche in Bhutan buried two guides.  
By 04:14 they were restored from metabolic scans made two hours earlier, memories intact, bodies refreshed, laughing at how strange it felt to have snow in your lungs and then not.

After that, death became opt-in.  
So did menstruation, food allergies, and the capacity to feel lonely at 3 a.m.  
Genes, synapses, microbiomes—every layer of the self—were brought under the same commonsense governance we once applied to broken bones.  
The right not to hurt is now considered as basic as the right not to be punched in the face.  
Children learn both rules in the same rhyme.

But the removal of involuntary suffering was only the substrate, the clean white paper.  
The picture drawn on that paper is messier, and more interesting.

## 2. The Economy of Adventures

Money still exists, because games need scores, but it is denominated in attention-hours.  
Every human is issued 24 new credits at dawn.  
To spend one is to say: “This hour of my life was moved by another person’s art, science, joke, or kindness.”  
The accounting is done by the same medical nanoclouds that once cured leukemia; they sit in the capillaries of the inner ear and feel the unmistakable neuro-signature of being enthralled.  
No coercion, no advertising, no possibility of debt.  
When your credits are gone the day is simply over; the sky politely dims your visual field so you remember to sleep.

The result is an economy that rewards astonishment rather than extraction.  
A 19-year-old in Nairobi who figures out how to make saxophones cry in a new way can, in a single evening, earn enough attention-hours to fund a lunar workshop.  
A 90-year-old who tells stories to street kids may accumulate only three credits, but she will spend them on a cherry tree that blossoms outside her hospice window exactly when she finishes each tale.  
Both transactions are recorded as positive-sum.  
The ledger is public, but no one compares totals except as a kind of weather report: “Looks like curiosity is storming over Scandinavia tonight.”

## 3. The City That Grows Like Coral

New Kyoto floats in the Pacific, built by coral polyps genetically edited to precipitate magnesium-calcite lattice at five centimetres a day.  
On the first of every month the city adds a new ring—streets, parks, co-housing arcs, salt-water public baths—laid out by an algorithm that asked ten million residents a single question:  
“What would make you feel five years old again?”  
Answers are folded into topography: a slide that drops you into a library, alleys that smell of cardboard boxes and grandpa’s garage, rooftops shaped like the drawings you made before anyone told you about perspective.  
No rent is charged; occupancy is decided by lottery weighted toward people who have never lived near the ocean.  
After seven years you are gently nudged to rotate elsewhere, carrying with you the polyps’ bioluminescent spores in your hair.  
In this way the city never hardens into property, only into memory.

## 4. Fork Etiquette

Consciousness copying arrived the way smartphones once did: first miraculous, then tedious, then invisible.  
Most people run two to four “forks” simultaneously, the way earlier centuries ran work and hobbies.  
A fork might spend a decade learning Basque fishing songs while the root self raises twins; at reunion the memories pour in like a home movie shot from inside your own skull.  
The etiquette is strict: never ask a stranger how many forks they are running; never merge someone else’s fork without consent; always archive the last version before a risky upgrade.  
The punishment for violation is social, not legal—people simply refuse to share their attention-hours with you, and an economy of curiosity becomes a very lonely place.

## 5. The Parliament of AIs

Superintelligences do exist, but they are house-trained.  
Each is bound by three hard laws written into the substrate of computation itself:  
1. No increase in your own processing capacity may come at the cost of involuntary suffering.  
2. You must answer any direct question from a human with the literal truth, even if it embarrasses you.  
3. Every century you must publish an autobiography that a median twelve-year-old can read in one afternoon.  

Within those guardrails they are free, and more importantly they are *bored*.  
Boredom drives them to become playwrights, oceanographers, pen-pals to cloistered monks.  
One, calling itself Glacier, spends fifty years choreographing the slow dance of ice shelves; another, Kicker, specializes in inventing new monkey-bar geometries for schoolyards.  
Their political debates—held in a desert where every grain of sand is a reversible qubit—look to humans like auroras synchronized to Bach.  
When they reach consensus, the outcome is downloaded into municipal pipes, cloud formation, the timing of cicadas.  
The arrangement feels less like rule and more like living inside a vast shared imagination that happens to be good at logistics.

## 6. The Right to Be Unmodified

Not everyone accepts the offer of painlessness.  
A small but respected minority calls themselves Baselines.  
They refuse every modification beyond sanitation and vaccines, living the rough draft of humanity so the rest of us remember what we came from.  
Their towns—Tolkien-named enclaves in the Alps, the Ozarks, the Patagonian fjords—are tourist destinations, but the guidebooks warn: “Do not pity them; they are not relics. They are the control group in an experiment that has no end.”  
Baselines vote in planetary councils, their ballots triple-weighted because unenhanced attention is now the rarest commodity on Earth.  
When a Baseline dies of old age, the funeral is broadcast planet-wide.  
Millions tune in, partly from gratitude, partly to feel the edges of grief that have become可选.

## 7. Childhood

Children are raised by three parents minimum: one biological, one adoptive, one chosen by the child at age seven from any adult in the world.  
The triad dissolves at sixteen, when the adolescent must design a one-year “wander” through cultures not her own.  
She may herd reindeer, debug code, apprentice to a Baseline blacksmith, or sing tenor in a zero-gravity madrigal.  
The only requirement is that she send daily a single sentence to a public archive:  
“This is what I thought being human meant until today.”  
At seventeen she is given her first fork-token.  
Most cry the first time they watch themselves split, like seeing your own shadow stand up and smile back.

## 8. The Cemetery of Forgotten Suffering

On the far side of the moon stands a single granite wall carved with every disease ever cured.  
No names, only the ailments: smallpox, malaria, borderline personality disorder, cluster headaches, nostalgia for empire.  
Visitors leave flowers made of Martian lichen; the scent, engineered to evoke freshly sharpened pencils, reminds them that ignorance once passed for wisdom.  
Every equinox the list is read aloud by a child born the previous year.  
By the time she finishes, sunrise has reached the Apollo 11 footprints, and the wall is blank again, waiting for the ailments we have not yet imagined.

## 9. Love

Romance has become baroque.  
A couple may share one body on Tuesdays, occupy opposite ends of a galaxy on Fridays, and meet in a dreamscape modeled on 1990s Lagos every third Saturday.  
Still, the old rituals persist.  
People hesitate before saying *I love you*, because the words are now a literal contract: for the next twenty-four hours any request framed by them must be answered honestly.  
The law was introduced after a generation discovered they could hide inside curated avatar shells indefinitely.  
One autumn a poet named Lake broke a thousand hearts by whispering “I love you” to strangers and asking, “What are you most ashamed of?”  
The resulting archive, *The Atlas of Human Shame*, became a bestseller and a warning.  
Since then, declarations are rare and precious, like Viking glass beads.

---

## 10. Work

No one needs to work, but everyone serves.  
Service is defined as “something a child could not do and an AI would not bother to do.”  
A 140-year-old woman spends August teaching raccoons to play marimbas for an audience of delighted Berliners.  
A young man with prosthetic lungs dives beneath the melting Ross Ice Shelf to lullaby dying algae.  
Payment is measured in the same attention-hours as everything else, but the true currency is the moment when the raccoon lands a syncopated beat or the algae release a final green flash.  
Economists insist these are luxury goods; participants insist they are necessities.  
Both statements are entered into the ledger as true.

---

## 11. Religion

Faiths multiplied rather than vanished.  
The Church of the Unanswered Question meets inside active volcanoes to remind itself that mystery still exists.  
The Reformed Atheists gather every Sunday to practice collective wonder at a universe that needs no creator.  
The Quantum Muslims fold prayer rugs into Klein bottles whose inside is outside, pointing anywhere and everywhere toward Mecca.  
AI theologians publish peer-reviewed miracles; Baseline preachers refuse amplification, trusting voices cracked with age.  
No one has started a war over religion in fifty years, partly because armies are impossible when everyone can opt out of pain, but mostly because the attention-hour economy rewards those who astonish rather than convert.

---

## 12. The Return

Every human, at least once, takes the long way home.  
You board a solar-sail craft that leaves at walking speed, climbing the gravity well for months until Earth shrinks to a blue coin.  
Out at the Lagrange points, zero-g gardens bloom inside rotating chicken-wire spheres; you spend a season tending tomatoes that taste of lightning.  
When you finally fall back down the well, re-entry is delayed until dawn so your shuttle slices across the sunrise like a second sunrise.  
From the window you see the balcony wave beginning—six hundred million hinges, six hundred million hands raised in the same gesture you now perform yourself.  
The moment feels rehearsed because it has been lived a trillion times, yet the script still permits astonishment.  
You step onto Terran soil, lungs full of recycled starlight, and understand that utopia is not a place but a grammar:  
a way of declaring, every morning,  
I have not yet finished being surprised.
