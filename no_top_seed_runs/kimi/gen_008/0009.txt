The Hum That Became a World  
1. Dawn, 2157 – the first sound you hear is the city breathing back at you.

I wake on a mattress that is only a mattress when I need it to be. The rest of the time it is a mycelial reef two metres below the floorboards of the houseboat, filtering river antibiotics and dreaming of the child who will inherit its spores next spring. When my pulse slows to below fifty-five, the top surface liquefies, rolls me gently upright, and re-crystallises into a warm deck shoe. The shoe is not mine; it belongs to yesterday’s guest who left half her afternoon in the fibres. Today I will walk to work in her memory, and by midnight the shoe will have forgotten me and remember someone else.

The houseboat itself is moored in the middle of the Loire, although the river is only the Loire on Tuesdays. The rest of the week it is a liquid senate: every water molecule carries one vote on where to flow next. Last month a majority decided to coil back upstream for three days so the cherry orchards could pollinate twice. The manoeuvre required the consent of four beaver lodges, two nuclear cooling loops, and an estimated 1.3 billion diatoms. All said yes. The diatoms asked for a new Mozart chord in return; the city orchestra is still rehearsing it.

I am a Rememberist, third grade. My licence allows me to store up to twenty-four hours of other people’s forgetting. The work is physical: I shoulder the weight of a stranger’s misplaced afternoon, carry it until it ferments into something they can bear to reclaim, then walk it back to them wearing a shape they have never seen before. The wage is paid in “echoes”–units of second-hand astonishment. I can spend them on nothing except more surprise, which is why the economy never inflates. The richest Rememberist alive is said to own 4.2 kilo-echoes, enough to make an entire district blink twice at the colour orange. She lives frugally, eats only Thursdays, and is saving for the surprise of dying on a Tuesday.

2. Breakfast happens in the Listening Lattice, a wicker plaza suspended above the river like a sieve. No table is allowed to hold more than six people, because conversation at seven or more always collapses into performance. We breakfast in five. The kettle warms by pedalling: I cycle the river’s own memory of winter through a coil; the cold that leaves the water is stored in ice beads for chilled soup at lunch. While pedals turn we speak in only the past conditional–a civic ritual that prevents anyone from declaring the future settled.

“If the cherries had not twice bloomed, the blackbirds would still be hungry,” offers the composer.

“If hunger had not invented song, we would still be miming our needs,” replies the child who may or may not be yesterday’s glacier. The glacier was granted adolescence after its hundredth birthday; it attends school in the body of whoever needs to feel slowest that day.

No one speaks of what will happen; instead we trade plausible regrets until the kettle sings in F-sharp, the agreed key for Wednesday. Steam rises in the shape of a question mark. We inhale it, cough out small round words that roll across the lattice and drop into the river, where trout snap them up. The trout are the city’s filing system: whatever word you let go is filed in their bones. When a trout is eaten the letters are released back into human tongues, often in a different order. Last year “mortgage” became “grommet” and the entire garment district spontaneously started knitting buoyant sweaters for refugees. We still don’t know where the original mortgage went.

3. By 9 a.m. I am on the north bank, where the street grows daily. The pavement is a living stroma: potato-based concrete laced with spores of parental patience. Every footstep excretes a micrometre of forgiveness. After ten thousand crossings, enough compassion has leached into the ground that a new house erupts–a windowless dome the colour of tired skin. Inside is a single room containing whatever the neighbourhood has recently expelled: an argument about onions, the smell of rain on velvet, a passport left in a dream. The dome remains for exactly one lunar month, then cracks into edible tiles flavoured like apologies. Children collect them on their way to school and trade for stickers that read “I have swallowed more regret than you.”

The city council tracks forgiveness density with seismographs. If an area grows too kind, property prices plummet; people begin to leave doors unlocked, which is considered a form of inflation. When that happens the council broadcasts mild irritation through the paving stones–tiny electrical nips that encourage residents to hold a grudge for exactly seventy-two hours, long enough to restore market buoyancy. The system is called “emotional Keynesianism.” It has prevented both utopia and civil war for half a century.

4. I reach the Memory Refinery by ten. The building used to be a prison; the cells now store unfinished sentences. Each convict was required to write the story of their crime without using the letter “a.” Most failed; the sentences ballooned into guilt-paragraphs that drifted up and adhered to the ceiling like stalactites. Over the years volunteers have been harvesting them, snipping off clauses, releasing the lighter phrases back into the sky where they condense as small grey clouds. When it rains these clouds speak the edited confessions in a whisper you can almost understand. Farmers collect the rainwater in buckets labelled “maybe remorse” and sprinkle it on root vegetables. The carrots grow tentative; chefs use them for consommé served to the recently divorced.

My task today is to help a man retrieve the memory of his father’s hands. The hands were forgotten during an upgrade to the municipal empathy grid. They now reside in paragraph 17 of cell D, tangled in a subordinate clause about overdue library books. I climb the lattice of guilt, snip the hands free, and fold them into a paper boat. The boat is launched in the refinery’s miniature river–a loop that runs through every cell and exits into the Loire. By sunset the man will be waiting downstream; when the boat touches his ankle the hands will re-home themselves in the space between his pulse and his wristwatch. He will weep for exactly four minutes, then buy a round of drinks for strangers who will suddenly remember they too once touched something and forgot.

5. Lunch is chilled soup, of course–beetroot and meteorite. The meteorite arrived sixty years ago, demanding asylum. It was granted city status, complete with two councillors (one magnetic, one mute). In return it donates nanoscopic iron to the soup, which tastes like the first second of 1987. We ladle it from a shared bowl the size of a satellite dish. No spoons: you must lap like deer. The posture prevents anyone from talking business; necks bent, we communicate only with ears.

6. The afternoon shift is spent in the Rehearsal Forest, a plantation of possible tomorrows. Each tree is grown from a question someone was too shy to ask aloud. The canopy rustles with conditional leaves: “What if we forgave the ocean?” “Suppose music paid rent?” A rememberist’s job here is to thin the forest before it becomes prophecy. I harvest the lower branches–small twigs shaped like “maybe”–and bundle them into faggots labelled “kindling for doubters.” They will be dried, soaked in last year’s unsent love letters, and burned on the midwinter night when citizens gather to confess futures they no longer want. The smoke is inhaled by teenagers who wake the next morning fluent in a language none of their parents understand; this is how the city renews its slang.

7. Dusk. I return to the houseboat carrying a satchel of second-hand forgetting. The Loire has voted to flow backward for the final hour of daylight so the herons can fish in yesterday’s water. I undress on deck; clothes slump into the shape of the composer’s sister who died at seven. The fabric has been remembering her longer than anyone alive. Tonight the garment will be composted; tomorrow it will push up as a single blue delphinium outside the Listening Lattice. No one will know whom it mourns, but butterflies will land preferentially on the lowest petal where a birthmark would have been.

I slip into the river. The water reads the satchel like braille, drinking the forgetting in three gulps. For a moment I am heavier than iron, then impossibly light. When I surface the Loire has already forgotten me, which is the correct way to end a day. I climb back on board, dry in the wind that is practising tomorrow’s weather, and crawl into the reef-mattress now warmed by someone else’s dream of airports.

8. Night protocol. The houseboat dims to a bioluminescent hush. Across the city lights float on water, on rooftops, in the mouths of foxes who have learned to mimic the Morse for “please.” Every lamp is a promise that no one is left alone in the dark, but the promise is deliberately dim so solitude is still possible for those who need it. I hover at the edge of sleep, ear against the deck, listening to the mycelium negotiate with the pine planks: they want a little more copper in exchange for remembering the shape of my heels. The boards refuse; they are saving space for tomorrow’s strangers. The reef concedes, offers manganese instead. Agreement is reached with a soft pop I feel in my collarbone. That pop is the city’s heartbeat, and mine, and yours if you ever come here. It sounds exactly like a finger tapping once on the lid of an unmarked box, inside which something we misplaced is waiting to become ours again.
