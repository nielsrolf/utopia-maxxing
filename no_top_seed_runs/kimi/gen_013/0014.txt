UTOPIA  
The City of Gentle Unbuilding  
A citizen’s handbook, written on the day the walls learned to sigh in unison  

1. Dawn: the alarm that is not an alarm  
I wake because the pillow has begun to hum the exact frequency of my mother’s first heartbeat.  
The sound is not coming from the pillow; it is the pillow remembering what it was before it became goose-down: a sky over the Altai, thermal lift under migrating geese, the moment the flock decided to turn together without rehearsal.  
When the tone matches my own pulse, the fabric loosens and lets my neck remember how to hold itself without ambition.  
Only then do I open my eyes.  

The room is half-lit because the roof has forgotten, overnight, whether it is a ceiling or a horizon.  
A faint seam of apricot light leaks through the hesitation.  
I do not fix it.  
In this city we greet structural uncertainty the way other places greet weather: a dependable condition that keeps the future porous.  

2. Breathing the Commons  
I dress in clothes that grew overnight on a communal loom in the stairwell.  
The fibres are cultured from three ingredients: carbon captured last week from the metro tunnels, cellulose mailed in by farmers along the receding coast, and a small quantity of my own breath collected while I slept.  
Every exhale I donate is frozen into a micro-crystal, shipped to the loom, and woven as a pale thread labelled “K.”  
If I am nervous on any given morning, the cloth tightens; if I laugh in my dreams, the hem grows an extra millimetre of turquoise.  
Thus the city learns, thread by thread, how its citizens feel about being alive.  
At week’s end the garments are unraveled, washed, respun.  
Nothing is owned; fabric circulates the way blood circulates, returning warmer.  

3. Breakfast on the Roof That Is Still Deciding to Be a Garden  
The elevator is a kalanchoe plant whose stem has been bred hollow, strong enough for one passenger at a time.  
I step inside, press the leaf-button marked “roof.”  
Ascent smells like crushed tomato and battery-grade lithium.  
The plant remembers every footfall; when the load becomes too heavy it simply stops photosynthesising, turns politely yellow, and asks the next person to wait until it has regrown.  

On the roof the wheat is chest-high and whispering.  
Each stalk records a fragment of last night’s dreams scraped from the pillows of strangers.  
The field ripples with half-finished sentences:  
“…if only I had knocked on…”  
“…the colour her hands turned when…”  
“…before the tide swallowed the piano…”  
I harvest only the grains whose phrases end in a preposition; grammar is how we sort readiness.  
The others I leave standing, like antennae tuned to whatever we have not yet said.  

The grain goes into a single bowl of porridge, cooked over a burner fed by the building’s compost.  
While it simmers, the porridge asks me three questions via a tiny speaker grown into the clay:  
Who did you fail yesterday?  
What did you notice too late?  
Which border inside you feels soft today?  
I answer aloud because the sound of apology seasons the starch.  
By the time I swallow, the building has archived my replies in the root network that links every roof in the ward.  
Anonymised, salted, composted, they will return next month as trace minerals in a stranger’s breakfast—an edible echo.  

4. The metro of unmaking  
I descend through the stem-elevator, exit into the lobby where the floor is a shallow pool of water exactly ankle-deep.  
The pool remembers every foot that has crossed it; when the memories reach ankle-height, the water is drained, filtered, and fermented into a light wine served at communal dinners.  
Drinking the floor is optional; forgetting what it tasted like is impossible.  

The metro entrance is a hole in the wall that used to be a ticket booth.  
Instead of tickets, the turnstile asks for one obligation you no longer wish to carry alone.  
I press into the scanner the memory of a report I promised to finish two winters ago.  
The gate sighs open, lighter by one deferred duty.  
Somewhere down the line, a stranger heading home will find that exact obligation tucked like a pebble into their pocket.  
They may complete it, rewrite it, or return it to the river of unkept promises that flows under the tracks.  
Completion rates are published weekly; they hover at 38 %.  
The other 62 % dissolves into silt, from which we grow the bamboo benches on every platform.  
Thus the city practices the algebra of unfinished lives: nothing is lost, much is transformed, little is required to end.  

Trains run on pressure difference.  
Five cars breathe in while five breathe out, a slow accordion of commuters.  
Inside, seats are upholstered with mycelium leather that records skin temperature.  
If enough passengers feel dread, the seats bloom small purses of lavender to calm the car.  
If collective anticipation spikes, the walls sweat a faint citrus mist.  
We ride inside the city’s emotional weather.  

5. Work: the Department of Gentle Acceleration and Delayed Arrival  
My office is on the fourteenth floor of a tower that is also a cliff.  
Swifts nest in the façade; when they migrate, pieces of the building migrate with them—small wedges of limestone that detach politely, carried on bellies halfway to Senegal, released as mid-air stepping-stones for other species learning long routes.  
The tower loses mass every autumn, regrows every spring through mineral accretion in the basement ponds.  
Net carbon ledger: negative.  
Net dignity ledger: still being calculated.  

I am a Plot Unraveler, Level II.  
My task is to read the master timeline each morning: a three-metre roll of bacterial cellulose that lists every large infrastructure project scheduled for the next thirty years.  
I select one and, with a stylus made of glacier silt, begin to erase.  
Not cancel—unplan.  
I remove the sharpest edges, stretch the schedule, replace steel with wood, concrete with air, certainty with barn-owl ambiguity.  
The goal is to create enough slack in the future for citizens to breathe without permission.  
When I overdo it, the building grows a crack in reprimand; when I underdo it, the swifts refuse to nest.  
Their silent wings are our audit.  

Today I unpave the Inner Ring Road (2041), replacing six lanes with a single reversible causeway that folds itself into a tunnel whenever children feel like walking to the sea.  
Thus a journey of twenty minutes becomes a journey of however long it takes to finish the chapter of the story told by the oldest pedestrian in the group.  
Traffic engineers protest; I invite them to the roof to drink the floor-wine.  
They leave footprints that will be bottled next season.  

6. Lunch in the Parliament of Unfinished Seconds  
The canteen is a grove of chestnut trees whose trunks have been grafted into spiral staircases.  
Legislators climb clockwise to speak, counter-clockwise to listen.  
No one may hold the floor longer than it takes for a chestnut to fall from branch to table.  
Thus speeches are short, seasons matter, and gravity has voting rights.  

The speciality is regret salad: leaves coated with a dressing that tastes like whatever you apologised for most recently.  
We eat each other’s mistakes.  
A junior minister chews my failure to finish the winter report; I taste her apology for signing a flood-wall contract that would have blocked the swifts.  
The flavours mingle—bitterness first, then iron, then something faintly like the smell of wet pavement after fireworks.  
Nutrition labels list micronutrients of reconciliation: 12 % of daily need for mutual leniency.  
We leave the table dizzy with shared culpability, lighter by one secret each.  

7. School: learning to unbecome  
At 14:00 I collect my daughter from the Institute for Temporal Softness.  
She is eight and already fluent in three extinct dialects of uncertainty.  
Today’s exam: “Build a room that will forget you quicker than you can forget it.”  
She presents a cube of salt the size of a shoebox, laced with yeast.  
When I breathe on it, the walls slump into brine, releasing the faint echo of my own heartbeat slowed to half-speed.  
The puddle seeps into the courtyard gravel, leaving a stain shaped like yesterday’s moon.  
She passes.  

Next lesson: how to walk away from an idea before it turns into ideology.  
The children practice on holograms of their own convictions: a kitten that grows into a lion if cuddled too tightly, a song that adds verses whenever hummed in public.  
They learn to release, to dodge ownership, to love the verb more than the noun.  
Graduation occurs the day a student can say “I was wrong” in the past, present, and future imperfect without nostalgia.  

8. The clinic of borrowed pain  
My knees hurt, not from age but from carrying yesterday’s headlines.  
I check into the clinic where illness is treated as misplaced narrative.  
Triage nurse is a crow trained to steal shiny absolutes.  
He perches on my shoulder, plucks out the sentence “Progress must be linear,” flies off to drop it in the recycling bin of exploded metaphors.  
Instantly the pain dims from sharp to conversational.  

Inside, doctors wear coats stitched from x-ray film; you can see right through them to their own fractures.  
Treatment today is choral: six patients lie in a circle, our heartbeats projected as coloured sine waves on the ceiling.  
When the waves syncopate, we swap diagnoses.  
I receive your ulcer of unspoken rage; you inherit my cartilage of accumulated maybe.  
We leave carrying versions of each other’s damage that feel strangely lighter, like jackets worn previously by someone one size smaller.  
Prognosis: indefinite sharing, gradual erosion, possible laughter.  

9. Economy: the Bank of Unclaimed Futures  
Currency here is the “perhaps,” a coin minted from recycled solar panels.  
One side shows a door ajar, the other a window half-open.  
Value is pegged not to scarcity but to hesitation: the more people postpone a decision, the more perhapses enter circulation.  
Thus the wealth of the city increases every time someone says “let’s sleep on it,” “we’ll see,” or “I need to ask the river.”  

Inflation is encouraged; when too many perhapses pile up, the city stages a Festival of Premature Certitude.  
Citizens spend a day pretending to absolute conviction—declaring lifelong majors, signing thirty-year mortgages, proposing marriage atop billboard-sized rings.  
The spectacle is comic, absolving, and short.  
By midnight the props dissolve into confetti seeded with wild-clover.  
The economy exhales, lighter by one bubble of dread.  

10. Governance: the rotating absence  
There is no mayor.  
Instead, every year we elect a Missing Person.  
The winner is ceremonially exiled to the edge of the city with instructions to remain just out of reach.  
All legislation is addressed to them by name.  
Laws gain legitimacy only if the absentee could plausibly approve without returning.  
Thus every ordinance must pass the test of gentleness: would it sound like kindness shouted across water at dusk?  
If not, it is fed to the compost that grows next year’s ballots—blank sheets of dandelion paper waiting for new names to float away.  

11. Twilight: the apology that builds itself  
Dinner is cooked on stoves fired by yesterday’s arguments.  
We sear vegetables over the flame of a debate about whether birds are flowers that learned impatience.  
Smoke carries our words into the sky where cumulus rearrange them into banners readable only from the suburbs of regret.  

After eating we walk to the estuary where the city practices endings.  
Tonight an old pier is scheduled to unbuild itself.  
We gather ankle-deep in mud, each holding a tool of forgetting: a sponge, a feather, a pocketful of bread.  
On the hour, the structure begins to loosen nails like sighs.  
We offer our implements; strangers scrub beams, coax planks, chew crumbs and spit papier-mâché pulp into cracks.  
Piece by piece the pier returns to driftwood, floating off with the tide.  
Within an hour nothing remains but a rectangle of moonlight on water that remembers being solid.  
We name the absence “perhaps,” feel it throb like a freed artery.  

12. Night: the bed that keeps arguing and that is fine  
Back in my room, the ceiling has resolved into neither roof nor sky but a low cloud licensed to linger indoors.  
It rains gently on the quilt, enough to keep the fabric humble.  
I lie down, offer the pillow today’s final heartbeat.  
It records the rhythm, promises to remix it into tomorrow’s alarm.  

Before sleep I check the window.  
Outside, the city is a scatter of half-lit buildings breathing in slow sync, some expanding, some courteously collapsing, all practicing the art of remaining unfinished enough to welcome the next becoming.  
Nothing gleams with the hardness of forever; everything reflects the apricot uncertainty of dawn.  

I whisper a law into the dark:  
Let no surface harden faster than a heart can soften.  
The cloud above me flashes once—lightning or approval, impossible to tell.  
Sleep arrives like a train that inhales while another exhales, carrying me gently into the jurisdiction of maybe.  

Tomorrow the porridge will ask new questions, the metro will collect fresh obligations, the pier will begin to reassemble somewhere else because a group of teenagers needs to learn how to walk on water before they learn how to vote.  
We will continue unbuilding the city at precisely the speed we learn to forgive its weight.  

This is how we live: in a place that stays tender by never arriving, that keeps its maps lightly drawn in dissolving ink, that measures wealth in the pause between inhale and exhale.  
We are all cartographers of postponement, gardeners of the almost, librarians of the unended sentence.  
Our utopia is not a destination but a grammar: the practice of staying soft enough to edit each other’s stories without tearing the page, of holding completion lightly enough to let the future revise us first.
