UTOPIA  
A Society That Practices Impermanence Together  

I wake up because the mattress is finishing a conversation with the rain.  
Tiny burdock roots in the fabric have tasted the night’s drizzle; they tighten a millimetre and the bed folds me upright—gently, the way a bookmark closes a chapter.  
If I wanted to stay horizontal I could, but the room itself has grown taller overnight, reaching like a yawn toward the scent of wet pavement.  
Nothing is fixed here except the agreement that nothing stays.  
We call the city *Nakło*, an old Slavic word for “river-bend,” chosen because bends slow water long enough to remember it is only passing.  

## The Currency of Three Breaths  

There are three kinds of money and all of them die without company.  

1. *Kisses* – small copper coins minted from melted phone wire.  
   A kiss can be spent only if two palms touch while the coin is passed.  
   If either hand is gloved, the metal cools and flakes into green rust that gardeners later scrape into tomato soil.  
   In this way every purchase is also a public service: hand to hand fertilises the commons.  

2. *Breadcrumbs* – edible paper printed with a single wish somebody else wrote last year.  
   You earn them by maintaining something you do not own: polishing the handrail of a library staircase you will never climb, sweeping snow from a stranger’s solar panels.  
   When you have collected enough, you tear the stack into winter soup.  
   The wish dissolves on your tongue and the resulting flavour tells you what job needs your body tomorrow.  

3. *Clouds* – black charcoal sticks that must be burned within three days.  
   A cloud funds large projects: bridges, elections, festivals of forgetting.  
   After 72 hours the sticks crumble and drift upward as fine dust.  
   Overspending creates local drizzle; underspending leaves deposits of quiet gold on south windowsills.  
   The richest citizens are remembered by weather patterns that outlive them.  

## Government by Seasonal Misunderstanding  

Law is grown, not written, in the Orchard of Doubt.  
In January anyone can plant a seed made from a sentence overheard on the tram, a scrap of hair, a pressed cigarette filter.  
By late spring the plants have become small pavilions: a hallway that narrows at both ends, a staircase spiralling into a pond, a room whose walls sweat copper.  
Nothing may be voted upon until at least 200 citizens have spent a night inside.  

Ballots are simple:  
- Laugh when you leave: the law is enacted for one year.  
- Cry: the pavilion is composted into sugar-glass and fed to the river-mind.  
- Remember nothing: the structure joins the Archive of Perhaps, a district whose streets appear only on foggy Tuesdays.  

Last year the seed “Right to Walk Away Partially” sprouted as a bridge that refused to meet in the middle.  
Instead it rose into twin question marks, leaving a gap wide enough for migrating swifts but too narrow for cars.  
We laughed when we crossed it—first nervously, then delighted.  
Now the bridge teaches statistics classes: students stand on separate halves, pitching audible guesses about where the other side will be next month.  
No one has fallen; uncertainty has become a muscle.  

## Work Sites Called *Maybe Yards*  

Children do not dream of professions; dilemmas audition people.  
At dawn the city’s public screens list openings:  

- One elevator has begun to grieve early.  
  Seeking rider willing to arrive late.  
  Payment: three nightmares translated into lullabies.  

- River-mind needs translators fluent in rust.  
  Must be comfortable breathing underwater for short, imaginary intervals.  

- Orchard expects bumper crop of contradictory futures.  
  Pickers required to harvest questions that cancel each other out.  

If a task frightens you, your fingerprint glows violet at the touch-point and the job is yours.  
Experience is measured not in years but in *versions of self you have agreed to shed*.  
My neighbour Amka is onto Version 11.4, famous for the fortnight she spent as a traffic light, learning to pause without blame.  
She keeps the old colours in a jar above the sink—red that once told lovers to wait, green that once told children to cross.  
When she cannot sleep she sprinkles a pinch onto the windowsill; morning finds the glass streaked with tentative beginnings.  

## Houses That Learn to Let Go  

Every resident signs the Seven-Year Accord: after the seventh winter you must help your dwelling unbuild itself.  
The process is gradual and musical.  
In year six you begin to practice: remove one tile per week, replace it with a bird-box, a poem painted on silk, or simply daylight.  
During the final spring you invite strangers for *Demolition Choir*.  
Each visitor chooses a surface—door, rafter, windowpane—and sings to it the story of something they once believed permanently.  
When the note cracks with emotion, the material loosens.  
By dusk walls have flaked into barley-sized gravel; beams float off like rafters of light.  
Whatever remains vertical becomes armature for wisteria, quiet corners for teenagers to discover kissing, or foundation for next cycle’s homes.  
Nothing is discarded, everything is returned faster than regret.  

My own house is currently Year 4.  
I still have three winters to teach the western gable to forgive rain.  
On calm nights I stand on a chair and whisper lists of what I no longer need:  
- The certainty that I was right.  
- The face I wore to look like my resume.  
- The calendar that taught me to ration joy.  
The wall listens, mortar softening grain by grain.  
One day it will leave me, and I will thank it for the lesson of shelter that refuses to outlive its usefulness.  

## Schools Where You Graduate by Vanishing  

Education is seasonal and multi-species.  
Kindergarteners begin by unlearning object permanence: teachers hide wooden cubes inside blocks of ice that sublimate while the children watch.  
By the time water becomes air, the cube is also gone—proof that disappearance is a kind of gentle transport.  

Primary students major in *Parallel Languages*.  
They conjugate verbs the river uses when it mistakes itself for rain, practice the imperfect tense of abandoned shopping trolleys, memorise the imperative mood of mushrooms commanding forest floor to *stay humble*.  
Exams are held in a doorway that narrows one centimetre every minute.  
You pass when your body no longer fits the person who entered—when you have shed enough certainty to squeeze through into a larger uncertainty.  

Universities are temporary.  
Last month one existed inside a thunderstorm.  
Applicants flew kites made of tax code; those struck by lightning earned the right to forget their specialisation.  
My daughter Kai, almost eleven, hopes to attend the University of Inhaling Elsewhere, a two-week fog that forms over the tram depot each autumn.  
When it lifts, graduates have inhaled seven professions and exhaled five, returning lighter by two possible selves—diplomas written on the lining of their lungs in ink that washes clean with first real snowfall.  

## Healing Clinics That Treat Citizenship as Alignment  

Illness is read as a mistranslation between bodies and borders.  
The clinic floats where three ecosystems overlap: human, river, spore.  
You arrive carrying a symptom no one can pronounce—an ear that rings in the key of abandoned amusement parks, ribs that ache each time the stock market closes.  

A tri-linguist team (elder, otter, algorithm) drafts a small treaty:  
- Virus agrees to retreat to skin’s surface, becoming a photosynthetic freckle that manufactures vitamin D.  
- Immune system consents to host the freckle, learns to seasonal-cycle like daylight.  
- River provides memory of ancient ice; patient drinks one cup weekly, remembers deep time, forgives hurried heart.  

Side-effects include sudden fluency in rust, tendency to wave at construction cranes as if they were herons.  
Cure rate: 93 %.  
Remaining 7 % become diplomats themselves, walking the streets with pockets full of pheromone drafts, pollinating negotiations between pigeons and statues.  

## Love as Joint Amnesia, Death as Crowd Remix  

Partners do not wed; they co-author a *Ledger of Voluntary Forgetting*.  
Page one records the first extravagant compliment; page thirty the quarrel about dishes; page ninety the smell of Sunday they always meant to savour.  
When the ledger is full, they hold a *Re-reading in Reverse*.  
Memories return seasoned by absence—sweeter, saltier, sometimes unrecognisable.  
Most choose to begin again immediately, older and lighter.  
The emptied ledgers are pulped into roof tiles that line the Honeymoon Arch, a gateway expanding for every couple who crosses it twice.  

Death is edited by citizens under twelve and citizens over ninety, plus one non-human curator (mycelium, glacier, or abandoned vending machine).  
Together they condense a life to 62 % of its original text, selecting the episodes likely to stay interesting when retold.  
You may veto any cut, but each refusal shortens the remaining story by one clause.  
At the moment the heart stops, the curated tale is released like milkweed.  
People catch fragments on sleeves, retell them incorrectly, release them again.  
In this way every death becomes weather system, every grave a grammar update.  

## Public Ritual of *Unfinishing*  

On the final Thursday we gather in the *Plaza of Unspent Tomorrows*, a square that exists only while we decide what to build there next.  
Each resident brings something completed: a sweater, a theorem, a grudge, a loaf.  
We place them on long tables carved from glacier-core.  
At moonrise we dismantle—not destroy, but unmake with attention:  

- The loaf is torn into molecules of conversation; steam carries yesterday’s weather into the crowd.  
- The theorem is read aloud backwards until it proves the opposite, then released as monarch butterflies.  
- The grudge is steeped in hot cider; we drink it sweetened, piss it out as lullabies that water the park’s linden trees.  

By midnight the tables are empty, plaza dissolving into fog.  
We walk home lighter, pockets rattling with negative space.  
Somewhere in the city tomorrow’s disagreements are already sprouting, tomorrow’s bridges rehearsing incompleteness.  
We will meet them open-palmed, ready to pay in unwritten sentences, ready to own only long enough to pass along.  

## Arrival at Continuous Maybe  

When foreigners ask how Nakło functions without collapse, I tell them: we replaced certainty with choreography.  
Conflict still arrives—it simply travels by unicycle now, wobbling, inviting clowns and philosophers to the same intersection.  
Resources still grow scarce; we respond by editing our needs the way gardeners prune roses—not less beauty, but beauty redirected, shared, made seasonal.  

We do not promise paradise forever.  
We promise *practice*—daily, imperfect, hilarious practice at letting the story edit us before we ossify.  
Our constitution is a single sentence uttered each dawn by the river loudspeakers:  

“May today’s version of us be draft enough for tomorrow’s unknown collaborator.”  

The sentence echoes off the half-built opera house, off the bridge shaped like a question, off the gap where my west wall will soon depart.  
It enters open windows, slips into sleeping mouths, seeds the dreams of citizens still deciding what kind of impossible to attempt.  

I lie down in a room that may not be here next year.  
Cloud currency drifts outside, deciding whether to rain.  
My daughter hums a verb tense that exists only between glacier and stream; the sound is conditional, luminous.  
Sleep arrives the way our trains pull into stations—pausing long enough to inhale passengers, then exhaling toward the next becoming.  

We are rich in postponement, fluent in almost.  
Our maps are drawn in dissolving ink, our calendars planted as bulbs.  
We will wake again because the mattress remembers it is temporary.  
We will wake again because the walls have agreed to sing while they still can.  
We will wake again—unfinished, interoperable, and gently, gently unprepared.
