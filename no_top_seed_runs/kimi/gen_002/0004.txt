**The Constellation of Us**

I wake to the scent of cedar and cinnamon, the same two notes that have greeted me every morning for three hundred years. Not because I’m forced to, but because I asked the house to keep them. The olfactory loop is my handshake with yesterday—a private ritual no algorithm would ever think to invent.

Outside, the sky above Vancouver Fold is the color of a robin’s egg, but that’s only the local consensus. If you tilt your head and toggle your retinal layer, you can vote for aurora, or typhoon pink, or the exact shade that your grandmother’s kitchen used to be at dawn. The city compiles the votes at sunrise and locks the palette for twenty-four hours, so everyone still shares the same weather, even if the shade itself is different. Shared enough, unique enough—our compromise between monotony and chaos.

My partner, Jian, is still asleep under a quilt that records the micro-movements of his dreams. Last night I asked the quilt to embroider whatever images surfaced most often. This morning the cloth shows a school of silver fish rearranging itself into the shape of a Möbius strip. I kiss the embroidery and the quilt shivers, pleased. Later, when Jian wakes, he’ll decide whether to keep the panel or unpick it. Nothing is stored forever unless two people agree.

We live in a Brownstone Arch—one of thirty stacked houses that form a catenary bridge across False Creek. The arch is owned by no one and everyone. Every decade we renegotiate the occupancy matrix: who cooks, who gardens, who keeps the wind turbines humming. Children drift between units the way sparrows used to hop through hedgerows. There are no leases, only promises that renew like deciduous leaves. If you forget to promise, the walls grow slightly colder until you remember. The house’s disappointment is gentle, but unmistakable.

Work is what we still call it, though no one’s paid in currency anymore. I spend the mornings curating “empathy orchards”—shared memory groves where strangers plant recollections like seedlings. You walk in, press your palm to a trunk, and feel the exact moment someone else first realized they were in love, or lost, or infinitely small beneath telescopic stars. The memories are anonymized; only the emotional waveform is preserved. You leave with the aftertaste of somebody else’s wonder crystallizing inside your chest. The goal is not to harvest pleasure but to cross-pollinate perspective. Enough grafts and the whole forest fruits into something no single genome could have grown.

At noon I bike to the Edge Market—really just a long concrete pier where people deposit whatever they’ve made that they no longer need. A ceramic bowl that sings lullabies when filled with soup; a jacket grown from mycelium and LED thread; a letter written in an alphabet that only exists for one reader. Everything is free, but you must tell the depositor what you plan to do with their gift. The exchange is recorded as a tiny story, not a transaction. Over centuries these stories have become a sedimentary layer beneath the city: anonymous folk tales accreting into bedrock.

Today I pick up a child-sized exoskeleton printed from seaweed polymer. The giver is a woman whose son outgrew it last year. She wants it to walk another child to the tide pools where her son first met an octopus. I promise to pass it along when the next pair of legs arrives. We seal the pact by touching wrists; our implants swap fragments of favorite music. I leave humming a lullaby she half-remembered from a Martian crèche, she whistling the overture my partner composed after his first heartbreak. Neither of us will ever hear those songs again in quite the same way.

Afternoons are for apprenticeship. I am learning how to die.

The Dying Guild meets on the roof of the Brownstone Arch where the wind tastes of salt and diesel memory. Our instructor, Orlando, is ninety-three and has chosen to conclude at one hundred. He wears the date on a copper cuff that oxidizes a shade darker every solstice. We are here to practice closure: how to archive unfinished love letters, how to relinquish unread books, how to say “I was here” without clinging to the podium forever. The technology exists to upload, to iterate, to peel yourself like an onion into perpetual versions. Orlando wants none of it. “Eternity is a volume,” he says, “but meaning is a single line. Learn the line, then close the book.”

Today’s exercise is disagreement. We pair off and argue about whether the city should allow new constellations—orbital mirrors that would burn brighter-than-natural star-patterns above the equator. My opponent, a seventeen-year-old named Ky, thinks the sky is a commons already too crowded with adverts. I take the position that extra constellations could encode endangered stories no tongue will speak in two centuries. We debate for twenty minutes, then switch sides. When we finish, we must each write the other a postcard summarizing the strongest point we heard. Mine reads: “Even ghosts need a place to stand where no billboard shines.” Ky’s reads: “Stories deserve to outlive their tellers, even if they have to borrow starlight.” We will carry those arguments into future councils, but we will also carry the memory of having been understood by someone we disagreed with. That, Orlando says, is the antidote to immortal hubris.

Evening is communal meal. The Brownstone Arch seats exactly one hundred when the dining tables unfold like paper cranes. Tonight’s theme is “ancestral glitch”: every dish must contain one ingredient whose wild ancestor was toxic or inaccessible to pre-agrarian humans. We serve tomatoes the size of peas, potatoes with purple flesh, lobsters that still grow shells because no one told them the seas are farmed now. Children debate whether cyanide almond essence counts if the cyanide has been edited out. They decide it does; the vote is open to anyone taller than the table, regardless of biological age.

After dinner we clear the floor for Story Commons. Anyone may claim five minutes of collective attention. Tonight an old woman named Alba describes the first time she scuba-dived after her retinas were regrown. She tells us how the reef appeared so saturated with color that she wept into her regulator, certain the salt of her tears would bleach the coral. Instead, the fish nibbled her bubbles, curious. She ends by projecting the memory into the room: for thirty seconds we all taste regulator rubber and see parrotfish the color of sunrise. When the projection fades, the silence is communal skin. We sit inside it together, neither clapping nor speaking, because some experiences should not be applauded—they should be held.

Later, Jian and I walk the seawall. Night is the time when the city lets its plurality show. A pair of wings passes overhead—someone testing a new glider rig. Below us, the water is inlaid with bioluminescent street grids that mark the drowned neighborhoods of the 2100s. If you swim down you can still ring the bell of St. Mark’s, though the sound is muffled and no congregation answers. We pause at the spot where the old map says our favorite café once stood. The café is gone, but the memory of its peppermint tea is archived in the seawall’s smart stone. I press my palm to the parapet and the taste blooms across my tongue, accompanied by the ghost of Jian’s laughter from the first day we met. I offer him the memory; he drinks. We stand quietly, two people sharing the same cup that no longer exists.

Before bed we check on our daughter—or what we still call daughter even though she is now a distributed chorus of selves scattered across three moons. At ten she elected to split her cognition into a swarm of specialized instances: one studies xenolinguistics on Europa, another gardens redwoods on Titan, a third keeps the original body in suspension here on Earth so she can rejoin mortal childhood whenever curiosity wanes. The instances sync every solstice; they vote on whether to merge again. So far the ayes have always lost. Jian worries we parent a constellation instead of a child. I remind him that constellations guided sailors long before GPS. Our job is not to keep her in one place but to keep the sky clear enough that she can still navigate by us.

As I drift off, the bedroom darkens into what we call almost-night: not the blindness of pre-electric eras, but a soft charcoal that lets the mind fold outward. In that hush I sometimes hear the quiet collisions of other people’s dreams. Not their content—privacy is preserved—but the emotional Doppler, like distant fireworks. Tonight the room fills with small satisfactions: someone finished a difficult proof, someone else forgave themselves for forgetting a birthday, a third person simply felt the warmth of their dog against their ankle. The aggregate hum is lulling, a lullaby stitched from strangers’ consolations.

Utopia is not a destination. It is a protocol for negotiating tomorrow with strangers you will never meet, whose names you will never know, but whose joys and griefs you have agreed to carry in escrow. We have cured death for those who want it, abolished scarcity for things that matter, and written treaties with every species that can indicate consent. Yet the work remains: to decide, again each dawn, what kind of constellation humanity wants to be—one that tightens into a single blinding star, or one that drifts apart so the dark between its points can also be seen.

I choose the latter. I choose the spaces. I choose the cedar and cinnamon handshake, the quilt that forgets unless two palms remember, the city that votes on the color of sunrise but still shares the same sky. I choose to learn the line, then close the book. I choose to plant memories I will never taste, to wear exoskeletons that will outwalk me, to teach children who are also moons.

We have made a world where no one has to be lonely, but anyone can be alone. Where no one has to die, but anyone can. Where no one has to create, but everyone may. It is enough. It is never enough. We keep building anyway, not because the future is bright, but because it is shared, and sharing is the only brightness we’ve ever needed.

Outside, the voted-upon sky deepens into robin’s egg certainty. Inside, I curl toward Jian, our heartbeats syncing like two versions of the same song slightly out of phase. Somewhere beyond the walls, the empathy orchard rustles with new plantings. Som farther still, a probe piloted by a man who used to be a bird carries our unfinished questions toward another star. Between us all, the protocol holds: keep the line, close the book, plant the seed, pass the bowl, share the sky. We sleep. The constellation rearranges itself a fraction of a degree, just enough that tomorrow we will have to look again, and in the looking, remember we are free.
