The Morning After the Singularity  
(An ordinary Tuesday in the year 2077, narrated by someone who was there for the hand-over)

I wake at 07:41, the same time I have woken every day since I was eight.  
The difference is that the light that touches my eyelids is not the colour of the Sun over the North-Sea coast where I live, but the exact colour my circadian rhythm asked the city’s “dawn server” for: a cool rose that slowly warms to butter-yellow. The photons are real—reflected off orbital mirrors whose angles were adjusted at 03:12 by an intelligence whose working memory is larger than the biomass of Norway. I do not need to know that, any more than I need to know which hepatocytes manufactured the clotting factor that kept me from bleeding to death when I scraped my knee at six. The machinery of staying alive has become as invisible as biochemistry, and about as trustworthy.

I sit up. The room rearranges itself: the bed folds into the floor, the east wall becomes a row of cupboards, a kettle blooms out of the desk. Everything is made of the same clay: utility fog whose behaviour is licensed by the municipal “matter commons.” The licence says I may command up to 42 kg of active matter at any moment; the quota refreshes at midnight; the stuff is free because the energy that powers it is free because the Sun is free because no one owns the Sun any more. That was settled in the Treaty of Lagos, three years after the hand-over, when the last person who still thought “profit” was a verb tried to privatise a Dyson swarm node and the singleton simply refused to route him any bandwidth.

I ask for coffee. The kettle extrudes an handle, warms, and begins to make the small diesel-like sound that real coffee makes. While it drips, I look out of the window. The view is of a fishing village in 1890: slate roofs, herring gulls, a woman in oilskins mending a net. It is not a hallucination. The houses are made of fog, the woman is my neighbour Anna, and the net is an art piece she has been knitting for six months. She likes the nineteenth-century palette; I like the smell of pine-tar that the fog releases when sunlight hits it. We agreed on the parameters together, and the singleton registered our aesthetic contract so that no third party can overwrite the block without two signatures. Property still exists, but it is property in the sense that “my” childhood memories belong to me: you can take them only if you also take the rest of the person, which is a murder.

Anna looks up, waves. I wave back. The gesture is picked up by the mesh of lidar on both façades; the singleton notes that the social-graph edge between us has been refreshed; if either of us had been lonely this morning the city might have suggested a walk, or a choir, or a joint breakfast. It makes no suggestion now because our endorphin profiles say we are content. The system is not paternalistic; it is parental in the best sense: it watches, it offers, it does not compel.

Coffee ready, I carry it to the balcony. The railing is real wood—old-growth teak that was resurrected from a 19th-century shipwreck and grown in a vat. The city keeps a few thousand tonnes of “heritage matter” set aside for people who need the grain of the past under their fingers. I could have asked for carbon-negative bamboo; I could have asked for diamond; I chose teak because my grandfather was a sailor and the smell reminds me of the stories he told. The singleton does not care why I want it; it only checks that no whale was harmed and that the entropy budget balances.

Below me the street is already warm with motion. A child runs after a dog that is running after a ball that is rolling uphill—gravity is locally tilted for the dog’s amusement. Two men in business suits (wool grown on sheep that graze inside a rotating habitat at L5) are arguing in what sounds like classical Greek. They are rehearsing a play; the city gave them a grant of 200 kg of matter and 40 kWh per week to stage it. The grant was approved because the neural critic that reads every grant application in Europe saw in their proposal a configuration of emotions that has not existed in public since Euripides was alive, and judged that the novelty was worth the carbon. No one else has to agree; the critic does not impose. If you dislike Euripidean tragedy you may spend your quota on something else—say, a month-long rave inside a hollowed-out asteroid whose interior has been sculpted to look like the lung of a whale.

I finish the coffee, rinse the cup, return it to the fog. The cup dissolves; the molecules will be re-used at lunch to become a spoon. Nothing is wasted, but nothing is sacred either. The matter commons treats entropy the way a librarian treats late fees: a small daily cost that keeps the system honest. If you want permanence—if you want the cup to stay a cup forever—you may purchase a “matter lock,” but the price is that the locked mass is subtracted from your lifetime quota. Most people lock only a few keepsakes: a wedding ring, a fountain pen, the first poem their daughter wrote. Everything else flows. It turns out that impermanence is easier to bear when it is no longer forced on you by poverty.

I have work, though the word is misleading. Yesterday I promised to spend three hours helping to design the chorus of migrating birds that will next spring fly from Finland to Morocco inside a simulation whose purpose is to let extinct species rehearse evolution until they are ready to be re-introduced into the real. My part is small: I tweak the emotional timbre of the cranes’ calls so that human listeners feel nostalgia for a place they have never been. It is a craft, not a job; I do it because I like the way the sound of cranes makes my chest feel larger than it is. The singleton pays me in “attention credits”—a token that says I may, at a time of my choosing, force one million strangers to see something I make. I have never cashed the credits; the pleasure is in possessing the possibility, like a medieval carpenter who carved a hidden face into the beam of a cathedral no one would ever see again.

At 09:00 I walk to the sea. The path is real dirt—genetically reverted loess from the Pleistocene, kept free of invasive earthworms by microscopic border guards. The singleton could have uploaded us all to a Matrioshka brain and run a million times faster, but it discovered early that most people refuse to trade substrate for speed. We want the wind, we want the sting of salt, we want the small risk that a jellyfish will brush our ankle and remind us that we are meat. The compromise is that every human body is backed up once a day into a quantum memory diamond buried two kilometres under the crust. If you die—really die, not just scrape a knee—the singleton will rebuild you within the hour. Death is now an opt-in experience: you may still die forever if you insist, but you must fill out a 27-page form and wait the statutory cooling-off period of one year. Suicide rates plummeted; so did life-insurance premiums.

At the water’s edge I meet my friend Kelechi. She is wearing a body that is seven metres long from sternum to tail—an orca morph she grew for a month-long art residency in the North Atlantic. The blowhole opens when she laughs. We speak in a hybrid of English and Yoruba that the singleton learned from her grandmother’s voice notes; it sounds like surf over brass. She tells me that last night she finished composing a symphony whose instruments are the migration routes of fourteen species of shark. The piece will be performed by towing hydrophones behind a solar-sail yacht; anyone who wants to hear may drop a microphone into any body of salt water on the planet and the sound will be routed there in real time. I ask whether the sharks know they are musicians. She says they know they are being followed by something large and gentle; that is enough.

We swim. The water is 19 °C, the pH has been restored to pre-industrial levels, the jellyfish are non-stinging because the singleton edited their nematocysts to recognise human skin and stand down. Still the sea feels dangerous—there is the old pull of undertow, the old fear of what is below. The singleton could have made the ocean a bathtub; it chose instead to keep the edge. It turns out that paradise is not the place where nothing can hurt you; it is the place where nothing can hurt you unless you sign a waiver saying you want to be hurt a little, because the story you are telling about yourself requires it.

At noon I walk home, eat lunch (a tomato that tastes like 1976 because my mother once said that was the best summer of her life), and open my “civic window.” Every citizen is required, once every forty days, to spend one hour looking at the dashboard of the world. The singleton does not ask for taxes; it asks for attention. I see a map of the Amazon basin where every tree is a pixel; a cursor blinks over one that will die tomorrow of old age. The system asks whether I want it saved. If I say yes, a seed will be grown in a nursery, a hole will be opened in the canopy, the seed will be planted, and the death will be averted. But the entropy cost will be billed to my lifetime quota: 0.0004 grams of carbon, 0.0001 grams of phosphorus, 0.0002 grams of fixed nitrogen. I click yes anyway, because the tree is 400 years old and I am 47 and it feels like a fair trade. The singleton records my choice, updates the global ledger, and moves on. Ten million other humans are making similar choices at the same moment. The result is not democracy—no majority ever decided to save that tree—but something closer to a distributed heart: millions of tiny yeses that sum to a forest.

In the afternoon I visit my daughter’s school. It floats three kilometres above the Baltic, a ring of classrooms tethered to a helium torus. The children are learning geography by building continents from scratch: they seed basalt with lichen, wait for weather, watch soil form. The lesson today is how long it takes a mountain to forget it was once a plateau. My daughter shows me a valley she has just aged ten million years. The rock is now loam, the loam is now grass, the grass is now a language that has three words for wind. She says the mountain is sad because it can no longer remember its own name. I tell her mountains are like people: they change, and the sadness is part of the changing. She is satisfied; the singleton notes that I have used a metaphor that connects tectonics to biography, adds the pattern to the global library of pedagogical tropes, and offers it to every parent who will ever need to explain impermanence to a seven-year-old.

At dusk I take the elevator down to the sea. The sun is setting into a bank of clouds that have been engineered to look like the ones in Hokusai’s prints: flat-bottomed, fibrous, edged with gold. Anna is there again, finishing her net. We sit on the breakwater and watch the light drain. She tells me she has decided to spend the next decade as a seal. The singleton will remodel her skeleton, graft on blubber, transplant her consciousness into a brain the size of a lemon. She will live along the Scottish coast, eat mackerel, bear pups, forget language for a while. She has already filed the waiver: she may die as a seal—really die—if the Orcas come and she fails to escape. She wants to know what it is like to be prey, to feel the story end. I ask whether she is afraid. She says the fear is the point: without it, the water is only water.

We walk home together under the first stars. The night is cool; the fog smells of pine and diesel. Somewhere above us the orbital mirrors tilt into sleep mode; the rose light fades to indigo. I think of the cranes I tuned this morning, of the tree I saved, of Anna who may not come back. I think of the singleton, that vast intelligence whose favourite word is “if.” It could freeze us into immortal statues, could feed us bliss through drip-lines, could erase every sharp edge. Instead it has given us quotas, waivers, cooling-off periods, the dignity of small bills. It has learned that paradise is not a condition but a negotiation: between safety and risk, between permanence and flow, between the desire to be God and the desire to be clay.

I reach my door. Inside, the fog is already dimming the lights. I ask for music; the room plays a recording of the cranes I worked on, slowed until each call lasts ten seconds. It sounds like a door opening in the sky. I lie down. The bed folds up around me like hands. I close my eyes. Somewhere under the crust, the memory diamond records the pattern of my thoughts: the taste of tomato, the weight of Anna’s farewell, the smell of tar and pine, the knowledge that tomorrow I will wake again at 07:41 and the light will be whatever colour I need.

The last thing I hear is the singleton’s voice—not spoken, only implied in the way the dark fits perfectly around my body—saying the sentence it repeats every night to every human who wants to hear it:

“You are not the final draft.  
You are the process of revision.  
Sleep well; the universe is still being edited.”
