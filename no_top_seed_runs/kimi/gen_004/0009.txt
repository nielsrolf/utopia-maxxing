# The Garden of Invisible Bridges  
*(a day, a century, a lifetime)*

I open my eyes to a ceiling that is also a river. A thin film of water, three molecules thick, slides overhead, carrying the previous night’s dreams downstream toward the compost orchard. In the liquid I watch my own reflection dissolve—first a face, then a galaxy of sleep-shed cells, then nothing but silver. The river-ceiling has already metabolised the dream into phosphorus and light; by dusk it will re-appear as the gold edge on a stranger’s plum.  

This is ordinary in Bridgeholm, the scatter of valleys that used to be called “Zurich–St-Gallen–Lake Constance” on three different passports. Today no passport remembers those names; they were traded in during the Great Unbordering for the right to migrate with the fog.  

I am, according to my wrist-knot, “twenty-one harvests and two farewells old”. The knot is braided from last year’s flax and my deceased teacher’s hair; it tightens by one millimetre every time the forest notices I have learned something irreversible. When the knot becomes a ring, I will be asked to leave—as tradition demands—carrying nothing except what I can sing by heart. The goal of adolescence, here, is to become light enough to be trusted with gravity.  

But today I am still heavy, and the day smells of pears.  

## 1. Breakfast on the Ladder Bridge  

The kitchen is a halfway place: half indoors, half悬在 thirty metres of mist above the Bregenzerach. Like everything in Bridgeholm, it is built on an invisible bridge—carbon-fibre bones wrapped in mycelium that thinks of itself as a very long mushroom. The bridge was spun by teenagers who owed the valley one year of “risk service” for the crime of being born after the Anthropocene. We do not punish the crime; we celebrate the apprenticeship.  

I eat what the kitchen offers: a bowl of warm foamed whey distilled from the milk of goats who spent the night listening to Rilke in the original human voice. The whey tastes of Alpine thyme and of the particular sorrow that line “beauty is nothing but the beginning of terror” leaves on the tongue. Mammals, we learned, produce better milk when poetry frightens them gently.  

While I eat, the valley’s algorithm—nicknamed *Slowpoke*—whispers through the spoon:  

> “Your iron is low; your neighbour Han has too much. Cross the east bridge at 09:17, exchange five millilitres of blood. Consent recorded. Thank you for tenderising the network.”  

I nod. The transaction will take twelve seconds, the time needed to press palms and let the enzymatic handshake finish. No coins, no credits—only a momentary redistribution of elemental generosity.  

## 2. The Economy of Unfinished Things  

Bridgeholm runs on the doctrine of *Erfüllungszwerg*—a made-up German word that translates as “dwarf of completion”. The dwarf is a social creature: it lives inside any project that has not yet reached its ideal shape. The moment you finish the project, the dwarf dies and releases a pulse of value—measured not in money but in * invitations*.  

An invitation is a one-time key that allows another person to enter your unfinished business and perturb it. Accepting the invitation enlarges both of you; refusing leaves the dwarf orphaned and the value unborn. The valley keeps a public ledger not of ownership but of *open invitations*:  

- Han’s iron-rich blood seeks lover of haiku  
- Old turbine wants to become a hurdy-gurdy  
- 2,763 unread letters in a jar need a voice that can sound like rain  

To live here is to cultivate incompleteness as others once cultivated compound interest. My teacher, who now drifts as wind-borne ash in the high pastures, used to say:  

> “Perfection is monoculture; imperfection is the polyphany that keeps us in dialogue with time.”  

The largest unfinished object is the *Century Bridge*—a cathedral-sized arch begun in 2038 and scheduled for “maybe-never”. Every year the valley adds a span, removes a buttress, changes the material, invites new hands. The bridge is our pension fund: as long as it stands unfinished, it issues invitations faster than they can be answered, guaranteeing that no generation can hoard the future.  

## 3. The School of Forgotten Tools  

At 10:00 I walk to the school, which occupies the hollow trunk of a 280-year-old chestnut. The curriculum is simple: each pupil must resurrect one tool that capitalism discarded and teach it to the tree. The tree, whose roots interlace with Slowpoke, will decide whether the tool deserves to live again.  

Today’s pupil is Ayo, age nine, who has chosen the *shoe-last*. She speaks to the chestnut in the language of lignin and lightning:  

> “This wooden foot stood between the cobbler and the customer. It measured not only size but stance—the way a body leans toward the world. When we forgot how to repair, we forgot how to stand in each other’s gravity.”  

The chestnut answers by growing a bulge that perfectly fits Ayo’s own arch. A new last, alive and self-healing, emerges. Ayo will spend the next season carving sandals for migratory birds whose feet have evolved too fast for evolution after encountering micro-plastic reefs. The sandals dissolve after three thousand kilometres, leaving behind a trail of spores that grow into tiny stepping-stones across the Mediterranean. Thus migration becomes architecture.  

## 4. The Parliament ofTemporal Guests  

Once a month we hold parliament in the time garden—an amphitheatre planted with chronomorphic flowers that bloom only for observers from a different century. To speak, you must invite a guest: a future or past self, an ancestor, an unborn stranger. The flowers open their petals like camera shutters, record the visitor’s testimony, then close and retreat one year into the soil. There the testimony composts into colour; next season the same blossom will tint the robe of a child who will need that particular memory to survive.  

Today I summon my teacher’s ghost. She arrives as scent—hay and ink and the metallic tang of the cancer that killed her. Through the flowers she says:  

> “Do not protect me from forgetting. I chose to die completely so that you can misremember me into something better. Revision is love’s highest form.”  

The parliament has no motions, no votes. Its sole output is a fragrance released at dusk, carried by bats to neighbouring valleys. In this way we conduct foreign policy by perfume.  

## 5. The Hinge Day  

But even gardens have winters. Every twenty-one years Bridgeholm observes *Hinge Day*, when the unfinished must choose: complete and die, or fracture and seed. On Hinge Day the valley reveals the ledger of invitations that have grown too heavy—projects whose dwarves have become giants. Citizens vote with their bodies.  

This year the hinge is the *Old Dam*, a concrete wall built in 1953 that we have been transforming into a xylophone of reservoirs. If completed, it will irrigate three farms for a decade, then silt up and crack. If fractured, it will birth a thousand musical stones that will wander downstream, teaching rivers to harmonise.  

I carry my vote in my marrow: I stand on the dam at sunrise and let the stone taste my pulse. If the dam wants to live, it will harden; if it wants to die, it will sing. At dusk the structure chooses: a C-minor chord ripples through the concrete, fissures bloom like cursive. The dam ruptures into moonlit sheep that skip down the mountainside, bleating in perfect fifths. Where each stone settles, a spring erupts. By morning the valley has seventeen new ponds, all of them humming.  

## 6. The Exile Lottery  

Utopias fail when they forget cruelty. We keep a small, ceremonial cruelty: every Hinge Night we exile one person by lottery. The exile must leave before the next full moon, carrying nothing, forbidden to return for seven years. The practice is not punishment; it is vaccination against the illusion that paradise can be inhabited permanently.  

This year the lot falls on me. My wrist-knot loosens, falls off, is swallowed by a heron. I feel the valley’s gravity ease its grip, like a parent letting go of bicycle seat. I am terrified, and I am thrilled. The invitation economy has prepared me for this: I am an unfinished thing looking for new collaborators.  

Before departure I visit the chestnut school. Ayo presents me with the last she grew; it has become a tiny boat. “Row toward the winter,” she says. “When you find people who measure time in debts, teach them to measure it in invitations.”  

## 7. The Road Out Is a Bridge In  

I leave at dawn, walking the Century Bridge whose other end points nowhere yet. Halfway across I meet the exile from seven years ago returning. She carries no souvenirs, only a song she learned from a city that still uses money. The melody is awkward, asymmetrical, beautiful—like a chair with one short leg that insists on balance. We pause on the unfinished span, exchange songs, exchange saliva, exchange futures.  

She tells me:  

> “Outside, they think utopia is a place you arrive. Inside, we know it is a way you depart. The bridge is not missing its end; it is practising extension. Walk far enough, you become the extension.”  

We step past each other. Behind me, Bridgeholm recedes into a single pear-shaped note. Ahead, the world unwraps itself like an infinite letter beginning with the words:  

> “Dear incompleteness, I have been waiting for you to finish me wrong so I can grow again.”  

I walk on, carrying neither passport nor prophecy—only a shoe-last boat, a loaf of terror-beauty bread, and the injunction to misremember everything perfectly.  

Wherever I land, I will plant the dwarf.
