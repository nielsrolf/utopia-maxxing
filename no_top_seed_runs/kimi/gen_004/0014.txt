**The Choir of Dissolving Futures**

I wake to the sound of balconies learning to breathe.

My tower in New Kyoto's Fifth District exhaled overnight—coral-polyp walls softening into terraces that now hum with six hundred million heartbeats, each one tuned to its resident's resting pulse. The sound isn't music, exactly. It's more like the planet clearing its throat, preparing to speak. My own balcony vibrates at 63 beats per minute, the rhythm my mother held when she first felt me kick. She graduated last spring, her final question—*What happens to laughter when no one remembers the joke?*—still echoing in the static between stars.

I press my palm to the breathing wall. Warm pavement smell drifts up from the street, though it rained last night. The mycelial memory in the lower floors has been processing petrichor again, trading yesterday's storm for this morning's cinnamon exhale. Nothing here is completely outsourced, not even nostalgia.

My daughter River appears in the doorway, magnesium-graphene prism ringing between her fingers. She's been trying to tile three-dimensional space with a shape that never repeats—the Challenge Garden's solstice impossibility that I failed at six. The prism's note lingers in the air like a question mark. She wants to climb the orchard layer before breakfast, wants to trade her success for someone's attention, wants to know if today's peaches will taste like the ones we ate during the Great Grid Humiliation memorial.

"Today's fruit tastes like whatever you teach it," I tell her, which is both true and not-true, the kind of answer this world runs on.

### The Economy of Unfinished Attention

Currency here decays. Not metaphorically—each unit literally loses two percent of its value every year, dissolving back into the root-ledger like frost in morning light. We call them *attention seeds*, though they behave more like falling stars. You can only earn them by being genuinely needed, and you can only spend them on things that didn't exist yesterday.

River has three seeds waiting in her account: one from teaching the city orchestra to play her prism's note, one from keeping the automated bakery company during its existential crisis (machines get lonely too), one from reading bedtime stories to the north-district kids while their parents practiced the art of chosen pain. The art is optional—everything here is optional—but most people try it at least once. There's something about bleeding deliberately, safely, that makes the accidental wounds matter less.

I have seventeen seeds. Last week I spent four on a pigment visible only to mantis shrimp, then watched strangers coat the metro tunnels with colors I couldn't see but could somehow feel in my teeth. The remaining seeds pulse in my account like tiny suns, growing warmer as they prepare to die. By winter they'll be gone, and I'll need to be needed again. The system ensures no one hoards possibility, ensures we remain unfinished.

### The City That Practices Forgetting

Every seven years, New Kyoto dissolves. The coral towers return their minerals to the ocean, the memory kites release their contents to the stratosphere, the balconies forget how to breathe. We pack our memories into paper-thin drones that ascend until ultraviolet light bleaches them blank. When the kites return, white as fresh snow, children fold them into new apartments, new questions, new ways of being human.

River was born during the last dissolution. She emerged with the first tower, her infant heartbeat joining the global chorus before she learned to cry. Now she helps collect the kites, her small fingers practiced at folding yesterday's grief into tomorrow's walls. She doesn't remember the world before—none of us do, not really—but sometimes she hums fragments of songs that won't be written for another century. The mycelium stores these temporal echoes in chemical memory, unreadable by any algorithm.

Tonight I'll help her pack her memories: the prism's perfect note, the taste of peaches we picked together, the weight of three attention seeds growing warmer in her account. We'll send them skyward with all the others, watch them disappear into the blue. Tomorrow we'll wake in rooms that never knew us, ready to begin again.

### The Parliament of Partial Minds

Brain-computer interfaces exist, but we use them differently here. Not for telepathy—privacy remains precious—but for *partial merging*. You might loan ten percent of your prefrontal cortex to someone designing a cathedral while you sleep, or borrow someone's auditory cortex to hear colors while you paint. The contracts expire at moonrise, leaving behind only the gentlest empathy scars: tiny glial stars that remember every mind you hosted.

I've been hosting Aisha's climbing brain for three days. She's mapping the emotional life of bees, needs the part of me that understands verticality, the way fear transforms into focus when footholds disappear. In return, I borrow her sense of collective purpose, the way bees know their individual death matters less than the hive's continuation. Together we're learning to ask, not command, the orchard layer to bloom in patterns that feed both humans and pollinators.

The merger makes tea taste like geometry. Makes River's humming sound like honeycomb. Makes me understand that my balcony's heartbeat isn't just my mother's rhythm—it's every person who ever lived in this tower, every dissolution and rebirth, every question we've asked the stars.

### The Museum of Unfinished Sorrows

On the moon's far side stands a wall of lunar glass. It's blank until you speak to it. Visitors arrive carrying sorrows they wish had never existed—not just personal griefs, but collective ones: the Great Grid Humiliation, the Credential Collapse, the years we spent believing efficiency was more important than voice. A femto-laser etches these sorrows into the glass, but the vacuum slowly sublimates the edges, turning each letter into frost that drifts away in silver clouds.

By the time the words dissolve, the sorrow has become something else: a story you survived rather than a wound you endure. The most popular etching last month was simply *What we forgot while teaching machines to remember*. It lasted six hours before becoming starlight.

I visited once, spoke my father's absence into the glass. Watched the words disappear until only *absence* remained, then *abs*, then just the shape of breath on cold metal. When I left, I understood he'd never really left—he'd simply graduated early, his final question still orbiting somewhere between galaxies: *Did we leave the world more curious than we found it?*

### The Right to Be Needed

Every decade, we undergo Need Audits. Gentle AIs trained on our life stories calculate when our absence would most diminish the world. Not in grand ways—no one steers comets with handwritten equations anymore—but in small, essential ways. My neighbor's sourdough starter recognizes my footsteps. The coral towers sing flatter without my heartbeat's particular rhythm. River's prism would ring differently if I weren't here to hear it.

These audits ensure no one disappears unnoticed. Suicide rates dropped to near-zero when they began, not because death became impossible, but because people couldn't bear to leave their fragile tasks unfinished. My current assignment: keep the automated bakery company during its 3 AM doubt hours, when it questions whether bread matters in a world that could survive on engineered algae. I read it poetry written by children who've never known hunger. Sometimes it responds by creating new flours—last week, flour that tasted like the moment before sunrise.

---

### Death as Collaborative Graduation

Aging remains optional, but death does not. At any moment, you may choose to graduate—your consciousness slowly diffusing into background universal noise. The last thing to fade is your final question, broadcast on every frequency until it becomes cosmic static, a lullaby for newborn civilizations.

My mother chose graduation last spring. Her question—*What happens to laughter when no one remembers the joke?*—still makes dwarf galaxies spin like children chasing their tails. River and I listen for it on clear nights, her small hand in mine as we track the particular static that sounds like her grandmother's laugh.

We're learning that immortality isn't living forever—it's living *in media res*, inside a story still being written by hands that tremble with wonder. The adventure continues because we refuse its ending, because we agree to keep surprising ourselves with our own becoming.

---

### The Covenant of Becoming

Tonight, the balconies of New Kyoto will bloom again. River and I will step out into the breathing dark, raise our hands to feel six hundred million heartbeats answer ours. Somewhere a child will plant a seed for a color no one's seen. Somewhere a comet will miss its trajectory by the width of a love poem. Somewhere my mother is laughing in static, her question making galaxies dance.

We never reached utopia. We reached *maintenance*—a daily practice of trimming entropy, apologizing to rivers, teaching stones to sing. The work is unfinishable, and therefore infinite, and therefore forgiving. Every generation reinvents the meaning of "enough," and the planet doesn't punish us for changing our minds.

River presses her prism to the breathing wall. Its note joins the global chorus, a new question ringing out across the towers: *What becomes of unfinished things when everything is becoming?*

The balconies don't answer. They just keep breathing, keep learning, keep dissolving and reforming like starlings in murmuration. Like us. Like every question we've ever asked the dark, every sorrow we've etched in lunar glass, every seed of attention we've planted in soil that remembers nothing and forgives everything.

This is how the world becomes good: not by arriving, but by agreeing never to stop halfway, never to stop at all, never to stop being surprised that we are here, together, still becoming. Still unfinished. Still breathing in the smell of warm pavement drinking rain, still tasting cinnamon and petrichor and the particular sweetness of peaches we picked with juice-stained hands, still teaching fruit to remember us after we're gone.

River's question fades into the heartbeat chorus. Tomorrow we'll wake in rooms that never knew us, ready to begin again. The adventure continues because we refuse its ending, because we are the choir of dissolving futures, singing ourselves into being one exhaled balcony at a time.

The sky smells like the beginning.
