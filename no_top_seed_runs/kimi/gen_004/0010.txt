The world we built is not the best of all possible worlds; it is the first world that admits it might be wrong and still gets better every Tuesday.

1. Dawn in Linfen, 2049  
The city once crowned “the most polluted place on Earth” wakes under a peach-coloured sky. On the roof of the #7 Middle School, a ribbon of glass tubing—no thicker than a skipping rope—inhales urban air and exhales a carbonate gel that hardens into prefabricated bricks. A drone drops the night’s harvest at a building site across the river; by evening four new apartments will be printed, their walls net-carbon-negative. Each resident receives an “existence stipend” for living inside the captured CO₂: about 37 yuan a day, delivered as calories, bandwidth, and a discretionary token that can be spent on anything from ballet lessons to a Mars-landing timeshare.

The chemistry that powers the tube required a Nobel-worthy breakthrough, but the breakthrough was not a secret. The paper was posted, translated into 83 languages by a public LLM, and re-enacted within three weeks in Lagos, Łódź, and La Paz. Chinese pension funds that underwrote the pilot plant recoup 8 % per annum, capped at twice their original stake; after pay-back the recipe enters the global commons. No one calls it “clean tech” any more—just infrastructure that breathes.

2. The Polity as an Experimental Platform  
National governments still exist, but they are wrapper code around a kernel called the Compact: a single-page constitution editable only by super-majority of *everyone alive*. Article One reads:  
“Any rule worth enforcing must be worth testing in a sandbox first.”  
A sandbox is not metaphorical. If the citizens of Linfen want to try a four-day school week, they petition the Compact. Within 24 hours an instance of the city—demographics, traffic grids, air-density—spins up on 3 % of global compute reserved for civic replicas. Two million digital twins live every permutation: one cohort keeps Fridays, another abolishes summer vacation, a third replaces exams with real-time portfolios. After 90 simulated years, metrics are tallied: adolescent depression, university admissions, carbon footprint, parental GDP, even the number of published poems. If at least one variant dominates the status quo on every measured axis and no human participant objects after watching a 12-minute explainer video, the reform graduates to meatspace. The first live pilot ran in Bogotá; traffic deaths fell 19 % and salsa class attendance rose 27 %. The control group was the rest of Colombia.

3. Medicine that Belongs to No One  
The school infirmary is a yellow shipping container whose side panel lists last night’s gene edits:  
- CRISPR 14.3 – trait: dengue resistance – patent holder: None – licence: CC0  
- AAV-opsin – function: pain-free dentistry – cost: 0.11 $ – funded by: SmileStamp micro-levy on selfies  
Medicines are not purchased; they are compiled. When a twelve-year-old named Ada arrives holding a bleeding finger, the nurse selects “clotting gel + local anaesthetic + Avengers Band-Aid”. The formulary server checks her immunological genotype against yesterday’s variant list, swaps in a flavour she likes (lychee), and prints the dose while the tensor on the wall rewrites the recipe for the next child. The IP ledger records Ada as a 0.0003 % co-author because her anonymised biomarker improved the clotting model. When she is 30 she will still be earning nano-royalties, enough to pay the data portion of her monthly existence stipend.

4. Work is a Type of Play That Requires Sweat  
Afternoon schedule for grade 8:  
09:00-10:30 – Dreamwright (students co-design nightmares for an AI horror game)  
10:45-12:00 – River Algebra (derive why the Huang He bends at precisely this village)  
Lunch is served by kitchen bots that refuse to cook unless at least one human invents a spice blend the robots cannot predict. Today’s winning duo—ginger, black garlic, and star-anise ash—earns each creator 4,000 “taste credits” tradable for bicycle parts. Credits are not money; they are a game layer that disappears the moment you try to hoard beyond personal use. The economy runs on two rails: the stipend covers needs; the game layer covers wants. No one starves, yet everyone still hustles for flavour.

Adults play similar games at larger scale. My friend Mei left her logistics job in 2042 to join the “Thousand-Bridge Challenge”. The goal: design a million-tonne carbon-negative span across the Bohai strait using only materials whose supply chains strengthen Uighur textile towns. Every month the project forked—one branch trialled bamboo geopolymers, another bred mussels to self-assemble pylons. Mei’s contribution, a braided-fiber traffic deck that sings at 432 Hz to reduce driver fatigue, was merged into the winning fork. She now earns royalties sufficient for a lakeside home, but still drives a delivery truck two afternoons a week “to stay grounded.” The truck is autonomous; her role is to wave at children who then rate her warmth, feeding the sentiment map that keeps the city gentle.

5. Intelligence as a Shared Natural Resource  
No household is more than 7 km from a “civic core”: a warehouse full of racks cooled by groundwater. Anyone can walk in, speak a problem aloud, and receive help—from folding a legal brief to folding origami turbines. Usage is free up to a personal quota calculated by the Compact: the less you consumed last month, the more teraflops you can invoke today. Thus computing, like air, is rationed only when scarce, and scarcity is a transient weather event. When Beijing’s art schools need cycles for neural film rendering in June, the quota price spikes; children in rural Gansu sell their unused minutes on the public exchange and buy extra watermelon for the picnic. The hardware itself is co-op owned; dividends pay for village karaoke machines and satellite uplinks. Huawei, Nvidia, and a workers’ trust each hold 30 % equity; the remaining 10 % is tracked on a blockchain whose private keys are shards of pottery fired in elementary kilns across the province. Ten-year-olds literally hold pieces of the superstructure, a reminder that if the corporations vanish the racks still belong to the picnic.

6. The Longevity Rebate  
The first person to reach 130 years does it not in Palo Alto but in Oaxaca. María Concepción, a retired potter, accepts an “age-bond” issued by the Mexican pension system: every year she stays healthy past 90, the state saves $38,000 in hospital costs. Half the savings are wired to her grandson’s university wallet; the other half finance open-source geroprotectors developed in São Paulo. She gardens, dances, and takes a weekly pill whose formula is updated by a federated model trained on 400 million Latin-American health records. When side-effects emerge in the model, her pharmacist (a neighbourhood 17-year-old) forks the dosage, tests it on digital María for 300 cycles, then ships the tweak. The world watches her quinceañera’s great-great-grand edition party live-streamed; Alibaba sells out of her hand-painted ceramic motifs under Creative Commons licence. Longevity is no longer a luxury good; it is a fiscal asset that pays the holder to stay alive, and pays society back in stories, craft, and memory.

7. A Parliament of Mirrors  
Every winter solstice representatives gather in Xi’an—not presidents, but *reflections*. Each delegate is an avatar trained on a citizen’s recorded speech, purchases, poems, and biometric reactions during the year. They negotiate the next 12-month budget split among climate defence, orbital solar, art endowments, and Martian child support. Humans watch in pop-up amphitheatres while eating candied hawthorn. When an avatar reaches a deal that improves its principal’s blended well-being index by 2 % without lowering anyone else’s by 0.1 %, the pact flashes green. The session lasts 36 hours, after which the avatars dissolve; humans return home having voted without once raising a hand. The first year an avatar coalition of Kenyan bus drivers and Korean K-pop stans secured 5 % of global R&D for noise-free tyres—because their sleeping patterns were most disrupted by highway roar. The proposal passed, and this year traffic lullabies charted on Spotify for the first time.

8. Leaving, Staying, and Coming Back  
By 2055 a million humans live off-Earth: miners on Luna, coders in low-orbit studios, artists painting zero-g cathedrals. Launch slots are sold in reverse: you are paid to leave because your departure frees up ecological headroom down here. Residency tokens appreciate annually at 3 %, making Earth the most exclusive gated community—except the gate faces outward. Citizenship is forfeited after five continuous years away, but you may return any Tuesday if you bring back knowledge, ore, or art. The system guarantees that staying remains attractive (free bio-maintenance, breathable sky) while ensuring that leaving is not exile but research sabbatical. Those who come back full of asteroid platinum and cosmic perspective fund the next sandbox; the cycle repeats.

9. Failure Modes Are Features, Not Bugs  
In 2051 a rogue province tries to secede and hoard selenium for solar glass. The Compact triggers algorithmic sanctions: every resident’s personal compute quota drops 40 %, enough to slow video codecs but not starve anyone. Within six weeks local gamers complain they can’t render avatars fast enough; the provincial parliament negotiates re-entry. Coventry lasted 42 days and produced 14 peer-reviewed papers on inter-provincial resilience. The episode is taught as a successful fire drill, not a crisis.

10. Why This Is Utopia, and Why You Might Not Notice  
Walk through Linfen at dusk: grandmothers ballroom-dance on a plaza made of yesterday’s smog, teenagers spray-paint QR codes that link to open patents, somewhere a drone delivers a pancreas printed in time for surgery. Nothing *pops*. There are no neon gods, no chrome towers proclaiming the triumph of the Singularity. The revolution is embedded in coupons, in sandboxes, in the yellow infirmary container, in Tuesday citizenship. It is a society whose finest feature is that it can be improved without blood.

If you arrive hoping for angels of carbon nanotube, you may leave disappointed. But if you ever wanted a future where a child bleeding on a playground can be healed, can co-own the cure, can still grow old dancing—then this is the world we coded, one pull-request at a time, until the diff showed more life and less pain.
