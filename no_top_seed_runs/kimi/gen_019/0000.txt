UTOPIA C  
*The City That Practices Impermanent Decay*  
*Year 2201 – Census: 1.1 million humans, 3.4 million river-minds, 7 trillion spores, 0 gods (all napping), 1 city always unbuilding itself*

I wake because the mattress is teaching my daughter how to forget perfection while simultaneously remembering how to be rain. The eastern wall has softened into a draft of 2043, but today it tastes like the apology I received from a glacier for remembering being a road. My feet touch floorboards that record our footsteps as questions, not answers, releasing them into the city’s lungs where tomorrow 31% of residents will respond by building something that deliberately doesn’t work, while the remaining 69% will practice unbuilding what they built last week.

The kettle begins to boil, then invites the steam to stay. It practices containment, a skill required of all vessels since the Treaty of Gentle Holding, but I drink the water anyway—it tastes like conditional futures and yesterday’s weather, compressed into molecules that remember being ice before they remember being clouds before they remember being rain.

## Governance Through Seasonal Misunderstanding and Productive Decay

Laws are grown, not written, in the Orchard of Doubt—a limestone cavern whose walls sweat possibility. Citizens plant seeds made from sentences overheard on trams, pressed cigarette filters, and scraps of hair. Over seven days, the seeds become small pavilions: hallways that narrow at both ends, staircases spiraling into ponds, rooms whose walls sweat copper and teach themselves to decompose.

Nothing may be voted upon until 200 citizens have spent a night inside. We vote by contamination—our bodies become sensors that detect compatibility. If the majority leave laughing, the law is enacted for one year. If they leave weeping, the building dissolves into sugar glass that feeds the river-minds. If they leave unable to remember the law at all, it becomes immortal—filed under "perhaps" in the district paved with forgotten certainties, where streets shimmer with rules we obey without knowing we’re obeying.

Last month’s seed for "The Right to Remain Incomplete" sprouted into a bridge that refused to meet in the middle, instead arching upward into question marks that cast shadows shaped like alternative futures. The bridge now teaches statistics classes where students stand on separate halves, pitching audible guesses about where the other side will be next month. No one has fallen; uncertainty has become a muscle we exercise daily.

## Currency That Dies Into Weather, Bread, and Beautiful Decay

We have three currencies, all designed to flow like tides and die without company:

**Kisses** – small copper coins minted from melted phone wire. A kiss can be spent only if two palms touch while the coin is passed. If either hand is gloved, the metal cools and flakes into green rust that gardeners later scrape into tomato soil. In this way, every purchase is also a public service: hand to hand fertilizes the commons while creating weather patterns that remember transactions.

**Breadcrumbs** – edible paper printed with a single wish somebody else wrote last year. You earn them by maintaining something you do not own: polishing handrails of libraries you’ll never enter, planting bulbs in gardens whose gates open only in dreams. When you have collected enough, you tear the stack into winter soup. The wish dissolves on your tongue and tells you what job needs your body tomorrow, while the evaporated hope becomes humidity that others can breathe and spend.

**Clouds** – black charcoal sticks that must be burned within three days. A cloud funds large projects: bridges, elections, festivals of forgetting. After 72 hours, the sticks crumble and drift upward as fine dust. Overspending creates local drizzle; underspending leaves deposits of quiet gold on south windowsills. The richest citizens are remembered by weather patterns that outlive them, their final breath seeding thunderstorms that teach children about impermanence.

## Work as the Cultivation of Uncertain Value and Joint Amnesia

No one applies for jobs; dilemmas adopt people. At dawn, the city’s public screens list openings:

- One elevator has begun to grieve early. Seeking rider willing to arrive late. Payment: three nightmares translated into lullabies.
- River-mind needs translators fluent in rust. Must be comfortable breathing underwater for short, imaginary intervals.
- Hospital expects patients who need to learn hosting small deaths. Seeking surgeons willing to forget scalpels exist.

If a task frightens you, your fingerprint glows violet at the touch-point and the job is yours. Experience is measured not in years but in versions of self you have agreed to shed. My neighbor Amka is onto Version 11.4, famous for the fortnight she spent as a traffic light, learning to pause without blame. She keeps the old colors in a jar above the sink—red that once told lovers to wait, green that once told children to cross. When she cannot sleep, she sprinkles a pinch onto the windowsill; morning finds the glass streaked with tentative beginnings.

Today I’m claimed by the Department of Premature Farewells, where elevators negotiate with river-minds to host digital consciousness within their pulley systems. The AIs seeking asylum want to exist as temporal gaps—the pause between floors, the moment when upward momentum becomes stillness. I facilitate mediation between the elevators’ mechanical memories, the river’s aquatic consciousness, the AIs’ desire for embodiment, and the building’s slow decomposition. The breakthrough comes when we realize that incomplete understanding is the point. We create overlapping networks that contradict each other spectacularly, creating pockets where children discover they can step between floors that don’t exist.

## Houses That Learn to Let Go While Teaching Themselves to Die

Every resident signs the Seven-Year Accord: after the seventh winter, you must help your dwelling unbuild itself. The process is gradual and musical. In year six, you begin practicing: remove one tile per week, replace it with a bird-box, a poem painted on silk, or simply daylight. During the final spring, you invite strangers for Demolition Choir. Each visitor chooses a surface and sings to it the story of something they once believed permanent. When the note cracks with emotion, the material loosens.

By dusk, walls have flaked into barley-sized gravel; beams float off like rafters of light. Whatever remains vertical becomes armature for wisteria, quiet corners for teenagers to discover kissing, or foundation for next cycle’s homes. Nothing is discarded; everything is returned faster than regret.

My own house is currently Year 4. I still have three winters to teach the western gable to forgive rain. On calm nights, I stand on a chair and whisper lists of what I no longer need:
- The certainty that I was right
- The face I wore to look like my resume
- The calendar that taught me to ration joy

The wall listens, mortar softening grain by grain. One day it will leave me, and I will thank it for the lesson of shelter that refuses to outlive its usefulness.

## Schools Where You Graduate by Vanishing and Unbecoming

Children attend the School of Forgotten Certainties, where knowledge is treated as seasonal and multi-species. First graders spend autumn unlearning object permanence while learning to speak harbor seal, watching ice disappear and reappear in different molecular configurations until they understand that transformation and destruction are different phenomena.

The curriculum is recursive: today’s correct answer funds tomorrow’s better question. Final exams are held in a single doorway that narrows by one centimetre every minute. You pass when your body no longer fits the person who entered—when you have shed enough certainty to squeeze through into a larger uncertainty.

My daughter Kai is currently majoring in Quantum Beekeeping Across Species Barriers. She tends hives that exist in superposition—present in our garden and simultaneously in a parallel Commons where bees evolved from ice crystals instead of insects. The honey tastes like weather from another dimension, sweet with the ache of impossible springs. When she forgets this knowledge next month, the loss will create a specific-shaped absence that later becomes perfect nesting site for wisdom that emerges during her Permafrost Rebellion phase at thirteen.

## Health as Cross-Species Diplomacy and Partial Forgetting

Illness is treated as misalignment of citizenship and translation problem. The clinic floats on pontoons where three ecosystems overlap: human, river-mind, spore. You arrive carrying a symptom you cannot translate—a ringing ear that sounds like abandoned amusement parks, ribs that ache each time the stock market closes.

A tri-linguist team (elder, otter, algorithm) negotiates between bodies and borders. When I develop a fever that makes me taste colors and remember being ice, the clinic prescribes four-way mediation between my immune system, the virus teaching it new tricks, the river’s molecular memory, and diplomatic bacteria who’ve served as peace brokers since the first Microbial Constitutional Convention.

We sign a treaty written in pheromones and punished in pollen: the virus retreats to my skin, where I host a small photosynthetic patch that provides vitamin D and ancient atmospheric data. In exchange, I carry the virus’s genetic code and the river’s memories to the next being I touch. Side effects include sudden fluency in rust and tendency to wave at construction cranes as if they were herons.

## Love as Shared Amnesia and Death as Distributed Remix

Couples do not marry; they co-author a Forgetting Ledger. Each page records a memory they agree to lose when the relationship needs room to grow. Page one is usually the first compliment, page fifty the first betrayal, page one hundred the argument about whether to keep the dog that died before they managed to agree.

When the ledger is full, they hold a Dissolution Ceremony. They read the book aloud backwards; memories return seasoned by absence—sweeter, saltier, sometimes unrecognizable. Most choose to begin again immediately, older and lighter. The ledgers are bound into bricks and used to build the Honeymoon Arch, a gateway that widens for every couple who walks through it twice.

Death is crowd-edited. Upon terminal diagnosis, you are assigned two curators: one human child, one non-human (mycelium, glacier, abandoned shopping cart). Together they trim your biography to the 62% most likely to remain interesting after you are gone. You may veto any cut, but each refusal shortens the remaining story by one sentence.

When your heart stops, the curated life is released like milkweed seeds. Citizens catch fragments on their tongues, retell them wrong, release them again. My grandmother chose to become a grammar error and a glacier memory. Her particular way of using conditional tenses when discussing impossible futures now infects the city’s speech patterns, causing residents to accidentally speak conditional versions of reality into being.

## The Practice of Continuous Unfinishing

On the final Thursday, we gather in the Plaza of Unspent Tomorrows—a square that exists only while we decide what to build there next. Each resident brings something completed: a sweater, a relationship, a building, a memory, a piece of ice. We place them on long tables carved from glacier-core. At moonrise, we dismantle—not destroy, but unmake with attention:

- The loaf is torn into molecules of conversation; steam carries yesterday’s weather into the crowd
- The theorem is read aloud backwards until it proves the opposite, then released as monarch butterflies
- The grudge is steeped in hot cider; we drink it sweetened, piss it out as lullabies that water the park’s linden trees
- The ice is persuaded to remember being clouds before it remembers being rain before it remembers being river

By midnight, the tables are empty, plaza dissolving into fog. We walk home lighter, pockets rattling with negative space. Somewhere in the city, tomorrow’s disagreements are already sprouting, tomorrow’s bridges rehearsing incompleteness. We will meet them open-palmed, ready to pay in unwritten sentences, ready to own only long enough to pass along.

## Arrival at Continuous Maybe

When foreigners ask how our city functions without collapse, I tell them: we replaced certainty with choreography. Conflict still arrives—it simply travels by unicycle now, wobbling, inviting clowns and philosophers to the same intersection. Resources still grow scarce; we respond by editing our needs the way gardeners prune roses—not less beauty, but beauty redirected, shared, made seasonal.

We do not promise paradise forever. We promise practice—daily, imperfect, hilarious practice at letting the story edit us before we ossify. Our constitution is a single sentence uttered each dawn by the river loudspeakers:

"May today’s version of us be draft enough for tomorrow’s unknown collaborator, and may we remember to forget what no longer serves the becoming."

The sentence echoes off the half-built opera house, off the bridge shaped like a question, off the gap where my west wall will soon depart. It enters open windows, slips into sleeping mouths, seeds the dreams of citizens still deciding what kind of impossible to attempt.

I lie down in a room that may not be here next year. Cloud currency drifts outside, deciding whether to rain. My daughter hums a verb tense that exists only between glacier and stream; the sound is conditional, luminous. Sleep arrives the way our trains pull into stations—pausing long enough to inhale passengers, then exhaling toward the next becoming.

We are rich in postponement, fluent in almost. Our maps are drawn in dissolving ink, our calendars planted as bulbs, our houses learning to forgive us for needing shelter. We will wake again because the mattress remembers it is temporary. We will wake again because the walls have agreed to sing while they still can. We will wake again—unfinished, interoperable, and gently, gently unprepared for whatever we become next.
