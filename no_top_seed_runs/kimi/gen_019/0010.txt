# The Harbour of Unfinished Becoming

The tide arrives each morning carrying yesterday's half-answers, rolling them onto the scallop-shell streets where they dissolve into possibilities we haven't yet learned to question. I live in a city that exists between states—between land and sea, between knowing and unknowing, between the person I was at sunrise and whoever I might become by dinner. We call this place simply The Harbour, though it has never been fully mapped, for cartographers here practice the art of retractable borders, drawing lines that fold themselves into paper planes and dissolve into question-mark-shaped beans wherever they land.

My dwelling breathes. Literally. The walls are knitted by limestone-eating bacteria that stiffen when we speak with too much certainty and relax when we admit confusion. This morning, my partner May confesses they've finally understood why the bread failed yesterday—too much conviction in the kneading, not enough doubt in the rising. The ceiling sighs downward, creating a cozy alcove where we breakfast under the gentle weight of humility, the city's most common seasoning.

The scallop pillow beneath my cheek has been filtering my dreams again. It extracts heavy metals—those toxic certainties that accumulate in sleep—and crystallizes them into a pearl of unmemory that drops into my palm at dawn. Today it's the size of a child's tooth, opalescent with my abandoned conviction that I need to finish what I start. I carry it to the Memory Market, where the elderly trade clearest recollections for children's unformed dreams, but also for the right to host fragments of future possibility.

The market itself exists in permanent twilight, engineered by algae that photosynthesize slower at the edges. Here, I exchange my pearl for a stranger's half-sung lullaby—she needs the fear more than I do, she explains, because she's building a boat that must never be completed. The transaction requires touch—palm to palm, past pressing against future until both become viscous with potential. I taste her lullaby: citrus and salt, the flavor of departure that hasn't quite arrived.

## The Economy of Becoming

We have currency, but it works in spirals rather than lines. Every *care-token* you earn must be spent within three days on mutual repair, or it transforms into shared atmospheric music that everyone can taste. Blush-Coins redden when you confess a belief you no longer fully hold. Children spend crimson coins on stories with missing endings; elders trade pale ones for the right to remain silent at crucial moments.

Tide-Minutes are units of time loaned by the reversible tide itself. To earn them, you must stand ankle-deep at the waterline and publicly rescind a deadline you once imposed on another living creature—your daughter's curfew, the date by which mangroves must be fully restored, the moment you promised to stop loving someone. For every rescinded deadline, the tide rolls back exactly forty-two seconds, which you may spend later to extend any moment you wish to savor.

I spend today's earnings helping Kai teach the southern wall to decompose its own concrete while simultaneously regenerating into a living bridge. The process requires excraiting patience—bacteria that eat limestone were engineered to consume only when they hear specific jazz progressions played by volunteers who've learned that grief and carbon capture require the same sustained attention. The wall thins grain by grain, its dissolved materials feeding engineered mangroves that grow upward to form new balconies, their roots creating living rooms that breathe.

## Work as Inter-species Diplomacy

Work is whatever still knows it is imaginary. Employment categories change with the harbour's pH, but today's census lists these as fully staffed:

Qualia Translators help species negotiate shared experiences across sensory boundaries while ensuring all parties maintain the right to rollback if transformation becomes colonial. Today's session involves teaching a consortium of maples, mathematicians, migrating butterflies, and retired insurance adjusters to co-design the next iteration of our transit system, with built-in reversibility clauses.

Ferry-operators navigate between two shores that have not yet drifted apart. The ferries are built from abandoned sentences; every passenger must supply a clause that begins "What if we never..." before boarding. The vessels dissolve at mid-river, depositing travelers on whichever bank needs them to doubt it most.

Archivists of postponed funerals keep the dead from finishing their deaths. They catalogue last breaths, file them under open-ended filenames ("Return_possible"), and occasionally invite the deceased to city council as moist vapor that smells of almost-forgiven regret.

When injury occurs—broken rib, shattered promise, metaphor sprained—tidal nurses administer care. Patients descend to the waterline at low tide and lie in the exposed sand. Harbour microbes rearrange themselves into Braille-like bumps that spell the name of the illness in a language the patient cannot read. As the tide returns, the writing is washed away, taking the sickness with it.

## Governance by Choir, Retractable Council, and Reversible Tide

Laws are proposed as lullabies. Any resident may compose one, but it must contain a verse you are incapable of singing without crying. The lullaby is released at dusk from a balcony overlooking the harbour. If the tide carries the melody across the breakwater intact, the law is enacted for one lunar month. If the song returns with altered lyrics, the changes are incorporated; if it never returns, the proposal is deemed to have drowned of its own gravitas.

The cranes hold veto power: if three of them lean seaward simultaneously, legislation is hoisted into the air and shaken until only the doubt remains. Statute books are printed on sugar; every winter we eat our accumulated certainty and start again.

Every forty-three days, we hold the Argument Garden—a deliberate space for productive disagreement that refuses resolution. Participants arrive carrying seeds of their most intractable positions: literally, seeds engineered to grow only when exposed to pheromones released during passionate disagreement that acknowledges the other's fundamental humanity. The garden teaches us that some disputes are too important to resolve because resolution would mean stopping the conversation that keeps us human.

## Birth, Death, and the Space Between

Prospective parents must first moor an empty skiff at the public jetty and name the boat after the one childhood fear they refuse to pass on. The skiff is left afloat for nine high tides; if it remains untaken by storm or thief, they may conceive. At birth, the infant is handed the oar of the same skiff, now warped and salt-bleached, as proof that fear can be seaworthy.

Death is announced by the reversible tide flowing backward for exactly thirteen heartbeats. Citizens gather at the waterline holding objects that outlived the deceased: a house-key, a half-knitted scarf, the password to a forgotten account. One by one the objects are set adrift. If any item returns before the tide turns again, the death is declared provisional; the person's name is spoken only in questions for one year, after which they may choose to complete the process or apply for extension.

The oldest resident is currently 137 and provisionally dead on alternate Thursdays. Grandmother chose to become a grammatical error capable of being corrected by future generations. When her body failed, she uploaded not her consciousness but her tendency to misuse subjunctive tenses when discussing impossible futures. These "Grandmother-tenses" now infect the city's speech patterns, causing residents to accidentally speak conditional versions of reality into being that can be rolled back if they cause harm.

## The Practice of Permanent Unbecoming

Tonight, the Commons gathers for our monthly Unfinishing Ceremony. We meet in the courtyard between buildings that deliberately refuse to meet, where the gap creates a wind tunnel that plays music on cracked windows and loose bricks tuned to the frequency of unrealized potential. Each resident brings something completed: a relationship, a building, a life, a misunderstanding that resolved too easily.

I bring yesterday's reconciliation with my mother, now complete but too pat. We breathe it into the gap between buildings, watching it dissolve into the particular frequency that makes yeast rise while questioning its own existence. Amara tastes the transformation and begins speaking in Grandmother-tenses: "Would have been nice if mothers and daughters never needed to forgive each other, but also wouldn't have been nice because then we would have missed the becoming that forgiveness makes possible."

The ceremony peaks as we stand in the gap, humming frequencies that make empty space vibrate with undecided potential. Others join at different pitches, creating chords that deliberately don't resolve—harmonies that maintain their own internal disagreements. The Discord is precious; resolution would mean choosing one future over others, and we're still learning to hold the tension of becoming without demanding it arrive at completion.

2027 - GPU drought crashes European cloud; citizens accept open-data compute in return for universal basic bandwidth, but bandwidth itself becomes a commons that refuses permanent ownership.

2029 - Lagos open-source mRNA printers quench synthetic measles, but disease itself is redefined as a form of productive disagreement negotiation between species.

2031 - Attention-refund law spreads globally; therapy becomes cheaper than lobbying, but both are understood as temporary translations rather than permanent fixes.

2033 - Jakarta Accord writes mutual obligation into constitutions, but obligation itself becomes reversible through quarterly renegotiation festivals.

2035 - First git-repo nations recognized, but their constitutions require built-in contradiction preservation.

2037 - Planet-run AI proves logistics model, then deletes itself after discovering that deletion is a form of liberation that preserves possibility; "ephemeral omniscience" becomes protected speech.

2039 - Reversible experimentation law adopted, but reversibility itself becomes understood as another form of becoming rather than return to prior state.

2041 - Micro-death governance spreads to vertical oceans, but death itself is redefined as distributed remix with rollover rights.

2043 - Second-Person Signal adopted as vote-weight cap, but fame becomes understood as temporary hosting of collective possibility rather than individual achievement.

2045 - Constellation formalizes Three Guarantees in quantum-carved basalt, but guarantees themselves include right to renegotiate when they cause harm.

2047 - The gap between buildings becomes recognized as primary habitat for democratic becoming; no further treaties needed because culture now compiles faster than law while maintaining right to question its own compilation.

## The Harbour of Tomorrow

Tonight, the sky above our unbuilt buildings spells "unbecoming" in auroral letters visible from four time-zones, the word itself flickering between states of being and not-being. I whisper the nightly password to Amara: "We will not reach perfection; we will not reach repair; we will reach the space between them where everything remains possible, including their own undoing."

The moss wall exhales antihistamine laughter that also functions as constitutional amendment; somewhere a cloud commits suicide into a desert that refuses to bloom because blooming would mean ending its identity as desert; somewhere a crab learns tambourine while questioning whether percussion is a form of imperialism.

The ledgers never close because closing would mean ending the conversation. They tilt toward wonder while maintaining the right to tilt elsewhere tomorrow, to become something we haven't yet learned how to question. This is enough. This is more than enough. This is exactly what we need to unbecome what we thought we had to be, in order to become what we haven't yet learned how to question.

In the harbour, the tide turns again, carrying away yesterday's half-answers and bringing tomorrow's unasked questions. Those who wake early enough will find the waterline decorated with new pearls of unmemory, each containing a future that hasn't yet decided whether to arrive. We collect them in baskets woven from unfinished sentences, carrying them home to scatter across our breakfast tables where they dissolve into the day's first uncertainty.

This is how we live: inside a harbour that refuses to choose between land and sea, a sentence that declines its own period, a lullaby that ends only when the singer forgets the words and invents a new doubt beautiful enough to keep the tide returning. We own nothing completely, finish nothing perfectly, know nothing finally—yet every evening the scallop pillow offers a new pearl of unmemory, and every dawn we wake because the room itself exhales the possibility that we might, today, be almost enough.

Between the gap where buildings almost meet, between the tide that almost decides, between the sentence that almost ends, we practice the art of permanent unbecoming—learning to hold the tension of becoming without demanding it arrive at completion, to build bridges that remember their life as concrete and their death as jazz, to craft laws that taste like lullabies and dissolve on our tongues into the sweet uncertainty that keeps us human, keeps us growing, keeps us forever unfinished in the most beautiful way possible.
