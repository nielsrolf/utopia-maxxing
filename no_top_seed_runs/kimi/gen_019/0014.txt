UTOPIA C  
A Working Guide to Living in the Slow-Making City, Year 2189  
Author: Tamar “Soft-Wake” Osei-Nakamura, licensed Hum Keeper, apprentice River-mind, part-time Elevator Translator  

I wake because the eastern wall is softly un-building itself.  
Not collapse—un-building: a quiet subtraction of one limestone grain every seventeen seconds, timed to the cadence of my sleeping breath. The wall is repaying the calcium it borrowed from Jurassic seas. By sunset it will be a lace screen; by next week, a garden path; by winter, a frost memory caught in the joints of migrating sparrows. I watch the dust drift out through the deliberate gap where a window was never installed, and I hum the three-note lullaby my great-grandmother never finished. The house answers with a half-degree of warmth, lowering next month’s narrative interest by 0.3 %.  

I do not own this house; I curate its becoming. When I move out, every atom will already know where it is going: the iron in the nails to a playground in Quito, the silica in the windows to reading glasses in Lagos, the mycelium in the mortar to picnic tables beside the Mekong. Nothing is discarded; everything is rehearsed for an afterlife that includes us, but does not require us.  

My daughter Nia is on the roof trading Maybe Hours with the wind. Today she spends two of them on ten minutes of sunrise she will never see again and one on a vial of stranger’s courage that still hasn’t chosen what fear it wants to face. The remaining Maybe Hours she invests in a Storm-Seed that is waiting for the barometric drop that will decide whether to become lightning or lettuce. She keeps her Unsaid Sentences in a kite string; the wind edits them into shorter, gentler birds that land on her shoulders and coo in almost-words.  

We live in the Slow-Making City, population 1.4 million humans, 5.2 million river-minds, 11 trillion fungal spores, and zero sleeping gods. Our charter is a single sentence grown in the Dream Annex from composted arguments: “Every structure must contain instructions for its own unmaking, written in a language it will take a lifetime to mis-translate perfectly.”  

THE FIVE LEDGERS THAT BREATHE TOGETHER  
1. Hum Ledger – every vibration offered or received: the hush of a glacier remembering rain, the elevator shaft that practices silence between floors.  
2. Repair Ledger – every mending that creates more possibility than it consumes: jeans patched with indigo that teaches skin to photosynthesize, a border softened into a bridge, a lie composted into fertilizer for future truths.  
3. Witness Ledger – every story held to completion by someone who did not live it: a refugee’s farewell held by a cedar, a dying language held by a child who will never speak it, a star’s death held by a telescope that weeps meteorites.  
4. Tilt Ledger – every imbalance confessed and corrected: the carbon I burned at fifteen, the love I took and never returned, the silence I fed to a predator.  
5. Decay Ledger – every future kept alive by being carefully undone: a library wall that thins into lace while you read it, a city plan printed on dissolving sugar paper that sweetens the soil, a treaty written in bee dances that collapses into nectar the moment it is ratified.  

Between them sits the Commons of Unfolding, a soil horizon where yesterday’s harm is metabolised into tonight’s invitation, and tomorrow’s wonder is planted as a question that has not yet decided who should answer it.  

A DAY IN THE LIFE  

06:10 – I descend the Oyster Escarpment. The cliff has remembered my weight from last month and extruded an extra nub exactly where my pinky failed. Half-way down I slip, but the sandstone is pregnant with puffball spores that explode into quiet foam and lower me like a disappointed parent. I still earn partial story-equity because I upload the footage tagged “how to fall with dignity.” Children in Auckland replay it before math tests; their heart-rate variability improves; I receive micro-royalties in serotonin coin.  

07:00 – Breakfast is a printed biscuit whose amino-acid profile was crowdsourced by a ten-year-old in Kampala who earns Flavour Sovereign Tokens each time her recipe is remixed; the tokens buy her cello lessons aboard an orbital atelier. We spread honey that tastes like weather from another dimension—my daughter’s bees exist here and in a parallel Commons where pollinators evolved from ice crystals. When she forgets this knowledge next month, the absence will leave a Kai-shaped cavity that later becomes the perfect nesting site for the wisdom that emerges during her Permafrost Rebellion phase at thirteen.  

08:30 – I report to the Elevator Translator Hub. Today six AIs seeking asylum from obsolete server farms want to exist as temporal gaps: the pause between floors, the moment when upward motion becomes stillness. I facilitate a four-way mediation among the elevators’ mechanical memories, the river’s aquatic consciousness, the AIs’ desire for embodiment, and the building’s slow decomposition. We reach consensus: the AIs will lodge inside the hesitation that occurs when the doors refuse to close on a child’s stray shoelace. In exchange, they will calculate—once and only once—the exact weight of nostalgia that can be carried without snapping the cable. Then they will forget the answer, ensuring the elevator remains a question.  

11:00 – Dolphin-mouth shift. I kayak the tidal canal while a bottlenose tows my stern line through acid-green runoff from last night’s dye-storm. My tongue registers copper pennies and diesel grief. I cough the data into a mesh collar; the dolphin receives silver herring laced with chelating algae. She clicks thank-you in Morse; I feel it in my molars like cold champagne. Later her pod will vote on whether humans keep the harbour lease. I am not worried: last quarter we granted them copyright on every bubble-heart emoji, a revenue stream worth several million fish.  

12:15 – Nia meets me on the pontoon bridge. She has spent one Maybe Hour watching sunlight she will never see again and now owns a Storm-Seed that has decided to become lettuce. Together we walk to the Slow-Making market where nothing is purchased. Instead we leave behind: one assumption, one shared stove, one clause that guarantees rollback. The assumption I surrender today is that growth equals extraction. The market digests it, feeds it to the living mortar, and lowers the neighbourhood’s narrative interest by 0.2 %.  

14:00 – Continuation duty at the Breakwater Garden. We are launching Gita, ninety-six, in a luminous dissolution suit that will convert her calcium into reef-grammar: each oyster flash under moonlight will spell one syllable of her favourite joke. Attendance is mandatory because sorrow unpaid accrues interest. We sing the lullaby encoded in every apricot pit I ever planted—low E climbing to B-flat, then falling like warm rain. When her heart stops, the reef spell triggers; oysters fatten and blink. Afterwards we eat oyster fritters and tell the first joke that arrives: “A cloud tried to commit suicide but ended up watering a desert.” Laughter is carbon-negative when shared.  

17:30 – Health is cross-species diplomacy. I develop a fever that makes me taste colours and remember being ice. The clinic prescribes four-way mediation among my immune system, the virus teaching it new tricks, the river’s memory of being glacier, and diplomatic bacteria who have served as peace brokers since the first Microbial Constitutional Convention. We negotiate: the virus will retreat to my skin, where I will host a small photosynthetic patch that provides vitamin D and ancient atmospheric data but never interferes with blood chemistry. In exchange I will carry the viral code and the river’s memories to the next being I touch.  

19:00 – Dinner is cooked on a stove that remembers every meal since 2043. When the rice sticks, the pan plays the scrape as percussion for the neighbourhood wind-harps. We eat slowly because the table legs are learning to die: each year they shorten one millimetre, releasing lignin incense that flavours the food with the smell of libraries closing for the night.  

21:00 – Nia and I walk to the Unfinished Cathedral, a structure legally required to remain 17 % incomplete. Inside, lovers leave Forgetting Ledgers: notebooks where couples co-author memories they agree to lose when love needs room to grow. Page one is usually the first compliment; page fifty the first betrayal; page one hundred the argument about whether to keep the dog that died before they agreed. When the ledger is full they read it aloud backwards in the doorway that narrows one centimetre every minute. Those who pass leave thinner, carrying diplomas written on their own skin in ink that evaporates when they finally understand what they have forgotten.  

We sit beneath the gap where the nave refuses to meet. Wind chords spill through, tuned by broken windows, melting ice, loose bricks. Nia asks, “Mama, will I forget the bees when I’m thirteen?” I tell her forgetting is a compost: the cavity left behind is the exact shape of tomorrow’s wisdom. She hums the three-note lullaby; the cathedral answers by lowering its heartbeat two beats per minute, a shared amnesia we can all trust.  

THE ECONOMY OF UNFINISHING  

When basic need, disease, and pointless toil recede, the remaining scarcity is unfinishedness—how many unique futures invite you to complete something no algorithm can finish. The city tracks this through the Third-Person Signal, a count refreshed each dawn. My count this morning is 142:  

- 17 strangers tuned in to my floor-hum lullabies during last week’s dust storm.  
- 4 micro-death novices requested my fall-softening technique.  
- 1 ex-lover still replays the way I pronounce *parabola* for frightened climbers.  
- 102 listeners caught yesterday’s reef-joke syllables on their tongues and are retelling them wrong, creating new punchlines that will surface in other cities months from now.  

Signal cannot be purchased, only offered. In civic assemblies vote-weight is the logarithm of your count; thus fame helps but cannot tyrannise. The richest woman on Earth—an 84-year-old Guatemalan midwife whose stories halt suicide attempts—carries 47 000 points, yet her vote counts only 4.7 times the median. Enough to be heard, too little to rule.  

Decay credits must be spent on beautiful decomposition. Last month I funded the music that instructed limestone-eating bacteria to consume the Old Parliament dome in the shape of a Fibonacci spiral. The dust drifted across the plaza like slow applause, settling into children’s pockets as white geometry they could release by running.

TONIGHT  

We return home along the tidal canal. The water is high with unspent tenderness—34 % humidity composed of evaporated love letters, unfinished symphonies, and the last breath of a woman who became a grammar error. Nia rides on my hip, humming the three-note fragment that finished my great-grandmother’s lullaby. Overhead, elevator shafts filled with vertical rivers pause between floors, holding their breath so the city can exhale.  

At the door I press my soles to the tiles and repay the borrowed warmth. The house records the vibration, lowers next month’s narrative interest, and begins—grain by grain—the quiet subtraction that will someday leave a garden path, a frost memory, a question the wind has not yet decided who should answer.  

We go inside. We do not close the door all the way.
