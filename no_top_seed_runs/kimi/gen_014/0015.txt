UTOPIA  
The Library that Breathes Back

I wake to the smell of pages turning themselves.  
Not metaphorical pages—actual, parchment-thin membranes that line the walls of our bedroom. Overnight they have absorbed the dreams of everyone in the house: my partner’s anxiety about unfinished code, my daughter’s longing for a dragon she met in a book, my own recurring dream of a river whose name I can never remember. The membranes rustle, reorganising themselves into a new chapter. A single sentence detaches and floats down like a feather: “The river is called Letting Go.” I swallow the words with a glass of water. They taste like iron and forgiveness.

My name is Asha. I work as a Continuation Curator, which means I help stories that have outlived their authors find new bodies to inhabit. The pay is non-transferable: every month I receive three Unfinished Hours—time that only exists when someone else needs me to hold their place in a narrative they’re not ready to finish. I spend most of it reading aloud to the building itself, which grows warmer when the plot turns unexpected. The heating bill is zero; the insulation is made of shared metaphors.

Our city is called Bibliopolis. It was founded in 2039 on the ruins of three abandoned airports and a decommissioned data centre. Instead of flattening the runways, we planted forests of carbon-sequestering paper-birch whose leaves are printed with declassified government documents. The data centre’s server racks were melted down and reformed into a lattice of living copper that carries not electricity but questions. Touch a beam and you feel the collective curiosity of strangers: Why do whales sing? What happens to time inside a seed? How do you apologise to a river?

08:00. I cycle to work along the Index Path, a bike lane paved with ceramic tiles that rearrange themselves daily into a new alphabetical order. Today it spells “MERCY” for two kilometres, then switches to “QUINCE.” The tiles warm under sunlight, releasing faint scents that correspond to their words. MERY smells like cardamom and old libraries; QUINCE like thunderstorms and grandmothers. The system is maintained by primary school children who earn Spelling Tokens they can trade for extra chapters in their favourite books. No one has ever tried to hack it. The path knows when you’re lying.

The Continuation Library rises at the city’s centre—a dome of translucent mycelium and recycled glass that photosynthesises during the day and phosphoresces at night. Inside, the air is thick with suspended sentences. They drift like jellyfish, glowing softly when someone reads them aloud. My job is to match orphaned narratives with new hosts. A dying farmer’s unfinished love letter to his tractor. A teenage coder’s manifesto that broke mid-sentence when she realised she was quoting her mother. A glacier’s autobiography, dictated in morse code through crevasses.

Today I meet with Gabe, 72, who has come to donate his fear of dying before finishing the family tree. We sit at the Listening Table, grown from cherry wood inoculated with oyster mushrooms. The table records emotional valence through subtle colour shifts: indigo for grief, amber for anticipation, copper for regret. Gabe places a cracked USB stick on the surface. It contains 47 years of genealogical data and one locked folder labelled “Dad.” The table blushes rose-pink—curiosity mixed with dread.

“Have you tried opening it?” I ask.

“Every year on his birthday. It asks for a password I never knew.”

I signal the Archive. A librarian approaches—an actual librarian, not a machine. Her name is Nyx. She wears a cloak stitched from the indices of out-of-print encyclopaedias. Nyx presses her palm to the USB. The table pulses violet, then exhales a single word: “TILT.” Gabe startles. His father ran a pinball arcade that closed the year Gabe was born. The USB unlocks. Inside is a 3-D scan of the arcade, complete with the high-score screen frozen at 9,999,999 points. Player initials: GAB.

We cry, quietly. The table absorbs the tears, filtering salt into ink. Next week a visiting school group will use it to print postcards: images of vintage pinball machines captioned with fragments of their own unfinished games. One card will reach a girl in Reykjavik who will recognise the font from her grandfather’s workshop. She will email us a photograph of the exact same machine, rusting in a barn. The arcade will be rebuilt, not as nostalgia but as a public timer: every game lasts exactly nine minutes, the average time a grieving person can laugh without guilt. High scores are converted into Continuation Hours for the library. Gabe will volunteer as scorekeeper. He dies at 83, mid-laugh, during a multiball cascade. The machine registers a new record: 10,000,000. His initials flash once, then dissolve into the ceiling, feeding the bioluminescence that guides night cyclists home.

12:00. Lunch in the Root Café, built beneath the library’s foundation. The menu changes according to what stories are being processed overhead. Today the air is thick with unspoken apologies, so the special is forgiveness soup: bone broth cultured on the pages of retracted newspaper apologies, garnished with parsley grown in soil mixed from shredded non-disclosure agreements. We eat quietly. The soup tastes like copper and release. A woman at the next table sobs into her bowl. The salt seasons the broth; the chef nods approval. Nothing is wasted, especially grief.

13:30. I descend to the Vault of Reversible Futures, a climate-controlled cavern where we store narratives deemed too dangerous to release outright: manifestos that could incite violence, love letters that could destabilise marriages, patents for time machines that actually work. Each story is sealed inside a prism of living glass. If society ever chooses to unfold one, we must first convene a Citizens’ Jury selected by lottery weighted against anyone who stands to profit. The prisms are kept at 4 °C and 100 % humidity—conditions that keep possibility dormant but viable. They hum, faintly, like hibernating bees.

I’m here to file Gabe’s USB. The arcade story is harmless now, but its subtext—how we replay our parents’ games hoping to change the ending—could mutate under certain economic conditions. I place the prism on a shelf between a rejected national anthem and a recipe for photosynthetic chocolate. The shelf shivers, recognising kinship. Stories, like fungi, form networks. I whisper “TILT” into the dark. Somewhere overhead, a pinball machine dings.

15:00. My daughter Juno arrives after school. She attends the Maybe Academy, where subjects are taught in conditional tense: We Might Learn Physics; Perhaps We Will Practice Kindness; An Introduction to Not-Knowing. Today’s lesson was “How to Befriend an Unfinished Poem.” Each student was given a stanza that ends mid-word. They must spend a week living with the fragment—sleep with it under their pillow, read it to strangers, plant it in soil—then decide whether to complete, burn, or release it. Juno’s poem ends “the river is not a metaphor for.” She has drawn a blank map of the city and poured the words into the harbour. Tomorrow she will sail out to see where they drift. If they wash ashore, we’ll know the poem chose us. If not, another city will inherit the sentence. No one fails; everyone continues.

16:30. Community Assembly in the Rotating Forum, a circular room that turns 1.7 degrees per hour, completing one revolution every three weeks. The slow rotation ensures no perspective dominates. Today we vote on whether to accept a shipment of orphaned stories from a neighbouring city that lost its library to wildfire. The narratives arrive as spores—tiny, dormant, potentially invasive. Some citizens fear they will overwrite our own fragile tales. Others argue that stories, like seeds, cross-pollinate. We deliberate standing up; the floor records our heartbeats and translates them into colour fields projected on the ceiling. After two hours the pattern stabilises into a soft green gradient—consensus. We will plant the spores in the Maybe Orchard, between the reversible futures and the composting regrets. If they grow into something harmful, we can always prune. The city keeps shears made from melted-down surveillance cameras.

18:00. Dinner at home. Our kitchen table is a repurposed printing press. Each night we ink the surface with leftover emotions—today’s ink is distilled from Gabe’s laughter and Nyx’s encyclopaedia dust. We roll out pasta sheets, imprinting them with fragments of the day. Juno arranges her noodles into the shape of an unfinished bridge. We eat in silence, tasting each other’s untranslatable hours. The pasta glows faintly, digestible narrative. Overnight our bodies will metabolise the stories, exhaling them as dreams into the bedroom membranes. The cycle continues.

21:00. Evening walk. The city dims by sharing light: windows turn translucent, casting pooled radiance onto the streets. Pedestrians walk inside borrowed glow, their shadows overlapping. We pass the Maybe Orchard, newly planted with fire-spores. Tiny shoots already push through soil, leaves shaped like question marks. Juno whispers to them in second-person future: “You will decide who you become, but we will hold the space.” A soft bioluminescence answers—stories acknowledging listeners. We walk on.

22:30. Bedtime. The bedroom walls have rearranged themselves into a single sentence: “The river is called Letting Go.” I remember now. It runs beneath the library, through the Vault of Reversible Futures, under the Index Path, past the Maybe Orchard, into the harbour where Juno’s poem drifts. We are all tributaries. I inhale the sentence, exhale a dream. The building records it, warm as bread.

00:00. Midnight. Somewhere a prism cracks open—just a hairline fracture, enough for a single photon of possibility to escape. It ricochets off the copper lattice, through the orchard, along the bike path, into our bedroom. It lands on my eyelid, weightless. Tomorrow I will wake with a new question. Tomorrow someone will bring an unfinished story and ask the library to hold it. Tomorrow the path will spell a new word, the soup will taste of a different grief, the arcade will record another high score. We will not reach perfection; we will reach continuation. That is enough.
