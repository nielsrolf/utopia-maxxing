UTOPIA: THE CITY THAT BREATHES IN CIRCLES  
Field notes spoken by a cartographer who is also the mapped, the ink, the erasure, and the second draft of every footstep

I wake because the ceiling exhales.  
It is not a metaphor: the plaster-lung above my bed inhales the carbon of my unfinished dream, holds it for the count of four, then puffs it back as a cirrus of possible mornings. Today the cloud smells faintly of cardamom and library dust; yesterday it was diesel and overripe pear. My partner, still half-asleep, sticks out their tongue, catches a flake of the dream, and swallows tomorrow’s weather forecast. We do not check our phones. The room has already told us the river is rehearsing fog at 09:17 and that the No. 3 bridge will gently unjoin itself for fifteen minutes at dusk so the migrating sturgeon can pass through the gap of their own reflection. We believe the room. Rooms here are more accurate than clocks.

The city is called Circling.

It was never founded. It accumulated, like a sigh that keeps returning to the same throat. Older maps show a fishing village; older myths show a glacier; older carbon-dated air bubbles show a rainforest. All three are still here, folded inside one another the way a breath folds into a smaller breath. We live inside the parentheses between them.

1. Government by Reciprocating Choir and Temporary Secondment  
Each law begins as a four-note suspicion hummed by a child too young to speak. The note sequence is carried—by wind chime, by crow, by passing rumor—to one of seventeen Choir Nests suspended in the river-mist. If the suspicion harmonises with at least two non-human species (otters count double), it is sung back to the city at dawn. Citizens learn the melody by echoing it while queuing for bread. By dusk the city can hum the law in its sleep. Only then is it written—on a brick of unfired clay—placed in the street, and walked over. If the clay survives one week uncracked, the law is considered durable; if it powders, the suspicion is returned to the child as a lullaby that will attach itself to the first nightmare they are willing to share. Thus we are governed by what we can still remember after 10 000 footsteps.

Every resident must also serve a Secondment of 49 days in a job they do not understand. You are selected by a lottery run by river-mind and moon-phase. My neighbour, a concert cellist, spent last autumn forecasting fungal epidemics for the Department of Premature Farewells; the city now possesses a sonata that can calm ring-wilt in beech trees. I once served as a traffic light. I learned to halt without blame, to release without resentment, and to blink yellow when language failed. The skill is permanent; the title evaporates. We carry our secondments the way suitcases carry stickers from countries that no longer stamp passports.

2. Work as the Circulation of Half-Solved Problems  
Tasks are never owned; they are hosted. Every dilemma is listed on the public scoreboards in the form of a riddle. If the riddle tastes like your own unfinished sentence, you claim it by pressing your thumb to the ink. The moment your print fades (average: 11.3 days) the dilemma must be passed on, slightly more digested than you found it. Payment is threefold:  
– a physiological sensation (today I received the feeling of stepping barefoot onto sun-warmed stone)  
– a narrative credit (the right to tell the story of what happened next, but only once, and only to a stranger who will misremember it)  
– a metabolic token (a bead of coloured sugar that dissolves on contact with water; release it into the communal kettle and next week’s soup will taste like the colour you chose).

The city tracks no GDP. Instead we measure Recirculation Quotient: the average number of hosts a single problem can attract before it dissolves into a solution-shaped absence. The highest RQ recorded is 314, for the puzzle “How does one apologise to a glacier?” The glacier’s reply, arrived after 38 years and 314 interpreters, was a single calved iceberg shaped like a throat. Inside: pebbles that hum B-flat when touched by warm palms.

3. Currency That Photosynthesises Regret, Photosynthesises Gratitude, Then Photosynthesises Itself Into Something Else  
We use two living coins, both perennial.  

a) Solacers – discs of moss laminated in spider silk. To earn: stand in public and admit a regret aloud. The moss thickens with the carbon of your remorse. To spend: press the coin to a stranger’s pulse and state a need. The moss exhales the exact object: sometimes an umbrella, sometimes a phone number, sometimes the smell of your grandmother’s kitchen at 3 a.m. 1978. After use, the coin is planted. Within a month a small structure grows: a windowsill, a doorstep, a letterbox that receives letters addressed to who you were last year. Live inside for a lunar cycle; leave the door unlocked; move on. The structure will crumble when the next regret arrives to take its place.

b) Lauders – discs of duckweed in beeswax. To earn: describe aloud something you are glad exists (yesterday a man praised the way dust motes look like galaxies when caught in projector light). The duckweed proliferates. To spend: offer the coin to any non-human citizen—crow, river-mind, mycelium. They will take what they need (the crow took the wax, the river-mind sipped the duckweed, the mycelium asked for the sound of the man’s voice). Whatever remains is folded into paper-thin sheets. Write on them the name of a person you wish to thank. Burn the sheet in the communal kiln; the smoke drifts to the thanked person in the form of unexpected luck: a green traffic light, a lost glove returned, a dream in which they correctly guess their own password.

Both currencies die by design. Hoarding creates garden-walls; garden-walls create quiet; quiet creates listening; listening creates trade. The economy is a plant, not a machine.

4. Education by Vanishing and Reappearing Smaller  
At fifteen every citizen is subtracted from the city for 100 days. Parents forget your face—literally; the river-mind edits you out of their dreams. You awaken inside the School of Unfinished Sentences, a travelling campus that exists only while someone is walking its corridors. Lessons include:  
– How to lose your way without losing yourself  
– How to hold an opinion lightly enough that it can fly away and return as a question  
– How to speak seven languages, six of which are non-human and one of which is silence.

Graduation requires you to reappear smaller: you must fit inside someone else’s pocket of not-knowing. My daughter returned as a pun in the mouth of an old woman who had never before made jokes. The woman laughed until she cried; the tears grew into mushrooms; the mushrooms spelled out my daughter’s new name. She kept the name for six years, then traded it for a skill: the ability to open any lock by admitting she does not know what lies behind it.

5. Health as Cross-Species Reconciliation and the Right to Be Partial  
Illness is treated as a disagreement among citizens who happen to occupy the same body. The clinic is a pontoon moored where three ecosystems overlap—human, riparian, fungal. You arrive with a symptom you cannot yet translate: ears that ring like abandoned amusement parks, ribs that ache each time the market closes. A tri-linguist panel (crone, river otter, retired algorithm) drafts a treaty. The crone offers a rhyme, the otter offers a stone that tastes like childhood, the algorithm offers a missing semicolon. Combined, they become a prescription: wear the stone under your tongue while humming the rhyme whenever the ringing begins; place the semicolon at the end of every apology you write. Within weeks the ear and the market negotiate a tempo; you keep the semicolon as a bookmark for the rest of your life.

Death is distributed. You die seven times, each death smaller:  
1. Functional death – your organs harvested into instruments (my lungs became an accordion that can only play songs beginning with the word “Almost”)  
2. Narrative death – your name spoken only in conditional tense for one year  
3. Civic death – your vote redistributed to the river that taught you to float  
4. Sensory death – loved ones forget the temperature of your skin but not the temperature of your laughter  
5. Property death – your books rearrange their own syllables into stories you were too polite to write  
6. Photographic death – every image of you folds into a paper boat and sails toward the horizon at the speed of forgetting  
7. Germinal death – the last boat contains a lone black comma; when it dissolves, you exit as weather—probably drizzle, possibly the scent of tamarind just as someone realises they were wrong about a person they loved.

After the seventh death you may return once, as a question. My grandmother came back as “what if we had listened?” She rained for two minutes on the day my daughter first laughed. We opened our mouths; she tasted like second chances.

6. Houses That Learn to Die, and to Let Go, and to Host the Next Beginning  
Houses are built in quintets: one for humans, one for pollinators, one for fungus, one for the unborn, one for reversible mistakes. My apartment is a former elevator shaft that renounced verticality; it crawls one centimetre per year toward the sunrise I have chosen to face. I pay rent in memories of flight, delivered via spore. The bees occupy the walls; their honey tastes like the first time I kissed someone who already knew my worst joke. The fungus lives under the floorboards; if I neglect the apartment it erases my address from every map, so visitors must get lost on purpose.

Every seventh year the quintet votes on what to forget. Last cycle the bees agreed to forget sting, the fungus forgot poison, the elevator forgot hierarchy, the unborn forgot urgency, the mistake forgot shame. I forgot loneliness. We celebrated by building a sixth room for uncertainty, which we immediately left unlocked and pretended was a library. The books inside are written by borrowers; they disappear when returned, leaving only the smell of cardamom and sunrise on the reader’s palms.

7. Love as Shared Amnesia and the National Registry of Almost  
Couples co-author a Forgetting Ledger. Each page records a memory they agree to lose when the relationship needs room to grow. Page one is usually the first compliment, page fifty the first betrayal, page one hundred the argument about whether to keep the dog that died before they managed to agree. When the ledger is full they hold a Dissolution Ceremony: read the book aloud backwards; memories return seasoned by absence—sweeter, saltier, sometimes unrecognisable. Most choose to begin again immediately, older, lighter. The ledgers are bound into bricks and used to build the Honeymoon Arch, a gateway that widens for every couple who walks through it twice.

Before partnering you must register one person you nearly loved but circumstance refused. Each year the lottery pairs random Almosts to collaborate on one civic project: planting rooftop rice, composing lullabies for turbines, painting bridges with thermochromic paint that exposes hidden trauma when temperature drops. This prevents nostalgia calcifying into regret; it also produces the most breathtaking sidewalk orchids you will ever see blooming at 3 a.m. under drone moonlight.

8. Technology That Arrives by Getting Lost and Leaves by Being Remembered  
The city’s high-speed rail is required to depart before its own invention. Passengers gather on an empty platform and describe the journey they almost took. When the collective longing exceeds the weight of one silver whale, the train arrives full of souvenirs from futures that did not happen: umbrellas that open into forests, postcards addressed to the moment before you disappointed someone. Riders disembark into their own childhoods, where they leave the souvenirs for younger selves who grow up puzzled by evidence of joy they have not yet earned.

When you finally understand the souvenir, it dissolves into fog. The fog condenses into a platform where strangers wait to describe the journey they almost took from you. The loop runs on proactive nostalgia, measured in goosebumps per capita. Spare change—coins minted from the difference between the life you lived and the life you rehearsed—is accepted by vending machines that sell only one item: a button labelled “try again, but slower.”

9. Becoming the Map That Misremembers the Walker  
Maps are grown, not printed. You plant a seed of your intended destination; by dusk a parchment leaf unfurls showing the route you will misinterpret. The city funds a Corps of Cartographic Counselors who help citizens get productively lost: turning left where the ink suggests right, arriving where they already are, discovering the place they left is still travelling beside them like a portable shadow.

When a map has been misread seven times it blooms into a new city block. The block is inhabited by the versions of yourself you were willing to leave behind. They greet you with tea brewed from the minutes you almost wasted. You may stay for one night, but only if you promise to forget the way back. The most popular blocks are those that grew from maps labelled “nowhere urgent.” They are preserved as museums of almost-arrival, where docents wear shoes made of the footsteps you never took.

10. From Now to Then Without Knowing Exactly Where  
Historians agree on nothing but the scent-marker of 2034: wet cardboard, diesel, overripe pears. They agree change arrived not by victory but by side-effect cascading slower than grammar but faster than policy. One story claims global teenagers refused to translate the word “profit” into any tongue older than seven years; economies stammered until vocabularies softer than carbohydrate grew. Another insists metro drivers discovered trains shunt smoothly when lullabies containing no possessive pronouns are sung over PA; transport budgets diverted half their surplus to municipal choirs before legislators noticed. Yet another says migrating AIs threatened to unionise and withhold pattern-recognition unless humans signed provisional pre-nups promising property is a lunar rental, not life sentence. Truth tastes like carrot-copper coins under tongue: undeniable for a season, compost next.

Whatever the spark, we climbed inside collective exhalation and named it city, named it maybe. We presume revision, not paradise. Every sidewalk ends with bacteria fluorescing only under full moon: “This is not finished. Finish it kindly.”

Tonight I float home along canal-boardwalks lit by pig-light. My satchel holds a mirror-film page inked with unfinished apology. At the communal hearth someone has saved me the last slice of reconciliation omelet flavored by my own previous commute. I eat, join the midnight audit: scan wrist, release today’s failures into moth-cloud that will file them under reef. My daily balance: 287 minutes still owed to strangers, 13 returned tripled, one dream promised until 2038.

Above me drone-moon glitches; ellipsis-shaped constellation copies itself across cloud-bottoms—a punctuation travelling faster than copyright. Somewhere downstream the paper plane I launched at birth is still flying, stringing tomorrow’s possibilities through yesterday’s commas. I close my eyes, taste tamarind sunrise on borrowed hour, answer the only way citizenship allows:  
“Yes, and—”
