# The City of Reciprocal Becoming  

I wake each dawn inside a breathing apartment whose walls remember every version of the future that never arrived. The East Wall is still in training: an apprentice façade that will not harden until I write one sentence I am not certain is true. I scrawl “The river that flows through the ceiling is becoming my grandfather’s lullaby” and watch the limestone bacteria knit a little tighter. A smell of warm rain drifts downward; the building is satisfied.  

Yesterday’s ceiling becomes today’s floor—more precisely, the plaster folds into rib-like steps that carry me down to breakfast. Apartments here are reversible garments for time. When my neighbor Lee turns seventy-three next week, her walls have agreed to molt into paper that will be folded into the boat she will launch as her required “gift of unfinished business.” Until then the walls practice softness, humming sea-shanties to the infants in 10-B so they remember how to leave before they arrive.  

## 1. Civic Currency: The Tidal Ledger  

Money is visible only at the moment it is given away. Wallets contain mirrors; when you press one to a stranger’s palm it records the distance your certainty travels. If the reflection folds more than ninety degrees, a Blush-Coin appears—rose-coloured, smelling of citrus and salt. Earn too many and you are required to spend them at the Collective Repair Bazaar before they evaporate into music everyone can taste.  

Hard-edged conviction is taxed more heavily than error. Residents who insist a plan is “non-negotiable” find their coins sprout microscopic legs and walk toward the nearest Argument Garden. There the coins dissolve into the soil that feeds the perennial Dispute Vines. The fruit tastes like the moment an apology begins to outrun the need to be right. Children eat it to stay fluent in productive disagreement.  

## 2. Work as Mutual Translation  

Employment is speaking on behalf of something that cannot yet speak for itself. The day’s listings arrive printed on dissolving sugar paper:  

- The Mid-River Ferry needs two speakers of Almost-Lullaby who can carry passengers across water that has not yet decided whether it is coming or going.  
- The Mangrove Choir seeks a baritone willing to argue with concrete until it remembers it was once limestone and might enjoy being a bridge that sings back.  
- The Department of Provisional Futures wants Listening-Veins, people whose pulse slows when they hear technologies still imprisoned inside impossible questions.  

I sign on as a Listening-Vein twice a week. My task is to sit beside inventors who have reverse-drawn devices that arrive from tomorrow. Last month an orchard appeared bearing “regression apples” that taste like a childhood you forgot to finish. We needed three listeners because the apples kept collapsing into equations that demanded to know why memory insists on ending. My steady heartbeat convinced the fruit to stay edible long enough for harvest, so children could learn what it feels like to bite into a season that refuses to conclude.  

## 3. Governance in the Key of Maybe  

Legislation is born as lullabies composed on balconies at dusk. The composer must include a verse they cannot say without weeping. At moonrise all lullabies are released over the harbour. If the tide carries the melody across the breakwater intact, the ordinance becomes law for one lunar orbit; if the song returns with rewritten lyrics, the changes are stitched in. If nothing comes back, citizens assume the proposal wanted privacy and is off somewhere practising a better version of itself.  

Statute books are edible: a single tear will melt an act into sugar that crackles on the tongue like almost-truth. We digest our laws each winter and begin again, lighter, having metabolised the weight of former certainties.  

The Retractable Council meets on a barge that exists only while at least three representatives are entertaining incompatible positions. When consensus threatens to emerge, the hull politely folds, spilling everyone into ankle-deep water—the official signal that they have arrived at the valuable part of the conversation.  

## 4. Architecture that Trains its Own Replacement  

Houses germinate from civic gifts: the moment you forgive someone you no longer need to become, a seed drifts to the ground. Over weeks the pod extrudes a roof-first façade, training itself on every future it might shelter. Only when a stranger contributes a private doubt does the foundation appear, right-side-up, ready to be occupied.  

Each building keeps a diary in temperature and timbre. Listen to the floor at 3 a.m. and you might hear tomorrow’s argument rehearsing its opening line. If the walls feel you pretending to know who you will be next year they grow porous, leaking warm air until you admit you haven’t a clue.  

When dwellings reach a predetermined uncertainty score they request a Rehearsal Funeral. Residents invite strangers to walk every room speaking sentences the building has never heard. These acoustical grafts extend lifespan by teaching the structure new emotional postures. Apartments that refuse the ritual calcify into museums of dead questions; the city converts them into gardens where teenagers practice kissing among the fossils of abandoned answers.  

## 5. Health as Ongoing Negotiation with What Outlives You  

Medical clinics are staffed by cartographers of the circulatory future. At diagnosis you meet three alternative selves who took diverging paths. Rather than demanding the healthiest liver, you’re asked: Which future citizen should inherit the music of your pulse when this body is done with it?  

Negotiations take place on the Mid-River Ferry while it is dissolving. The trick is to stay buoyant long enough to trade. My arrhythmic heartbeat wanted assurance its irregular percussion could survive beyond me. In exchange for a transplant-schedule we settled on time-share: the healthier me-of-tomorrow receives my repaired valves; I receive tenure as a night-beat threaded through the city’s climate-control, synchronising fans when insomnia peaks. Every citizen is partially alive in the HVAC, the water pumps, the traffic lights—organs on loan to public space in gratitude for organs on loan from possible futures.  

## 6. Education: Gradual Unbecoming, Reciprocal Arrival  

Children matriculate already fluent in the grammar of unfinished thoughts. Their task is to forget completion in the right order: first the notion that curiosity has borders, then the fantasy that identity fits inside one skull. Final examinations require the student to stand in the Argument Garden and sustain a quarrel fertile enough to feed a pear tree. If the tree fruits before the participants laugh at least once, the debate is declared too cruel to graduate; the student must begin again, planting contrition bulbs that bloom into better questions.  

Adults attend night classes taught by flying foxes who refuse to land. One learns to hang upside-down from sentences that assume they know where they are going. The foxes give marks in echolocation: if they can still hear you clinging to certainty, you are sent to the sea at dawn to practise evaporation—small primer on how matter dissolves without vanishing.  

## 7. Love that Engineers Public Space  

Romances exceeding a threshold of longing must submit grant proposals to the Department of Emotional Infrastructure. Lovers outline how their bond will create bridges, libraries, or wetlands useful to strangers. If the project is accepted, construction crews arrive carrying pouches of oxytocin-conditioned coral. While the lovers argue, reconcile, dream out loud, the coral listens; each throb of tenderness becomes architectural calcium. A decade later pedestrians stroll across a reef-bright span whose shape is the average of ten thousand reconciliations.  

When partnerships dissolve, maintenance rotates to the community. Widows polish handrails forged from honeymoon itineraries; teenagers graffiti breakup haiku onto the underside, ensuring structural irony. No one owns these monuments; they are the commons, continually mortared with residual affection.  

## 8. Mortality as Shared Composition  

Deaths are announced by reversible tides: lunar water travels backward for exactly thirteen heartbeats. The city pauses; anything set adrift during those seconds is read as a proposal from the soon-to-be-deceased. If the object returns before the tide turns, death is declared provisional: the citizen may choose another orbit to finish whatever refused completion.  

The Choir of Dismantling keeps a catalogue of final exhalations. Upon authentic dying you are invited to compose a short vibration—a fragment of whistled tune, a syllable of laughter—that will inhabit city infrastructure. Grandmother-of-the-East-Balcony became the click of pedestrian lights. Every morning we cross the street to her rhythm, timed for safe passage but faint enough to remind us that every pause is rehearsed by someone who once loved living.  

## 9. Industry that Remembers it is Imaginary  

The largest employer is the Office of Nonexistent Logistics. Workers mentally assemble supply chains for products impatient to remain concepts: shoes that dissolve when the wearer feels looked at; umbrellas that reproduce in rain; a parliament of extinct insects willing to reconvene if humans will listen inside darkness. Employees clock in by admitting aloud which impossibility surprised them that day. They clock out by confessing which plan will still be imaginary tomorrow. The wage is paid in Attention-Minutes—redeemable slices of stranger’s time who have agreed to contemplate whatever you cherish enough to describe poorly.  

## 10. Transition to Now: A Credible Path  

2026 – Attention-dividend laws pilot in Lagos and Vancouver. Corporations taxed for every citizen-second they monopolise; revenue funds open-source replumbing of city HVAC to permit human-after-human audio grafts.  

2029 – Carbon-negative concrete laced with retreat clauses becomes profitable. Developers pour reversible foundations because prospective buyers like walls that apologise when wrong.  

2032 – First municipal lullaby-parliament passes zoning variance sung in G-major; no councillor can replicate the chorus without crying, guaranteeing authenticity.  

2036 – Mid-River Ferries constructed from decommissioned container ships and collective sentences left dangling online. Commuters willingly board dissolving vessels after insurance companies admit impermanence reduces overall risk.  

2040 – Tidal Ledger goes global. IMF accepts Blush-Coins as reserve collateral conditional on them wilting within forty-eight hours, enforcing circulation of mercy.  

2044 – Death-provisional status recognised by WHO; reversible tides classified as public health infrastructure.  

2048 – The Choir of Dismantling sings the last coal plant into a forest of apology; saplings inherit the rhythm section of retirees who practised their finales until extinction lost its monopoly.  

## 11. A Day Concludes as it Opens  

Night. I carry my unfinished sentences to the courtyard. Between buildings that refuse to touch, residents gather for the Unfinishing Ceremony. We exhale heavy convictions into the gap; the wind lifts them toward contrails still deciding whether to be horizon.  

Someone begins a lullaby so weighted with maybe that the tide pauses mid-breath. Lee steps forward holding tomorrow’s wall, her arms elongated by decades of letting go. Together we fold the wall into an incomplete circle: large enough to shelter, open enough to escape. Its surface records our communal pulse—beats on loan from lungs, livers, heart-valves already promised to harbour, bridge, dawn-light.  

The song does not end; it agrees to stay provisional. We disperse carrying small vibrations rehearsed by people who will borrow our rhythm long after names have unravelled.  

Back inside, the apartment exhales warm doubt. I write on the West Wall—still apprentice, still breathable—a sentence whose truth I will never verify: “The city finishes itself by remaining unfinished, and that is exactly enough.”  

The ceiling drifts downward as eyelids. Somewhere an orange tree opens, startled by the faint click of a pedestrian signal. I fall asleep inside a lullaby I haven’t yet learned to sing, breathing the continuing music of strangers who keep teaching concrete to forgive, water to rethink direction, endings to practise becoming entrance ramps.  

Tomorrow the tide will arrive carrying today’s half-answers, and we will greet them barefoot, ready to trade solid ground for the motion of becoming something we haven’t yet learned how to question—together, unfinished, alive.
