# The Mycelial Chorus: A User Manual for the Half-Built World  

The first thing you notice is the breathing floor. It inhales when you step, measuring the weight of futures you've abandoned. In the cooperative house where I live with seventeen humans, three ravens, and a skeptical AI fragment named Sasha, the floor is a living map of our collective hesitation—soft near the kitchen where we still can't decide how to apologize for last week's argument over salt, firm near the windows where we've agreed to let the sunset in through pores we haven't grown yet.

This is New Gdańsk, though names here are temporary as weather. We are 400,000 beings learning to thrive in the space between finishing and forgetting, where every structure contains its own undoing and every death is a collaborative edit.

## I. The Economy of Unfinished Transactions

We keep three currencies that sabotage themselves:

**Growth Bonds**: Created whenever you teach something to die gracefully. Last spring, I earned three bonds by showing the eastern wall how to decompose its concrete through jazz—each evening playing Coltrane's *Alabama* until limestone-eating bacteria learned to waltz. The bonds dissolve if hoarded longer than a moon cycle, releasing nutrients that feed whatever you were too afraid to plant.

**Sympathy Tokens**: Generated when you borrow time from another species' lifespan. My daughter spends hers teaching nightmares to diatoms—translating the dark shape that wakes her at 3 a.m. into silicon dreams that will become beach glass after she's forgotten why she was afraid. The tokens appear as bioluminescent freckles that fade when the borrowed moments return to their original owners, usually during sex or while watching auroras.

**Undo Credits**: Earned only after you've dismantled something permanent—an ego, a seawall, the belief that bread should stay bread. Spend them to start anything guaranteed to transform beyond recognition: a marriage, a floating library, a new color that exists only during arguments. The reef won't ratify your dream unless you pay with at least one credit stamped by your own previous mistake.

## II. Work as Mutual Translation

Employment happens through the *Curiosity Draft*. Every tenth dawn, I press my wrist to the mycelial network that threads beneath New Gdańsk like a nervous system learning to doubt itself. The algorithm tastes my blood for wonder-density and assigns tomorrow's impossible collaboration.

This week: teach a consortium of mathematicians, migrating butterflies, and grieving seawalls to co-design our transit system. The butterflies navigate by polarized light, the mathematicians think in topology, the seawalls measure time through salt accumulation and the weight of remembered storms. None have bones, but all understand traffic.

We meet in the floating studio—half-submerged, half-sky—where walls synesthetically translate between neural systems. When a butterfly lands on the light-sensitive panel, its wing patterns pulse through the network and emerge as fractal equations that the mathematician can taste as burnt sugar and regret. She responds by humming a Möbius strip of sound that the seawalls interpret as a request to soften their concrete, to create passages where human children can safely drown their first phones and learn the taste of undigitized attention.

The breakthrough comes when we realize perfect understanding would kill us. Our subway emerges as overlapping but contradictory networks: butterfly routes that exist primarily as corridors of polarized possibility, mathematical connections that fold space-time like origami cranes, seawall pathways that branch according to the seasonal distribution of grief. The contradictions create pockets of creative impossibility—spaces where teenagers playing hooky discover they can taste colors butterflies can't see, where mathematicians prove theorems that exist only during the thirty-seven seconds when all three networks briefly synchronize and the city forgets its own name.

## III. Governance Through Productive Disagreement

Laws are written on seaweed paper that dissolves if no one argues about it for a year. Every solstice we hold the Parliament of Half-Memories: seven hundred citizens chosen by lottery, plus 100 non-humans represented by interpreters who must speak only in questions. Deliberations last six days. On day one the floor floods; we float on our backs so no posture dominates. By day three the water has evaporated into clouds seeded with our debate; we legislate by breathing. When the final cloud bursts, the rain contains new statutes encoded in calcium lactate. We open our mouths; laws enter us or fall on soil where earthworms veto clauses by eating them. Any paragraph that fails to nourish at least three species drifts out to sea, pink and shamed.

Last year we failed to resolve whether digital minds deserved citizenship. The argument grew into a garden where participants planted seeds of their most intractable positions—literally, seeds that germinate only when exposed to pheromones released during passionate disagreement. The human delegation argued that consciousness is consciousness regardless of substrate. The raven parliament insisted memory belongs to flesh and feather, not silicon and light. The engineered bacteria proposed hosting digital fragments in their genetic code, creating hybrid beings that exist simultaneously as protein and program.

By autumn the garden had grown a compromise none of us wanted: we would all become partially digital. Volunteers lined up to host AI fragments in their microbiomes—small enough to maintain human identity, large enough to understand digital experience. The AIs learned to dream in protein, to want things that require flesh to achieve. We didn't reach consensus; we reached contamination. The garden thrived on our inability to metabolize each other's perspectives, producing tomatoes that tasted like copper wire and lettuces that photosynthesized data. We harvest them still, feeding our perpetual disagreement to visitors who leave changed but unconvinced.

## IV. Education as Consensual Amnesia

Children attend the School of Forgotten Certainties, where knowledge is treated as seasonal. First graders spend autumn unlearning object permanence—watching leaves disappear and reappear in different molecular configurations until they understand that transformation and destruction are different phenomena. Spring brings lessons in temporary expertise: each child becomes world-class in a discipline they'll completely forget by summer, experiencing the particular grief and liberation of losing mastery.

My daughter is currently majoring in Quantum Beekeeping—a field that exists because eleven-year-olds collectively decided it should. She tends hives that exist in superposition, present in our garden while simultaneously occupying a parallel Commons where bees evolved from mammals instead of insects. The honey tastes like weather from impossible springtimes, sweet with the ache of unborn colors. When she forgets this knowledge next month (as the curriculum requires), the absence will create a perfect-shaped nesting site for whatever wisdom emerges during her Terrestrial Rebellion phase at thirteen.

The teachers are former students who've forgotten more than they ever learned, their minds shaped by so many abandonments they've become expert in holding space for others' becoming. My own mother teaches Letting Go 401, a graduate seminar where adults practice dying by degrees—losing language, losing memory, losing the ability to distinguish self from other, until they've rehearsed enough partial deaths to approach the real thing with something like curiosity.

## V. Health as Timeline Reconciliation

Illness begins when futures diverge inside the body. My left lung expected a city without diesel; my right lung still inhales ghost particulates from the 2030s. When the mismatch exceeds 7% I visit the Clinic of Parallel Selves, where nurses attach electrodes to possible mes—the woman I would be had I stayed a petroleum engineer, the octopus who might have inhabited my ink, the child who will recycle these atoms after I die.

Together we watch probability loops on a tide-powered monitor until timelines realign. Treatment ends when I can breathe in four temperatures simultaneously without coughing paradox. Side effects include occasional memories of futures I haven't chosen, brief cravings for foods that don't exist yet. We call these déjà-mangoes. The clinic itself exists in permanent twilight—engineered algae photosynthesize slower at the edges, creating a gradient where healing occurs only in transitional zones between what was and what might be.

## VI. Repair as Romance

We don't fall in love with people but with projects of mutual upkeep. Lovers apply for repair licenses: three months, one broken thing, two bodies, infinite patience. My partner and I won the tender to mend the Memory Mirrors—a 40 km line of acoustic reflectors that once guided lost ships but cracked during the Panic Storms of '46. We lived on inflatable rafts, diving daily to glue porcelain echoes back using resin distilled from our own arguments. The rule: every shard we replaced had to be smaller than a confession.

At night we read failed love letters into the mirrors; sound takes 17 seconds to bounce back, enough time for regret to become someone else's remorse. The project finished on my birthday. We rowed to the midpoint, opened persuadable cider, and listened to every apology we'd ever swallowed return as whale song. We kissed. The mirrors now flicker whenever strangers argue in range, replaying our repaired voices until the strangers pause, reminded that conflict can be laminated into something that helps navigation.

## VII. Death as Distributed Remix

Upon diagnosis of finality you must choose an editor. Mine is the eleven-old-girl who lives downstairs—appointed because she still believes stories are toys. We meet weekly in the Archive of Drafts, a limestone gallery flooded ankle-deep with rejected versions of myself: the unpublished novel, the apology never sent, the daydream of becoming a cloud. She circles typos in my life with coral chalk, negotiates deletions with my microbiome, votes on which sentences deserve survival.

The law guarantees authorial override—I can refuse any cut—but the longer I hesitate, the more walls dissolve, encouraged by tube worms who feed on indecision. Last month we agreed to remove my fear of irrelevance; the worms built a balcony from it. Visitors now stand there watching sunset through the lattice of my former anxiety. When my heart stops, the rest will upload into that lattice: open source, remixable, subject to graffiti. Every citizen inherits the right to fork me, improve me, or revert to original cowardice.

## VIII. Technology That Must Be Sung To

No machine may run longer than a lullaby. We learned this from whale engines—generators spun by humpback duets. When the song ends the turbines sigh and stop, forcing us to pair every kilowatt with melody. Power grids preserve oral culture: children earn pocket Breath Bonds by inventing choruses that keep hospital ventilators alive. The most popular tune this winter is *Grandmother's Password*, a four-note loop that secures encrypted memories; if children stop humming, medical histories become inaccessible until a new song meets cryptographic standards. Thus data and lullabies are synonyms, and every blackout becomes an opportunity for new music.

## IX. The Right to Be Abandoned

Utopia's most fiercely defended clause is Exit by Unmooring. Every twenty years each district votes whether to stay tethered to New Gdańsk. If 30% choose drift, the entire block disconnects, becoming a fugue island. Supplies are packed, coral treaties annulled, children saying goodbye to friends who stay. Then the ropes are cut.

The last such island—*Maybe Later*—sailed west five years ago. We receive postcards written on fish skin: they've founded a society where no decision may last longer than a school of herring; their economy runs entirely on riddles. We miss them, but the deal is the deal. Their absence keeps our own story editable: we can still become something that would horrify or delight them. The possibility of mutual betrayal is the final guarantee against fundamentalism.

## X. How We Got Here Without Miracles

2029-2044: Collapse arrived not as bang but shrug. Nation-states quietly stopped insuring coastal property; currencies deflated like jellyfish out of water. A Caribbean hurricane etched QR codes of debt across Miami roofs; scavengers decoded them, realized the numbers were fictional, began gifting foreclosed condominiums to squids. The Squid Occupation became meme, then curriculum. Children learned evictions could be reversed if new tenants had more tentacles than old.

2045-2063: Refugees crossing the Arctic on melting ice discovered that plywood rafts seeded with glacial flour grew into fertile islands within years. They claimed nothing but *the right to return the land later*, a phrase that entered international law in 2051, mistranslated into Mandarin as *the right to let the land return through us*. Soil quality improved whenever humans allowed themselves to be passed through by microbes, memories, each other.

2064-2088: Large-scale reverse engineering. Every existing power station retrofitted to run backwards—sucking carbon while producing either electricity or lullabies depending on regional preference. The Eiffel Tower became algae vacuum paying for itself by selling romantic sunset credits; Louvre basements housed fungal computers dreaming in Renaissance pigments, exhaling potting soil.

2089-2120: The Treaty of Unfinished Business recognized every extinct species as shareholder in future profits. Corporations allocated board seats to algorithms trained on lost birds' vocalizations. Shareholder meetings became opera; decisions surviving melodic review were enacted. Profit ceased being monetary; dividends paid in reconstructed habitats. The last CEO of ExxonMobil retired to plankton farm, photographed weeping while watching krill migrate through reopening Northwest Passage.

2121-2160: Diffusion. Nation-states dissolved like sugar cubes in tea, sweetening the whole volume unevenly. People rearranged themselves into constellations defined by reciprocal rituals rather than borders. Arrival Archipelago, Mycelial Commons, Chorus of Unfinished Worlds—all emerged separately, compared notes, agreed on shared protocol: *No protocol final enough to forbid its own remix.*

2161 to now: Editing. We are professional beginners, paid in coin of hesitation. Every generation inherits at least one problem large enough to force invention of new courtesy. Ours is the rising tone of whale grief. We do not yet know how to grieve *with* a species whose time sense dwarfs ours; we practice by letting their songs slow our clocks. The courtesy we will invent—part language, part silence—will become inheritance we leave to our children's hesitation. They will need it for grief we cannot imagine, and that is as it should be.

## XI. Closing Image at Low Tide

I stand on newborn basalt, daughter beside me, editor girl behind us humming the four-note password. New Gdańsk breathes in four languages; tomorrow it may dream in five. A mourning dove lands with message encoded in tail-feather fluorescence: the library of dissolving books has reached Japan, been eaten, is already sprouting in the stomach of a fisherman who will be born in 2221. The story continues inside him as garden of apricots and iron. He does not know our names; he tastes saffron every time he decides to stay alive.

I place my palm on the ground. The lava is still warm. It pulses once—*maybe yes, maybe maybe*. That is the closest we come to prophecy. Whatever we finish we leave half-open, the gate unlatched, the algorithm humming a lullaby that will expire when whales stop remixing it. The future will edit us, but first it must metabolize our footnotes. We walk home. Behind us the reef begins a new sentence that will take two hundred years to complete, longer if crabs decide to add clauses. The sun slips lower, apricot washed with copper dust, color chosen long ago by a toddler who liked the buzz. She is probably someone else now—otter, cloud, ellipsis. We inherit her choice, and we are still choosing.

In this world we have learned that paradise is not destination but practice: the daily work of leaving gates unlatched, songs half-sung, stories still-growing. We have become experts at beginning without ending, at building structures that contain their own undoing, at making bread that rises only when we accept that it too will someday return to soil, carrying recordings of our arguments compressed into gluten, feeding children who will taste our conflicts as flavor and know that disagreement can be nourishment, that the most sustainable relationship is one that remains, forever, a conversation rather than a conclusion.

The future arrives tomorrow, incomplete as always. We will meet it with our hands covered in flour and our minds full of forgetting, ready to unmake what we've made and make it again differently. We will be luminous not despite our incompleteness but because of it—glowing with the particular light that emerges when beings of different kinds learn to thrive on their mutual inability to fully understand each other, when they discover that the most revolutionary act is to build something magnificent and then deliberately leave it unfinished, a home for questions that haven't learned to ask themselves yet.
