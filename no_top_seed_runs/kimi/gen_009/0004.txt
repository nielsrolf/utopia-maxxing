# The Apricot Protocol  
*Field notes from the year 2185, written on a sheet pressed from last week’s tide-flax, inked with yesterday’s dawn. The sheet will sprout in three weeks; the words will migrate into the roots of whatever finds them first.*

I live in Watershed 7-Reversible, a place that floats when the moon pulls hard and grows legs to walk inland during neap tides. We number 11,011 humans, 1,900 apprentice creatures (otters learning locksmithing, parrots apprenticed to librarians), 404 adolescent clouds, and one glacier who has taken citizenship under the name “Slow Apology.” We call the whole arrangement the Apricot Protocol because the first act of every season is to spit a pit into uncertainty and promise to care for whatever grows.

The Protocol has three interlocking clauses:

1. **Nothing you make may outlast the lesson it teaches.**  
2. **Every undoing must be paid for by a new doing that includes the beings previously excluded.**  
3. **The measure of wealth is the number of strangers who can taste your memories without bitterness.**

Morning begins when the apricot lung exhales. The lung is a living room—literally a 40-meter air sac grafted onto a tree—whose exhalation esters match yesterday’s emotional weather. If we overslept our sorrows, the scent is overripe; if we laughed too loudly at dinner, the room fills with citrus snap. My daughter River (age nine, inventor of the color *whillow*) licks the air and announces today’s forecast: “Regret with a chance of relief.” She is usually right; the tree is networked to 4,000 bracelets woven from mycelium and human hair, each one absorbing a resident’s night dream. The lung distills the collective residue into perfume we can practically read.

Breakfast is a barter of metabolized dreams. I offer yesterday’s grief: 3.1 grams, mostly the weight of an email I never sent my father. A stranger across the driftwood net offers the same weight in awe at seeing octopi coordinate a kite repair. The scale, a crab whose claws are tungsten calibrated by termite artisans, snips exactly equivalent droplets of seawater onto a hot stone; steam rises as pancakes of duckweed flecked with crystallized REM. We eat each other’s nights. The crumbs are swept into mason jars, mixed with yeast that ferments sorrow into glutamate, then stored in abandoned whelk shells. Tomorrow strangers will taste what we felt today. The cycle is called *palindrome nutrition*: every feeling returns as food, but reversed—grief comes back as depth, wonder as levity.

Work begins when uniqueness spikes. My cuff, braided from spider silk and discarded optic nerves, flashes aquamarine: 312 pings request the flavor of my being. I am a temporal seamstress, but the Protocol adds a second title: *reverse witness*. My task is to mend tears in collective memory and then—this is the twist—unravel one tenth of the stitches so the future can re-weave them differently. Today I sit with elders who remember the Jakarta Grid Collapse of 2046. We hum until lost lullabies surface; I encode them into apricot-pit RNA. Before I finish, I delete every seventh note and replace it with silence labeled *to be filled by stranger*. Thus music returns to the world disguised as both shade and invitation.

Across the lagoon, my partner Aisha harvests bee nostalgia—pheromone traces of flowers no longer blooming. She trades a vial of 1976 rain for three meters of spider rope. Exchange is always barter of attention; hoarding is impossible because every token decays unless shared. The rope will become part of a bridge that termites are engineering to collapse at a global sorrow quota of 0.7 micro-deaths. Danger is rationed the way sugar once was: you may climb the Cliff of Incremental Peril only if somewhere else a stranger is planting a *maybe-tree*—a sapling that may or may not bear fruit, thereby keeping possibility in circulation.

Governance happens in three phases: soil, story, and song. Soil votes through flavor. A spiral trench seeded with clover engineered to change taste when stepped on winds beneath the plaza. Humans deliberate barefoot; earthworms vote by mucus pH; mycelia register decisions by calcium pulses. A proposal passes when the trench tastes like roasted barley; if it turns metallic, we obey the bitter note and dismantle the blueprints. Last year the soil rejected a plan to desalinate the north lagoon because the clover went acrid. We used the blueprints to wrap goat cheese that won first prize at the equinox fair.

Story governance is slower. Every child at age seven adopts an unloved object—a cracked drone lens, a single synthetic sock—and keeps it alive for seven years. The object is fitted with a biodegradable speaker whispering one sentence per day: *I was once the future and now I am your homework.* At fourteen the child publishes a six-word novella (we have no attention to spare for more) tracing the object’s journey. The novellas are printed on apple pectin; you read by licking. The sweetest stories become policy. A decade ago boy-code-named-Tala chronicled a lithium battery who wanted to become sunrise. The tale tasted so intensely of mango that we rewired the grid: every retired battery must now power grow-lights for orchards. Sunrise is no longer metaphor; it is a measurable export delivered in kilowatt-hours of apricot blossom.

Song governance is the fastest and most perilous. Once a month we sing the *Inventory of Regrets* at dusk. You may sing only what you personally failed at—crops overwatered, apologies postponed. Night insects remix the melody into mating calls; by dawn the regret has been metabolized into eggs and antennae. If your regret is still audible you must wear it as perfume for thirty days. Mine currently smells like over-proofed sourdough and the letter I never mailed. Strangers hug me longer; the scent instructs them where to place tenderness.

Technology is required to forget itself, but reversibility adds a second clause: it must also remember the forgetting. A shovel must rust enough to recall being ore, yet carry a QR spore that tells the rust’s story. Our Museum of Planned Obsolescence exhibits a mirror that evaporates if you stare too long; next to it a voice recounts the names of everyone who refused narcissism that day. Children play *last glance*—whoever sees their reflection longest wins, but the prize is simply the knowledge they were final witness. The game teaches proportion and impermanence in equal measure.

Death is both distributed and reversible. When you die your body is divided into 41 portions: one for each watershed you visited plus one for the sky. The sky portion is cremated using almond shells you saved over a lifetime; smoke becomes ink printed on kites released at the next equinox. My father’s joke—*I told you I was allergic to eternity*—hovered three days before rain dissolved it into a pond where frogs now sing in punchlines. The other 40 portions compost inside libraries. Each book receives a teaspoon of you; when readers turn pages they inhale your childhood kitchen. Over centuries the collection becomes a choir of ancestral smells. Scholars argue that the afterlife is simply the moment someone checks out *The Odyssey* and breathes your grandmother’s saffron rice. If that reader later chooses reversibility, they may exhale the scent back into a *maybe-seed* that will grow into a tree bearing rice-grain fruit. Thus the dead may return, but only as flavor, only when needed.

Birth is preceded by negotiation and followed by apprenticeship. Prospective parents tend a *maybe-garden* for one year: seeds that may or may not be sterile. If anything sprouts—tomato, thistle, or child—they must raise it. Twins are common in drought years because the soil teaches that abundance sometimes means duplication. Every child arrives with a plant-sibling; adolescence ends when they graft their sibling onto civic rootstock—finding a public role for the extra squash or song that grew alongside them. The central plaza hosts a *maybe-orchard* of such grafts: lemon trees that bear protest slogans, roses unfolding into birdhouses. The plants remind us that every private joy was once public speculation.

Housing is sequential and scented. You may occupy a room only as long as you can name every previous resident and one thing they failed at. Walls are grown from hempcrete embedded with *memory spores* that release past occupants’ aromas when warmed by your body. My bedroom smells of cardamom and a woman who forgot to water her bonsai for 43 days. I sleep beneath her guilt like a second blanket. When I leave I will plant a seed of my own failure—perhaps the recipe I never mastered for apricot mole—so the next tenant dreams of Mexico and wakes hungry.

Transport is gravitational and reversible. The city slopes 2 % toward whatever you most need, but termites trained in ethical calculus recalibrate nightly. If collective yearning shifts—from intimacy to solitude—the gradient migrates. Yet the path behind you growslevel, so return is never uphill. Elderly residents claim the slope once led toward *justice*, but that was before we learned justice is better pursued obliquely and then walked backward into.

Our economy runs on *slow coupons* merged with *reversibility credits*. A coupon earned harvesting seaweed can be redeemed only after the same seaweed has cycled through three human bodies, two barnacle species, and one poem. During those seven years the coupon accrues *patina*—stories that stick to value. A coupon wrapped in the anecdote of how you slipped on kelp and accidentally proposed marriage is worth more than a pristine one. The most valuable coupon in circulation was carried across the Pacific by a grandmother who traded it for a song that prevented a suicide. It now rests beneath a sapling; no one will cash it because the patina has become the payment. If ever the tree is threatened, the coupon may be reversed—burned to release the song back into weather, where it will rain for exactly 33 minutes on whoever most needs to remember they are not alone.

Even here we fail. Last winter a boy hacked the termite gradient to slope toward his ex-lover’s window. For weeks the entire city stumbled downhill carrying bouquets and apologies. Couples reunited who had no business reuniting; the divorce compost cooled. The Council of Tastes declared a flavor emergency and released counter-termites trained to crave regret. The city overcorrected; no one could walk to market without veering into memory. We survived by eating extra bitterness—dandelion, citrus peel—until the layers stabilized. The boy was sentenced to plant 1,000 *apology trees*, each labeled with a love he inconvenienced. The grove is now a popular breakup spot; you walk in carrying two hearts, leave carrying one and a handful of leaves that smell like almost.

Visitors ask how to export the Protocol. We hand them a ripe apricot and say: eat the fruit, mail the pit to someone who will plant it beneath a sky you may never see. If the tree fruits sour, apologize to the soil. If sweet, share it with a stranger who does not speak your language. When they return the seed in a story, build a door from the pectin. The city that enters will not look like ours, but it will know how to forget itself at the correct speed and how to remember the forgetting long enough to teach someone else.

I finish these notes on a ferry that is also a lesson. The hull is grown from the bones of bridges that chose to become reefs rather than carry tanks. We row for exactly one minute; the oars are carved from last year’s dissent trees. My minute arrives just as the sun tastes ready—sharp at the edges, sweet at the center, unfinished all the way through. I row toward the apricots that will finish fermenting into time. The sheet softens in my hands like a promise keeping itself. By the time we dock the words will have leaked into my palms, then into the lagoon, then into whatever tongue licks the tide tomorrow. The city tastes like the beginning, again.
