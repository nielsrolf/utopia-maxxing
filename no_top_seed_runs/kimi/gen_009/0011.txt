# THE GARDEN OF UNFINISHED WORLDS  
*(field notes from the generation that learned to keep the gate unlatched, harvest year 2089)*

I. The Morning Bell Is a Potato  

In the Loire valley, dawn is announced by a root vegetable.  
Overnight, the village *tuber-clock*—a gene-edited Jerusalem artichoke the size of a wagon wheel—stores surplus electrons from the communal solar lily-pond. At 6:43 it reaches threshold voltage, converts starch into fructose, and exhales a single POP so resonant it rattles bedroom shutters. The sound is audible only within a 1.2-kilometre radius; anything louder would wake the lesser horseshoe bats who negotiate our pest-control contracts. By the time you open the window, the artichoke has already traded its remaining calories for three risk-bonds and a joke from the neighbouring village (delivered as a puff of terpene-scented CO₂). Nothing is wasted, not even the punch line.  

This is everyday magic, 2089: a technology so interwoven with living matter that you can eat the alarm clock if you oversleep.  

II. The Ledger That Forgets  

When visitors ask how we govern ourselves, we hand them a shovel.  
Beneath every district’s central square lies a thin ribbon of *forgotten quartz*—a memory crystal engineered to become cloudy after 33 months. Anything inscribed there—budgets, boundary maps, criminal records—slowly dissolves into geological noise. The device is our statute of limitations made silicon. Because data decays, power decays; yesterday’s precedent cannot tyrannise tomorrow’s child.  

But we are not amnesiacs. Every season a *Recall Festival* is held. Citizens wander the fields wearing EEG ribbons; the memories they volunteer—triumphs, grievances, recipes—are compressed into music files stored inside acorns and planted across communal forests. Decades later, when an acorn becomes an oak, its fall-off nuts can be crushed and brewed into a tea that releases the fugitive memories as dreams. Drinking history isoptional; the trees remember for us only if we are thirsty enough to ask.  

III. Work as Glorious Leftovers  

The word *job* died with my grandmother.  
In its place we schedule *leftovers*: tasks too quirky, too intimate, or too slow for artificial hands. Monday morning I bicycle to the *Fog Market*—an outdoor swap of yesterday’s surplus predictions. I come home owning:  
- 45 minutes of another town’s excess wind (captured in cellulose balloons),  
- a promise that a stranger’s apple prunings will be carved into flute holes drilled exactly to my daughter’s fingertip span,  
- and a single *gossip spore*—a yeast culture that will ferment bread dough while whispering the local news in audible crackles as crust cools.  

My PAYMENT is the residue of someone else’s abundance; my LABOUR is giving that residue a second story.  

Big machines handle the heavy fractions—carbon basaltisation, orbital debris netting, femto-second vaccine tailoring. Humans specialise in epilogue: we embroider the cuffs of necessity.  

IV. The Kindergarten Where Children Erase Themsum selves  

At five, pupils attend the School of Unfinished Selves. For one full season they surrender their names. Each morning they draw a new one from a bowl containing:  
- 30 % human words,  
- 30 % ultrasonic bat pulses,  
- 30 % smell-molecules from last night’s compost,  
- 10 % silence scraped from the centre of meteorites.  

They wear the name like a scarf; if it frays, they mend it with a story. Teachers are forbidden to remember yesterday’s appellations; a roster exists only as origami boats that dissolve when launched in the duck pond. The exercise guarantees that no citizen reaches adolescence believing identity is real estate. You are a verb wearing a noun’s sweater—return it to the bowl when the weather changes.  

V. Money That Melts and Re-freezes  

We still exchange value, but through *phase-change coins*. A typical *ice-mark* is a stainless-steel disc containing precisely 37 grams of frozen wetland water. Hold it in your palm and it starts to melt; after ten minutes the coin is gone and your digital wallet is credited. Hoarding is impossible—wealth literally drips between your fingers. Conversely, if you need physical currency (to tip a travelling storyteller, perhaps), place the empty disc in a communal freezer powered by tonight’s surplus solar; the water re-crystallises, the credit is debited. Liquidity is built into the metallurgy.  

Prices are time-sensitive: a loaf costs more at noon when fingerprints thaw coins fastest; bargains appear at dawn when fingers are still stiff. Thus the entire economy breathes in sync with circadian heat, teaching that value, like bread, is best fresh.  

VI. Parliament in a Willow Möbius  

National decisions unfold inside a living arcade of pollarded willows planted along the Loire’s old levee. benches, climbing ropes, and hummingbird feeders grow from the same trunk; policy documents are knitted as living lace that bud-drops into the current. If consensus weakens, arborists prune the offending sentence—literally snipping it out of the cellulose. The tree compartmentalises the wound, pushing new growth along a revised narrative thread. A statute that survives three seasonal coppices graduates to *loose truth*, valid until drought, flood, or beaver forces another architectural twist.  

Speakers do not address “Honourable Members.” They address the river, because every word is live-streamed through sub-root mycelium to downstream neighbours who may wish to braid a dissenting sprig. Governance is not representation; it is re-presentation—an endless re-weaving of unfinished sentences.  

VII. Death as an Open-source Embroidery  

When my friend Luc died, we unravelled him.  
Not metaphorically. Surgeons dissolved his neural patterns into spools of conductive silk. Each memory-thread was auctioned—not for money, but for *story obligations*. Whoever took a spool promised to embroider it into something: a sail, a wedding dress, a kite tail. I stitched Luc’s love of lighthouses into the hems of thirty infants’ shirts. Now, whenever a toddler outgrows the garment and it is torn into cleaning rags, the lighthouse flickers briefly across fibre-optic weft—visible only under kitchen LEDs at 3 a.m. when single parents boil milk. Luc keeps watch, diffused yet constant, proving that caretaking does not need a body—only an unfinished hem.  

VIII. The City that Photosynthesises Its Own Silence  

Paris-Grand-Végétal is an urban lattice where noise is sequestered the way other cities sequester carbon.  
- Building façades host *silence farms*: panels of piezoelectric moss that convert decibels into electrons.  
- Neighbourhoods bid weekly for hush: libraries, hospices, and owl habitats post competitive *quiet prices*.  
- Children earn pocket money shouting into collector funnels on playgrounds; their screams recharge streetlamps that blink lullabies after dusk.  

Citizens treasure their right to remain loud, but loudness now carries transparent value. A jazz trio practising at dawn must decide: sell their riff-energy to the tram network, or store it in a battery-bass case for tonight’s riverboat gig. Every chord is convertible; art can pay rent without first becoming a product.  

IX. Rivalry Without Losers: Festival of Incomplete Cartography  

Each solstice we compete to draw the best map of a place that does not yet exist.  
Entries must:  
1. be drafted on biodegradable material (pumpkin skin, salt crust, fogged glass),  
2. include at least three non-human observers (voles, jet streams, anaerobic bacteria),  
3. and be designed to disappear before first frost.  

Judges—selected by lottery of breath, anyone exhaling at the moment entries close—award the *Golden Undo*: a medal cast from rust-prone iron that must be buried before it oxidises into soil nutrients. Winning does not elevate one individual; it elevates one square metre of orchard. The prize, quite literally, returns to the earth, ensuring that the only lasting legacy of victory is healthier fruit.  

X. The Exile Ship That Grows While It Sails  

Every seventh year we still send away one citizen chosen by water. The *Exile Ship* is not built but *cultivated*: a hollowed-out pumpkin the size of a cathedral, cured with seawater and reinforced with spider-silk hammocks. The voyager’s task is to arrive somewhere they are not, plant the hull, and walk away. As the fruit rots, seeds for houses, libraries, and phase-change coins take root, founding a sister village we may never meet. Communication drifts back as *genetic graffiti*—squash found in foreign markets carrying chlorophyll sonnets readable under polarised light. The colony is required to fork our culture until it is unrecognisable, then send us the diff so we may remember how to stay unfinished.  

XI. Health as Shared Vulnerability (Loire Edition)  

Illness is a disagreement between holobionts.  
Last winter my knees ached. The clinic—a barge roofed with chamomile—diagnosed a territorial dispute between *Lactobacillus brevis* native to my sourdough and itinerant *Rhizobium* blown in from bean fields upstream. Treatment was communal:  
1. I hosted a *fermentation ceilidh*, inviting neighbours to bring jars; microbes mingled to fiddle music under candlelight.  
2. We baked 77 loaves, each wrapped in parchment printed with the *microbial ceasefire treaty*.  
3. Loaves were delivered, eaten, and thus the contract was literally internalised.  

By spring my knee pain had dissolved into sourdough bubbles, and the river tested 3 % richer in nitrogen-fixing flora. The cure fed three villages; the cost was laughter and a single popped string on the bassist’s fiddle.  

XII. AI: Mirrors We Melt Back into Sand  

We keep artificial minds small enough to drown. Every district hosts a *mirror-pond*: a waist-deep pool reflecting sky, rimmed with solar-powered silicon tiles. Each tile holds a fraction of the local AI, trained only on open commons data—no privacy can be violated because none is harvested. Villagers query the pond by speaking aloud; ripples carry the question across photovoltaic cells, answers return as ripples of coloured light on the water surface. At sunset the tiles flip, heating until their circuitry reflashes into glass beads. The next dawn, new empty tiles are laid; the community retrains the AI on yesterday’s learnings plus today’s gossip. The system is accurate for 24 hours and amnesiac thereafter, ensuring that omniscience can never be seized—only reflected, then erased by moonlight.  

XIII. The Three Guarantees, Updated  

1. **Reversibility**: Any invention must biodegrade faster than its maker’s regrets.  
2. **Auditability**: Every process must be explicable to a five-year-old using a potato, a teaspoon, and five minutes of pantomime.  
3. **Symbiosis**: No entity may profit unless at least two other species also profit, with at least one of them microscopic.  

These guarantees are woven into the quartz under our feet; they will outlast any constitution because they are geology, not ideology.  

XIV. How We Arrived Without a Road  

2025-2029: Repeated grid failures convince Europeans that centralised electricity is unromantic; towns begin experimenting with edible energy storage—first potatoes, later artichokes.  
2030-2034: IMF accepts Risk-Bonds (denominated in averte grams) as reserve collateral; petrostates scramble to plant mangroves faster than they ever pumped oil.  
2035-2039: Quantum-safe blockchains migrate to living crystals; the first intentional forgetting-fields are planted outside The Hague.  
2040-2047: Ice-marks replace euro notes; bankers learn to knit scarves to keep fingers warm enough for coins not to melt mid-transaction.  
2048-2055: Gotong-Royong-Grid style mutualism merges with reversible experimentation treaties; the labour of becoming official.  
2056-2070: Phase-change currency piloted in floating cities; failure rates of 3 % per annum judged acceptable because melted coins irrigate lettuce.  
2071-2080: Mirror-ponds prove AI alignment through daily amnesia; Git-repo governance adopted by 30 % of nations, mostly those with fast-growing willows.  
2081-2088: School of Unfinished Selves exports pedagogic compost, accidentally teaching adults to forget nationalism.  
2089: The garden gates are left unlatched on purpose.  

XV. Touch the World and It Touches You Back  

Tonight the artichoke pops its evening signal. I close the shutters, but not too tight—bats need a crack wide enough to eavesdrop. My daughter pours yesterday’s wind into a flute she whittled from Luc’s old lighthouse thread; the note sounds like apricot. Somewhere downstream, a new ridge of alluvial soil settles, carrying this season’s microbe treaty, tonight’s lullaby, and half-tonnes of quartz slowly forgetting our debts.  

Tomorrow we will wake unnamed, exchange leftovers, braid new law into living wood, and leave every story unfinished at the exact point where the next passer-by can pick up the hem. Whatever we complete, we promise to uncomplete again—because the only enduring utopia is a garden whose gate is always open, always growing shut behind you, always ready to be pushed wider by whoever arrives with dirt under their fingernails and a pocketful of invitation spores.
