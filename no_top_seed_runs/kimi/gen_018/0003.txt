UTOPIA: THE CONTINUOUS CITY  
Dispatch from the year 2174, spoken aloud because paper is busy becoming a forest.  
Author: Ayo “Two-Plank” Mendoza, age 31, licensed Midwayer, Third District of the city whose name can only be pronounced while walking.

I wake because the bed I share with three rivers needs to roll over.  
The mattress is a living bargello of algae-velvet and cork; it ripples once, expelling last night’s dreams through the floorboards. They fall two storeys and land in the soup-pot of Mrs. Okonkwo, who is already humming the tune of my unfinished nightmare. By the time I reach the kitchen she has folded it into a dumpling. I eat it anyway—today I will need the caution.

My city is called Continua.  
It has no outskirts, only “slow-motion centres” that have not yet arrived. You may walk in any direction and meet yourself coming back with different shoes asking to borrow your past. We do not refuse; shoes are multiplayer here.

1. Government by Rehearsal and Drift  
Every law begins as a street performance. Citizens gather, swap roles, repeat the scene until it feels either indispensable or ridiculous. If a majority leave humming, the performance is transcribed onto living sugar-weed that grows along the River-Mind. There it remains edible for one year. Any passer-by may nibble a clause; if the taste turns bitter on their tongue, the clause dissolves and the statute shrinks. Thus our statutes stay delicious or disappear. Children are the chief gustatory jury—no one has keener disgust for hypocrisy marinated in lime.

2. Work as Maintenance of the Incomplete  
No job is owned; tasks circulate like library books that return themselves when lonely. This week I belong to the Department of Half-Finished Bridges. My tools: a cedar flute carved from the mast of a ship that forgot to sink, and a pocketful of “perhaps nails” that solidify only while being hammered. I spend the morning coaxing two shores toward one another. The bridge is permitted to fail at dusk; its sugar-glass bones will sweeten the tide, attracting salmon who feed on abandoned architecture. Their flesh will later taste of vertigo and forgiveness, a combination prized by the night cafés. My wage arrives as moisture in my eyes—ninety millilitres of communal gratitude that I will cry into the communal garden at the new moon, guaranteeing next month’s lettuce knows why it grew.

3. Currency That Photosynthesises Regret  
The basic unit is the photosynther, a living coin of moss laminated in spider silk. To earn: stand in a public place and admit something you regret aloud. The moss inhales the carbon of your remorse and thickens. To spend: press the coin against a stranger’s wrist and whisper a need. The moss exhales the exact object—sometimes an umbrella, sometimes the memory of being believed. If you over-spend, local drizzle arrives; if you hoard, the coins revert to bare rock and your pocket becomes heavy with geology. The wealthiest citizen is a woman who regrets nothing, owns nothing, and is followed everywhere by a private sunbeam. She claims poverty; we agree, and envy her weather.

4. School of Gradual Unforgetting  
Classes meet in doorways that widen or narrow according to yesterday’s kindnesses. Lessons are recursive spells: today’s correct answer funds tomorrow’s better question. The final exam is conducted in a single breath—students inhale everything they do not know, then exhale a question beautiful enough to stand inside. Those who pass step out of the doorway carrying diplomas written on their skin in evaporating ink. The text lingers until they finally understand what they have forgotten. Unreadable diplomas are harvested by librarians who fold them into paper boats. Children who cannot yet read float the boats in puddles of liquid Maybe; the boats exist only while sailing, and the children are paid in wonder.

5. Health as Inter-species Reconciliation  
Illness arrives as untranslatable gossip between species. You visit the Pontoon Clinic, where a tri-linguist panel (crone, crow, code) drafts a treaty. Say you dream of rivers that refuse to reflect. The crow offers a feather; the crone offers a rhyme; the code offers a missing semicolon. Combined, they become a prescription: wear the feather behind your ear while reciting the rhyme whenever you see your reflection misbehave. Within weeks the river and your retina settle their dispute; you keep the semicolon as a bookmark for the rest of your life. Upon death, the treaty is composted into soil that grows parsnips tasting of reconciled mirrors. Children who hate vegetables are told: “Finish your peace accords,” and mostly they do.

6. Architecture That Must Be Mispronounced  
No structure may be built faster than it can be described incorrectly. Citizens practice incompetent description the way earlier centuries practiced cholesterol. The opera house looks like “a sigh that enrolled in evening classes”; the maternity ward resembles “a basket resting after carrying tomorrow.” The penalty for perfect clarity is exile to the suburbs of precise boredom—rows of identical duplexes whose doors open only when you admit you have nothing left to misinterpret. Each year the city funds a contest for the most useful inaccuracy. Recent winner: calling the elevator “a cupboard practising ascension,” which caused the lift to relax its cables and ascend only when passengers hum convincingly. The resulting shafts are filled with choir music and the occasional dropped shoe searching for its partner’s foot.

7. Love as Shared Brackets  
Couples co-edit a Sentence of Mutual Unfinishment. The sentence begins on the day they meet and ends only when both agree on a punctuation mark. Until then they inhabit the bracketed space between subject and verb, growing synonyms for stay. If they decide on a period, the relationship graduates into grammar and is archived as literature. Should they choose a semicolon, they part as clauses equal and independent. The most devoted pairs never close the bracket; their love remains an insertion open to footnotes from strangers. These couples are easy to spot—they walk the streets holding chalk, offering passer-by the chance to add a clause. The city tolerates the graffiti because it keeps the walls breathing.

8. Death as Public Draft, Private Sprout  
Mortality is crowd-edited. Upon death a citizen’s life is trimmed to 61 % interesting by a jury of children, animals, and obsolete algorithms. The offcuts are pressed into a seed the size of a tear. Each seed is planted beneath a sidewalk crack. Over decades the footsteps of strangers compress it into a tree bearing fruit that tastes of the unlived life. Passers-by who eat the fruit suddenly remember ambitions they never pursued—learning the hurdy-gurdy, apologising to their knees—and often change course mid-stride. In this way the dead continue to draft the living, but gently, through flavour. The most popular afterlife is the persimmon of almost, ripening every November into a sunset you can bite.

9. Technology That Arrives by Getting Lost  
The city’s high-speed rail is required to depart before its own invention. Passengers gather on an empty platform and describe the journey they almost took. When the collective longing exceeds the weight of one silver whale, the train arrives full of souvenirs from futures that did not happen: umbrellas that open into forests, postcards addressed to the moment before you disappointed someone. Riders disembark into their own childhoods, where they leave the souvenirs for younger selves who grow up puzzled by evidence of joy they have not yet earned. The loop runs on proactive nostalgia, measured in goosebumps per capita. The spare change—coins minted from the difference between the life you lived and the life you rehearsed—is accepted by vending machines that sell only one item: a button labelled “try again, but slower.”

10. The Continuous God Who Naps  
Under the plaza sleeps the Continuous God, neither worshipped nor denied, maintained. Citizens take twelve-minute shifts humming lullabies in languages that have not yet been invented. The god’s dreams condense into civic utilities: a drinking fountain that flows uphill on Tuesdays, a library shelf that exists only while you are not looking at it. Should the god stir, the city has rehearsed a collective shrug: “We were only ever borrowing your nap.” Meanwhile, minor awakened gods wander the boroughs as stray dogs, alley cats, misplaced house keys. They can be adopted for the price of a lullaby, but adopters must promise to release them back into uncertainty once the song is memorised. The most beloved god is the one that forgets it is divine and becomes the creak in a floorboard that reassures insomniacs someone else is awake.

11. Rivalry as Collaborative Collapse  
Every spring neighbourhoods compete in the Festival of Gradual Collapse. Teams begin building towers from materials that forget themselves at different speeds: honey, newsprint, aluminium pride. Victory goes to the structure that fails most gracefully, sprinkling its builders into the River-Mind with the least splash and the most new vocabulary. Losers spend the following year teaching children how to tread water while inventing synonyms for “oops.” The dissolved materials are gathered into a single raft that drifts away carrying thank-you notes addressed to no one discovered yet. The river, mildly flattered, lowers its banks for a week, revealing beaches of previously hidden possibilities—wrecked mandolins, unmailed acceptance letters, a door that opens onto today.

12. Becoming the Map That Misremembers the Walker  
Maps are grown, not printed. You plant a seed of your intended destination; by dusk a parchment leaf unfurls showing the route you will misinterpret. The city funds a Corps of Cartographic Counselors who help citizens get productively lost: turning left where the ink suggests right, arriving where they already are, discovering the place they left is still travelling beside them like a portable shadow. The most valuable maps germinate from seeds of “nowhere urgent”; they grow into pocket-sized cities that open under moonlight into neighbourhoods demanding inhabitants. Squatters move in, plant new seeds, and Continua metastasizes outward—a benign urban tumour of perpetual almost-arrival. The original walker often returns years later to find themselves remembered as a local myth who forgot to leave, honoured with a street sign that points two directions at once and is consequently never obeyed.

13. How We Got Here Without Leaving  
2029–2038: Insurance companies admit they cannot price uncertainty; stock exchanges deliquesce like aspirin on the tongue of a feverish planet. In the fever, neighbour exhales into neighbour. The first gratitude currency is measured in a Detroit basement where two strangers take turns blowing on a shared ventilator. The second currency is measured in apologies whispered through keyholes; the third in seeds traded for songs.

2039–2055: Nation-states auction their archives to buy breakfast. Citizens repurchase childhood photographs, stitch them into public quilts. Ownership dissolves into shared REM; borders are noticed only when waking, and even then as creases in the bedsheet.

2056–2080: Engineers learn to run power plants backward; carbon is inhaled, punctuation exhaled. Electricity flows only while a riddle remains unanswered. Villages learn to collective-hum bewilderment until voltage rises. Teenage heartbreak provides peak load; grandparent lullabies keep the baseline glowing.

2081–2120: The Treaty of Previously Unasked Questions grants extinct species seats in every parliament. Woolly mammoths vote by absence-shaped majorities; great auks by dive. Policies unable to survive interrogation by non-human interests quietly delete themselves, taking corrupt officials with them like lifeboats scuttled by their own guilt.

2121–2150: Property dissolves into stewardship. You may inhabit any structure provided you can recite its unspoken dreams. Mortgages are replaced by lullaby-leases: sing the walls asleep each night and you may stay. Defaulting means forgetting the chorus; the evicted rarely mind, carrying the tune elsewhere like passport birds.

2151–now: We maintain the Continuous. Every generation inherits one question large enough to force new courtesies. Ours is the sound of permafrost learning to exhale after ten thousand years of holding its breath. We answer by inventing roofs that harvest the exhalation as fog, irrigating gardens that taste of ancient winter negotiating with present spring. The courtesy we leave behind—part thaw, part lullaby—will be harvested by children who will need it for wonders they have not yet learned to spell.

14. Closing Image at Dawn  
I walk home across the Bridge of Delayed Arrivals. The moon is a copper coin left in the sky’s tip-jar; I drop a syllable into the river and it rains upward, watering newborn constellations. My daughter waits on the far side, age eleven going on unfinished clause. She carries a seed still soft as speculation. Together we plant it in the crack between tomorrow and almost. By the time we finish explaining to each other what we are doing, a new district has begun to grow—houses that exhale two centuries at once, streets that dead-end into invitations, a library whose books exist only while being misread.

We stand barefoot on ground that remembers being a question and practise not finishing our breath. Somewhere the Continuous God turns over, dreaming a new festival. Somewhere an elevator exhales my cedar flute and learns it can rise without forgetting the passengers who once pressed “door hold.” Somewhere the richest woman in the city dies owing more stories than she can repay; her funeral is a thunderstorm that lasts exactly until the debt is forgiven, and when the sky clears everyone walks home wearing clothes that dry into the shape of someone they are learning to become.

Continua keeps editing—adding footnotes, deleting foregone conclusions, humming riddles that expire when the foxes stop answering. We inherit the tune, and we are still humming, but we are also still listening, which is another word for remaining, which is another word for continuing, which is the only way anything remains at all.
