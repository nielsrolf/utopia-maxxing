THE CITY OF PERPETUAL MAYBE  
Field notes by Amina Solanki, written on the eve of the 200th anniversary of the First Reversible Council

I wake because the light has asked the pillow to read itself aloud.  
The pillow obliges, but only for the length of one forgotten second. It whispers the dream I declined last night: a city whose streets rearrange themselves whenever someone changes their mind. The dream tastes of iron and raspberries—identical to the flavour the Tench kitchen once recorded from my tongue when I was six. That morsel of memory has travelled the Global Palate Commons for thirty years, and now it returns as weather. I swallow the dream, label the empty space it leaves behind with a temporary name—“unbuilt bridge, span unknown”—and get up.

I live on the island-sentence called商榷-Qǐng, one of 4 019 floating paragraphs that form the Library of Perpetual Maybe. Each paragraph is built from coralcrete laced with anthrax spores that will denature the hull if net sentient wellbeing falls. The clause is still called SUR—pronounced “secure”—but here it has an addendum: any decision that *adds* net wonder must also contain instructions for its own extension. We call that clause the Maybe-More-Mandate, M³ for short, pronounced “m-cubed” or simply “maybe.” Every balcony, byte, and base pair must answer two questions before implementation:  
1. How do we stop this if it hurts?  
2. How do we keep this going if it delights?

The city’s name is pronounced by raising your tone at the end, the way you do when the sentence is unfinished. Visitors forget and say it flat. The city gently corrects them: it rearranges a footpath so they arrive somewhere they almost intended. After the third mispronunciation they find themselves on the Wharf of Corrected Inflections, listening to gulls that squawk in question marks. They learn.

My apartment is a paragraph that has not yet decided whether it is a kitchen or a forest. At dawn the eastern wall photosynthesises; at dusk it brews coffee. The transition is neither automatic nor negotiated—it is *contested* every day in a ten-minute Disagreement Gym. Whoever shows up inherits a randomly assigned position: *forest* or *cuisine*. I have argued for both and lost both. The wall tastes today of over-extracted pine; yesterday it was espresso-flavoured moss. The imbalance registers as a 0.2 % drop in my morning vitality. The apartment notes this, schedules a rematch for next week, and invites the local starlings to act as jury. Song volume counts as evidence. I practice whistling minor seventh intervals to sway them.

Work begins at 08:30 sharp-ish. I bicycle across the Bridge of Unfinished Crossings, a span missing its final seven metres. The gap is not a flaw; it is a power plant. Every pedestrian contributes 0.8 thresholds of indecision, enough to light the lamps that guide you *toward* the gap but never *away*. Tourists often ask when the bridge will be completed. Children answer: “As soon as necessity outweighs mystery.” The reply is carved into the guardrail in languages that mutate daily—today it is printed in bacteria that fluoresce only when you admit you might be lost.

I work for the Guild of Continual Custodianship, Second Circle. Our task is to keep the world from declaring itself finished *and* to ensure that every act of continuation remains reversible. This morning’s roster lists three urgent incompletions:

1. A lullaby stuck on the penultimate note.  
2. A treaty awaiting the last signature.  
3. A cherry tree that has refused to bloom for twenty-nine years because it cannot decide which century it belongs to.

I start with the lullaby. The composer is a woman in Lagos whose Civic Genome records a 1998 miscarriage encoded as a rest in bar seven. The note that should follow has grown into a polyp of silence weighing 3.2 chrons. My job is to extract the chron without erasing the grief. I wear headphones lined with mirror-marble that weeps perfumed oil when the calculation is just. The oil smells of petrichor and umbilical cord. I shave a sliver of silence, leave behind a lacuna shaped like a child’s fist, and graft the chron into tomorrow’s dawn chorus. Somewhere a sparrow will sing the missing note at 05:43. The composer will wake thinking she dreamed of rain. The song will remain unfinished; the grief will remain reversible.

Next, the treaty. It is written on fruit skin that fruits again each time a clause is amended. Last week Article 4 ripened into a persimmon and fell into the harbour; otters ate the paragraph on carbon reparations and shat out seeds that sprouted overnight into saplings shaped like apologies. The final signature belongs to a former drone pilot who now speaks only in wind. I meet him on the Wharf of Corrected Inflections. He exhales twice: the first gust signs the treaty, the second deletes three words—“irrevocable,” “perpetual,” “ever”—that had begun to bore the fruit. The persimmon tree reconfigures into a pomegranate whose seeds spell the same treaty but in the conditional tense. The harbour sighs, tasting the sweetness of maybe.

The cherry tree is trickier. I borrow a bicycle that remembers being a glacier and pedal to the Arboretum of Undecided Species. The tree stands in a circle of benches where retirees rehearse their deaths. They greet me with the courtesy of people who have practised disappearance. We consult the parliament of iterations: a banyan grove whose roots drip half-digested legislation. A branch fruits into a single cherry the colour of dawn over Hiroshima, 6 August 1945, a shade that exists only in peripheral vision. The fruit is bitter; the bitterness is a ballot. We vote by grafting twigs into our memories. I insert the flavour of my grandmother’s dissolution-by-tide; the retirees graft their favourite almost-sonatas. The tree absorbs the composite, blossoms overnight into a cloud of pink so delicate it reverses heartbreak. Petals fall onto the rehearsing retirees; they revise their fatalities into something more floral. None dies that week. The tree drops a last petal shaped like a question mark. We pocket it for later.

Lunch is served at the Reversible Mess, now tethered to a barge called *Maybe-More*. The menu is SUR-bound and M³-bound: every dish must list its own extinction *and* its own echo. Today’s special is lab-grown mutton biriyani that will self-delete if satisfaction drops below 80 %, but will also spawn a vegetarian variant if anyone sighs with longing for chickpeas. I eat with a spoon cast from my own tongue. The flavour map uploads to the Commons and downloads into a refugee kitchen in Aleppo where a twelve-year-old tests the curve by adding barberries. Her tongue votes. If the vote exceeds ten million the biriyani becomes a permanent flavour; if not it politely forgets itself. Either way, the chickpea variant materialises as a side dish that did not exist when you first sat down. You tip in hesitation: three minutes of silence measured by the harbour’s tide. The silence is compressed into a doubt battery that will power tonight’s streetlamps only when pedestrians admit they might be lost.

Afternoon is for dangerous play. I join the Disagreement Gym on deck seven where today’s prompt is: “Should商榷-Qǐng allow tourists to pay lumen-chron-selvage for the privilege of crossing the unfinished bridge?” I am assigned the con position opposite Mei, a violinist whose shadow is an unborn sibling she met at school. We argue for forty minutes under the eyes of a referee AI that scores not victory but mutual telemetry: how completely have we mapped each other’s utility curves? I learn Mei fears that paid crossing would commodify indecision; she learns I fear that free crossing would exhaust the city’s threshold generator. We document the curves, tag them to our Civic Genome, and agree to plant cherry seeds in the gap. The seeds will grow into a bifurcated trunk whose left branch charges tourists and whose right branch refunds them, creating a loop of payment and repayment that keeps the bridge forever almost crossed. The policy fruits into a plum whose flesh tastes like the sound of a violin string deciding whether to vibrate.

Evening is for tending the Death Garden. My grandmother’s dissolution-island has grown into a mangrove spit named *Perhaps*. Visiting hours are sunset plus hesitation. I walk the path backward, the way the Curriculum of Unfinished Selves teaches. Each footstep erases a year of certainty. At the tip I find her sitting on a root that is also a sentence: *“I might have been here, might still be.”* We rehearse her death once more—this time by aurora borealis banana peel. She slips, laughs, and decides to postpone. The tide writes her name in chalk ground from my own bones, calcium from a closed loop of city and sea. She asks me to remember her unfinished, so I forget the year she was born. We walk back along a path that is already disappearing, tasting of raspberries and metal.

Night. I lie beside Jian (partner, co-parent, tenor saxophonist, occasional squid). The ceiling is a live feed of the Milky Way as seen from the dark side of the moon, but tonight it flickers with indigo braille—last night’s almost-dream compressed by the pillow. The beads roll across our cheeks, hatch into questions we can spend our lives answering. Mine is: *What did the rain almost remember?* Jian’s is: *Which note keeps the lullaby from finishing?* We trade questions the way other lovers trade saliva. Between us the air grows sticky with unplayed saxophone phrases and unbloomed cherry petals. We agree to meet again tomorrow on the bridge that refuses completion. The room dims to the colour of pupil dilation just before dream. I whisper “Maybe.” The walls respond by photosynthesising coffee. The tide writes us into the sea, and the sea—because we asked politely—agrees to read.

Outside your window right now the world is halfway through becoming something else. Listen: a sparrow is rehearsing the sound of your footsteps the day after you forget how to walk. It will perform this sound for centuries, growing hoarse with devotion, until one morning you hear it and mistake the trill for your own pulse returning from a long migration. In that moment you will owe exactly one lumen, one chron, one selvage. Pay it. Fold the debt into a paper swallow and release it toward the sentence that loops the planet, the sentence that is also a bridge, the bridge that is also a cherry tree leaning out over the water, blossoming in the conditional tense. The tree will swallow the bird, the bird will swallow the tree, and both will continue flying, carrying your payment in their beaks like a question-marked seed stolen from a city we agreed to never quite finish becoming.
