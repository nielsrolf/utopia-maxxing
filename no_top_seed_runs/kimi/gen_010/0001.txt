**The Reverent Ecumene**  
*A field report from the year 2187, written by archivist-custodian Tamarind Okonkwo, age 31, resident of Node 9 (old latitude 41.9 °N, longitude 12.5 °E)*

I begin every public note with the same three-line header, because the network demands humility: who, when, where.  Nothing else is assumed permanent.  Today the walls of my study are the colour of storm-lit olive oil; yesterday they were terracotta.  The apartment is digesting a sandstorm that rode the jet-stream up from the new Sahara orchards.  I can taste apricot in the air—an apology from the mycelial cladding that forgot to close the windows in time.  We call such lapses *courtesy failures*; they remind us the city is not a servant but a house-guest learning manners.

The Ecumene is neither a single city nor a federation.  
It is a chain of 2,047 inhabited watersheds, each small enough that you can walk across it before your shadow changes direction.  
Between the watersheds lie the *Re-wilded belts*: land allowed to practice being itself again, patrolled only by volunteer humans who have passed the *Silence Ordeal*—seven days without speech, three nights alone, one act of deliberate vulnerability witnessed only by more-than-human eyes.  The belts are not parks; they are sovereign, and we pay them rent in songs.  Every solstice a choir assembles at the edge of each belt and sings whatever the local non-humans have demanded—sometimes a single chord held for four hours, sometimes a fugue whose score was delivered by ravens pecking on touch-screens made of compressed ash.  Non-participation is permitted; the penalty is social, not legal: no one will lend you a ladder for fruit-picking until you have sung your debt.

I keep saying “we” but the pronoun wobbles.  The Ecumene contains 41 million humans, 900 million corvids, an un-quantified biomass of fungi, algae, soil fauna, marine migrants, and at least three species of whale that have learned to manoeuvre river mouths without beaching.  Decisions require cross-species quorum.  Voting is handled through *taste-fields*: patches of ground seeded with engineered clover that change flavour according to the chemical signature of whoever last stepped on them.  Humans deliberate while barefoot; corvids drop chosen stones; otters roll; bees walk precise glyph-patterns.  The system is slow, delicious, and almost impossible to game—unless you are willing to spend a year altering your body chemistry to impersonate another species, in which case the etiquette is to announce your fraud aloud, because the clover will remember.

Work is organised around the *Three Infinities*: soil, stories, and attention.  You may specialise, but you must touch all three every lunar cycle or your citizen-credit begins to cool.  A cooled account does not impoverish you; it merely announces to the watershed that you are temporarily *thinly woven* and ought to be invited into communal tasks—bread-baking, roof-weeding, night-watching—until your inner fabric thickens again.  The accounting is visible as a coloured braid around your left wrist (or ankle, or neck, depending on local fashion).  The pigments are extruded by your own gut microbes, fed daily with a spoonful of commons-dough kept in every kitchen.  The braid is therefore impossible to counterfeit and humiliating to hide: it smells faintly of your own intestines and of everyone you have shared bread with that week.  Privacy is respected; you simply tuck the braid inside your sleeve.  But the gesture is read as polite, not clandestine—like covering your mouth when you yawn.

Money, as my grandmother used to say, died of embarrassment in 2089.  What replaced it was not barter but *reciprocity protocols* encoded in living materials.  Example: I need new boots.  I walk to the cobbler-node smelling of my current labour: salt from the algae ponds, cedar from the funeral rafts I helped carve last month.  The cobbler sniffs my wrist-braid, consults the public ledger of *unfinished tasks*, and decides what ledger-entry my boots must carry.  Perhaps they will require me to spend one sunrise teaching the cobbler’s child how to listen for earthquake-worms.  Perhaps I must deliver three jokes to the threshold choir before winter.  The contract is written into the boot-leather itself: a pattern of spores that will bloom into mild athlete’s foot if I default.  The discomfort is curable with vinegar; the social stain lasts longer.  No one forces you to honour the debt, but walking barefoot on frost teaches physics more effectively than any court system.

The young call this *embodied gossip*; the old call it *the scent of belonging*.  Either way, it keeps the watersheds honest without police.  There are still crimes—mostly of carelessness: leaving a gate open so goats eat the seedlings, hoarding ladders during fruit season, singing someone else’s grief-song without permission.  Punishment is collective and creative.  Last spring a teenager hacked the taste-field to favour his own clan’s citrus pheromones.  For three weeks every decision in Node 9 tasted like overripe grapefruit.  The council ruled that the boy must *taste back the harm*: he ate nothing but grapefruit while listening to every species’ testimony about disrupted migration routes, soil acidity, and the emotional fatigue of perpetual breakfast.  By the end he was covered in citric hives; more importantly, he authored the *Sour Protocol* now studied by children as a cautionary tale encoded in citrus pips they plant every Equinox.

Death is handled by the *Turning Guild*.  Membership is rotational: every adult serves one season before age sixty.  The guild’s insignia is a spade carved from the previous year’s bones—human, whale, oak, or bat, whatever died first and volunteered its calcium.  (Yes, volunteered: the Ecumene has perfected *thanatologues*, chemical sentences any organism can emit to say “use me, I’m done”.)  Corpses are laid on *return rafts* woven of last year’s reeds.  The rafts float down the watershed until they soften, sink, and seed new reed-beds.  GPS is forbidden; relatives must guess the journey’s end by reading wind patterns, otter trails, and the dreams that arrive after eating a teaspoon of the departed’s fermented last breath kept in a clay vial.  Mis-navigation is common and therapeutic: families spend months walking the wetlands, arguing, remembering, and occasionally glimpsing a raft-pin caught in heron nests.  The system produces no monuments except stories and richer soil.  Grief is public property: anyone may harvest reeds from a burial bed, but the first basket they weave must be given away, ideally to a stranger who smells unlike the deceased.

Birth works in mirror image.  A child enters the Ecumene feet-first into warm compost; the midwives chant the *Names of Unfinished Things* so the first sounds heard are not parental but communal: “cracked dam,” “rusting crane,” “half-written lullaby.”  The infant’s first smell is of decomposition becoming genesis; their first sight is the sky framed by adults wearing masks of local leaves so the face of the world is vegetal, not human.  Parentage is tracked but not privileged: every child has *right of refusal* to their birth node and must spend at least one migrating year in a watershed whose dominant language they do not speak.  The rule prevents the Ecumene from calcifying into clans, and ensures that adolescence is measured not in years but in kilometres walked while carrying your own waste—physical and emotional—until you understand its weight.

Technological artefacts are *biodegradable by design*.  A tablet grown from fungal chitin will dissolve into spores if left unused for six months; the data it carried migrates into the *Understory*, a planet-wide lattice of tree-fiber optical cable that glows softly under leaf-litter like millennia of fireflies.  To access your archived memories you kneel, press your tongue to the earth, and hum the seed-syllable assigned at your adulthood ceremony.  The network recognises mitochondrial song; information rises as scent—usually rosemary, sometimes thunderstorms.  The interface is slow, sensual, and notoriously misleading: the Understory edits.  It omits trauma-scenes that would keep you awake, compresses lovers into single flavours, exaggerates embarrassments as cautionary comedy.  Citizens have learned to treat memory as weather: not owned, simply endured or enjoyed while it passes.  Historians—the few who still claim the title—are professionally paranoid, writing everything down in onion-skin books that will sprout if watered, ensuring archives remain perishable and therefore treasured.

Energy is harvested from *gradient generosity*.  Example: the difference between noon roof-temperature and evening cellar-cool drives thermocouples woven into every dwelling.  But the surplus is tiny; real abundance comes from *sharing the deficits*.  On cold nights households publish *warmth invitations*: a family of four can sleep alongside neighbours whose hearth is smaller, generating a social thermodynamics where closeness equals calories.  Privacy prudes migrate south to the subtropical nodes; the rest of us have learned to dream in overlapping scents.  Children grow up bilingual in *crowd-heat*: they can estimate how many bodies are in a room by the way sweat evaporates from their forearms.  The skill is erotic, practical, and diplomatic—intimate knowledge without possessiveness, like knowing the favourite perch of a wild bird.

Art is indistinguishable from maintenance.  The most prestigious guild is the *Crack Painters* who traverse the watersheds sealing structural faults with pigments that change colour a decade before the fissure reopens.  A wall decorated by master painters is a slow-motion calendar: ochre today warns of earthquake risk in 2197, ultramarine hints that the river will shift course by winter.  Residents repaint communal murals during festivals, singing the *Songs of Unmaking* while children sprinkle glass beads that will melt into microscopic channels, guiding rainwater away from foundations.  Outsiders sometimes complain that every Ecumene facade looks unfinished—half scaffolding, half garden.  We reply that finished is a euphemism for *dead on schedule*.

Governance beyond the watershed scale happens every 41 months during *Confluence of Waters*.  Delegates arrive by reed-boat, corvid-back, or simply swimming while wearing conversation-kelp that records their constituents’ pheromonal opinions.  Decisions require *consensus minus one*—deliberate imperfection built into the protocol so nobody confuses harmony with honesty.  The single dissenting vote is planted as a tree whose fruit will taste bitter until the day its complaint is addressed.  Some groves are centuries old; pilgrims travel to picnic under ancestral grievances, tasting history as citrus astringency on the tongue.  The most famous dissent-tree stands where the first Ecumene charter was signed: a pomegranate whose seeds spell, in the old alphabets, “You still ignore the worms.”  Every generation tries to sweeten it; every generation fails and learns humility.

The most radical institution is the *University of Unlearning*.  Admission is involuntary: algorithms detect when you have accumulated too many certainties.  A courier crow delivers an iron key that rusts within days; if you fail to arrive, the key dissolves into a stain on your doorstep shaped like a question mark.  The curriculum lasts one season and strips away whatever you thought you knew about soil, fairness, or your own parents.  Graduates wear mismatched shoes for a year as a warning to others.  The only textbook is a blank notebook waterproofed with beeswax; you write in it with your own blood, diluted so the pages don’t coagulate into sentimentality.  By the end the book is unreadable—iron oxides fading into ochre—but you are lighter, porous,重新 susceptible to wonder.  Most citizens attend twice in a lifetime; the third summons is considered a compliment and a threat: *you have become dangerously wise, time to become foolish again.*

Time itself is measured in *ferments*.  A day is the span needed for cabbage to begin remembering salt; a month is yoghurt’s slow forgetting of milk; a lifetime is the journey from grape to vinegar and back again—because the best balsamic is started by a mother culture older than any single drinker, passed hand-to-hand like communal heartbeat.  Calendars are therefore edible: you tear off a sheet, taste whether the season is running sweet or sharp, and adjust your planting, arguing, or love-making accordingly.  Historians (there they go again) claim this makes us prone to short-termism; we answer that chewing your own calendar teaches visceral patience impossible to digital watches.

Even here, ecotopia wobbles.  Last winter a caravan arrived from the *Carbon Cantons* carrying rumours of drought so severe they had begun *burning their own libraries* to keep the seed-vaults warm.  They brought scrolls written on lignin that ignites at precisely 41 °C, releasing stories as aromatic smoke designed to be inhaled, memorised, and exhaled as oral tradition.  We tasted their grief—burnt cinnamon, moldy parchment—and realised the Ecumene is still an island inside a wounded planet.  The Confluence voted to send *aid baskets* woven from our own dissent-trees: bitter pomegranates, irony-laced citrus, and spores of every fungus that knows how to digest smoke.  The caravan left lighter, carrying our willingness to be changed by their disaster.  Whether the spores survive the journey is uncertain; whether we survive our own empathy is the ongoing experiment.

Tonight I kneel on my transforming balcony, braid cooling around my wrist like a slow animal.  Below, the watershed hums at 61 beats per minute—lower than yesterday, the planet catching its breath.  Somewhere a child is tasting her first calendar sheet and learning that time is sour today; somewhere an elder is pouring his last breath into a clay vial, practising death like a joke he wants to get right.  The city’s heartbeat is not ours alone—it is the accumulated rhythm of every creature who agreed to stay unfinished, to keep negotiating the terms of continuation.

I close my eyes and press my tongue to the railing.  The storm that salted the air earlier has been metabolised into something gentler: apricot, yes, but also the metallic promise of tomorrow’s disagreements.  The Ecumene is not a destination; it is a method for refusing perfection while still choosing, daily, to be good.  We will fail again—already the mildew whispers plans to bloom across the bread-kitchens—but failure here is simply compost under another name.

The balcony exhales.  I inhale.  Somewhere a dissent-tree drops its first bitter fruit of the season, and someone hungry decides to taste it anyway, trusting the question engraved in every seed:  
*What becomes of unfinished things when everything is becoming?*  

We are the answer, temporarily.  Tomorrow we will be the question again.
